

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (inference accuracy) in crowdsourcing systems. The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, where the taskmaster acts as a transmitter, the crowd workers represent noisy communication channels, and the crowdsourcer functions as a receiver. By framing the problem in this information-theoretic rate-distortion framework, the authors establish fundamental limits on the minimum number of queries required to achieve a target fidelity level. They derive two key theorems: one for scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK), and another for when skill levels are perfectly known (SL-CS). The authors then analyze a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which presents multiple items to workers simultaneously and asks them to identify which items share the same label. They compare kIC's performance against the theoretical bounds and provide numerical results.

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant theoretical connection between crowdsourcing and information theory. The authors successfully bridge two distinct fields, demonstrating how fundamental rate-distortion limits can inform crowdsourcing system design. This conceptual contribution is significant as it opens new avenues for applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing problems, which could inspire future research in this area. The derivation of the theoretical bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) is technically sound and well-supported by rigorous information-theoretic arguments. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides concrete insights into how query design affects system performance.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution by addressing the budget-fidelity trade-off in a comprehensive manner. Unlike previous works that focus on specific aggregation algorithms (e.g., majority voting, Dawid-Skene), this paper identifies the fundamental limits that any query scheme must respect. The authors provide clear theoretical justification for why certain query designs might outperform others, which is particularly useful for practitioners designing crowdsourcing systems.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the modeling assumptions about worker reliability may not fully capture real-world crowdsourcing dynamics. The paper treats worker skill levels as a single channel parameter, merging reliability and skill into one abstraction. In practice, workers often have varying skill levels that could be leveraged in task assignment (e.g., assigning more complex tasks to higher-rated workers), but the paper assumes uniform query allocation across all workers regardless of their skill. This simplification may limit the practical applicability of the results.

Second, the channel models (MSC and SHC) may not adequately represent the full spectrum of worker behavior. The SHC model distinguishes between "spammers" and "hammers," but real workers often exhibit more nuanced behaviors. The paper does not address how the model would handle workers who intentionally degrade quality (adversarial behavior), which could significantly impact system performance. The authors should discuss how their framework might accommodate more complex worker behavior models.

Third, the paper's presentation has several clarity issues. The assumption that the crowdsourcer knows P(B(X)) (stated on page 6, line 246) should be introduced much earlier in the paper, as it's a critical modeling assumption. Several figure references are incorrect (e.g., "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1b" on page 4, line 135), which creates confusion. The numerical results section (Section 4.2) needs better explanation of the curves in Figure 2, particularly the meaning of k=1 (which corresponds to direct labeling queries, as the authors note). The paper also inconsistently uses "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" without proper numbering.

Regarding significance, while the paper establishes interesting theoretical limits, it's unclear how these results would translate into practical improvements for real crowdsourcing systems. The paper would benefit from more concrete examples or case studies demonstrating how the theoretical bounds could guide real-world system design.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-structured, but the connection between the information-theoretic framework and the crowdsourcing application could be made more explicit. The transition from the communication model to the crowdsourcing context is somewhat abrupt in places. The authors could enhance clarity by providing a more detailed discussion of how the theoretical results map to practical crowdsourcing scenarios.

The paper's originality is strong in its theoretical approach, though the k-ary incidence coding scheme itself is not entirely novel (as noted in the references to prior work on incidence coding). The main contribution is the information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing as a whole, rather than the specific query scheme.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with an interesting theoretical contribution to the crowdsourcing literature. The fundamental limits established provide valuable insights for system designers, though the practical applicability of the results could be strengthened by addressing the modeling assumptions. The paper would benefit from clearer presentation of key assumptions and better explanation of the numerical results.

Strengths:
- Novel information-theoretic framework connecting crowdsourcing to rate-distortion theory
- Rigorous derivation of fundamental limits (Theorems 2 and 3)
- Well-motivated analysis of k-ary incidence coding
- Clear theoretical justification for query design

Weaknesses:
- Over-simplified worker reliability model (merges skill and reliability)
- Limited discussion of practical applicability
- Insufficient explanation of key assumptions (P(B(X)) knowledge)
- Several clarity issues (figure references, numerical results explanation)
- Channel models don't fully capture worker behavior complexity

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding crowdsourcing systems, which is valuable for the NIPS community. While the modeling assumptions could be strengthened, the theoretical insights are compelling and well-supported. The paper would benefit from addressing the clarity issues noted above, but these are relatively minor compared to the paper's theoretical contribution.
