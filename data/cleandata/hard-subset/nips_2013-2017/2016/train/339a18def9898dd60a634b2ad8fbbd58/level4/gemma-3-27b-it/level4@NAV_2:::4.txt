The paper investigates fundamental limits in digital crowdsourcing, modeling it as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyzing it through an information-theoretic lens. The core idea is to characterize the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy of inference) in a crowdsourcing system where workers possess varying and imperfect skill levels. This is achieved by mapping the crowdsourcing process to a joint source-channel coding framework, where the taskmaster is the transmitter, the crowdsourcer the receiver, and workers represent noisy channels. The authors establish theoretical lower bounds on the required budget for a given fidelity and then analyze the performance of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, in relation to these bounds. The parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing setup is a compelling contribution, potentially opening avenues for applying information-theoretic tools to the analysis and design of crowdsourcing systems.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel application of information theory to a practical problem, providing fundamental limits on crowdsourcing performance. The rigorous analysis and derivation of theoretical bounds are well-executed. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding and its subsequent analysis offer a concrete example and a potential avenue for practical system design. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, presenting a logical flow of ideas from system modeling to theoretical analysis and practical considerations.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The assumption of directly mapping the crowdsourcing problem to a point-to-point communication model, while facilitating the application of existing information-theoretic results, may oversimplify the real-world complexities of crowdsourcing. Specifically, the model aggregates worker reliability and skill level into a single channel representation. In practice, workers often have associated ratings reflecting their skill, and the proposed model doesn’t readily accommodate this. Further, the channel models employed may not fully capture the nuances of worker behavior, particularly the potential for adversarial or strategically misleading responses. The explanation of the numerical results is somewhat limited; the curves in Fig. 2 could benefit from clearer labeling and a more detailed discussion of the observed performance trends. For example, the meaning of the k=1 case is unclear. The assumption that P(B(X)) is known, mentioned on page 6, line 246, would be more appropriately stated earlier in the paper. There are minor typographical errors (e.g., page 4, lines 135-136, referencing incorrect figures; repeated use of ‘Theorem’, ‘Lemma’, and ‘Corollary’ without specific numbering).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, offering fundamental insights.
*   Rigorous analysis and derivation of theoretical bounds.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Potential to stimulate further research in this area.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Simplifying assumptions that may limit the practical relevance of the results.
*   Limited discussion and explanation of the numerical results.
*   Minor typographical errors and stylistic inconsistencies.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing. While the simplifying assumptions should be acknowledged, the novel application of information theory and the rigorous analysis justify acceptance, contingent upon addressing the minor issues regarding clarity of the numerical results and typographical corrections. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
