This paper frames the problem of label crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic context to derive fundamental limits on the trade-off between the query budget and the final label fidelity. The authors model the process as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. In this analogy, the true labels of a dataset are an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster acts as an encoder designing queries, the crowd workers function as a noisy channel with a random state (representing their skill levels), and the crowdsourcer performs the role of a decoder to infer the final labels. Using this model, the paper applies established JSCC results to find the asymptotic minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error rate. The analysis considers scenarios where worker skills are unknown to the encoder and either known or unknown to the decoder. The paper also investigates a specific query scheme, "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC), and derives a performance lower bound for it under a spammer-hammer worker model.

Overall, the paper is well-written and introduces an interesting and novel perspective on the crowdsourcing problem. This JSCC formulation is a commendable first step toward a rigorous, information-theoretic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems.

However, there are several points that could strengthen the work:

1.  The central results in Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 are derived using the source-channel separation theorem. The paper applies this theorem to a channel with a random state unknown to the encoder. The validity of the separation principle in this specific setting is not self-evident. The paper would be more technically sound if it provided a formal justification, either by including a proof or citing existing literature that establishes this result for channels with state unknown at the transmitter.

2.  The proposed model simplifies the crowdsourcing process by assuming that the encoder and decoder only have access to the labels, not the items themselves (e.g., the images being labeled). This may be an overly restrictive assumption, as the item content can serve as valuable side information in practical scenarios. A more convincing and realistic model would incorporate this side information, particularly at the decoder or at both the encoder and decoder, which could significantly alter the derived performance limits.

3.  The asymptotic nature of the analysis, while providing valuable theoretical insight, may limit its direct relevance to practical crowdsourcing applications that involve finite datasets and budgets. A non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits in the finite blocklength regime would be more impactful. The authors may find recent work on non-asymptotic JSCC, such as that by Kostina and Verdu (e.g., "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE T-IT, 2013), to be a useful direction for extending this analysis to settings of more practical significance.

4.  A minor point on clarity: in Section 4.2, the discussion of Figure 2 states that the lower bounds for kIC "improve" as k and q grow. A more precise phrasing would be that the bound becomes smaller, suggesting better potential performance for the kIC scheme. The term "improve" is ambiguous without a corresponding upper bound to compare against.
