

The paper addresses crowdsourcing aggregation through an information-theoretic lens, establishing fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity tradeoff. The authors' insights and model present a compelling, potentially useful approach to designing aggregation techniques. However, the exposition could be significantly strengthened to better convey these contributions.

While I appreciate the authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to non-information-theory readers, the heavy reliance on channel coding terminology (e.g., "distortion," "codes," "channels") makes the core ideas difficult to follow. I recommend rephrasing most key results directly in terms of the crowdsourcing application—e.g., expressing fidelity guarantees in terms of consensus accuracy or error rates rather than information-theoretic distortion measures. The high-level intuition from the results (e.g., the concluding remarks of Section 3) should be highlighted more prominently early in the paper, alongside a clear, concise takeaway for practitioners. A brief summary paragraph outlining the practical implications would greatly enhance the paper’s impact.

The distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" in Section 3 (lines 106–107) caused significant confusion. Given that "crowdsourcing" is a portmanteau of "crowd" and "outsourcing," implying the process begins *before* worker involvement, the terminology feels conceptually inconsistent. Clarifying this distinction or revising the terms would improve readability.

Additionally, the related work section should cite the growing line of research on robust economic incentives for workers (often termed "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"). This work increasingly addresses varying worker abilities and explores the integration of incentives with sophisticated aggregation methods—highly relevant context for the paper’s contributions. Omitting this risks positioning the work as disconnected from a major parallel strand of research.

Minor points:
- Line 84: "is reviewed in" → "are reviewed in"
- Line 106: Clarify the "crowdsourcer" vs. "taskmaster" distinction (see above).
- Line 122: Correct typo: "A A(X1,X2)" → "A(X₁,X₂)"
- Lines 275–276: The phrase "In case..." is grammatically unclear; rephrase for directness.
- Figure 2: The caption lacks sufficient detail to interpret the results. A more descriptive caption would clarify the axes, key observations, and their relevance.

Overall, the paper’s theoretical framework is valuable, but its accessibility and practical relevance would be substantially improved by grounding the results more explicitly in crowdsourcing terminology and practices. The core ideas merit attention, but the current presentation obscures their applicability.
