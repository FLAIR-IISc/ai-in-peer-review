This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of digital crowdsourcing, establishing lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a specified probability of error. The authors model workers as parallel, independent channels, considering both symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to account for malicious or unengaged workers who respond randomly with probability *q*. A key constraint is the assumption of non-adaptive coding – the queries are determined upfront and do not depend on previous responses.

The analysis explores two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer possesses complete knowledge of each worker’s skill level, and another where this information is unavailable. In both cases, the taskmaster, responsible for task distribution, is assumed to have no prior knowledge of worker skills and therefore assigns an equal number of tasks to each worker. A particularly interesting result is the derivation of a necessary condition for the minimum required rate – specifically, the number of queries per task – when employing *k*-ary incidence coding with a worker pool modeled as SHC(q). This coding scheme involves presenting workers with *k* items and asking them to determine if they belong to the same or different categories.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors convincingly argue for the novelty of their approach, asserting – and as far as I can tell, correctly – that this is the first information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem. The potential impact is significant; providing theoretical lower bounds allows designers of crowdsourcing projects to estimate the minimum query rate needed to achieve a desired level of accuracy, which could inform pricing strategies and system design. 

However, the paper would benefit significantly from empirical validation. The absence of experiments or simulations to demonstrate how current, real-world crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., those used on Amazon Mechanical Turk) compare to these theoretical limits is a notable weakness. Understanding the gap between theory and practice would strengthen the contribution considerably. 

Regarding the presentation, Section 2.4 feels somewhat lengthy and could be streamlined. Similarly, the material in Section 3.1 appears either unnecessary or could be significantly condensed. Prioritizing proof sketches over exhaustive detail within the main body of the paper would improve readability and focus. While the theoretical results are interesting, accessibility is hampered by the lack of intuition gained from seeing *how* the proofs unfold. A more concise exposition of the preliminary material and a greater emphasis on the core ideas driving the proofs would be beneficial. 

I did not evaluate the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the completeness or correctness of the proofs contained therein. 

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and novel theoretical contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. Addressing the lack of experimental validation and streamlining certain sections would substantially strengthen the work and increase its impact. Despite these minor concerns, I believe the paper represents a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference.
