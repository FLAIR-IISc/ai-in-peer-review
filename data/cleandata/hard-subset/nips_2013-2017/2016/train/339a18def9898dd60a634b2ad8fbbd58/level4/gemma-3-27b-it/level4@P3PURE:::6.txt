This work presents an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff for label crowdsourcing, leveraging rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of this bound is sound, and if this is indeed the first application of these tools to the crowdsourcing problem, it represents a potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the novelty and practical significance of the paper remain questionable. The derivation of the information-theoretic bound appears relatively straightforward, and the bound itself risks being excessively loose. 

The core issue lies in the differences between the crowdsourcing setting and the classical channel coding problem. While channel coding enjoys significant flexibility in codebook design—allowing for techniques like repetition codes, Hamming codes, and LDPC codes—it is unclear whether comparable freedom exists in designing the sequence of channel inputs, *u<sup>n</sup>*, in the context of crowdsourcing. Specifically, equation (3) implies that nearly all mappings from B(X)<sup>n</sup> to U<sup>(∑mi)</sup> should represent valid, implementable codebooks; otherwise, the resulting channel capacity will be a poor bound. In crowdsourcing, repetition coding (and thus majority voting decoding) is a plausible approach, but beyond this, it’s unclear how to design *u<sup>n</sup>* to enhance error correction. 

A critical distinction is that we cannot, in general, repeatedly query the same worker with the same task—a strategy permissible in channel coding due to the i.i.d. channel assumption—as doing so violates the i.i.d. assumption in equations (1) or (2). Repeated queries would likely elicit identical responses from the same worker, invalidating the assumption of independent and identically distributed outputs. This is just one illustration; more broadly, the constraints inherent in crowdsourcing, as reflected in equation (3), are likely far more restrictive than those faced in traditional channel coding, where an encoding mapping from {0,1}<sup>nR</sup> to {0,1}<sup>n</sup> operates with minimal constraints. Consequently, the proposed information-theoretic bound may be loose due to unacknowledged limitations on possible encoding schemes.  A comparison between the derived bound and the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods would help to assess its tightness and practical value.
