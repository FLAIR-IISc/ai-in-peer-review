

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing using information-theoretic rate-distortion and channel coding frameworks. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system where the dataset represents the source, workers correspond to parallel noisy channels, and query schemes constitute coding strategies. The work derives theoretical bounds for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The authors also introduce and analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme with error-correcting capabilities, providing numerical results that compare against information-theoretic bounds.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by applying information theory to crowdsourcing, a field previously dominated by algorithmic approaches like Dawid-Skene (DS) inference and majority voting. This perspective aligns with recent NIPS work on theoretical foundations in machine learning, such as Zhang et al.'s "Spectral methods meet EM" (NIPS 2014) which established theoretical guarantees for crowdsourcing inference. The authors successfully bridge communication theory with crowdsourcing, drawing appropriate connections to Cover and Thomas's Elements of Information Theory (1991) and El Gamal and Kim's Network Information Theory (2011).

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The theoretical derivations are technically sound, with clear theorems (2 and 3) establishing fundamental bounds. The paper carefully distinguishes between SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios and provides rigorous proofs in the appendices. The kIC analysis (Theorem 4) is particularly strong, demonstrating how query design can leverage error-correcting capabilities.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The communication system model (Figure 1) effectively conveys the core conceptual framework. The distinction between MSC and SHC channel models is well-explained, and the kIC concept is intuitively illustrated with Figures 1b and 1c. The paper adequately informs readers about how to reproduce the results, particularly with the detailed mathematical formulations.

*Originality*: This is the first work I'm aware of that applies rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing for deriving fundamental performance limits. While prior NIPS work has addressed crowdsourcing from algorithmic perspectives (e.g., Whitehill et al. NIPS 2009, Zhang et al. NIPS 2014), this paper provides a theoretical foundation that has been missing from the field. The kIC query scheme represents a novel approach to incorporating error correction into crowdsourcing design.

*Significance*: The work provides a theoretical benchmark for evaluating crowdsourcing systems. The derived bounds establish what is fundamentally achievable, which is critical for evaluating practical systems. The analysis of kIC provides actionable insights for query design that could improve real-world crowdsourcing platforms. The paper's framework also opens doors for future theoretical work on human-in-the-loop computation.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality*: The paper's main weakness is the potential looseness of the information-theoretic bounds. The authors acknowledge that the coding constraints in crowdsourcing (Equation 3) may be significantly more restrictive than in standard channel coding. Specifically, unlike communication systems where the same channel input can be repeated (e.g., using repetition codes), crowdsourcing cannot reuse the same query with the same worker due to the non-iid nature of worker responses to identical queries. This constraint may make the theoretical bounds overly optimistic. The authors should clarify whether the derived bounds are achievable with practical coding schemes or if they represent fundamental limits that cannot be reached in practice.

*Significance*: The paper would be strengthened by a more concrete comparison between the theoretical bounds and state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods. While the paper references DS algorithm and majority voting, it lacks a direct performance comparison showing how much current systems fall short of the theoretical limits. A table comparing the error rates of existing methods against the derived bounds would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

*Clarity*: The interpretation of Theorem 4 requires more elaboration. The draft review correctly questions whether the condition ln(1-q) < 0 is properly handled in the bound derivation. The authors should clarify the relationship between ε̄S and ε̂ in the theorem statement. Additionally, the expression H_M(E(ε)) in Theorem 3 needs better explanation or citation to prior work, as it appears to be a novel extension of standard information-theoretic notation.

*Originality*: While the information-theoretic approach is novel, the paper could better distinguish its contribution from related work. For instance, the "k-ary incidence coding" concept bears some resemblance to the binary incidence matrix approach in Korlakai Vinayak et al.'s "Graph clustering with missing data" (NIPS 2014), though the current paper extends it to a theoretical framework. A clearer comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to crowdsourcing research by establishing fundamental performance limits. The information-theoretic approach is well-motivated and technically sound, and the kIC analysis provides concrete insights for practical implementation. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the potential looseness of the bounds through a more detailed discussion of achievable coding schemes and by including concrete comparisons with existing methods. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

Given the current state of the manuscript, I recommend **weak acceptance**. The theoretical contribution is significant and novel, but the practical relevance could be better demonstrated through additional comparisons with existing crowdsourcing methods and a more thorough discussion of the achievability of the derived bounds. The authors should address the specific concerns regarding Theorem 4 and the interpretation of H_M(E(ε)) in the revision.
