This paper models the problem of label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework, drawing an analogy between crowdsourcing systems and communication over noisy channels. The authors apply rate-distortion theory to derive fundamental bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off. Specifically, they establish the minimum number of queries per item (budget) required to achieve a target error rate (fidelity) under different assumptions about workers' skill levels (e.g., known vs. unknown). The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC).

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work lies in its novel application of information-theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing problem. Framing the budget-fidelity trade-off in terms of rate-distortion theory provides a new and principled perspective for understanding the fundamental limits of what can be achieved. If this is the first work to formally connect these two fields, it could be a significant conceptual contribution. The derivations of the information-theoretic bounds appear to be technically sound and are the main contribution of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical framework is elegant, its practical relevance and the tightness of the derived bounds are questionable due to a potentially flawed analogy between channel coding and crowdsourcing.

The main concern is that information-theoretic bounds (like channel capacity) are meaningful because a vast and well-understood space of codes (e.g., LDPC, Turbo, Polar codes) exists that can provably approach these limits. In contrast, the space of practical "codes" for crowdsourcing—that is, the set of valid and implementable query schemes—seems far more constrained. For the derived bounds to be tight, the encoding mapping in Equation (3) must allow for a rich set of functions. It is unclear, however, if such a rich set of query schemes exists in practice. Beyond simple repetition coding (where majority voting is the natural decoder), the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how more complex error-correcting "codes" could be designed and implemented as query strategies.

A critical difference that highlights this issue is the i.i.d. channel assumption. In communications, one can send the same symbol multiple times over a channel and assume the noise is i.i.d. for each transmission. In crowdsourcing, asking the same worker the same query multiple times would violate this assumption, as the worker would likely give the same (potentially incorrect) answer repeatedly, providing no new information. This is a fundamental constraint on "code design" that does not exist in the classical channel coding problem.

Because the set of permissible query schemes is likely subject to many such fundamental constraints not present in traditional communication systems, the capacity-based bounds derived in the paper risk being very loose. They may represent a theoretical ideal that is unachievable by any practical crowdsourcing strategy.

To address this, it would be highly beneficial to compare the derived bounds with the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms. This would provide a valuable calibration, helping to quantify the gap between the theoretical limit and current practical performance, and thus clarifying the true significance of the bounds.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   In Equation (8), the definition of the rate-distortion function should be more precise. The minimization should be explicitly stated as being over all codes (C, C') that satisfy the distortion constraint, where `E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D_t`.
*   The proof of Theorem 4 is quite brief and could benefit from further elaboration to make the steps clearer to the reader.
*   In the condition presented in Theorem 4, `R < (1/k) * ln(ε̂/ε̄_S) / ln(1-q)`, the term `ln(1-q)` is negative for `q ∈ (0, 1)`. For the inequality to be non-trivial, `ln(ε̂/ε̄_S)` must also be negative, which implies `ε̄_S > ε̂`. Could the authors clarify if this is an implicit assumption or a necessary condition for the theorem to apply?
*   In Equation (11), the denominator includes the term `H_M(E(ε))`, which corresponds to the entropy of the average error probability. Could the authors provide a brief justification or a citation for why the capacity of the "average worker" is used here, as opposed to the average of the workers' capacities, `E[H_M(ε)]`, which appears in Theorem 3 (Equation 12)? A short explanation of the underlying reasoning would improve clarity.
