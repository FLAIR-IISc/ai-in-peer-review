- Paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity (queries per item vs. error) bounds.
- Labels B(X) are the source; workers are noisy channels with two channel families: M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel SHC(q).
- Main results: rate–distortion style lower bounds on minimum queries per item for target error under SL-UK (skills unknown) and SL-CS (skills known).
- k-ary incidence coding (kIC) is analyzed as a query code with built-in invalid-response filtering; necessary rate condition derived for SHC worker pools.
- Paper proposes a simple pricing heuristic for kIC that scales with k.
- Complements prior algorithmic aggregation work by providing fundamental information-theoretic limits any method must respect.
- Technical soundness: formulation and bounds follow standard rate–distortion and channel-capacity arguments and are internally consistent.
- SL-UK vs. SL-CS comparison is insightful: unknown skills reduce effective capacity compared to known-skill decoding.
- Key assumptions (independent responses, non-adaptive querying, memoryless channels) are clean but idealized and limit applicability.
- The SHC(q) oracle decoding bound is correctly labeled as a necessary condition; the effect of k (static feedforward) needs clearer formal explanation.
- Empirical support is minimal; numerical section illustrates trends but should include worked numerical examples translating bounds into queries per item.
- Paper is generally well written but difficult for readers without an information-theory background.
- Recommend restating key theorems in crowdsourcing terms, adding intuition and small numerical examples after each theorem.
- The roles of “taskmaster” (encoder) and “crowdsourcer” (decoder) should be defined earlier and more clearly.
- Figure 2 needs a more informative caption, clearer axes/parameters, and explicit statement of takeaways.
- Originality: novel framing of budget–fidelity trade-offs via source–channel coding and fresh analysis of kIC and pricing.
- Significance: provides theoretical benchmarks to guide system designers in budget setting and query-type selection.
- Strengths: principled information-theoretic framework, clear SL-UK/SL-CS comparison, kIC formalization, and practical pricing angle.
- Weaknesses: accessibility issues, limited empirical validation, and some modeling/notation clarity problems.
- Suggested fixes: add worked examples, explain main drivers of each theorem, define variables consistently (X, B(X), N, M, U/V, n, L), and summarize Appendix E cost normalization in main text.
- Discuss adaptivity, worker selection, heterogeneous allocations, and whether adaptivity changes fundamental limits or constants.
- Add related work on incentive-compatible crowdsourcing (peer-prediction, gold questions, bonuses) to cover mechanism-design aspects.
- Fix notational/typo issues and wording (e.g., lines 84, 106, 122, 275–276) and ensure consistent use of N vs. M and handling of invalid options.
- Pros for acceptance: principled novel limits, new lens on query design, and a bridge between information theory and crowdsourcing literature.
- Cons for acceptance: presentation/accessibility issues, strong idealized assumptions, and missing incentive-mechanism discussion.
