- Paper frames crowdsourced label aggregation as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem, modeling dataset as a source, workers as noisy channels, queries as encoder, and inference as decoder.  
- Authors derive fundamental budget-fidelity bounds for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills, analyzed for M-ary Symmetric Channels (MSC) and Spammer-Hammer Channels (SHC).  
- Introduces a practical query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), and analyzes its performance to connect theory with practice.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior work by aiming to establish algorithm-agnostic fundamental limits rather than designing specific aggregation algorithms.  
- Strength: novel and significant conceptualization that provides benchmarks for any crowdsourcing system.  
- Strength: solid theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) including the insight that unknown skills limit capacity by the average worker while known skills relate to the average of worker capacities.  
- Strength: bridging theory and practice via the kIC query scheme.  
- Weakness: paper is difficult for non-experts; heavy information-theory language obscures practical crowdsourcing intuition.  
- Suggestion: add a dedicated conclusion summarizing actionable takeaways for ML/crowdsourcing practitioners.  
- Suggestion: more prominently highlight high-level insights (e.g., Section 3 result) and restate main theorems in accessible, application-oriented terms.  
- Related-work gap: omits literature on crowdsourcing mechanism design and incentive-compatible worker models that address heterogeneity and quality.  
- Presentation issues: clarify the encoder/decoder roles (taskmaster vs crowdsourcer), improve Figure 2 caption, rephrase unclear sentence(s), and fix typos (lines 84 and 122).  
- Overall: highly original and impactful theoretical framework whose main shortcoming is accessibility; improving clarity would make it a strong conference contribution.
