The paper "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" presents a novel approach to modeling the problem of labeling data from crowdsourcing using information theory. The authors model each worker as a noisy channel, specifically considering two types of channels: symmetric memoryless channels without feedback and spammer/hammer channel models. This formulation reduces the problem to a coding problem for the channel, allowing the authors to study the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.

The paper explores the trade-off between distortion and budget, where the budget is mapped to the rate of the code. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity, considering two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). These bounds serve as ultimate performance limits, oblivious to the type of code used.

One of the key contributions of the paper is the introduction of k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a query scheme that requires workers to label a subset of k items at a time. The authors analyze the performance of kIC with a spammer-hammer worker pool and derive a lower bound on its performance. They also present numerical results, which demonstrate that the coding-theoretic lower bounds for kIC improve as k and the reliability of the workers (q) grow.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers with a basic understanding of information theory. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain the context and significance of their research.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to modeling crowdsourcing using information theory.

Weaknesses:

* The analysis is limited to two specific channel models, and it is unclear how the results would generalize to other models or scenarios.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical implications of the results and how they could be applied in real-world crowdsourcing systems.
