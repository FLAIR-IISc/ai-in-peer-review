This paper tackles the aggregation of worker outputs in crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools and terminology, and it establishes fundamental limits for the budget–fidelity tradeoff. I did not fully understand the paper (see below), but I found the model and insights to be very interesting and potentially useful for designing crowdsourcing aggregation methods. I would have liked the high-level intuition derived from these results (e.g., the discussion at the end of Section 3) to be emphasized more prominently, along with a brief conclusion summarizing the key takeaways (I realize space is limited).

While I appreciate the authors’ efforts to make the paper accessible to readers not deeply familiar with information theory, it was still quite difficult to follow. I recommend restating most of the results directly in the language of the crowdsourcing application, rather than relying on the terminology of channels, codes, and distortion.

Please also include in the related work the complementary (and increasingly connected) literature on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This line of work addresses heterogeneous worker abilities and, more recently, how to combine incentive schemes with more sophisticated aggregation methods.
