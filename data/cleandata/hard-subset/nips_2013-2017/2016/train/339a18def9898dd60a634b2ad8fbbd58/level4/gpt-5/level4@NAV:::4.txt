The paper studies a crowdsourcing framework in which a taskmaster decomposes a large task (e.g., an inference problem) into smaller queries that are assigned to workers. A crowdsourcer collects the workers’ responses and processes them to complete the original inference. The model allows workers to have varying skill levels and to be unreliable in reporting. The system’s goal is to achieve a target fidelity or quality of the final outcome at the crowdsourcer, even when some workers provide incorrect information. This necessitates careful design of the small tasks (queries) assigned by the taskmaster. The total number of queries issued to workers is treated as the budget of the crowdsourcing system. The paper examines the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of the crowdsourcer’s inference). This trade-off is derived by mapping the crowdsourcing pipeline to joint source–channel coding: the taskmaster acts as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable worker-to-crowdsourcer links form the communication channel. Under this mapping, a lower bound on the required budget for a desired fidelity follows from standard information-theoretic results. This bound is independent of the specific query design at the taskmaster and the processing algorithm at the crowdsourcer. The authors then analyze a particular taskmaster scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and study its performance, comparing it against the information-theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model.

The reviewer finds the analogy between the rate–distortion framework and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers compelling, and expects it could encourage further research applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing. However, the reviewer is unsure whether the presented results reflect real-world crowdsourcing systems in a sufficiently realistic way. One possible reason is the desire to align the crowdsourcing problem exactly with a point-to-point communication model so that existing information-theoretic results can be applied with minimal effort. It would be helpful if the authors could address the following points.
1) It appears that worker reliability and skill are merged into a single channel model. The paper also assumes the taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of skill. In practice, workers often have ratings that reflect proficiency, which could inform differentiated allocation.
2) How realistic are the channel models used to capture worker unreliability? How are workers who possess task knowledge yet behave adversarially to degrade fidelity taken into account?

The explanation of the numerical results is not very clear. The labeling of the curves in Fig. 2 can be improved. What does the k=1 case represent? With k=1 one cannot present a pair and ask whether they share the same label; does this case correspond to directly asking for the label of a single item?

On page 6, line 246, the authors assume knowledge of P(B(X)). This assumption should have been stated much earlier.

On page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b.” On page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c.”

The manuscript repeatedly uses “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” even when these are not accompanied by specific numbers. In such instances, using lowercase “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” would be more appropriate.
