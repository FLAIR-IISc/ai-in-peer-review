This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. In this analogy, the set of true labels for a dataset is treated as an information source, the taskmaster acts as an encoder, the crowd workers are modeled as parallel noisy channels, and the workers' varying skill levels correspond to random channel states. Finally, the crowdsourcer, which aggregates the workers' responses, functions as the decoder.

Using this framework, the paper derives asymptotic fundamental limits on the trade-off between the query budget (rate) and the achievable fidelity (distortion). The analysis leverages the source-channel separation theorem to establish the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error probability. The authors consider two scenarios: one where worker skill levels are unknown to the decoder (SL-UK), and another where they are known (SL-CS). Additionally, the paper introduces a specific query scheme called "k-ary Incidence Coding" (kIC) and derives a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer-hammer worker model.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel application of a JSCC framework to model the crowdsourcing process. This provides a new and principled perspective for understanding the ultimate performance limits of crowdsourcing systems, moving beyond the analysis of specific algorithms to establish bounds that hold for any possible query and inference scheme. This is a valuable theoretical contribution that could inspire new ways of thinking about crowdsourcing system design.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The analogy between the components of a crowdsourcing system and a communication system is clearly explained, making the information-theoretic model accessible.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Technical Justification of the Source-Channel Separation Theorem:** The core results in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on the direct application of the source-channel separation theorem. However, the standard theorem applies to a fixed, memoryless channel. The model in this paper involves a channel with a random state (the worker's skill level), which is unknown at the encoder and may be known or unknown at the decoder. While it is plausible that the separation theorem still holds in this context, this is a non-trivial extension that requires formal justification. The authors should either provide a proof sketch or cite an existing result from the information theory literature that establishes the validity of the separation principle for channels with random states unknown to the transmitter. This is a crucial point for ensuring the technical soundness of the main results.

2.  **Model Realism and Side Information:** The proposed model is somewhat restrictive because it assumes that the encoder (taskmaster) and decoder (crowdsourcer) only have access to the labels (the source `B(X)`) and the workers' responses, respectively. In many practical crowdsourcing applications, both parties also have access to the underlying data items (`X`, e.g., the images themselves). This rich data can be modeled as side information. In a JSCC context, side information available at the decoder, or at both the encoder and decoder, can significantly improve performance and change the fundamental limits. A model that incorporates this side information would be more realistic and could lead to more compelling and practically relevant bounds.

3.  **Practicality of Asymptotic Results:** The derived limits are asymptotic, assuming a large number of data items (`L -> ∞`) and a large block length (`n -> ∞`). While theoretically elegant, the practical implications for real-world crowdsourcing tasks, which operate on finite datasets and with finite budgets, are not immediately clear. A non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be significantly more useful for practitioners. The authors are encouraged to explore recent advances in finite-blocklength information theory, which provide tools for such an analysis (e.g., Kostina and Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Trans. IT, 2013).

4.  **Minor Point on Clarity:** In Section 4.2, when discussing Figure 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC "improve" as k and q increase. This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. It would be clearer to state that the performance of the kIC scheme is expected to improve (i.e., the required rate for a given fidelity decreases), and thus the derived lower bound on the error becomes tighter or more informative.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a novel and interesting information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing.
    *   It provides a valuable theoretical perspective by establishing fundamental performance limits.
    *   The work is well-written and the core ideas are clearly presented.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The technical validity of the main results hinges on an unstated assumption about the applicability of the source-channel separation theorem to the specific channel model used.
    *   The model's simplifying assumptions (e.g., ignoring side information from the data items) limit its practical relevance.
    *   The reliance on asymptotic results makes it difficult to draw concrete conclusions for practical, finite-sized crowdsourcing problems.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces an interesting and original approach to analyzing crowdsourcing. The framing of the problem in a JSCC context is a definite strength. However, the technical arguments require more rigorous justification, and the model itself could be extended to be more realistic. The post-rebuttal addition of a reference to non-asymptotic analysis is a strong suggestion; addressing the finite-length regime would substantially increase the impact of this work. While the core idea is promising, the current manuscript feels more like a first step than a complete piece of work due to the aforementioned weaknesses.
