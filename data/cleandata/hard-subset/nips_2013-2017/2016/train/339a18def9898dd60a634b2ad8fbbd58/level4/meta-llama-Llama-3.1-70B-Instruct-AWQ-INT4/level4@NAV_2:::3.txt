The problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing is addressed using the language and tools of information theory, and the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff are given. The paper presents a novel and interesting take on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, but some parts of the presentation were not fully clear. The high-level intuition and insights stemming from these results could be highlighted more prominently, and a conclusion summarizing the key takeaways would be beneficial. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, but the use of information theory terminology and concepts may make it challenging for non-experts to follow. Restating the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than retaining the language of channels and codes, could improve clarity. Additionally, the authors could provide more context and explanations for the crowdsourcing framework and the different components involved, such as the distinction between the crowdsourcer and taskmaster.

The related work section is comprehensive, but it could be improved by mentioning the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, which also discusses varying worker abilities and combining incentives with aggregation methods.

The technical content of the paper appears to be sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the crowdsourcing problem using information theory. The results, including Theorems 2 and 3, provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. The presentation of the k-ary incidence coding scheme and its performance analysis is also clear and well-organized.

Some minor suggestions for improvement include correcting typos (e.g., "is reviewed in" should be "are reviewed in" on page 84) and providing more detailed captions for figures (e.g., Figure 2). Additionally, some sentences could be rephrased for clarity, such as the one on pages 275-276.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting take on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques.
* The use of information theory provides a unique perspective on the problem.
* The results provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper may be challenging for non-experts to follow due to the use of information theory terminology and concepts.
* The presentation could be improved by providing more context and explanations for the crowdsourcing framework and the different components involved.
* The related work section could be expanded to include the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a valuable contribution to the field. With some revisions to improve clarity and presentation, it has the potential to be a strong contribution to the conference.
