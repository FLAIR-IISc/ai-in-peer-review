

This paper investigates digital crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, establishing lower bounds on the minimum number of worker queries per task required to achieve a specified error probability. The authors model workers as parallel independent channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMCs) represent skilled workers' error patterns, while a spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) models spammers who provide random answers with probability 1-q. The coding scheme is assumed nonadaptive, and the analysis considers two scenarios: (1) the taskmaster (who assigns tasks) knows all worker skill levels, and (2) the taskmaster lacks any skill information. Critically, in both cases, the taskmaster assigns an equal number of tasks to every worker, reflecting a practical constraint where worker skills are unknown during task distribution. The paper derives a necessary condition on the minimum query rate (queries per task) for k-ary incidence coding—where workers compare k items for similarity/difference—under an SHC(q) worker pool.

The work is well-structured and clearly written, making the technical contributions accessible. However, the absence of empirical validation is a notable limitation. The paper does not include experiments or simulations demonstrating how existing crowdsourcing methods (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) perform relative to the derived theoretical bounds. Such analysis would strengthen the practical relevance of the results by quantifying the gap between current practices and information-theoretic limits.

Regarding structure, Section 2.4 could be condensed to improve flow, and Section 3.1 appears redundant or overly detailed given the paper's focus. The authors would benefit from replacing this material with concise proof sketches for the key theorems, which would better serve readers seeking to grasp the core arguments without wading through excessive detail.

The novelty of the approach is compelling. To the best of my knowledge, this represents the first information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, establishing rigorous bounds where prior work lacked such formal guarantees. This framework offers significant practical value: it enables designers to determine the minimum query rate needed to achieve a target error probability for a given task, directly informing cost estimation and pricing strategies for crowdsourcing platforms. The insights could guide efficient resource allocation, particularly in scenarios where worker skill heterogeneity is unknown.

The paper’s central contribution—providing achievable and necessary rate conditions under realistic worker models—addresses a critical gap in the field. The SHC(q) model effectively captures spammer behavior, while the SDMC framework handles skilled workers, creating a robust foundation for the analysis. The derivation of the k-ary incidence coding rate bound under SHC(q) is particularly valuable for practical implementation.

Minor suggestions for improvement include: (1) clarifying the relationship between the taskmaster (assigner) and the crowdsourcer (decision-maker) in the problem setup; (2) briefly motivating why k-ary incidence coding is preferable to simpler schemes for the stated error model; and (3) explicitly stating the implications of the "no skill knowledge" case for real-world deployment, where worker profiling is often infeasible.

I did not review the appendix; thus, I cannot comment on the completeness of the proofs presented there. Given the paper’s main contributions, however, the core results appear sound based on the presented arguments.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The work advances the field by providing the first information-theoretic foundation for crowdsourcing rate analysis, offering designers a principled way to estimate minimal resource requirements. While the lack of empirical validation is a drawback, the theoretical rigor and novelty justify publication, especially given NIPS’s focus on foundational advances. The paper would benefit from minor structural edits to enhance readability, but these do not undermine its core value. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the structural suggestions above.
