

The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item. They model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, where the channel state (representing worker skill levels) is unknown at the encoder (taskmaster) but may or may not be known at the decoder (crowdsourcer). The item labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as the encoder observing only the labels, the crowd workers as multiple channel uses, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder observing only the workers' labels. Using existing JSCC results, the authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits for query complexity. They further establish a lower bound on the error probability for "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC) under a specific model. Overall, this is a well-written paper that represents a promising first step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing.  

Technical comments:  
1. The authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem (Theorems 2–3) to derive results for JSCC with random channel state. However, they do not justify that separation holds when the channel state is unknown at the encoder (but may be known at the decoder). This justification is essential; the authors should either prove the separation theorem under these conditions or cite an existing proof.  
2. The model assumes the encoder (taskmaster) and decoder (crowdsourcer) observe only the labels, ignoring their potential access to the original item data. This original data constitutes valuable side information: if available at both encoder and decoder, or solely at the decoder, it could significantly improve performance. The model would be more convincing and realistic if it incorporated such side information.  
3. In Section 4.2, the authors state that "the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow" (referring to Fig. 2). This phrasing is misleading; the lower bound *decreases* as k and q increase, implying potentially better achievable performance (not that the bound itself improves, as no upper bound is provided for comparison).  

Edited following authors' response: The JSCC approach is interesting, but as noted by another reviewer, a nonasymptotic characterization of crowdsourcing’s fundamental limits would be more practically useful. The authors should consider recent nonasymptotic JSCC results, such as Kostina and Verdu (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, 2013), which establish finite-blocklength converses for lossy JSCC.
