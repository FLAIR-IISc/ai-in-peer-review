- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem and uses rate-distortion theory to derive budget–fidelity limits.
- Models true labels as a source, workers as parallel noisy channels, and queries/inference as encoding/decoding.
- Provides separate analyses for unknown and known worker skill scenarios and introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC).
- Strength: novel, principled application of rate-distortion/information theory to crowdsourcing, connecting to fundamental limits.
- Strength: theoretical derivations and information-theoretic bounds appear technically sound and clearly presented.
- Weakness: practical validity of the crowdsourcing ↔ channel-code analogy is questionable because human-query design is far more constrained than code design in communications.
- Weakness: derived bounds may be too loose to be practically informative, with little evidence that capacity-approaching query schemes exist beyond simple repetition.
- Weakness: assumption of workers as memoryless channels is unrealistic since repeated queries to the same worker yield correlated (not i.i.d.) answers.
- Suggestion: empirically compare state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, spectral methods) on the rate–distortion plane to demonstrate tightness and practical relevance.
- Comment: Equation (8) notation should follow standard rate-distortion form as a minimization over P(Ẑ|Z), not over specific codes C, C′.
- Comment: Theorem 4’s proof needs expanded detail for clarity and self-containment.
- Comment: Clarify the interpretation of ln(1−q) being negative and any implied condition (e.g., ε_S > ε̂) for the bound to be meaningful.
- Comment: Provide justification for the H_M(E(ε)) term in Equation (11), e.g., via Jensen’s inequality and entropy concavity.
