- Novel framing of crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion joint source-channel coding problem linking taskmaster/ crowdsourcer to transmitter/receiver.
- Models workers as noisy channels and studies the budget (queries) vs. fidelity (accuracy) trade-off.
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorems 2 & 3) on queries required for target error rates under unknown vs. known worker skills.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and compares its performance to the theoretical bounds.
- Strength: clear, well-motivated theoretical framework applying information theory to a practical ML problem.
- Strength: rigorous derivations and solid connection to prior work (e.g., Dawid–Skene, majority voting).
- Strength: provides fundamental, scheme-independent limits useful as theoretical benchmarks for crowdsourcing design.
- Key concern: assumption that all workers receive the same number of queries is unrealistic; framework should handle selective assignment by skill/reputation.
- Key concern: channel models (MSC and SHC) are oversimplified—SHC’s spammer/hammer dichotomy ignores graded worker reliability.
- Key concern: practical applicability of the bounds needs more discussion; independence from query scheme may not imply easy implementation.
- Experimental concern: numerical results (Figure 2) are unclear—clarify what k=1 represents and improve curve labels.
- Methodological concern: assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is only stated late and should be declared earlier.
- Presentation issues: typographical errors in figure references ("Figure 1.a" vs "Figure 1a") and inconsistent use/numbering of Theorem/Lemma/Corollary.
- Suggested revisions: extend discussion to nuanced worker models and selective task allocation, clarify experiments, and fix presentation/notation.
- Overall assessment: theoretically interesting and potentially influential but requires clarification of practical modeling and experimental details before acceptance.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, rigorous limits, clear kIC analysis, strong ties to prior work.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical implementation discussion, unrealistic worker assumptions, unclear numerical results, and missing detail on P(B(X)).
