

The paper proposes a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling the taskmaster as a transmitter and the crowdsourcer as a receiver in a joint source-channel coding problem. The core contribution lies in establishing a fundamental trade-off between the budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of inference) using standard information-theoretic lower bounds. This mapping is conceptually interesting and may inspire future work applying information theory to crowdsourcing. However, the realism of the model and several technical points require significant clarification.

The authors model worker unreliability as a communication channel, collapsing worker skill levels and reliability into a single channel parameter. This simplification raises concerns about real-world applicability. In practice, workers possess varying skill levels (e.g., via ratings), and optimal query allocation should account for this heterogeneity. The paper assumes uniform query allocation across all workers, which contradicts typical crowdsourcing platforms where high-skill workers receive more complex tasks. The authors should explicitly address whether their model accommodates worker-specific skill metrics or if this is a limitation.

The channel model’s realism is also questionable. The analysis assumes workers behave stochastically (e.g., random errors), but real systems face adversarial workers who may deliberately degrade fidelity. The paper does not discuss adversarial models or how the proposed scheme handles such cases. Furthermore, the channel model lacks justification for its specific error characteristics—e.g., why it assumes symmetric errors rather than task-dependent biases. The authors should clarify the assumptions underlying their channel model and discuss their alignment with empirical crowdsourcing data.

The numerical evaluation (Fig. 2) suffers from poor presentation. The curves are unlabeled, and the meaning of $k=1$ is unclear. As stated on page 6, line 246, $k=1$ corresponds to direct reporting (no encoding), where workers provide the correct label without query transformation. This should be explicitly defined in the figure caption or main text. The current labeling ("k=1", "k=2", etc.) without context misleads readers about the scheme’s mechanics. The caption should specify: "k=1: Direct reporting; k=2: Binary encoding, etc."

A critical technical oversight is the paper’s inconsistent use of "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary." These terms appear repeatedly without reference to specific numbered results (e.g., "Theorem 1" or "Lemma 3"). This violates standard mathematical writing conventions and undermines rigor. The authors should either assign clear labels or replace these terms with descriptive phrases (e.g., "the following result" or "we establish").

Additional minor issues require correction:
- Page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1a" and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1b" (consistent formatting).
- Page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.b" should reference "Figure 1c" (typo in figure sequence).
- Page 6, line 246: The assumption of knowledge of $P(B|X)$ (not $P(B(X))$) should be introduced earlier, as it is foundational to the analysis. Its omission on page 4 creates a gap in the exposition.

While the theoretical connection to joint source-channel coding is insightful, the model’s simplifications may limit its practical relevance. The authors should:
1. Explicitly discuss how worker skill heterogeneity could be incorporated (e.g., via channel-state information).
2. Justify the channel model against real-world worker behavior (stochastic vs. adversarial).
3. Clarify the $k$-ary coding scheme’s operational meaning (e.g., $k=1$ as direct reporting).
4. Correct figure labels, notation ($P(B|X)$), and terminology ("Theorem" vs. descriptive terms).

The paper’s strength is its theoretical novelty, but its impact hinges on addressing these realism and clarity issues. Without them, the results may not resonate with crowdsourcing practitioners who deal with heterogeneous, potentially adversarial workers. If the authors can strengthen the model’s grounding in real-world dynamics and clarify the technical exposition, this work could make a valuable contribution. As currently written, the gap between the idealized model and practical crowdsourcing systems remains too large.
