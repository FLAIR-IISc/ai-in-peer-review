This paper provides an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff for label crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The authors derive this bound rigorously, and if this is the first application of rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem to the crowdsourcing problem, it could be a significant contribution to the field.

However, the novelty and practical value of this paper are still questionable. The derivation of the information-theoretic bound is relatively straightforward, and the bound may be very loose. A key issue is that, unlike the channel coding problem, where there is considerable freedom in designing codebooks or codewords (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, convolutional codes, RS codes, low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes), it is unclear whether similar freedom exists in designing the sequence of channel inputs u^n. In equation (3), almost all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(\sum mi) (except degenerate ones) should be valid or implementable codebooks; otherwise, the channel capacity may be a loose bound.

For crowdsourcing, simple encoding schemes like repetition codes are possible, where the natural decoding method is majority voting. However, it is unclear how to design u^n to improve error-correcting capabilities beyond this. A striking difference between the channel coding problem and the crowdsourcing problem is that the same query cannot be assigned to the same worker multiple times, as this would violate the iid assumption in equation (1) or (2). In contrast, in communication channels, it is common to assume that the channel is iid, and the same query can be used multiple times for the same channel.

This difference is just one example of the fundamental constraints in equation (3) that are likely to exist in the crowdsourcing problem, unlike in channel coding, where encoding mappings {0,1}^{nR} -> {0,1}^n have few constraints. These constraints may render the proposed information-theoretic bound very loose. To strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial to compare the performance of state-of-the-art methods with the derived information-theoretic bound.
