This paper presents an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, modeling it as joint source-channel coding (JSCC) with a random channel state to derive fundamental, asymptotic limits on the number of queries required per item. In this model, item labels are an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster is an encoder, crowd workers represent multiple uses of a channel, worker skill levels are the random channel states, and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. The channel state is unknown to the encoder and may or may not be known to the decoder. By applying existing results on coding rates for JSCC, the authors establish these fundamental limits. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability for a specific "k-ary incidence coding" scheme under a particular crowdsourcing model. In summary, this is a well-written paper that introduces a novel JSCC-based modeling approach to crowdsourcing.

Some technical comments:

1.  The analysis models the crowdsourcing problem as JSCC with a random channel state and applies the standard source-channel separation theorem to obtain the main results (Theorems 2 and 3). However, the validity of this approach depends on whether the separation theorem holds for channels with a random state that is unknown at the encoder (and either known or unknown at the decoder). While this is likely the case, the authors should provide a proof or cite existing literature to formally justify this critical step in their argument.

2.  The proposed model may be overly restrictive. It assumes the taskmaster (encoder) observes only the item labels and the crowdsourcer (decoder) observes only the worker-provided labels. This overlooks the common scenario where both parties also have access to the original items, which constitutes side information in the JSCC problem. While side information at the encoder alone may not be helpful, its presence at the decoder, or at both encoder and decoder, can significantly improve performance. The model would be more convincing and realistic if it incorporated the potential role of this side information.

3.  In Section 4.2, when commenting on Figure 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) "improve" as k and q grow. Presumably, this is meant to suggest that the code's performance may get better, as the lower bound on the error probability decreases. However, a smaller value for a lower bound does not necessarily mean the bound itself is better (i.e., tighter). Without a corresponding upper bound for comparison, describing this change as an "improvement" of the bound is misleading.

*Edited following authors' response:*
The use of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is an interesting direction. However, as another reviewer pointed out, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practically useful. The authors should consider recent results on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, such as: V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime", IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013.
