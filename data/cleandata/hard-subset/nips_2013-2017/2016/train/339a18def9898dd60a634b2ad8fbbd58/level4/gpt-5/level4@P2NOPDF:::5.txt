This paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework and derives lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a target error probability. Workers are modeled as independent parallel channels. Two channel models are considered: (i) symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture random worker errors, and (ii) the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), where a worker is a perfect “hammer” with probability q and a “spammer” with probability 1−q. The coding scheme is nonadaptive.

The analysis treats two information regimes for the decision-maker (the “crowdsourcer” who aggregates worker responses): one in which the crowdsourcer knows all workers’ skill levels, and one in which it has no knowledge of them. In both regimes, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks) is assumed to be unaware of worker skills, so each worker receives the same number of tasks. The paper also provides a necessary condition on the minimum query rate (queries per task) for k-ary incidence coding—where a worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to the same or different categories—under an SHC(q) worker pool.

The paper is well organized and clearly written. The problem formulation appears novel; to my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, there has been little to no prior analysis of crowdsourcing from a rigorous information-theoretic perspective. The results could be practically useful: they offer guidance on the minimum rate required to meet a desired error probability, which could inform both system design and pricing.

However, the paper lacks empirical validation. There are no experiments or simulations to quantify how close commonly used schemes (e.g., on AMT) come to the proposed limits. Even a small-scale simulation study would strengthen the contribution by illustrating the tightness of the bounds and the potential performance gap in realistic settings.

Regarding exposition, Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 seems unnecessary or could be condensed. In contrast, brief proof sketches or intuitive explanations for the main theorems would be highly valuable for readers, especially those not steeped in information theory.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. It offers a principled foundation for understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing with noisy and heterogeneous workers. I did not read the appendix and thus cannot evaluate the proofs’ completeness or technical correctness.
