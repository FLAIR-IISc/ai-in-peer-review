The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, establishing fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic setting. They model the crowdsourcing problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state. Specifically, the item labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as multiple channel uses, worker skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. Utilizing existing results on coding rates in JSCC, the authors derive fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a specific crowdsourcing model (or, equivalently, channel within their information-theoretic framework). Overall, this is a well-written paper and a potentially valuable first step towards modeling crowdsourcing through the lens of JSCC. 

Here are some technical comments:

1. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as JSCC with random channel state and subsequently apply the standard source-channel separation theorem to obtain their results (Theorems 2 and 3). However, they should first justify the applicability of the separation theorem in this context, where the channel possesses a random state unknown to the encoder but potentially known to the receiver. While I believe the separation theorem does indeed hold under these conditions, the authors should either provide a proof or cite an existing one.

2. The current model depicts the taskmaster as an encoder with access only to item labels, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder with access only to worker responses. This may be overly restrictive, as it neglects the possibility that both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer might have access to the original data (the items themselves). This access to the original data represents potential side information for both the encoder and decoder within the JSCC framework. While side information available only at the encoder may not be beneficial, its presence at both the encoder and decoder, or even at the decoder alone, could significantly improve performance. I would find the model more convincing and realistic if the authors had considered incorporating side information.

3. In Section 4.2, the authors observe that the lower bounds for kIC improve as *k* and *q* increase, referring to the curves in Figure 2. I believe they intend to convey that the lower bound *decreases* as *k* and *q* grow, indicating potentially better code performance. However, it's important to note that a decreasing lower bound doesn’t necessarily represent an “improvement” in the bound itself without a corresponding upper bound for comparison.

Edited following authors’ response: The application of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting. However, as another reviewer noted, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practically useful. The authors should investigate recent work by Kostina and Verdu concerning nonasymptotic converses for JSCC: V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," *IEEE Transactions on Information Theory*, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
