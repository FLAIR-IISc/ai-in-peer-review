- Paper maps crowdsourcing to a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) framework, yielding scheme-agnostic information-theoretic lower bounds on budget for a target fidelity.  
- Analyzes a concrete query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compares its performance to the derived lower bounds.  
- Strength: The JSCC/rate–distortion lens is original and clarifies fundamental limits independent of algorithmic details.  
- Strength: Clear separation between universal lower bounds and specific schemes (kIC) with informative performance comparison.  
- Weakness: Modeling conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel and assumes uniform query allocation across workers, limiting realism.  
- Weakness: Treatment of adversarial behavior is not clearly articulated, raising doubts about robustness to malicious workers.  
- Weakness: Presentation issues—numerical section, figure labeling, and some explanations hinder clarity.  
- Soundness: Information-theoretic derivations are principled under assumptions, but practical relevance depends on channel-model fidelity and handling of heterogeneity/adversaries.  
- Clarity: High-level narrative is clear but several technical points should be introduced earlier and numerical results better explained.  
- Major Q1 (heterogeneity): Can the framework extend to multiple channels or adaptive budget allocation (heterogeneous subchannels or side information at the transmitter), and how would lower bounds change?  
- Major Q2 (channel realism): Do the models capture real worker behaviors (random guessing, bias, correlated errors), abstentions, class-dependent confusion, or context-dependent reliability, and what modifications are natural?  
- Major Q3 (adversaries): How do lower bounds and kIC change under Byzantine or worst-case adversarial workers, and is the current bound robust without explicit adversarial modeling?  
- Major Q4 (kIC clarity): Clarify numerical results and figure legends; explain what k=1 represents (degenerate case or direct labeling) and its channel interpretation.  
- Major Q5 (P(B(X))): The assumption of known P(B(X)) is substantial and should be stated earlier; discuss sensitivity to misspecification and the effect of learning P(B(X)) from data.  
- Scope/impact: The rate–distortion connection is stimulating but would gain applicability by integrating worker reputation, adaptive tasking, abstentions, and adversarial robustness.  
- Suggestions: Incorporate heterogeneous worker channels and side information, allow adaptive/unequal budgets, model adversarial behavior, expand behavioral plausibility discussion, and clarify numerical plots and k=1.  
- Minor editorial: Fix figure labels (Fig.1 a/b/c), number/format theorem/lemma/corollary consistently, define notation on first use, and proofread for grammar and figure readability.  
- Conclusion: The paper offers a compelling theoretical bridge between crowdsourcing and information theory, but addressing realism and presentation issues would substantially strengthen its relevance and impact.
