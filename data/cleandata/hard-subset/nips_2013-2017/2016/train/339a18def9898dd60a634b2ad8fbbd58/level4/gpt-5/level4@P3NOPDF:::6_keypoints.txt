- Summary: Derives an information-theoretic budget–fidelity bound for crowdsourcing using rate–distortion theory and channel coding; derivations look sound and could be moderately significant if novel.

- Strength: Draws a clear connection between labeling accuracy (distortion) and required annotation budget via information-theoretic quantities.

- Strength: Converse-style reasoning is well motivated and aligns with established coding/compression principles.

- Strength: May help unify disparate results on redundancy, error rates, and worker noise under a single information-theoretic framework.

- Main concern: The channel-coding freedom assumed by the converse may not hold for crowdsourcing because many code mappings are not implementable as task allocations.

- Main concern (per-worker constraints): Re-assigning the same query to one worker breaks i.i.d. response assumptions and induces dependence across uses.

- Main concern (unknown/heterogeneous reliabilities): Unknown or varying worker reliability means the channel law may be partially known or must be learned, undermining achievability.

- Main concern (allocation/interaction constraints): Platform limits, varying costs, limited queries per worker, and ethical/practical constraints restrict feasible encoders.

- Main concern (item difficulty dependence): Shared latent item difficulty or context can induce correlations not captured by a memoryless channel model.

- Main concern: Because of these constraints, the capacity-style converse may substantially overestimate achievable performance and yield loose bounds.

- Main concern: It is unclear how to construct structured redundancy beyond simple repetition/majority voting; lack of feasible encoder constructions reduces practical value.

- Main concern: Novelty is unclear—if prior work already applied these tools to crowdsourcing, the contribution may be a straightforward extension without new achievability or tightness results.

- Question: Precisely what class of encoders is allowed in Eq. (3)—arbitrary or constrained to crowd-implementable protocols (no intra-worker repeats, bounded load, no adaptation)?

- Question: Are assignments adaptive and are worker reliabilities known, partially known, or learned online; how do these choices affect the capacity analogy and converse?

- Question: Do responses assume conditional independence given labels and worker parameters, and are item difficulty/context effects modeled?

- Question: Is there any achievability result (even simple) demonstrating the gap to the converse under realistic constraints?

- Suggestion: Empirically validate tightness by comparing the bound to budget–error curves of state-of-the-art algorithms on synthetic and real data across canonical worker models.

- Suggestion: Derive a constrained converse that explicitly encodes practical assignment constraints (no intra-worker repetition, per-worker load limits, unknown reliabilities).

- Suggestion: Provide implementable achievability baselines (stratified majority, skill-aware allocation, adaptive querying) with performance guarantees to bracket the converse.

- Suggestion: Include numeric examples translating the bound into concrete budgets for target error rates under common worker models for interpretability.

- Minor: Clarify Eq. (8) minimization domain (C, C′) subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.

- Minor: Theorem 4 needs more detailed derivation and clarity on signs/inequalities involving ln(1−q) and ε terms.

- Minor: Explain or cite why H_M(E(ε)) appears in Eq. (17), citing any extremal property if used.

- Minor: Define B(X), U, u^n, ∑ m_i when first introduced and ensure Eq. (3) domains/codomains are consistent.

- Minor: Explicitly state independence/stationarity assumptions and note when realistic protocols violate them.

- Overall assessment: Clean, potentially useful information-theoretic perspective, but currently risks being loose and of limited practical utility without addressing implementability, assumptions, achievability, and benchmarking.
