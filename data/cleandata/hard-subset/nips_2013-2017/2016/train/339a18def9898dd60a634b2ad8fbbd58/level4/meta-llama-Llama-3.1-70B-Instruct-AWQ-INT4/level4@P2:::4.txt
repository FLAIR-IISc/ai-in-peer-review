The paper "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" presents an interesting approach to studying the crowdsourcing problem using information theoretic tools. The authors map the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source channel coding in information theory literature, where the taskmaster is treated as a transmitter and the crowdsourcer is mapped to a receiver/decoder. The unreliable links from the workers to the crowdsourcer constitute a communication channel. This parallel allows the authors to derive a lower bound on the budget (number of queries) for a desirable fidelity level, which is independent of the schemes used by the taskmaster to design the queries and the algorithm used at the crowdsourcer to process the information collected from the workers.

The authors then consider a specific scheme at the taskmaster, called k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance. They compare the performance of this encoding scheme with the obtained information theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model. The results are interesting, and this work may invite other researchers to study the crowdsourcing framework using information theoretic tools.

However, the reviewer has some concerns regarding the realism of the model. Firstly, the reliability and skill level of the workers are merged together and modeled as a single channel. The paper assumes that the transmitter (taskmaster) allocates the same number of queries to all the workers irrespective of their skill level. In real systems, workers may carry some rating with them that represents their skill level, and this is not taken into account. The authors should comment on this issue.

Secondly, the channel models used to take the unreliability of the workers into account may not be realistic. The models do not account for workers who can have some knowledge of the task at hand and try to degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner. The authors should discuss the limitations of their models and potential extensions to make them more realistic.

Additionally, the explanation of the numerical results is not very clear. The labeling of various curves in Fig. 2 can be improved. Specifically, the $k=1$ case is not well-explained. Does it correspond to the worker directly giving the correct label of the presented query, as it is not possible to present a pair and ask if they share the same label in this case?

The paper assumes knowledge of $P(B(X))$, but this is not stated clearly until page 6, line 246. This should have been stated much earlier in the paper.

Finally, the authors repeatedly use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' even when these terms are not accompanied by a specific theorem or lemma number. Using 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' without a number would be more appropriate in such cases.
