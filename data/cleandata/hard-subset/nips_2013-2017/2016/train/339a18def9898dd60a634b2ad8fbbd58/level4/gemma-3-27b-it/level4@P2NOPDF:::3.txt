This paper investigates the problem of aggregating responses from workers in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. While I found the core ideas and proposed model to be interesting and potentially valuable for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I struggled to fully grasp the details of the presentation. 

The authors make a commendable effort to explain the concepts for readers unfamiliar with information theory, but the technicality remains a significant barrier to understanding. A key strength of the work lies in the high-level insights developed – for example, the discussion at the end of Section 3 – yet these are not sufficiently emphasized. I recommend highlighting these takeaways more prominently, and including a concise concluding section summarizing the practical implications of the results. Even acknowledging space constraints, a clearer articulation of *what* a practitioner should do with these findings would be highly beneficial. 

Specifically, I suggest the authors re-express the results more directly in the context of crowdsourcing, moving away from the language of channel coding and distortion. Framing the findings in terms of worker responses, budget allocation, and resulting accuracy would significantly enhance accessibility.

The related work section would also benefit from acknowledging the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for crowdsourcing workers – often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This literature frequently addresses varying worker abilities and, increasingly, combines incentive structures with sophisticated aggregation methods, creating a natural point of connection with this work.

Several minor points could improve clarity. On line 84, “is reviewed in” should likely be “are reviewed in.” Lines 106 and earlier descriptions of the system were confusing regarding the roles of the “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster.” Given that “crowdsourcing” inherently combines “crowd” and “outsourcing,” the distinction felt unclear regarding when workers become involved in the process. On line 122, please ensure the expression “A A(X1,X2)” is correctly typeset. The sentence beginning “In case…” on lines 275-276 was difficult to parse and would benefit from rephrasing. Finally, Figure 2 provided limited insight, and a more detailed caption explaining the plotted quantities and their relationship to the theoretical results would be extremely helpful.
