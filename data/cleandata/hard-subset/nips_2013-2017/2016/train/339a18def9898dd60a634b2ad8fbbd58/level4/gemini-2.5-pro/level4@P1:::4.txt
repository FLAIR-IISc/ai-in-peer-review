This paper models a crowdsourcing system in which a taskmaster divides a large project into smaller tasks, which are then assigned to a group of workers. A crowdsourcer collects the responses from these workers—who may have varying and imperfect skill levels—and processes this information to complete the overall inference task. The central problem is to achieve a desired level of fidelity (i.e., quality) in the final result while adhering to a budget, defined as the total number of queries posed to the workers.

The paper's main contribution is to frame this budget-fidelity trade-off using the information-theoretic joint source-channel coding framework. The taskmaster is modeled as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the collective of unreliable workers as a noisy communication channel. Using this analogy, the authors derive a scheme-independent lower bound on the budget required to achieve a certain fidelity, drawing on standard results from rate-distortion theory. The paper then introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compares its performance to the derived information-theoretic bound for a particular worker model.

**Strengths:**
A key strength of this work is its novel application of the rate-distortion framework to the crowdsourcing problem. Drawing this parallel is interesting and provides a new lens through which to analyze the fundamental limits of such systems. This work has the potential to inspire further research using information-theoretic tools to study crowdsourcing.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
While the information-theoretic framing is elegant, a central concern is the realism of the proposed model. The direct mapping to a point-to-point communication problem appears to oversimplify several aspects of practical crowdsourcing systems, perhaps for the convenience of applying existing information theory results. It would be beneficial if the authors could comment on the following points to clarify the model's assumptions and limitations:

1.  **Worker Skill Model:** The current model seems to merge worker reliability and skill level into a single channel characterization. It also assumes the taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their skill. In many real-world platforms (like Amazon Mechanical Turk), workers have reputation scores or ratings that a taskmaster can use to assign queries more effectively. How might the model and its resulting bounds change if this prior information about worker skill levels were incorporated?

2.  **Realism of Channel Models:** The paper utilizes standard channel models (MSC and SHC) to account for worker unreliability. How realistic are these models for capturing the nuances of human error? Furthermore, could the framework be extended to handle more complex or malicious behaviors, such as adversarial workers who have some knowledge of the task and actively try to degrade the system's fidelity?

**Clarity and Presentation:**
There are several areas where the paper's clarity and presentation could be improved:

*   **Numerical Results:** The explanation of the numerical results is not very clear. In Figure 2, the labeling of the curves could be improved for better readability.
*   **k=1 Case:** The analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a key part of the paper. What does the `k=1` case correspond to in this context? If a single item is presented, a worker cannot be asked if it shares the same label with another item. Does this simply mean the worker is asked to directly provide the label? This should be clarified.
*   **Assumption on P(B(X)):** On page 6, line 246, the authors state the assumption that the source distribution P(B(X)) is known. This is a significant assumption for the rate-distortion framework, and it should be stated much earlier in the paper, perhaps in the modeling section.
*   **Typos:**
    *   Page 4, line 135: "Figure 1a" should likely refer to "Figure 1b".
    *   Page 4, line 135: "Figure 1b" should likely refer to "Figure 1c".
*   **Style:** The authors repeatedly use capitalized words like 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' in the running text when not referring to a specific numbered item. The lowercase versions ('theorem', 'lemma', 'corollary') would be more appropriate in these instances.
