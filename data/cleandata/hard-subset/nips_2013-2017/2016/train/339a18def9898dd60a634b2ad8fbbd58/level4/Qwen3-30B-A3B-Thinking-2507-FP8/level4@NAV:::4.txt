

This paper examines a crowdsourcing framework where a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller subtasks assigned to workers. The crowdsourcer aggregates worker responses and processes them to complete the inference, accommodating workers with heterogeneous skill levels and unreliable reporting. The system aims to guarantee a target fidelity in the final outcome despite worker errors, necessitating optimal design of subtasks (queries) by the taskmaster. The budget is defined as the total number of queries, and the paper investigates the fundamental trade-off between budget and fidelity by mapping the system to joint source-channel coding in information theory. Here, the taskmaster acts as the transmitter, the crowdsourcer as the receiver, and unreliable worker-to-crowdsourcer links constitute the channel. This mapping yields an information-theoretic lower bound on the required budget for a given fidelity, independent of specific query design or processing algorithms. The authors further analyze a specific k-ary incidence coding scheme and compare its performance against the derived bound under a particular channel model. The reviewer finds the parallel between rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers conceptually interesting and potentially inspiring for future work. However, the realism of the model is questionable. Specifically:  
1) Worker reliability and skill level are conflated into a single channel model, and the taskmaster allocates identical query volumes to all workers regardless of skill, which may not reflect real systems where worker ratings exist.  
2) The channel models for worker unreliability lack realism; they do not account for adversarial workers who might deliberately degrade fidelity after understanding the task. Additionally, the explanation of numerical results is unclear, and Figure 2's curve labels require improvement (e.g., clarify what the $k=1$ case represents—does it imply workers directly provide correct labels without pairwise queries?).
