- Paper maps crowdsourcing to a joint source-channel coding problem and uses rate-distortion theory to derive budget-fidelity fundamental limits.
- Main contribution: novel information-theoretic framework that provides query- and algorithm-independent bounds for crowdsourcing.
- Distinguishes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and compares its performance to theoretical bounds with supporting numerical results.
- Builds on Dawid–Skene, majority voting, and classical information-theory results (source-channel separation, rate-distortion).
- Strength — originality: introduces a genuinely new perspective to crowdsourcing literature.
- Strength — significance: bounds give important guidance for efficient resource allocation in practical labeling tasks.
- Strength — quality: rigorous theoretical development with well-constructed proofs for main theorems.
- Strength — clarity: generally well organized and clear communication-system analogy.
- Weakness — realism: oversimplified worker-skill model (single channel parameter) and uniform query allocation not reflective of real platforms.
- Weakness — adversarial behavior and worker-specific variation not captured by MSC/SHC channel models.
- Weakness — clarity issues: assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) introduced too late; incorrect figure references; generic unlabeled “Theorem/Lemma/Corollary” usages.
- Weakness — numerical results: Figure 2 labeling unclear, k=1 case not explicitly explained, and legend/curve clarity needs improvement.
- Pro acceptance: establishes a solid theoretical foundation and shows how bounds can guide practical scheme design (kIC).
- Con acceptance: limited practical relevance due to unrealistic assumptions and late/murky presentation of key assumptions.
- Overall assessment: significant and elegant theoretical contribution but needs stronger discussion of practical applicability and clearer presentation.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Key revisions requested: move P(B(X)) assumption to problem statement; correct figure references; clarify k=1 and improve Figure 2 labels/legends; discuss extensions to realistic worker-skill heterogeneity and targeted allocation; replace generic theorem references with specific numbering.
