- Paper formulates the budget–fidelity tradeoff in crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives rate–distortion lower bounds on queries per item.  
- Models two skill-information settings: unknown worker skills at decoder (SL-UK) and skills known to decoder (SL-CS).  
- Primarily models workers as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channels (SHC).  
- Key theoretical finding: with skills known, effective capacity is the average worker capacity; with skills unknown, capacity corresponds to the capacity at the average worker quality.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where constrained valid responses provide error-correction benefits.  
- For SHC workers, derives necessary rate conditions for kIC to meet an error target and offers a pricing rule of thumb.  
- Connects to prior aggregation and worker-modeling literature (Dawid–Skene EM, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral/EM), but provides Shannon-style, algorithm-agnostic limits.  
- Strength: principled use of rate–distortion and separation theorems to obtain agnostic budget–fidelity lower bounds.  
- Strength: clear comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS that quantifies the value of estimating worker skills.  
- Strength: kIC provides a concrete, interpretable query scheme with intuitive necessary-rate results under SHC.  
- Strength: framework may inspire coding-like query designs and inform redundancy vs. efficiency tradeoffs.  
- Weakness: unrealistic modeling assumptions (iid items, memoryless errors, no adaptivity) that ignore instance difficulty, worker correlations, learning, and strategic behavior.  
- Weakness: assumes uniform rate allocation across workers and does not analyze adaptive or targeted assignment common in practice.  
- Weakness: stylized channel models (MSC, SHC) do not cover adversarial workers or collusion; robustness is not addressed.  
- Weakness: assumes knowledge of P(B(X)) without early statement or discussion of misspecification/unknown priors.  
- Weakness: limited empirical results and no comparisons to practical baselines (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral-EM); finite-sample effects not shown.  
- Weakness: exposition and figures need clarity; some notational/editorial issues.  
- Quality: theoretical development appears sound and appropriately uses information-theoretic tools, but relies on asymptotics and lacks finite-sample guidance.  
- Clarity issues: declare assumptions (P(B(X)) known, non-adaptivity, iid) earlier; improve Figure 2 labeling and clarify k=1 interpretation.  
- Editorial fixes requested (figure labels, consistent theorem/lemma/corollary usage, earlier statement of P(B(X)), ratio formatting in Appendix E).  
- Originality: novel application of rate–distortion/separation to crowdsourcing limits and a useful instantiation via kIC.  
- Significance: offers a unifying theoretical benchmark; practical impact depends on extensions to adaptive/instance-dependent/adversarial models and empirical validation.  
- Key questions suggested: effects of adaptive allocation/bandits, instance-dependent/stateful channels, robustness to unknown priors, sample complexity to estimate skills for SL-CS, adversarial/Byzantine analyses, and kIC gains for N>2 labels.  
- Suggested experiments: compare kIC to majority/DS/spectral-EM under matched/mismatched models and show finite-sample gaps to information-theoretic bounds.  
- Pros summary: clean information-theoretic framework, insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, concrete kIC analysis with pricing guidance.  
- Cons summary: strong simplifying assumptions limiting realism, limited empirical validation, and some unclear presentation and assumptions.
