This paper provides an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, modeling it as a joint source channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The authors use existing results on the coding rate in the JSCC problem to establish the fundamental limits of the asymptotic number of queries per item in crowdsourcing. The paper is well-written, and the authors' approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem is interesting.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of fundamental limits on the number of queries per item under the proposed model. The authors provide two theorems, which establish the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity in crowdsourcing, given the skill levels of the workers are unknown or known to the crowdsourcer. These theorems provide a useful bound on the performance of crowdsourcing systems.

The authors also provide a lower bound on the error probability of a coding scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowd-sourcing model. This result is useful in understanding the performance of this coding scheme in crowdsourcing.

However, there are some technical comments and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the authors should argue that the source-channel separation theorem still holds when the channel has a random state, where the state is unknown at the encoder, but may or may not be known at the receiver. While it is likely that the separation theorem still holds in these situations, the authors should provide a proof or cite an existing proof.

Secondly, the authors' model may be too restrictive, as it ignores the fact that both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer may have access to the original data (the items). This knowledge of the original data can provide side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem. The authors should consider side information in the model to make it more convincing and realistic.

Thirdly, the authors should be more precise in their language when commenting on the curves in Fig 2. Instead of saying that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow, they should say that the lower bound becomes smaller as k and q grow, which means that the performance of the code may get better.

Finally, the authors may want to consider recent results on nonasymptotic converses for JSCC, such as the work by Kostina and Verdu, to provide a more useful characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem.
* The authors derive fundamental limits on the number of queries per item under the proposed model.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors' approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem is interesting.
