This paper studies the aggregation of worker responses in crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens and characterizes the fundamental limits of the budget–fidelity tradeoff. Although I did not grasp all of the technical details, I found the modeling approach and insights compelling and potentially useful for designing aggregation methods.

I recommend foregrounding the high-level intuition and practical takeaways from the results—especially the points at the end of Section 3—and adding a short conclusion that clearly states what practitioners should learn from the work (I appreciate that space is limited). Despite the authors’ efforts to make the paper accessible to readers less familiar with information theory, the presentation remains difficult to follow. The paper would benefit from restating many of the results directly in the language of crowdsourcing (tasks, workers, error rates, budgets) rather than primarily in terms of channels, codes, and distortion. Translating the key theorems into concrete guidance—e.g., how label redundancy, worker heterogeneity, and budget interact to achieve a target error—would substantially improve clarity.

Related work: Please discuss the complementary and increasingly intersecting literature on robust economic incentives for workers, usually framed as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This line of research considers heterogeneous worker abilities and, in recent work, integrates incentive design with more sophisticated aggregation strategies. Positioning your contribution relative to that literature would strengthen the paper’s impact.

Clarity and terminology: I was confused by the distinction between the “crowdsourcer” and the “taskmaster” (see line 106 and the earlier introduction). Because “crowdsourcing” evokes outsourcing before workers are engaged, it was unclear which entity holds which responsibilities. A concise early section or figure defining all actors and their roles would help.
