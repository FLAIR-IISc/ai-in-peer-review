- Investigates fundamental limits in digital crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework.
- Models crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding: taskmaster = transmitter, crowdsourcer = receiver, workers = noisy channels.
- Characterizes trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (inference accuracy).
- Establishes theoretical lower bounds on the budget required for a given fidelity.
- Analyzes performance of k-ary incidence coding relative to the derived bounds.
- Draws a compelling parallel between rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing design.
- Novel application of information theory that could open new research directions.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis and derivation of bounds; clear and well-organized exposition.
- k-ary incidence coding offers a concrete example with potential practical relevance.
- Simplifying assumption of point-to-point mapping may oversimplify real-world crowdsourcing complexities.
- Model aggregates worker reliability into a single channel parameter and doesn’t handle individual worker ratings.
- Channel models may not capture adversarial or strategically misleading worker behaviors.
- Numerical results are under-explained; Fig. 2 needs clearer labeling and the k=1 case is unclear.
- The assumption that P(B(X)) is known should be stated earlier in the paper.
- Minor typographical errors and inconsistent numbering/labeling of Theorems/Lemmas/Corollaries.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address clarity of numerical results and typographical issues.
