

The paper formulates digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to establish lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per task required to attain a specified error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, with symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) representing worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel model (SHC(q)) capturing scenarios where a worker acts as a spammer with probability 1−q. The coding scheme is assumed nonadaptive. Two analytical cases are examined: (i) when the crowdsourcer (who aggregates worker responses to determine task outcomes) knows all worker skill levels, and (ii) when the crowdsourcer possesses no prior knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks to workers) is assumed unaware of worker skills, resulting in uniform task assignment per worker. A necessary condition for the minimum rate (queries per task) in k-ary incidence coding—where a worker evaluates k items for similarity/dissimilarity under an SHC(q) worker pool—is derived. The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, the absence of experimental or simulation results to quantify the gap between existing schemes (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) and the derived theoretical limits is notable. Section 2.4 could be condensed, and Section 3.1 is redundant or could be shortened; prioritizing proof sketches for the theorems would better serve the paper’s contribution. The work appears novel, as the paper correctly asserts no prior information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing exists. Its utility is significant, enabling designers to determine the minimum query rate for tasks with guaranteed error probability, thereby informing project pricing and design. The appendix was not reviewed, so no assessment of the proofs is provided.
