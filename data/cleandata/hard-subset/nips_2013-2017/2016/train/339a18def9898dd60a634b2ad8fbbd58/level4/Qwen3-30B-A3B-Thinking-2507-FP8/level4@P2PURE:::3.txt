

The paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using information theory, establishing fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While I did not fully grasp the paper's details (see specific concerns below), I found the insights and model itself to be a highly interesting and potentially useful approach to designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. I recommend highlighting the high-level intuition from the results (e.g., at the end of Section 3) more prominently, as well as providing a clear concluding statement on the key takeaways (acknowledging space constraints). Although I appreciated the authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to non-information-theory readers, it remained difficult to follow. I suggest restating most results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than retaining the channel/coding/distortion terminology. The related work section should also mention the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers (typically termed "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"), which increasingly addresses varying worker abilities and integrates with sophisticated aggregation methods.
