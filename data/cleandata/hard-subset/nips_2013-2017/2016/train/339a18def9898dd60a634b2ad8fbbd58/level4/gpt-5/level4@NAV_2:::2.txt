The paper frames crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens and derives asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item. Concretely, the problem is cast as joint source–channel coding (JSCC) with random channel state: the state (worker skill) is unknown to the encoder (taskmaster) and may or may not be available to the decoder (crowdsourcer). Item labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster acts as the encoder; each worker corresponds to a channel use; worker skill levels are the channel state; and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. Within this setup, the paper invokes standard JSCC rate results to characterize fundamental limits on the asymptotic query rate per item (Theorems 2 and 3). The authors also derive a lower bound on the error probability for a specific coding/querying scheme—k-ary incidence coding—under a particular worker model. Overall, the manuscript is clearly written and represents a promising initial step toward a JSCC-based analysis of crowdsourcing.

Some technical comments:
1. The crowdsourcing model corresponds to JSCC with random channel state. The derivations for Theorems 2 and 3 appeal to the usual source–channel separation theorem. The paper should first justify that separation continues to hold when the channel has a random state that is unknown at the encoder and either known or unknown at the decoder. I expect separation to remain valid under these assumptions, but a proof or a reference establishing this is needed.
2. The modeling choice that the taskmaster only observes labels and the crowdsourcer only observes worker outputs may be overly restrictive. In many practical settings both parties can access the raw items, which constitute side information in the JSCC formulation. While encoder-only side information may not help, side information at the decoder (or at both encoder and decoder) can be beneficial. Incorporating side information would make the model more realistic and potentially strengthen the results.
3. In Sec. 4.2, when discussing Fig. 2, the paper states that the kIC lower bounds “improve” as k and q increase. Presumably the intended meaning is that the lower bound value decreases with larger k and q, suggesting better achievable performance for the scheme. However, calling this an “improvement” of the lower bound is somewhat misleading in the absence of a corresponding upper bound for comparison.

Edited following authors’ response: The JSCC-based perspective is interesting, but, as another reviewer noted, nonasymptotic (finite-blocklength) characterizations would be more actionable. The authors may wish to consult recent finite-blocklength converses for JSCC, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Trans. Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
