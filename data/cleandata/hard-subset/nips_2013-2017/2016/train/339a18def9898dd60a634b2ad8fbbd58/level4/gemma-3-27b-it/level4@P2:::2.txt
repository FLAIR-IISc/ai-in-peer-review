This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, framing it as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with randomly skilled workers. The authors derive fundamental limits on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a given fidelity, considering scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown or known to the crowdsourcer. The core idea—modeling crowdsourcing through the lens of JSCC—is novel and potentially valuable for understanding the inherent trade-offs in this increasingly important paradigm. The paper is generally well-written and provides a solid foundation for future research.

The authors model the crowdsourcing task as a communication system: items to be labeled are the source, workers are noisy channels, queries are signals, and the aggregation of worker responses is decoding. This allows them to leverage existing information-theoretic results, particularly the source-channel separation theorem, to establish asymptotic limits on performance. They also investigate a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and provide a lower bound on its error probability. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Perspective:** Applying information theory to crowdsourcing provides a rigorous framework for analyzing its fundamental limits. This is a significant contribution as it moves beyond purely empirical or algorithmic studies.
*   **Clear Modeling:** The mapping of crowdsourcing components to information-theoretic concepts (source, channel, encoder, decoder) is clearly articulated and intuitive.
*   **Established Bounds:** The derivation of lower bounds on query complexity based on established JSCC theory is a valuable result.
*   **Consideration of Worker Skill:**  The analysis explicitly accounts for the imperfect skill levels of crowd workers, a crucial aspect of real-world crowdsourcing systems.
*   **Exploration of a Specific Coding Scheme:** The investigation of k-ary incidence coding offers a concrete example and potential for practical application.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reliance on Source-Channel Separation:** The paper heavily relies on the source-channel separation theorem. While generally valid, the authors do not explicitly justify its applicability in this specific scenario, where the channel state (worker skill) is unknown *a priori* to the encoder. A brief discussion or citation of relevant work supporting this assumption would strengthen the argument.
*   **Limited Side Information:** The model assumes the taskmaster and crowdsourcer only have access to labels, neglecting the potential for utilizing the original data (items) as side information. Incorporating this side information could lead to tighter bounds and a more realistic model.  The original data could provide crucial context for both encoding and decoding.
*   **Asymptotic Nature:** The derived limits are asymptotic, based on a large number of items and workers. While theoretically sound, these results may not directly translate to practical scenarios with limited resources. A discussion of the implications of this asymptotic approximation would be useful.
*   **Interpretation of kIC Bounds:** The claim in Section 4.2 that the lower bounds for kIC “improve” as *k* and *q* grow could be misinterpreted.  It would be clearer to state that the lower bound *decreases* as *k* and *q* increase, suggesting better performance.
*   **Lack of Non-Asymptotic Analysis:** As pointed out by another reviewer, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits, perhaps leveraging recent work by Kostina and Verdu (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC, would greatly enhance the practical relevance of this work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to analyzing crowdsourcing.
*   The derived bounds provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing performance.
*   The work opens up new avenues for research in information-theoretic crowdsourcing.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its technical contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on asymptotic analysis limits the immediate applicability of the results.
*   The model's simplification (lack of side information) may not fully capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing systems.
*   The lack of explicit justification for applying the source-channel separation theorem is a minor weakness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-defined contribution. While the asymptotic nature of the results and the simplified model are limitations, the novel perspective and theoretical rigor justify acceptance. The paper has the potential to influence future research in crowdsourcing and information theory. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors address the minor weaknesses outlined above, specifically providing justification for the source-channel separation theorem and acknowledging the limitations of the asymptotic analysis.  Consideration of side information in future work would also be highly beneficial.
