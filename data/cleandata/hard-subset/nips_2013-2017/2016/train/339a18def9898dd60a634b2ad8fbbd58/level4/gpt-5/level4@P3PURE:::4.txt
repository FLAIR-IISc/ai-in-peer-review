This paper studies a crowdsourcing setting in which a taskmaster decomposes a large task (e.g., an inference problem) into smaller queries for workers. The crowdsourcer aggregates workers’ responses to complete the inference. The model allows workers to have varying skill levels and to be unreliable, and the overall objective is to guarantee a target level of fidelity (or “goodness”) in the final outcome despite erroneous reports. Achieving this goal requires careful design of the small tasks (queries) assigned to workers. The total number of queries posed to workers is defined as the system’s budget. The paper analyzes a fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of the crowdsourcer’s inference).

The key idea is to map the crowdsourcing system to a joint source–channel coding problem. The taskmaster is viewed as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable worker responses as a communication channel. Under this mapping, a lower bound on the required budget to achieve a desired fidelity follows from standard information-theoretic results; this bound does not depend on the particular query design at the taskmaster or the specific decoding algorithm at the crowdsourcer. The authors then analyze the performance of a concrete scheme at the taskmaster—k-ary incidence coding—and compare it with the information-theoretic lower bound under a specific channel model.

The parallel between the rate–distortion framework and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers is interesting and could encourage further use of information-theoretic tools in this area. That said, I am not convinced the current modeling choices capture real crowdsourcing systems with sufficient realism. One reason may be that the paper aims for an exact mapping to a point-to-point communication problem so existing results can be applied with minimal additional modeling effort.

I would appreciate the authors’ comments on the following points:
1) Worker heterogeneity: The paper appears to merge worker reliability and skill into a single channel model, and it assumes the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers regardless of their skill. In practice, many platforms maintain worker ratings that reflect ability, and task allocation is often adaptive or targeted. How would the framework accommodate such heterogeneous and potentially rating-informed assignment policies?
2) Channel realism and adversaries: How realistic are the chosen channel models for capturing worker unreliability? What if some workers have domain knowledge and behave adversarially, intentionally degrading fidelity? Can the analysis be extended to adversarial or semi-adversarial models?

Comments on presentation and results:
- The numerical results need clearer explanation. The labeling of the curves in Figure 2 should be improved.
- Please clarify what the k = 1 case represents. With k = 1, it seems you cannot present a pair and ask whether they share the same label; does k = 1 instead correspond to directly querying the label of a single item? How does this fit within the k-ary incidence coding framework?
- On page 6, line 246, you assume knowledge of P(B(X)). This assumption should be stated earlier in the paper.
- On page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b,” and “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c.”
- The manuscript repeatedly capitalizes “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without associated numbers. When not referring to a numbered result, using lowercase (“theorem,” “lemma,” “corollary”) would be more appropriate.

Overall, the information-theoretic mapping and the comparison between k-ary incidence coding and the derived lower bounds are appealing. Addressing the realism of the worker model, allocation policies, and potential adversarial behavior, as well as clarifying the numerical experiments and presentation issues, would substantially strengthen the paper.
