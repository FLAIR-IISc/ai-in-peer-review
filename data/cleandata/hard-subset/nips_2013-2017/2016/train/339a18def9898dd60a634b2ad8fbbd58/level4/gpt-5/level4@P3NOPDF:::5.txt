This paper develops an information-theoretic framework for digital crowdsourcing that yields lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a target error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. The paper analyzes two channel models: (i) symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture worker error, and (ii) the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), in which a worker is a “hammer” with probability q and a spammer with probability 1−q. The allocation and decoding code is nonadaptive. The analysis covers two regimes for the crowdsourcer (the decoder): one in which it knows all workers’ skill levels and one in which it has no knowledge. In both cases, the taskmaster (the allocator) is assumed to have no knowledge of worker skills and therefore assigns the same number of tasks to each worker. The paper also provides a necessary condition on the minimum rate (queries per task) for k-ary incidence coding—where a worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to the same or different classes—under an SHC(q) worker pool.

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized exposition with a principled formulation of crowdsourcing as parallel noisy channels.
- Novelty: to my knowledge (and as claimed), this is the first paper to develop converse-style information-theoretic bounds for crowdsourcing with these channel models.
- Potential impact: the derived lower bounds can inform the design and costing of large-scale annotation efforts by quantifying the minimal query rate required to meet target accuracies.
- Modeling choices (MSC and SHC(q)) are interpretable and widely used in the literature on noisy labels and crowdsourcing.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Lack of empirical or simulation evidence: The paper would be much stronger with simulations showing how close practical, widely used schemes (e.g., majority vote, simple EM, or state-of-the-art aggregation) are to the theoretical lower bounds, even on synthetic data. A brief illustration on realistic settings (e.g., AMT-style label noise) would help calibrate the gap and the practical significance of the bounds.
- Main-text proofs: Given the theoretical nature of the paper, brief proof sketches of the central theorems in the main text would improve readability and trust in the results. As written, the derivations are largely deferred to the appendix.
- Organization: Section 2.4 can likely be shortened without loss of clarity, and Section 3.1 could be trimmed or merged. The freed space could be used for proof sketches, intuition about tightness, or a small simulation.
- Assumptions: The restriction to nonadaptive codes is clean but potentially conservative. A discussion of whether the bounds extend to, or can be contrasted with, adaptive task assignment would be valuable. Similarly, the assumption that the taskmaster assigns an equal number of tasks to each worker overlooks practical heterogeneity and known-worker settings; some commentary on how unequal allocations or partial knowledge would affect the bounds would be useful.
- Modeling details: Please clarify early and precisely the definition of MSC (e.g., M-ary symmetric channel assumptions) and the independence assumptions across workers and tasks. If label priors (class imbalance) or task difficulties matter, state whether the results assume uniform priors and homogeneous tasks.

Questions for the authors
- Tightness: Are there known achievable schemes that meet these lower bounds up to constants or logarithmic factors? Do the bounds match known upper bounds in any special cases (e.g., binary labeling with MSC or SHC(q))?
- Parameter knowledge: In the case where the crowdsourcer “knows” worker skills, what is the exact knowledge assumed (channel parameters, priors, correlations)? How sensitive are the bounds to misspecification?
- Estimation of q in SHC(q): In practice, q must be estimated. Can the results accommodate estimation error, or can you provide robustness bounds?
- k-ary incidence coding: Is the necessary condition believed to be order-tight? Are there known constructions that come close to it?

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well written and well structured. That said, streamlining Section 2.4 and Section 3.1 would improve pacing, and replacing some background with proof intuition would better serve expert readers.
- A brief “takeaways” subsection that maps the theoretical results to practical design guidelines (e.g., “to achieve error ε with SHC(q)=… and MSC parameters …, you need at least R queries/task”) would increase accessibility.

Overall assessment
This is a well-crafted and novel theoretical contribution that brings information-theoretic tools to a timely problem. The results have the potential to guide the design and pricing of crowdsourcing efforts by quantifying fundamental limits on query complexity. My main reservations are the lack of simulation or empirical illustration and the absence of proof sketches in the main text. If the proofs in the appendix are sound (I did not verify them) and the authors can either include brief proof sketches or small-scale simulations to contextualize the bounds, I would be inclined to recommend acceptance.
