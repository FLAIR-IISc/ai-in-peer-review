Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies label crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens. It models workers as noisy channels (MSC and SHC models) and casts the budget–fidelity trade-off as a rate–distortion problem with joint source–channel coding. The main contributions are: (i) information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item to achieve a target error (Hamming distortion) under two scenarios—unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly known worker skill levels at the crowdsourcer (SL-CS)—using Theorems 2 and 3; (ii) analysis of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), including a lower bound under a spammer–hammer worker model (Theorem 4) and a pricing rule-of-thumb; and (iii) specialization of the general bounds to the SHC model (Corollary 1) to provide a benchmark for any coding scheme.

Relative to prior crowdsourcing work (e.g., majority voting, Dawid–Skene/EM-style inference, spectral/EM hybrids, bandit allocation, and convex relaxations for clustering), this paper focuses not on proposing a new inference algorithm, but on fundamental limits under abstract channel models, positioning the results as ultimate benchmarks for budget vs. accuracy.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding, with careful distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS and appropriate use of Shannon’s separation theorem to derive limits.
- The MSC and SHC worker models are standard and broadly interpretable; the bounds are clearly stated and interpretable (e.g., dependence on H(B(X)), HM(.), and q).
- The kIC discussion is concrete and highlights how query design can induce error-correction structure; the oracle-decoder bound offers a clean, necessary condition.
- The work could serve as a baseline for evaluating practical algorithms and query schemes.

Weaknesses and main concerns:
- Novelty/significance: If this is indeed the first application of rate–distortion and channel coding bounds to label crowdsourcing, it is a reasonable contribution. However, the derivations themselves are fairly standard instantiations of known theorems; the main risk is that the bounds are loose in practice because the feasible encoder/decoder class in crowdsourcing is far more constrained than in communication.
- Constraints on encoding (Eq. (3)): Unlike classical channel coding, where we can design arbitrary codebooks (repetition, Hamming, convolutional, RS, LDPC, etc.), it is unclear what fraction of mappings C: B(X)^n → U^{∑ mi} are actually implementable as crowdsourcing query schemes without violating task semantics, budget/time constraints, or platform limitations. If the implementable encoder class is drastically restricted, capacity/rate–distortion limits may be far from tight.
- Reuse and independence: In communication, we can transmit the same codeword multiple times over an iid channel. In crowdsourcing, re-asking the exact same query to the same worker would break the iid/no-feedback assumptions in (1)–(2) (the same worker is likely to repeat the same answer), and platform rules may preclude repeated assignments. This undermines direct analogy to repetition or other classical codes and may further loosen the bounds. Clarifying which forms of “repetition” across different workers maintain the assumed independence would help.
- Limited design freedom for u^n: Beyond simple redundancy (multiple workers per item) and schemes like kIC, it is unclear how to systematically design u^n to realize error-correcting structures comparable to channel codes. The paper would benefit from an explicit discussion of admissible encoder classes, constraints (e.g., no duplicate queries to the same worker, balanced load, limits on k), and the implications for achievability.
- Empirical connection: The paper does not compare the derived limits to the performance of state-of-the-art practical methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM) under comparable budgets, or to empirical performance of kIC. Without such comparisons, it is hard to gauge tightness and practical value.

Quality:
- The core derivations are technically sound given the assumptions. However, the proof of Theorem 4 is terse and would benefit from more detail, especially regarding the role and sign of ln(1−q) and the precise dependence on ε̄S.
- Some steps (e.g., the appearance of HM(E(ε)) in (17)) need clearer justification or a citation.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, and the modeling choices are explained. It would help to:
  - Explicitly delineate the constraints on C and C′ imposed by implementability on crowdsourcing platforms.
  - Discuss how independence assumptions are maintained when the same item appears in multiple queries, and what is allowed/prohibited with respect to assigning identical queries to the same worker.
  - Provide intuition for the tightness or looseness of the bounds with finite budgets and realistic worker/task constraints.

Originality:
- Moderate. The framing is interesting, and connecting query design (kIC) to error-correction intuition is nice. But the theoretical bounds are largely straightforward applications of classical results, and the novelty hinges on the domain mapping and kIC analysis.

Significance:
- Potentially useful as a benchmark. Practical impact depends on demonstrating that the limits are not too loose under real constraints, and that they meaningfully inform query design or pricing.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing with interpretable bounds.
- Concrete analysis of kIC and an oracle lower bound that can guide query pricing and design.
- Could stimulate further work on implementable joint source–channel codes for human-in-the-loop systems.

Cons against acceptance:
- Risk of loose bounds due to strong assumptions and limited encoder/decoder implementability.
- Lack of empirical validation or comparison to strong baselines.
- Some proofs and explanations (e.g., Theorem 4, Eq. (17)) need clarification.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Clearly specify the feasible encoder class in Eq. (3) (e.g., constraints on repetition, worker reuse, adaptivity, k limits), and discuss how these constraints affect achievability and tightness.
- Provide empirical comparisons: plot the derived limits alongside performance of majority vote, Dawid–Skene, and spectral/EM methods under the same budget and worker models; include simulations for kIC.
- Elaborate the proof of Theorem 4; clarify the sign issue with ln(1−q) < 0 and whether ε̄S ≥ ε̂ is required for the bound to be meaningful.
- Justify or cite the step leading to HM(E(ε)) in (17).
- Discuss the separation theorem’s reliance on large n and random coding, and how finite-budget, task semantics, and platform constraints may affect practical tightness.

Minor comments:
- In (8), the minimization should be over C, C′ (equivalently over P(Ẑ|Z)) subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ Dt.
- The proof of Theorem 4 should be more detailed; explicitly handle the negativity of ln(1−q), define ε̄S precisely, and state conditions under which R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln ε̄S is informative.
- In Theorem 4, since ln(1−q) < 0, please clarify whether ε̄S > ε̂ and how the inequality direction is affected.
- In (17), explain why HM(E(ε)) appears or provide a citation supporting this form.
