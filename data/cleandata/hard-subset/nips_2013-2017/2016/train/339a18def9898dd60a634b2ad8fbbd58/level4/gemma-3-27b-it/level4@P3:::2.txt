This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with imperfect workers represented as noisy channels. The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve a given fidelity, considering scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown or known to the crowdsourcer. They also analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and provide a performance bound for it. The paper is well-written and presents a novel perspective on crowdsourcing using tools from information theory.

The core idea of framing crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem is insightful, potentially offering a rigorous foundation for understanding performance limitations and guiding system design. The analysis provides theoretical bounds that, while asymptotic, establish a baseline for evaluating practical algorithms. The consideration of both unknown and known worker skill levels adds nuance to the model. The investigation of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete example and a starting point for analyzing specific query strategies.

However, the paper relies heavily on established results from information theory, particularly the source-channel separation theorem. While the authors state the theorem, a more thorough justification of its applicability to this specific scenario – where the channel state (worker skill) is unknown to the encoder – would strengthen the work. The assumption of i.i.d. data sources, while common in information theory, may not always hold in real-world crowdsourcing applications, and the impact of deviations from this assumption is not discussed. 

A significant limitation is the purely asymptotic nature of the analysis. While theoretically valuable, these bounds may not be directly applicable to practical settings with finite datasets and budgets. The reviewer's comment regarding Kostina and Verdu’s work on finite-blocklength analysis is well taken; exploring non-asymptotic bounds would greatly enhance the paper’s practical relevance. The modeling choice of the taskmaster and crowdsourcer having access *only* to labels, rather than the original data items, feels restrictive. Incorporating the original data as side information at either the encoder or decoder would lead to a more realistic and potentially more insightful model. The authors acknowledge the importance of query cost variations, but this aspect isn't fully integrated into the analysis. A more detailed discussion of how query pricing strategies relate to the proposed bounds would be beneficial. 

**Strengths:**

*   Novel application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Insightful modeling of workers as noisy channels.
*   Consideration of both unknown and known worker skill levels.
*   Analysis of a specific query scheme (k-ary incidence coding).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Heavy reliance on existing information theory results without thorough justification of their applicability to the crowdsourcing context.
*   Asymptotic nature of the analysis limits practical applicability.
*   Restrictive modeling choice of limited data access for taskmaster/crowdsourcer.
*   Limited discussion of query cost variations and pricing.
*   Assumption of i.i.d. data sources.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for analyzing crowdsourcing.
*   The results provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing performance.
*   The work has the potential to inspire new algorithms and system designs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The asymptotic nature of the analysis limits the paper’s immediate practical impact.
*   The modeling assumptions may not accurately reflect real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
*   The paper primarily applies existing information theory results rather than presenting significant new theoretical contributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field, offering a valuable theoretical perspective on crowdsourcing. However, the limitations related to the asymptotic analysis and modeling assumptions prevent me from recommending unqualified acceptance. I would suggest accepting the paper with minor revisions, specifically addressing the justification of the source-channel separation theorem in the context of unknown channel state and acknowledging the limitations of the asymptotic analysis. Exploring the potential impact of incorporating side information (the original data items) into the model would also be beneficial.
