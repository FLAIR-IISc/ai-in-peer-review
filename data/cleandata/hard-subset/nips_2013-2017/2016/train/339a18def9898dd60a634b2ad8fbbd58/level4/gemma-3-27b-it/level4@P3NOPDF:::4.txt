This paper investigates a crowdsourcing framework for large-scale inference tasks, where a taskmaster decomposes a problem into smaller sub-tasks distributed to a crowd of workers with potentially varying skill levels and reporting reliability. The core objective is to understand the fundamental trade-off between the budget (number of queries issued) and the fidelity of the final inference, even in the presence of inaccurate worker responses. The authors establish a compelling connection between this crowdsourcing setup and the joint source-channel coding problem in information theory, framing the taskmaster as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver, and the worker responses as signals transmitted over unreliable channels. Leveraging this mapping, they derive a lower bound on the required budget to achieve a desired fidelity level, independent of specific task decomposition or aggregation schemes. They then analyze the performance of a concrete task decomposition scheme, k-ary incidence coding, comparing it to the information-theoretic bound under a specific channel model.

The central idea – viewing crowdsourcing as a communication problem – is insightful and has the potential to attract further research applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing design. The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical foundation and the clear articulation of the budget-fidelity trade-off. However, the reviewer has concerns regarding the realism of the model and the clarity of some presented results.

While the connection to rate-distortion theory is elegant, the simplification required to map the crowdsourcing problem to a point-to-point communication model feels restrictive. The authors should acknowledge more explicitly the limitations imposed by this simplification. Specifically, the model currently conflates worker skill and unreliability into a single channel representation. A more realistic system would account for differing worker skill levels, potentially utilizing worker ratings to inform query allocation – a feature absent in the current framework which assumes uniform query distribution. 

Furthermore, the chosen channel models for worker unreliability warrant further discussion. The assumption of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) errors may not hold in practice, particularly if workers possess some understanding of the task and can intentionally provide misleading responses (i.e., behave adversarially). Addressing the robustness of the framework to such strategic behavior would strengthen the work.

Several presentation aspects require improvement. The explanation of the numerical results is unclear. The labeling in Figure 2 needs to be more informative; specifically, it’s unclear what the $k=1$ case represents. If $k=1$, does this imply workers directly provide the correct label, circumventing the pair-wise comparison inherent in incidence coding? The statement on page 6, line 246, regarding knowledge of $P(B(X))$ should be presented earlier in the paper to avoid ambiguity. There are also minor errors: page 4, lines 135 & 136 incorrectly refer to figures (1.a -> 1.b, 1.b -> 1.c). 

Finally, the authors frequently use “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without providing specific numbering. Using “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” (lowercase) when not referencing a numbered result would be more appropriate. 

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to analyzing crowdsourcing systems. While the model’s simplification raises concerns about its real-world applicability, the work provides a valuable starting point for future research exploring the application of information theory to crowdsourcing. Addressing the reviewer's points regarding worker heterogeneity, adversarial behavior, and clarity of presentation would significantly enhance the impact of this work.
