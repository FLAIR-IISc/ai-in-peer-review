

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. The authors characterize fundamental limits on the query budget required to achieve a desired fidelity in crowdsourcing tasks. They consider two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The work establishes theoretical bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a specified error probability, using the source-channel separation theorem. Additionally, the authors analyze a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), providing a lower bound on its error probability under the spammer-hammer channel model.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing an information-theoretic perspective to crowdsourcing, which has not been extensively explored in prior work. The connection to JSCC is particularly novel, as most existing crowdsourcing literature focuses on empirical algorithmic approaches rather than fundamental performance limits. The theoretical results provide a useful benchmark for evaluating practical crowdsourcing systems. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough literature review that contextualizes their work within the broader crowdsourcing and machine learning literature.

Regarding technical strengths, the authors successfully frame crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic model, identifying key relationships between budget constraints and fidelity. The derivation of Theorems 2 and 3 provides meaningful insights into how worker skill levels affect system performance. The kIC analysis offers a concrete example of how coding theory principles can be applied to crowdsourcing design, with the lower bound in Theorem 4 providing a useful performance metric.

However, the paper has several important limitations that warrant consideration. First, the authors rely on the source-channel separation theorem without establishing that it applies to their specific setting with random channel states (where the state is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the decoder). While the theorem likely holds in this context, the authors should either prove this or cite an existing reference that establishes it for their model. This omission weakens the theoretical foundation of their key results.

Second, the model is somewhat restrictive as it assumes the taskmaster and crowdsourcer only have access to the labels (not the original items), which is not reflective of most real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. In practice, both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer typically have access to the original data (e.g., images, text), which could serve as valuable side information for the JSCC problem. The authors should consider how this side information would affect their theoretical bounds and whether it would improve performance.

Third, the interpretation of the results in Section 4.2 is somewhat confusing. The authors state that "the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow," but this is misleading. What they mean is that the lower bound on the required query rate decreases as k and q increase, meaning that better performance can be achieved with fewer queries. However, the phrase "the lower bound improves" suggests the bound itself becomes tighter, which is not accurate. The authors should clarify this point to avoid confusion.

Additionally, while the asymptotic analysis provides valuable theoretical insights, the authors would benefit from including a discussion of how their results might extend to finite-blocklength settings. As noted by another reviewer, a non-asymptotic characterization would be more immediately useful for practical crowdsourcing applications. The authors should consider incorporating insights from recent work on finite-blocklength JSCC (e.g., Kostina and Verdu, 2013) to strengthen their analysis.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by its narrow focus on asymptotic performance. While the theoretical framework is sound, the lack of practical implementation details or empirical validation of the proposed bounds reduces its immediate utility for practitioners. The authors could strengthen the significance by discussing how their theoretical results might guide the design of real-world crowdsourcing systems or by providing a comparison to existing practical algorithms.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though some technical details (particularly in the channel model and kIC analysis) could be presented more accessibly. The figures (especially Figure 2) would benefit from clearer labeling and interpretation to better convey the relationship between the theoretical bounds and the kIC performance.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes an interesting contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing systems. The information-theoretic approach is novel and well-motivated, and the results provide valuable insights into the fundamental trade-offs in crowdsourcing. However, the model's limitations and the lack of practical validation reduce its immediate impact. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical gaps and providing a more concrete connection to practical crowdsourcing applications.

Strengths:
- Novel information-theoretic perspective on crowdsourcing
- Clear theoretical framework establishing fundamental performance limits
- Well-written and well-organized presentation
- Thorough literature review contextualizing the work

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on source-channel separation theorem without establishing its validity for the specific channel model
- Restrictive model that ignores side information from original data
- Confusing interpretation of the kIC performance results
- Limited discussion of practical implications and finite-blocklength extensions

Recommendation: The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to crowdsourcing that could be valuable for future research. However, the current version has significant technical limitations that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication. I recommend major revision to strengthen the theoretical foundation, address the model limitations, and clarify the interpretation of results.
