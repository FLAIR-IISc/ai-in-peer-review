- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries for desired fidelity.  
- Models workers as noisy channels with possibly unknown skill levels and analyzes query budget–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a concrete query scheme, comparing its performance to derived bounds.  
- The information-theoretic framing is novel and could inspire further research applying these tools to crowdsourcing.  
- Theoretical contributions include Theorems 2 and 3 (benchmarks) and Theorem 4 + Corollary 1 (analysis of k-ary incidence coding).  
- Paper is well-motivated and clearly connects to rate-distortion and joint source-channel coding concepts.  
- k-ary incidence coding provides practical value by offering a concrete scheme and performance evaluation.  
- Simplifying assumptions (point-to-point mapping) may overlook key crowdsourcing dynamics and limit realism.  
- Treating worker reliability/skill as a single channel parameter and assigning uniform queries ignores worker heterogeneity and reputation systems.  
- Considered channel models (MSC and SHC) may not capture adversarial or strategic worker behaviors.  
- Discussion of query pricing is brief and could be expanded for practical relevance.  
- Numerical results (Figure 2) need clearer explanation and labeling, especially clarifying why k=1 equals direct labeling.  
- Suggest stating the assumption of knowing P(B(X)) earlier (Section 2.1) for clarity.  
- Minor fixes: correct Figure 1 references (1.a→1(b), 1.b→1(c)), and refine use of “theorem/lemma/corollary” terminology when informal.  
- Overall recommendation: valuable theoretical contribution with minor revisions recommended for addressing model limitations and clarity issues.
