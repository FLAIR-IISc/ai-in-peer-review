The paper presents an information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, deriving lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a specified probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels, specifically symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to represent potential spammers. The analysis considers nonadaptive coding schemes and two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer knows the skill level of all workers, and one where this information is unavailable. In both cases, the taskmaster is assumed to have no knowledge of worker skills and assigns an equal number of tasks to each worker. A necessary condition relating the minimum required rate – the number of queries per task – to k-ary incidence coding with a worker pool modeled by SHC(q) is also provided. The paper is well-organized and clearly written. 

However, the lack of experimental evaluation or simulations demonstrating the gap between current crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., on AMT) and these theoretical limits is a notable omission. Section 2.4 could be condensed, and Section 3.1 appears either unnecessary or could be significantly shortened. Prioritizing proof sketches over detailed expansions would be beneficial.  To the best of my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this work offers a novel analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective.  The results appear highly valuable for designers of crowdsourcing projects, providing a means to estimate the minimum query rate needed to achieve a desired error probability and potentially informing project pricing. I have not reviewed the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the completeness of the proofs.
