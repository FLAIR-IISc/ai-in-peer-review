The paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While the paper presented a challenging read, I found the modeling approach and resulting insights to be interesting and potentially valuable for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. I believe highlighting the practical implications of the results, particularly the intuition gained from the theoretical analysis (as briefly discussed at the end of Section 3), would significantly strengthen the paper. A more concise conclusion summarizing the key takeaways would also be beneficial. Although the authors made commendable efforts to make the paper accessible to readers less familiar with information theory, the technical details remained difficult to follow at times. I suggest restating the core results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, moving away from the specialized language of channels, codes, and distortion whenever possible. 

The related work section could benefit from mentioning the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This area also considers varying worker abilities and increasingly explores the combination of incentives with advanced aggregation methods.

Specifically, a few points could be clarified: on line 84, “are reviewed in” would be more accurate than “is reviewed in”; on line 106, the distinction between “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” was unclear – perhaps elaborating on their respective roles would improve understanding; on line 122, there appears to be a typo: “A A(X1,X2)” should likely be “A(X1,X2)”; and lines 275-276 contained a complex sentence structure that was difficult to parse. Figure 2, while potentially informative, lacked sufficient detail in its caption to facilitate interpretation.

The paper models crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyzes it using an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework, aiming to identify the ultimate fidelity achievable with a given budget. The approach utilizes a joint source-channel coding scheme to represent query design and inference over parallel noisy channels representing workers with imperfect skills. The authors also introduce and analyze a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding and investigate optimized query pricing in this setting. 

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous application of information theory to a practical problem. Establishing fundamental limits provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating existing and future crowdsourcing algorithms. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding offers a potentially effective query strategy, and the initial analysis of query pricing is a promising direction. 

However, the paper’s complexity and reliance on information-theoretic terminology present a significant barrier to accessibility. The mathematical derivations, while likely correct, are dense and difficult to follow without a strong background in the field. The connection between the theoretical results and concrete implications for designing real-world crowdsourcing systems is not always clear.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Establishment of fundamental limits on budget-fidelity tradeoff.
*   Introduction of a potentially useful query scheme (k-ary incidence coding).
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Low accessibility due to technical complexity and jargon.
*   Weak connection between theory and practical implications.
*   Limited discussion of related work in mechanism design for crowdsourcing.
*   Figure 2 is not readily interpretable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. However, its complexity and limited accessibility may hinder its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity and accessibility concerns outlined above, particularly by providing more intuitive explanations of the results and strengthening the connection to real-world applications. Specifically, a greater focus on translating the information-theoretic insights into actionable design principles would significantly enhance the paper’s value.
