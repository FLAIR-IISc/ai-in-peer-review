This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, providing lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a specified probability of error. The authors model workers as parallel independent channels, employing symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to account for spammers with probability 1-q. Assuming nonadaptive coding, the analysis considers two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer is aware of the workers' skill levels and another where this information is unknown. In both cases, the taskmaster is assumed to be uninformed about worker skills, resulting in uniform task assignment. A necessary condition is derived for the minimum required rate in k-ary incidence coding with an SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well-organized and well-written. However, it lacks experimental or simulation-based comparisons to existing schemes, such as those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be condensed, with a greater emphasis on proof sketches for the theorems. The paper's novelty lies in its information-theoretic approach to crowdsourcing, providing designers with valuable insights into the minimum rate required to accomplish tasks with a specified probability of error, which can inform project pricing. As the appendix was not reviewed, the proof's validity cannot be confirmed.
