- Paper frames budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing using rate-distortion and channel coding theory.  
- Authors derive an information-theoretic bound on achievable fidelity for a given budget.  
- Novelty is promising if this is the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, but novelty/practical relevance is questionable.  
- Core challenge: applying standard channel coding concepts to crowdsourcing is limited by how source data maps to worker queries (C in eq. 3).  
- Repeating queries to the same worker breaks i.i.d. assumptions because workers tend to repeat responses, undermining model validity (eqs. 1–2).  
- The derived bound may be overly optimistic or loose due to crowdsourcing-specific encoding constraints absent in standard channel coding.  
- Paper should include a much deeper discussion of these inherent constraints and their impact on bound tightness.  
- Needs empirical comparison of the bound with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, majority voting) to assess practical tightness.  
- Analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a useful concrete example but insufficient as the sole empirical comparison.  
- Strength: novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.  
- Strength: derivations appear mathematically sound under stated assumptions.  
- Strength: paper identifies key human-related and i.i.d. limitations.  
- Weakness: potential looseness of the bound due to unaddressed crowdsourcing constraints.  
- Weakness: lack of empirical validation/comparison with existing crowdsourcing methods.  
- Weakness: limited discussion and analysis of encoding constraints specific to crowdsourcing.  
- Minor technical issues: clarify minimization in eq. (8) over coding/decoding schemes under distortion constraint.  
- Minor technical issues: provide more detailed proof of Theorem 4.  
- Minor technical issues: clarify implication of ln(1−q)<0 and its relation to ε_S and ̂ε in Theorem 4.  
- Minor technical issues: justify or cite the term H_M(E(ε)) in eq. (17).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, useful starting-point bounds, and concrete k-ary coding analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: uncertain practical relevance without experiments, insufficient discussion of crowdsourcing constraints, and possibly too-loose bounds.  
- Overall recommendation: reject but encourage revision to include empirical comparisons and deeper constraint analysis to strengthen the paper.
