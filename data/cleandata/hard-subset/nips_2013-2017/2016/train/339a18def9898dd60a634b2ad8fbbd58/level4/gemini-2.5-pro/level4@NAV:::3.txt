This work frames the problem of aggregating crowdsourced worker outputs through the lens of information theory, deriving the foundational boundaries of the tradeoff between budget and fidelity. While I found some aspects of the paper challenging to follow (detailed below), the proposed model and its core insights represent a novel and potentially valuable approach to designing aggregation methods in crowdsourcing.

The paper would be strengthened by giving greater prominence to the high-level intuition derived from the results, such as those at the end of Section 3. A more explicit conclusion summarizing the key takeaways for practitioners or researchers would also be beneficial, even with space constraints. The authors' efforts to make the material accessible to a broader audience are commendable; however, for a reader not deeply versed in information theory, the manuscript remains difficult to penetrate. To improve clarity, I suggest translating many of the formal results from the vernacular of information theory (e.g., channels, codes, distortion) into the practical language of the crowdsourcing domain.

In the related work section, please acknowledge the parallel and increasingly convergent body of work on robust economic incentives for workers, often found under the terms "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This literature is relevant as it also addresses issues like heterogeneous worker skill levels and, more recently, the integration of incentive structures with advanced aggregation techniques.
