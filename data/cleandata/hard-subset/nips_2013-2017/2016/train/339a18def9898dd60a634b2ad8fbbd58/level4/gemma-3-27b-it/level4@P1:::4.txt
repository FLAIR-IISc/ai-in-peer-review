The paper considers a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster divides a large task (e.g., an inference problem) into smaller tasks, presented to a crowd of workers. A crowdsourcer then collects and processes the information provided by these workers to complete the inference task. This setup accounts for varying worker skill levels and the potential for unreliable reporting. The objective is to ensure a certain level of fidelity or quality of the final outcome, even with potentially inaccurate information from workers. This requires careful design of the queries presented to the workers, with the total number of queries representing the system’s budget. The paper studies the fundamental trade-off between this budget and fidelity, framing it as a joint source-channel coding problem in information theory. The taskmaster is modeled as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable worker responses as a communication channel. This mapping allows the authors to establish a lower bound on the budget needed for a desired fidelity level, independent of specific query design schemes or decoding algorithms. The authors then analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and compare its performance to the derived information-theoretic bound using a particular channel model. The reviewer finds the connection between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing problem with unreliable workers insightful, and believes this work may encourage further research using information-theoretic tools in this area. 

However, the reviewer questions the realism of the model. The paper appears to prioritize mapping the crowdsourcing problem to a standard point-to-point communication problem to leverage existing information theory results, potentially simplifying the real-world complexities. The authors should comment on the following: 1) The model merges worker reliability and skill level into a single channel. In practice, workers often have ratings reflecting their skill level, and taskmasters may allocate queries accordingly. 2) How realistic are the chosen channel models for representing worker unreliability? What about adversarial workers who intentionally degrade fidelity? 

The explanation of the numerical results is not entirely clear. The labeling of curves in Fig. 2 could be improved. Specifically, what does the $k=1$ case represent? If k=1, does the worker directly provide the label, eliminating the pairwise comparison inherent in the incidence coding scheme? On page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowing $P(B(X))$ is mentioned, but should have been stated earlier in the paper. 

Minor issues: On page 4, line 135, “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b” and “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c”. The authors frequently use “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without specific numbering; using “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” would be more appropriate in these instances.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information-theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing problem.
*   Clear identification of fundamental limits and trade-offs.
*   Well-defined model and analysis of k-ary incidence coding.
*   Potential to stimulate further research in this area.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The model may be overly simplistic and not fully capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing systems.
*   The assumptions regarding worker skill levels and channel models require further justification.
*   The explanation of numerical results is not entirely clear.
*   Minor stylistic and notational issues.
