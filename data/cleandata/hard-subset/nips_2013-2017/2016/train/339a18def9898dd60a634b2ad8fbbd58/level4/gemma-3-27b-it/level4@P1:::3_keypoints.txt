- Paper tackles worker-output aggregation in crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools to derive budget–fidelity limits.  
- Modeling approach and theoretical insights are interesting and potentially valuable for designing aggregation techniques.  
- Authors should highlight practical implications and intuition from the theoretical analysis (especially end of Section 3).  
- A more concise conclusion summarizing key takeaways is needed.  
- Technical presentation remains difficult to follow for non–information-theory readers despite accessibility efforts.  
- Restate core results in crowdsourcing terms and avoid specialized channel/code/distortion jargon where possible.  
- Related work should cite mechanism design and robust incentive literature for crowdsourcing.  
- Clarify roles and distinction between “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” (line 106).  
- Fix typos and unclear phrasing: “are reviewed in” (line 84), “A A(X1,X2)” → “A(X1,X2)” (line 122), and rework lines 275–276 for clarity.  
- Figure 2 needs a more detailed caption and better interpretability.  
- Paper models crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation using a rate–distortion framework to find fidelity limits for a given budget.  
- Uses joint source–channel coding to represent query design and inference over parallel noisy worker channels.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a query scheme and studies optimized query pricing.  
- Strengths: novel application of information theory, rigorous analysis, fundamental limits as benchmarks, and a promising query scheme.  
- Weaknesses: high technical complexity and jargon, unclear practical design implications, limited discussion of mechanism-design literature, and an uninterpretable Figure 2.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions focused on improving clarity, accessibility, and translation of theory into actionable design guidance.
