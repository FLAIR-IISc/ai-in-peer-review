The paper frames digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic paradigm to derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a specified error probability. Workers are modeled as independent parallel channels. The channel models considered are the M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) to capture worker errors and the spammer–hammer model SHC(q), where a worker is a hammer with probability q (spammer with probability 1−q). The coding/query policy is assumed non-adaptive. Two settings are analyzed: one in which the crowdsourcer (who infers task values from collected responses) knows all worker reliabilities, and one in which the crowdsourcer has no information about them. In both cases, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks) is assumed to have no knowledge of worker skills, so each worker receives the same number of tasks. The paper also provides a necessary condition on the minimum rate—queries per task—for k-ary incidence coding, where a worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to the same or different categories, under an SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well organized and clearly written. There were no experiments or simulations to indicate how far commonly used schemes (e.g., on AMT) are from these limits. Section 2.4 could be tightened, and Section 3.1 is unnecessary or could be shortened; concise proof sketches of the main theorems would be more valuable. The work appears novel; to my knowledge and as claimed, crowdsourcing has not previously been analyzed from an information-theoretic viewpoint. It seems useful in practice, enabling designers of crowdsourcing projects to determine the minimum query rate needed for a target error probability and to inform pricing. I did not read the appendix, so I cannot comment on the proofs.
