This paper frames the problem of label aggregation in crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework. The goal is to establish the fundamental limits of the trade-off between the budget allocated (modeled as "rate" or queries per item) and the fidelity of the final aggregated labels (modeled as "distortion" or error rate). The core idea is to model the dataset as an information source, the crowd workers as parallel noisy communication channels, the query scheme as an encoder, and the inference algorithm as a decoder.

Using this model, the authors derive theoretical bounds on the minimum budget required to achieve a target fidelity under two conditions: 1) when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and 2) when skill levels are perfectly known by the aggregator (SL-CS). The analysis is presented for worker pools modeled as M-ary Symmetric Channels (MSC) and Spammer-Hammer Channels (SHC). Furthermore, the paper introduces a practical query scheme called k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), where workers are asked to compare k items at a time, and analyzes its performance. This work distinguishes itself from much of the existing literature (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.), which tends to focus on the design and analysis of specific aggregation algorithms, by instead seeking to define the ultimate performance bounds achievable by *any* query and inference scheme.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and powerful conceptualization of the crowdsourcing problem. Applying the tools of information theory provides a fresh perspective and allows for the derivation of fundamental, algorithm-agnostic limits. Establishing such bounds is a significant contribution, as it provides a benchmark against which all practical crowdsourcing systems can be measured.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the budget-fidelity functions in Theorems 2 and 3 is a solid theoretical result. The insight at the end of Section 3—that with unknown skills the system capacity is limited by the *average worker*, whereas with known skills it relates to the *average of worker capacities*—is particularly interesting and intuitive.

3.  **Bridging Theory and Practice:** The introduction and analysis of k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) is a valuable attempt to connect the abstract theoretical framework to a concrete, practical query design. This demonstrates how the information-theoretic lens can be used not just for analysis but also for inspiring new methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** While the authors have made an effort to introduce information theory concepts, the paper remains very difficult to follow for readers who are not experts in that field. The heavy reliance on the language of channels, codes, and distortion often obscures the practical crowdsourcing intuition. The paper's impact could be significantly increased by translating the key results and their implications back into the language of crowdsourcing.
    *   **Suggestion:** I strongly recommend adding a dedicated "Conclusion" section to summarize the main takeaways for a machine learning or crowdsourcing practitioner. What should someone designing a crowdsourcing system learn from this work?
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be highly beneficial to more prominently highlight the high-level insights, such as the one mentioned at the end of Section 3. This is a key result that is currently somewhat buried.
    *   **Suggestion:** Restating the main theorems in more accessible terms would also help. For example, Theorem 2 could be explained as: "To achieve an error rate no higher than X, you must ask at least Y queries per item on average, regardless of how cleverly you design your questions or aggregation algorithm."

2.  **Related Work:** The related work section focuses on aggregation algorithms but omits a significant and increasingly related line of research on designing robust economic incentives for workers, often discussed under the terms "crowdsourcing mechanism design" or "incentive compatibility." This literature also addresses issues of worker heterogeneity and quality and has recently begun to merge with work on sophisticated aggregation methods. Acknowledging and positioning this paper relative to that body of work would strengthen the submission.

3.  **Presentation Details:**
    *   The distinction between the "taskmaster" (encoder) and "crowdsourcer" (decoder) was initially confusing. While standard in a communication model, in the context of crowdsourcing these roles are often performed by the same entity. A brief clarification could help.
    *   The caption for Figure 2 is too sparse. It is difficult to understand what is being plotted and what conclusions to draw from it without re-reading the main text carefully. A more detailed caption explaining the meaning of the different bounds (e.g., "The solid blue line represents the ultimate theoretical limit for any possible query scheme...") would make the figure much more informative.
    *   The sentence on lines 275-276 ("In case...") is difficult to parse. Please consider rephrasing for clarity.
    *   Line 84: "is reviewed in" should likely be "are reviewed in".
    *   Line 122: There appears to be a typo ("A A(X1,X2)").

In summary, this paper presents a highly original and potentially impactful theoretical framework for understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The main weakness is its accessibility to the broader NIPS audience. If the authors can significantly improve the clarity by better translating their results and insights into the domain language of crowdsourcing, the paper would be a strong contribution to the conference.
