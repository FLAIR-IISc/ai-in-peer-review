The paper formulates digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework and derives lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to attain a specified error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. The channel models considered are symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture worker errors and the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), in which a worker is a spammer with probability 1−q. The coding is assumed to be nonadaptive. Two cases are analyzed: when the crowdsourcer, who makes the final decision on each task after aggregating worker responses, knows all worker skill levels, and when it knows nothing about them. In both cases, the taskmaster, who assigns tasks to workers, is assumed to be unaware of worker skills, so each worker receives the same number of tasks. Finally, the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum required rate—queries per task—for k-ary incidence coding, where the worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to similar or different breeds, under an SHC(q) worker pool.

The paper is well organized and clearly written. There are no experiments or simulations showing how far currently used schemes (e.g., on AMT) are from the derived limits. I believe Section 2.4 can be shortened and Section 3.1 is unnecessary or could be condensed; instead, sketch proofs of the theorems would be more valuable. The contribution appears novel; as claimed and to the best of my knowledge, there has not been prior information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem. The results seem practically useful, allowing designers to determine the minimum rate needed to achieve a target error probability and aiding in pricing such projects. I did not read the appendix, so I cannot comment on the proofs.
