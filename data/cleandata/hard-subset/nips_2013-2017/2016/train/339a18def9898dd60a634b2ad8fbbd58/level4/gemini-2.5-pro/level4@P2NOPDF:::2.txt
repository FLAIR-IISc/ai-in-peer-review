This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the crowdsourcing problem. The authors model crowdsourcing as a Joint Source-Channel Coding (JSCC) problem where item labels are the source, the taskmaster is the encoder, crowd workers represent multiple uses of a channel, and their unknown skill levels correspond to a random channel state. Leveraging this model, the paper derives asymptotic fundamental limits on the number of queries required per item. Additionally, the authors provide a lower bound on the error probability for a specific "k-ary incidence coding" scheme.

The primary contribution of this work is its application of the JSCC framework to crowdsourcing. This information-theoretic perspective offers a principled way to establish fundamental performance limits and is a promising direction for future research. The paper is well-written and the proposed model is interesting. However, there are several key areas where the analysis and modeling assumptions could be significantly strengthened.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Justification of the Source-Channel Separation Theorem:** The core results in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on the applicability of the source-channel separation theorem. However, the paper's model involves a channel with a random state that is unknown to the encoder and may or may not be known to the decoder. While the separation theorem likely holds in this scenario, its validity is not self-evident and requires rigorous justification. The authors should either provide a proof establishing the theorem's applicability under these specific conditions or cite existing work that does so.

2.  **Lack of Side Information in the Model:** A significant limitation of the proposed model is its restrictive assumption about the information available to the taskmaster (encoder) and crowdsourcer (decoder). The model assumes they only access the item *labels* (the source). In many practical crowdsourcing scenarios, both parties also have access to the *items* themselves (e.g., the images to be labeled, the text to be classified). This item data constitutes powerful side information. The presence of side information, particularly at the decoder or at both the encoder and decoder, could fundamentally alter the problem and its associated information-theoretic limits. A model incorporating this feature would be more realistic and convincing.

3.  **Focus on Asymptotic Limits:** While the asymptotic analysis provides valuable theoretical insight, its practical relevance to real-world crowdsourcing—which operates with a finite number of items and queries—is limited. A non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be significantly more impactful for the community. The authors are strongly encouraged to investigate and discuss their problem in the finite blocklength regime. The recent work by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC appears highly relevant and could provide a path toward such an analysis:
    *   V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013.

**Minor Comment:**

1.  **Clarity in Figure Discussion:** In the discussion of Figure 2 (Section 4.2), the phrasing "the lower bounds for kIC improve" is ambiguous. It would be clearer to state that the lower bound on the error probability *decreases* as k and q grow, which suggests better potential performance for the code. This is distinct from the bound itself becoming tighter or "improving" relative to an upper bound.

In summary, this paper introduces an interesting and potentially fruitful direction for modeling crowdsourcing. However, the theoretical claims need stronger justification, and the model could be extended to better reflect practical scenarios, particularly by considering side information and providing a more useful non-asymptotic analysis.
