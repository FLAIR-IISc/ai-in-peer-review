This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget (queries per item) and fidelity. The authors frame the crowdsourcing problem as one of joint source-channel coding (JSCC). In this model, the true labels of the items are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster acts as an encoder, the crowd workers are modeled as parallel noisy channels, and their varying skill levels are represented by random channel states. The crowdsourcer then acts as the decoder.

Using this framework, the paper leverages the source-channel separation theorem to establish asymptotic lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve a certain error probability. The analysis considers two scenarios: one where the worker skill levels are unknown to the decoder, and another where they are known. The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme called "k-ary incidence coding," providing a performance lower bound for this scheme under a spammer-hammer worker model.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that introduces a novel and potentially fruitful direction by applying a formal information-theoretic lens to the crowdsourcing problem.

**Technical Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Justification for the Source-Channel Separation Theorem:** The main results in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on the standard source-channel separation theorem. However, the paper applies this theorem to a channel with a random state that is unknown at the encoder (and may or may not be known at the decoder). While it is likely that the separation principle still holds in this setting, this is a non-standard application that requires justification. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors provided a brief proof or cited an existing result from the information theory literature that establishes the validity of the separation theorem under these specific conditions.

2.  **Absence of Side Information in the Model:** The proposed model may be overly restrictive as it ignores the features of the items being labeled (e.g., the images themselves). In many practical crowdsourcing scenarios, both the taskmaster (encoder) and the crowdsourcer (decoder) have access to this data. This information could serve as valuable side information. While side information known only to the encoder might not change the fundamental limits, side information available at the decoder, or at both encoder and decoder, can be very powerful. A more realistic and compelling model would incorporate this aspect. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how item features as side information could be integrated into the JSCC framework and how they might affect the derived limits.

3.  **Clarity on "Improving" Bounds:** In Section 4.2, the discussion of Figure 2 states that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) "improve" as k and q increase. This phrasing is ambiguous. A lower bound on error probability does not "improve" in the same way performance does. It would be clearer to state that the bound becomes smaller (i.e., the value of the lower bound on the error probability decreases), which suggests that the code's potential performance is better. This is a minor point of clarification.

**Comments After Author Rebuttal:**

The application of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing remains an interesting and novel direction. However, as also noted by another reviewer, the practical utility of these asymptotic results is limited. A non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be significantly more useful for system designers. The authors are encouraged to investigate recent work on the finite blocklength regime, which provides the tools for such an analysis. For instance, the following paper provides a relevant starting point for non-asymptotic analysis of JSCC:

V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013.
