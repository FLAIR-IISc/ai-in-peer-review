This paper presents an information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing and derives asymptotic fundamental limits on the number of queries per item. The problem is cast as joint source–channel coding (JSCC) with a random channel state. The state is unknown at the encoder but may or may not be known at the decoder. In the model, item labels form an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster acts as the encoder; crowd workers constitute multiple uses of a channel; worker skill levels are the channel’s random states; and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. Using this framework, the authors apply existing JSCC coding-rate results to establish limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model. Overall, the paper is well written and appears to be a first step toward JSCC-based modeling of crowdsourcing.

Technical comments:
1. The crowdsourcing problem is modeled as JSCC with a random channel state. The authors invoke the standard source–channel separation theorem to obtain their main results (Theorems 2 and 3). However, they should first justify that separation still holds when the channel has a random state that is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. I believe separation does hold under these circumstances, but the paper should either provide a proof under the stated assumptions or cite an appropriate existing proof.

2. The taskmaster is modeled as an encoder that observes only the item labels, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder that observes only the workers’ labels. This seems overly restrictive, as in practice both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer may have access to the original items. Such access constitutes side information in the JSCC problem. While side information only at the encoder may not help, side information at both terminals—or even at the decoder alone—can be highly beneficial. The model would be more convincing and realistic if side information were incorporated and its impact analyzed.

3. In Section 4.2, when discussing Fig. 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC “improve” as k and q grow. I assume they mean the lower bound decreases with k and q, which would suggest potential performance gains for the code. However, this does not by itself imply that the bound “improves,” since there is no accompanying upper bound for comparison.

Edited following the authors’ response: Modeling crowdsourcing via JSCC is interesting, but—as another reviewer also noted—a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be more useful. The authors should consider recent nonasymptotic converses for JSCC, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, 59(5):2545–2575, May 2013.
