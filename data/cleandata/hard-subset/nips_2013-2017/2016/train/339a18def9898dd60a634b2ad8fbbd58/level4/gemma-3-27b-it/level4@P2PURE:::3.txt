This paper tackles the problem of aggregating responses in crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While I found the insights and the proposed model to be interesting and potentially valuable for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I struggled to fully grasp the details of the presentation (see below). I believe the key high-level takeaways from the results – such as those discussed at the end of section 3 – would benefit from more prominent emphasis, and a concluding summary articulating the practical implications of this work would be helpful, even given space constraints. The authors made commendable efforts to explain the concepts to readers less familiar with information theory, but the paper remained challenging to follow. I recommend re-expressing the main results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, shifting away from the technical language of channels, codes, and distortion. The related work section should also acknowledge the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This line of work also considers heterogeneous worker abilities and, increasingly, integrates incentive design with advanced aggregation methods.

Line 122: Please correct the notation to “A A(X1,X2).” Lines 275-276: The phrasing “In case…” was difficult to parse.
