- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives rate–distortion lower bounds for queries per item under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills, plus analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

- Relates to standard aggregation literature (majority voting, Dawid–Skene, spectral-EM, graph-based clustering) and should also connect to incentive-compatible crowdsourcing/mechanism-design work.

- Strength — Originality: The rate–distortion framing and the SL-UK vs SL-CS split are novel and insightful.

- Strength — Significance: Provides fundamental lower bounds as practical benchmarks and introduces kIC as a natural, error-correcting query family.

- Strength — Technical quality: Results are carefully derived and the oracle lower bound for spammer–hammer kIC is clearly stated.

- Weakness — Clarity: Presentation is dense with information-theoretic terminology; theorems should be immediately restated in crowdsourcing terms with intuitive summaries and concrete takeaways.

- Weakness — Practical interpretability: Figure 2 and results need clearer captions, parameter regimes, and worked examples translating bounds into “queries per item” for typical settings.

- Weakness — Cost normalization: Must make explicit the cost model for varying-k kIC queries and unambiguously justify the proposed pricing heuristic.

- Weakness — Assumptions & achievability: The oracle assumptions (e.g., perfect spammer identification) need discussion of achievable schemes, gaps for simple decoders, and clarification of adaptive vs non-adaptive and independence assumptions.

- Weakness — Related work: Paper should include discussion of robust incentive/mechanism-design literature and its interaction with worker heterogeneity and aggregation bounds.

- Quality rating: Technically solid but presentation hampers verification and practical uptake.

- Clarity rating: Needs more crowdsourcing-language restatements, improved figures/captions, and a short conclusion with actionable insights.

- Originality rating: High — novel perspective and kIC error-correction analysis.

- Significance rating: Potentially high if made more accessible and practically interpretable.

- Pros for acceptance: novel theoretical framing with clean lower bounds; insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction; natural kIC query family with pricing implications.

- Cons for acceptance: poor accessibility for non-IT audiences; limited empirical/achievability discussion; ambiguity around cost normalization and pricing.

- Specific edits: clarify “taskmaster” vs “crowdsourcer,” fix typos (lines 84, 122, 275–276), rewrite unclear sentences, expand Figure 2 caption with parameters/examples, and add one-line, units-explicit restatements after each theorem.

- Overall assessment: Interesting and potentially benchmark-setting theoretical contribution that requires clearer crowdsourcing restatements, improved figures/examples, and a concise conclusion to reach practical impact.
