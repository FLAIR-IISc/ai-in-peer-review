This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of digital crowdsourcing, establishing lower bounds on the number of queries required per task to achieve a specified error probability. The work models workers as independent parallel channels, specifically utilizing symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMC) to represent worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to account for malicious workers. The analysis focuses on nonadaptive coding schemes and considers scenarios where the crowdsourcer either knows or does not know the skill levels of the individual workers. In both cases, the taskmaster is assumed to have no prior knowledge of worker skill and distributes tasks uniformly. A key contribution is a necessary condition, expressed as a minimum rate (queries per task), for k-ary incidence coding with a worker pool modeled as SHC(q) channels. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. However, the lack of experimental validation or simulations demonstrating the practical implications of these bounds – for example, in the context of platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk – is a limitation. Specifically, Section 2.4 could benefit from concision, and Section 3.1 appears somewhat extraneous or could be condensed. Prioritizing proof sketches over detailed derivations would improve readability. 

The work appears to be novel; the authors are not aware of prior information-theoretic analyses of crowdsourcing, a claim supported by the literature review. This approach has the potential to be highly valuable for designers of crowdsourcing systems, providing a theoretical foundation for determining minimum query rates and informing project pricing strategies based on desired error probabilities. The appendix was not reviewed and therefore cannot be commented upon.
