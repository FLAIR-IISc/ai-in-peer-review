- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem to study the budget (queries) vs. fidelity (accuracy) trade-off.  
- Uses an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework to derive fundamental limits on minimum queries for target fidelity.  
- Presents two main theorems for unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known worker skills (SL-CS).  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) query scheme and compares its performance to theoretical bounds.  
- Strength: novel theoretical connection between crowdsourcing and information theory.  
- Strength: rigorous, technically sound derivation of fundamental limits (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- Strength: concrete insights from kIC analysis on how query design affects performance.  
- Strength: identifies fundamental limits independent of specific aggregation algorithms.  
- Weakness: worker reliability model oversimplifies by merging skill and reliability and assumes uniform query allocation across workers.  
- Weakness: channel models (MSC and SHC) are limited—do not capture nuanced or adversarial worker behaviors.  
- Weakness: key modeling assumption that crowdsourcer knows P(B(X)) is introduced too late and needs clearer justification.  
- Weakness: presentation and clarity issues (incorrect figure references, unclear explanation of Figure 2 and k=1, inconsistent theorem/lemma/corollary labeling).  
- Weakness: practical applicability unclear—paper lacks concrete examples or case studies showing real-world impact.  
- Originality: strong theoretical approach though kIC itself is not entirely novel; main novelty is the information-theoretic analysis.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant theoretical contribution with manageable clarity/modeling issues to address.
