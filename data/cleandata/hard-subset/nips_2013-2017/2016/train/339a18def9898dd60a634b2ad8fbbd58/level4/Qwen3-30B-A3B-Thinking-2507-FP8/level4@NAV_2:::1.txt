

The paper addresses the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing systems using an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework. It models crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem where workers are represented as parallel noisy channels (specifically, M-ary symmetric channels and spammer-hammer channel models), with the taskmaster's query design and crowdsourcer's inference algorithm forming a joint source-channel coding scheme. The authors establish theoretical bounds on the budget-fidelity tradeoff (where budget corresponds to query rate) for two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). A notable contribution is the introduction and analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which poses queries involving k items simultaneously and leverages error-correcting properties to improve performance. This work extends previous NIPS contributions on crowdsourcing (e.g., [5, 8, 11, 12, 13]) by shifting from algorithmic design to fundamental performance limits, providing a theoretical foundation for optimizing crowdsourcing systems that previous works only approached empirically.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous derivations of fundamental bounds (Theorems 2-3) and performance analysis of kIC (Theorems 4, Corollary 1). The information-theoretic approach provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the budget-fidelity tradeoff, which is a significant advancement over previous works that focused on specific aggregation algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting) without establishing fundamental limits. The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-structured mathematical formulations and clear explanations of the communication system model. The main limitation is that the analysis assumes independent workers and specific channel models, which may not fully capture real-world worker behavior where correlations and more complex error patterns exist.

Strengths: 
1. Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing fundamental information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing, which is a novel perspective compared to the algorithmic focus of previous NIPS papers (e.g., [1, 5, 8, 11, 12, 13]).
2. Quality: The theoretical derivations are sound, with complete proofs provided in the appendices. The framework unifies various crowdsourcing scenarios under a single information-theoretic lens.
3. Significance: The results provide a theoretical foundation for optimizing crowdsourcing systems, with potential applications in platform design (e.g., query pricing via the kIC analysis in Appendix E) and understanding fundamental performance constraints.
4. Clarity: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the system model and the connection between coding theory and crowdsourcing.

Weaknesses:
1. The channel models (MSC and SHC) may be too simplistic for real-world crowdsourcing, where worker behavior may exhibit more complex patterns beyond spammer-hammer dynamics.
2. The analysis assumes large datasets (L → ∞) and iid assumptions, which may not hold for all practical crowdsourcing applications.
3. While the kIC analysis is theoretically interesting, the paper does not provide sufficient empirical validation of the theoretical bounds with real data.
4. The paper could better connect to recent NIPS work on crowdsourcing (e.g., [1, 12, 13]) to more explicitly differentiate its contributions.

Pro-acceptance arguments: This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of crowdsourcing by establishing fundamental limits that previous algorithmic works could not address. The information-theoretic approach provides a unifying framework that advances the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing systems, with clear implications for practical design. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and significantly extend the state of the art.

Con-acceptance arguments: The theoretical results may be too abstract for practical implementation without empirical validation, and the channel models could be more realistic to better reflect real-world crowdsourcing dynamics. The paper would benefit from more concrete connections to current crowdsourcing platforms and their specific challenges.

Overall, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of crowdsourcing systems. While the assumptions may limit immediate practical applicability, the fundamental insights are valuable for both theoretical and applied crowdsourcing research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the connection to real-world applications and to better contextualize the theoretical results within the broader NIPS literature on crowdsourcing.
