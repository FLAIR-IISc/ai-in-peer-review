- Novel information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication system, unifying query design, worker reliability, and inference.  
- Technically sound derivations applying rate-distortion and joint source-channel coding; Theorems 2 and 3 appear correct.  
- Potentially important contribution if this is the first rigorous rate-distortion treatment of budget–fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing.  

- Core analogy is questionable: assumes a richness of query “codes” comparable to channel coding that likely does not exist in practice.  
- I.i.d. (memoryless) worker assumption is unrealistic because human responses can be correlated (e.g., repetition yields same answer), breaking the channel model.  
- Derived bounds risk being overly optimistic and possibly unachievable due to practical constraints on valid encoding/query schemes.  
- No empirical comparison to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods to assess the tightness or practical relevance of the bounds.  
- Theoretical novelty is limited: mainly a new application/ formulation rather than new information-theoretic tools.  

- Add empirical evaluation comparing bounds to practical algorithms on real or synthetic datasets.  
- Include a dedicated discussion of the limitations of the channel-coding analogy and constraints on valid query codes.  
- Clarify Eq.(8) by minimizing over p(Z_hat|Z) per standard rate-distortion formulation.  
- Expand and clarify the proof of Theorem 4 and explicitly state the condition hat-epsilon < bar-epsilon_S.  
- Justify using the average worker capacity H_M(E(ε)) in Theorem 2 (e.g., reference compound-channel results or unknown-channel capacity rationale).
