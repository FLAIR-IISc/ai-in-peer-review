- Proposes a novel information-theoretic framework mapping crowdsourcing to a joint source-channel coding problem, establishing a budget–fidelity trade-off via information-theoretic bounds.  
- Conceptually interesting and potentially inspiring for future work applying information theory to crowdsourcing.  
- Simplifies worker unreliability to a single channel parameter, collapsing skill and reliability, which undermines real-world applicability.  
- Assumes uniform query allocation across workers, conflicting with platforms that allocate tasks based on worker skill; model should address worker-specific heterogeneity.  
- Channel model assumes stochastic errors and symmetric error characteristics without justification; ignores adversarial workers and task-dependent biases.  
- Numerical evaluation (Fig. 2) is poorly presented: unlabeled curves and unclear meaning of "k=1" (direct reporting) and other k-values.  
- Critical technical oversight: inconsistent and unnumbered use of "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary," violating mathematical writing conventions.  
- Notational and formatting errors: Figure 1 labeling inconsistencies ("Figure 1.a" vs "Figure 1a", wrong subfigure reference), and $P(B|X)$ is introduced too late.  
- Recommendations: incorporate worker skill heterogeneity (e.g., channel-state information), justify channel assumptions against real data and adversarial cases, and clarify operational meaning of k-ary coding.  
- Fix presentation and rigor: label figures and curves, define k-values in captions/text, introduce $P(B|X)$ earlier, and number/label theoretical results correctly.  
- Overall: strong theoretical novelty but limited practical impact unless realism and exposition issues are addressed.
