- Paper maps crowdsourcing with unreliable workers to a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries vs. fidelity that are algorithm-independent.  
- Authors analyze a specific querying scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compare its performance to the derived lower bounds under a particular worker-channel model.  
- The information-theoretic perspective and the rate–distortion analogy are appealing and likely to stimulate further work.  
- Concern: the point-to-point communication reduction may over-simplify and not faithfully capture real-world crowdsourcing systems.  
- Modeling conflates worker skill and response reliability into a single channel abstraction and assumes equal query allocation to all workers; need to consider adaptive assignment/pricing based on worker histories or partial skill knowledge.  
- Channel models (symmetric noise, spammer–hammer) may be unrealistic: some workers have partial knowledge or act adversarially/strategically—need discussion of robustness and whether analysis can accommodate such behaviors.  
- Numerical results need clarity: improve curve labels in Figure 2 for readability.  
- Clarify the meaning of k=1 in kIC—does k=1 correspond to direct labeling (standard labeling) since pairwise incidence queries aren’t possible?  
- The assumption of known P(B(X)) (page 6, line 246) underpins bounds and should be stated earlier in the manuscript.  
- Minor fixes: incorrect figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b, Figure 1.b → 1.c) need correction.  
- Stylistic: avoid capitalizing “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” when not referencing numbered results; use lowercase in those cases.
