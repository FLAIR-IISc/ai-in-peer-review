

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems using information-theoretic tools, specifically focusing on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication problem over parallel noisy channels, where workers' skill levels are represented as channel characteristics. They derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries required to achieve a target fidelity (error probability), considering two scenarios: when workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly estimated (SL-CS). The paper also analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides performance bounds for this scheme under a spammer-hammer channel model.

The work builds upon previous studies of crowdsourcing aggregation methods such as majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm, but approaches the problem from a fundamentally different angle by leveraging information theory rather than statistical modeling. This is a valuable contribution as it provides ultimate performance bounds that previous works on crowdsourcing aggregation could not address. The paper's approach is particularly relevant to NIPS as it bridges information theory and human-in-the-loop computation, two areas of interest to the community.

Strengths:
- The theoretical framework is novel and provides fundamental limits that previous works on crowdsourcing aggregation could not address.
- The derivation of the distortion-rate function for crowdsourcing is rigorous and well-motivated.
- The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides concrete insights into how query design affects performance.
- The paper connects to recent NIPS work on crowdsourcing (e.g., Zhang et al. 2014, Whitehill et al. 2009) while offering a distinct theoretical perspective.

Weaknesses:
- The paper is overly abstract and uses information-theoretic terminology (channels, codes, distortion) without sufficiently translating results into practical crowdsourcing insights. The connection to actual crowdsourcing design is not made clear enough for readers without information theory background.
- The distinction between "taskmaster" and "crowdsourcer" is confusing and not properly clarified, potentially misleading readers.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results, especially how they inform real-world crowdsourcing system design.
- Figure 2 lacks sufficient explanation, making it difficult to interpret the results.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and the theoretical derivations appear correct. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their bounds, which is a strength. However, the lack of experimental validation or concrete examples limits the paper's completeness as a scientific contribution.

Clarity: The paper is challenging to follow for readers unfamiliar with information theory. While the authors attempt to make the paper accessible, the heavy reliance on channel coding terminology creates a barrier. The paper would be significantly clearer if results were re-expressed in terms of crowdsourcing metrics (e.g., "to achieve 90% accuracy, you need at least X queries per item" rather than "the rate-distortion function is R*(D) = ...").

Originality: The paper's use of information theory to analyze crowdsourcing is original and represents a significant departure from previous work that has focused on statistical models of worker behavior. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

Significance: The work provides fundamental limits that are useful for understanding the theoretical boundaries of crowdsourcing systems. However, the lack of practical guidance on how to apply these limits to real-world systems reduces the paper's impact. The authors could strengthen the significance by discussing how their bounds inform the design of specific crowdsourcing applications.

Pro-con list for acceptance:
- Pro: Novel theoretical framework that provides fundamental limits for crowdsourcing
- Pro: Rigorous proofs and analysis of the budget-fidelity tradeoff
- Pro: Analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides practical insights
- Con: Overly abstract presentation that doesn't connect well to practical crowdsourcing design
- Con: Confusing terminology (taskmaster vs. crowdsourcer) and lack of clear practical guidance
- Con: Limited discussion of related work on economic incentives for workers (e.g., mechanism design for crowdsourcing)

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of crowdsourcing, but its presentation and lack of practical insights limit its impact. I recommend rejection, but would consider a resubmission with a significantly revised presentation that translates the theoretical results into practical crowdsourcing design guidelines.
