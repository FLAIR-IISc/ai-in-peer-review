The paper addresses the problem of data labeling using crowdsourcing, framing it as a channel coding problem. Each worker is modeled as a noisy channel—specifically, the authors consider both a symmetric memoryless channel without feedback and a spammer/hammer channel model. They then analyze the problem through the lens of coding theory, relating the crowdsourcing budget to the achievable rate of the code. The paper investigates tradeoffs between distortion and budget, and proposes a basic coding scheme, termed k-incidence coding, which requires workers to label subsets of *k* items at a time. 

I found the paper to be fairly interesting. However, as the work falls somewhat outside my primary area of expertise, I am unable to offer detailed technical comments or a thorough critique of the technical approach. 

I appreciate the novel perspective of applying channel coding to crowdsourcing. The connection between the budget and the coding rate is a clever way to formalize the cost-accuracy tradeoff inherent in crowdsourced labeling. The consideration of different worker models (symmetric and spammer/hammer) adds to the robustness of the analysis. 

The description of k-incidence coding is clear, and its rationale—to potentially improve labeling accuracy by requiring workers to consider relationships between items—is intuitive. Further exploration of this coding scheme and comparisons to other existing crowdsourcing methods would be valuable.

Despite my limited expertise in this specific area, the paper appears well-motivated and the presented approach is logically sound. The mathematical formulation seems appropriate for the problem, and the results, while not deeply scrutinized by me, appear to be presented clearly.

Overall, this is a potentially valuable contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing from an information-theoretic perspective. While I cannot provide an in-depth assessment, I believe it warrants further consideration by reviewers with more expertise in this field.
