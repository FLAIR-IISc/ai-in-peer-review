This paper investigates a crowdsourcing framework in which a central taskmaster decomposes a large inference problem into a set of micro-tasks. These micro-tasks are subsequently distributed to a pool of workers. A crowdsourcer then aggregates the responses from these workers to arrive at a final solution for the original problem. The model accommodates workers with heterogeneous skill levels and allows for the possibility that they provide unreliable responses. The primary objective is to guarantee a specified level of fidelity, or quality, in the final outcome, even when faced with erroneous information from some workers. This necessitates a careful design of the micro-tasks, or queries, assigned by the taskmaster. The total number of queries distributed to the workers is defined as the system's budget.

The work studies the fundamental trade-off between the budget (the number of queries presented by the taskmaster) and the fidelity (the quality of the final inference). To analyze this, the authors establish a novel mapping from the crowdsourcing problem to the information-theoretic framework of joint source-channel coding. In this analogy, the taskmaster is equated to a transmitter, while the crowdsourcer acts as a receiver and decoder. The collective unreliability of the workers is modeled as a communication channel. This formulation allows the derivation of a lower bound on the required budget for a desired fidelity level, drawing upon established results in information theory. Notably, this bound is independent of any specific query design strategy at the taskmaster or processing algorithm at the crowdsourcer.

Following the derivation of the general bound, the authors propose and analyze a concrete query design scheme termed "k-ary incidence coding." They then evaluate the performance of this specific scheme and compare it against the derived information-theoretic lower bound under a particular channel model.

The analogy drawn between the rate-distortion framework and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers is compelling and novel. This perspective has the potential to stimulate further research at the intersection of information theory and crowdsourcing. However, there are concerns regarding the realism of the proposed model. The formulation appears to be tailored to map directly onto the classic point-to-point communication problem, potentially to leverage existing information-theoretic results with minimal modification, which may compromise its applicability to practical crowdsourcing scenarios. It would be beneficial if the authors could address the following points:

1) Worker reliability and skill appear to be conflated into a single channel model. Furthermore, the framework assumes a uniform allocation of queries to all workers, irrespective of their individual capabilities. This contrasts with many real-world systems where worker ratings or past performance are used to guide task assignment.

2) Could the authors elaborate on the realism of the chosen channel models for representing worker unreliability? For instance, the current model does not seem to account for adversarial workers who might possess knowledge of the task and intentionally submit malicious responses to degrade system performance.

The discussion of the numerical results could be clarified. Specifically, the labeling of the curves in Figure 2 is difficult to interpret and could be improved for better readability. What is the physical interpretation of the $k=1$ case? Does it imply that a worker is directly asked for the correct label of a single item, as forming a pair for comparison seems impossible?

The assumption regarding the knowledge of the prior distribution $P(B(X))$, mentioned on page 6, line 246, is a critical detail that should be introduced much earlier in the manuscript.

There appear to be incorrect figure references on page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.a" should likely be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".

Finally, the terms 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' are frequently capitalized when not referencing a specific numbered result. Using lowercase ('theorem', 'lemma', 'corollary') would be more appropriate in these instances.
