- Paper frames crowdsourcing budget–fidelity trade-off in a rate–distortion / joint source–channel coding framework and derives fundamental limits for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a practical query scheme leveraging multi-item comparisons for error correction.  
- Technical derivations are well-structured and mathematically sound, correctly applying rate–distortion and channel coding theory.  
- Main strength: provides a rigorous, unifying information-theoretic foundation for analyzing crowdsourcing limits.  
- Novelty and practical significance are limited: derivations follow established theory and bounds may be loose for real crowdsourcing constraints.  
- Critical practical constraint ignored: the same worker cannot receive identical queries repeatedly (iid assumption violation), which severely limits feasible encoding mappings.  
- Claim that “almost all mappings” are valid is misleading given real-world restrictions on query assignment and worker reuse.  
- Comparing crowdsourcing to standard channel coding is misleading because channel coding allows repeated inputs while crowdsourcing does not, making channel-capacity-based bounds overly optimistic.  
- Paper lacks empirical comparison with state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods to show whether theoretical bounds are meaningful in practice.  
- Recommendation: include experimental comparisons and/or revise bounds to account for crowdsourcing-specific constraints and worker assignment limitations.  
- Minor technical fixes needed: minimization in Eq. (8) should constrain E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t; clarify Theorem 4 proof and parameter relationships.  
- Clarify interpretation of ln(1−q) since it is negative and affects error-term handling.  
- Explain or cite the term H_M(E(ε)) in Eq. (12) more clearly.  
- Overall: theoretically interesting and rigorous, but limited practical value unless constraints are addressed and empirical validation is provided.
