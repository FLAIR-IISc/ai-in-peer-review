- Paper provides an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per task to achieve a target error probability.
- Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) for skilled workers and the spammer-hammer channel SHC(q) for spammers.
- Analysis assumes nonadaptive coding and studies two taskmaster scenarios: full knowledge of worker skills and no prior knowledge (equal task assignment).
- The crowdsourcer (aggregator) is assumed to have no worker skill information in both scenarios.
- Derives a necessary condition for minimum query rate under SHC(q) using k-ary incidence coding where workers compare k items.
- Novelty: likely the first information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing, providing a foundational framework for query efficiency.
- Practical value: theoretical results help practitioners compute minimal query rates for given error tolerances, informing pricing and system design.
- Writing and structure are clear with logical flow from formulation to results.
- Insightful modeling: SHC(q) effectively captures realistic spammer behavior; distinguishing taskmaster vs crowdsourcer knowledge clarifies problem complexity.
- Major limitation: no empirical validation—no simulations or experiments (e.g., Mechanical Turk) to test how practical schemes compare to the derived bounds.
- Presentation issues: Sections 2.4 (channel capacity) and 3.1 (nonadaptive case) are overly long or redundant and should be condensed.
- Recommend adding proof sketches for main theorems (1–3) in the main text to convey intuition; full proofs in the appendix are insufficient for reader understanding.
- Without experiments, readers cannot assess tightness or practicality of the bounds; empirical benchmarking is recommended.
- Overall recommendation: theoretically strong and novel work, but accept with major revisions to add simulations, streamline sections, and include proof sketches.
