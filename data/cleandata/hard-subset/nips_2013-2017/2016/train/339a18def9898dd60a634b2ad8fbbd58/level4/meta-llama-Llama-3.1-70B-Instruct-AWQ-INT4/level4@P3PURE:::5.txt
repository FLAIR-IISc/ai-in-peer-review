The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on the required number of queries per task to achieve a given probability of error. The workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, specifically symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMCs), to capture errors made by workers, and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to account for the scenario where a worker is a spammer with probability 1-q. The coding scheme is assumed to be non-adaptive. The authors consider two cases for analysis: one in which the crowdsourcer, responsible for deciding the value of each task after collecting results from workers, is aware of the skill level of all workers, and another in which the crowdsourcer has no knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster, who distributes tasks to workers, is assumed to be unaware of worker skills, resulting in each worker being assigned the same number of tasks. The authors also provide a necessary condition on the minimum required rate – the number of queries per task – for k-ary incidence coding, where workers are shown k items and asked if they belong to the same or different breeds, using an SHC(q) worker pool.

The paper is well-organized and well-written. However, it lacks experimental or simulation results to demonstrate the gap between current schemes and the derived limits, such as those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be shortened or even removed, as they do not contribute significantly to the overall understanding of the paper. In contrast, providing proof sketches for the theorems would be more valuable. The paper appears to be novel, as it presents the first analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective, as claimed by the authors and, to the best of my knowledge, not explored before. The results seem highly useful, as they enable designers of crowdsourcing projects to determine the minimum rate required to accomplish a task with a given probability of error, which can aid in pricing such projects. I did not review the appendix and, therefore, cannot comment on the proofs.
