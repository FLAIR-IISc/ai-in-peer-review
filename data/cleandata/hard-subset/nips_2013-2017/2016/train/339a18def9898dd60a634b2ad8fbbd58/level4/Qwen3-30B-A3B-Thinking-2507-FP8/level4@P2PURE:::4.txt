

The paper examines a crowdsourcing framework in which a taskmaster decomposes a large-scale task (e.g., an inference problem) into smaller subtasks assigned to workers. After workers complete their assigned tasks, the crowdsourcer aggregates their responses and processes them to derive the final inference result. This setup accommodates workers with heterogeneous skill levels and unreliability in reporting information. The system aims to guarantee a specified fidelity level for the final outcome at the crowdsourcer despite erroneous inputs from workers. This necessitates careful design of subtasks (queries) by the taskmaster, with the total number of queries constituting the system's budget. The authors investigate the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of inference). They establish this trade-off by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the joint source-channel coding problem in information theory, where the taskmaster acts as the transmitter and the crowdsourcer as the receiver/decoder. The unreliable worker-to-crowdsourcer communication channels correspond to the channel in this model. Consequently, an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget for a given fidelity level follows directly from standard results, independent of the task design scheme or crowdsourcer processing algorithm. The authors then analyze a specific task design scheme—k-ary incidence coding—and compare its performance against the derived lower bound under a specific channel model. The reviewer finds the parallel between rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers conceptually interesting and notes potential for inspiring further research using information-theoretic tools in crowdsourcing. However, the reviewer questions whether the results adequately model real-world crowdsourcing systems. The primary concern is that the paper forces an exact mapping to a point-to-point communication problem to leverage existing information theory results with minimal adaptation, potentially sacrificing realism. The authors should address the following issues:  
1) The model conflates worker reliability and skill into a single channel parameter, assuming uniform query allocation to all workers regardless of skill. In practice, workers often possess skill ratings that should inform query allocation.  
2) The channel models for worker unreliability lack realism. The paper does not account for adversarial workers who may intentionally degrade fidelity by leveraging task knowledge.  
Additionally, the explanation of numerical results is unclear. Figure~2's curve labels require improvement: Specifically, the interpretation of the $k=1$ case must be clarified—does this correspond to workers directly reporting the correct label for a presented query (since no pairwise comparison is possible)? On page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ should have been stated earlier in the paper. Page 4, line 135 contains two figure reference errors: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c". The manuscript repeatedly uses "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" without accompanying numbering; these terms should be replaced with "theorem," "lemma," and "corollary" in such cases.
