- Models crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion problem, deriving fundamental limits on queries per item for a target error probability.  
- Represents workers as parallel noisy channels (M-ary symmetric and spammer-hammer) and analyzes known-skill (SL-CS) and unknown-skill (SL-UK) scenarios.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): show k items to workers and ask which pairs share the same label.  
- Derives information-theoretic bounds on the minimum query rate required for a given fidelity.  
- Novelty: first information-theoretic framework applied to crowdsourcing in the NIPS literature, filling a gap left by algorithmic/empirical prior work.  
- Technical rigor: proofs (Theorems 2 and 3) and derivation of the distortion-rate function are sound and well-motivated.  
- Practical significance: results give designers theoretical minimum query budgets and inform pricing and system design; kIC shows coding can improve efficiency.  
- Clear contextualization against prior methods (Dawid–Skene, majority voting, bandits) that lack fundamental limits.  
- Clarity issue: background (Section 3.1) and parts of Section 2.4 are overly detailed and should be tightened.  
- Experimental validation lacking: no simulations or empirical comparisons (e.g., on MTurk) to show gap between practice and bounds.  
- Practical implementation discussion limited: needs more on worker-skill estimation, adaptive querying, and translating theory to real systems (beyond Appendix E).  
- Overall assessment: high-quality theoretical contribution that merits acceptance with minor revisions to address clarity and empirical/practical discussion.
