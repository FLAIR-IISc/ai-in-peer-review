- Presents a novel information-theoretic framework giving lower bounds on queries per task to achieve a target error probability.  
- Models workers as parallel independent channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMC) and spammer-hammer channel SHC(q).  
- Assumes non-adaptive coding and that the taskmaster assigns equal numbers of tasks because they do not know workers' skills.  
- Analyzes two crowdsourcer knowledge scenarios: full knowledge of worker skills and no knowledge.  
- Paper is well-organized and well-written.  
- Lacks experimental or simulation results comparing current schemes (e.g., AMT) to the theoretical limits.  
- Recommends showing how far existing practical schemes are from the derived limits.  
- Suggests condensing or omitting Sections 2.4 and 3.1 as they add little value.  
- Recommends including proof sketches for the theorems instead of lengthy background sections.  
- Novelty: first information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing, valuable for project designers.  
- Practical usefulness: helps determine minimum labeling rate for a desired error and assists in pricing crowdsourcing tasks.  
- Reviewer did not read the appendix and cannot comment on the formal proofs.
