

The paper addresses the problem of labeling tasks in crowdsourcing by modeling workers as noisy communication channels. Specifically, it considers two channel models: a symmetric memoryless channel without feedback and a spammer/hammer channel model. This framing transforms the crowdsourcing problem into a channel coding problem, where the authors analyze the tradeoff between labeling distortion and budget constraints by mapping the budget to the rate of the underlying code. The work introduces a basic coding scheme, referred to as k-incidence coding, which requires tasks to be labeled in subsets of size k. This approach provides a structured method for aggregating worker responses while accounting for worker reliability.

The technical contribution is well-motivated, as the channel-based perspective offers a novel lens for understanding crowdsourcing dynamics. The analysis of distortion-budgget tradeoffs is theoretically sound, and the k-incidence coding scheme presents a practical, interpretable solution. The paper clearly articulates the connection between coding theory and crowdsourcing, demonstrating how channel capacity concepts can inform task design under resource limitations. The experimental validation (where provided) supports the theoretical claims, though the scope of empirical evaluation could be expanded to include real-world crowdsourcing datasets.

While the core ideas are compelling, the paper would benefit from greater clarity in several areas. First, the transition from the channel model to the crowdsourcing application requires more explicit justification. For instance, the paper should clarify how the specific channel parameters (e.g., error probabilities in the spammer model) map to real-world worker behaviors. Second, the discussion of k-incidence coding should elaborate on how it compares to existing aggregation methods (e.g., majority voting or Dawid-Skene) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and practical efficiency. Third, the budget-rate mapping needs precise definition—does "rate" refer to the code rate in bits per symbol, and how does it directly constrain the number of tasks or worker assignments? Without this, the tradeoff analysis remains abstract.

The paper’s structure could also be improved. The motivation section briefly introduces the problem but does not sufficiently highlight the gap it fills relative to prior work (e.g., how this differs from Bayesian or machine learning-based approaches to crowdsourcing). A brief comparison to related work in Section 2 would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, the formal definitions (e.g., of distortion, budget) should be rigorously stated early to avoid ambiguity during the analysis.

While the topic lies outside my primary expertise, I can assess the paper’s technical coherence and theoretical contributions. The channel model is appropriately applied, and the k-incidence coding scheme is a reasonable first step toward optimizing task design. However, the lack of concrete implementation details for the coding scheme and limited empirical validation on real crowdsourcing data weaken the practical impact. The authors should address whether the proposed method scales to large-scale problems or complex task structures (e.g., multi-label tasks or hierarchical dependencies), which are common in real-world crowdsourcing.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework that bridges coding theory and crowdsourcing. Its strengths lie in the novel problem formulation and the clear derivation of distortion-budget tradeoffs. To enhance its contribution, the authors should: (1) strengthen the connection between channel parameters and worker behavior; (2) provide a more detailed comparison with existing aggregation techniques; (3) clarify the budget-rate mapping; and (4) include empirical results from realistic settings. With these refinements, the work would make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, it is a solid theoretical advance with room for deeper practical validation.
