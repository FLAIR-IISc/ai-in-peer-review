The authors present an information-theoretic formulation of crowdsourcing and derive fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in the asymptotic regime. More specifically, they cast crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The channel state is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. In their model, item labels form an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster acts as the encoder; crowd workers correspond to multiple uses of a channel; workers’ skill levels are the random channel states; and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. Using known results on JSCC coding rates, they characterize the asymptotic query complexity. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model. Overall, the paper is well written and appears to be a first step toward a JSCC-based treatment of crowdsourcing.

Technical comments:
1. The problem is modeled as JSCC with random channel state, and Theorems 2 and 3 follow by invoking the standard source–channel separation theorem. However, the authors should first justify that separation still holds when the channel has a random state that is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. I believe separation continues to hold in these cases, but the paper should include a proof or a citation to an existing one.

2. The taskmaster is modeled as an encoder that observes only the item labels, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder that observes only the workers’ outputs. This seems overly restrictive because in many settings both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer have access to the original items. Such access constitutes side information in the JSCC framework. While side information only at the encoder may be of limited value, side information at both encoder and decoder—or even at the decoder alone—can be very helpful. Incorporating side information would make the model more realistic and convincing.

3. In Section 4.2, when discussing Figure 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q increase. I interpret this as the lower bound becoming smaller with larger k and q, which suggests the code’s performance may improve. However, a smaller lower bound does not mean the bound itself has improved in a meaningful sense, since there is no accompanying upper bound for comparison.

Edited following authors’ response: The use of JSCC to model crowdsourcing is interesting, but—as another reviewer noted—a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be more useful. The authors should consider recent finite-blocklength converses for JSCC, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
