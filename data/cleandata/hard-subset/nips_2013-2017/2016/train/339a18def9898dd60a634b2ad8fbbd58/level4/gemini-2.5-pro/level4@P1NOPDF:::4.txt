This paper considers a crowdsourcing setup where a "taskmaster" divides a large inference task into smaller micro-tasks, which are then distributed to a crowd of workers. A "crowdsourcer" collects the responses from these workers and processes them to solve the original task. The model accounts for workers having varying skill levels and being potentially unreliable in their responses. The system's objective is to ensure a certain level of fidelity in the final outcome, even when some workers provide incorrect information. To achieve this, the taskmaster must carefully design the micro-tasks, or "queries," assigned to the workers. The paper defines the total number of queries as the system's "budget" and studies the fundamental trade-off between this budget and the fidelity of the final inference.

The core contribution is to analyze this trade-off by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source-channel coding from information theory. In this mapping, the taskmaster is framed as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the collective unreliability of the workers as the communication channel. Given this framework, a lower bound on the budget required to achieve a desired fidelity level is derived from standard results in information theory. This bound is general, holding irrespective of the specific query design scheme used by the taskmaster or the aggregation algorithm used by the crowdsourcer. The authors then propose and analyze a specific query design scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," and compare its performance to the information-theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model.

I find the parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers to be quite interesting. This work could inspire further research into applying information-theoretic tools to the study of crowdsourcing systems.

However, I am not convinced that the model presented in the paper is a realistic representation of real-world crowdsourcing systems. It seems that the model may have been oversimplified to map cleanly onto the classic point-to-point communication problem, allowing for the direct application of existing information theory results with minimal modification. I would appreciate it if the authors could comment on the following issues:

1) The model appears to conflate worker reliability and skill level into a single channel parameter. Furthermore, it assumes the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their individual skill. In many real-world systems, workers have reputation scores or ratings that reflect their skill level, and tasks can be allocated adaptively based on this information. How does the proposed framework account for, or how could it be extended to handle, heterogeneous and known worker skill levels?

2) How realistic is the chosen channel model for worker unreliability? The current model seems to assume random, independent errors. What about more complex scenarios, such as adversarial workers who have some knowledge of the task and intentionally try to degrade the system's performance?

Additionally, there are several points that could be clarified or corrected:

*   The explanation of the numerical results is somewhat unclear. In particular, the labeling of the curves in Figure 2 could be improved for better readability. For instance, what does the k=1 case correspond to? If k=1, a worker cannot be presented with a pair of items to compare. Does this mean the worker is simply asked to provide the label for a single item directly?

*   On page 6, line 246, the authors state the assumption that P(B(X)) is known. I believe this is a significant assumption that should be stated much earlier, perhaps in the problem formulation section.

*   There appear to be typos in the figure references on page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.a" should likely be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".

*   The authors frequently use capitalized terms like 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' in the text when not referring to a specific numbered result (e.g., "Theorem 1"). Standard convention would be to use the lowercase versions ('theorem', 'lemma', 'corollary') in these instances.
