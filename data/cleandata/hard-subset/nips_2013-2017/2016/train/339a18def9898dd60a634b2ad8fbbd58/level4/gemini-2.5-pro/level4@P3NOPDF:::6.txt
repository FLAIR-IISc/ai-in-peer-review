This paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing by applying principles from rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The authors model the crowdsourcing process as a communication problem, providing a principled way to analyze its fundamental limits. The technical derivation of the bound is well-executed and clear. If this represents the first application of these information-theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing domain, it is a potentially significant contribution that offers a new and valuable perspective.

However, the practical relevance and novelty of the work are questionable due to two main concerns.

The primary issue is that the analogy between channel coding and crowdsourcing may be fundamentally flawed, which could render the derived bound too loose to be of practical use. The channel coding theorem relies on the existence of a vast space of possible codebooks (encoding schemes) that can be designed to approach the channel capacity. For instance, in communications, we have immense freedom to design codes like repetition codes, LDPC codes, or convolutional codes to optimize performance.

In contrast, the "encoding" in crowdsourcing corresponds to the task assignment strategy—the mapping from tasks to workers, as represented in Equation (3). This mapping is subject to severe practical constraints not present in classical channel coding. A striking example is the implicit i.i.d. assumption for worker responses (e.g., in Equations (1) and (2)). In communications, transmitting the same signal multiple times through a memoryless channel results in i.i.d. outputs. However, one cannot assign the same task to the same worker multiple times and expect i.i.d. responses; a worker will likely provide the same, highly correlated answer. This invalidates simple "repetition code" strategies and breaks the i.i.d. assumption central to the model.

This is just one example of the fundamental constraints on the set of implementable "codebooks" in crowdsourcing. The set of valid task assignment strategies is likely a very small subset of the mappings theoretically available in channel coding. Because the derivation of the information-theoretic bound implicitly assumes the freedom to choose an optimal "code" from a largely unconstrained set, the resulting bound on performance (i.e., the capacity) may be unachievable in practice. This risk of the bound being very loose significantly diminishes its utility as a benchmark for real-world systems.

A second, related concern is that while the application of the theory is novel, the technical derivation itself is a relatively straightforward application of standard information-theoretic results. The core contribution is the conceptual mapping of crowdsourcing to a channel coding problem. Given the potential weaknesses in this core analogy, the overall contribution is less impactful.

To strengthen the paper and validate the tightness of the bound, it is essential to compare the derived theoretical limit with the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., those based on expectation-maximization or belief propagation) on benchmark datasets. Such a comparison would provide crucial evidence as to whether the bound is a useful theoretical tool or an unachievably loose upper limit. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a dedicated discussion on the practical constraints of the crowdsourcing "channel" and how they differ from classical communication channels.

*   The proof for Theorem 4 could be elaborated with more intermediate steps to enhance clarity for the reader.
