This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, with the goal of establishing lower bounds on the query complexity required to achieve a specified error probability. The authors model workers as a set of parallel, independent channels. Two channel models are considered: Symmetric Discrete Memoryless Channels (DMCs) to account for honest worker errors, and the Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC(q)) model to capture scenarios where a worker acts as a spammer with probability 1-q. The analysis assumes a nonadaptive coding scheme.

The theoretical analysis is conducted under two distinct scenarios concerning the aggregator's knowledge: one where the aggregator (crowdsourcer) is aware of individual worker skill levels, and another where it has no such information. In both settings, the taskmaster, who distributes the work, is assumed to be oblivious to worker skills, leading to a uniform task assignment across the worker pool. A key result is the derivation of a necessary condition on the minimum query rate for k-ary incidence coding under the SHC(q) worker model.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
*   The application of information theory to analyze fundamental limits in crowdsourcing appears to be a novel contribution, as claimed by the authors and to the best of my knowledge.
*   The work has significant practical implications. The derived bounds can provide valuable guidance to crowdsourcing platform designers for determining the minimum redundancy needed to ensure a target level of accuracy, which can also aid in project pricing and resource allocation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   A notable limitation is the absence of experiments or simulations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a comparison of the theoretical bounds against the performance of practical schemes used on platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT), which would help quantify the gap between theory and practice.
*   The manuscript could be improved by shortening Section 2.4 and either condensing or removing Section 3.1. The space gained would be better used to include proof sketches for the main theorems within the body of the paper, which would improve its accessibility and impact.

Please note that this review does not comment on the correctness of the proofs, as the appendix was not examined.
