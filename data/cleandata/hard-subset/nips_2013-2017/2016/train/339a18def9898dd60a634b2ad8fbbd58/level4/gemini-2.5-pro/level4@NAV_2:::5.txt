This paper presents an information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of the trade-off between budget and fidelity in label crowdsourcing. The authors model the problem as a communication system where the dataset to be labeled is the source, the crowd workers are a set of parallel noisy channels, and the query and inference mechanisms constitute a joint source-channel coding scheme. The main contribution is the derivation of lower bounds on the required number of queries per item (the budget) to achieve a given error probability (the fidelity). The analysis considers two channel models for workers: the M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) to represent general worker errors and the Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) to model a mix of perfect and random-behaving workers. The paper derives fundamental limits for two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown to the aggregator, and when they are perfectly known. Finally, the paper introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) under the SHC model, providing a necessary condition on the rate required to achieve a target error rate.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The paper's main strength is its novel approach. Framing the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective to derive fundamental limits is, to my knowledge, a new and insightful contribution to the field. It provides a principled way to reason about the ultimate performance achievable by any crowdsourcing system.
*   **Significance:** The derived bounds are of high theoretical and practical importance. They provide a concrete benchmark against which existing and future crowdsourcing algorithms can be measured. For system designers, these results offer guidance on the minimum budget required to achieve a desired quality level and could inform optimal pricing strategies for different query types.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The analogy to a communication system is well-motivated and effectively used throughout the paper to structure the problem and the proposed analysis.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Quality:** While the theoretical framework is compelling, the paper lacks any experimental validation. The claims would be significantly strengthened by simulations or experiments on real-world datasets (e.g., from platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk). Such experiments could demonstrate the gap between the performance of popular algorithms (like Majority Voting or Dawid-Skene) and the derived fundamental limits, which would provide valuable context and highlight the potential for future algorithmic improvements. While the proofs are deferred to the appendix, their technical soundness is assumed for this review.
*   **Clarity:** The presentation could be improved by making the paper more self-contained and accessible. The background section on information theory (Section 3.1) is rather extensive and could be shortened, as the target NIPS audience is likely familiar with these concepts. The space gained could be better utilized by including proof sketches or high-level intuitions for the main theorems (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) in the main body of the paper. This would allow readers to grasp the core arguments without having to refer to the full proofs in the appendix. Section 2.4 could also be made more concise.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    1.  Introduces a novel and foundational information-theoretic perspective on the crowdsourcing problem.
    2.  Provides significant results in the form of fundamental bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off.
    3.  The analysis is thorough, covering different worker models and knowledge assumptions.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    1.  The complete lack of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the theoretical bounds.
    2.  The exposition could be improved by including proof sketches in the main text to enhance reader understanding.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that introduces a novel and valuable perspective. The lack of experiments is a notable drawback, but the originality and significance of the theoretical contribution are high.
