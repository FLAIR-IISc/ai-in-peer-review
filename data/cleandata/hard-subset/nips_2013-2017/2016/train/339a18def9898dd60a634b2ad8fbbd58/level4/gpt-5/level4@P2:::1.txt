Summary
This paper models crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic limits on the budget–fidelity trade-off. Workers are modeled as noisy channels, with two concrete models: (i) an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). The “budget” is mapped to the code rate (queries per item), and “fidelity” is quantified as expected distortion (Hamming error). The analysis treats two scenarios: SL-UK, where worker skill levels are unknown to the system, and SL-CS, where the crowdsourcer perfectly knows worker skills at inference time. The paper proves lower bounds on the minimum queries per item needed to achieve a target error, and introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), in which each query groups k items and asks for their within-group cluster relations. For kIC under SHC, the authors derive a lower bound (with an oracle decoder) and discuss an approximate pricing rule comparing different k based on worker effort.

Relation to prior work
The paper contrasts with classical aggregation/inference approaches such as majority voting and the Dawid–Skene EM algorithm, as well as later probabilistic, spectral, and convex-optimization methods for label inference and worker modeling. While prior work optimizes practical algorithms and allocations (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; spectral + EM), this paper focuses on fundamental rate–distortion limits that any querying/inference scheme must obey, thus providing a complementary benchmark.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic rate–distortion framework, yielding clean, interpretable bounds on budget vs. accuracy.
- Distinguishes the impact of worker-skill knowledge at the decoder: SL-UK vs. SL-CS bounds make precise how skill estimation (if perfect) increases effective “capacity.”
- k-ary incidence coding is a natural, practically relevant query design; the observation that it has an inherent error-correction effect (by ruling out invalid configurations) is insightful.
- The SHC-based lower bound with an oracle decoder usefully separates algorithmic suboptimality from intrinsic difficulty, offering a baseline for future methods.
- The pricing heuristic for kIC (cost scaling roughly with k/log k) is a practical takeaway for task design on platforms.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions are strong: workers/channels are memoryless and independent; coding is non-adaptive; blocklength is asymptotically large; and SL-CS assumes perfect skill identification. The practical gap between these settings and real deployments is not quantified.
- The main kIC analysis is limited to binary labeling (N=2) and to the SHC model; it would be valuable to extend to multi-class settings and to more realistic worker models (e.g., per-class confusion matrices).
- Empirical validation is minimal. Simulation studies comparing kIC (with feasible decoders) to DS/spectral/convex baselines on synthetic and real data would strengthen the case for significance.
- The link from “rate” to actual budget is discussed but not fully operationalized. Query heterogeneity (time, cognitive load) and worker fatigue/learning are not modeled, yet they influence practical pricing and allocation.
- Some notation and exposition could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of M vs. N, clearer definitions of HN and HM, and early statement of assumptions about P(B(X))). Minor typos and formatting issues detract from readability.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of information-theoretic derivations and converses. The results are clearly stated, with proofs deferred. The oracle-based lower bound for kIC is appropriately labeled as a necessary condition. However, the reliance on idealized assumptions and the lack of empirical tests limit the strength of the claims about practical performance.

Clarity
Overall well organized and readable for an audience familiar with information theory. Still, the paper would benefit from: (i) a running example that maps abstract quantities (rate, distortion, M, N) to a concrete labeling task; (ii) a clearer discussion of how invalid kIC responses enable error correction; and (iii) a concise summary table aligning assumptions, worker models, and bounds.

Originality
Applying joint source–channel coding and rate–distortion theory to derive fundamental crowdsourcing limits is a fresh angle relative to the predominant ML literature on inference/aggregation. The kIC framing is related to prior incidence/binary-queries work but the error-correction interpretation and bounds are novel.

Significance
The bounds provide a useful benchmark for what any CS system can hope to achieve under the stated assumptions, and may guide the design of query schemes and pricing. Practical impact would increase with empirical demonstrations and broader worker models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add simulations comparing achievable performance of practical decoders for kIC against the lower bounds, under both MSC and SHC, and across N>2.
- Discuss robustness to model misspecification (e.g., non-iid items, correlated workers, adversarial behavior).
- Clarify cost models and provide guidance for translating rate to platform-specific monetary/time budgets.
- Improve notation consistency and fix minor typographical errors; provide an intuitive explanation of Theorems 2–3 (e.g., “capacity of an average worker” vs. “average of capacities”).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and rigorous framework with clear theoretical contributions.
- Provides fundamental limits that can calibrate future empirical work.
- Practical insights into query design via kIC and pricing heuristics.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and narrow modeling choices reduce immediate practical impact.
- Strong idealizations (oracle skill knowledge, asymptotics, non-adaptivity) leave open questions about applicability.
- kIC analysis restricted to binary labels and SHC.

Overall evaluation
A theoretically interesting paper that frames crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens and offers clear budget–fidelity bounds and insights into multi-item query design. I view it as borderline: valuable as a foundational contribution, but it would be strengthened considerably by empirical evidence and broader models.
