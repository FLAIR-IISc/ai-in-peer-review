- Models crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding: taskmaster=encoder, workers=noisy channels, crowdsourcer=decoder; derives asymptotic lower bounds on queries/item for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) plus pricing rule-of-thumb.  
- Complements prior NIPS work (aggregation/inference algorithms) by providing abstract, information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits rather than algorithm-specific analysis.  

Strengths:
- Clean JSCC mapping with explicit assumptions (i.i.d. labels, Hamming distortion, non-adaptive codes).  
- Theorems 2–3 give interpretable lower bounds illustrating gap between decoder knowledge of worker skill.  
- kIC analysis shows structured queries can induce error-correcting effects; SHC-based bound is simple and intuitive.  
- Paper articulates a budget proxy via rate and discusses cost normalization across query types.  

Weaknesses / suggestions:
- Key reliance on source–channel separation with random channel state is assumed but not justified; needs explicit conditions, proof sketch, or citations for DMCs with i.i.d. state.  
- Analysis is asymptotic; lacks finite-blocklength characterization relevant to practical crowdsourcing (cite Kostina–Verdú).  
- Theorem 4 kIC bound depends on an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers, making the bound overly optimistic; need achievability with realistic decoders or non-oracle converses or empirical gap analysis.  
- Does not consider side information at encoder/decoder (items seen by taskmaster/crowdsourcer), which can change rate–distortion and budget implications.  
- Cost/time normalization across k values needs clearer placement in main text and preferably empirical validation (e.g., MTurk pilot).  

Clarity and presentation issues:
- Rephrase claims like “lower bounds improve” to avoid implying achievability without matching upper bounds.  
- Inconsistent notation and undefined terms (N vs M, pmax, HN, HM, U vs V); clarify kIC partition effects and state assumptions upfront.  
- Explicitly state independence assumptions (workers, items, queries) and note kIC analysis is for N=2; discuss generalization.  
- Fix typos, missing reference years, placeholders, and improve figure captions and symbol definitions.  

Originality:
- Novel framing of crowdsourcing via JSCC and rate–distortion limits for this community; kIC bound adds insight into structured query design.  

Significance:
- Provides conceptual outer benchmarks for budget/fidelity and can inform adaptive querying, pricing, and aggregation; impact would grow with finite-blocklength results, constructive schemes, and empirical validation.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Neat JSCC formulation bridging information theory and crowdsourcing.  
- Clear, interpretable lower bounds separating roles of worker-skill knowledge.  
- Insightful structured-query (kIC) analysis and cost discussion.  

Cons against acceptance:
- Unjustified reliance on asymptotic separation with random state.  
- Practical utility limited by oracle assumptions, lack of achievability/finite-blocklength results, and restricted kIC analysis (N=2).  
- Modeling omissions (side information, item difficulty, adaptivity) reduce realism.  

Actionable recommendations:
- Add formal statement and citations for source–channel separation with i.i.d. channel state and conditions for Theorems 2–3.  
- Discuss and, if possible, include finite-blocklength converses/achievability (e.g., Kostina–Verdú) to bridge theory and practice.  
- Provide a constructive scheme approaching the bounds under MSC/SHC or stronger converses that avoid oracle SI; empirically compare practical decoders to the oracle.  
- Expand discussion of encoder/decoder side information and its implications for query/decoding design.  
- Clarify kIC lower bound interpretation, generalize beyond N=2 if possible, and foreground cost normalization (with empirical timing/cost data) in main text.  

Overall:
- A well-written, insightful first step toward an information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourced labeling that would be substantially strengthened by formal separation justification, finite-blocklength or constructive results, and empirical validation.
