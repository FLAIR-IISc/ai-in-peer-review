- Models crowdsourcing labeling as an information-theoretic coding problem, representing workers as noisy channels.  
- Considers two worker channel types: symmetric memoryless channel (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel (SHC).  
- Maps budget to coding rate and studies distortion versus budget trade-offs.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where tasks label subsets of k items.  
- Provides comprehensive literature context and links to real-world platforms (AMT, Yelp).  
- Addresses crowdsourcing challenges: unknown skills, spammers, and lack of ground truth.  
- Technical analysis is sound with clear model assumptions and derivations.  
- Derives fundamental limits (Theorems 2 and 3) for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill cases.  
- Analyzes kIC performance under a spammer-hammer worker pool and gives a lower bound.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written for an information-theory audience.  
- Strength: novel information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing as a coding problem.  
- Strength: introduces and analyzes a new coding scheme (kIC) with numerical discussion.  
- Weakness: limited scope focused mainly on labeling tasks.  
- Weakness: simplified worker behavior model may not capture real-world complexities.  
- Suggested improvements: extend to more complex tasks (clustering, regression) and richer worker models (fatigue, bias, skill variability).  
- Suggested improvements: include more detailed numerical results and comparisons with existing crowdsourcing algorithms.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, theoretical bounds, new coding scheme, practical implications for budget/query design.  
- Rejection arguments: narrow scope, oversimplified worker assumptions, limited empirical comparisons.
