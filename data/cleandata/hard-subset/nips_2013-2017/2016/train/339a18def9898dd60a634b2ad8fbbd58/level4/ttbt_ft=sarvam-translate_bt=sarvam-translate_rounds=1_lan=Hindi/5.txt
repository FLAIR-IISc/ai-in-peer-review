This research paper utilizes digital crowdsourcing within an information theoretical framework to provide a lower bound on the number of queries required per task to achieve a given probability of error. The workers are modeled as parallel independent channels. The channels considered are the symmetric discrete memoryless channel (MSC), which models errors made by workers, and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q), which models the situation when a worker is a spammer with probability 1-q. The code is considered non-adaptive. Two cases are considered for analysis: first, the case when the crowdsourcer, who determines the value of each task after collecting results from the workers, knows the skill level of all the workers, and second, the case when he knows nothing about them. In both cases, the taskmaster, who distributes the tasks to the workers, is assumed to know nothing about the workers' skills, so each worker is assigned the same number of tasks. Finally, the minimum required rate – the number of queries per task – is a necessary condition on k-ary incidence coding, where the worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to the same or different races, SHC(q) worker pool was provided. Well-organized and well-written research paper. There was no experiment or simulation to show how far the currently used schemes are from the limits, for example in AMT. I think section 2.4 could be shortened and 3.1 is not necessary or can be shortened. Instead, proof diagrams of theorems are more important. It seems new, to my knowledge and as claimed by the paper, there is no analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information theoretical point of view. It seems very useful where designers of such crowdsourcing projects can know the minimum rate required to complete a task with a given probability of error and this can help in pricing such projects. I did not read the appendix so I cannot claim anything about the proof.
