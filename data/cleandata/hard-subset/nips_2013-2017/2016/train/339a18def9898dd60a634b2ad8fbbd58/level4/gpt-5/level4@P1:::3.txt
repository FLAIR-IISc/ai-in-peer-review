Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental budget–fidelity limits via rate–distortion theory. The source is the latent label function B(X) to be inferred; workers are modeled as noisy channels (either M-ary symmetric channels or a spammer–hammer mixture); and the query scheme plus inference act as encoder/decoder. The paper derives lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) needed to achieve a target error (distortion), in two scenarios: (i) worker skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and (ii) perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). It also analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query shows k items and asks workers to group by label, highlighting its implicit error-correcting constraints and proposing a simple pricing heuristic.

The work connects to a large aggregation literature in crowdsourcing, including majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM and its variants, spectral-EM approaches, and graph-based/convex formulations for clustering with pairwise queries. It complements these by providing information-theoretic limits that any aggregation scheme must respect. I also recommend discussing the parallel and increasingly intersecting literature on incentive-compatible “crowdsourcing mechanisms” (mechanism design for crowdsourcing/peer prediction), which addresses worker heterogeneity and strategic behavior and could interact with your bounds and kIC design.

Strengths
- Originality: Casting crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and deriving rate–distortion-style limits is fresh and potentially impactful for NIPS. The split between SL-UK and SL-CS provides clean insight: with unknown skills, the system behaves like an “average worker channel,” whereas with known skills it benefits from the average of worker capacities.
- Significance: Fundamental lower bounds can serve as benchmarks for any aggregation/inference scheme, guide query design, and inform pricing. The kIC framework is a natural generalization of pairwise/ternary queries used in practice and its built-in validity constraints are a compelling source of error correction.
- Quality (technical): The results appear carefully derived, and the information-theoretic framing is sound. The oracle lower bound for kIC with a spammer–hammer pool is clear about its role as a necessary condition.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity and accessibility: Despite the efforts to make the paper accessible, it remains difficult to follow for readers not steeped in information theory. Many results are stated in terms of channels/codes/distortion without being restated in crowdsourcing terms (e.g., “queries per item needed to get error ≤ ε for binary labeling with spammer fraction 1−q”). I recommend:
  - Restating each main theorem immediately in crowdsourcing language (data assumptions, worker model, target error, resulting rate).
  - Summarizing the key intuition at the end of Section 3 more prominently, and adding a brief conclusion with concrete takeaways (e.g., how much budget reduction is possible when moving from SL-UK to SL-CS for typical worker pools).
- Practical interpretability: Figure 2 is hard to interpret without a detailed caption explaining axes, regimes (achievable vs impossible), and how k, q, and M influence the bound. Consider adding a table or examples translating the bounds to “queries per item” under typical parameters and a clear cost model.
- Cost normalization: Since kIC queries vary in worker effort, please make explicit the cost model and how rate compares across different k. The pricing rule-of-thumb π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ … needs to be stated unambiguously and justified.
- Assumptions and achievability: Theorem 4 assumes an oracle decoder that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers; this is a necessary condition. It would help to discuss achievable schemes that approach the bound (even qualitatively) or to bound the gap for simple decoders. Also clarify non-adaptive vs adaptive querying and independence assumptions; briefly comment on how feedforward (task difficulty) could change the limits.
- Related work: Please add discussion of robust incentive/mechanism design for crowdsourcing/peer prediction and its interaction with worker heterogeneity and aggregation.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance (NIPS criteria)
- Quality: Technically solid and careful, but parts are hard to verify due to presentation. More explicit restatements and examples would increase confidence.
- Clarity: Organization is reasonable, but the heavy use of information-theoretic terminology without parallel crowdsourcing restatements makes it hard to digest. Improve figures, captions, and a concluding section with actionable insights.
- Originality: High. The rate–distortion perspective and kIC error-correction analysis are novel within the NIPS crowdsourcing literature.
- Significance: Potentially high as a benchmark-setting paper, contingent on clearer guidance for practitioners.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel theoretical framing with clean lower bounds.
- Insightful distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS regimes.
- Natural, practical query family (kIC) with an error-correction angle and pricing implications.

Cons for acceptance
- Accessibility: difficult for non-information-theorists to extract actionable insights.
- Limited empirical/achievability discussion; oracle-based bounds may feel distant from practice.
- Ambiguity around cost normalization and pricing.

Specific comments and minor edits
- Please clarify the roles of “taskmaster” vs “crowdsourcer.” The distinction is confusing in places (e.g., line 106) and could be tightened with consistent terminology and a brief glossary early on.
- Line 84: “are reviewed in” (plural).
- Line 122: Typo “A A(X1,X2)”.
- Lines 275–276: The sentence beginning “In case...” is hard to parse; please rewrite.
- Figure 2: Add a detailed caption explaining axes, parameter settings (k, q, M), and how to read achievable vs impossible regions; consider annotated examples.
- Throughout: After each theorem, add a one-line, units-explicit restatement (e.g., “To achieve error ≤ ε, you need at least R queries per item”).

Overall assessment
Interesting and potentially useful theoretical contribution with strong originality, but currently hard to follow. With clearer restatements in crowdsourcing terms, improved figures, and a short conclusion highlighting concrete takeaways, this paper could have substantial impact as a benchmark for aggregation and query design.
