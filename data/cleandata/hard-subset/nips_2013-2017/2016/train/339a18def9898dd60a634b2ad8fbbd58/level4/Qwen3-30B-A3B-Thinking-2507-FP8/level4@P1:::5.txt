

This paper models digital crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem to establish fundamental limits on the query budget (number of queries per item) required to achieve a target fidelity (probability of error) in crowdsourced labeling tasks. The authors model workers as parallel noisy channels (specifically, M-ary symmetric channels and spammer-hammer channels) and analyze two key scenarios: (1) when the crowdsourcer (who aggregates worker responses) knows worker skill levels (SL-CS), and (2) when the crowdsourcer does not know worker skill levels (SL-UK). The authors introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where workers are shown k items and asked to identify which pairs belong to the same category, and derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum query rate required for a given error probability.

The paper makes an important contribution by approaching crowdsourcing from an information-theoretic perspective, which is novel in the NIPS literature. Prior work on crowdsourcing (e.g., Dawid-Skene algorithm, majority voting, bandit-based approaches) has focused on algorithmic design and empirical evaluation without establishing fundamental limits. The authors properly contextualize their work against these prior approaches in Section 1, noting that existing methods do not provide theoretical guarantees on the minimum query budget required for a target fidelity.

Strengths:
1. Originality: The information-theoretic framework is novel for crowdsourcing, providing fundamental performance bounds that no existing algorithm can exceed. The paper correctly identifies this as a significant contribution, as no prior work has established such theoretical limits for crowdsourcing systems.

2. Quality: The theoretical development is technically sound, with rigorous proofs for the key theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) in the appendix. The derivation of the distortion-rate function for crowdsourcing is well-motivated and consistent with information theory fundamentals.

3. Significance: The results have clear practical implications for crowdsourcing system designers, providing a theoretical foundation for determining minimum query budgets and informing pricing strategies. The kIC analysis demonstrates how coding techniques can improve crowdsourcing efficiency, which could guide future system design.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity: The paper would benefit from a more concise presentation of background material. Section 3.1 (Background on joint source-channel coding) is unnecessarily detailed and could be shortened to focus on the aspects directly relevant to the crowdsourcing problem. The description in Section 2.4 could also be tightened.

2. Experimental Validation: The paper lacks empirical results or simulations to demonstrate how current crowdsourcing approaches (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) compare to the theoretical bounds. While the paper is fundamentally theoretical, including a brief simulation showing the gap between current practice and the information-theoretic limits would strengthen the practical relevance.

3. Practical Implementation Discussion: The paper discusses query pricing (Appendix E) but could better elaborate on how the theoretical results translate to real-world implementation considerations, particularly regarding worker skill estimation and adaptive query schemes.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written overall. The main contributions are well-articulated, and the technical content is sound. The authors have appropriately addressed related work in the introduction, distinguishing their information-theoretic approach from existing algorithmic work.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, providing fundamental performance bounds
- Rigorous theoretical development with well-motivated theorems
- Clear practical implications for crowdsourcing system design and pricing
- Proper contextualization against existing work in the field

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of empirical validation to demonstrate how current crowdsourcing approaches compare to the theoretical bounds
- Background material (Section 3.1) is overly detailed and could be more focused
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges beyond the theoretical model

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in crowdsourcing research. While the absence of empirical results is a limitation, the paper's theoretical novelty and significance outweigh this weakness. The results provide a valuable foundation for future work on crowdsourcing system design, and the information-theoretic approach is well-suited to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues noted above.
