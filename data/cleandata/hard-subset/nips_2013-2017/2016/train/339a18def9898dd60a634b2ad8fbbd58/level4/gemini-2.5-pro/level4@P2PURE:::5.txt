This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for digital crowdsourcing to establish lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a desired error probability. The authors model workers as parallel, independent channels, using symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) model to account for spammers who are present with probability 1-q. The analysis assumes a non-adaptive coding scheme.

Two primary scenarios are analyzed: one where the crowdsourcer (who aggregates the results) knows the skill levels of all workers, and one where these skills are unknown. In both settings, the taskmaster (who distributes tasks) is assumed to be unaware of worker skills, resulting in each worker being assigned the same number of tasks. Finally, the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum rate (queries per task) for a specific k-ary incidence coding scheme, applied to a worker pool modeled by the SHC(q) channel.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Its primary strength lies in its novelty; to my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this is the first analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from a fundamental, information-theoretic perspective. This approach has significant practical implications, as the derived bounds can help designers of crowdsourcing systems determine the minimum resources needed to achieve a target quality level and can also inform the pricing of such projects.

A notable weakness is the lack of experiments or simulations to contextualize the theoretical bounds. For example, a comparison with the performance of existing schemes on platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk would be valuable to demonstrate how close they are to the derived limits. The presentation could also be improved. Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 seems non-essential and could be condensed or removed. The space saved would be better utilized by including proof sketches for the main theorems within the body of the paper. Please note that I have not reviewed the proofs in the appendix and therefore cannot comment on their correctness.
