This paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework and derives lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a target error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. Two channel types are considered: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture worker errors, and the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), where a worker is a spammer with probability 1−q. The coding/assignment scheme is assumed to be nonadaptive. 

The analysis treats two regimes for the crowdsourcer (the decision maker who aggregates worker responses): one in which the crowdsourcer knows the skill level of every worker, and one in which it has no knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster (who distributes tasks) is assumed to be ignorant of worker skills and therefore assigns the same number of tasks to each worker. The paper also provides a necessary condition on the minimum required rate—i.e., the number of queries per task—for k-ary incidence coding, in which a worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to the same or different categories, under an SHC(q) worker pool.

Overall, the paper is well organized and clearly written. The topic appears novel: to the best of my knowledge (and as claimed by the authors), there has not been a prior information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing that establishes such fundamental limits. The results are potentially very useful: they can guide the design of crowdsourcing systems by specifying the minimum query rate needed to achieve a desired error probability, and they could inform pricing and budgeting decisions.

However, the paper lacks empirical or simulation results to illustrate how close commonly used schemes (e.g., on AMT) are to the derived limits. Such comparisons would substantially increase the paper’s practical impact and help readers assess the tightness and relevance of the bounds.

Regarding presentation, Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 seems unnecessary or could be condensed. In contrast, brief proof sketches for the main theorems would be more valuable to build intuition and help readers understand the key ideas behind the bounds without relying solely on the appendix. 

I did not read the appendix, so I cannot comment on the correctness or completeness of the proofs.
