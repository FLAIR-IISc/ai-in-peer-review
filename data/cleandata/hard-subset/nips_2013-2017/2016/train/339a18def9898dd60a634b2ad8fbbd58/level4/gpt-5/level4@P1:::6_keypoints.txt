- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem, modeling workers as noisy channels (MSC and SHC) and deriving budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion.  
- Main contributions: information-theoretic lower bounds for unknown and known worker skill scenarios (Theorems 2–3), analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) with a spammer–hammer bound (Theorem 4), and specialization to the SHC model (Corollary 1).  
- Positions results as fundamental benchmarks rather than novel inference algorithms, complementing prior work like majority voting and Dawid–Skene.  
- Strength: clear, principled formulation with appropriate use of Shannon’s separation theorem and interpretable bounds.  
- Strength: MSC and SHC models are standard and the bounds’ dependence on key quantities (e.g., H(B(X)), HM(.), q) is transparent.  
- Strength: kIC analysis highlights how query design can induce error-correction structure and provides an oracle-decoder necessary condition.  
- Potential baseline value for evaluating practical algorithms and query schemes.  
- Concern: novelty is moderate since derivations largely apply standard information-theoretic results to a new domain.  
- Major concern: bounds may be loose because practical encoder/decoder classes in crowdsourcing are far more constrained than in classical communication.  
- Concern about implementability: Eq. (3) allows arbitrary codebooks, but many mappings C may be infeasible under crowdsourcing task semantics, budgets, and platform limits.  
- Reuse/independence issue: re-asking identical queries to the same worker violates iid assumptions and platform rules, undermining analogies to repetition codes.  
- Limited design freedom for u^n: unclear how to systematically design query assignments to realize strong error-correcting structures beyond simple redundancy and kIC.  
- Empirical gap: no comparisons to practical methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM) or simulations to gauge bound tightness or kIC performance.  
- Quality: derivations are technically sound under assumptions, but Theorem 4’s proof is terse and needs more detail (e.g., ln(1−q) sign, dependence on ε̄S).  
- Clarity: generally well written but should explicitly state implementability constraints on C/C′ and clarify independence when items appear in multiple queries.  
- Originality: moderate—interesting mapping and kIC connection, but main theoretical content is straightforward application of known theorems.  
- Significance: potentially useful as a benchmark, but practical impact depends on demonstrating bounds are not too loose under real constraints.  
- Pros for acceptance: clean information-theoretic framework, interpretable bounds, concrete kIC analysis, and potential to stimulate further work.  
- Cons against acceptance: risk of loose bounds from strong assumptions, lack of empirical validation, and unclear/underexplained proofs (Theorem 4, Eq. (17)).  
- Key suggestions: specify feasible encoder class and practical constraints, provide empirical comparisons and kIC simulations, elaborate Theorem 4 proof, justify appearance of HM(E(ε)) in Eq. (17), and discuss finite-n and platform impacts on separation theorem applicability.
