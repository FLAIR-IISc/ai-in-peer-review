- Maps crowdsourcing to joint source–channel coding and derives a budget–fidelity lower bound independent of specific schemes.
- Proposes and analyzes a specific taskmaster scheme: k-ary incidence coding, and compares it to the information-theoretic bound for a chosen channel model.
- Reviewer finds the rate–distortion analogy interesting and potentially inspiring for further research.
- Concern: worker reliability and skill are collapsed into a single channel model, losing distinction between skill ratings and reporting noise.
- Concern: assumption that all workers receive the same number of queries ignores realistic heterogeneity and adaptive task allocation.
- Concern: channel models may not capture realistic worker behaviors, especially adversarial or informed workers who intentionally degrade fidelity.
- Numerical results and Fig. 2 are unclear; curve labels need improvement and the meaning of the k=1 case is ambiguous.
- Modeling assumption P(B(X)) is introduced late (page 6) and should be stated earlier in the paper.
- Incorrect figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b and 1.b → 1.c are wrong).
- Terminology issue: repeated use of capitalized 'Theorem', 'Lemma', 'Corollary' without numbering; lowercase generic terms would be more appropriate.
- Overall quality: technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis, but practical relevance is questionable.
- Clarity: well-written overall but contains minor errors and unclear explanations.
- Originality: novel application of information-theoretic tools but may be a direct application of existing results.
- Significance: potential contribution to crowdsourcing and information theory, but practical impact is doubtful.
- Pros for acceptance: novel framework and potential to spur further study.
- Cons for acceptance: possibly unrealistic modeling, limited practical relevance, and minor presentation errors.
