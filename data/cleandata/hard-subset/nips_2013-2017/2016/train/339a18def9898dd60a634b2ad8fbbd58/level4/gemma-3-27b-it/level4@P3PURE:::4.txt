This paper considers a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster decomposes a large task (e.g., an inference problem) into smaller subtasks for distribution to a crowd of workers. The crowdsourcer then collects and processes the workers’ responses to arrive at a final inference. The system explicitly accounts for varying worker skill levels and potential unreliability in reported information. The core objective is to guarantee a certain level of fidelity, or quality, in the final outcome despite potentially inaccurate worker responses. Achieving this requires careful design of the subtasks (queries) allocated to workers, subject to a budget constraint representing the total number of queries issued. The paper investigates a fundamental trade-off between this budget and the achievable fidelity.

This trade-off is elegantly framed by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source-channel coding in information theory. The taskmaster is modeled as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable communication links from workers to the crowdsourcer as a communication channel. Leveraging established results from information theory, the authors derive a lower bound on the required budget (number of queries) to achieve a desired fidelity level. Importantly, this bound is independent of the specific query design employed by the taskmaster or the information processing algorithm used by the crowdsourcer.  The authors then focus on a specific query design scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance, comparing it to the derived information-theoretic lower bound under a particular channel model.

The reviewer finds the connection drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers to be highly insightful. This work has the potential to encourage further research applying information-theoretic tools to the study of crowdsourcing. However, the reviewer questions the realism of the model in representing practical crowdsourcing systems. This simplification stems from the desire to directly map the crowdsourcing problem to a standard point-to-point communication problem, facilitating the application of existing information-theoretic results with minimal modification. 

The authors should consider commenting on the following points: 1) The paper appears to conflate worker reliability and skill level into a single channel model. The assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their skill level (potentially represented by a rating), seems unrealistic. 2) The realism of the chosen channel models in capturing worker unreliability is also a concern. The models do not appear to account for workers who might possess some knowledge of the task and intentionally degrade fidelity in an adversarial manner. The explanation of the numerical results is somewhat unclear, and the labeling of curves in Fig.~2 could be improved. Specifically, the meaning of the $k=1$ case is unclear – does this represent a scenario where workers directly provide the correct label without requiring a pairwise comparison? On page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ is stated, but this should have been introduced earlier in the paper. Furthermore, on page 4, lines 135 and 136, the figure references are incorrect: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1.b" and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c." Finally, the authors repeatedly use the terms ‘Theorem,’ ‘Lemma,’ and ‘Corollary’ without accompanying numbers; using ‘theorem,’ ‘lemma,’ and ‘corollary’ (lowercase) would be more appropriate in these instances.
