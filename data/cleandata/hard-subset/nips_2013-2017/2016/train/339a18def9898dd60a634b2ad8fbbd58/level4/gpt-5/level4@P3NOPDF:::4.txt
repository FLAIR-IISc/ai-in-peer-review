Summary and contributions:
The paper studies a crowdsourcing setting in which a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller queries assigned to workers with heterogeneous skills and reliability. The crowdsourcer aggregates the returned answers to complete the inference. The central contribution is to formalize a budget–fidelity trade-off—where budget is the total number of queries and fidelity is the quality of the final inference—by mapping this setup to a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) framework. In this mapping, the taskmaster is a transmitter, the crowdsourcer is a receiver/decoder, and worker unreliability induces a communication channel. This perspective yields information-theoretic lower bounds on the required budget for a given fidelity level that are scheme-agnostic. The paper then analyzes a particular query design, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compares its performance to the bound for a specific channel model.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths:
  - The JSCC/rate–distortion lens on crowdsourcing is original and intellectually appealing. It clarifies fundamental limits that are independent of algorithmic details and can spur further cross-pollination between information theory and human-in-the-loop systems.
  - The separation between universal limits (lower bounds) and concrete schemes (kIC) is valuable, and the performance comparison is informative.
- Weaknesses:
  - Several modeling assumptions limit realism, notably the merging of worker skill and reliability into a single channel model and uniform query allocation across workers.
  - The treatment of adversarial behavior is not clearly articulated, and it is unclear how well the adopted channel models capture real-world worker behavior.
  - Some presentation issues hinder clarity, particularly in the numerical section and figure labeling.

Soundness:
The information-theoretic derivations appear principled under the stated assumptions, and the independence of the lower bound from specific taskmaster/crowdsourcer schemes is well-motivated. However, the paper’s practical relevance depends on how faithfully the channel models reflect worker behavior and how the framework accommodates heterogeneity and adversarial responses.

Clarity and presentation:
The high-level narrative is clear, but several points need clarification or earlier exposition, and the numerical results could be better explained and labeled.

Major comments and questions:
1) Modeling of worker heterogeneity:
- The paper seems to conflate skill and reliability into a single channel abstraction and assumes identical query budgets for all workers. In practice, platforms maintain worker ratings and adaptively allocate more (or different) queries to more reliable workers.
- Question: Can the framework be extended to multiple channels with known or estimated reliabilities (e.g., a mixture model), or to adaptive budget allocation policies? What would the lower bound look like with heterogeneous “subchannels” and possibly side information at the transmitter (taskmaster)?

2) Realism of channel models:
- Please discuss how well the assumed channels capture common worker behaviors: random guessing, systematic bias, and error correlation across similar items.
- Question: Do your models allow a worker to sometimes abstain, exhibit class-dependent confusion, or show context-dependent reliability? If not, what modifications are most natural within your framework?

3) Adversarial workers:
- The current exposition focuses on unreliable (noisy) workers, but adversarial workers intentionally degrade fidelity.
- Question: How would the lower bounds and kIC analysis change under a fraction of Byzantine workers, or under worst-case adversarial channels subject to rate constraints? Is the current bound robust to such deviations, or does it require explicit adversarial modeling?

4) k-ary incidence coding:
- The explanation of the numerical results is not fully clear. Figure labels and legends should be improved, and axes/curves should be clearly identified.
- Question: What precisely does the k=1 case represent? Incidence queries (e.g., “do these two items share a label?”) are undefined when k=1; does this degenerate to directly asking for a single item’s label, and if so, what is the channel interpretation and how is it evaluated?

5) Assumption on P(B(X)):
- On page 6, line 246, the paper assumes knowledge of P(B(X)). This is a substantial assumption and should be stated earlier, since it underpins the information-theoretic analysis.
- Question: How sensitive are the results to misspecification of P(B(X))? Can the framework accommodate learning P(B(X)) from data, and how would that affect the bounds?

Scope and impact:
The connection to rate–distortion theory is stimulating and can motivate broader use of information-theoretic tools in crowdsourcing. That said, better integration of practical features—worker reputation, adaptive tasking, abstentions, and adversarial robustness—would enhance the applicability and impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Elaborate on how the JSCC framework could incorporate:
  - Heterogeneous worker channels and known/estimated reliabilities (e.g., side information at the encoder).
  - Adaptive or unequal budget allocation strategies and their information-theoretic implications.
  - Adversarial behavior, including worst-case bounds or robustness guarantees.
- Expand the discussion on the channel models’ behavioral plausibility and provide empirical motivation or references.
- Clarify the numerical section:
  - Improve figure legends and axis labels in Fig. 2; ensure every curve is unambiguously identified.
  - Explain the meaning and interpretation of k=1 for kIC or remove it if it is not a valid configuration.
  - Provide more narrative around how close kIC comes to the lower bound and in which regimes the gap is largest/smallest.
- Move the assumption on P(B(X)) to an earlier section and discuss its role in the analysis.

Minor comments and editorial issues:
- Page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b,” and “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c.”
- The paper repeatedly uses “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without specific numbering. Use lowercase (“theorem,” “lemma,” “corollary”) when not referring to a numbered result.
- Ensure consistent notation and define all symbols at first use.
- Proofread for minor grammatical issues and improve figure readability.

Conclusion:
This paper offers a compelling and rigorous bridge between crowdsourcing and information theory, yielding clean lower bounds and a concrete scheme (kIC) for comparison. Its main limitation is the realism of the behavioral models and the omission of heterogeneous and adversarial settings. Addressing the modeling concerns, clarifying the assumptions and numerical results, and tightening the presentation would substantially strengthen the paper and broaden its relevance.
