This paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a given probability of error. The authors model workers as parallel, independent channels. Two types of channels are considered: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker errors, and a spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to model workers who are spammers with probability 1-q. The analysis assumes nonadaptive coding.

The paper analyzes two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer (aggregator) knows the skill level of each worker, and one where the crowdsourcer has no prior knowledge about them. In both cases, the taskmaster, who distributes the tasks, is assumed to be unaware of worker skills, resulting in each worker being assigned the same number of tasks. Finally, the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum query rate for a k-ary incidence coding task under the SHC(q) worker model.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
*   The primary strength of this work is its novel application of information theory to the crowdsourcing problem. To my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this appears to be the first such analysis.
*   The derived theoretical bounds are potentially very useful. They can help designers of crowdsourcing projects determine the minimum rate needed to accomplish a task with a given probability of error and could aid in the pricing of such projects.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   The paper lacks experiments or simulations to show how the derived theoretical bounds compare to the performance of schemes currently used on platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk. Such a comparison would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance.
*   The presentation could be improved by including proof sketches for the main theorems in the body of the paper. This would provide readers with greater insight into the technical arguments. To make space, Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 could be condensed or removed as it does not seem essential.

**Note:** I did not review the appendix, so I cannot comment on the correctness of the proofs.
