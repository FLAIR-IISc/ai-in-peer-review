

The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. The authors establish fundamental asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item under this model. Specifically, item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder observing only labels, crowd workers as multiple channel uses, worker skill levels as unknown channel states (known or unknown at the decoder), and the crowdsourcer as a decoder observing worker outputs. Using existing JSCC results, the authors derive asymptotic query complexity bounds. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability for "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific channel model. Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a promising first step toward JSCC modeling for crowdsourcing.

However, three technical concerns warrant attention:

1.  **Source-Channel Separation Justification:** The authors directly apply the standard source-channel separation theorem (Theorems 2 & 3) to the JSCC problem with unknown encoder-side channel states. Crucially, they do not establish whether the separation theorem holds in this specific scenario. The authors must either prove that separation remains valid when the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the decoder, or cite a rigorous existing proof for this case. Without this justification, the derivation of their asymptotic limits lacks foundation.

2.  **Omission of Side Information:** The model assumes the encoder (taskmaster) and decoder (crowdsourcer) only observe labels, ignoring their potential access to the original items (the "data" being labeled). This side information is a critical practical aspect of crowdsourcing. While side information at the encoder alone may offer limited benefit, side information available to both encoder and decoder, or solely to the decoder, can significantly improve performance in JSCC. The model's restriction to label-only observation appears overly simplistic and less realistic. Incorporating this side information would strengthen the model's applicability and make the results more compelling.

3.  **Misstatement of Lower Bound Behavior:** In Section 4.2, the authors state that "the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow." This is misleading. Lower bounds on error probability *decrease* (become smaller) as k and q increase, indicating a *tighter* bound or potentially *better* achievable performance. The phrase "improve" incorrectly suggests the bound itself gets better (e.g., tighter), but the bound is a lower limit; a smaller bound is less restrictive. The authors should clarify that the *value* of the lower bound decreases with larger k and q, implying the error probability could be smaller.

**Additional Recommendation:** As noted in the authors' response, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be more practically useful than the asymptotic analysis presented. The authors should consider incorporating recent non-asymptotic results for JSCC, such as those by Kostina and Verdu (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, 2013), which provide finite-blocklength converses and bounds relevant to real-world crowdsourcing applications. This would significantly enhance the paper's impact and utility.
