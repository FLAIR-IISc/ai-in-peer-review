Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoff. Items are mapped to latent labels B(X); workers are modeled as noisy channels, with two channel families considered: M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with heterogeneous error rates, and spammer–hammer channels (SHC). Two scenarios are analyzed: worker skill levels unknown to the decoder (SL-UK) versus known at the decoder (SL-CS). Using rate–distortion theory and Shannon’s separation theorem, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target average error. They also introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a query presents k items and asks for within-query clustering, which implicitly rules out invalid patterns and thus offers error-correction structure. For SHC, they derive a lower bound on the rate needed by kIC to achieve a target error and relate kIC “price” to k. Numerical comparisons illustrate gaps between the information-theoretic limits and the kIC lower bounds.

This places the paper within a growing literature on the statistical and algorithmic foundations of crowdsourcing, including EM-based aggregation (Dawid–Skene and variants), spectral/graphical methods and sample-complexity analyses (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah), and bandit formulations for worker selection. The use of a clean rate–distortion formulation to articulate ultimate budget–fidelity limits is an appealing complement to those works.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The source–channel abstraction, the two worker channel models, and the two scenarios (SL-UK vs SL-CS) are presented cleanly.
- Explicit limits: Theorems 2 and 3 yield interpretable expressions for the minimal rate as a function of source entropy, target error, and worker reliability distributions; the SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is insightful.
- kIC case study: Formalizes a widely used “relative” query type and shows how invalid response patterns introduce redundancy; the SHC-based analysis and the simple pricing heuristic are useful starting points.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Tightness and feasibility of the separation-based bounds: Shannon separation presumes extremely flexible code design (arbitrary block mappings and large blocklengths) and unconstrained channel uses. In crowdsourcing, the encoder C in Eq. (3) is subject to nontrivial constraints: (i) one cannot present the same query to the same worker repeatedly without introducing dependence; (ii) task interfaces and platform policies constrain U and V; (iii) budget and fairness considerations often enforce near-uniform query loads across workers; and (iv) kIC induces coupling between channel uses (shared items across queries). These constraints can significantly reduce the feasible encoding space compared to classical channel coding and make the separation-based converse potentially loose. The paper should delineate the admissible encoding class more explicitly and discuss how the proofs adapt to these constraints.
- IID and memoryless assumptions: Workers are modeled as memoryless channels with independent uses, but human answers can be correlated across closely related queries, and re-asking identical (or near-identical) queries to the same worker violates independence. kIC also creates statistical dependence across responses involving shared items. The potential impact on the bounds (both achievability and converse) is not discussed.
- Achievability vs oracle assumptions: Several bounds (e.g., Theorem 4) rely on an oracle decoder that knows worker types. While Theorem 3 addresses SL-CS in the information-theoretic limit, it remains unclear whether practically implementable encoders/decoders under realistic constraints can approach these limits; showing a concrete scheme that narrows the gap would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirical grounding: The paper would be stronger with empirical comparisons showing how close current state-of-the-art aggregation methods (e.g., majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM hybrids) come to the derived limits under standard synthetic models (MSC/SHC) and realistic budgets. This would help assess the practical utility and tightness of the bounds.
- Cost model for kIC: While Appendix E is mentioned, the main text should make the per-query cost explicit (time/cognitive effort grows with k). The pricing heuristic π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 is reasonable but should be justified either by user-time measurements or platform pricing norms.

Quality
The theoretical development is mostly sound given the modeling assumptions, and the SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast is well argued. However, several proofs (e.g., Theorem 4) are terse, and the link from communication-theoretic assumptions to crowdsourcing constraints is not fully justified. Careful discussion of encoding constraints and dependence would improve technical rigor.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but some notation and statements need tightening. A few equations would benefit from precise conditions and citations, and some editorial issues remain (see Minor comments).

Originality
Moderately novel. Applying rate–distortion/separation as a unifying lens for crowdsourcing is interesting and, to my knowledge, not standard in this exact form. The kIC analysis under SHC appears new. However, parts of the development (e.g., separation-based limits) are relatively straightforward once the abstraction is fixed.

Significance
Potentially useful as a conceptual baseline and to motivate query design beyond independent label requests. The practical significance will increase if the authors (i) formalize realistic encoding constraints and prove bounds within that class, and/or (ii) provide algorithms that empirically approach the limits.

Suggestions for improvement and questions
- Encoding constraints: Precisely define the admissible set of encoders C in Eq. (3) reflecting: no repeated identical queries to the same worker, per-worker load caps, and coupling induced by kIC. Revisit the converse under these constraints or argue why the current converse still applies.
- Dependence: Discuss how dependence across channel uses (due to repeated workers or shared items in kIC) affects the applicability of separation. Are there mixing conditions under which your bounds remain valid?
- Achievability: Provide at least one concrete, implementable scheme (e.g., randomized kIC with simple aggregation) and show how close it gets to the limits on synthetic MSC/SHC populations.
- Empirical comparison: Plot performance of majority vote, Dawid–Skene, and a spectral/EM method versus your bounds under matched synthetic models.
- kIC cost: State and use a per-query cost model in the main text when comparing k’s; justify the pricing rule empirically or via a cognitive-effort model.

Minor comments and corrections
- Eq. (8): Clarify that the minimization is over conditional distributions P(Ẑ|Z) (equivalently over source encoders/decoders) subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ Dt. Provide a citation (e.g., Cover & Thomas, Thm. 10.3.2).
- Theorem 4: ln(1 − q) < 0. Please check the sign and monotonicity: the denominator ln(ε̂/ε̄S) or ln(ε̂) vs ln(ε̄S) should be handled carefully; make explicit whether ε̄S > ε̂ is required for the bound to be meaningful. A more detailed proof sketch would help.
- Eq. (12)/(17): Justify the appearance of E[HM(ε)] when worker reliabilities are known at the decoder; cite a standard result on channels with random states known at the receiver (e.g., El Gamal & Kim, Ch. 7), where capacity is the expected mutual information conditioned on the state.
- Clarify notation: Define pmax when first used; specify M vs N consistently (alphabet size for worker response vs number of true labels); define Hb and HM explicitly.
- Editorial: Several references lack years and full venues; fix “Wiely”→“Wiley,” and remove placeholder text like “th Conference.” Ensure all appendices fit within venue constraints or move key proofs into the main text.

Pros
- Clear, principled framework connecting crowdsourcing to information theory.
- Interpretable limits for SL-UK and SL-CS; insightful comparison.
- kIC formulation and SHC-based analysis are concrete and potentially useful.

Cons
- Bounds may be loose under realistic encoding and dependence constraints.
- Oracle assumptions for some results; limited achievability/empirical validation.
- Clarity issues in proofs and some notation/citations.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With a clearer modeling of feasible encoders, tighter discussion of dependence, and empirical comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, this could become a solid contribution.
