This paper addresses the aggregation of worker outputs in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework and characterizes fundamental limits of the budget–fidelity tradeoff. Although I did not fully understand all parts of the paper (see below), I found the model and its insights to be interesting and potentially useful for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques.

I would like the high-level intuition and practical takeaways to be emphasized more prominently. In particular, please foreground the intuition distilled at the end of Section 3 and add a brief conclusion summarizing what a practitioner should take away from the results (I recognize space is tight). Despite the authors’ efforts to make the paper accessible to readers less familiar with information theory, it remains difficult to follow. I recommend restating many of the main results directly in the language of the crowdsourcing application—e.g., tasks, workers, aggregation rules, budget, and accuracy/error—rather than relying primarily on channels, codes, and distortion.

Related work: Please discuss the complementary and increasingly intersecting line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often described as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” Some of this work models heterogeneous worker abilities and, more recently, studies how to combine incentive mechanisms with more sophisticated aggregation methods. Positioning your results relative to this literature would strengthen the paper.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Line 84: “is reviewed in” should be “are reviewed in.”
- Line 106: The distinction between “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” is confusing (also earlier in the initial setup). The term “crowdsourcing” is a portmanteau of “crowd” and “outsourcing,” which suggests outsourcing occurs before workers get involved; this may contribute to the ambiguity. Please clarify these roles early and use them consistently.
- Line 122: Typo: “A A(X1,X2).”
- Lines 275–276: The sentence beginning “In case …” is very hard to parse; please rewrite for clarity.
- Figure 2: I could not glean much from this figure. A more detailed caption explaining what to look for and how it supports the main claims would help.

Overall, the paper’s perspective and results appear promising, but improved exposition—especially clearer high-level intuition, application-grounded statements of the results, and tighter role definitions—would make the contribution much more accessible and impactful.
