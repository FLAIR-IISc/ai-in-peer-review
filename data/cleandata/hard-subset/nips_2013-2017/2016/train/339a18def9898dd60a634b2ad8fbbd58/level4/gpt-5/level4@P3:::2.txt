Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper models label crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, casting the taskmaster as an encoder, workers as noisy channels, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. Labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source and worker reliability as stochastic channel parameters. Using joint source–channel coding (JSCC), the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error rate under two cases: (i) worker skill unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) skill perfectly known at the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC), deriving a lower bound on achievable error with a spammer–hammer worker model and discuss a pricing rule-of-thumb.

Relative to prior NIPS work that focuses on inference and aggregation algorithms (e.g., majority vote, Dawid–Skene EM, spectral/EM hybrids, and graph-based methods such as Karger–Oh–Shah and Zhang et al.), this paper contributes abstract, information-theoretic limits on budget–fidelity tradeoffs, independent of any specific aggregation algorithm. Similar in spirit to sample-complexity analyses in operations research and learning theory, this work offers a JSCC framing that is broader but more asymptotic.

Strengths:
- Clear, clean mapping of crowdsourcing to JSCC with explicit assumptions (i.i.d. labels, Hamming distortion, non-adaptive codes).
- Theorems 2 and 3 give interpretable lower bounds on queries per item under MSC workers, illuminating the gap between unknown-versus-known skill at the decoder.
- The kIC analysis highlights how constraining workers to valid joint responses can induce an error-correcting effect; the SHC-based bound is simple and intuitive.
- The paper articulates a budget proxy via rate and discusses cost normalization for different query types.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality (soundness and support):
- Separation with random channel state: The main limits (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on source–channel separation. Because the channel has a random state (worker reliability) unknown to the encoder and possibly known at the decoder, the paper should explicitly state conditions under which separation holds (i.i.d. state, memoryless channel, average distortion criterion) and provide a proof sketch or cite a formal result for DMCs with random state (e.g., Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim; channel capacity with i.i.d. state known/unknown to the receiver). As written, this crucial step is assumed.
- Nonasymptotic relevance: The analysis is asymptotic in blocklength and dataset size. For practical crowdsourcing, finite-blocklength characterization would be valuable. Consider referencing and leveraging nonasymptotic converses/achievability for lossy JSCC (e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, IEEE TIT 2013) to provide finite-sample guidance and bounds.
- Oracle assumptions in kIC: Theorem 4 relies on an oracle decoder that perfectly identifies spammers vs. hammers. This makes the bound optimistic. It would be helpful to (i) give an achievability result for a realistic decoder operating under SHC/MSC, or (ii) provide a converse that does not require oracle SI, or (iii) empirically show how far a practical decoder is from the oracle bound.
- Side information at encoder/decoder: In many crowdsourcing settings, both taskmaster and crowdsourcer see the raw items (images, text), which can serve as side information (e.g., Wyner–Ziv/Heegard–Berger settings). Decoder side information can materially change the rate–distortion function and the implied budget. Please discuss this extension and its potential impact.
- Cost normalization: Comparing kIC across different k requires careful cost/time normalization. While Appendix E addresses this, consider moving key caveats to the main text and, if possible, provide empirical time/cost measurements (e.g., MTurk pilot) to make the pricing guidance more actionable.

Clarity:
- Interpret “lower bounds improve” (Sec. 4.2): As k or q increases, the lower bound decreases (becomes less restrictive), suggesting the potential for better performance; it does not by itself prove improvement without a matching upper bound or achievability. Please rephrase accordingly.
- Notation and definitions: Ensure consistent use of N (number of classes) and M (number of answer choices), and clearly map specific query types to M. Define pmax, HN(·), HM(·) on first use. Clarify whether U = V throughout, and how restricting to valid kIC partitions modifies the effective channel.
- Assumptions: State independence assumptions explicitly (across workers, items, and queries), and whether item difficulty is homogeneous. Note that kIC analysis is for N=2; discuss generalization or limits for N > 2.
- Editorial: Fix typos, missing years in references, and placeholders (e.g., “th Conference”). Improve figure captions and ensure that all symbols appearing in figures are defined in the text.

Originality:
- Framing crowdsourcing via JSCC and deriving rate–distortion-type limits is novel in this community and complements prior NIPS work on aggregation/inference. The kIC bound adds insight into structured query designs.

Significance:
- The results provide conceptual “outer” benchmarks that can inform the design of adaptive querying, pricing, and aggregation. Impact would increase with (i) finite-blocklength results, (ii) at least one constructive scheme approaching the bounds under realistic worker models, and (iii) empirical validation or simulation studies across MSC/SHC settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually neat JSCC formulation bridging information theory and crowdsourcing.
- Clear, interpretable lower bounds that separate the roles of worker skill knowledge.
- Insightful analysis of structured queries (kIC) and cost implications.

Cons against acceptance:
- Reliance on asymptotic separation with random state without a formal justification or citation tailored to this setting.
- Practical utility limited by oracle assumptions, absence of achievability/finite-blocklength results, and restricted kIC analysis (N=2).
- Modeling omissions (side information, item difficulty, adaptivity) limit realism.

Actionable recommendations:
- Add a formal statement and citation for source–channel separation with random, i.i.d. channel state unknown to the encoder (and known/unknown to the decoder), including conditions under which Theorems 2–3 hold.
- Discuss finite-blocklength bounds and, if space permits, include nonasymptotic converse/achievability curves (e.g., following Kostina–Verdú) to calibrate the gap between theory and practice.
- Either present a constructive scheme whose performance approaches the limits under MSC/SHC or provide tighter converses that do not rely on oracle SI.
- Expand the discussion on side information and the implications for query design and decoding.
- Clarify interpretation of the kIC lower bounds, generalize beyond N=2 if possible, and foreground cost normalization in the main text.

Overall, this is a well-written, insightful first step toward an information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourced labeling. With the clarifications above and some finite-blocklength or constructive results, the contribution would be considerably strengthened.
