

The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item. They model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state, where the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may or may not be known at the decoder. Specifically, the item labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder observing only the labels, the crowd workers as multiple channel uses, the workers' skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder observing only the workers' labels. Leveraging existing JSCC results, the authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits for the query rate. They additionally establish a lower bound on the error probability for a "k-ary incidence coding" scheme under a specific crowdsourcing model. Overall, the paper is well-written and represents a promising initial step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing.  

Technical comments:  
1. The authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem (Theorems 2 and 3) to derive their results. However, they must first justify that this theorem holds when the channel state is unknown at the encoder (though possibly known at the decoder). This justification is required, as the standard theorem assumes a fixed channel.  
2. The model restricts the taskmaster (encoder) and crowdsourcer (decoder) to observe only the item labels, ignoring potential side information from the original item data. Since both parties could access this data, incorporating it as side information would enhance model realism and potentially improve performance—especially if available at both encoder/decoder or solely at the decoder. The current model appears overly restrictive.  
3. In Section 4.2, the authors state that "the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow" when discussing Figure 2. This is misleading: the lower bounds *decrease* as k and q increase, indicating potentially better code performance (not that the bounds themselves improve, as there is no reference upper bound).  

Edited following authors' response: The JSCC approach is interesting, but as another reviewer noted, a nonasymptotic characterization of crowdsourcing's fundamental limits would be more practically useful. The authors should consider recent nonasymptotic JSCC results, such as those by Kostina and Verdu ("Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013).
