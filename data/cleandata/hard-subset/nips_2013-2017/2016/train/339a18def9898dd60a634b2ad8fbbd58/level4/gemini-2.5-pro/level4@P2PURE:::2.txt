This paper presents an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, deriving fundamental, asymptotic limits on the required number of queries per item. The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state. In this framework, the item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, the crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, their skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. The channel state is unknown to the encoder but may be known to the decoder. Leveraging this model, the authors apply existing results on JSCC coding rates to establish their limits. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability for a specific "k-ary incidence coding" scheme.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that introduces a novel and interesting JSCC perspective on the crowdsourcing problem. However, there are several points that weaken the contribution and should be addressed.

**Major Comments:**

1.  While the use of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting, the paper's focus on asymptotic characterization limits its practical utility. A non-asymptotic analysis of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be significantly more useful. The authors are strongly encouraged to consult recent results on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, particularly: V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013.

2.  The model may be overly restrictive by ignoring that both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer often have access to the original items, not just the labels. This access to the item data constitutes side information within the JSCC framework. While side information available only at the encoder might not be beneficial, its presence at the decoder—or at both encoder and decoder—could significantly improve performance. The model would be more convincing and realistic if it accounted for this possibility.

**Technical Comments:**

1.  The core results (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on the application of the source-channel separation theorem. However, the paper applies this theorem to a channel with a random state that is unknown at the encoder, without providing justification for its validity in this specific scenario. Although the theorem likely holds, the authors must either provide a proof or cite existing work to substantiate its use here.

2.  In Section 4.2, the discussion of Figure 2 states that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) "improve" as k and q grow. This phrasing is ambiguous. A lower value for an error probability bound suggests better potential performance for the code, but one cannot conclude that the bound itself is improving (i.e., becoming tighter) without a corresponding upper bound for comparison.
