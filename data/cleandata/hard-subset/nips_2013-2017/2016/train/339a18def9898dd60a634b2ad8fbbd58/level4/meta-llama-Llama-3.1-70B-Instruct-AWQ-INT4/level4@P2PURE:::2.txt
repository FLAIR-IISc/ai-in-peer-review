The authors provide an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic sense. They model the crowdsourcing problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state, where the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the decoder. The labels of the items are represented as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, the crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, the workers' skill levels as random states of the channel, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder. By leveraging existing results on the coding rate in the JSCC problem, the authors derive the fundamental limits of the asymptotic number of queries per item in crowdsourcing. Additionally, they provide a lower bound on the error probability of a coding scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowd-sourcing model.

In summary, this is a well-written paper that may be a first step toward the JSCC modeling of the crowdsourcing problem. However, some technical comments are in order:

1. The authors model the crowd-sourcing problem as JSCC with a random channel state and apply the standard source-channel separation theorem to obtain their results (Theorem 2 and 3). Nevertheless, it is essential to justify the applicability of the source-channel separation theorem in the presence of a random channel state, where the state is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the receiver. Although it is likely that the separation theorem still holds in these situations, the authors should provide a proof or cite an existing one to support this claim.

2. The taskmaster and crowdsourcer are modeled as an encoder and decoder, respectively, who only observe the labels of the items and the labels produced by the workers. However, this model may be overly restrictive, as it neglects the fact that both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer may have access to the original data (the items). This knowledge of the original data can provide side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem, potentially improving the coding efficiency. It would be more convincing and realistic if the authors had incorporated side information into their model.

3. In Sec 4.2, when discussing the curves in Fig 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow. Presumably, this means that the lower bound becomes smaller as k and q increase, indicating potential improvements in the code's performance. However, it is essential to clarify that the lower bound itself does not improve in an absolute sense, as there is no upper bound for comparison.

Following the authors' response, it is worth noting that the use of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting, but a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practical. The authors may benefit from exploring recent results by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, specifically "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime" (IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013).
