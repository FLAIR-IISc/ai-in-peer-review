

This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem within a rate-distortion framework. The authors establish fundamental limits for crowdsourcing systems using joint source-channel coding principles, deriving bounds for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The paper also introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a query scheme that leverages error correction capabilities by presenting multiple items simultaneously for comparison.

The paper's technical contribution is well-structured and builds upon established information theory concepts. The derivation of the distortion-rate function using rate-distortion theory and channel coding theorems is mathematically sound. The authors correctly identify the key challenge in crowdsourcing: balancing query efficiency with the need for redundancy to overcome worker errors. The paper's main strength lies in its formal framework that unifies crowdsourcing under a communication-theoretic lens, providing a rigorous foundation for analyzing fundamental limits.

However, the novelty and practical significance of the work warrant careful consideration. While the information-theoretic approach is conceptually sound, the derivation of the bounds appears relatively straightforward given the established theoretical framework, and the resulting bounds may be loose in practice due to critical constraints specific to crowdsourcing that don't exist in standard channel coding problems.

The primary concern is that the authors' proposed framework doesn't fully account for the unique constraints of crowdsourcing systems. In standard channel coding, we have considerable freedom in designing codewords (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, LDPC codes), but in crowdsourcing, the encoding mappings face severe limitations. Specifically, the constraint that the same worker cannot receive the same query multiple times (which would violate the iid assumption in equations (1) and (2)) significantly restricts the set of possible encoding schemes. This constraint isn't addressed in the paper, and it's unclear how the authors' theoretical framework would account for such practical limitations. The paper states that "almost all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(∑mi) should be valid," but this overlooks the fundamental practical constraints of crowdsourcing that would severely limit implementable encoding schemes.

The authors' comparison to standard channel coding is misleading because the two problems have fundamentally different constraints. In channel coding, we can repeatedly use the same input to the channel, but in crowdsourcing, presenting the same query to the same worker multiple times would produce identical responses (violating the iid assumption) and is therefore impractical. This key difference suggests that the channel capacity may be an overly optimistic bound for crowdsourcing systems.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison of the derived information-theoretic bounds with state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods. The authors should demonstrate how their theoretical bounds relate to practical performance, especially since they propose k-ary incidence coding as a practical implementation. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the theoretical limits are meaningful in practice.

Minor technical points that require attention:
- In equation (8), the minimization should be over C, C' such that E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D^t
- The proof of Theorem 4 could be more elaborated to clarify the relationship between the parameters
- The expression ln(1-q) is negative, which requires careful interpretation of the error terms
- The term H_M(E(ε)) in equation (12) needs clearer explanation or citation to prior work

While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by framing crowdsourcing within information theory, the practical relevance of the derived bounds is questionable due to the unaddressed constraints of crowdsourcing systems. The authors should more clearly articulate the gap between the theoretical framework and implementable crowdsourcing systems. Without addressing these constraints or providing empirical comparisons with existing methods, the paper's contribution to the field remains somewhat abstract and limited in practical value.

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the gap between the theoretical bounds and practical implementation. The authors should acknowledge the constraints of crowdsourcing systems that limit the applicability of standard channel coding techniques and potentially revise their bounds to account for these constraints. Without such refinement, the proposed bounds may be overly optimistic and not directly applicable to real-world crowdsourcing systems.

Overall, the paper presents a theoretically interesting framework that successfully applies information theory to crowdsourcing. However, the practical significance of the results is limited by the authors' failure to address the critical constraints of crowdsourcing systems that differentiate them from standard channel coding problems. The paper would be substantially improved with a clearer articulation of these constraints and a comparison between the theoretical bounds and practical crowdsourcing performance.
