- Uses rate-distortion theory and channel coding to derive a theoretical lower bound for crowdsourcing; derivation appears sound and could be novel if first application.  
- Practical significance and novelty are questionable given potential looseness of the bound.  
- Core concern: channel-coding design freedoms may not exist in crowdsourcing, so the derived bound may be overly conservative.  
- Unclear whether most mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σ mi) are implementable as codebooks in crowdsourcing, which is required for a tight capacity-based bound.  
- Repetition coding (same query to multiple workers) is feasible, but beyond that it’s unclear how to design U^n to improve error correction like in classical coding.  
- Fundamental difference: you cannot repeatedly present the same query to the same worker without violating the i.i.d. assumptions; classical channels allow repeated transmissions.  
- Many additional, stricter constraints likely exist in crowdsourcing (absent in channel coding), suggesting the information-theoretic bound may be loose.  
- Recommendation: empirically compare the theoretical bound to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods to quantify the theory–practice gap.
