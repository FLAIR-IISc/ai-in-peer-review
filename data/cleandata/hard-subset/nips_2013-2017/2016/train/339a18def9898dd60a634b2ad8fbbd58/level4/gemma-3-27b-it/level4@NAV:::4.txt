This paper investigates a crowdsourcing paradigm where a taskmaster decomposes a complex task into smaller sub-tasks for distribution to a crowd of workers. The crowdsourcer then aggregates and processes the workers' responses to infer the solution to the original task. A key consideration is maintaining a desired level of accuracy – or fidelity – in the final inference, despite potential inaccuracies or varying skill levels among the workers. The work focuses on the fundamental trade-off between the ‘budget’ (total number of queries issued to workers) and the achieved fidelity. 

The authors establish a compelling connection between this crowdsourcing framework and the established field of joint source-channel coding from information theory. By mapping the taskmaster to a transmitter and the crowdsourcer to a receiver, and modeling worker responses as signals passing through noisy channels, they leverage information-theoretic principles to derive a lower bound on the required budget for a given fidelity level. Importantly, this bound is scheme-agnostic, holding regardless of the specific query design used by the taskmaster or the aggregation algorithm employed by the crowdsourcer. 

The paper then focuses on a specific query design scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyzes its performance relative to this theoretical lower bound under a defined channel model. The analogy drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the proposed crowdsourcing system is insightful and has the potential to inspire further research utilizing information-theoretic tools within the crowdsourcing domain.

However, the reviewer has some reservations regarding the practical realism of the presented model. The simplification to a point-to-point communication problem, while enabling the direct application of existing information theory results, may sacrifice nuance present in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. Several points warrant further discussion by the authors:

1.  The model currently combines worker reliability and skill into a single channel representation. In practice, taskmasters often assign queries differentially based on worker skill level, represented by ratings or past performance. This aspect is not accounted for in the current framework.
2.  The channel models used to represent worker unreliability should be further justified and their limitations acknowledged. The model does not currently address the possibility of adversarial worker behavior, where workers with task knowledge might deliberately submit inaccurate responses. 
3.  The presentation of the numerical results lacks clarity. The labeling of curves in Fig.~2 could be improved for better understanding, and the meaning of the $k=1$ case requires clarification – does this represent workers directly providing correct labels without a paired comparison?
4.  The assumption of known $P(B(X))$ is stated late in the paper (page 6, line 246) and should be presented earlier for clarity.
5.  Minor errors exist in figure references (page 4, lines 135-136: Figure 1.a should be Figure 1.b, and Figure 1.b should be Figure 1.c).
6.  The authors frequently use the terms ‘Theorem,’ ‘Lemma,’ and ‘Corollary’ without accompanying numbering. Using these terms as descriptive labels rather than formal designations would be more appropriate.
