The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. Their approach models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. Specifically, items are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as channel uses representing varying skill levels (random channel states), and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. Leveraging existing JSCC results, the authors establish fundamental limits on the asymptotic query complexity. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability for k-ary incidence coding within their defined model. Overall, this is a clearly written paper and represents a potentially valuable initial step in applying JSCC to the crowdsourcing problem. 

However, several technical points warrant consideration:

1. The core of the argument relies on applying the source-channel separation theorem to this JSCC formulation with random states, where the state is unknown to the encoder but potentially known to the decoder. The authors should explicitly justify the applicability of the separation theorem in this scenario, either through a proof or citation of existing work.

2. The model currently restricts the taskmaster to only observing item labels and the crowdsourcer to only observing worker responses. This may be overly restrictive, as both entities could potentially have access to the original data (the items themselves). Incorporating this potential side information – particularly if available at both the encoder and decoder – could lead to a more compelling and realistic model, potentially improving performance within the JSCC framework.

3. In Section 4.2, the discussion of Fig. 2 states that the lower bounds for kIC improve as *k* and *q* increase. This likely refers to a decrease in the lower bound value, suggesting potential performance gains, but doesn’t constitute an “improvement” in the bound itself without a corresponding upper bound for comparison.

Furthermore, while the asymptotic analysis is valuable, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would likely be more practically useful. The authors should consider recent work by Kostina and Verdu on nonasymptotic converses for JSCC [V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime", IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013].
