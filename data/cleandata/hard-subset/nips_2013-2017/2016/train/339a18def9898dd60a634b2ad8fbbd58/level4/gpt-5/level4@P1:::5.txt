Summary
The paper models digital crowdsourcing in an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a target probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. Two channel families are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) to capture generic worker errors, and (ii) the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), where a worker is a perfect “hammer” with probability q and a random “spammer” otherwise. Coding is assumed non-adaptive.

The analysis treats two scenarios: (1) the crowdsourcer (decoder), who aggregates responses, knows each worker’s skill level; and (2) the crowdsourcer does not know any worker skills. In both scenarios, the taskmaster (encoder), who assigns tasks, is assumed not to know worker skills, so each worker receives the same number of tasks. The main results give information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum rate (queries per task) needed to achieve a desired error probability in both settings. The paper also analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a worker is shown k items and asked to indicate which belong to the same class (e.g., “breed”), and provides a necessary condition on the minimum rate for kIC under an SHC(q) worker pool. The authors discuss how such bounds can inform pricing.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem, with clean lower bounds on rate vs. error.
- Modeling workers as channels (MSC and SHC) is natural and provides interpretable results.
- The kIC analysis is interesting and suggests error-correction benefits by restricting invalid responses.
- Well organized and well written. The results appear useful for practitioners to size budgets and set prices for targeted fidelity.

Weaknesses
- No experiments or simulations to show how far common schemes (e.g., on AMT) are from the theoretical limits, or to illustrate the tightness of the bounds in realistic settings.
- Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 (background) seems unnecessary or could be condensed. Proof sketches of the main theorems would be more valuable in the main text.
- I did not read the appendix, so I cannot assess the proofs.

Quality
The technical claims are plausible and the modeling choices are well motivated. The core results are lower bounds derived under clearly stated assumptions (non-adaptive coding; MSC and SHC worker models; equal task assignment due to unknown skills at the taskmaster). However, the paper would benefit from empirical or simulated validation to gauge the practical gap to these limits. Because I did not read the appendix, I cannot verify the proofs.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and generally clear. That said, Section 2.4 is longer than needed for the main message, and Section 3.1 can likely be compressed or moved to an appendix. Including brief proof sketches for the central theorems in the main text would substantially improve readability and help readers understand the core arguments without diving into the appendices.

Originality
To the best of my knowledge (and as claimed by the authors), this is the first analysis of crowdsourcing from an explicit information-theoretic rate–distortion perspective. This is a novel and timely angle that complements algorithmic and statistical work on aggregation and worker modeling.

Significance
The results can inform the design of crowdsourcing systems by quantifying the minimum query rate needed for a target error probability and by guiding pricing. The kIC bound under SHC(q) is particularly relevant for designing more structured queries. Demonstrating, even in small-scale simulations, how standard methods compare to the derived limits would further increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add simulations or case studies (e.g., AMT-style binary labeling with majority vote or EM/Dawid–Skene) to illustrate the gap between practical methods and the theoretical bounds.
- Shorten Section 2.4 and condense or move Section 3.1 to the appendix; instead, provide proof sketches for the main theorems in the main text.
- For kIC, include a brief practical discussion of how query cost scales with k and how the necessary condition translates into pricing in a realistic budget.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel information-theoretic formulation with clear lower bounds.
- Useful guidance for budgeting and pricing in real systems.
- Well written and organized; kIC analysis is insightful.

Cons:
- No empirical or simulation results to quantify the practical gap to the bounds.
- Background material could be shorter; missing proof sketches in the main text.
- Conclusions depend on specific channel models and non-adaptive codes, with limited discussion of robustness.
