This paper considers a crowdsourcing framework where a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller queries. These queries are distributed to a group of workers, and a crowdsourcer then aggregates their responses to complete the original task. The model accounts for workers who have varying skill levels and may be unreliable. The central objective is to guarantee a certain level of fidelity, or quality, in the final inference outcome, even when some workers provide incorrect information. This requires the taskmaster to carefully design the queries.

The paper defines the total number of queries presented to the workers as the system's budget and studies the fundamental trade-off between this budget and the resulting fidelity. The core contribution is to analyze this trade-off by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source-channel coding. In this analogy, the taskmaster is treated as a source encoder/transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable workers collectively form a communication channel. Drawing on this mapping, the authors use standard results from information theory to derive a lower bound on the budget required to achieve a desired fidelity level. This bound is powerful because it is independent of the specific query design strategy used by the taskmaster and the aggregation algorithm used by the crowdsourcer.

The authors then propose a specific query design scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," analyze its performance, and compare it against the information-theoretic lower bound for a particular channel model.

The parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers is a novel and interesting contribution. This work has the potential to encourage further research into applying information-theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing framework.

However, I have some concerns about how realistically the proposed model captures real-world crowdsourcing systems. My suspicion is that the problem may have been simplified to map cleanly onto the classic point-to-point communication problem, allowing existing information-theoretic results to be applied directly. It would be helpful if the authors could comment on the following issues:

1.  The model appears to conflate worker reliability and skill level into a single channel parameter. Furthermore, it assumes the taskmaster allocates an equal number of queries to all workers, regardless of their individual skill. This contrasts with practical systems where worker ratings or reputation scores often influence task allocation and weighting.

2.  How realistic are the proposed channel models for worker unreliability? The paper should provide more justification for their applicability. A notable omission is the consideration of adversarial workers, who might possess knowledge of the task and actively attempt to degrade the system's fidelity. How would the proposed framework handle such scenarios?

**Minor Issues:**

*   The explanation of the numerical results is unclear, and the labeling of the curves in Figure 2 could be improved for better readability.
*   Could the authors clarify the interpretation of the k=1 case? Does this correspond to a worker directly providing the label for a presented item, as it seems one cannot ask if a pair shares the same label in this scenario?
*   The assumption that the distribution P(B(X)) is known, mentioned on page 6, line 246, is a critical one and should be stated much earlier, ideally in the problem formulation section.
*   There appear to be incorrect figure references on page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.a" should likely be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".
*   The terms "Theorem", "Lemma", and "Corollary" are frequently capitalized even when not referring to a specific numbered result. Standard practice would be to use lowercase (e.g., "theorem", "lemma") in these general instances.
