- Core idea: Model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation and analyze budget–fidelity tradeoffs using an information-theoretic rate–distortion framework.  
- Worker modeling: Workers treated as noisy channels (memoryless symmetric or spammer-hammer) to derive fundamental limits.  
- Contributions: Proposes joint source–channel coding view and analyzes a specific query design, k-ary incidence coding, plus optimized query pricing.  
- Novelty: Applying classical information theory to crowdsourcing is a new, principled perspective on fundamental performance limits.  
- Theoretical rigor: Uses information-theoretic tools to derive bounds and offers a detailed analysis of k-ary incidence coding.  
- Clarity: Model and exposition are generally well-structured and easy to follow.  
- Practical implication: Query pricing analysis suggests potential real-world benefits of the proposed scheme.  
- Major assumption concerns: Relies on i.i.d. sources, memoryless channels, and in one case perfect skill estimation, which may not hold in practice.  
- Complexity and applicability: The heavy theoretical analysis may limit immediate applicability; bridging bounds to practical system design needs more work.  
- Empirical validation lacking: No simulations or experiments to demonstrate k-ary incidence coding effectiveness or bound tightness.  
- Related work gap: Related work discussion should better connect to active learning and other querying literatures.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically sound contribution that advances foundational understanding and introduces a useful query design.  
- Rejection concerns: Simplifying assumptions and absence of empirical results reduce practical impact and make relevance to real crowdsourcing uncertain.  
- Overall: Solid theoretical paper worthy of acceptance, but would be strengthened by empirical validation in future revisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
