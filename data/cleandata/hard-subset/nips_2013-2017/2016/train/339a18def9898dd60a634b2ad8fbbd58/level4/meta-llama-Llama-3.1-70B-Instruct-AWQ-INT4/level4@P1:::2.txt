This paper provides an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, and obtains fundamental limits on the number of queries per item under such a model. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as an information-theoretic problem of joint source channel coding (JSCC) with random channel state, where the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may or may not be known at the decoder. The labels of the items are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, the crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, and the workers' skill levels as random states of the channel, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder.

The authors use existing results on the coding rate in the JSCC problem to establish the fundamental limits of the asymptotic number of queries per item in crowdsourcing. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of a coding scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowd-sourcing model. The paper is well-written, and the authors' approach to modeling the crowdsourcing problem as a JSCC problem is a promising first step towards understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.

However, there are a few technical comments that need to be addressed. Firstly, the authors should provide a more detailed justification for using the standard source-channel separation theorem in information theory to obtain their results. Specifically, they should argue that the separation theorem still holds when the channel has a random state, where the state is unknown at the encoder but may or may not be known at the receiver.

Secondly, the authors' model may be too restrictive, as it ignores the fact that both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer may have access to the original data (the items). This knowledge of the original data can provide side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem, which may be helpful in certain scenarios. The authors should consider incorporating side information into their model to make it more convincing and realistic.

Finally, the authors' comment on the curves in Fig 2 could be rephrased for clarity. Instead of saying that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow, they could say that the lower bound becomes smaller as k and q grow, indicating that the performance of the code may get better.

In terms of originality, the paper's use of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting, but it would be more useful to have a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of CS. The authors may want to look into recent results on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, such as the work by Kostina and Verdu.
