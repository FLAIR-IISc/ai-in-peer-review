

This work establishes an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of the bound is technically sound, and if this represents the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, it could constitute a meaningful contribution. However, the novelty and practical value of the paper remain questionable, as the derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound may be overly conservative due to unaddressed constraints in the crowdsourcing setting.

The key issue lies in the fundamental mismatch between channel coding and crowdsourcing. In channel coding, we enjoy significant freedom in designing codebooks (e.g., repetition, Hamming, LDPC codes), as the channel is memoryless and identical queries can be reused without violating assumptions. Crucially, in crowdsourcing, assigning the same query to the same worker multiple times violates the iid assumption (Equations 1–2), as the worker will likely provide identical answers, breaking the model's core premise. While repetition coding (with majority voting) is feasible, it is unclear how to design the input sequence \(u^n\) to improve error correction beyond this trivial case. 

More broadly, the set of permissible encoding schemes in Equation (3) faces stringent constraints absent in channel coding. In communication, the encoding map \(\{0,1\}^{nR} \to \{0,1\}^n\) has minimal restrictions, whereas crowdsourcing imposes hard constraints on valid mappings from \(B(X)^n\) to \(U^{\sum m_i}\). For instance, the requirement to avoid repeated queries for the same worker severely limits the design space for \(u^n\). These constraints likely render the proposed channel capacity bound loose, as it assumes a flexibility in encoding that does not exist in practice. The paper should explicitly address how such constraints affect the tightness of the bound.

To strengthen the contribution, the authors should compare the derived bound against the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene, Bayesian approaches). Without this empirical validation, the practical relevance of the bound remains speculative.

- The proof of Theorem 4 requires greater elaboration, particularly regarding the handling of \(\ln(1-q) < 0\). Clarify whether \(\epsilon_S > \hat{\epsilon}\) and justify this condition.
