- Derivation uses rate–distortion and channel coding; appears sound and could be noteworthy if it's the first such application to crowdsourcing.
- Main concerns about novelty and limited practical impact due to a straightforward derivation and potentially loose bound in realistic settings.
- Fundamental gap between channel coding and crowdsourcing: channel coding allows flexible encoder/codebook design, but analogous freedom for designing u^n in crowdsourcing is unclear.
- Equation (3) effectively counts many encoder mappings that may not be implementable in crowdsourcing, so capacity-based bounds may not be tight.
- Repetition with majority voting is a natural crowdsourcing “code,” but constructing other implementable, error-correcting u^n consistent with model assumptions is unclear.
- Reusing the same worker for identical queries induces correlated responses and violates the i.i.d. assumptions, unlike repeatable i.i.d. channel uses in communications.
- Crowdsourcing constraints (task assignment limits, worker reuse, response dependencies, etc.) restrict admissible encoders and likely widen the gap between the bound and achievable performance.
- Recommendation: explicitly characterize admissible encoders under realistic crowdsourcing constraints and quantify how those constraints affect achievable rates.
- Recommendation: empirically compare the derived bound to performance of state-of-the-art aggregation methods over various budgets and worker models to assess tightness and relevance.
