- Paper studies task decomposition in crowdsourcing, aggregating heterogeneous and unreliable worker responses to achieve desired final-fidelity.  
- Focus is on the budget–fidelity trade-off (number of queries vs achievable accuracy).  
- Maps the crowdsourcing problem to joint source–channel coding: taskmaster=transmitter, crowdsourcer=receiver, workers=channel.  
- Derives an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget required for a target fidelity, independent of specific query/aggregation schemes.  
- Proposes and analyzes a specific query design, k-ary incidence coding, and compares its performance to the theoretical bound under a specific channel model.  
- Reviewer finds the rate–distortion analogy insightful and a useful direction for applying information theory to crowdsourcing.  
- Major concern: the model’s realism is questionable due to enforcing a direct point-to-point communication mapping.  
- Critique: worker skill and reliability are conflated into a single channel representation, and the assumption of equal query allocation to all workers is unrealistic.  
- Critique: the channel model may not capture adversarial workers who intentionally degrade fidelity; needs discussion of adversarial behavior.  
- Presentation/data issue: numerical results lack clear explanation; Fig.2 curve labels need improvement and the meaning of k=1 should be clarified.  
- Modeling/timepoint issue: the assumption that P(B(X)) is known appears only on page 6 (line 246) and should be stated earlier.  
- Minor corrections: page 4 line 135 has incorrect figure references (“Figure 1.a”→“Figure 1.b”, “Figure 1.b”→“Figure 1.c”).  
- Formatting suggestion: authors use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', 'Corollary' without numbers; recommend using lowercase or proper numbering.
