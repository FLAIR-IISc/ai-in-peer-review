This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states, representing items as a source, the taskmaster as an encoder, workers as channel uses, worker skill as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. By leveraging existing JSCC results, they derive theoretical bounds on the necessary queries per item. Furthermore, they provide a lower bound on the error probability for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) within a specific crowdsourcing model. Overall, this is a clearly written and potentially impactful paper, representing a valuable initial step towards applying JSCC principles to understand crowdsourcing.

However, while the JSCC framing is intriguing, the paper relies heavily on the source-channel separation theorem without explicitly justifying its applicability in this context. Specifically, the theorem is invoked to derive results (Theorem 2 and 3) assuming a random channel state unknown to the encoder but potentially known to the decoder. The authors should provide a proof or cite existing literature demonstrating that the separation theorem continues to hold under these conditions. Establishing this validity is crucial for the theoretical foundation of the work.

A further limitation lies in the model's restrictive nature. The taskmaster is modeled as encoding only item labels, and the crowdsourcer decodes solely from worker responses. This neglects the potential for both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer to possess access to the original data (the items themselves). Such side information could significantly impact the JSCC problem, providing valuable cues to both the encoder and decoder. While side information available only to the encoder might offer limited benefit, its presence at both ends, or even at the decoder alone, could substantially improve performance. A more realistic and convincing model would incorporate the possibility of side information at the taskmaster or crowdsourcer.

Regarding the empirical analysis in Section 4.2, the authors observe that the lower bounds for kIC improve with increasing *k* and *q*. This statement is potentially misleading. While the lower bound *value* decreases as *k* and *q* grow – suggesting improved code performance – the term "improve" typically implies progress relative to an upper bound. Without an accompanying upper bound, it’s difficult to interpret the significance of this decreasing lower bound.

Finally, as highlighted in discussion with the authors and echoed by another reviewer, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be significantly more valuable than the current asymptotic results. While the JSCC approach is interesting, practical crowdsourcing applications often involve a limited number of queries. Recent work by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC (V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime", *IEEE Trans. on Information Theory*, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013) offers a potential pathway for extending this work in a more practically relevant direction.
