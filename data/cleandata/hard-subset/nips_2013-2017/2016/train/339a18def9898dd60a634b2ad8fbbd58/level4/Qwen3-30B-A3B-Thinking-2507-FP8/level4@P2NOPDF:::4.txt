

This paper investigates a crowdsourcing framework where a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller subtasks assigned to workers with heterogeneous skill levels and potential unreliability. The crowdsourcer aggregates worker responses to infer the final result, aiming for a target fidelity level despite noisy inputs. The core contribution lies in modeling this system through the lens of information theory, specifically mapping it to joint source-channel coding. Here, the taskmaster acts as a transmitter, workers form unreliable channels, and the crowdsourcer functions as a receiver. The authors derive a fundamental lower bound on the required budget (number of queries) for a given fidelity, leveraging established information-theoretic results. This bound is scheme-independent, providing a theoretical benchmark.

The authors then propose a specific query design scheme—k-ary incidence coding—and analyze its performance against the derived bound under a particular channel model. The parallel between rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing is insightful and potentially valuable for motivating future work using information-theoretic tools in crowdsourcing. However, the reviewer questions the realism of the model's assumptions, which appear tailored to enable direct application of existing information theory results with minimal adaptation.

Key concerns include:
1.  **Conflation of Skill and Reliability:** The model merges worker skill and reliability into a single channel parameter. Crucially, the taskmaster allocates identical query volumes to all workers regardless of individual skill, which contradicts real-world practices where workers often possess skill ratings. The paper should address how to incorporate worker-specific skill levels into query allocation.
2.  **Channel Model Realism:** The channel model may not sufficiently capture real-world worker behavior. It does not account for adversarial workers who might deliberately provide incorrect answers based on task knowledge. The authors should discuss the robustness of their approach against such malicious actors.
3.  **Clarity in Results:** The explanation of numerical results (Fig. 2) is unclear. The meaning of the $k=1$ case requires clarification: does it imply workers directly report the correct label (since pairwise comparisons aren't possible), and how does this compare to other $k$ values? The labeling of curves in Fig. 2 needs improvement for better interpretability.
4.  **Missing Justification:** On page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ is stated without justification or prior introduction. This critical assumption should be clearly motivated and defined earlier in the paper.
5.  **Figure and Terminology Errors:**
    *   Page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1.b" and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".
    *   The manuscript repeatedly uses "Theorem", "Lemma", and "Corollary" without corresponding numbered statements. These terms should be replaced with "result", "claim", or "observation" unless specific numbered theorems are presented.

While the theoretical connection to information theory is intellectually stimulating and the derived bound is technically sound, the paper's utility hinges on the plausibility of its core assumptions. The model prioritizes theoretical elegance and direct applicability of information theory over modeling key aspects of real crowdsourcing dynamics, such as worker skill heterogeneity and potential adversarial behavior. The authors should explicitly discuss these limitations and provide a clearer justification for their modeling choices. Addressing these points—particularly the realistic incorporation of worker skill and adversarial scenarios—would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and relevance to practical crowdsourcing systems. The current model offers a useful theoretical foundation but may not provide actionable insights for real-world deployment without substantial refinement of its assumptions.
