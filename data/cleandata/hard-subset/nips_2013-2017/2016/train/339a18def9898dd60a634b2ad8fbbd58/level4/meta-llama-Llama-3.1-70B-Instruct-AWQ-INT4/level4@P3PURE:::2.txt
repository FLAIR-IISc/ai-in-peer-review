The authors provide a novel information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, obtaining fundamental limits on the number of queries per item (in an asymptotic sense) under this model. Specifically, the crowdsourcing problem is modeled as an information-theoretic problem of joint source channel coding (JSCC) with random channel state. The channel state is assumed to be unknown at the encoder, but may or may not be known at the decoder. The labels of the items are modeled as an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster is modeled as an encoder; the crowd workers are modeled as multiple uses of a channel; the workers' skill levels are modeled as random states of the channel; and the crowdsourcer is modeled as the decoder. With this model, the authors leverage existing results on the coding rate in the JSCC problem to establish the fundamental limits of the asymptotic number of queries per item in crowdsourcing. The authors also provide a lower bound on the error probability of a coding scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowd-sourcing model (or channel under their information-theoretic model).

In summary, this is a well-written paper that may be a first step toward the JSCC modeling of the crowdsourcing problem. However, there are several technical aspects that warrant further discussion. Firstly, the authors model the crowdsourcing problem as JSCC with random channel state and utilize the standard source-channel separation theorem in information theory to obtain their results (Theorem 2 and 3). Nevertheless, it is essential to argue that the source-channel separation theorem still holds when the channel has a random state, where the state is unknown at the encoder but may or may not be known at the receiver. While it is believed that the separation theorem still holds in these situations, the authors should provide a proof or reference an existing one to substantiate this claim.

Secondly, the taskmaster is modeled as an encoder who only sees the labels of the items, and the crowdsourcer is modeled as a decoder who only sees the labels produced by the workers. However, this model may be overly restrictive, as it neglects the fact that both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer may have access to the original data (the items). This knowledge of the original data can provide side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem, which may be beneficial, especially if present at both the encoder and decoder or even at the decoder alone. Incorporating side information into the model would make it more convincing and realistic.

Thirdly, in Sec 4.2, when commenting on the curves in Fig 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow. It is presumed that the authors mean that the lower bound becomes smaller as k and q grow, indicating potential improvement in the code's performance. However, it is essential to clarify that the lower bound itself does not improve, as there is no upper bound for comparison.

Furthermore, the use of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting, but a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of CS would be more useful. The authors may benefit from exploring recent results by Kostina and Verdu, which pertain to nonasymptotic converses for JSCC (V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime", IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013).
