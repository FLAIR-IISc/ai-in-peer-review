The paper studies a crowdsourcing setting in which a taskmaster partitions a large task (e.g., an inference problem) into small tasks (queries) that are assigned to workers. The crowdsourcer collects the workers’ responses and aggregates them to solve the original inference problem. Workers may have heterogeneous skill levels and may be unreliable in their reports. The objective is to guarantee a target level of fidelity (or “goodness”) in the final output at the crowdsourcer, even when some workers provide incorrect information. This, in turn, requires careful design of the small tasks/queries by the taskmaster. The total number of queries posed to workers is defined as the system’s budget.

The paper investigates a fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of the inference at the crowdsourcer) by mapping the above crowdsourcing system to joint source–channel coding. In this mapping, the taskmaster is the transmitter, the crowdsourcer is the receiver/decoder, and the unreliable worker responses constitute a communication channel. Standard information-theoretic results then yield a lower bound on the budget required to achieve a desired fidelity level. This bound does not depend on the specific query-design scheme at the taskmaster or the decoding algorithm at the crowdsourcer. The authors further analyze a particular taskmaster scheme—k-ary incidence coding—and compare its performance with the information-theoretic lower bound under a specific channel model.

The parallel with the rate–distortion framework for unreliable workers is interesting and could inspire further use of information-theoretic tools in crowdsourcing. However, it is not clear that the presented results capture real systems with sufficient realism. One possible reason is the insistence on an exact mapping to a point-to-point communication problem to enable direct application of existing information-theoretic results with minimal adaptation.

It would be helpful for the authors to address the following:
1) The model appears to conflate worker reliability and skill into a single channel abstraction, and it assumes the taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers regardless of skill. In practice, workers often carry ratings that reflect their skill or past performance, and task assignment commonly depends on those ratings.
2) How realistic are the channel models used to capture worker unreliability? In particular, how do the models account for workers who understand the task but act adversarially to degrade fidelity?
