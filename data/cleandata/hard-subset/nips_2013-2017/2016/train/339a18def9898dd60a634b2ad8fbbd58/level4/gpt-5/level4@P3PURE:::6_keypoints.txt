- Paper derives an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget–fidelity trade-off for crowdsourcing using rate–distortion and channel-coding; derivation is careful and potentially novel.  
- Strength: clear, well-executed derivation of a fidelity-vs-budget lower bound.  
- Strength: principled framing linking crowdsourcing to classical information-theory results.  
- Major concern: novelty and practical impact are limited because the derivation follows standard information-theoretic tools and the bound may be loose in practice.  
- Major concern: unlike channel coding, crowdsourcing likely lacks the design freedom to realize arbitrary codebooks, so the capacity-based bound may overestimate achievable performance.  
- Major concern: crowdsourcing-specific constraints (e.g., cannot repeatedly query the same worker i.i.d., task/worker allocation limits) restrict admissible encoders and reduce error-correction opportunities beyond repetition.  
- Major concern: empirical relevance is unclear—no experiments show how close real algorithms come to the derived bound.  
- Suggestion: explicitly characterize implementability constraints on the mapping in equation (3) (worker models, task allocation, adaptivity, no repeated queries, etc.).  
- Suggestion: discuss how those constraints affect bound tightness and adapt the analysis to restricted encoder families for tighter bounds.  
- Suggestion: provide empirical comparisons or synthetic gap analyses between the bound and state-of-the-art algorithms.  
- Suggestion: clarify whether nontrivial coding gains beyond repetition/majority voting are possible under realistic models and how u^n would be designed.  
- Minor: in equation (8), the minimization should be over C, C′ subject to E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor: the proof of Theorem 4 should be elaborated for clarity.  
- Minor: in Theorem 4, clarify inequality directions and thresholds given ln(1−q) < 0—does ε_S exceed ẑε?  
- Minor: in equation (17), justify the appearance of H_M(E(ε)) or provide a citation for that step.
