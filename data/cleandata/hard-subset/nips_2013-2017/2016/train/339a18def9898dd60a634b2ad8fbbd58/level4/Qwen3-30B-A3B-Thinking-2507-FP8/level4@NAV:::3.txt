

The paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing through the lens of information theory, establishing the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While I found the insights and model itself highly interesting and potentially valuable for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I did not fully grasp the paper (see specific concerns below). I recommend emphasizing the high-level intuition and key takeaways from the results—particularly at the end of Section 3—more prominently, as well as providing a concise conclusion outlining actionable implications (acknowledging space constraints). Although the authors made commendable efforts to make the paper accessible to non-information-theory readers, the technical depth remained challenging to follow. I suggest restating most key results in crowdsourcing-specific terminology rather than retaining the channel/coding/distortion framework. Additionally, the related work should cite the parallel line of research on robust economic incentives for workers (typically termed "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"), which also addresses varying worker abilities and recent integrations with advanced aggregation methods. Minor points: Line 84: "is reviewed in" should be "are reviewed in"; Line 106: The distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" remains unclear (noting that "crowdsourcing" derives from "crowd" + "outsourcing," which may cause confusion regarding roles); Line 122: Correct the notation to "A_{A(X_1,X_2)}"; Lines 275–276: The phrase "In case..." is difficult to parse; Figure 2 provided limited insight; a more detailed caption is recommended.
