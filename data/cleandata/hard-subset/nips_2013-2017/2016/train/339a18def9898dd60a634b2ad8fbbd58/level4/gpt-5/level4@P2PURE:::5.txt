The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a target probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. The channel models considered are symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture worker errors and the spammer–hammer channel model SHC(q), in which a worker is a spammer with probability 1−q. The coding scheme is assumed to be nonadaptive. Two decision scenarios are analyzed: the crowdsourcer (who decides the value of each task after aggregating worker responses) either knows the skill levels of all workers or knows nothing about them. In both scenarios, the taskmaster (who distributes tasks) is assumed to have no knowledge of worker skills, so every worker is assigned the same number of tasks. Finally, the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum required rate—number of queries per task—for k-ary incidence coding, where the worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to the same or different breeds, under an SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well organized and clearly written.

However, there are no experiments or simulations showing how close commonly used schemes (e.g., on AMT) are to the derived limits. Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 is unnecessary or could be condensed; in their place, proof sketches for the main theorems would be more valuable. The work appears novel: to my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, there has been no prior information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem. The results seem practically useful, enabling designers to determine the minimum query rate required to achieve a given error probability and to inform pricing. I did not read the appendix, so I cannot comment on the proofs.
