1. Establishes information-theoretic lower bounds on minimum worker queries per task to achieve a target error probability in crowdsourcing.

2. Models workers as parallel independent channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMCs) for skilled workers and spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) for spammers.

3. Assumes nonadaptive coding and equal task assignment to every worker, reflecting practical constraints when skills are unknown.

4. Analyzes two scenarios: taskmaster knows worker skill levels, and taskmaster has no skill information.

5. Derives a necessary condition on the minimum query rate for k-ary incidence coding under an SHC(q) worker pool.

6. Paper is well-structured and clearly written, making technical contributions accessible.

7. Lack of empirical validation (experiments or simulations) is a notable limitation that weakens practical relevance.

8. Recommends condensing Section 2.4 and shortening or replacing Section 3.1 with concise proof sketches to improve flow and readability.

9. Claims novelty as the first information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, filling a gap with formal bounds.

10. Highlights practical value: enables designers to estimate minimal query rates, inform cost/pricing, and guide resource allocation when worker skills are unknown.

11. Central contribution provides achievable and necessary rate conditions under realistic worker models (SHC and SDMC).

12. k-ary incidence coding rate bound under SHC(q) is particularly valuable for practical implementation.

13. Minor suggested clarifications: relationship between taskmaster and crowdsourcer, motivation for k-ary incidence coding, and implications of the no-skill-knowledge case for deployment.

14. Reviewer did not check the appendix but finds core results sound and recommends acceptance with minor revisions.
