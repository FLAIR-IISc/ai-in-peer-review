The paper addresses the aggregation of crowd worker outputs using an information-theoretic framework and derives fundamental limits for the budget–fidelity tradeoff. I did not fully grasp the paper (see below), but I found the model and resulting insights to be novel and potentially useful for the design of crowdsourcing aggregation methods. I would have liked the high-level intuition arising from these results (e.g., the end of Section 3) to be more prominently emphasized, as well as a concluding summary of key takeaways (I recognize space is limited). While I appreciate the authors’ efforts to make the work accessible to readers not steeped in information theory, like myself, it was still quite challenging to follow. I recommend restating many of the results in the language of the crowdsourcing application itself rather than relying on channels, codes, and distortion terminology. In related work, please also mention the alternative (and increasingly convergent) line of research on robust economic incentives for workers (often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing”). Some of this literature considers heterogeneous worker abilities and, more recently, how to combine incentive schemes with more sophisticated aggregation procedures.
