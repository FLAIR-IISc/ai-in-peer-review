- Paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity tradeoff for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and channel coding; derivation is sound within that framework.  
- If this is the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, it could be a valuable contribution.  
- Novelty and practical relevance are limited: the derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound may be loose in practice.  
- Fundamental mismatch between channel coding and crowdsourcing: channel coding allows flexible codebook design, but crowdsourcing query mappings (eq.3) appear significantly constrained.  
- If many mappings in eq.3 are not implementable, the resulting channel capacity may be an overly loose upper bound.  
- Repetition-style coding (majority voting) is plausible, but it’s unclear how to design query sequences u^n to achieve stronger error correction.  
- Key constraint: you cannot repeatedly query the same worker with identical tasks without violating the i.i.d. assumptions in eqs.(1)/(2), since workers will likely repeat answers.  
- More generally, crowdsourcing imposes encoding constraints absent in standard channel coding, so the information-theoretic bound risks being overly optimistic.  
- Empirical evaluation should include comparisons between state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms and the derived theoretical bound.  
- Minor: In eq.(8), clarify minimization over C and C' subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor: Theorem 4’s proof needs more detail.  
- Minor: In Theorem 4, since ln(1−q)<0, clarify whether ε̂S is larger than ε̂.  
- Minor: In eq.(17), justify or cite the inclusion of H_M(E(ε)).
