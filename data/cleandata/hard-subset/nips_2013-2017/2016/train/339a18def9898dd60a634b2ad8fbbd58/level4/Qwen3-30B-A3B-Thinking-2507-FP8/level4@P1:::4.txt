

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy) in label crowdsourcing systems. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication problem within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework, where the taskmaster acts as a transmitter and the crowdsourcer as a receiver. Workers' imperfect skill levels are modeled as noisy channels, and the system's performance is analyzed through the lens of joint source-channel coding. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds on the budget required to achieve a desired fidelity level, and they analyze a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) to assess its performance relative to these bounds.

The paper makes a novel connection between crowdsourcing and information theory, which is particularly valuable for the NIPS community. By framing crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, the authors derive fundamental limits that are independent of specific query or inference schemes. This approach provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing systems. The derivation of Theorems 2 and 3, which establish the minimum number of queries required for a given error probability under different scenarios (unknown vs. known worker skill levels), represents a significant contribution to the field. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding (Section 4) further demonstrates how specific query schemes perform relative to these theoretical limits.

The paper's strengths include its clear theoretical framework, well-motivated problem formulation, and the interesting application of information theory to a practical machine learning problem. The connection to existing work (e.g., Dawid-Skene algorithm, majority voting) is appropriately contextualized, and the paper makes a strong case for the theoretical value of this approach. The authors effectively demonstrate how their framework can guide the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems.

However, several concerns warrant attention. First, the paper assumes all workers receive the same number of queries regardless of skill level, which may not reflect realistic crowdsourcing platforms where workers often have reputation scores or skill ratings. The authors should clarify how their framework would accommodate systems where taskmasters can selectively assign tasks to higher-skilled workers. Second, the channel models (MSC and SHC) may not fully capture the complexity of worker behavior. In particular, the SHC model treats workers as either "spammers" or "hammers," but real crowdsourcing systems often contain workers with varying degrees of reliability, not just binary categories. The paper would benefit from discussing how their framework might extend to more nuanced worker models.

The numerical results in Section 4.2 (Figure 2) are somewhat unclear. The reviewer requests clarification on what the k=1 case represents (it seems to correspond to direct labeling queries, not pairwise comparisons), and the labeling of curves in the figure could be improved. The paper states on page 6, line 246 that it assumes knowledge of P(B(X)), but this assumption should have been explicitly stated earlier in the paper. Additionally, the paper contains several typographical errors: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1a" (page 4, line 135), and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1b" (page 4, line 135). The authors repeatedly use "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" without specific numbering, which is confusing; these terms should be used only when referring to formally defined theorems, lemmas, or corollaries.

The paper's main contribution lies in establishing fundamental information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing, which provides a valuable theoretical benchmark for evaluating existing and future crowdsourcing systems. However, the practical applicability of these limits could be strengthened by addressing the concerns about worker skill modeling and query allocation. The authors' claim that their bounds are "independent of the query scheme" is theoretically sound but may require more discussion of how these bounds translate to practical implementation.

In summary, this paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by applying information theory to crowdsourcing, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The framework is well-motivated and the technical results are sound. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the concerns about worker skill modeling, clarifying the numerical results, and improving the presentation of theorems and figures. The work has potential to inspire future research on applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing, but its current form would benefit from more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical framework connecting crowdsourcing to information theory
- Rigorous derivation of fundamental limits
- Clear analysis of k-ary incidence coding
- Strong connection to relevant prior work in crowdsourcing

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations
- Assumptions about worker skill modeling may not reflect reality
- Numerical results and figures need clarification
- Some technical details (e.g., knowledge of P(B(X))) are not sufficiently explained

Overall, this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution that would be strengthened by addressing the concerns raised above. It demonstrates significant potential to advance our understanding of crowdsourcing systems, but requires clarification on several points before it can be confidently accepted.
