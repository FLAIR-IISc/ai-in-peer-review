

The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to establish lower bounds on the required number of queries per task to achieve a specified probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, with symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) representing worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) capturing scenarios where a worker acts as a spammer with probability 1−q. The coding scheme is assumed nonadaptive. The analysis considers two distinct cases: (1) when the crowdsourcer (who aggregates worker responses to determine task values) knows all worker skill levels, and (2) when the crowdsourcer knows nothing about worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks to workers) is assumed unaware of worker skills, resulting in each worker receiving an equal number of tasks. The paper derives a necessary condition on the minimum required rate—defined as the number of queries per task—for k-ary incidence coding (where a worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to similar or different categories) under a SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, it lacks experimental or simulation results demonstrating how current crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) compare to the derived bounds. Section 2.4 could be condensed, and Section 3.1 may be omitted or shortened; proof sketches for the theorems would be more valuable. The work appears novel, with the authors correctly asserting that no prior information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing exists. The results are highly useful, enabling crowdsourcing designers to determine the minimum rate required to achieve a target error probability, which can inform project pricing. I did not review the appendix, so I cannot comment on the proofs.
