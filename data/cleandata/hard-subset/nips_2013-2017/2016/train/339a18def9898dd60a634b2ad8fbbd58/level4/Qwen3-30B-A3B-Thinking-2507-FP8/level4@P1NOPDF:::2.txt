

The authors present an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, deriving fundamental asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item. They model the problem as joint source-channel coding (JSCC) with a random channel state, where the channel state (representing worker skill levels) is unknown at the encoder (taskmaster) but may be known at the decoder (crowdsourcer). The item labels form an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster acts as the encoder, crowd workers correspond to multiple channel uses, and the crowdsourcer serves as the decoder. Using established JSCC results, they derive asymptotic query complexity bounds. They also establish a lower bound on the error probability for "k-ary incidence coding" under their model. Overall, the paper offers a novel JSCC perspective on crowdsourcing, though the model and arguments warrant refinement.  

**Major concerns:**  
1. **Source-channel separation justification.** The authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem (Theorems 2–3) without addressing whether it holds when the channel state is unknown at the encoder. While separation likely remains valid in this setting, the authors must explicitly justify this (e.g., by citing or proving the extension of the separation theorem to random channel states with encoder ignorance). Omitting this weakens the theoretical foundation.  

2. **Missing side information.** The model assumes the taskmaster and crowdsourcer only observe item labels, ignoring access to the original data (e.g., images, text). In practice, both parties typically have this side information, which could significantly improve performance. Crucially, side information at both encoder and decoder (or even just the decoder) can reduce the required query rate. The authors should discuss how incorporating this side information would alter their bounds, as the current model may be unrealistically restrictive.  

3. **Misstatement of lower bounds.** In Section 4.2, the authors claim "the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow." This is misleading: a *smaller* lower bound (not an "improved" bound) indicates a *worse* error probability guarantee. The bound decreases as k and q increase, meaning the code’s performance *could* improve (i.e., error probability might decrease), but the bound itself becomes looser. The phrasing must be corrected to avoid confusion.  

**Additional note:** As another reviewer noted, the asymptotic analysis, while theoretically interesting, has limited practical utility. A nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be more valuable for real-world crowdsourcing. The authors should consider recent nonasymptotic JSCC results, such as Kostina and Verdu’s work on finite-blocklength converses (*IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory*, 2013), which provides tighter bounds applicable to practical scenarios.  

**Summary:** The paper makes a promising initial step in applying JSCC to crowdsourcing, but the theoretical claims require stronger justification (separation theorem, side information) and clearer technical exposition (bound interpretation). Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution.
