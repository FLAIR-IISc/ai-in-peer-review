This paper studies the aggregation of worker outputs in crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens and characterizes fundamental limits of the budget–fidelity tradeoff. Although I did not fully follow every technical detail, I found the formulation compelling and the potential implications for the design of aggregation schemes significant.

Strengths
- Novel, principled framing: Casting aggregation as a rate–distortion-like problem with explicit fidelity constraints is original and yields clear theoretical limits.
- Generality: The framework appears flexible enough to model heterogeneous workers and multiple aggregation strategies.
- Potential impact: If translated into design guidelines, the results could inform practical choices of redundancy and budget allocation.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Accessibility: Despite the authors’ efforts, the exposition remains difficult for readers not steeped in information theory. Much of the core content is expressed solely in terms of channels, codes, and distortions, which obscures the crowdsourcing implications.
- Limited high-level intuition: The qualitative insights (e.g., end of Section 3) are valuable but too buried. A concise set of takeaways would help non-experts understand what to do differently in practice.
- Insufficient connection to related work in incentives: The paper omits a complementary line of research on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing, including models with heterogeneous abilities and methods that combine incentives with sophisticated aggregation.

Suggestions for improving the paper
- Restate key results in crowdsourcing terms: For each main theorem/corollary, provide a plain-language paraphrase. For example: “To achieve error ≤ ε with workers of reliability parameter p under budget B, the minimum number of labels per task scales as …” Clarify the mapping between “distortion” and task error metrics (e.g., misclassification rate), and between “rate” and standard crowdsourcing costs (e.g., labels or dollars per task).
- Add a succinct design recipe: A short subsection or boxed summary translating the limits into actionable guidance. For example:
  1) Given a target error ε and worker reliability distribution, compute the minimum redundancy per task. 
  2) If worker reliabilities vary, allocate additional labels adaptively to uncertain tasks. 
  3) Under budget B, the optimal tradeoff suggests stopping rules when marginal fidelity gains diminish.
- Clarify the model and terminology early: The distinction between “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” was confusing. Please define roles precisely and motivate why both are needed. If “taskmaster” is an internal controller or code designer, say so explicitly and relate it to common pipelines (requester, platform, workers).
- Provide a running example: Walk through a simple binary labeling task with two worker classes (reliable vs. noisy) and show how the theory predicts the required redundancy and budget to reach a target error.
- Expand on robustness: Discuss how sensitive the limits and recommended designs are to misspecified worker models, adversarial behavior vs. random noise, and task difficulty heterogeneity. If the theory assumes known worker reliabilities, suggest practical estimation or calibration procedures.
- Strengthen related work: Please cite and situate your contributions with respect to crowdsourcing mechanism design and incentive-compatible labeling (e.g., peer prediction, gold-standard calibration, payments that elicit effort and truthful reporting). Recent work that blends incentives with advanced aggregation should be acknowledged, and contrasts drawn between incentive limits and your information-theoretic limits.
- Improve figures and captions: Figure 2 was hard to interpret. Label axes and parameters clearly, explain what curves represent, specify fixed vs. varying quantities, and tie each visual to a specific takeaway aligned with theorems.

Questions for the authors
- Does the optimal tradeoff depend critically on knowing the worker reliability distribution, or can it be approached with online estimation? 
- How do the limits change under adaptive task assignment (e.g., routing harder tasks to better workers)?
- Can the framework accommodate strategic workers whose effort depends on payment, and how might that interact with your limits?

Minor comments and line edits
- Line 84: “is reviewed in” → “are reviewed in.”
- Line 106: Clarify the distinction between crowdsourcer and taskmaster; the current description is confusing given the common meaning of “outsourcing.”
- Line 122: Typo in “A A(X1, X2).”
- Lines 275–276: The sentence beginning “In case…” is hard to parse; please rewrite for clarity.
- Figure 2: Add a more detailed caption explaining axes, parameters, and the main qualitative message.

Overall evaluation
The paper offers a fresh and potentially influential perspective by establishing fundamental limits for crowdsourced aggregation. However, its impact would be much greater with clearer exposition, stronger translation of theory to practice, and integration with the incentives literature. I encourage acceptance contingent on significant improvements to accessibility and practical framing.
