This paper models crowdsourced labeling within an information-theoretic framework. Each worker is represented as a noisy channel, with two specific models considered: (i) an M-ary symmetric memoryless channel without feedback, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel. The labeling problem is thus recast as a coding problem over these channels, where the crowdsourcing budget corresponds to the code rate. Within this setup, the authors analyze the trade-off between budget (rate) and fidelity (distortion), deriving bounds on achievable performance. They also study a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, in which each query asks a worker to jointly label a subset of k items; the scheme’s performance is characterized within the same rate–distortion perspective.

I found the paper interesting, particularly the clean mapping from crowdsourcing to joint source–channel coding and the use of rate–distortion tools to quantify budget–fidelity trade-offs. However, this work lies outside my primary area of expertise, so I am not able to provide detailed technical comments or a thorough assessment of the proofs and tightness of the bounds, nor of the comparative merits of k-ary incidence coding relative to alternative aggregation or inference methods.
