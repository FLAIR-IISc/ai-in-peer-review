- Models crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding: labels = source, taskmaster = encoder, workers = noisy parallel channels with skill-level states, crowdsourcer = decoder.  
- Derives asymptotic fundamental limits on query budget (rate) vs fidelity (distortion) using the source-channel separation theorem.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: worker skill levels unknown to decoder (SL-UK) and known to decoder (SL-CS).  
- Proposes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) and derives a lower bound on its error under a spammer-hammer worker model.  
- Strength: Novel application of JSCC to provide principled, algorithm-independent performance bounds for crowdsourcing.  
- Strength: Paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the communication-crowdsourcing analogy.  
- Weakness: Main theorems hinge on applying source-channel separation to channels with random states, but lack formal justification or citation for this non-trivial extension.  
- Weakness: Model omits side information (original items X) available to encoder/decoder, reducing realism and potentially altering fundamental limits.  
- Weakness: Results are asymptotic (L, n → ∞), limiting immediate practical relevance for finite datasets and budgets.  
- Recommendation: Provide a proof sketch or cite information-theoretic results that validate separation for random-state channels unknown to the transmitter.  
- Recommendation: Extend the model to include side information at encoder and/or decoder to reflect practical crowdsourcing settings.  
- Recommendation: Investigate finite-blocklength (non-asymptotic) analyses (e.g., Kostina & Verdú) to improve practical applicability.  
- Minor clarity: Rephrase the statement that kIC bounds “improve” with k and q to specify that required rate decreases or error-bound tightens.  
- Overall: Promising and original theoretical first step, but needs more rigorous justification and model/finite-length extensions to be a complete contribution.
