The paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. This offers a novel and potentially valuable perspective on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. While the paper presented a steep learning curve – particularly for those less familiar with information theory – the core insights and modeling approach are compelling. I believe highlighting the key takeaways and practical implications stemming from these results, particularly at the end of section 3, would significantly enhance the paper's impact. A more application-focused conclusion would also be beneficial.

The authors should consider restating the results using the terminology of crowdsourcing itself, rather than relying heavily on the language of channels, codes, and distortion. This would improve accessibility and clarity for a broader audience. Additionally, the related work section would benefit from acknowledging the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers – often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This research area also addresses varying worker abilities and the integration of incentives with advanced aggregation methods.

Specific comments: on line 84, "is reviewed in" should be "are reviewed in." On line 106, the distinction between the “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” was initially unclear; clarifying their roles would be helpful, especially given the combined meaning of “crowdsourcing.” On line 122, there is a typographical error: "A A(X1,X2)". Lines 275-276 contained a complex sentence that was difficult to parse. Figure 2 lacked sufficient detail in its caption, hindering its interpretability.

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work, though its accessibility could be improved. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a unique and promising approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis provides fundamental limits on performance, offering a valuable benchmark for practical systems.
*   **Potential Impact:** The insights could lead to more efficient and reliable crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Accessibility:** The heavy reliance on information-theoretic terminology makes the paper challenging to understand for non-experts.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** While theoretically sound, the paper lacks concrete guidance for practitioners on how to translate the results into practical system designs.
*   **Figure Clarity:** Figure 2 is not sufficiently informative.



I believe this work represents a significant contribution to the field, despite the accessibility challenges. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity and practical relevance concerns outlined above.
