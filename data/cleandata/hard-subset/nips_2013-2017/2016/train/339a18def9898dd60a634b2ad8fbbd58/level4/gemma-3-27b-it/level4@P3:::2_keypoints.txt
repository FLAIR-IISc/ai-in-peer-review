- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, modeling imperfect workers as noisy channels and deriving information-theoretic lower bounds on queries for a target fidelity.
- Considers both unknown and known worker skill scenarios, deriving bounds for each.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a concrete query scheme and provides a performance bound.
- Paper is clear, well-written, and presents a novel information-theoretic perspective on crowdsourcing.
- Asymptotic bounds provide a theoretical baseline but may not translate to finite, practical settings.
- Heavy reliance on established information-theory results (e.g., source-channel separation) without full justification for unknown channel state.
- Assumes i.i.d. data sources, an assumption that may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing and whose violations are not discussed.
- Lacks non-asymptotic/finite-blocklength analysis (e.g., Kostina and Verdú-style results) to improve practical relevance.
- Modeling that the taskmaster/crowdsourcer only has access to labels (not original data) is restrictive and unrealistic.
- Query cost and pricing variations are noted but not fully integrated into the theoretical analysis.
- Theoretical results are insightful and could inspire new algorithms and system designs.
- Paper primarily applies existing information-theory tools rather than introducing major new theoretical contributions.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions to justify source-channel separation applicability, acknowledge asymptotic limitations, and consider incorporating side information.
