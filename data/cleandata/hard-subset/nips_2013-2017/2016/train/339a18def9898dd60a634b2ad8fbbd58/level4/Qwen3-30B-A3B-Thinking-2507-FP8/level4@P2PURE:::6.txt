

This work establishes an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of the information-theoretic bound is technically sound. If this represents the first application of rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem to crowdsourcing, it could constitute a meaningful contribution. However, the novelty and practical significance of the paper remain questionable, as the bound derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound risks being overly loose. The core issue lies in the lack of design freedom for the channel input sequence \(u^n\) compared to standard channel coding. In channel coding, extensive freedom exists in designing codebooks (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, convolutional codes, Reed-Solomon codes, LDPC codes), but it is unclear whether equivalent freedom applies to \(u^n\) in equation (3). Specifically, almost all mappings from \(B(X)^n\) to \(U^{\sum m_i}\) (excluding degenerate cases) should be valid or implementable codebooks for the channel capacity to serve as a meaningful bound; otherwise, the capacity may be loose. While a repetition code (with majority voting as natural decoding) is feasible in crowdsourcing, it is unclear how to design \(u^n\) to enhance error correction beyond this. Crucially, a fundamental distinction exists between channel coding and crowdsourcing: in crowdsourcing, assigning the same query to the same worker multiple times violates the iid assumption in equations (1) or (2), as the worker would likely repeat the same answer. In contrast, communication channels assume iid behavior and allow repeated use of the same query. This is one example, but the crowdsourcing problem likely imposes significant constraints on the set of valid encoding schemes in equation (3), unlike channel coding where the encoding mapping \(\{0,1\}^{nR} \to \{0,1\}^n\) has minimal constraints. Consequently, the proposed information-theoretic bound likely suffers from looseness due to these unaccounted constraints. Finally, a comparison between the derived bound and the performance of state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the paper.
