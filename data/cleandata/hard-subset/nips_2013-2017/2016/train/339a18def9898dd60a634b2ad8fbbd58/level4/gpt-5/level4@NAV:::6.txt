This paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity trade-off for label crowdsourcing by leveraging rate–distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of the bound is sound. If this is indeed the first application of rate–distortion theory and the channel coding theorem to crowdsourcing, the contribution could be reasonably significant. That said, the novelty and practical impact remain uncertain: the derivation appears relatively straightforward, and the resulting bound risks being quite loose.

The central concern is that, unlike classical channel coding where there is substantial freedom in designing the codebook/codewords (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, convolutional codes, RS codes, LDPC codes), it is unclear whether an analogous degree of freedom exists in specifying the channel input sequence u^n for crowdsourcing. In equation (3), nearly all mappings from B(X)^n to U^{(\sum m_i)} (excluding degenerate cases) would need to be valid/implementable “codebooks”; otherwise, channel capacity may serve only as a loose upper bound. While in crowdsourcing one can emulate repetition coding with majority voting as a natural decoder, beyond this it is unclear how to construct u^n to enhance error-correcting capability. In particular, a key discrepancy with channel coding is that we cannot present the same query to the same worker multiple times without violating the i.i.d. assumption in equations (1) or (2): the same worker would likely produce the same response to the same query. By contrast, in communication channels we often assume i.i.d. behavior and can reuse the same input multiple times on the same channel. This is just one example; more generally, the crowdsourcing setting likely imposes substantial constraints on equation (3), unlike channel coding where the mapping {0,1}^{nR} -> {0,1}^n is only lightly constrained. Consequently, there should be important restrictions on feasible encoding schemes in equation (3), and these restrictions may render the proposed information-theoretic bound quite loose. Finally, it would strengthen the work to compare the performance of state-of-the-art methods against the derived bound.
