This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem to study the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy) under worker unreliability. The taskmaster (encoder) designs queries; workers act as noisy channels with varying skill; the crowdsourcer (decoder) aggregates responses to infer labels or cluster assignments. Using standard information-theoretic tools, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum query rate required to achieve a target error under different assumptions about knowledge of worker skill. They then analyze a specific query design, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which asks workers to group k items into clusters, and compare its performance to the information-theoretic limits under a spammer–hammer channel model. The paper aims to provide general, scheme-agnostic limits and to show how structured queries like kIC can exploit inherent consistency constraints for error-correction.

Relation to prior work: Most crowdsourcing research at NIPS has focused on aggregation and inference—e.g., majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM, spectral/EM hybrids, and convex formulations for pairwise or triplet constraints. In contrast, this paper shifts perspective to fundamental limits via rate–distortion and channel capacity, yielding bounds independent of specific inference algorithms. The kIC setup is related to pairwise/ternary/triplet-query approaches in crowdsourced clustering, but here is analyzed through an information-theoretic lens.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean mapping of crowdsourcing to rate–distortion/communication-theoretic limits, yielding simple, interpretable lower bounds on the budget required for a target error rate.
- Clear separation of cases with unknown worker skill (SL-UK) versus skill known to the decoder (SL-CS), and insightful comparison of the resulting limits.
- Analysis of kIC under a spammer–hammer model, including an oracle lower bound and a practical “rule of thumb” for pricing as a function of k.
- Potential to stimulate cross-fertilization between information theory and crowdsourcing.

Weaknesses and questions:
1) Worker model and allocation. The paper appears to merge reliability and skill into a single channel parameter and assumes the taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers irrespective of skill. In many real platforms, workers carry reputations or ratings, and taskmasters adaptively allocate more to trusted workers. Please clarify how the framework could incorporate heterogeneous, possibly known worker skill at the encoder and adaptive query allocation.

2) Channel realism and adversaries. How realistic are the MSC and spammer–hammer models for modern platforms? In particular:
- Workers may be partially informed and adversarial (e.g., colluding or strategically degrading fidelity). How would the bounds change under adversarial or Byzantine models?
- Task difficulty can interact with worker reliability; the model currently treats channels as memoryless and independent of the input except in kIC. Can the framework handle input-dependent error (e.g., harder items) or feedback/adaptive coding?

3) Numerical results and presentation. The explanation of the numerical results is not very clear, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 should be improved. Please explain what the k = 1 case represents. With k = 1, one cannot ask pairwise “same cluster?” questions; does k = 1 correspond to direct single-item labeling, and how is it instantiated under the chosen channel model?

4) Assumptions stated earlier. On page 6, line 246, the paper assumes knowledge of P(B(X)). This is a strong modeling choice and should be stated clearly in the problem setup (earlier in the paper), along with discussion of sensitivity to misspecification or unknown priors.

Clarity and presentation:
- Figure references: on page 4, line 135, “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b,” and “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c.”
- Consistency in terminology: The paper repeatedly uses “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” in generic discussion without numbers. Use lowercase “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” unless referring to a numbered result.
- Improve figure axes/legends and explicitly tie each plotted curve to the analytical expression (e.g., Corollary 1 vs. Theorem 4). Briefly state the takeaways from the plots in the caption and the text.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The derivations appear to follow standard information-theoretic arguments and are sound under the stated assumptions. However, several assumptions—identical query allocation, simple channel models, known priors, independence—limit direct applicability. Please more explicitly discuss the scope and limitations of the bounds and what would break under more realistic settings.
- Clarity: The high-level structure is clear, but some explanations (especially numerical results) and figure references need refinement. Define key assumptions (e.g., P(B(X))) early.
- Originality: Framing crowdsourcing via rate–distortion and deriving scheme-agnostic lower bounds is novel and interesting relative to aggregation-centric prior work.
- Significance: The results provide useful baselines and may inform query design and pricing, but practical impact depends on extending the framework to more realistic, possibly adaptive and adversarial settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and elegant theoretical framing yielding clean bounds.
- Bridges two research communities and could inspire new work.
- Provides insight into structured queries (kIC) and pricing heuristics.

Cons against acceptance:
- Modeling assumptions may be too restrictive for real systems (homogeneous allocation, simple worker channels, known priors).
- Limited empirical or simulation evidence connecting the bounds to realistic datasets or modern crowdsourcing behaviors.
- Presentation issues (figure labeling, clarity of numerical discussion).

Suggestions:
- Discuss how to incorporate adaptive allocation, known worker ratings, and input-dependent reliability.
- Comment on robustness to prior misspecification and partial knowledge of skill at the encoder/decoder.
- Clarify the k = 1 baseline and improve figure annotations and captions.
