This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing. Leveraging concepts from rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem, the authors derive a theoretical bound on this tradeoff. The derivation of the bound is technically sound. If this is the first work to apply this theoretical framework to the crowdsourcing problem, it represents a notable conceptual contribution to the field.

However, the novelty and practical significance of the work are less clear, primarily due to concerns about the tightness of the derived bound. The central issue stems from the paper's analogy between crowdsourcing and channel coding. In classical channel coding, one has considerable freedom to design codebooks (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, LDPC codes) to construct channel inputs that approach the theoretical capacity limit. It is unclear whether a similar degree of freedom exists in the crowdsourcing setting.

The primary limitation of this analogy lies in the fundamental operational constraints of crowdsourcing, which are not present in communication channels. The set of permissible "encoding" schemes—the mapping from tasks to worker queries as described in equation (3)—is likely much more restricted. For example, a key difference is that one cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times. Doing so would be impractical and would violate the i.i.d. assumption underpinning the model in equations (1) and (2), as a worker is likely to provide the same, correlated answer. This contrasts sharply with a memoryless communication channel, where repeated transmissions of the same signal are independent and form the basis of simple error-correction schemes like repetition coding.

This example highlights a broader point: the crowdsourcing "codebook" is constrained by factors like task design, worker availability, and the need to maintain statistical independence. In channel theory, an encoding function has few, if any, such structural constraints. Consequently, the achievable performance in a real-world crowdsourcing system may be severely limited by these practical constraints. This raises the concern that the proposed information-theoretic bound, which implicitly assumes the existence of an optimal (unconstrained) code, may be too loose to serve as a useful benchmark for practical algorithms.

To address this concern and better demonstrate the practical value of the derived bound, it would be highly beneficial for the authors to compare the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms against it. Such an empirical analysis would help quantify the gap between the theoretical limit and current practice, providing valuable insight into how much room for improvement exists and how tight the bound truly is.
