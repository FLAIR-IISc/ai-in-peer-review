This paper models labeling via crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens and derives asymptotic budget–fidelity tradeoffs. The authors cast the workflow as joint source–channel coding (JSCC) with random channel states: labels form an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster is the encoder; workers correspond to parallel, noisy channel uses with random, worker-dependent states (skill levels); and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. Two regimes are analyzed: worker skills unknown to the encoder and decoder (SL-UK) versus known to the decoder (SL-CS). Using separation-based arguments, the paper gives lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability under an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) worker model. The paper also studies a concrete querying strategy, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and, under a spammer–hammer channel (SHC), derives a converse bound on the achievable error for kIC and offers a pricing heuristic.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to the literature on aggregation and inference in crowdsourcing (e.g., Dawid–Skene EM models; spectral/graph-based methods; majority voting) by reframing the entire pipeline as an information transmission problem. While past work focuses on inference algorithm performance and task allocation, this paper pursues ultimate limits on achievable fidelity given a budget, akin to capacity/rate–distortion results in information theory. The analysis draws on classical results (e.g., source–channel separation, capacity of discrete memoryless channels) and complements algorithmic work in NIPS and elsewhere with converse-style statements.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of crowdsourcing as JSCC that yields interpretable budget–fidelity limits.
- Distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS regimes clarifies how knowledge of worker skills impacts achievable rates.
- kIC analysis under the SHC model provides additional insight into how query design affects effective channel reliability and suggests a simple pricing rule of thumb.
- Writing is generally clear and the paper is well organized.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
1) Separation with random channel state: The main bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) appear to rely on standard source–channel separation. However, the worker pool introduces random channel states that are unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. The paper should justify that separation (and the associated capacity/rate–distortion expressions) holds under the stated state information assumptions, or cite a precise result establishing this. If separation does not hold in general, the paper should state the additional conditions under which the presented bounds are valid.

2) Side information at encoder/decoder: The model treats the encoder as observing only labels and the decoder as observing only worker outputs. In many crowdsourcing pipelines, both taskmaster and crowdsourcer have access to the original items (images, text, etc.), which can serve as side information at the encoder and/or decoder. Decoder (or common) side information can strictly improve rate–distortion performance. The paper would be more realistic and complete if it discussed models with side information (e.g., Wyner–Ziv–type settings) and their implications for budget–fidelity limits, even if full analysis is deferred.

3) Asymptotic focus: All results are asymptotic in blocklength. For practical crowdsourcing, nonasymptotic characterizations would be more informative. Please consider finite-blocklength converses/approximations for JSCC (e.g., Kostina and Verdú, IEEE Trans. IT, 2013) to provide sharper guidance on the number of queries needed at realistic dataset sizes.

4) Clarify statements about k and q: In Sec. 4.2 and Fig. 2, the text says the “lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow.” What improves is the achievable performance for a fixed budget, reflected by a decrease in the converse bound (i.e., a smaller minimum rate is necessary), not the tightness of the bound itself. Please rephrase to avoid ambiguity, or contrast with any available achievability results to demonstrate tightness.

5) Modeling assumptions and scope: Several assumptions are strong and should be made explicit in the main text: i.i.d. labels; independence across workers and tasks; non-adaptive querying; uniform random task assignment; and for kIC, N = 2 in the main bound. A brief discussion of how violations (e.g., class imbalance, adaptive task allocation, correlated worker errors, more than two classes) might affect the conclusions would broaden applicability.

6) Empirical or numerical illustration: Beyond the plots of the converse/IT limits, it would be valuable to include achievability baselines (e.g., majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM methods) under the same worker models to show the gap to the limits and to assess the practical value of kIC relative to standard querying.

Quality: The technical arguments are sound in spirit but would benefit from a rigorous treatment of separation with random channel state and a clearer accounting of assumptions. The kIC converse under SHC is useful, though limited in scope.

Clarity: Generally well written. Suggested improvements: (i) explicitly list modeling assumptions; (ii) clarify the role of state information; (iii) rephrase the “improve” claim about k, q; (iv) clearly state where N = 2 is assumed.

Originality: Framing crowdsourcing as JSCC and deriving high-level budget–fidelity limits is a novel perspective relative to prior NIPS work focused on inference algorithms and task allocation.

Significance: Establishes conceptual limits that can guide system design and motivate future work on finite-blocklength bounds, side information, and adaptive schemes. Practical impact will grow if the asymptotic theory is complemented with nonasymptotic results and empirical validation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a clean, unified information-theoretic framework for budget–fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing.
- Highlights the role of worker skill knowledge and query design (kIC).
- Potential to influence both theory and practice by setting performance targets.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Reliance on asymptotic separation with random channel state is not fully justified; finite-blocklength relevance is limited.
- The side-information scenario common in practice is not modeled.
- kIC analysis is restricted (binary labels, SHC), and no achievability results are provided to gauge tightness.

Actionable recommendations:
- Justify or cite separation with channel state (unknown at encoder, possibly known at decoder).
- Discuss and, if possible, analyze decoder side information.
- Add a finite-blocklength perspective (e.g., via Kostina–Verdú) and empirical baselines.
- Clarify language around Fig. 2 and enumerate assumptions prominently.
