Paper presents an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity tradeoff for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion and channel coding; derivation is mathematically sound.  
If this is the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, the work could be a significant contribution.  
Novelty and practical value are questionable: derivation is straightforward and the bound may be too loose for practice.  
Primary concern: the analogy between crowdsourcing and channel coding is flawed because channel coding assumes wide freedom in designing encodings.  
Crowdsourcing likely lacks the coding flexibility (many implementable mappings from labels to queries), so many theoretical codebooks may be unrealizable.  
Repetition codes (querying multiple workers, majority voting) map naturally to crowdsourcing, but sophisticated error-correcting codes do not have clear implementations.  
A key constraint: one cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times without violating i.i.d. assumptions, unlike memoryless communication channels.  
These fundamental constraints mean the crowdsourcing encoding map is far more restricted than standard channel coding, reducing the bound’s practical tightness.  
Recommendation: empirically compare state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms to the theoretical limit to quantify bound tightness and relevance.
