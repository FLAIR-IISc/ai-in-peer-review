The paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, providing lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a specified probability of error. The workers are represented as parallel independent channels, with two types of channels considered: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMC) to model worker errors and spammer-hammer channel models SHC(q) to account for spammers with a probability of 1-q. The code is assumed to be non-adaptive. The analysis considers two cases: one where the crowdsourcer has knowledge of the workers' skill levels and another where no such knowledge is available. In both cases, the taskmaster is assumed to be unaware of the workers' skills, resulting in an equal task assignment for all workers. Furthermore, a necessary condition for the minimum required rate is provided for k-ary incidence coding with an SHC(q) worker pool.

The paper is well-organized and well-written. However, it lacks experimental or simulation results to demonstrate the gap between current schemes and the proposed limits, such as those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be condensed or removed, as proof sketches for the theorems are more crucial. The paper's novelty lies in its information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem, which has not been explored previously. This work is particularly useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, as it provides a means to determine the minimum required rate to accomplish a task with a specified probability of error, ultimately aiding in project pricing. Unfortunately, the appendix was not reviewed, so the proof's validity cannot be confirmed.
