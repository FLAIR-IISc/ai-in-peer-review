- Paper applies rate–distortion and channel coding to crowdsourcing to derive budget–fidelity limits; derivations are careful.
- If this is the first such application, it could be a meaningful contribution, but novelty and practical impact are uncertain.
- Main concern: derivations are relatively direct and the resulting theoretical limits may be very loose.
- Central technical issue: unlike classical channel coding, it’s unclear whether the same freedom to design encoder mappings u^n exists in crowdsourcing.
- Nearly all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(∑ m_i) would need to be valid/implementable encoders; if not, capacity-style bounds risk being too weak.
- Repetition-like queries and majority voting are feasible, but beyond repetition it’s unclear how to design u^n to provide error-correcting capability.
- Specific discrepancy: repeating identical queries to the same worker breaks iid assumptions (workers would repeat answers), unlike iid channel uses in standard coding models.
- More broadly, crowdsourcing likely imposes substantive constraints on feasible encoders, narrowing the encoding space and potentially loosening the proposed limits.
- Empirical validation is needed: compare state-of-the-art algorithmic performance against the derived theoretical limits.
- Minor corrections: in (8) the minimization should be over C, C′ with E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.
- Minor: the proof of Theorem 4 should be elaborated further.
- Minor: clarify in Theorem 4 whether ε_S is larger than ̂ε given ln(1 − q) < 0.
- Minor: justify the appearance of H_M(E(ε)) in (17) or provide an appropriate citation.
