Summary
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic bounds on the achievable trade-off between budget (queries per item) and fidelity (error/distortion). The dataset labels B(X) are treated as the source; workers are modeled as noisy channels, with two concrete channel families: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC), representing workers of varying but stationary reliability; and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC(q)), where workers are either perfect (“hammers”) with probability q or respond uniformly at random (“spammers”). The main theoretical results are rate–distortion style lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error rate under two scenarios: SL-UK (worker skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer) and SL-CS (skill levels known to the crowdsourcer). The paper also studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query asks a worker to partition k items into same/different bins, yielding a form of built-in error correction by disallowing invalid partitions. For kIC with an SHC worker pool, the authors derive a necessary condition on the rate to achieve a target error and discuss a pricing heuristic that scales with k.

Relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere, the paper complements algorithmic aggregation methods (e.g., majority voting; Dawid–Skene EM; spectral/graph-based methods and message passing; Karger–Oh–Shah budget-optimal allocation; convex relaxations for clustering) by providing fundamental information-theoretic limits that any approach must respect. The kIC analysis connects to work on pairwise/ternary queries for clustering with missing data.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation and bounds follow standard rate–distortion and channel capacity arguments, with appropriate caveats about asymptotics (large block length and dataset size). The SL-UK vs. SL-CS comparison is insightful and technically consistent: when skills are unknown, performance reflects the capacity of an “average worker”; when known to the decoder, performance reflects the average of worker capacities.
- Assumptions: Independence across worker responses and tasks, non-adaptive querying, and memoryless channels are clean but idealized. The SHC(q) oracle decoding bound (Theorem 4) is clearly labeled as a necessary condition, but the “static feedforward” induced by k (affecting the channel) could be explained more carefully.
- Empirical support: The paper focuses on theory. The numerical section is limited but adequate for illustrating trends. It would help to include a simple numerical example translating a bound into “queries per item” for typical q, k, and target error.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written but remains difficult to follow for readers without an information theory background. The contributions would be more accessible if key theorems were restated in crowdsourcing terms (queries per item versus error rate), with brief intuition and small numerical examples. The distinction between “taskmaster” (encoder) and “crowdsourcer” (decoder) needs clearer, earlier definition. Figure 2 needs a more informative caption and explanation of axes, parameters, and takeaways. A concise concluding section summarizing actionable implications would be valuable.

Originality
Casting budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing as a source–channel coding problem and providing explicit rate lower bounds under MSC/SHC models is novel and timely. The analysis of kIC as a code with inherent error correction and the derived pricing heuristic are fresh contributions.

Significance
The results provide theoretical benchmarks that can guide system designers in setting budgets and choosing query types (e.g., selecting k in kIC) given quality targets. Even if practical systems violate some assumptions (adaptivity, dependence, heterogeneous costs), these limits offer a useful compass and motivate future work on algorithms that approach the bounds.

Strengths
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framework yielding interpretable rate lower bounds.
- Insightful comparison of known- vs. unknown-skill scenarios (SL-CS vs. SL-UK).
- kIC formalization as a code with built-in invalid-response filtering; necessary condition under SHC(q).
- Practical angle via a simple pricing heuristic for kIC.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Accessibility: Restate theorems in application terms. For example, immediately after Theorem 2/3, give a sentence such as: “For N=2, uniform labels, and M=2 response options, to achieve 5% error with average worker error ε, one needs at least R ≥ … queries per item.” Provide 1–2 worked examples.
- Intuition: After each theorem, briefly explain the key driver (e.g., “unknown skills reduce effective capacity by replacing E[H(ε)] with H(E[ε])”).
- Modeling clarity: Define, early and consistently, X, B(X), N (number of classes), M (response choices), U/V (query/response alphabets), n (block length), L (dataset size). Clarify the mapping from kIC to M and the effective alphabet size after removing invalid partitions.
- Practical cost normalization: When comparing kIC across different k, be explicit about how time/effort costs scale, and how “queries per item” are normalized to reflect those costs (Appendix E could be summarized in the main text).
- Adaptivity and worker selection: Discuss whether adaptivity or worker selection could change the fundamental limits or only the constants. Clarify if bounds extend to heterogeneous query allocations.
- Mechanism design: Please add related work on incentive-compatible crowdsourcing mechanisms (e.g., peer-prediction methods, bonus schemes with gold questions, and contests) and recent attempts to combine incentives with advanced aggregation. This literature also models heterogeneous worker abilities and could enrich the discussion of budget–quality trade-offs.
- Figure 2: Expand the caption to define all parameters, clarify which curves are information-theoretic limits vs. kIC bounds, and state the main takeaway (e.g., how increasing k and q shifts the curves).
- Notational/typo fixes and wording:
  - Line 84: “are reviewed in”.
  - Line 106: Clarify the roles of “taskmaster” vs. “crowdsourcer” early; perhaps rename to “query designer” and “aggregator/decoder”.
  - Line 122: “A(X1, X2)”.
  - Lines 275–276: Rewrite the sentence beginning “In case…” for readability; consider splitting into two sentences.
  - General: Ensure consistent use of N (labels) vs. M (response options), and state when U=V and when invalid options are removed.

Pros for acceptance
- Provides principled, novel theoretical limits that can serve as benchmarks for the community.
- Offers a new lens on query design (kIC) with concrete implications for pricing and redundancy.
- Bridges information theory and crowdsourcing, relating to established NIPS work on aggregation.

Cons for acceptance
- Accessibility and presentation could be improved; some readers may struggle to extract actionable guidance.
- Assumptions (non-adaptive querying, independence, oracle knowledge in a bound) limit direct applicability; empirical validation is minimal.
- Related work on incentives/mechanism design is missing, leaving out an important dimension of budget–quality trade-offs.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and potentially impactful paper. With improved exposition, clearer crowdsourcing-oriented restatements of the results, and a brief discussion of incentives and practical costs, it would be a strong contribution.
