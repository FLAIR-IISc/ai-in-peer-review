The paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools, providing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While I found the insights and model presented to be very interesting and potentially useful for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I did not fully understand all aspects of the paper (detailed below). I believe the high-level intuition and practical implications stemming from the results – such as those discussed at the end of section 3 – could be more prominently highlighted, and a concluding summary of key takeaways would be beneficial, even given space constraints. 

Despite the authors’ commendable efforts to make the paper accessible to readers less familiar with information theory, I still found it challenging to follow in places. I recommend restating many of the results in the context of the crowdsourcing application itself, moving away from the terminology of channels, codes, and distortion. In the related work section, please include discussion of the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers – often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This work frequently addresses varying worker abilities and increasingly explores combinations of these incentives with more advanced aggregation methods.

Minor points: on line 84, “is reviewed in” should likely be “are reviewed in.” On line 106, the distinction between the “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” was unclear, perhaps due to the intuitive understanding of “crowdsourcing” as a combination of “crowd” and “outsourcing” occurring prior to worker involvement. On line 122, there appears to be a typographical error: it should read "A A(X1,X2)". Lines 275-276 contained a difficult-to-parse sentence structure (“In case…”). Finally, Figure 2 did not clearly convey its information; a more detailed caption would significantly improve its interpretability.
