This paper applies the tools of information theory to the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing, establishing the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. Although I found the technical details difficult to follow, the model itself presents an interesting and potentially useful perspective on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques.

The paper would be significantly improved by more prominently highlighting the high-level intuition stemming from the results (e.g., at the end of Section 3) and by adding a conclusion that summarizes the key takeaways. While I appreciate the authors' efforts to make the work accessible to readers not deeply familiar with information theory, it remains challenging for a non-expert. To improve clarity, I strongly recommend restating the main results in the context of the crowdsourcing application, rather than retaining the abstract language of channels, codes, and distortion.

The related work section should be expanded to discuss the alternate and converging line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, typically found under the terms "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This body of work is relevant as it also addresses varying worker abilities and, more recently, the integration of such incentives with sophisticated aggregation methods.
