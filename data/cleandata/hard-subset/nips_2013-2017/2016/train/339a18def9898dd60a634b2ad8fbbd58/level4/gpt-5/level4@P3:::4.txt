Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models budget–fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens. A dataset X is associated with a latent discrete function B(X) (e.g., class labels or cluster indices). Workers are modeled as noisy channels; the taskmaster encodes queries (source coding) and the crowdsourcer decodes aggregated responses (channel decoding). The authors cast the design objective as a rate–distortion problem: minimize expected distortion (e.g., Hamming error) subject to a query budget, or equivalently minimize budget subject to a target distortion. Using the source–channel separation framework, they derive lower bounds on the minimum queries per item needed to achieve error ≤ ε under two settings: (i) worker skill levels unknown (SL-UK) and (ii) worker skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). Workers are modeled by either an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) with a skill parameter, or a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with spammer probability 1 − q.

Beyond these general lower bounds, the paper proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query shows k items and asks for within-query clustering. This yields implicit error-correction by ruling out invalid response patterns. For SHC and binary labels, the authors derive a necessary rate condition for kIC to achieve error ≤ ε (assuming an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers vs. hammers), and compare this to an information-theoretic lower bound specialized to SHC (Corollary 1). They also discuss approximate pricing guidance for different k via a cost-per-effective-constraint heuristic.

This work complements prior crowdsourcing research that focuses on inference/aggregation (majority voting; Dawid–Skene EM; spectral/EM hybrids) and task allocation (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; bandit survey formulations). It offers a unifying coding-theoretic perspective and budget lower bounds that are independent of specific inference algorithms.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A clean mapping of query design and aggregation to joint source–channel coding yields interpretable lower bounds on queries vs. accuracy.
- Clear separation of SL-UK vs. SL-CS scenarios, illuminating how knowledge of worker skills affects achievable budgets.
- kIC is a natural, practically motivated scheme that provides built-in consistency constraints; the analysis highlights its error-correction potential.
- Potentially useful guidance on pricing different query types and on how “hard” fidelity targets translate to minimal budgets.
- Results are stated in general form (arbitrary label alphabets, generic distortion), increasing potential applicability.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Modeling fidelity to real systems is limited. The MSC/SHC abstractions omit adversarial behavior, item difficulty, worker learning/fatigue, and dependence across responses.
- The SL-CS assumption of perfect knowledge of worker skills is strong; the “oracle decoder” used in the kIC bound amplifies this gap to practice.
- The framework assumes non-adaptive tasking with equal rate per worker when skills are unknown; real systems often exploit ratings or online performance to allocate adaptively.
- Rate–distortion results are asymptotic (large blocklength, large worker pools); finite-sample effects and task heterogeneity are not quantified.
- Numerical results are sparse; comparisons are limited to lower bounds rather than to practical algorithms (e.g., DS, spectral methods) or real datasets.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and grounded in standard information-theoretic results. The separation-based bounds are appropriate as benchmark lower limits, and the kIC analysis captures the structure-induced gains under SHC. That said, several assumptions (oracle identification, perfect SL-CS, iid/uniformity) should be more clearly stated up front, with discussion of robustness and finite-sample implications. Proof sketches or intuition would help readers connect the bounds to the modeling choices.

Clarity
The high-level narrative is clear, and the paper does a good job motivating kIC. However, several presentation issues impede readability:
- Define P(B(X)) and the assumption of iid items early (Section 2.1), as they are central to the bounds. The mention on page 6 (line 246) is too late.
- Clarify the meaning of k = 1 in Fig. 2 and in the text: for kIC, k = 1 degenerates to direct per-item labeling; pairwise incidence is not applicable.
- Improve the legend and axis labels in Fig. 2 (e.g., “queries per item,” “error probability ε,” specify k values, q, and M), and ensure curves are distinguishable.
- Ensure consistent notation for M (number of answer choices) and N (number of classes/clusters); highlight when U = V = M.
- Use “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” in lowercase in generic references, and reserve capitalized Theorem/Lemma/Corollary with numbers for specific statements.
- Fix figure cross-references: page 4 line 135 should read “Figure 1b” and “Figure 1c” (not 1a/1b).
- Minor typos and bibliography entries lack years or have formatting issues (e.g., publisher names, “th Conference”).

Originality
Applying rate–distortion and separation theorems to crowdsourcing’s budget–accuracy trade-offs is novel and timely. The kIC formalization and its error-correction viewpoint are original and practically relevant. While information-theoretic analyses exist for related inference/allocation problems, this paper provides a coherent, general benchmark.

Significance
As a conceptual and theoretical benchmark, the results are valuable: they set fundamental limits any system must respect, independent of algorithmic details. Practical impact will depend on bridging the gap between the idealized models and real-world platforms (adaptive allocation, partial skill knowledge, adversarial or strategic behavior, heterogeneous costs).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Worker heterogeneity and allocation: How would the bounds change if the taskmaster has access to ratings or online performance signals and can allocate non-uniformly (adaptive coding)? Can you bound the potential gains over uniform allocation?
- Adversarial/strategic workers: Can the framework incorporate a fraction of adversaries (e.g., flipping labels) or a contamination model, and how would the bounds shift?
- Unknown P(B(X)): In practice, class priors may be unknown or skewed. Can you extend the analysis to unknown priors (e.g., worst-case over priors) or to online estimation of priors?
- Finite-blocklength effects: Can you provide non-asymptotic bounds or scaling laws to guide budgets for realistic dataset sizes?
- Empirical validation: It would strengthen the paper to compare kIC (with a practical decoder) to DS/spectral baselines on synthetic and real AMT-like datasets, showing proximity to the lower bounds.
- kIC costs and pricing: Expand the cost model (Appendix E) to reflect cognitive load and platform pricing; discuss when higher k provides net gains given worker fatigue/dropout.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, principled theoretical framework with general lower bounds.
- Insightful analysis of kIC and its built-in error correction.
- Potential to influence how the community reasons about budgets and pricing.

Cons:
- Simplifying assumptions limit realism (oracle skill knowledge, SHC/MSC, non-adaptive allocation).
- Limited empirical or finite-sample validation.
- Some clarity and presentation issues (figures, notation, references).

Minor comments and edits
- Page 4 line 135: “Figure 1a → Figure 1b” and “Figure 1b → Figure 1c.”
- Ensure all theorems/lemmas/corollaries are numbered; use lowercase when referring generically.
- Clearly state the iid and prior assumptions in Section 2.1; define M and N once and use consistently.
- Clarify the interpretation of k = 1 in kIC and in Fig. 2.
- Improve Fig. 2 legends, label axes and parameters (k, q, M), and distinguish curves more clearly.
- Clean up references (complete author lists, years, venues, and publisher details).

Overall, this is an interesting and well-motivated theoretical contribution. Addressing the modeling and clarity issues, and adding empirical evidence or finite-sample guidance, would substantially strengthen the paper.
