The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework to model digital crowdsourcing, providing lower bounds on the required number of queries per task to achieve a specified probability of error. The workers are represented as parallel independent channels, with two types of channels considered: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMC) to model worker errors, and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to account for workers who may be spammers with probability 1-q. The coding scheme is assumed to be nonadaptive. The analysis considers two cases: one where the crowdsourcer, who determines the task value after collecting worker results, has knowledge of all workers' skill levels, and another where this information is unknown. In both cases, the taskmaster, responsible for distributing tasks to workers, is assumed to be unaware of worker skills, resulting in an equal task assignment to all workers. A necessary condition for the minimum required rate, specifically for k-ary incidence coding with an SHC(q) worker pool, is also provided. The paper is well-organized and well-written. However, it lacks experimental or simulation results to demonstrate the gap between current schemes and the proposed limits, such as those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). Some sections, such as 2.4 and 3.1, could be condensed, while proof sketches for the theorems would be more valuable. The paper appears to be novel, as it is the first to analyze the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective, as claimed. This framework can be useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, enabling them to determine the minimum rate required to accomplish a task with a given probability of error, which can aid in pricing such projects. Unfortunately, I did not review the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the proof.
