This paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing by applying the language and tools of information theory. The primary contribution is the characterization of the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff, framing the problem in terms of rate-distortion theory to connect the number of worker queries (budget) to the accuracy of the final aggregated result (fidelity).

The core strength of the paper is its novel and principled approach. Framing crowdsourcing aggregation through the lens of information theory offers a unique perspective that could be very valuable to the field. The model itself is interesting, and the theoretical results establishing the budget-fidelity tradeoff are potentially very useful for understanding the design space of crowdsourcing systems. I also appreciate the authors' effort to make the paper accessible to readers, like myself, who are not deeply versed in information theory.

However, despite these efforts, the paper remains quite difficult to follow for a non-expert. My main suggestion is to improve the paper's clarity and impact by translating the theoretical results back into the language of the application domain. While the formalism of channels, codes, and distortion is central to the derivation, the key insights would be more impactful if they were consistently rephrased in terms of worker models, aggregation rules, and error rates. This would help a broader audience understand the practical implications of the work.

Specific suggestions for improvement are as follows:

**1. Emphasize High-Level Intuition and Takeaways:**
The practical insights derived from the theoretical results are a key part of the contribution but are not highlighted prominently enough. For example, the discussion at the end of Section 3 contains valuable intuition that could be emphasized more in the introduction or abstract. Furthermore, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated conclusion section. Such a section should summarize the main takeaways for a practitioner or a researcher in the crowdsourcing field. What key principles for designing aggregation systems can be learned from these results?

**2. Broaden the Scope of Related Work:**
The related work section should acknowledge the alternate and increasingly convergent line of research on designing robust economic incentives for workers. This literature, often using terms like "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing," also deals with varying worker abilities and, more recently, has focused on how to combine these incentive structures with sophisticated aggregation methods. Situating this work in contrast to or in concert with that literature would provide a more complete picture of the field.

**3. Address Clarity in Specific Sections:**
*   The distinction between the "crowdsourcer" and the "taskmaster" (line 106) was confusing, particularly since "crowdsourcing" itself implies outsourcing to a crowd. Clarifying this terminology early on would be helpful.
*   The sentence on lines 275-276 ("In case...") was very difficult to parse and could be rewritten for clarity.
*   Figure 2 was difficult to interpret. A more detailed caption explaining what the axes represent in practical terms and what the key takeaway from the plot is would make the figure much more effective.

**Minor Comments:**
*   Line 84: "is reviewed in" should likely be "are reviewed in".
*   Line 122: There appears to be a typo in "A A(X1,X2)".

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting approach to a classic problem in crowdsourcing. The core ideas are promising, but the paper's potential impact is currently limited by its accessibility. By focusing on translating the information-theoretic results into practical crowdsourcing insights and improving the overall clarity of the exposition, the authors could produce a much stronger and more influential paper.
