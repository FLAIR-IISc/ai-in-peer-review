This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of digital crowdsourcing, establishing lower bounds on the number of queries required per task to achieve a specified error probability. The authors model workers as parallel, independent channels, utilizing symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker error and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to account for malicious or unengaged workers who respond randomly with probability 1-q. Importantly, the analysis assumes a non-adaptive coding scheme – the queries are determined before observing any responses.

The paper investigates two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer possesses complete knowledge of each worker’s skill level, and another where the crowdsourcer has no prior knowledge. In both scenarios, the taskmaster, responsible for task distribution, is assumed to be unaware of individual worker skills, assigning an equal number of tasks to each worker. A key result is a necessary condition, expressed as a minimum rate (queries per task), for achieving a desired performance level with k-ary incidence coding, specifically when employing a worker pool modeled with the SHC(q) channel. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, the lack of experimental validation or simulations is a significant drawback. It would be beneficial to see a comparison of existing crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk) against the derived theoretical limits, to understand the practical gap between current performance and optimal bounds. 

Regarding the presentation, section 2.4 feels somewhat lengthy and could benefit from concision. Similarly, section 3.1 appears either unnecessary or expandable; streamlining this section would improve the paper’s focus. Conversely, providing more detailed proof sketches for the core theorems would significantly enhance the paper’s accessibility and impact. Currently, the level of mathematical detail feels insufficient for a full understanding of the results without referring to the appendix (which I did not review).

The work appears novel; to the best of my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this is the first information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem. This is a valuable contribution. The established bounds are potentially very useful for designers of crowdsourcing systems, offering a means to determine the minimum query rate necessary to achieve a target error probability. This, in turn, could inform pricing strategies for such projects.

While the mathematical proofs were not assessed due to my lack of review of the appendix, the overall approach and presented results are promising.
