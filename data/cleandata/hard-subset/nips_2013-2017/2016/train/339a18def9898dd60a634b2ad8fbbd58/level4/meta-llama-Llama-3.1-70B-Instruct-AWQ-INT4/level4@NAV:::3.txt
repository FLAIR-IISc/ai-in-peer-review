The paper tackles the challenge of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing through the lens of information theory, shedding light on the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. Although I found certain aspects of the paper difficult to grasp (as discussed below), I appreciated the novel and potentially valuable perspective it offers on designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. I would have liked to see the key insights and intuition derived from these results (e.g., the end of section 3) more prominently highlighted, as well as a clear conclusion summarizing the main takeaways (acknowledging the space constraints). While I appreciated the authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to non-experts in information theory, such as myself, I still found it challenging to follow. I recommend rephrasing many of the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than relying on the language of channels, codes, and distortion. In the related work section, it would be beneficial to mention the parallel and increasingly convergent line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." Some of this work also explores varying worker abilities and the integration of these incentives with more sophisticated aggregation methods. Minor corrections and suggestions include: line 84, consider changing "is reviewed in" to "are reviewed in"; line 106, the distinction between the crowdsourcer and taskmaster was unclear, possibly due to the term "crowdsourcing" being a portmanteau of "crowd" and "outsourcing"; line 122, correct the typo in "A A(X1,X2)"; lines 275-276, rephrase the sentence "In case..." for improved clarity; and Figure 2, consider adding a more detailed caption to enhance its interpretability.
