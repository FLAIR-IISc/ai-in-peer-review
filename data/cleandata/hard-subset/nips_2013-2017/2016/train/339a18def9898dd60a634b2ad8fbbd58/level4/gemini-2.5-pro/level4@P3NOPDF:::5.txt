This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of digital crowdsourcing. The primary goal is to establish lower bounds on the number of queries required per task to achieve a desired probability of error. The authors model workers as a set of parallel, independent channels. Two specific channel models are considered: Symmetric Discrete Memoryless Channels (DMCs) to represent inherent worker error rates, and a Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) model to capture scenarios where a worker may be a "spammer" with a certain probability.

The analysis is conducted for non-adaptive task assignments, where the entity distributing tasks (the "taskmaster") is assumed to be unaware of individual worker skills. Consequently, tasks are distributed uniformly across the worker pool. The framework then derives performance bounds under two conditions for the entity aggregating the results (the "crowdsourcer"): one where the skill levels of all workers are known, and another where they are unknown. Finally, as a specific application of the framework, the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum required rate (i.e., number of queries per task) for a k-ary incidence coding task under the SHC worker model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its novel application of information theory to the crowdsourcing problem. To my knowledge, and as the authors claim, this is the first paper to establish fundamental performance limits for such systems from this perspective. This theoretical foundation is a significant contribution to the field.

2.  **Potential Impact:** The results have the potential to be very useful for practitioners. By providing concrete lower bounds on the required number of queries, the work offers valuable guidance for designing and pricing crowdsourcing systems. These bounds can help platform designers understand the inherent trade-offs between cost (number of workers per task), reliability (error probability), and task complexity, leading to more efficient and principled system design.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making a complex theoretical framework accessible to the reader.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be significantly enhanced by including experiments or simulations. A comparison of the derived lower bounds with the performance of existing heuristics or schemes on real-world platforms, such as Amazon Mechanical Turk, would be invaluable. This would help ground the theoretical results and provide crucial insight into how far current practical systems are from these fundamental limits.

2.  **Paper Structure and Content:** The presentation could be improved to better highlight the core technical results. For instance, Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 feels less essential and could be condensed or moved to an appendix. The space saved would be better utilized by including proof sketches for the main theorems directly in the body of the paper. This would make the paper more self-contained and provide readers with a better intuition for the key technical arguments without forcing them to consult the appendix.

**Final Assessment:**

This is a novel and well-executed theoretical paper that opens a new and promising direction for analyzing crowdsourcing systems. The application of information theory provides a rigorous foundation for understanding the limits of what can be achieved. While the lack of empirical comparison is a notable weakness, the theoretical contribution is strong enough to be of significant interest to the NIPS community.

*Please note: I have not verified the detailed proofs in the appendix; my assessment is based on the claims and arguments presented in the main body of the paper.*
