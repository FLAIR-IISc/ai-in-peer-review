Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper formulates crowdsourcing as a rate–distortion problem and analyzes fundamental trade-offs between budget (queries per item) and fidelity (error rate). Workers are modeled as independent noisy channels: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) for generic, class-agnostic noise, and (ii) the spammer–hammer model SHC(q), where each worker is a perfect “hammer” with probability q and a random “spammer” otherwise. The focus is on non-adaptive querying. Two scenarios are studied: (1) SL-UK, where the crowdsourcer does not know worker skill levels; and (2) SL-CS, where the crowdsourcer knows (or perfectly infers) them. In both cases the taskmaster lacks skill information and assigns the same number of tasks to all workers.

Using the source–channel separation theorem, the paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum query rate needed to achieve a target Hamming distortion. It then analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), in which each query presents k items and asks for within-set incidence (same vs different classes), showing that invalid response patterns give kIC an error-correcting flavor. For SHC(q), the paper provides a necessary condition on the rate for kIC to meet a target error, plus a pricing rule-of-thumb based on the relative effort of different k.

This line of work complements NIPS literature on aggregation and inference—majority vote, Dawid–Skene EM, spectral/EM hybrids, and bandit-based allocation—by providing fundamental limits that any algorithm must respect, regardless of the specific aggregator or redundancy scheme.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled modeling of crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding; the separation-based bounds for SL-UK and SL-CS are clean and interpretable.
- Explicit comparison between the cases with and without worker-skill knowledge clarifies how skill estimation impacts achievable budget.
- The kIC analysis is concrete and practically relevant; the link to pricing is especially useful for practitioners.
- Well organized and generally well written.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- No empirical or simulation results are provided to gauge the tightness of the bounds or to situate common practices (e.g., majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral methods) relative to the theoretical limits. Even small-scale simulations on synthetic and AMT-style datasets would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
- The non-adaptive assumption and the MSC/SHC models simplify reality; discussion of difficulty-dependent noise, inter-worker correlations, or adaptive querying/assignment would help bound the scope of applicability.
- Section 3.1 (information theory background) can be shortened in favor of more problem-specific intuition or proof sketches; Section 2.4 could also be tightened.
- Proof sketches in the main text for key theorems (with full details in the appendix) would improve accessibility and credibility.
- The kIC pricing discussion could more carefully justify the cost model (time/effort vs monetary price) and clarify when the rule-of-thumb holds.
- Minor presentation issues: ensure consistent notation (e.g., M, N, q), complete and properly formatted references (publisher names, years), and correct a few grammatical slips.

Quality:
- The theoretical development appears sound, and the use of separation to derive lower bounds is appropriate. However, without experiments, it is hard to assess tightness or practical relevance in realistic regimes (finite blocklength, heterogeneous workers, difficulty effects). The kIC necessary condition under SHC(q) is clearly stated and useful as a benchmark.

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly clear and well structured. Trimming generic background (Sec. 3.1) and expanding problem-specific intuition and proof sketches would improve readability. A small illustrative example quantifying the SL-UK vs SL-CS gap would help.

Originality:
- Framing crowdsourcing as a rate–distortion problem and deriving separation-based lower bounds is, to my knowledge, novel in this literature and distinct from prior NIPS work focused on inference algorithms and task allocation.

Significance:
- The results provide valuable benchmarks to guide system design (e.g., redundancy planning, pricing) and to interpret the best possible performance under specified models. The practical significance will be much higher if supported by empirical comparisons and a more detailed discussion of modeling assumptions and their implications.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, novel information-theoretic framing with clear lower bounds.
- Bridges theory and practice by analyzing a concrete scheme (kIC) and offering pricing guidance.
- Well written and likely to stimulate further research on principled budget–fidelity trade-offs.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of empirical validation or simulations to assess tightness and practical relevance.
- Strong simplifying assumptions (non-adaptive querying; MSC/SHC channels; iid items) limit immediate applicability.
- Space used for general IT background could be repurposed for problem-specific insights and proof sketches.

Overall assessment:
A strong and timely theoretical contribution that establishes fundamental limits for crowdsourcing under clear models and offers actionable insights for kIC and pricing. I recommend acceptance if the authors can (i) add at least basic simulations comparing common aggregators to the bounds, and (ii) streamline background sections to include proof sketches and more domain-specific discussion; otherwise, it is borderline due to the lack of empirical support.
