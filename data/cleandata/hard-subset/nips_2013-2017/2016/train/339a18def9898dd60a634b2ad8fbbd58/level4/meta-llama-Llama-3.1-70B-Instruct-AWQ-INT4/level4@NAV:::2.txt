The authors present an information-theoretic approach to the crowdsourcing problem, deriving fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic sense. Specifically, they model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, where the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the decoder. The labels of items are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, the crowd workers as multiple channel uses, and the workers' skill levels as random channel states. The crowdsourcer is represented as the decoder. By leveraging existing results on JSCC coding rates, the authors establish asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item in crowdsourcing. Additionally, they provide a lower bound on the error probability of the "k-ary incidence coding" scheme under a specific crowdsourcing model.

In summary, this well-written paper may be a pioneering step in applying JSCC modeling to the crowdsourcing problem. Some technical comments are in order:

1. The authors model crowdsourcing as JSCC with random channel state and rely on the standard source-channel separation theorem to derive their results (Theorem 2 and 3). However, they should first justify the applicability of the separation theorem when the channel has a random state unknown at the encoder but possibly known at the receiver. While the separation theorem likely still holds in this scenario, the authors should provide a proof or reference an existing one.

2. The taskmaster and crowdsourcer are modeled as an encoder and decoder, respectively, with limited access to information. However, this assumption may be overly restrictive, as it disregards the potential for both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer to have access to the original data (items). This additional information could serve as side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem, potentially enhancing the model's realism and accuracy.

3. In Sec 4.2, the authors comment on the curves in Fig 2, stating that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow. It appears they intend to convey that the lower bound becomes smaller as k and q increase, suggesting potential performance improvements for the code. However, this does not necessarily imply that the lower bound itself improves, as there is no upper bound for comparison.

Following the authors' response, it is worth noting that the application of joint source-channel coding to crowdsourcing is intriguing, but a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practical. The authors may want to explore recent results by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, specifically their paper "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime" (IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013).
