The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. They model the crowdsourcing problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state. In this framework, items to be labeled are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as channel uses, worker skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. Leveraging existing JSCC results, the authors establish asymptotic limits on the number of queries required per item. Additionally, they provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) within a specific crowdsourcing model – equivalently, a channel under their information-theoretic formulation. Overall, this is a well-written paper that represents a potentially valuable first step towards modeling crowdsourcing using JSCC. 

However, several technical points require attention. First, the authors apply the source-channel separation theorem to derive their results (Theorems 2 and 3). It is crucial to justify the applicability of this theorem when the channel possesses a random state unknown to the encoder, but potentially known to the receiver. While the separation theorem likely holds in this setting, the authors should provide a proof or cite an existing one. Second, the model restricts the taskmaster to observing only item labels and the crowdsourcer to observing only worker labels. This may be overly restrictive, as both entities could reasonably have access to the original data (the items themselves). Such access introduces potential side information at the encoder and/or decoder, which could significantly improve performance in the JSCC framework. The authors should consider incorporating side information into their model to enhance its realism and persuasiveness. Finally, in Section 4.2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC improve as *k* and *q* increase. This likely refers to a decrease in the lower bound value, suggesting potential performance gains for the code, but does not constitute an improvement in the bound itself without a corresponding upper bound for comparison.

Following review of the authors’ response, it remains the case that while the application of joint source-channel coding to crowdsourcing is interesting, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be more practically useful. The authors should investigate recent work by Kostina and Verdu on nonasymptotic converses for JSCC [V. Kostina and S. Verdu, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” *IEEE Trans. on Information Theory*, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013].
