The authors present an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. They model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. Specifically, the labels of the items are modeled as an independent and identically distributed source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, worker skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. By leveraging existing results on coding rates in JSCC, the authors derive fundamental limits for the asymptotic number of queries per item. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a specific crowdsourcing model, effectively framing it within their information-theoretic channel model. Overall, this is a well-written paper and represents a potentially valuable initial step toward a JSCC-based modeling of crowdsourcing. 

I have several technical comments. First, the authors model crowdsourcing as JSCC with random channel state and then apply the standard source-channel separation theorem to derive their results (Theorem 2 and 3). However, they should first justify the applicability of the separation theorem in this context, where the channel has a random state unknown to the encoder, but potentially known to the receiver. While I believe the separation theorem likely holds in such scenarios, the authors should either provide a proof or cite an existing one. 

Second, the model restricts the taskmaster to observe only item labels, and the crowdsourcer to observe only worker labels. This may be overly restrictive, as both parties could potentially have access to the original data (the items themselves). This original data represents potential side information that could benefit the encoder and decoder within the JSCC framework. Side information available only at the encoder may offer limited benefit, but its presence at both the encoder and decoder, or even at the decoder alone, could significantly improve performance. Considering side information would strengthen the model's realism and persuasiveness.

Third, in Section 4.2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC improve as *k* and *q* grow when discussing Figure 2. I interpret this to mean the lower bound decreases as *k* and *q* grow, potentially indicating improved code performance. However, improvement requires comparison to an upper bound, which is currently absent.

Following review of the authors’ response, I maintain that while the use of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practically useful. The authors should investigate recent work by Kostina and Verdu on nonasymptotic converses for JSCC: V. Kostina and S. Verdu, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” *IEEE Trans. on Information Theory*, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
