The problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing is addressed using the language and tools of information theory, and the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff are given. I found the insights and model itself to be a very interesting and potentially useful take on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. However, I had difficulty fully understanding the paper, and I believe that the high-level intuition and insights stemming from these results could be highlighted more prominently.

The paper's use of information theory to analyze the crowdsourcing problem is novel and provides a unique perspective. The authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to those not familiar with information theory are appreciated, but I still found it challenging to follow at times. I would recommend restating many of the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than retaining the language of channels and codes and distortion. This would help to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience and facilitate a deeper understanding of the results.

The related work section could be improved by mentioning the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This line of research also discusses varying worker abilities and how to combine these incentives with more sophisticated aggregation methods.

There are a few minor errors and suggestions for improvement. On page 84, "is reviewed in" should be "are reviewed in." On page 106, the distinction between the crowdsourcer and taskmaster could be clarified, as the term "crowdsourcing" is a portmanteau of "crowd" and "outsourcing." On page 122, there is a typo in "A A(X1,X2)." The sentence on pages 275-276 starting with "In case..." is difficult to parse and could be rephrased for clarity. Finally, the caption for Figure 2 could be more detailed to help readers understand the significance of the results presented.

Overall, I believe that the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing, but could benefit from some revisions to improve clarity and accessibility.

Strengths:

* Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing
* Unique perspective on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques
* Efforts to make the paper accessible to a broader audience

Weaknesses:

* Difficulty in following the paper due to the use of information theory language
* Limited discussion of related work on robust economic incentives for workers
* Minor errors and suggestions for improvement

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to analyzing the crowdsourcing problem

* The authors have made an effort to make the paper accessible to a broader audience

Arguments Against Acceptance:
