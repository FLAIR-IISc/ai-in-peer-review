- Paper models crowdsourced workers as noisy channels (symmetric memoryless or spammer/hammer) and frames task allocation/aggregation as a coding problem mapping budget to code rate.
- Introduces k-incidence coding where workers label joint subsets of k items to exploit cross-item structure and improve reliability.
- Strength: elegant information-theoretic framing enabling budget–distortion analysis using coding tools.
- Strength: two worker models capture useful stylized behaviors and bound practical scenarios.
- Strength: interpretable budget-to-code-rate mapping that clarifies cost–accuracy trade-offs.
- Strength: k-incidence coding is a simple, concrete departure from per-item labeling with potential reliability benefits.
- Weakness: worker models may be restrictive—real workers are heterogeneous, class-dependent, and time-varying.
- Weakness: practicality concerns for k-incidence tasks—higher cognitive load, longer completion time, and platform support questions.
- Weakness: analysis assumes no feedback and non-adaptive assignment; relevance to adaptive strategies is unclear.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to existing coding-theoretic and probabilistic crowdsourcing literature.
- Weakness: primarily theoretical with no (or limited) empirical validation on synthetic or real data.
- Weakness: unclear extension to multiclass labels and class-imbalanced settings.
- Question: what distortion metric is used and how sensitive are results to metric choice?
- Question: precise definition of budget (interactions, time, monetary cost) and how k-dependent worker effort affects effective rate?
- Question: do the bounds include matching converses and achievability, or are there gaps and where do they matter?
- Question: how does k-incidence decoding work algorithmically, what's the computational complexity, and are scalable decoders available?
- Question: how should one choose k under a fixed budget—are there principled rules or phase transitions?
- Question: robustness to heterogeneous and unknown worker reliabilities and incorporation of priors/estimates?
- Suggestion: expand discussion on modeling assumptions, robustness, and extensions to heterogeneous/class-dependent errors.
- Suggestion: add a related-work comparison highlighting differences and complements to prior approaches.
- Suggestion: provide empirical illustrations (synthetic validation and small-scale real-data experiments) to show budget–distortion behavior and k-incidence overheads.
- Suggestion: discuss platform and human factors for k-incidence (task design, UI, completion time, worker quality).
- Suggestion: clarify impact of adaptivity or partial feedback on rate–distortion trade-offs and needed analysis changes.
- Suggestion: generalize to multiclass settings or explicitly state binary-only assumptions.
- Presentation: make budget–rate mapping explicit early and use a running example.
- Presentation: separate results for the two channel models and summarize key takeaways in a figure/table.
- Presentation: introduce k-incidence with an intuitive example and short pseudocode of the decoder.
- Overall: promising and interesting information-theoretic perspective with potential impact, but needs stronger connection to practical constraints, prior work, and empirical validation.
