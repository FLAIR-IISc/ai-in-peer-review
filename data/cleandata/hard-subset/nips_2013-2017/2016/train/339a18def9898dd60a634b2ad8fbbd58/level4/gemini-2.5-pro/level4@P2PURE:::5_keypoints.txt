- Presents an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on queries per task for desired error probability in digital crowdsourcing.
- Models workers as parallel, independent symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) and uses the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to model spammers.
- Assumes a non-adaptive coding scheme for task assignment and aggregation.
- Analyzes two scenarios: crowdsourcer knows worker skills, and crowdsourcer does not know worker skills.
- Taskmaster is assumed unaware of worker skills, so each worker receives the same number of tasks.
- Provides a necessary condition on minimum query rate for a k-ary incidence coding scheme under the SHC(q) worker model.
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and claims novelty as the first information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing.
- Practical implications: derived bounds can guide resource allocation and pricing for crowdsourcing projects.
- Major weakness: no experiments or simulations to contextualize theoretical bounds or compare with real platforms like MTurk.
- Presentation issues: Section 2.4 is too long and Section 3.1 seems non-essential and should be shortened or removed.
- Recommends reallocating space to include proof sketches for main theorems in the body; reviewer did not verify appendix proofs.
