- Paper models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state and derives asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item.
- Specific modeling: item labels are i.i.d. source, taskmaster = encoder, workers = channel uses with skill levels as random states unknown at encoder (may be known at decoder), crowdsourcer = decoder.
- Authors apply existing JSCC coding-rate results to obtain their theorems and give a lower bound on error probability for a "k‑ary incidence coding" (kIC) scheme under a specific crowd model.
- Paper is well-written and may represent a first step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing.
- Technical issue: the authors must justify or cite that source–channel separation holds when the channel has random state unknown at the encoder (and possibly known at the decoder).
- Modeling concern: the formulation omits possible side information (the original items) available to encoder/decoder, which can significantly affect JSCC performance and should be considered.
- Clarification needed in Sec. 4.2 / Fig. 2: saying lower bounds “improve” as k and q grow is misleading — the bounds decrease, which may indicate better code performance but does not itself constitute an improvement without upper bounds.
- Suggestion: include nonasymptotic (finite blocklength) analysis; recommend examining Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite‑blocklength JSCC converses.
- Quality assessment: technically sound in application of existing JSCC results to the crowdsourcing setting.
- Clarity assessment: manuscript is clear and easy to follow.
- Originality assessment: modeling crowdsourcing via JSCC is interesting, but the paper relies heavily on existing information‑theoretic results and offers no new theoretical advances.
- Significance assessment: useful preliminary work, but limited by asymptotic-only results; nonasymptotic limits would increase practical relevance.
- Pro-acceptance points: clear presentation and an original JSCC modeling perspective.
- Con-acceptance points: asymptotic focus, heavy reliance on prior results, and a potentially too‑restrictive model that omits side information.
