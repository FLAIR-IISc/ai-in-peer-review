- Problem setup: decomposes a large inference task into micro-tasks distributed to unreliable, variably skilled workers, studying the trade-off between total queries (budget) and final inference fidelity.  
- Main analogy: frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem—taskmaster as encoder, crowdsourcer as decoder, worker unreliability as a communication channel.  
- Theoretical contribution: derives a general information-theoretic lower bound on the budget needed to achieve a target fidelity, independent of specific query designs or aggregation algorithms.  
- Practical contribution: proposes k-ary incidence coding and empirically evaluates its performance against the theoretical bound for a specific channel model.  
- Novelty: novel application of the rate-distortion framework to crowdsourcing, offering an elegant information-theoretic perspective.  
- Limitation — homogeneous worker model: current model conflates skill and reliability into a single uniform channel and assumes uniform task allocation; paper should consider heterogeneous workers and adaptive allocation to more reliable workers.  
- Limitation — adversarial behavior: channel models capture random noise but not strategic/adversarial workers or spammers; robustness to adversarial behavior is not addressed.  
- Minor — figure clarity: labeling of curves in Figure 2 is hard to parse; physical meaning of k=1 is unclear (is it single-item labeling vs. comparisons?).  
- Minor — prior assumption: the assumption that the prior P(B(X)) is known is critical and should be stated earlier in the problem formulation.  
- Minor — typographical errors: incorrect figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a→1.b, 1.b→1.c).  
- Minor — style: inconsistent capitalization of 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' when not referring to numbered results.
