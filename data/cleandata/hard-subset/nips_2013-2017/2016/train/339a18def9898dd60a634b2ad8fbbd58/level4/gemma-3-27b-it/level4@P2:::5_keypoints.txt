- Models crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation and analyzes minimum queries for given fidelity using information theory.  
- Workers modeled as parallel independent noisy channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channels (SHC(q)), with known/unknown skill scenarios.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding and provides a lower bound on its required rate.  
- Builds on prior work (e.g., Dawid & Skene) and critiques simple methods like majority voting.  
- Claims novelty by providing a theoretical budget–fidelity framework absent in prior literature.  
- Strength: novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing addressing a clear gap.  
- Strength: strong mathematical rigor with clear information-theoretic lower bounds.  
- Strength: generally clear, well-organized presentation.  
- Strength: results have potential practical impact for task allocation, query design, and pricing.  
- Strength: comprehensive modeling of multiple worker types and skill-knowledge scenarios.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation—no experiments or simulations to show practical relevance.  
- Weakness: Section 2.4 (distortion) is too long; Section 3.1 (joint source–channel coding background) should be streamlined or moved.  
- Weakness: proofs relegated to appendix; main text needs proof sketches for accessibility.  
- Weakness: k-ary incidence coding discussion feels isolated and under-justified; pricing rule is speculative.  
- For acceptance: genuinely novel, theoretically sound, and clearly presented work that could stimulate further research.  
- Against acceptance: practical impact weakened by missing empirical comparisons and some presentation bloat.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution with unproven practical relevance due to lack of experiments.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, contingent on adding simulations, streamlining sections, and including proof sketches.
