This paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing by applying the language and tools of information theory to derive the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While the technical depth made some aspects of the paper challenging to fully grasp, the information-theoretic approach and the core insights appear both novel and potentially impactful for the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques.

The paper's impact could be significantly strengthened by more prominently highlighting the high-level intuition behind the results, particularly at the end of key sections like Section 3. A concluding section that summarizes the practical takeaways for designing crowdsourcing systems would also be highly beneficial, despite the space constraints.

Although the authors have clearly made an effort to make the paper accessible to a broader audience, its heavy reliance on information-theoretic formalism may still present a barrier to readers not already expert in the field. To improve readability and impact, I strongly recommend that the authors reframe many of the key results in the concrete terms of the crowdsourcing application, rather than relying solely on the abstract language of channels, codes, and distortion.

The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including the parallel and increasingly convergent line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often discussed under the banners of "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This literature is relevant as it also addresses issues like varying worker abilities and, more recently, the integration of incentive structures with sophisticated aggregation methods.
