The paper considers a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster divides a large task – such as an inference problem – into smaller sub-tasks. These sub-tasks are then presented to a crowd of workers. The crowdsourcer collects the information provided by these workers upon task completion and processes it to reach a conclusion regarding the original inference task. This setup accounts for varying worker skill levels and the potential for unreliable reporting of information to the crowdsourcer. The overall objective is to ensure a certain level of fidelity, or quality, in the final outcome at the crowdsourcer’s end, even when some workers provide incorrect information. Achieving this requires the taskmaster to carefully design the sub-tasks (or queries) allocated to the workers. The paper defines the total number of queries presented to the workers as the budget of the crowdsourcing system. The work investigates a fundamental trade-off between this budget (the number of queries) and fidelity (the quality of the inference). This trade-off is established by mapping the described crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source-channel coding from information theory. The taskmaster is modeled as a transmitter, and the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder. The unreliable communication links between workers and the crowdsourcer constitute a communication channel. This mapping allows the authors to derive a lower bound on the budget (number of queries) required to achieve a desired fidelity level, based on standard results in information theory. Importantly, this bound is independent of the specific query design scheme employed by the taskmaster and the information processing algorithm used by the crowdsourcer. The authors then consider a specific scheme at the taskmaster, namely k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance. They compare this scheme's performance to the information-theoretic lower bound established for a specific channel model. 

The reviewer finds the parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and a crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers particularly interesting. This work has the potential to encourage other researchers to explore the crowdsourcing framework using information-theoretic tools. However, the reviewer questions how realistically the paper’s model represents actual crowdsourcing systems. This may stem from the authors’ desire to map the crowdsourcing problem precisely to the point-to-point communication problem, enabling the direct application of existing information theory results with minimal modification. It would be helpful if the authors could address the following points:

1) The paper appears to conflate worker reliability and skill level, modeling them as a single channel. The assumption that the transmitter (taskmaster) allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their skill level, may not hold in real-world systems where workers often have ratings reflecting their expertise.

2) How realistic are the channel models used to represent worker unreliability? What about workers who may possess some knowledge of the task and intentionally attempt to degrade fidelity in an adversarial manner?

The explanation of the numerical results is unclear. The labeling of the curves in Fig.~2 could be improved. Specifically, what does the $k=1$ case represent? Does this mean the worker directly provides the correct label for the presented query, as asking if a pair shares the same label is not possible in this scenario? 

On page 6, line 246, the authors mention assuming knowledge of $P(B(X))$. This assumption should have been stated earlier in the paper. 

On page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” should read “Figure 1.b”. 
On page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.b” should read “Figure 1.c”.

Finally, the authors repeatedly use ‘Theorem’, ‘Lemma’, and ‘Corollary’ without accompanying specific theorem or lemma numbers. In such instances, ‘theorem’, ‘lemma’, and ‘corollary’ (lowercase) would be more appropriate.
