This paper models a crowdsourcing scenario where a large inference task is decomposed into smaller micro-tasks, which are then distributed to a group of workers. The model accounts for workers who may have varying skill levels and may provide unreliable responses. The central challenge is to design these micro-tasks, or queries, in a way that guarantees a desired level of fidelity (i.e., accuracy) in the final aggregated result, despite the unreliability of individual workers. The paper frames this challenge by analyzing the fundamental trade-off between the total number of queries (the "budget") and the final inference fidelity.

To analyze this trade-off, the authors draw a compelling analogy to the joint source-channel coding problem from information theory. In this mapping, the taskmaster acts as a source encoder/transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the collective unreliability of the workers is modeled as a communication channel. Leveraging this framework, the paper derives an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget required to achieve a target fidelity. A key strength of this approach is that the bound is general, holding irrespective of the specific query design or the aggregation algorithm used. The authors then propose a practical scheme, termed k-ary incidence coding, and evaluate its performance against this theoretical bound for a specific channel model.

The primary contribution of this work is its novel application of the rate-distortion framework to the problem of crowdsourcing with unreliable workers. This is an elegant and interesting perspective that could inspire further research at the intersection of information theory and crowdsourcing.

However, the paper's strength in drawing a clean analogy to point-to-point communication may also be its primary limitation. The simplification required to fit the problem into this classical framework raises questions about its applicability to real-world crowdsourcing systems. I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points:

1.  **Homogeneous Worker Model:** The current model appears to merge worker skill and reliability into a single, uniform channel characteristic. Furthermore, it assumes that queries are allocated uniformly across all workers. In practice, platforms often have reputation systems or worker ratings, allowing for differentiated task assignment. How might the model be extended to incorporate heterogeneous workers and adaptive query allocation strategies that assign more or different tasks to more reliable workers?

2.  **Adversarial Behavior:** The channel models considered seem to capture random, non-malicious errors. A significant challenge in real-world systems is the presence of adversarial workers (or spammers) who might strategically provide incorrect answers to degrade performance. Does the current framework have the capacity to model or provide robustness against such adversarial, rather than simply noisy, behavior?

**Minor Comments and Suggestions:**

*   The explanation of the numerical results could be clarified. Specifically, the labeling of the curves in Figure 2 is difficult to parse. Additionally, the physical meaning of the `k=1` case is unclear. Does this correspond to a scenario where a worker is simply asked for the label of a single item, rather than comparing a pair?
*   The assumption that the prior distribution $P(B(X))$ is known (mentioned on page 6, line 246) is a critical one. This should be stated much earlier, perhaps in the problem formulation section, as it significantly impacts the scope of the results.
*   There appear to be minor typographical errors in the figure references on page 4, line 135 (Figure 1.a should likely be 1.b, and 1.b should be 1.c).
*   The terms 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' are consistently capitalized even when not referring to a specific numbered result. The lowercase versions ('theorem', etc.) would be more appropriate in these instances.
