This paper models digital crowdsourcing in an information theoretic framework to provide lower bounds on the needed number of queries per task to achieve a given probability of error. The workers are modeled as parallel independent channels. The type of channels considered are symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to model errors done by workers and spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to model the case when a worker is a spammer with probability 1-q. The code is assumed to be nonadaptive. Two cases are considered for analysis: the case when the crowdsourcer, who decides the value of each task after collecting the results from the workers, knows the skill level of all of the workers and when it do not know anything about them. In both cases the taskmaster, who distributes the tasks to the workers, is assumed to not know anything about the workers skills, so every worker is assigned the same number of tasks.

The paper is well organized and well written. The quality of the paper is good and the analysis is technically sound. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory. The paper's main contribution is providing a fundamental limit on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a given probability of error in crowdsourcing. The paper also provides a necessary condition on the minimum needed rate for k-ary incidence coding with SHC(q) worker pool.

However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed. The paper would benefit from the inclusion of experiments or simulations to show how far current used schemes are from the limits, for example in AMT. Section 2.4 can be shortened and 3.1 is not necessary or can be shortened. Instead, proof sketches to the theorems are more important. The paper's originality and significance are high, and it seems very useful where designers of such crowdsourcing projects can know the minimum rate needed to accomplish a task with a given probability of error and it can help in pricing such projects.

Arguments For Acceptance:
- The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory.
- The paper's main contribution is providing a fundamental limit on the number of queries per task needed to achieve a given probability of error in crowdsourcing.
- The paper is well organized and well written.
- The paper's originality and significance are high.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- The paper does not include experiments or simulations to show how far current used schemes are from the limits.
- Some sections, such as 2.4 and 3.1, can be shortened or removed.
- Proof sketches to the theorems are not provided.
