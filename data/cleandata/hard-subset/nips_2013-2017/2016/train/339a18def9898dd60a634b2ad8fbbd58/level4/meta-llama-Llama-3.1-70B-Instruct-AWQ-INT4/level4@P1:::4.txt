The paper considers a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster divides a large task into small tasks that are then presented to a crowd of workers. A crowdsourcer collects the information provided by these workers and processes it to conclude the inference task. This setup allows for workers to have varying skill levels and be unreliable in reporting their information to the crowdsourcer. The objective of the system is to ensure a certain level of fidelity or 'goodness' of the final outcome at the crowdsourcer, even in the presence of some incorrect information presented by the participating workers. This requires the taskmaster to carefully design small tasks (or queries) to be allocated to the workers.

The paper defines the total number of queries presented to the workers as the budget of the crowdsourcing system. It studies the fundamental trade-off between the budget (number of queries presented to the workers by the taskmaster) and fidelity (quality of the inference task performed at the crowdsourcer). This trade-off is obtained by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source channel coding in information theory literature. The taskmaster is treated as a transmitter, and the crowdsourcer is mapped to a receiver/decoder. The unreliable links from the workers to the crowdsourcer constitute a communication channel. Given this mapping, a lower bound on the budget (number of queries) for a desirable fidelity level follows from the standard results in information theory literature. This bound is independent of the schemes used by the taskmaster to design the queries and the algorithm used at the crowdsourcer to process the information collected from the workers.

The authors then consider a specific scheme at the taskmaster, called k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance. They compare the performance of this encoding scheme with the obtained information theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model. The reviewer finds the parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers quite interesting. This work may invite other researchers to study the crowdsourcing framework using information theoretic tools.

However, the reviewer is not sure if the results presented in this paper model real crowdsourcing systems in a very realistic way. One reason behind this could be that the paper wants the crowdsourcing problem to exactly map to the point-to-point communication problem so that the existing results from the information theory literature can be directly applied with minimal efforts.

It would be great if the authors could comment on the following issues:

1. It seems that the reliability and skill level of the workers are merged together and modeled as a single channel. The paper assumes that the transmitter (taskmaster) allocates the same number of queries to all the workers, irrespective of their skill level. This may not be true in real systems where workers may carry some rating with them, which represents their skill level.

2. How realistic are the channel models in taking the unreliability of the workers into account? What about workers that can have some knowledge of the task at hand and try to degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner?

The explanation of numerical results is not very clear. The labeling of various curves in Fig. 2 can be improved. What does the $k=1$ case correspond to? Does the worker directly give the correct label of the presented query, as one cannot present a pair and ask if they share the same label in this case?

In page 6, line 246, the authors mention that they assume the knowledge of $P(B(X))$. The reviewer believes that this should have been stated much earlier in the paper.

In page 4, line 135, "Figure 1.a --> Figure 1.b" and "Figure 1.b --> Figure 1.c" are incorrect references.

The authors repeatedly use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' even when these terms are not accompanied by a specific theorem or lemma number. The reviewer believes that 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' would be more appropriate in such cases.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. However, the reviewer is not convinced about the practical relevance of the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but there are some minor errors and unclear explanations, as mentioned above.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel application of information theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing problem. However, the reviewer is not sure about the originality of the results, as they seem to be a direct application of existing information theory literature.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential to contribute to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory. However, the reviewer is not convinced about the practical impact of the results.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

For acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel application of information theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing problem.
* The results have the potential to contribute to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory.

Against acceptance:
