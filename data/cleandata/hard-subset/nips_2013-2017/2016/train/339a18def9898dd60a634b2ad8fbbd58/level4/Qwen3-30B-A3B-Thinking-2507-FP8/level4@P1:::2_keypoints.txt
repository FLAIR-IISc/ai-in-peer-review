1. Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem with random channel states.  
2. Establishes fundamental limits on query budget needed to achieve target labeling fidelity.  
3. Characterizes minimum queries per item under unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
4. Leverages the source-channel separation theorem to derive asymptotic limits.  
5. Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides a lower bound on its error under a spammer-hammer model.  
6. Novel perspective: applies information theory to crowdsourcing, complementing algorithmic literature.  
7. Strong relation to prior crowdsourcing work and NIPS contributions; situates the approach well.  
8. Theoretical foundation is rigorous; Theorems 2 and 3 give asymptotic performance limits with standard information-theoretic derivations.  
9. kIC analysis (Theorem 4) concretely connects query design to fundamental limits.  
10. Clarity and structure are good; problem and model are presented logically and kIC is well-described.  
11. Paper should more explicitly state modeling assumptions (e.g., iid source, memoryless worker channels).  
12. Main concern: reliance on source-channel separation without justification when encoder lacks channel-state information.  
13. Main concern: restrictive model that ignores potential side information from original items (X) available to taskmaster/crowdsourcer.  
14. Lacks non-asymptotic (finite-sample) characterization; should consider non-asymptotic converses (e.g., Kostina & Verdú 2013).  
15. Practical relevance is unclear due to asymptotic L → ∞ results; more discussion on finite-data implications is needed.  
16. Overall: technically sound and significant as a theoretical benchmark, but needs justification for separation, broader modeling (side information), and non-asymptotic analysis.
