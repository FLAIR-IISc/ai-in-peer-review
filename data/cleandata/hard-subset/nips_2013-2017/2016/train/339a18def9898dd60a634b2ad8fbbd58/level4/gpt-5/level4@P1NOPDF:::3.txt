The paper studies the aggregation of worker outputs in crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools and characterizes the fundamental limits of the budget–fidelity tradeoff. Although I did not fully follow all of the technical details (see below), I found the model and the resulting insights very interesting and potentially useful for designing aggregation techniques.

I would have liked the high-level intuition to be emphasized more prominently—especially the takeaways at the end of Section 3—and for the paper to close with a concise statement of what practitioners should learn from these results (I realize space is tight). Despite the authors’ efforts to make the work accessible to readers not steeped in information theory, the exposition was still difficult to follow for me. I recommend restating many of the key results directly in the language of crowdsourcing—workers, tasks, labels, budgets, accuracies—rather than relying mainly on channels, codes, and distortion. Mapping each formal construct to its crowdsourcing counterpart would greatly improve clarity.

Related work: Please discuss the complementary and increasingly connected literature on robust economic incentives for workers, commonly referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This line of work often models heterogeneous worker abilities and, more recently, explores how to combine incentive schemes with sophisticated aggregation methods. Positioning your contributions relative to that literature would strengthen the paper.

Minor comments and clarity issues:
- Line 84: Consider “are reviewed in” rather than “is reviewed in.”
- Line 106: The distinction between the “crowdsourcer” and the “taskmaster” was unclear here (and earlier). Please clarify their roles and timing. The term “crowdsourcing” (crowd + outsourcing) suggests outsourcing occurs before workers are involved, which added to my confusion.
- Line 122: Typo in “A A(X1,X2).”
- Lines 275–276: The sentence beginning “In case …” was hard to parse; please rewrite for clarity.
- Figure 2: I could not extract much insight from the figure. A more detailed caption explaining the axes, setup, and key message would help.

Overall, I appreciate the novelty of framing crowdsourced aggregation through information theory and the attempt to identify fundamental limits. With clearer mapping from the formalism to the crowdsourcing setting, stronger high-level guidance, and a brief discussion of incentive-compatible mechanisms, the paper would be significantly more accessible and impactful.
