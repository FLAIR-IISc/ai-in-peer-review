The paper "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" presents a novel approach to understanding the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing by applying information-theoretic principles. The authors model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyze the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing using a joint source-channel coding framework.

The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the problem, the proposed approach, and the results. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, including existing crowdsourcing models and algorithms. However, I would recommend mentioning the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, which also discusses varying worker abilities and combining incentives with sophisticated aggregation methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a unified framework for understanding the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity, considering both scenarios where the worker skill levels are unknown and known to the crowdsourcer. These bounds provide a fundamental limit on the performance of any crowdsourcing algorithm and can serve as a benchmark for evaluating the efficiency of different algorithms.

The paper also presents a novel query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, which is shown to have improved performance compared to existing schemes. The authors provide numerical results and information-theoretic bounds for this scheme, demonstrating its potential for achieving high fidelity with a reduced number of queries.

However, I found some parts of the paper challenging to follow, particularly the sections on information-theoretic background and the proof of the theorems. While the authors provide a good overview of the necessary information-theoretic concepts, some of the notation and terminology may be unfamiliar to non-experts. I would recommend rephrasing some of the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than retaining the language of channels and codes.

Minor comments:

* 84: "is reviewed in" should be "are reviewed in"
* 106: The distinction between the crowdsourcer and taskmaster could be clarified
* 122: "type in" should be corrected to "A A(X1,X2)"
* 275-276: The sentence is hard to parse and could be rephrased for clarity
* Figure 2: A more detailed caption would be helpful to understand the results

Overall, I think the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory. With some minor revisions to improve clarity and accessibility, it has the potential to be a highly influential work in the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and unified framework for understanding the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing
* The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity
* The paper presents a novel query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, which is shown to have improved performance compared to existing schemes
* The authors provide numerical results and information-theoretic bounds for this scheme, demonstrating its potential for achieving high fidelity with a reduced number of queries

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Some parts of the paper may be challenging to follow for non-experts in information theory
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed conclusion summarizing the key takeaways and implications for crowdsourcing
* The related work section could be expanded to include the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers.
