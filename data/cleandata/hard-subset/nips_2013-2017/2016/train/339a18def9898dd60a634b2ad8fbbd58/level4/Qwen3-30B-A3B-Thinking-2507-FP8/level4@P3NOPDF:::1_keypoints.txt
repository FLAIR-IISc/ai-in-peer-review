- Frames crowdsourced labeling as a channel coding problem, mapping budget to code rate.  
- Models two channel types: symmetric memoryless channels (SMC) and spammer/hammer channels.  
- Proposes k-incidence coding where workers label subsets of k items as a practical baseline.  
- Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of distortion–budget tradeoffs via coding-theoretic principles.  
- Connects coding rate to budget, offering a tangible metric for resource allocation.  
- Strength: clear theoretical foundation and well-motivated k-incidence scheme showing tradeoffs between budget and accuracy.  
- Major weakness: no empirical validation on real or synthetic crowdsourcing data and no comparisons to baselines (e.g., majority voting, EM).  
- Practical concerns: lacks discussion of computational complexity, scalability, and implementation details for large-scale problems.  
- Writing issues: occasional imprecision (e.g., wording and undefined terms like "distortion") and unclear mapping of spammer/hammer to channel parameters.  
- Missing extensions: limited treatment of worker heterogeneity, mixed populations, dynamic/adaptive task allocation, and non-i.i.d. behaviors.  
- Recommendation: reject unless authors add experiments, baseline comparisons, scalability analysis, and clearer discussion of limitations and extensions.
