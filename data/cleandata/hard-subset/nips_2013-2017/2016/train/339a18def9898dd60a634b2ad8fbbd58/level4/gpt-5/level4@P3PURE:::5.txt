Summary
- The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a target error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels.
- The channel models considered are symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture worker error and the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), in which a worker is a spammer with probability 1−q.
- The coding scheme is nonadaptive. Two cases are analyzed: (i) the crowdsourcer (who decides the value of each task after aggregating worker responses) knows the skill levels of all workers, and (ii) the crowdsourcer has no knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks) is assumed to have no knowledge of worker skills and therefore assigns the same number of tasks to every worker.
- For k-ary incidence coding—where a worker is shown k items and asked if they belong to similar or different breeds—the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum rate (number of queries per task) under an SHC(q) worker pool.

Strengths
- The problem is clearly formulated, and the analysis provides informative lower bounds on query complexity for crowdsourced labeling under explicit worker-channel models.
- The use of MSC and SHC(q) models is natural in this context, and the nonadaptive coding assumption is clearly stated.
- The work appears novel; to my knowledge (and as claimed by the paper), there has not been prior analysis of crowdsourcing from this information-theoretic perspective.
- The results are potentially useful in practice, enabling designers to estimate the minimum rate required to achieve a desired error probability and to inform pricing of such projects.

Weaknesses and concerns
- There are no experiments or simulations demonstrating how existing schemes (e.g., on AMT) compare to the derived limits. This makes it hard to assess how tight or practically relevant the bounds are in real deployments.
- The exposition could be more concise in places.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include experiments or simulations to show how far common methods (for example, on AMT) are from the theoretical limits, even if only in simplified settings.
- Provide proof sketches for the main theorems in the main text. These would be more valuable to most readers than some of the current exposition.
- Streamline Section 2.4; it can be shortened without loss of clarity.
- Section 3.1 is not strictly necessary and could be removed or substantially shortened to make room for the proof sketches and/or empirical illustrations.

Clarity and organization
- The paper is generally well organized and well written. The roles of the “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” are clearly distinguished, and the modeling assumptions are stated.

Novelty and impact
- The approach appears novel and valuable. By quantifying fundamental limits, the paper can guide the design and pricing of crowdsourcing pipelines targeting specific error guarantees.

Proofs
- I did not read the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the correctness or completeness of the proofs.
