This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for the problem of data labeling via crowdsourcing. The core idea is to model each crowd worker as a noisy communication channel, effectively transforming the label aggregation task into a channel decoding problem. The authors specifically investigate two well-motivated channel models: a symmetric memoryless channel to represent uniformly noisy workers, and a spammer/hammer model to capture workers who may be malicious or exceptionally accurate.

By framing the problem in this way, the authors are able to leverage powerful tools from coding theory. A key insight is the mapping of the crowdsourcing budget—the total number of labels that can be collected—to the rate of an error-correcting code. This formulation allows for a rigorous analysis of the fundamental trade-off between the desired labeling accuracy (measured as distortion) and the available budget.

A significant strength of the paper is this elegant and principled formulation. Connecting the practical problem of crowdsourcing to the well-established field of coding theory provides a new lens for analysis and has the potential to yield fundamental performance limits. As a concrete instantiation of their framework, the authors propose and analyze a practical coding scheme termed "k-incidence coding." This scheme corresponds to designing micro-tasks where workers are asked to provide labels for a subset of k items at a time, which is an intuitive and practical approach.

While the theoretical contributions are clear and compelling, the paper could be strengthened by addressing a few points. The chosen worker models serve as excellent starting points, but a discussion of how these models align with real-world worker behavior or how the framework might be extended to accommodate more complex, worker-dependent channel models would enhance the practical relevance. Furthermore, while the focus is on information-theoretic limits, a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the corresponding decoding algorithms for label aggregation would be valuable for practitioners looking to implement these methods.

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper that introduces a valuable new perspective to the crowdsourcing literature. The information-theoretic approach is novel and provides fundamental insights into the budget-accuracy trade-off. While the specific techniques from coding theory fall slightly outside my primary area of expertise, the problem formulation and its implications were presented with sufficient clarity for the main contributions to be appreciated. I believe this work will be of significant interest to researchers working at the intersection of machine learning, crowdsourcing, and information theory. I recommend acceptance.
