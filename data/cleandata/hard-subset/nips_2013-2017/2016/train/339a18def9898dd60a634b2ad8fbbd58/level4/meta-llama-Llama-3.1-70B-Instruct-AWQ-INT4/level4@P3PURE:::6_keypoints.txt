- Paper derives an information-theoretic budget–fidelity bound for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and channel coding; derivation is sound.  
- If this is the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, the contribution could be notable.  
- Novelty and practical value are questionable because the derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound may be very loose.  
- Main concern: unlike channel coding, crowdsourcing may not allow arbitrary design of the input sequence u^n, so the encoding freedom assumed may be unrealistic.  
- The assumed mapping from B(X)^n to U^(Σ m_i) may not be implementable in crowdsourcing, risking that channel-capacity-based bounds are loose.  
- Repetition codes (majority voting) are possible, but it is unclear how to design u^n beyond repetition to improve error correction in crowdsourcing.  
- Crucial difference: you cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times without breaking the iid assumptions, unlike repeated channel uses.  
- More generally, crowdsourcing imposes constraints on feasible encoding schemes (equation (3)) that are absent in classical channel coding, which can weaken the bound.  
- To strengthen the paper, compare the derived information-theoretic bound to performances of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods.  
- Minor: clarify minimization in equation (8) over C, C' with E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor: expand the proof of Theorem 4 for clarity.  
- Minor: explain sign/relationship issue in Theorem 4 (since ln(1−q) < 0, it’s unclear whether ε_S > ε̂).
