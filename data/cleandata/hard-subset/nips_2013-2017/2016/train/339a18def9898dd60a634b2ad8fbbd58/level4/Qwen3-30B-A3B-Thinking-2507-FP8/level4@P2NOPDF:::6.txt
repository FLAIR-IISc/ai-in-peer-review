

This paper introduces an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing, derived using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. While the derivation is mathematically sound, the novelty and practical significance of the contribution remain questionable. The bound appears straightforward to derive, and its tightness is doubtful due to fundamental constraints in the crowdsourcing setting that are absent in standard channel coding problems.  

The core issue lies in the applicability of channel coding principles to crowdsourcing. In channel coding, the encoder has significant freedom to design codebooks (e.g., repetition, Hamming, LDPC codes), enabling flexible error correction. However, in crowdsourcing, the encoding mapping (from worker responses to labels) is constrained by practical and structural limitations. Specifically, the requirement for independent worker responses (violated if identical queries are assigned to the same worker multiple times) prevents the use of standard code design techniques like repetition coding. For instance, while majority voting corresponds to a repetition-based approach, extending this to more sophisticated error-correcting schemes is infeasible. The same query cannot be reassigned to a single worker without violating the independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) assumption in Equations (1) and (2), as the worker would likely produce identical responses. This is a critical distinction: channel coding assumes an i.i.d. channel where repeated transmissions are permissible, but crowdsourcing does not.  

Consequently, the set of valid encoding schemes in Equation (3) is severely restricted compared to channel coding, where mappings from {0,1}^{nR} to {0,1}^n face minimal constraints. These constraints—such as the inability to reuse queries for the same worker—likely render the proposed information-theoretic bound overly loose. The paper does not sufficiently address how these constraints limit achievable error-correcting capabilities, undermining the bound’s practical relevance. Without a rigorous analysis of these constraints, the bound’s utility for guiding real-world crowdsourcing design remains unclear.  

To strengthen the contribution, the authors should:  
1. Explicitly quantify the impact of crowdsourcing-specific constraints (e.g., query-worker assignment rules) on the achievable channel capacity.  
2. Compare the derived bound against state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene, Bayesian approaches) to demonstrate its practical relevance. The current lack of such comparisons weakens the argument for the bound’s value.  

Minor comments:  
- In Equation (8), clarify that the minimization is over codes C, C' satisfying E[d(Z, \hat{Z})] ≤ D^t.  
- The proof of Theorem 4 requires greater elaboration; specifically, justify the step where the bound is tightened.  
- In Theorem 4, note that ln(1−q) < 0. Clarify whether ε_S > \hat{ε} and why this inequality matters.  
- In Equation (17), explain the origin of H_M(E(ε)) or cite prior work establishing this entropy term.  

In summary, while the theoretical framework is conceptually interesting, the paper fails to convincingly bridge channel coding theory with the practical constraints of crowdsourcing. The bound’s looseness due to unaddressed structural limitations—particularly the i.i.d. violation from query reuse—significantly diminishes its novelty and utility. The authors must rigorously analyze these constraints and validate the bound against existing methods to justify the contribution. Without this, the work risks being perceived as a mechanical application of established information theory rather than a meaningful advance for the crowdsourcing community.
