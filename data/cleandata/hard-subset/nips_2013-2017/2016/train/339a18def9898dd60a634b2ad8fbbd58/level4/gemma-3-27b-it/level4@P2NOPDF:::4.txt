This paper investigates a crowdsourcing system where a taskmaster decomposes a complex inference problem into smaller tasks for distribution to a crowd of workers. The system accounts for worker heterogeneity in skill and potential unreliability in reporting results. The core objective is to achieve a desired level of accuracy in the final inference, despite potentially noisy worker responses, by strategically designing the queries presented to the workers – a process constrained by a pre-defined budget (total number of queries).

The authors establish a compelling connection between this crowdsourcing setup and the joint source-channel coding problem from information theory. By mapping the taskmaster to a transmitter, the crowdsourcer to a receiver, and worker responses through a noisy channel, they derive a fundamental trade-off between budget and fidelity. Critically, this trade-off is expressed as a lower bound on the required budget, derived from existing information-theoretic results, and is *independent* of specific query design or decoding algorithms. The paper then focuses on a particular query design scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyzes its performance relative to this theoretical lower bound under a specific channel model. 

The parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and this crowdsourcing model is insightful and offers a novel perspective. This work has the potential to stimulate further research applying information-theoretic tools to the study of crowdsourcing systems. 

However, the reviewer has some reservations regarding the realism of the model. The formulation appears to prioritize a direct mapping to the point-to-point communication problem to leverage existing theoretical results, potentially at the expense of modeling complexities present in real-world crowdsourcing. Specifically, the authors should address the following points:

1) The model currently conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel representation. In practice, workers often have pre-existing ratings reflecting their demonstrated skill level, and the taskmaster may rationally allocate queries non-uniformly based on these ratings. The current assumption of equal query allocation across all workers seems unrealistic.

2) The adopted channel models, while providing a starting point, may not fully capture the nuanced unreliability of workers. The possibility of adversarial behavior – where workers intentionally provide misleading information – is not considered. Exploring more sophisticated channel models that account for strategic worker responses would strengthen the analysis.

Further, several presentation issues detract from the clarity of the paper. The explanation of the numerical results is unclear, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 could be improved. The meaning of the $k=1$ case requires clarification; does it imply that workers directly provide correct labels, bypassing the need for pairwise comparison queries? On page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ should have been stated earlier in the paper. Additionally, there are minor errors in figure references (page 4, lines 135-136: Figure 1.a should be 1.b, and Figure 1.b should be 1.c). Finally, the authors repeatedly use “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without accompanying numbers; using lowercase “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” would be more appropriate in these instances.
