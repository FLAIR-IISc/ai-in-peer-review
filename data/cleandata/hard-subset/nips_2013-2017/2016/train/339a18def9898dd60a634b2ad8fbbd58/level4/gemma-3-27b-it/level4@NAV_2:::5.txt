The paper investigates fundamental limits in digital crowdsourcing by modeling it as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyzing it through an information theoretic lens. Specifically, the authors aim to identify the minimum query budget (number of queries per item) required to achieve a desired fidelity (accuracy) in crowdsourced tasks. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent noisy channels, with consideration given to both symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel models (SHC). The analysis considers two scenarios: when worker skill levels are known to the crowdsourcer and when they are unknown. Finally, the paper examines the performance of a k-ary incidence coding scheme and provides a lower bound on its rate requirements.

This work builds upon prior research in crowdsourcing, such as the use of majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5] for label aggregation, and work on bandit survey problems [1] and worker skill estimation [6]. It differs from these approaches by taking a fundamental, information-theoretic approach to understanding the limits of crowdsourcing performance, rather than focusing on algorithmic improvements. The paper also connects to broader literature in network information theory [7] and rate-distortion theory [4], adapting these concepts to the unique challenges of human-based computation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of information theory to analyze the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing is a novel contribution. As the authors claim, there is limited prior work addressing the problem from this perspective.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing the trade-off between budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing. The derivations of the lower bounds (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) are well-structured.
*   **Clear Modeling:** The modeling of workers as parallel noisy channels is a natural and intuitive way to represent the inherent uncertainty in crowdsourced data.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The results have the potential to inform the design and pricing of crowdsourcing systems, providing insights into the minimum resources required to achieve a desired level of accuracy. The discussion of pricing based on k-ary incidence coding (Appendix E) is a good example of this.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks experimental results or simulations to demonstrate the tightness of the derived bounds and their relevance to real-world crowdsourcing platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk. Comparing the theoretical limits to the performance of existing algorithms in practice would significantly strengthen the work.
*   **Section Lengths:** Section 2.4 feels somewhat lengthy and could be streamlined. Similarly, the background material in Section 3.1, while relevant, could be more concise, potentially moving some details to the appendix.
*   **Proof Details:** While the theorems are stated clearly, the absence of detailed proofs within the main body of the paper makes it difficult for readers to fully assess the validity of the results without consulting the appendix. Providing proof sketches would improve accessibility.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of non-adaptive coding may be limiting. Exploring the potential benefits of adaptive query schemes, where the queries are adjusted based on worker responses, could lead to further improvements.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental question in crowdsourcing with a novel and rigorous approach.
*   The theoretical results provide valuable insights into the limits of crowdsourcing performance and have the potential to guide system design.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation raises concerns about the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   The paper could benefit from a more concise presentation and inclusion of proof sketches in the main text.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. While the lack of empirical validation is a weakness, the novelty and rigor of the work justify acceptance, particularly if the authors address the minor concerns regarding presentation and proof details. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest adding a discussion of the potential limitations of the assumptions made, exploring avenues for future research including adaptive coding schemes, and providing more accessible proof sketches.
