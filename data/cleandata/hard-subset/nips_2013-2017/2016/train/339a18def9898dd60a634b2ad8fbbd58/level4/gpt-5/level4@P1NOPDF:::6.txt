This paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity trade-off for label crowdsourcing using rate–distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation appears sound. If this is indeed the first application of rate–distortion and channel coding to crowdsourcing, the contribution could be noteworthy. However, I have concerns about both novelty and practical impact. The derivation is relatively straightforward, and the resulting bound risks being very loose in realistic settings.

The main issue is the gap between classical channel coding and crowdsourcing. In channel coding, one has substantial freedom to design codebooks and encoders (e.g., repetition, Hamming, convolutional, RS, LDPC codes). In contrast, it is unclear whether an analogous freedom exists in designing the sequence of channel inputs u^n in crowdsourcing. In equation (3), nearly all mappings from B(X)^n to U^{∑ m_i} (except degenerate ones) would need to be valid or implementable encoders for the channel capacity to be a tight bound. For crowdsourcing, this assumption seems doubtful.

Repetition is a natural “code” in crowdsourcing, with majority voting as the decoder. Beyond that, however, it is unclear how to construct u^n to enhance error-correcting capability in a way that is both implementable and consistent with the model assumptions. A key difference from channel coding is that we cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times without violating the i.i.d. assumptions in equations (1) or (2): the same worker is likely to produce identical or highly correlated responses to the same query. In contrast, in communication channels we can typically assume i.i.d. uses of the channel and repeat symbols without inducing such dependencies.

This is just one example, but more generally, crowdsourcing imposes fundamental constraints on the feasible encoding mappings in equation (3) that do not arise in standard channel coding, where an encoding mapping {0,1}^{nR} → {0,1}^n is essentially unconstrained. These constraints likely include limitations on task assignment, worker reuse, and dependence structures across responses, among others. As a result, the proposed information-theoretic bound may be substantially looser than suggested if many of the mappings counted in the analysis are not actually implementable.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend clarifying the set of admissible encoders in equation (3) under realistic crowdsourcing constraints, and quantifying how these constraints affect the achievable rates. It would also be valuable to compare the derived bound with the empirical performance of state-of-the-art aggregation methods across a range of budgets and worker models, to assess tightness and practical relevance.
