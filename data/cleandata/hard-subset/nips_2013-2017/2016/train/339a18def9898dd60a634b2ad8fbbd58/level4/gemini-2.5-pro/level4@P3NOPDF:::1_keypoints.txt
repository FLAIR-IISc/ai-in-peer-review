- Novel information-theoretic framework modeling crowd workers as noisy communication channels for data labeling.
- Investigates two channel models: symmetric memoryless (uniform noise) and spammer/hammer (malicious vs highly accurate).
- Maps the crowdsourcing budget to an error-correcting code rate, enabling rigorous budget–accuracy (distortion) trade-off analysis.
- Leverages coding theory to derive fundamental performance limits for label aggregation.
- Proposes a practical "k-incidence coding" scheme where workers label subsets of k items.
- Strength: elegant, principled formulation that connects crowdsourcing to well-established coding theory tools.
- Weakness: needs discussion of how the worker models match real-world behavior and extensions to more complex, worker-dependent models.
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational complexity of the decoding/aggregation algorithms for practical implementation.
- Overall positive: clear contributions, likely significant interest to ML/crowdsourcing/information-theory researchers; recommendation to accept.
