- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem to derive budget–fidelity trade-offs using information-theoretic tools.  
- Authors derive lower bounds on minimum query rate for target error under different worker-skill knowledge assumptions.  
- Paper analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a spammer–hammer model and compares it to information-theoretic limits.  
- Aim is to provide scheme-agnostic fundamental limits and show structured queries can exploit consistency for error correction.  

- Novelty: shifts focus from aggregation algorithms to rate–distortion/channel-capacity limits, offering interpretable baseline bounds.  
- Clear distinction between unknown-skill (SL-UK) and skill-known (SL-CS) decoder cases with insightful comparisons.  
- Provides oracle lower bound and practical pricing “rule of thumb” for kIC under the spammer–hammer model.  
- Potentially stimulates cross-fertilization between information theory and crowdsourcing research.  

- Worker model conflates reliability and skill into one parameter and assumes equal query allocation to all workers, ignoring reputation-based adaptive allocation.  
- Questionable channel realism: MSC and spammer–hammer may not capture partially informed, colluding, or adversarial workers (Byzantine behavior).  
- Model ignores input-dependent difficulty and memory/adaptation; unclear how bounds extend to item-dependent error or adaptive coding.  
- Numerical results unclear: Figure 2 labels need improvement and k = 1 case is ambiguous—does it mean single-item labeling under the chosen channel model?  
- Strong assumption of known P(B(X)) stated late; sensitivity to prior misspecification or unknown priors is not addressed.  

- Presentation issues: incorrect Figure references (Figure 1.a/b/c), inconsistent use/capitalization of “Theorem/Lemma/Corollary,” and poor figure axes/legends.  
- Plots should explicitly tie each curve to analytical results (e.g., Corollary 1 vs Theorem 4) and captions/text should state takeaways.  

- Technical quality: derivations follow standard information-theoretic arguments and are sound under assumptions, but several restrictive assumptions limit applicability.  
- Clarity: high-level structure is clear but explanations of numerical results and some assumptions need earlier and clearer statements.  
- Originality: framing and scheme-agnostic bounds are novel relative to aggregation-centric prior work.  
- Significance: provides useful theoretical baselines and potential insights for query design/pricing, but practical impact requires extensions to realistic/adaptive/adversarial settings.  

- Pros for acceptance: novel elegant theoretical framing, bridges communities, and offers insights into structured queries and pricing.  
- Cons against acceptance: restrictive modeling assumptions, limited empirical connection to real datasets/behavior, and presentation flaws.  

- Suggested improvements: discuss incorporating adaptive allocation, known worker ratings, and input-dependent reliability into the framework.  
- Recommend analyzing robustness to prior misspecification and partial skill knowledge at encoder/decoder.  
- Clarify k = 1 baseline, improve figure annotations/legends/captions, and fix terminology and figure references.
