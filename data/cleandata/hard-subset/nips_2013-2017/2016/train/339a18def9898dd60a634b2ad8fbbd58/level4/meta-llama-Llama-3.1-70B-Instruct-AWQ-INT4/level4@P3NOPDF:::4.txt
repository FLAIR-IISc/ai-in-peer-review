This paper explores a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster divides a large task into smaller tasks, which are then assigned to workers. The workers complete their tasks and provide information to the crowdsourcer, who processes this information to complete the inference task. This setup allows for workers with varying skill levels and reliability in reporting their information. The primary objective is to ensure a certain level of fidelity or 'goodness' of the final outcome at the crowdsourcer, even when some workers provide incorrect information.

The paper defines the total number of queries presented to the workers as the budget of the crowdsourcing system. It studies the fundamental trade-off between the budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of the inference task). This trade-off is obtained by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source channel coding in information theory literature. The taskmaster is treated as a transmitter, and the crowdsourcer is mapped to a receiver/decoder. The unreliable links from the workers to the crowdsourcer constitute a communication channel. Given this mapping, a lower bound on the budget (number of queries) for a desirable fidelity level follows from standard results in information theory literature. This bound is independent of the schemes used by the taskmaster to design the queries and the algorithm used at the crowdsourcer to process the information collected from the workers.

The authors then consider a specific scheme at the taskmaster, called k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance. They compare the performance of this encoding scheme with the obtained information theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model. The parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers is quite interesting. This work may invite other researchers to study the crowdsourcing framework using information theoretic tools.

However, it is unclear whether the results presented in this paper model real crowdsourcing systems in a realistic way. One reason behind this could be that the paper wants the crowdsourcing problem to exactly map to the point-to-point communication problem, so that existing results from the information theory literature can be directly applied with minimal effort. The authors should comment on the following issues:

1. The reliability and skill level of the workers seem to be merged and modeled as a single channel. The paper assumes that the transmitter (taskmaster) allocates the same number of queries to all workers, irrespective of their skill level. This may not be true in real systems, where workers may carry some rating that represents their skill level.

2. How realistic are the channel models in taking the unreliability of the workers into account? What about workers who may have some knowledge of the task at hand and try to degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner?

The explanation of numerical results is not very clear. The labeling of various curves in Fig. 2 can be improved. Specifically, it is unclear what the $k=1$ case corresponds to. Does it mean that the worker directly gives the correct label of the presented query, as it is not possible to present a pair and ask if they share the same label in this case?

Some minor issues were also noted:

- In page 6, line 246, the authors mention that they assume knowledge of $P(B(X))$. This assumption should have been stated earlier in the paper.

- In page 4, line 135, the references to Figure 1 should be corrected (Figure 1.a --> Figure 1.b and Figure 1.b --> Figure 1.c).

- The authors repeatedly use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' even when these terms are not accompanied by a specific theorem or lemma number. Using 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' without numbers would be more appropriate in such cases.
