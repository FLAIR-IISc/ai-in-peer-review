

The paper investigates the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (inference quality) in crowdsourcing systems by modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem in information theory. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds for the minimum number of queries required to achieve a target fidelity level under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). They derive these bounds using standard rate-distortion theory and then analyze a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), comparing its performance to the theoretical bounds under a spammer-hammer channel model. The work builds on previous crowdsourcing research that has primarily focused on aggregation algorithms like majority voting and Dawid-Skene, but differs by taking an information-theoretic approach to establish fundamental limits rather than proposing specific inference algorithms.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling connection between crowdsourcing and information theory, which is novel within the crowdsourcing literature. The theoretical bounds derived in Theorems 2 and 3 represent a significant contribution by establishing fundamental limits that any crowdsourcing scheme must satisfy, regardless of the specific query design or inference algorithm used. The kIC scheme is well-motivated and the analysis of its performance provides valuable insights into how query design can improve crowdsourcing efficiency. The paper also demonstrates a clear understanding of related work, particularly the connection to Dawid-Skene and other aggregation approaches.

Weaknesses: The paper's modeling assumptions limit its applicability to real-world crowdsourcing systems. Specifically, the authors merge worker reliability and skill level into a single channel model without distinguishing between different types of worker errors or allowing for worker-specific query allocation based on skill (point 1 in draft review). The channel models (MSC and SHC) appear oversimplified compared to real worker behavior, particularly in their treatment of adversarial workers who might intentionally provide incorrect responses (point 2 in draft review). The numerical results (Figure 2) are difficult to interpret due to unclear labeling, and the paper does not adequately explain what the k=1 case represents (which corresponds to direct labeling queries rather than pairwise comparisons). The assumption about knowledge of P(B(X)) is introduced late in the paper (page 6, line 246) and should be stated earlier as a key modeling choice. There are also several minor issues: Figure references in the text (page 4, lines 135) are inconsistent (Figure 1.a should be 1.b, and 1.b should be 1.c), and the paper inconsistently uses "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" without proper numbering.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The theoretical framework is sound and establishes meaningful fundamental limits; the information-theoretic perspective is novel for crowdsourcing; the kIC analysis provides concrete insights into query design; the paper makes a clear contribution to understanding the fundamental trade-offs in crowdsourcing systems.

Con arguments for rejection: The modeling assumptions are too simplistic to reflect real crowdsourcing systems; the channel models do not adequately capture worker behavior; the numerical results are poorly explained; the paper contains several technical inconsistencies and minor errors.
