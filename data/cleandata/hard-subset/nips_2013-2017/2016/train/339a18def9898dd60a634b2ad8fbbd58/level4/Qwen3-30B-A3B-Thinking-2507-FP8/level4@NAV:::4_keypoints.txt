- Paper studies decomposing a large inference task into subtasks for crowdsourcing, aggregating noisy worker responses to reach target fidelity.  
- Models the taskmaster as transmitter and crowdsourcer as receiver, mapping budget–fidelity trade-off to joint source-channel coding and deriving an information-theoretic lower bound on required budget.  
- Analyzes a specific k-ary incidence coding scheme and compares its performance to the derived bound under a particular channel model.  
- Positive: the analogy to rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers is conceptually interesting and potentially inspiring.  
- Major concern: worker reliability and skill are conflated into a single channel model, ignoring heterogeneous skill ratings found in real systems.  
- Major concern: taskmaster allocates identical query volumes to all workers irrespective of skill, which is unrealistic.  
- Major concern: channel models do not capture adversarial workers who might intentionally degrade fidelity after understanding the task.  
- Major concern: numerical results lack clear explanation, and Figure 2 curve labels are unclear (e.g., what does k=1 represent?).  
- Minor issue: assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) on page 6, line 246 should be stated earlier.  
- Minor issue: figure references on page 4, line 135 are incorrect ("Figure 1.a"→"Figure 1.b", "Figure 1.b"→"Figure 1.c").  
- Minor issue: inconsistent use of unnumbered "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" — should use lowercase or provide numbers for consistency.
