The paper studies a crowdsourcing framework in which a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller queries assigned to a pool of workers. A crowdsourcer aggregates the workers’ responses—despite heterogeneous skill levels and potential unreliability—to complete the inference with a target fidelity. The system’s objective is to achieve a specified accuracy despite noisy or erroneous inputs, by carefully designing the queries (tasks) and the aggregation/inference procedure. The paper defines the total number of queries as the crowdsourcing budget and analyzes the fundamental trade-off between budget (queries per item) and fidelity (quality of the inferred labels). This is done by mapping the system to a joint source–channel coding problem from information theory: the taskmaster plays the role of the transmitter/encoder, the workers induce a noisy channel, and the crowdsourcer is the receiver/decoder. Under this mapping, lower bounds on the necessary budget to achieve a desired fidelity follow from classical information-theoretic results and do not depend on the specific querying or inference algorithms. The authors then study a particular querying scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), analyze its performance, and compare it against the information-theoretic lower bound under a specific worker-channel model. The parallel drawn between rate–distortion theory and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers is appealing and may stimulate further use of information-theoretic tools in this area.

That said, it is not clear how faithfully the proposed models capture real-world crowdsourcing systems. Part of the concern is that the paper seeks an exact reduction to a point-to-point communication model, which simplifies analysis but may overconstrain the modeling choices. It would be helpful if the authors could address the following points.

1) The modeling appears to conflate worker skill and response reliability into a single channel abstraction, and the taskmaster allocates an equal number of queries to all workers irrespective of their skill. In practice, platforms often maintain worker ratings or histories that inform assignment and pricing; how would the framework change if the taskmaster could adapt query rates to heterogeneous (possibly partially known) skill levels?

2) The realism of the channel models warrants discussion. Beyond symmetric noise or spammer–hammer mixtures, some workers may possess partial task knowledge or act adversarially to reduce fidelity. How robust are the results to such behaviors, and can the analysis accommodate adversarial or strategic workers?
