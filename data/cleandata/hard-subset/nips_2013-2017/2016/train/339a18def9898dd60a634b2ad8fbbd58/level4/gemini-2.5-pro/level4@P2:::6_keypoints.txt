- Novel framework: applies an information-theoretic, joint source-channel coding view to crowdsourcing budget-fidelity trade-offs.  
- Theoretical rigor: derivations (Theorems 2 and 3) correctly apply rate-distortion and channel coding theory.  
- Insight on skill knowledge: SL-UK performance limited by the capacity of an “average worker,” SL-CS by the “average of workers’ capacities.”  
- Main concern: the channel-coding analogy may be unrealistic—practical/cognitive constraints on query design are not modeled, questioning the practical tightness of the bounds.  
- Query-design constraints: real human responses violate memoryless assumptions and limit use of typical coding strategies (e.g., repetition, LDPC).  
- Cognitive load issue: complex schemes like k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) may change worker error behavior and be impractical for large k.  
- Restricted encoder set: the space of feasible crowdsourcing “codes” (query mappings) is likely much smaller than assumed and not discussed.  
- Lack of empirical validation: no comparison to standard algorithms (e.g., Dawid & Skene EM, spectral methods) or benchmark datasets to assess bound tightness.  
- Need for experiments: compare theoretical limits to empirical performance to establish practical relevance and gap.  
- kIC practicality unresolved: analysis gives lower bounds but lacks user studies or realistic simulations showing worker behavior with complex queries.  
- Pricing model weak: Appendix E’s pricing justification is heuristic and needs stronger justification or empirical support.  
- Notational/detail clarifications: rate-distortion minimization (Eq. 8) should clarify minimization over P(Ẑ|Z) under distortion constraint.  
- Proof clarity requests: expand proof of Theorem 4 and clarify the relationship between ε̂ and ε̄_S for the given rate condition.  
- Jensen/entropy step: in Theorem 2’s proof, explicitly note the convexity/Jensen argument for H_M(E(ε)) vs E(H_M(ε)) or provide a citation.
