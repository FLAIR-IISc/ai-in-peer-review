This work provides an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff for label crowdsourcing, leveraging rate-distortion theory and channel coding theorem. The derivation of this bound is sound. If this is the first application of these tools to the crowdsourcing problem, it represents a potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the novelty and practical significance of the paper remain questionable, as the derivation appears relatively straightforward and the bound risks being overly loose. 

The core issue lies in the differences between crowdsourcing and traditional channel coding. In channel coding, we have considerable flexibility in designing codebooks and codewords – employing techniques like repetition codes, Hamming codes, convolutional codes, RS codes, and low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes. It’s unclear if we possess comparable freedom in designing the sequence of channel inputs, *u<sup>n</sup>*, in the crowdsourcing context. Specifically, equation (3) suggests that almost any mapping from B(X)<sup>n</sup> to U<sup>(∑mi)</sup> should be a valid, implementable codebook; otherwise, the channel capacity will be a loose bound. While a repetition-code-like approach is feasible in crowdsourcing, with majority voting as a natural decoding method, it’s unclear how to design *u<sup>n</sup>* to further improve error correction. 

A crucial distinction between channel coding and crowdsourcing is that we cannot repeatedly assign the same query to the same worker. Doing so would violate the i.i.d. assumption in equations (1) or (2), as a worker is likely to provide the same answer to an identical query. Conversely, in communication channels, we can generally assume an i.i.d. channel and reuse queries multiple times. This is just one example illustrating how crowdsourcing likely faces significantly more fundamental constraints than channel coding, where encoding mappings from {0,1}<sup>nR</sup> to {0,1}<sup>n</sup> are relatively unconstrained. Consequently, the set of possible encoding schemes in equation (3) is likely subject to important restrictions, increasing the risk that the proposed information-theoretic bound will be very loose.

Ultimately, a stronger evaluation would involve comparing the performance of state-of-the-art methods against the derived information-theoretic bound.
