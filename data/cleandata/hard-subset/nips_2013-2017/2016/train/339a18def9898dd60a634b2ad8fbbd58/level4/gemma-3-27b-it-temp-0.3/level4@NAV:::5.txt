This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of digital crowdsourcing, establishing lower bounds on the number of queries required per task to achieve a specified error probability. The work models workers as independent parallel channels, specifically utilizing symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMC) to represent worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to model malicious workers. The analysis focuses on nonadaptive coding schemes and considers two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer knows the skill levels of all workers, and another where this information is unavailable. In both scenarios, the taskmaster is assumed to have no prior knowledge of worker skills, assigning an equal number of tasks to each. A crucial result is a necessary condition for the minimum required rate – queries per task – when employing k-ary incidence coding with a worker pool modeled as SHC(q). 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. However, the absence of experimental validation or simulations comparing the derived bounds to existing crowdsourcing systems (e.g., Amazon Mechanical Turk) is a limitation. Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could benefit from concision, with a greater emphasis on providing proof sketches for the presented theorems. 

The work appears novel; to the best of my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this is the first information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem. This approach has significant practical value, potentially enabling designers of crowdsourcing projects to determine the minimum query rate needed to meet performance targets and informing pricing strategies. I have not reviewed the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the completeness of the proofs.
