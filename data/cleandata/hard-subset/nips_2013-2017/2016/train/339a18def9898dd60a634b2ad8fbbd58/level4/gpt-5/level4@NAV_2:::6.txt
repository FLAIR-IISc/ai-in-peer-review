This paper develops information-theoretic limits on the budget–fidelity trade-off for label crowdsourcing by leveraging rate–distortion theory and channel coding results. The bounds are carefully derived. If this is indeed the first work to apply rate–distortion and channel coding theorems to the crowdsourcing setting, it could be a reasonably meaningful contribution. That said, both the novelty and the practical impact remain uncertain because the derivations are relatively direct and the resulting limits may be quite loose. The central concern is that, unlike classical channel coding—where one has broad freedom to design codebooks/codewords (e.g., repetition, Hamming, convolutional, Reed–Solomon, LDPC)—it is unclear whether comparable freedom exists for constructing the sequence of channel inputs u^n. In (3), nearly all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(∑ mi) (aside from degenerate ones) would need to be valid/implementable encoders; otherwise, capacity-style bounds risk being too weak. While repetition-like querying is feasible in crowdsourcing and naturally decoded by majority voting, beyond this it is unclear how to design u^n to enhance error-correcting capability. In particular, a notable discrepancy with standard channel coding is that we cannot assign the identical query to the same worker multiple times without compromising the iid assumptions in (1) or (2), since a worker would likely produce the same response to the same query; in contrast, communication channels are typically modeled as iid across uses, allowing repeated inputs to the same channel. This is just one example; more broadly, crowdsourcing likely imposes substantive constraints on (3) that do not arise in conventional encoders from {0,1}^{nR} to {0,1}^n. Consequently, the feasible encoding space may be much narrower, and the proposed information-theoretic limits risk being overly loose. Finally, it would strengthen the paper to compare state-of-the-art algorithmic performance against the derived theoretical limits.
