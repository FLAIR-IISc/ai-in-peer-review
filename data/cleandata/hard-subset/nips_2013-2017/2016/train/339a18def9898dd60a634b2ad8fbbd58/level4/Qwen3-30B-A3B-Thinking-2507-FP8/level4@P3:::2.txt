

The authors present a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. They model the crowdsourcing process as a communication system where the dataset (source) is encoded into queries, transmitted through noisy channels (workers with varying skill levels), and decoded to reconstruct the desired labels. The paper establishes fundamental limits on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity, considering two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown to the taskmaster but known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS), and when they are unknown to both (SL-UK). Additionally, the authors analyze the performance of k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a specific query scheme where workers are asked to identify items with the same label when presented with k items simultaneously.

This work builds upon previous crowdsourcing literature that typically focuses on heuristic inference algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting) or optimization frameworks for task allocation (e.g., Karger et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2014). While prior work has examined specific algorithms for crowdsourcing inference, this paper provides the first information-theoretic characterization of fundamental performance limits, offering a theoretical foundation for understanding the trade-offs between query budget and fidelity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting crowdsourcing to information theory and establishing fundamental limits through JSCC. This is a fresh perspective that moves beyond algorithmic proposals to address the theoretical boundaries of the problem. The kIC analysis provides concrete insights into how query design affects performance.

2. **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the fundamental limits (Theorems 2 and 3) is technically sound, and the authors correctly identify the role of worker skill levels as channel states in the JSCC framework. The information-theoretic approach provides a rigorous foundation that complements the empirical work in the crowdsourcing literature.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the model and key concepts. The connection between crowdsourcing and information theory is made accessible through careful definitions and examples.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Justification for Source-Channel Separation Theorem:** The paper uses the standard source-channel separation theorem to derive its results (Theorems 2 and 3), but fails to justify its applicability when the channel state (worker skill levels) is unknown at the encoder. While the separation theorem likely still holds in this context, the authors should either provide a proof or cite an existing result that establishes this for random channel states with partial state knowledge at the decoder. This is a critical gap in the theoretical foundation.

2. **Missing Side Information:** The model assumes the taskmaster (encoder) only sees the source labels (B(X)), while the crowdsourcer (decoder) only sees worker responses. However, both parties typically have access to the original items (X), which constitutes side information that could significantly improve performance. The paper should consider how this side information could be incorporated into the JSCC model, particularly since side information at the decoder can substantially reduce the required query budget.

3. **Ambiguous Interpretation of Bounds:** In Section 4.2, the authors state that "the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow," which is confusing. The lower bound itself doesn't improve; rather, the error probability bound decreases (meaning better performance is achievable) as k and q increase. This should be clarified to avoid misinterpretation of the results.

4. **Limited Practical Relevance:** The asymptotic analysis (L → ∞) provides fundamental limits but may not be immediately useful for practical crowdsourcing applications where datasets are finite. The authors should address how their results translate to non-asymptotic regimes, possibly referencing recent work by Kostina and Verdu (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution by establishing fundamental information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing. The JSCC framework provides a valuable new perspective on the problem, and the results on kIC offer practical insights for query design. However, the paper's theoretical foundation would be significantly strengthened by addressing the gaps related to the source-channel separation theorem and side information. The asymptotic nature of the results also limits their immediate applicability, though this could be addressed with a brief discussion of non-asymptotic extensions.

The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. As it stands, the technical contributions are significant but the theoretical justification is incomplete, and the practical relevance is somewhat limited.

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The authors should address the gaps in the theoretical justification (particularly the source-channel separation theorem for random channel states), incorporate side information into the model, clarify the interpretation of bounds, and briefly discuss non-asymptotic implications. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
