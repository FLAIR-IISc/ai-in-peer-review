This paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker outputs in crowdsourcing by applying the language and tools of information theory to derive fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. The authors model the crowdsourcing system as a communication problem, where workers are noisy channels, queries are an encoding scheme, and inference is a decoding scheme. Using this framework, they establish theoretical bounds on the performance achievable by any querying and inference algorithm for a given budget.

While some aspects of the paper were difficult to follow (detailed below), the information-theoretic model and the resulting insights present a very interesting and potentially useful perspective on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. The paper could be improved by more prominently highlighting the high-level intuition stemming from the theoretical results, such as the observation at the end of Section 3. A dedicated conclusion section summarizing the key takeaways would also be beneficial, despite the space constraints.

I appreciate the authors' effort to make the paper accessible to readers unfamiliar with information theory. However, the presentation remains difficult to follow for non-experts. The clarity could be significantly improved by restating many of the results in the context of the crowdsourcing application, rather than relying heavily on the terminology of channels, codes, and distortion.

The related work section could be strengthened by discussing the alternative line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often termed "crowdsourcing mechanism design." This body of work also addresses issues like varying worker abilities and, more recently, has explored how to combine incentive design with sophisticated aggregation methods.

The term "crowdsourcing" itself (a portmanteau of "crowd" and "outsourcing") makes this two-party distinction less intuitive.
