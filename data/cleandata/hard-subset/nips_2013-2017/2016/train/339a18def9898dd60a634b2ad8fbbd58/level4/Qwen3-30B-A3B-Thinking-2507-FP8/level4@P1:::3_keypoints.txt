- Paper models crowdsourcing as communication over parallel noisy channels and studies the budget–fidelity tradeoff using information theory.  
- Workers’ skill levels are treated as channel characteristics; bounds derived for unknown skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
- Derives distortion-rate (rate-distortion) bounds on minimum queries needed to reach a target error probability.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides performance bounds under a spammer–hammer channel model.  
- Novel theoretical framework that applies information-theoretic limits to crowdsourcing.  
- Rigorous, well-motivated derivations with formal proofs for the presented bounds.  
- Connects to prior crowdsourcing work but offers a distinct theoretical perspective.  
- Paper is overly abstract and relies heavily on information-theoretic terminology without translating results into practical crowdsourcing metrics.  
- The distinction between “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” is unclear and potentially misleading.  
- Lacks experimental validation, concrete examples, or actionable guidance for real-world system design.  
- Figure 2 and its caption lack sufficient explanation, making results hard to interpret.  
- Paper would benefit from re-expressing results in interpretable terms (e.g., queries per item for a target accuracy).  
- Limited discussion of related work on economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing.  
- Overall technically sound but presentation and lack of practical insights limit impact.  
- Recommendation: reject, with suggestion to resubmit after significantly revising presentation and adding practical guidance.  
- Minor issues: several typos and unclear sentences (Line 84 grammar, Line 106 term definitions, Line 122 typo, Lines 275–276 phrasing) and insufficient caption for Figure 2.
