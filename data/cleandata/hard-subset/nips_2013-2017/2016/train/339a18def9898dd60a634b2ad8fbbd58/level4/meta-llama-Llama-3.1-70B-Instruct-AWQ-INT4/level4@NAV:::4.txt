This paper explores a crowdsourcing framework where a taskmaster decomposes a complex task into smaller sub-tasks, which are then assigned to workers. The crowdsourcer aggregates the workers' responses and processes the information to complete the task. The system accommodates workers with varying skill levels and reliability in reporting their findings. The primary objective is to ensure a certain level of fidelity in the final outcome, despite potential inaccuracies in the workers' responses. To achieve this, the taskmaster must carefully design the sub-tasks, taking into account the trade-off between the budget (number of queries) and fidelity.

The authors establish a fundamental relationship between budget and fidelity by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source-channel coding in information theory. By treating the taskmaster as a transmitter and the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, they derive a lower bound on the budget for a desired fidelity level. This bound is independent of the query design scheme and the information processing algorithm used by the crowdsourcer.

The authors then analyze the performance of a specific scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and compare it to the obtained information-theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model. The reviewer finds the parallel between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers intriguing, and this work may inspire further research on crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools.

However, the reviewer questions the realism of the model, as it may oversimplify real-world crowdsourcing systems. One concern is that the reliability and skill level of workers are merged and modeled as a single channel, assuming the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their skill level. In reality, workers may have varying skill levels, and the taskmaster may need to adapt the query allocation accordingly.

Another concern is the realism of the channel models used to account for worker unreliability. The reviewer wonders about workers who may have knowledge of the task and intentionally degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner. The explanation of numerical results is unclear, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 could be improved. Specifically, the case of k=1 is not well-explained, and it is unclear what it means for a worker to directly provide the correct label for a query.

Additionally, the reviewer notes that the assumption of knowledge about P(B(X)) should have been stated earlier in the paper. There are also some minor errors, such as incorrect figure references and the misuse of terms like "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary." The reviewer suggests using more descriptive language, such as "theorem," "lemma," and "corollary," when not referencing specific theorem or lemma numbers.
