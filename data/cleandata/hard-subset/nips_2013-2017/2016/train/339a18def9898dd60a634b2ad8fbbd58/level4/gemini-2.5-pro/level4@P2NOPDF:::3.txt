This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing, characterizing the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. I found the core approach to be novel and the model itself to be an interesting and potentially impactful take on the design of aggregation techniques. However, the paper's heavy reliance on specialized terminology made it difficult to fully grasp the technical details and their broader implications.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Accessibility and Framing:** My primary concern is the paper's accessibility to the broader machine learning and crowdsourcing community. While I appreciate the authors' effort to make the material accessible, the heavy and persistent use of information-theoretic language (e.g., "channels," "codes," "distortion") tends to obscure the practical implications of the results. To significantly increase the paper's impact, I strongly recommend restating the main findings and insights in the language of the crowdsourcing application. For example, concepts could be framed directly in terms of task complexity, worker reliability, budget constraints, and final aggregation accuracy.

2.  **Highlighting High-Level Intuition:** The valuable, high-level intuitions derived from the theoretical results are not given sufficient prominence. The insights mentioned, for instance, at the end of Section 3, are precisely the kind of takeaways that would be most useful to the NIPS audience. These should be brought to the forefront, perhaps in the introduction and abstract. Furthermore, the paper would benefit greatly from a dedicated conclusion section that summarizes what a practitioner or researcher should learn from this work. Even a brief summary of key takeaways would be immensely helpful.

3.  **Related Work:** The related work section should be expanded to include the parallel and increasingly convergent line of research on designing robust economic incentives for crowdsourcing workers. This field, often referred to as "mechanism design for crowdsourcing," also deals with challenges like varying worker abilities and has recently begun to explore how to integrate sophisticated aggregation methods with incentive structures. Situating the current work relative to this literature would provide a more complete context for its contributions.

**Minor Comments and Clarifications:**

*   **Line 84:** "is reviewed in" should likely be "are reviewed in".
*   **Line 106:** The distinction between the "crowdsourcer" and the "taskmaster" was confusing, particularly in the initial setup. The term "crowdsourcing" itself (a portmanteau of "crowd" and "outsourcing") suggests a direct relationship, so the role of an intermediary taskmaster needs a clearer definition and justification.
*   **Line 122:** There appears to be a typo in "A A(X1,X2)".
*   **Lines 275-276:** The sentence beginning "In case..." was very difficult to parse. I recommend rephrasing it for clarity.
*   **Figure 2:** It was difficult to glean much information from this figure. A more detailed caption explaining the axes, the plotted quantities, and the key message the reader should take away from the visualization would be very helpful.
