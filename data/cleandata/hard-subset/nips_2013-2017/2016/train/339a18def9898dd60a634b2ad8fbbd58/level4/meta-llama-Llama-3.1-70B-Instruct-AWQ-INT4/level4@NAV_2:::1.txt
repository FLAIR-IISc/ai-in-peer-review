This paper models the problem of labeling data from crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework. The authors represent each worker as a noisy channel, specifically considering two types of channels: a symmetric memoryless channel without feedback (MSC) and a spammer-hammer channel model (SHC). By casting the problem as a coding problem for these channels, they study the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, mapping the budget used onto the rate of the code for analysis. The paper explores distortion versus budget trade-offs and introduces a basic coding scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where crowdsourced tasks involve labeling a subset of k items at a time.

The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the problem, relating it to previous work in the field. The authors establish a clear connection between their model and real-world applications, such as Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) and online rating platforms like Yelp. They also discuss the challenges associated with crowdsourcing, including the unknown skill levels of workers, possible spamming, and the lack of ground truth.

The technical content of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem. They derive information-theoretic bounds for the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity in crowdsourcing. Specifically, they present Theorems 2 and 3, which provide fundamental limits for the cases where worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS), respectively. The paper also examines the performance of kIC with a spammer-hammer worker pool, deriving a lower bound on its performance.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model, assumptions, and results. The text is well-written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with information theory and coding. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the kIC scheme, including numerical results and a discussion of its performance.

Strengths of the paper include its novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a coding problem, its thorough analysis of the problem, and its introduction of a new coding scheme (kIC). The paper also provides a clear connection between the theoretical results and real-world applications.

Weaknesses of the paper include its limited scope, as it primarily focuses on labeling tasks. Additionally, the paper assumes a simplified model of worker behavior, which may not accurately capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper provides a new perspective on crowdsourcing by applying information-theoretic principles to the problem. The introduction of kIC as a coding scheme is also a novel contribution.

Regarding significance, the paper's results have implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems, particularly in terms of optimizing budget allocation and query assignment. The paper's findings can also inform the development of more efficient coding schemes for crowdsourcing applications.

To improve the paper, the authors could consider expanding their analysis to more complex tasks, such as clustering or regression. They could also explore more realistic models of worker behavior, incorporating factors like worker fatigue, bias, and variability in skill levels. Additionally, the authors could provide more detailed numerical results, including comparisons with existing crowdsourcing algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a coding problem.
* The authors derive information-theoretic bounds for the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity in crowdsourcing.
* The paper introduces a new coding scheme (kIC) and provides a detailed analysis of its performance.
* The results have implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems and the development of more efficient coding schemes.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's scope is limited to labeling tasks.
* The authors assume a simplified model of worker behavior.
* The paper could benefit from more detailed numerical results and comparisons with existing crowdsourcing algorithms.
