The authors present an information-theoretic framework for the crowdsourcing problem and derive fundamental limits on the number of queries per item (asymptotically) under this model. Specifically, they cast crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The channel state is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. Item labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster acts as the encoder; the crowd workers correspond to multiple channel uses; worker skill levels constitute random channel states; and the crowdsourcer functions as the decoder. Within this setup, the authors leverage known JSCC coding-rate results to establish the asymptotic query-per-item limits for crowdsourcing. They also derive a lower bound on the error probability of the “k-ary incidence coding” scheme under a particular crowdsourcing/channel model. Overall, the paper is clearly written and appears to be an initial step toward modeling crowdsourcing via JSCC.

Some technical comments:
1. The crowdsourcing setup is modeled as JSCC with random channel state, and the authors invoke the standard source–channel separation theorem to obtain Theorems 2 and 3. However, the paper should first justify that separation continues to hold when the channel has a random state that is unknown at the encoder and possibly known or unknown at the decoder. I believe separation does hold in these scenarios, but the authors should either provide a proof or cite an appropriate reference.
2. The model treats the taskmaster as an encoder who observes only item labels and the crowdsourcer as a decoder who observes only worker-produced labels. This may be overly restrictive, as it overlooks the possibility that both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer can access the original items. Such access induces side information at the encoder/decoder in the JSCC formulation. While side information available only at the encoder may not help, it can be beneficial if available at both ends or even at the decoder alone. The model would be more convincing and realistic if side information were incorporated.
3. In Sec. 4.2, when discussing Fig. 2, the paper states that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q increase. Presumably this means the lower bound decreases with larger k and q, suggesting potential performance gains for the code. However, this does not imply that the lower bound itself “improves” in an absolute sense, since there is no accompanying upper bound for comparison.

Edited following authors’ response: The use of JSCC to model crowdsourcing is interesting, but—as another reviewer noted—a nonasymptotic characterization of CS fundamentals would be more useful. The authors should consider recent finite-blocklength converses for JSCC, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
