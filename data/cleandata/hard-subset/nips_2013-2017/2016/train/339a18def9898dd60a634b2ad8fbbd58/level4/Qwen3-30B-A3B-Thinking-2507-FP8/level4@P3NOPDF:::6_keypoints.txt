- Proposes an information-theoretic budget–fidelity bound for crowdsourcing using rate–distortion and channel coding; derivation is mathematically rigorous and could be significant if novel.  
- Main concerns: potential looseness of the bound and weak justification for applying channel coding principles to crowdsourcing.  
- Core modeling treats crowdsourcing as a communication channel with arbitrary encodings u^n (Eq.3), which ignores crowdsourcing-specific constraints.  
- Critical constraint: you cannot safely repeat identical queries to the same worker without inducing correlated (non-i.i.d.) responses, unlike reusable channel inputs in coding.  
- Encoding freedom assumed in channel coding (e.g., repetition, LDPC, convolutional codes) is not available in crowdsourcing due to query-repetition and response-correlation limits.  
- The claim that “almost all mappings” are valid (Eq.3) is problematic because practical implementation of structured error-correcting encodings likely requires distinct queries and may be infeasible.  
- The paper fails to quantify how the no-repetition/response-correlation constraint reduces the feasible encoding space and effective channel capacity, making the bound possibly optimistic.  
- Recommended analysis: explicitly characterize feasible encodings under crowdsourcing (distinct-query limits, minimal query diversity for i.i.d., impact on capacity).  
- Without such analysis or empirical comparison, the bound remains theoretically interesting but of unclear practical relevance; compare against Dawid–Skene and Bayesian methods to assess tightness.  
- Minor fixes: Eq.(8) should state minimization over C, C' subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Theorem 4 needs clarification on how ε_S relates to \hat{ε} (note ln(1−q)<0) and whether ε_S > \hat{ε}.  
- Eq.(17): justify or cite the term H_M(E(ε)) and its relevance/definition in the crowdsourcing context.  
- Overall: framework is sound but overextends the channel coding analogy; to be impactful the authors must model encoding constraints, derive tighter bounds, and provide empirical validation.
