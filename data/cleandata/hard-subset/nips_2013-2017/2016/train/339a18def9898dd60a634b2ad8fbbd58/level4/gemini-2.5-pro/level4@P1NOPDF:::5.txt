This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing digital crowdsourcing, providing lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a given probability of error. The framework models workers as parallel, independent channels. The analysis considers two types of channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to model worker errors, and a spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to model a worker pool where each individual is a spammer with probability 1-q.

The analysis is based on nonadaptive coding and examines two key scenarios. In the first, the aggregator (who determines the final task outcome) knows the skill level of each worker. In the second, the aggregator has no knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks) is assumed to be unaware of worker abilities, resulting in a uniform task distribution. Finally, the paper derives a necessary condition on the minimum rate for k-ary incidence coding under the SHC(q) worker model.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. To my knowledge, the analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective is a novel contribution, as claimed by the authors. This theoretical approach is highly valuable, as it provides fundamental limits that can guide practitioners. For example, designers of crowdsourcing platforms can use these bounds to estimate the minimum rate needed to accomplish a task with a desired accuracy, which in turn can help in the pricing and design of such projects.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions**

The primary weakness of the paper is the absence of experiments or simulations. The theoretical bounds would be much more impactful if compared against the performance of practical schemes used on platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk. Such a comparison would provide valuable context on how far current systems are from the theoretical limits.

The paper's presentation could also be improved. Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 is either not essential or could be significantly condensed. The space saved would be better used to provide proof sketches for the main theorems within the body of the paper. Including these sketches would make the theoretical contributions more accessible to readers and allow them to grasp the core ideas of the proofs without needing to consult the appendix.

**Note on Proofs**

I did not review the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the correctness of the full proofs.
