

The paper investigates a crowdsourcing framework where a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller subtasks assigned to workers, who provide potentially unreliable responses. The crowdsourcer aggregates these responses to achieve a desired inference fidelity, despite worker unreliability and varying skill levels. The core contribution establishes a fundamental trade-off between the budget (total queries allocated) and fidelity by mapping the system to joint source-channel coding in information theory. Here, the taskmaster acts as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver, and worker unreliability as a communication channel. This mapping yields an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget required for a target fidelity, independent of specific query-design or aggregation schemes. The authors then analyze a specific scheme—k-ary incidence coding—and compare its performance against this bound under a particular channel model.  

The parallel between rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing is insightful and may inspire further application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing. However, the realism of the modeling assumptions warrants discussion. Specifically:  
1. The paper conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel parameter, assuming uniform query allocation across workers regardless of individual skill. Real systems typically incorporate worker ratings or skill profiles, which could enable adaptive query allocation.  
2. The channel model does not account for adversarial worker behavior (e.g., workers with task knowledge deliberately degrading fidelity). The realism of the assumed channel models requires justification.  

Additionally, several presentation issues need addressing:  
- The explanation of numerical results is unclear. Figure~2’s curve labels should be clarified (e.g., what does $k=1$ correspond to? For $k=1$, the worker directly responds to the query without coding, not that responses are always correct).  
- On page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ should be stated earlier in the paper.  
- On page 4, lines 135: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1.b" and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".  
- The manuscript repeatedly uses "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" without associated numbering; these terms should be replaced with "theorem," "lemma," or "corollary" for consistency.  

While the theoretical framework is novel, the authors should address these points to strengthen the paper’s relevance to practical crowdsourcing systems.
