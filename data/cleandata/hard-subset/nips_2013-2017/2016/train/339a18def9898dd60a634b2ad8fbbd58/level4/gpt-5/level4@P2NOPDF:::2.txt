This paper develops an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing by modeling it as joint source–channel coding (JSCC) with random channel state, and derives asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item. In the model, item labels form an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster acts as an encoder, crowd workers correspond to multiple uses of a channel, worker skill levels are random channel states, and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. The channel state is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. Using known results on JSCC coding rates, the authors obtain fundamental limits on the asymptotic query complexity. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model. Overall, the paper is clearly written and represents a promising first step in applying JSCC to crowdsourcing.

Technical comments:
1) Justification of separation with state. The main limits (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on source–channel separation. However, the channel here has random state unknown at the encoder and possibly known at the decoder. The authors should justify that the separation theorem applies in this setting—either by providing a proof or by citing an established result that covers channels with random state under the stated knowledge patterns. I believe separation still holds under these assumptions, but the paper should make this explicit and precise.

2) Missing side information from the original items. The model assumes the taskmaster/encoder observes only the true labels and the crowdsourcer/decoder observes only the workers’ noisy outputs. In practice, both parties often have access to the original items (e.g., images or text), which can serve as side information in the JSCC formulation. While side information known only at the encoder may not help, side information at the decoder—or at both encoder and decoder—can significantly improve performance. Incorporating such side information would make the model more realistic and potentially change the fundamental limits, and would strengthen the paper’s applicability.

3) Interpretation of kIC lower bounds. In Sec. 4.2 and Fig. 2, the paper states that the lower bounds for kIC “improve” as k and q increase. Presumably this means the lower bound decreases with larger k and q, which suggests the code’s performance could be better in that regime. However, without a corresponding upper bound, it is misleading to say the lower bound itself “improves”; it merely becomes smaller. Please clarify the wording to avoid overinterpretation of what the bound conveys.

Edited following the authors’ response: The use of JSCC to model crowdsourcing is interesting and timely. That said, a nonasymptotic (finite-sample/finite-blocklength) characterization of crowdsourcing limits would be particularly valuable for practice. I recommend connecting the asymptotic results with recent finite-blocklength converses for JSCC, such as:
- V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, 59(5):2545–2575, 2013.
Bridging to these tools could provide meaningful accuracy–query tradeoffs at realistic scales and further elevate the paper’s impact.
