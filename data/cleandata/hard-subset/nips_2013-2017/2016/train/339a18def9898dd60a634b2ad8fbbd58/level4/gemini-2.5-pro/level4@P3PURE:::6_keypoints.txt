- Paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity tradeoff for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and channel coding; derivation is rigorous and well-presented.  
- If this is the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, it could be a significant contribution.  
- Novelty is questionable because the derivation is a relatively straightforward application of established theory.  
- Practical value is doubtful because the resulting bound may be too loose to guide real systems.  
- The core issue is the analogy between crowdsourcing and channel coding: crowdsourcing lacks the freedom in designing codebooks that makes channel capacity bounds tight.  
- In crowdsourcing, valid encoding/assignment schemes are constrained by worker behavior, unlike the largely unconstrained encoding maps in channel coding.  
- Assigning the same query repeatedly to the same worker violates the i.i.d. worker model (answers will be highly correlated), limiting the applicability of repetition-like coding.  
- While simple repetition across different workers and majority voting are feasible, designing more sophisticated error-correcting schemes for worker responses (u^n) is unclear.  
- Because of these fundamental constraints on the design space of query assignments, the theoretical bound may be very loose in practice.  
- The authors should compare the bound to empirical performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms to quantify the gap between theory and practice.
