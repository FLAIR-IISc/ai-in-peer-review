Summary
This paper models labeling crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoffs. The dataset labels are viewed as a discrete source; worker responses are modeled as outputs of parallel noisy channels. Two worker models are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) capturing stochastic errors, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), where a worker is perfect with probability q and otherwise answers uniformly at random. The code is non-adaptive (no feedback). The authors analyze two scenarios: SL-UK, where the crowdsourcer does not know worker skill levels; and SL-CS, where skill levels are perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (not to the taskmaster). They derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) to achieve a target error probability under Hamming distortion. They also study k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query shows k items and asks about co-cluster relations, and provide a necessary condition on the rate needed under the SHC(q) model. They further discuss implications for query pricing.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to a large literature on aggregation/inference from noisy crowd labels (Dawid–Skene EM; Karger–Oh–Shah; Raykar et al.; Whitehill et al.; Zhang et al. spectral/EM), which typically analyzes algorithmic performance under generative worker models or task allocation. In contrast, this work adopts a rate–distortion perspective (Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim) to establish fundamental limits independently of any specific inference algorithm. To my knowledge, casting crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel problem and deriving rate–distortion style lower bounds is novel or at least rare in the NIPS literature. The analysis of kIC relates to pairwise/graph-based clustering with partial labels (e.g., Smyth et al.; Vinayak et al.), but here the focus is on limits rather than algorithms.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation that separates source complexity, channel noise, and budget via established information-theoretic tools.
- General lower bounds for SL-UK vs SL-CS that cleanly expose the value of estimating worker skills (average capacity of an “average worker” vs average of capacities).
- The kIC analysis provides intuition for how structured queries induce error-detecting capability and how rate scales with k and worker quality q.
- Potentially useful guidance for practitioners (e.g., budget planning, rough pricing rules) independent of a particular decoding algorithm.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Lack of empirical or simulation evidence: No experiments compare practical schemes (majority vote, DS-EM, spectral/EM) to the derived limits under MSC/SHC. Even synthetic simulations would help quantify gaps and demonstrate tightness.
- Proof accessibility: Section 3.1 recaps standard information theory at length; space would be better used for proof sketches of Theorems 2–4, highlighting the key ideas (e.g., Fano-type converses, separation/achievability constructions, mixture-channel capacity for SL-UK vs SL-CS).
- Cost model and rate normalization: For kIC, each query involves k items. Although Appendix E addresses pricing, a concise main-text derivation mapping “queries per item” across different k to an equivalent budget would improve fairness of comparisons and readability.
- Modeling assumptions: Independence across workers and tasks, stationarity of worker noise, and non-adaptivity are strong; a brief discussion of how adaptivity or task-difficulty-dependent channels (feedforward) could change the bounds would add value.
- Notation clarity: The roles of N (number of classes), M (number of response options), and their relationship in different settings should be clarified in the main text near Theorems 2–4 to avoid confusion.
- Practicality of SL-CS: Perfect knowledge of worker skills by the crowdsourcer is idealized; it would help to discuss robustness if skill estimates are imperfect (e.g., bounds that degrade gracefully with estimation error).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of rate–distortion and separation to derive necessary conditions appears sound; the SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction is insightful. Please add high-level proof sketches and explicit assumptions for the converse steps (e.g., how the MSC mixture is handled in SL-UK).
- Completeness: The core bounds are presented, but the lack of empirical evaluation and limited discussion of tightness leave open questions about practical attainability.
- Evaluation: No simulations; adding them would strengthen the paper significantly.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. Section 2.4 could be streamlined, and Section 3.1 shortened or moved to an appendix in favor of proof sketches, examples, and intuitive explanations of the bounds.
- Minor editorial issues (typos in references; consistent notation for rates and budgets) should be fixed.

Originality
- Framing crowdsourcing in an explicit rate–distortion framework and deriving budget–fidelity limits is, to my knowledge, new in this community. The kIC analysis under SHC(q) is also novel and informative.

Significance
- The bounds provide a principled baseline for algorithm designers and can inform task design and pricing. If complemented with simulations and tighter comparisons, this work could serve as a reference for understanding limits of crowdsourced labeling.

Questions for the authors
- How tight are the lower bounds in practice? Can you provide synthetic experiments under MSC/SHC comparing majority vote, DS-EM, and spectral/EM to your limits?
- In SL-UK, is the “average worker” capacity formalized as the capacity of the mixture channel? Please add intuition for why SL-CS leads to averaging entropies vs SL-UK leading to capacity of the averaged channel.
- For kIC, can you provide explicit expressions or approximations for the spammer error term and discuss monotonicity in k, and the regime where the bound is most informative?
- How would limited adaptivity (e.g., re-querying disputed items) affect the rate?
- Can you comment on extensions beyond binary labels, especially when M ≠ N?

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Principled, novel information-theoretic framing with clear, interpretable bounds.
- Highlights the value of skill estimation and structured queries (kIC).
- Potentially broad impact on the design and budgeting of crowdsourcing systems.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- No empirical validation; unclear tightness relative to practical algorithms.
- Some exposition space used for textbook background rather than proof insights.
- Idealizations (perfect skill knowledge in SL-CS; non-adaptivity) limit immediate applicability.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and technically solid paper that opens a useful direction by providing fundamental budget–fidelity limits for crowdsourcing. With added simulations showing how close common aggregation methods come to these limits and with streamlined exposition (more proof intuition, clearer cost normalization for kIC), this work would make a strong and impactful contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the main concerns.
