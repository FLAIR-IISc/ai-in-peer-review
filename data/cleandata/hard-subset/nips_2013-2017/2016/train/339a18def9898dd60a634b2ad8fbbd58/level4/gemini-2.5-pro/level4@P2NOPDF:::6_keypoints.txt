- Paper offers an information-theoretic analysis of the budget-fidelity tradeoff in crowdsourcing using rate-distortion and channel coding, with a technically sound derivation.  
- If this is the first application of this framework to crowdsourcing, it is a notable conceptual contribution.  
- Main concern: novelty and practical significance are unclear due to potential looseness of the derived bound.  
- Core issue: the analogy between crowdsourcing and channel coding may be invalid because channel coding permits flexible codebook design that crowdsourcing does not.  
- Key practical constraint: one cannot realistically assign the same query multiple times to the same worker without violating i.i.d. assumptions, unlike repeated independent transmissions in channels.  
- More generally, crowdsourcing “codebooks” are restricted by task design, worker availability, and the need for statistical independence, unlike unconstrained channel encoders.  
- These operational constraints may make the theoretical bound too loose to be a useful practical benchmark.  
- Recommendation: empirically compare the bound to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms to quantify the gap and assess tightness.  
- Minor: In Eq. (8) clarify that minimization is over all joint distributions P(C,C') satisfying E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor: Expand the proof of Theorem 4 to make the logical steps clearer.  
- Minor: Clarify the implication of ln(1−q) being negative on the relationship between ε_S and ε̂ in Theorem 4.  
- Minor: Justify or cite the appearance of H_M(E(ε)) in Eq. (17).
