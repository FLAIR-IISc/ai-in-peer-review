- Paper frames crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem, modeling data as a source and workers as parallel noisy channels.  
- Main contribution: fundamental lower bounds on query budget (queries per item) needed to reach a target fidelity (error probability).  
- Bounds derived for two scenarios: known worker skills (SL-CS) and unknown skills (SL-UK), with taskmaster unaware in both.  
- Worker reliability modeled using M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) for general errors and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) for mixed perfect/random workers.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) and provides a performance bound under the SHC model.  
- Distinct from prior work (e.g., Dawid-Skene) by focusing on fundamental limits rather than specific inference algorithms.  
- Strength — Originality: first rigorous use of information theory to analyze fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.  
- Strength — Significance: establishes theoretical benchmarks that can inform budgeting, pricing, and system design.  
- Strength — Clarity: well-written, clear analogy to communication systems that aids accessibility.  
- Weakness — Lack of experimental validation: no simulations or real-world experiments to compare practical algorithms to the theoretical bounds.  
- Weakness — Presentation/focus: background on coding theory is lengthy and could be condensed to include proof sketches for main theorems in the main text.  
- Weakness — Technical soundness caveat: proofs are in the appendix and were not verified line-by-line by the reviewer.  
- Suggested improvements: add simulations or real-data experiments (e.g., Mechanical Turk) and condense background to present main-theorem proof sketches.  
- Overall: novel and significant theoretical contribution that opens a new research direction, recommended for acceptance despite lacking experiments.  
- Acceptance arguments: originality, fundamental limits utility, theoretical and practical relevance, clear exposition.  
- Rejection arguments: purely theoretical without experimental context, background could be more concise to allow deeper technical exposition.
