- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication system to derive lower bounds on the budget (queries per item) vs fidelity (error) trade-off.

- Models workers with two channel types: M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) for general error, and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) for perfect workers vs random guessers.

- Derives minimum queries per item required to achieve a target error under two knowledge regimes: SL-UK (worker skills unknown) and SL-CS (skills known to crowdsourcer), with the taskmaster unaware of skills.

- Analyzes a practical query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), within the same framework.

- Emphasis is on establishing fundamental performance limits (benchmarks) rather than proposing new aggregation algorithms.

- Strength: highly original application of rate-distortion/information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing.

- Strength: provides significant theoretical benchmarks useful for quantifying gaps, setting budgets, and informing pricing for query types.

- Strength: technically well-defined model and clear distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS cases, yielding useful insights on value of skill estimation.

- Weakness: entirely theoretical with no experiments or simulations to validate how tight or loose the bounds are compared to practical algorithms (e.g., Majority Voting, Dawid–Skene).

- Suggestion: include empirical comparisons (synthetic or real data) to assess practical relevance of the bounds and room for algorithmic improvement.

- Weakness: organization could be improved—background on source/channel coding is overly long for the audience and proofs are deferred to the appendix.

- Suggestion: shorten background, add proof sketches for main theorems in the main text, and discuss impacts of idealized assumptions (i.i.d tasks, asymptotic regime, memoryless/independent workers, non-adaptive schemes) on real-world applicability.
