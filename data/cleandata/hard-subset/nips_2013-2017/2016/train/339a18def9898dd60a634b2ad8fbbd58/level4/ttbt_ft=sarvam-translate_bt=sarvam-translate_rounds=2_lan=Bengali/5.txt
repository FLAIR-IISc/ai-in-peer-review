This paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information theoretical framework, providing a lower bound on the number of queries required for each task to achieve a certain probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels. The Error and Spamer-Hammer Channel Model (SHC(q)) is used when a worker is a spamer with probability 1-q; the symmetric memory-sufficient channel (MSC) is considered to model this situation. The code is assumed to be non-adaptive. Two cases are considered for analysis: firstly, when crowdsourcing, after collecting results from workers, the skill level of each worker is known, and when he knows nothing about them. In both cases, the taskmaster, who distributes the work among the workers, is assumed to know nothing about the workers' skills, so each worker is given the same number of tasks. Finally, the minimum required rate – the number of queries for each task – is a necessary condition on k-ary incidence coding, where the worker is shown k items and asked whether they are of the same type or different types, where SHC(q) was provided for the worker's pool. A well-organized and well-written paper. There were no tests or simulations to show how far the schemes currently used in AMT are from the limit. I think section 2.4 can be shortened and section 3.1 is not needed or can be shortened. Instead, the proof sketches of the theorems are more important. It seems novel, there is no analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information theoretical point of view, as far as I know and the paper claims. It seems very useful where designers of such crowdsourcing projects can know the minimum required rate to complete a task with a certain probability of error, and it can help in determining the value of such projects. I have not read the appendix so I cannot claim anything about the proof.
