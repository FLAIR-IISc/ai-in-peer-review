- Establishes information-theoretic fundamental limits for crowdsourcing by modeling workers as noisy channels (MSC and SHC(q)) and analyzing minimum query rate for target error.  
- Derives distortion-rate functions for known-skill (SL-CS) and unknown-skill (SL-UK) scenarios.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where workers compare k items for cluster consistency and proves a lower bound on required rate under SHC(q).  
- Novelty: first NIPS paper applying rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing, shifting from algorithmic design to information-theoretic limits.  
- Practical impact: SL-UK/SL-CS framework clarifies how skill uncertainty constrains performance and informs pricing/design (e.g., π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁k₂).  
- kIC insight: query structure inherently enables error correction beyond aggregation-focused prior work.  
- Theoretical quality: rigorous development, precise channel modeling, and clear proofs (e.g., Theorem 2’s bound on R_min).  
- Clarity: well-structured paper, effective figures illustrating kIC, and consistent mathematical notation and role distinctions.  
- Major weakness: no empirical validation or simulations (e.g., AMT experiments or comparisons to majority voting/DS algorithm).  
- Novelty claim needs stronger justification against general rate-distortion/channel coding literature (e.g., Cover & Thomas).  
- Presentation issue: Section 3.1 is overly technical and could be condensed; Section 2.4 is redundant with Section 3.  
- Limited discussion on applicability of SL-UK bounds when worker skill distributions are estimated in practice (e.g., via EM).  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to add simulations, shorten background, and clarify the conditions under which bounds (Theorem 4 vs Theorem 2) apply.  
- Overall conclusion: significant theoretical contribution showing that crowdsourcing performance is fundamentally bounded by channel capacity under worker noise, meriting acceptance after minor fixes.
