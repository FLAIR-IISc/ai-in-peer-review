The paper presents an information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, deriving lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a specified probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. The authors consider symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker errors, and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to model scenarios where a worker responds randomly with probability 1-q. The analysis assumes non-adaptive coding schemes. The paper investigates two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer, who determines the task value based on worker responses, knows the skill level of all workers; and another where the crowdsourcer has no prior knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster, responsible for task distribution, is assumed to be unaware of individual worker skills, assigning each worker the same number of tasks. Furthermore, the paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum required rate – measured as the number of queries per task – for k-ary incidence coding, where workers are presented with k items and asked to determine if they belong to the same or different categories, within a worker pool modeled by the SHC(q). 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, it lacks experimental validation or simulations demonstrating how current, real-world schemes (e.g., those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk) compare to these theoretical limits. I suggest streamlining section 2.4 and either removing or significantly shortening section 3.1. Prioritizing proof sketches over detailed derivations would also improve the paper’s readability. 

The approach appears novel. To the best of my knowledge, and as the authors claim, this is the first analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective. This work has the potential to be highly useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, providing insight into the minimum query rate needed to achieve a desired error probability, and potentially informing pricing models. I have not reviewed the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the validity of the proofs presented there.
