This paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using the language and tools of information theory, establishing fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While I found the paper’s insights and overall model to be a very interesting and potentially useful approach to designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I didn’t fully grasp all the details (see comments below). I believe the high-level intuition and key takeaways from the results – for example, those discussed at the end of section 3 – could be highlighted more prominently. A concluding summary outlining practical implications would also be beneficial, even acknowledging space constraints. 

I appreciated the authors’ effort to make the paper accessible to readers less familiar with information theory, like myself; however, it remained challenging to follow in places. I recommend restating many of the results specifically in terms of the crowdsourcing application, rather than consistently using the terminology of channels, codes, and distortion. Furthermore, the related work section should mention the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This work frequently addresses varying worker abilities and, increasingly, explores combining these incentives with more advanced aggregation methods.

A few minor points: on line 84, “is reviewed in” should likely be “are reviewed in.” On line 106, the distinction between the “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” was unclear, potentially because “crowdsourcing” itself is a blend of “crowd” and “outsourcing,” suggesting a process that precedes direct worker involvement. On line 122, please insert a space between the two "A"s in "A A(X1,X2)". Lines 275-276 contained a phrase ("In case...") that was difficult to parse. Finally, I found Figure 2 uninformative; a more detailed caption could significantly improve its clarity.
