The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information theoretic framework, deriving lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a specified probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels. The authors consider symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to represent worker errors, and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to model workers who respond randomly with probability 1-q. The analysis assumes nonadaptive query strategies. Two scenarios are examined: one where the crowdsourcer, who aggregates worker responses to determine the task value, knows the skill level of each worker; and another where the crowdsourcer has no prior knowledge of worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster, responsible for task distribution, is assumed to be unaware of individual worker capabilities, assigning the same number of tasks to each worker. Finally, a necessary condition is provided on the minimum required rate – the number of queries per task – for k-ary incidence coding, specifically when using a worker pool modeled by SHC(q).

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, it lacks experimental evaluation or simulations to demonstrate how current crowdsourcing schemes, such as those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT), compare to these theoretical limits. I suggest shortening section 2.4, and either removing or significantly condensing section 3.1. Prioritizing proof sketches for the presented theorems would be more valuable. 

The work appears novel; to my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this is the first analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information-theoretic perspective. This approach is potentially very useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, providing insights into the minimum rate needed to achieve a desired error probability and aiding in project pricing. I have not reviewed the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the proofs contained therein.
