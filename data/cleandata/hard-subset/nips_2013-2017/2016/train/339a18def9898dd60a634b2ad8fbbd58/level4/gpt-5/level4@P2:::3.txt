Summary
This paper models labeling by a crowd as a communication problem and analyzes the fundamental limits of the budget–fidelity trade-off using information-theoretic tools. Items to be labeled are treated as a source; workers are modeled as noisy channels; and the overall crowdsourcing process is framed as a joint source–channel coding problem. The authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) needed to achieve a target error probability (distortion), under two scenarios: (i) workers’ skill levels are unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) skill levels are perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They show that knowledge of skill levels effectively increases capacity, leading to less redundancy required for the same fidelity.

The paper further analyzes a specific query design—k-ary incidence coding (kIC)—in which each query presents k items and asks the worker to group them by label. For a spammer–hammer worker model, the authors give a necessary condition on the rate for achieving a target error and derive a simple pricing rule-of-thumb comparing different k values. The work connects to established aggregation methods (e.g., majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM and its extensions, spectral/graph methods, convex approaches) by focusing on ultimate performance bounds rather than specific algorithms.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clear, principled framework for reasoning about crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem, yielding capacity-like and rate–distortion-like limits.
- Novelty: To my knowledge, the explicit budget–fidelity bounds under MSC and SHC worker models and the contrast between SL-UK and SL-CS are new and insightful.
- Practical insight: The kIC analysis highlights how constraining response options can create error-correction effects; the pricing heuristic relating cost across different k is appealing.
- Relevance: Provides a unifying lens that can inform the design of redundancy, worker assignment, and query formats across many labeling tasks.

Weaknesses
- Accessibility: Despite attempts to be self-contained, the reliance on information-theoretic notation (channels, capacities, distortions) makes the paper hard to follow for many NIPS readers. Results would be clearer if restated in crowdsourcing terms (e.g., “queries per item,” “error rate,” “worker reliability”).
- Assumptions: The analysis assumes iid items, symmetric errors (MSC), non-adaptive querying, and, in places, an oracle that perfectly knows worker types (SL-CS). These choices are reasonable for clean theory but limit immediate applicability.
- Scope of kIC results: The main bound for kIC is under SHC and binary labels, and uses an oracle decoder; discussion of how close practical decoders can get to the bound is limited.
- Empirical validation: There is no experimental evaluation or comparison with existing aggregation methods to illustrate the tightness of bounds or the benefits of kIC in practice.
- Presentation: The distinction between “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” is unclear; some sentences are hard to parse; Figure 2 needs a more informative caption and interpretation.

Quality
The technical claims appear sound at a high level, with proofs deferred to the appendices. The modeling choices are clearly stated. However, additional intuition, worked numerical examples, and a brief sketch of key proof ideas (especially for Theorems 2–4) would help readers assess correctness and implications.

Clarity
Organization is good, but readability suffers from heavy information-theoretic terminology. Restating main theorems with concrete interpretations (e.g., “to reach 5% error with workers of average error ε, you need at least R queries per item…”) and adding a concise concluding section with design takeaways would substantially improve clarity.

Originality
The information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing budget–fidelity limits and the kIC analysis are novel contributions relative to predominant probabilistic EM/spectral/graph-based inference literature.

Significance
The results offer useful guidance for setting redundancy levels, evaluating the value of estimating worker skills, and designing query formats. Their practical impact will grow with additional work bridging from lower bounds to implementable algorithms and adaptive strategies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Restate key results in application terms with small numerical examples; add a brief “Design Takeaways” section summarizing how to set R, when to invest in skill estimation, and when higher-k queries pay off.
- Clarify terminology and roles: define “taskmaster” vs “crowdsourcer” once and stick to them; or collapse to a single role if possible.
- Expand related work to include robust incentives/mechanism design for crowdsourcing (e.g., peer-prediction, effort elicitation, incentive-compatible quality control). This literature considers heterogeneous abilities and can complement your bounds.
- Discuss limitations: iid assumptions, MSC/SHC idealizations, non-adaptivity; outline how adaptive querying or difficulty-dependent noise might fit your framework.
- Improve Figure 2: add axis labels with units, describe curves, explain parameter settings, and annotate regions (“achievable,” “impossible”).
- Provide a short intuition for the difference between SL-UK and SL-CS bounds (average worker vs average of worker qualities), and for the error-correction effect in kIC.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel theoretical framework with clear relevance to NIPS.
- Insightful bounds that can guide future algorithm and system design.
- Bridges information theory and crowdsourcing, potentially catalyzing new research.

Arguments against acceptance
- Accessibility and presentation issues limit impact for the NIPS audience.
- Practical implications remain somewhat abstract without experiments or closer connection to implementable aggregation schemes.
- kIC results are restricted in scope (binary labels, oracle decoder).
