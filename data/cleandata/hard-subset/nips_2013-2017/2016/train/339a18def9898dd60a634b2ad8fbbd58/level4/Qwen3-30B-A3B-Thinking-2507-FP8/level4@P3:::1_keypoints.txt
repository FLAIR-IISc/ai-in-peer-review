- Paper proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as noisy channels (M-ary symmetric and spammer-hammer) to analyze budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion optimization.  
- Derives distortion–rate functions for unknown skill (SL-UK) and perfectly estimated skill (SL-CS) scenarios, linking performance to channel capacity.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a combinatorial query scheme that provides inherent error correction by querying subsets of items.  
- Proves lower bounds on kIC performance under the spammer-hammer worker model (Theorem 4).  
- Major strength: novel, rigorous establishment of fundamental performance limits for crowdsourcing, filling a gap left by prior algorithmic work.  
- Major strength: solid theoretical analysis and proofs applying joint source–channel coding and Shannon’s separation theorem, with nontrivial bounds (Theorems 2–3).  
- Strength: kIC analysis offers insightful connection to coding theory and shows how combinatorial query design reduces error.  
- Moderate clarity: core ideas are clear but notation inconsistencies (e.g., "R" used variably) and Section 2.3 could be more intuitive.  
- Weakness: practical trade-offs of kIC are underexplored—higher per-query cost vs. error reduction lacks empirical validation and detailed cost analysis.  
- Weakness: SL-CS assumes perfect skill estimation without analyzing degradation due to realistic estimation errors.  
- Weakness: kIC resembles prior "binary incidence coding" and relationship/generalization to existing work should be clarified.  
- Pros for acceptance: first to set fundamental budget–fidelity limits, rigorous bounds, principled kIC design, strong coding-theory connection.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited discussion of real-world applicability/costs, idealized SL-CS assumption, and notation issues reducing accessibility.  
- Recommendation: Accept (strongly recommended) with minor revisions to standardize notation, add kIC cost-trade discussion, and clarify SL-CS limitations.
