- Models crowdsourcing as a communication problem with workers as noisy channels and query/inference as joint source-channel coding.  
- Establishes fundamental information-theoretic limits on the budget (queries per item) vs. fidelity (error probability) trade-off.  
- Derives two key theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) characterizing minimum queries per item for unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a novel query scheme that asks workers to label k-item subsets simultaneously.  
- Proves theoretical bounds on kIC performance (Theorem 4) and shows improved performance with larger k and higher hammer probability q.  
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs in the appendices and well-justified theorems.  
- Information-theoretic approach is novel and complements prior algorithmic crowdsourcing work.  
- kIC is an interesting, concrete example exploiting error-correcting properties via query design.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful illustrative figures for kIC.  
- Weak connection to recent NIPS crowdsourcing literature; needs more explicit comparison to works like Zhang et al. 2014, Whitehill et al. 2009, Karger et al. 2013.  
- Main limitations: assumes independent channels and no feedback, which may be unrealistic; limits adaptive or correlated-skill scenarios.  
- Limited numerical results and comparisons with existing algorithms; practical implementation details of kIC are under-discussed.  
- Original contribution: first fundamental limits for crowdsourcing and a novel query design; distinct from prior aggregation-focused studies.  
- Overall assessment: significant theoretical contribution with clear implications for system design; recommendation to accept.
