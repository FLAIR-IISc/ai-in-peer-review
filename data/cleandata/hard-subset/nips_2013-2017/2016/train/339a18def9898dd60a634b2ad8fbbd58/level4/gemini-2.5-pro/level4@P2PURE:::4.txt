This paper models a crowdsourcing system where a taskmaster decomposes a large inference problem into smaller queries for a group of workers. A crowdsourcer then aggregates the workers' responses to solve the original problem. The model accounts for workers having varying skill levels and being potentially unreliable. The central goal is to achieve a desired level of fidelity in the final inference, despite noisy or incorrect responses. This is accomplished by carefully designing the queries assigned by the taskmaster. The paper defines the total number of queries as the system's "budget" and investigates the fundamental trade-off between this budget and the resulting fidelity.

To analyze this trade-off, the paper draws an analogy to the joint source-channel coding problem from information theory. In this framework, the taskmaster acts as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable workers collectively form a communication channel. This mapping allows the authors to leverage standard information-theoretic results to derive a lower bound on the budget required to achieve a specific fidelity. Notably, this bound is universal, holding independently of the specific query design and aggregation algorithms used. The paper then proposes a concrete query design scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," analyzes its performance, and compares it against the information-theoretic lower bound for a particular channel model.

The core contribution of this paper—framing the crowdsourcing problem within the rate-distortion framework—is novel and interesting. This information-theoretic perspective has the potential to inspire further research in this area.

However, the reviewer has concerns about the realism of the proposed model. The formulation appears tailored to map directly onto a classic point-to-point communication problem, which may allow for the straightforward application of existing information theory results but at the cost of modeling fidelity. The authors should address the following concerns regarding the model's assumptions:

1.  The model conflates worker reliability and skill level into a single, uniform channel characteristic. Furthermore, it assumes the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their individual skill. This contradicts many real-world systems where workers have reputation scores or ratings that could be used for more efficient, non-uniform task allocation.
2.  The realism of the channel models used to represent worker unreliability is questionable. The paper does not seem to account for more complex scenarios, such as strategic or adversarial workers who might intentionally provide incorrect information to sabotage the outcome.

Additionally, the paper has several presentation issues that should be corrected:

*   **Numerical Results:** The explanation of the numerical results is unclear. In Figure 2, the labeling of the curves could be improved for better readability. The physical meaning of the k=1 case is also ambiguous: if queries consist of pairs of items, how can a worker be asked if they share the same label when k=1 (i.e., only a single item is presented)?
*   **Assumption Placement:** The assumption that P(B(X)) is known, mentioned on page 6, line 246, is a critical piece of information. This should be stated much earlier in the paper, likely in the problem formulation section.
*   **Typos:** There appear to be incorrect figure references on page 4, line 135: "Figure 1.a" should likely be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".
*   **Formatting:** The terms "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" are capitalized throughout the text, even when not referring to a specific numbered statement. Standard practice is to use lowercase ("theorem," "lemma," "corollary") in these cases.
