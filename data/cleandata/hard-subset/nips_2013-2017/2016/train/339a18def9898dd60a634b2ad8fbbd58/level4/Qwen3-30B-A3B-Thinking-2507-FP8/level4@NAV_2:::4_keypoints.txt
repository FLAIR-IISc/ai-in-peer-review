- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem and studies the budget–fidelity trade-off using information theory.  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries needed for a target fidelity in two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
- Uses rate-distortion theory to obtain the bounds and analyzes the k-ary incidence coding (kIC) query scheme against these bounds.  
- Compares kIC performance under a spammer-hammer channel (SHC) model and contrasts with theoretical limits.  
- Novel contribution: introducing an information-theoretic perspective to establish fundamental limits for crowdsourcing, distinct from aggregation-algorithm work.  
- Theorems 2 and 3 are highlighted as significant results that set limits any scheme must satisfy.  
- kIC scheme is well-motivated and provides actionable insights on how query design affects efficiency.  
- Strength: strong connection to prior work (e.g., Dawid-Skene) and clear theoretical framing.  
- Weakness: model collapses worker reliability and skill into a single channel, limiting realism and excluding worker-specific query allocation.  
- Weakness: channel models (MSC and SHC) are oversimplified and do not capture adversarial or complex worker behaviors.  
- Weakness: numerical results (Figure 2) are poorly labeled and k=1 case (direct labeling) is not properly explained.  
- Weakness: key modeling assumption about knowledge of P(B(X)) is introduced too late and should be stated earlier.  
- Minor issues: incorrect figure references and inconsistent use/numbering of Theorem/Lemma/Corollary terminology.  
- Pro-acceptance: sound theoretical framework, novel perspective, and useful kIC analysis.  
- Con-rejection: unrealistic modeling assumptions, inadequate worker behavior modeling, unclear numerical presentation, and several technical inconsistencies.
