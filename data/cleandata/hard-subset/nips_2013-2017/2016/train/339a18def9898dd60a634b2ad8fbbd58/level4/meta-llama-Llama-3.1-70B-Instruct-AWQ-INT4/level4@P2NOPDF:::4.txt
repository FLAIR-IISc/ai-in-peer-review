The paper explores a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster breaks down a large task, such as an inference problem, into smaller tasks that are then assigned to a crowd of workers. The workers complete their tasks and provide information to the crowdsourcer, who processes this information to draw conclusions about the original task. This setup accommodates workers with varying skill levels and allows for unreliable reporting. The primary objective is to achieve a certain level of fidelity or 'goodness' in the final outcome, despite potential inaccuracies in the information provided by workers. To achieve this, the taskmaster must carefully design the smaller tasks, or queries, to be allocated to workers. The paper defines the total number of queries presented to workers as the budget of the crowdsourcing system and investigates the fundamental trade-off between this budget and the fidelity of the final outcome.

This trade-off is established by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source channel coding in information theory literature. The taskmaster is treated as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the unreliable links from workers to the crowdsourcer as a communication channel. Using this mapping, a lower bound on the budget for a desired fidelity level is derived from standard results in information theory literature. This bound is independent of the schemes used by the taskmaster to design queries and the algorithm used by the crowdsourcer to process worker information. The authors then analyze the performance of a specific scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and compare its performance to the obtained information theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model.

The parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers is quite interesting. This work may inspire other researchers to study crowdsourcing frameworks using information theoretic tools. However, it is unclear whether the results presented in this paper accurately model real-world crowdsourcing systems. One possible reason for this is the desire to map the crowdsourcing problem to a point-to-point communication problem, allowing for the direct application of existing information theory results with minimal effort.

Several issues require further clarification from the authors:

1. The reliability and skill level of workers appear to be merged and modeled as a single channel. The paper assumes that the transmitter allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their skill level. However, in real systems, workers may have ratings that reflect their skill level, which is not accounted for in this model.

2. The realism of the channel models in capturing worker unreliability is questionable. The paper does not consider workers who may have knowledge of the task and intentionally attempt to degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner.

The explanation of numerical results is unclear, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 can be improved. Specifically, it is unclear what the $k=1$ case corresponds to, as it implies that workers directly provide the correct label for a presented query, which is not possible when presenting pairs of queries.

Additionally, several minor issues were noted:

- The assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ is not stated until page 6, line 246, and should be mentioned earlier in the paper.

- The figure references in page 4, line 135, are incorrect and should be swapped.

- The authors repeatedly use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' without accompanying theorem or lemma numbers, which can be confusing. Instead, 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' would be more appropriate in these cases.
