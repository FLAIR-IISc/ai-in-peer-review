This work establishes an information-theoretic bound for the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing, leveraging rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of this bound appears sound. If this represents the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, it could be a valuable contribution. However, the novelty and practical significance remain questionable, as the derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound risks being overly loose. A key concern is the limited design freedom for the input sequence *u<sup>n</sup>* compared to traditional channel coding. While channel coding allows extensive codebook design options (repetition codes, Hamming codes, LDPC codes, etc.), it is unclear how to effectively design *u<sup>n</sup>* to enhance error correction in the crowdsourcing context. Specifically, equation (3) implicitly assumes a wide range of mappings from B(X)<sup>n</sup> to U<sup>(∑mi)</sup> are valid and implementable; otherwise, the resulting channel capacity may be a poor approximation. Repetition coding – and thus majority voting – is achievable in crowdsourcing, but extending beyond this is challenging. 

A fundamental difference arises from the inability to repeatedly query the same worker with the same task, unlike channel coding where i.i.d. assumptions permit repeated transmissions. This constraint violates the i.i.d. assumption underlying equations (1) and (2), as repeated queries would likely elicit identical responses from a single worker. This is just one illustration; in general, crowdsourcing likely faces significant constraints not present in standard channel coding scenarios, where encoding mappings from {0,1}<sup>nR</sup> to {0,1}<sup>n</sup> have minimal restrictions. Consequently, the set of feasible encoding schemes in equation (3) is likely limited, increasing the risk that the proposed information-theoretic bound is loose. A comparison of the derived bound to the performance of existing state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods would strengthen the paper.
