- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, treating true labels as source, queries as encoder, worker responses as noisy parallel channels, and worker skills as channel states.  
- Derives asymptotic lower bounds on required query budget vs fidelity for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) settings.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a spammer-hammer worker model, providing a lower bound on its performance.  
- Strength: novel, elegant information-theoretic formulation framing budget–fidelity as a rate-distortion problem.  
- Strength: significant—establishes fundamental benchmarks that could guide and inspire new query/aggregation designs.  
- Strength: clear and well-organized exposition mapping crowdsourcing components to communication-system elements.  
- Weakness: main theorems rely on source–channel separation in a nonstandard setting (random channel state unknown at encoder) without sufficient proof or citation.  
- Weakness: model omits item content (side information) available to encoder/decoder, which can fundamentally alter rate-distortion limits and harms realism.  
- Weakness: asymptotic results (n → ∞) limit practical relevance; lacks finite-blocklength analysis (e.g., Kostina–Verdú style) for real datasets.  
- Weakness: minor clarity issue—ambiguous phrasing in Section 4.2 about how k and q affect kIC performance.  
- Overall: promising and original but preliminary; needs stronger theoretical justification and model refinements to be a complete, impactful contribution.
