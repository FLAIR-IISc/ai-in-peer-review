

The authors present an information-theoretic framework for the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item. Specifically, they model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, where the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may or may not be known at the decoder. The item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as the encoder, crowd workers as multiple channel uses, worker skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder. Leveraging existing JSCC coding rate results, they derive fundamental limits for the asymptotic query rate. Additionally, they provide a lower bound on the error probability for "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowdsourcing model. Overall, this is a well-written paper that constitutes a promising initial step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing. Key technical comments:  
1. The authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem (Theorems 2 and 3) without establishing its validity under random channel states with encoder-side ignorance. They should prove or cite a proof for this scenario.  
2. The model assumes the taskmaster and crowdsourcer only observe item labels, ignoring potential side information from the original items. This side information could be beneficial (especially if available at both encoder/decoder or decoder alone), and its omission limits model realism.  
3. In Section 4.2, the claim that "lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow" is misleading; the lower bound *decreases* (indicating potentially better code performance), but this does not constitute an improvement of the bound itself without an upper bound for comparison.  
Edited following authors' response: The JSCC approach is interesting, but as another reviewer noted, a nonasymptotic characterization of crowdsourcing limits would be more valuable. The authors should incorporate recent nonasymptotic JSCC converse results, such as those by Kostina and Verdu (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013).
