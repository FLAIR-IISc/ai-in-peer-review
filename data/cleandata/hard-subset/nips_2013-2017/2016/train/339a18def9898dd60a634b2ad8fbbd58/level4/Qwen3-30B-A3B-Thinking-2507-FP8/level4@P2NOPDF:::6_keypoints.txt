- Derivation is mathematically sound but novelty and practical significance are doubtful; the bound seems straightforward and likely loose.  
- Channel coding assumptions (freedom to design codebooks and reuse transmissions) do not hold in crowdsourcing due to query-assignment constraints.  
- The independence (i.i.d.) assumption is violated if identical queries are assigned to the same worker, preventing repetition or standard error-correcting schemes.  
- The set of valid encoding mappings is severely restricted compared to channel coding, so the proposed bound likely overestimates achievable performance.  
- The paper does not rigorously analyze how crowdsourcing-specific constraints limit error-correcting capabilities or achievable capacity.  
- Authors should explicitly quantify the impact of query–worker assignment rules on achievable channel capacity.  
- The bound should be compared against state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, Bayesian approaches) to demonstrate practical relevance.  
- Minor: In Equation (8), clarify that the minimization is over codes C, C' satisfying E[d(Z, \hat{Z})] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor: Expand the proof of Theorem 4 and justify the step where the bound is tightened.  
- Minor: In Theorem 4, note ln(1−q) < 0 and clarify whether ε_S > \hat{ε} and why that inequality matters.  
- Minor: In Equation (17), explain the origin of H_M(E(ε)) or cite prior work establishing this entropy term.  
- Overall: Without rigorous analysis of structural constraints and validation against existing methods, the work risks being perceived as a mechanical application of established information theory rather than a meaningful crowdsourcing advance.
