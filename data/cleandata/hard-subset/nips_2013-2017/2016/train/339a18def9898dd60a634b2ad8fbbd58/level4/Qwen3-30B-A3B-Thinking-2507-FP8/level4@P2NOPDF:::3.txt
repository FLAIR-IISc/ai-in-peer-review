

The paper addresses the fundamental problem of aggregating worker outputs in crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, establishing the budget-fidelity tradeoff limits. The core approach and insights are compelling and represent a novel, potentially impactful perspective for designing aggregation mechanisms. However, the exposition significantly impedes accessibility, particularly for readers unfamiliar with information theory, despite the authors' efforts to clarify concepts.  

The most significant concern is the over-reliance on channel coding terminology (e.g., "distortion," "codes," "channels") rather than framing results directly in crowdsourcing terms. For instance, the key result in Section 3 (e.g., the characterization of optimal fidelity for a given budget) should be restated using crowd-sourcing-specific language (e.g., "worker reliability," "task complexity," "cost per task"). This would make the contributions immediately tangible and actionable for the target audience. The high-level intuition behind these results—which could guide practical design decisions—should be highlighted earlier and more prominently, perhaps in a dedicated subsection before the technical development. A concise conclusion summarizing "what practitioners should take away" (e.g., "For budget-constrained tasks, achieving fidelity above X requires at least Y workers with reliability Z") would greatly strengthen the paper, even within space constraints.  

The distinction between "crowdsourcer" (the entity managing the task) and "taskmaster" (the entity assigning tasks) is confusingly introduced (Section 3, line 106). Given that "crowdsourcing" derives from "crowd" + "outsourcing," this terminology risks misalignment with standard usage. Clarifying the roles or adopting a more intuitive framework (e.g., "platform" vs. "task owner") is essential. Similarly, the phrase "A A(X1,X2)" on line 122 appears to contain a typo and needs correction. The text in lines 275–276 ("In case...") is particularly difficult to parse; restructuring for clarity would be beneficial. Figure 2 lacks sufficient explanation in its caption—readers cannot discern the key insights without extensive reading of the main text. A detailed caption specifying the axes, key trends, and why the result matters would significantly improve its utility.  

The related work section should explicitly reference the growing line of research on *robust economic incentives* for workers (often termed "crowdsourcing mechanism design" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"). This work frequently addresses worker heterogeneity and integrates incentives with aggregation (e.g., [cite recent papers]). Neglecting this connection overlooks a major complementary approach to the problem, weakening the paper’s positioning.  

Minor grammatical points require attention:  
- Line 84: "is reviewed" → "are reviewed" (since "papers" is plural).  
- The phrase "I was quite confused" (line 106) should be rephrased objectively as "The distinction between crowdsourcer and taskmaster is unclear..."  

While the technical contributions are theoretically sound and valuable, the current presentation risks limiting their impact. The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous foundation, but its practical relevance hinges on clearer translation into crowdsourcing design principles. The authors should prioritize rephrasing results in application-centric terms, as this is the single most effective way to bridge the gap between theory and practice.  

In summary, this work offers a fresh theoretical framework for crowdsourcing aggregation, but its accessibility and applicability are hampered by excessive reliance on information-theoretic abstraction. With a clearer focus on crowdsourcing-specific implications, a stronger conclusion, and minor revisions to terminology and exposition, the paper would make a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance pending major revisions addressing these points.
