This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for the crowdsourcing problem, deriving fundamental limits on the number of queries required per item in an asymptotic regime. The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state. In this elegant formulation, the item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, the crowd workers as parallel uses of a channel, their varying skill levels as the random channel state, and the final aggregator as the decoder. Using this model, the paper leverages existing results on JSCC coding rates to establish the fundamental limits on the query complexity of crowdsourcing. Furthermore, the authors analyze a specific scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," providing a lower bound on its error probability within their proposed framework.

The primary contribution of this work is its novel and principled formulation of the crowdsourcing problem using the lens of information theory. This perspective is insightful and provides a formal basis for understanding the inherent trade-offs in collecting and aggregating noisy labels from multiple workers. The paper is well-written, and the analogy between crowdsourcing components and the JSCC setup is clear and compelling. This approach represents a promising direction for theoretically grounding the analysis of crowdsourcing systems.

Despite the novelty of the approach, there are several key areas where the paper could be strengthened, both in its theoretical justification and its modeling assumptions.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Justification for the Source-Channel Separation Theorem:** The main results in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on the application of the source-channel separation theorem. However, the model considers a channel with a random state that is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. This is a non-standard setting, and the validity of the separation principle is not immediately obvious. The authors should provide a formal proof that the theorem holds under these specific conditions or, alternatively, cite existing literature that rigorously establishes this result. Without this justification, the theoretical foundation of the paper's central claims is incomplete.

2.  **Lack of a Non-Asymptotic Analysis:** While the asymptotic analysis provides valuable theoretical insights, its practical utility for real-world crowdsourcing applications is limited. Crowdsourcing tasks typically involve a finite number of items and a finite budget for queries. Therefore, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits, which captures the trade-offs for a finite "blocklength" (i.e., number of items), would be significantly more impactful. The authors are strongly encouraged to explore recent advances in finite-blocklength information theory to extend their analysis. For instance, the work of Kostina and Verdu ("Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Trans. on IT, 2013) provides relevant tools for analyzing JSCC problems in the non-asymptotic regime and could serve as an excellent foundation for future work.

3.  **Restrictive Model Ignoring Side Information:** The proposed model assumes that the taskmaster (encoder) and crowdsourcer (decoder) only have access to the item labels. This overlooks a crucial aspect of most crowdsourcing scenarios: both parties typically have access to the items themselves (e.g., the images, text, or audio being labeled). This item data can serve as powerful side information. While side information available only at the encoder might not alter the channel capacity, side information available at the decoder (or at both encoder and decoder) can dramatically improve performance, as seen in classic problems like source coding with side information (Wyner-Ziv). Incorporating item features as side information would make the model more realistic and could lead to fundamentally different and tighter performance bounds.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  **Clarity on Figure 2:** In Section 4.2, the authors state that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding "improve as k and q grow." This phrasing is ambiguous. It seems the intended meaning is that the lower bound on the error probability *decreases*, which suggests that the code's performance may improve. However, without a corresponding upper bound, it is difficult to say whether the bound itself is improving (i.e., becoming tighter). A more precise description would enhance clarity.

In summary, this paper introduces a creative and promising information-theoretic model for crowdsourcing. The JSCC formulation is a notable contribution. However, the work in its current form is limited by a lack of justification for a key theoretical assumption, the restrictive nature of the model which omits side information, and the focus on an asymptotic regime that may not be relevant to many practical applications. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and its potential impact.
