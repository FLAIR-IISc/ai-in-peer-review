

This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of digital crowdsourcing, establishing lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per task required to achieve a specified error probability. The authors model workers as parallel independent channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) for skilled workers and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) for spammers (with probability 1−q of being a spammer). The analysis assumes nonadaptive coding and considers two scenarios: (1) the taskmaster (who assigns tasks) knows all worker skill levels, and (2) the taskmaster has no prior knowledge of worker skills (and thus assigns equal tasks to all). Notably, in both cases, the crowdsourcer (who aggregates results) is assumed to have no worker skill information. The paper derives a necessary condition for the minimum query rate under SHC(q) using k-ary incidence coding (where workers compare k items for similarity).

The work is highly novel: to the best of my knowledge, this represents the first information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing, offering a foundational framework for understanding query efficiency. The theoretical results are valuable for practitioners, enabling them to determine the minimal query rate for a given error tolerance—a key consideration for pricing and designing crowdsourcing systems. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to results.

However, the paper has two significant limitations. First, it lacks empirical validation. The authors provide no simulations or experiments (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) to demonstrate how current practical schemes compare to the derived theoretical bounds. Such evidence would strengthen the practical relevance of the results and show whether the bounds are tight or conservative. Second, Sections 2.4 and 3.1 are unnecessarily lengthy. Section 2.4 (on channel capacity) could be condensed, and Section 3.1 (on the nonadaptive case) appears redundant given the paper’s focus on nonadaptive coding. Instead, the authors should prioritize including proof sketches for the main theorems (e.g., Theorems 1–3), which would aid reader comprehension and highlight the core technical contributions without adding bulk.

The paper’s core contribution—establishing information-theoretic lower bounds for crowdsourcing—merits attention. The use of SHC(q) to model spammers is particularly insightful, as it captures a realistic failure mode in crowdsourcing. The distinction between the taskmaster’s and crowdsourcer’s knowledge is also well-motivated and clarifies the problem’s complexity. That said, the absence of experimental results is a notable gap. While theoretical bounds are essential, their utility is amplified when contextualized against real-world implementations. Without this, the reader cannot assess whether the derived rates are achievable in practice or require impractical overhead.

Regarding organization, the paper’s clarity is a strength, but the suggested cuts to Sections 2.4 and 3.1 would improve focus. The proof sketches recommendation is critical: theorems like the lower bound for SHC(q) rely on non-trivial channel coding arguments, and a brief sketch (e.g., "The proof adapts the sphere-packing bound to the SHC(q) channel structure...") would make the results more accessible. The appendix appears to contain full proofs, but omitting them from the main text without a summary leaves readers without a high-level intuition.

In summary, this is a theoretically sound and novel contribution with clear practical implications. The information-theoretic perspective fills a significant gap in crowdsourcing literature. To elevate the work further, the authors should: (1) add simulations benchmarking current schemes against the bounds, and (2) streamline Sections 2.4 and 3.1 while adding proof sketches. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for publication. As it stands, it is a promising theoretical advance but lacks the empirical grounding needed to fully convince practitioners of its applicability. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing these points.
