

This work establishes an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of the bound is technically sound. If this represents the first application of rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem to crowdsourcing, it could constitute a meaningful contribution. However, the novelty and practical significance remain questionable, as the derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound risks being overly loose. The primary issue lies in the lack of equivalent design freedom for the channel input sequence $u^n$ compared to channel coding. In channel coding, extensive flexibility exists in codebook design (e.g., repetition, Hamming, convolutional, LDPC codes), but in crowdsourcing, it is unclear whether such freedom applies to the mapping from $B(X)^n$ to $U^{\sum m_i}$ in Equation (3). While degenerate mappings are excluded, most mappings should be valid or implementable; otherwise, the channel capacity may yield a loose bound. For instance, repetition coding (with majority voting) is feasible, but it is unclear how to design $u^n$ to enhance error correction beyond this. Crucially, a fundamental distinction exists: in crowdsourcing, reassigning the same query to the same worker violates the iid assumption (as identical answers would likely occur), whereas channel coding assumes an iid channel allowing repeated transmissions. This constraint, and others, likely impose significant restrictions on feasible encoding schemes in Equation (3—unlike channel coding, where $\{0,1\}^{nR} \to \{0,1\}^n$ mappings face minimal constraints. Consequently, the proposed bound is likely loose due to unaccounted-for constraints. Comparing the bound with state-of-the-art methods' performance would strengthen the paper. Minor comments:
