The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. Their approach models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. Specifically, items and their labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as channel uses representing varying skill levels (random channel states), and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. Leveraging existing JSCC results, they establish fundamental limits on the asymptotic query complexity. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding within this framework. Overall, this is a well-written paper representing a promising initial step in applying JSCC to model crowdsourcing. 

Several technical points warrant consideration:

1. The modeling of crowdsourcing as JSCC with random channel state relies on the source-channel separation theorem. The authors apply this theorem directly (in Theorems 2 and 3) but should justify its continued validity when the channel state is random and unknown to the encoder. While likely true, a proof or citation of an existing proof would strengthen the argument.

2. The current model restricts the taskmaster to only observing item labels and the crowdsourcer to only observing worker responses. This may be an oversimplification, as both parties often have access to the original data (items themselves). Incorporating this potential side information into the JSCC framework – particularly if available at both the encoder and decoder, or even at the decoder alone – could lead to a more realistic and compelling model.

3. In Section 4.2, the authors’ interpretation of the behavior of the lower bounds in Fig. 2 requires clarification. While they state the lower bounds improve with increasing *k* and *q*, it’s more precise to say the lower bound *value* decreases, potentially indicating better code performance, but without an upper bound, the term “improve” is ambiguous.

Following review of the authors’ response, I note that while the JSCC modeling is interesting, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practically valuable. The authors should investigate recent work by Kostina and Verdu on nonasymptotic converses for JSCC (V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime", IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013).
