- Paper studies large-scale inference via crowdsourcing where tasks are split into micro-tasks, workers are unreliable, and a crowdsourcer aggregates responses under a fixed query budget.
- Main contribution: an information-theoretic mapping of crowdsourcing to joint source-channel coding that yields a scheme-independent lower bound on budget vs. fidelity.
- Authors propose and analyze a concrete query design ("k-ary incidence coding") and compare its performance to the derived information-theoretic bound for a specific channel model.
- Strength: novel and insightful connection to the rate-distortion framework, offering a principled way to analyze fundamental limits and inspiring further information-theoretic work in crowdsourcing.
- Concern: the model conflates worker skill and reliability into a single uniform channel and assumes equal query allocation, which may not reflect practical heterogeneous or reputation-based task assignment.
- Question: how can the framework be extended to handle heterogeneous workers and adaptive task allocation based on reputation or skill?
- Concern: the channel models for worker unreliability need stronger justification; adversarial or strategic workers (intentional errors) are not addressed.
- Question: how would adversarial or strategically malicious workers affect the framework and the derived budget-vs-fidelity bounds?
- Presentation issue: numerical results and Figure 2 lack clear explanation and descriptive curve labels, making performance comparison hard to interpret.
- Clarification requested: practical interpretation of the k=1 incidence coding scheme—does it correspond to asking a worker directly for an item's label?
- Minor issue: the critical assumption that the prior P(B(X)) is known is stated late (page 6) and should appear earlier in the main problem formulation.
- Minor issue: probable typos in figure references on page 4 (e.g., "Figure 1.a" / "Figure 1.b" likely misreferenced).
- Minor issue: inconsistent capitalization of generic terms "theorem," "lemma," and "corollary"—lowercase should be used when not referring to numbered statements.
