This work establishes an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing, leveraging rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of this bound is mathematically sound. Should this represent the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, it could constitute a noteworthy contribution to the field. However, the practical significance and novelty remain uncertain. While the derivation is correct, it is relatively direct, and the resulting bound may be overly conservative. 

A key concern is the applicability of standard channel coding concepts to this problem. In traditional channel coding, there is considerable design freedom in constructing codebooks (e.g., repetition, Hamming, LDPC codes). However, it is not immediately obvious if similar flexibility exists in designing the input sequence *u<sup>n</sup>* in equation (3). For the bound to be tight, a wide range of mappings from B(X)<sup>n</sup> to U<sup>(∑mi)</sup> must correspond to viable encoding schemes; otherwise, the calculated capacity could be an underestimation. While techniques like repetition coding – and majority voting decoding – are feasible in crowdsourcing, extending beyond this to improve error correction is unclear. 

A crucial distinction between channel coding and crowdsourcing lies in the independence assumption. Channel coding allows for repeated transmission of the same message to leverage i.i.d. channel properties. In contrast, the i.i.d. assumption in equations (1) or (2) is violated if the same query is presented to the same worker multiple times, as their responses are likely correlated. This is just one example illustrating that crowdsourcing likely faces far more significant constraints on encoding schemes (as represented in equation (3)) than standard channel coding, where mappings from {0,1}<sup>nR</sup> to {0,1}<sup>n</sup> have minimal restrictions. Consequently, the proposed bound may be loose due to these inherent constraints on possible encoding schemes.

To strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial to compare the derived information-theoretic bound with the performance of existing state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods.
