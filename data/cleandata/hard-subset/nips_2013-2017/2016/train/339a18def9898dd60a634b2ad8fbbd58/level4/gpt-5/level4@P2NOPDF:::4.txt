This paper studies a crowdsourcing framework in which a taskmaster decomposes a large inference problem into small queries assigned to workers with heterogeneous skills and potentially unreliable reporting. The crowdsourcer aggregates the workers’ responses to solve the original task. The goal is to guarantee a target fidelity (quality) of the final inference despite erroneous answers, while minimizing the total number of queries (the budget). The authors cast this setup as a joint source–channel coding problem: the taskmaster is the transmitter, the crowdsourcer is the receiver/decoder, and the worker responses form a noisy channel. Using this mapping, they derive an information-theoretic lower bound on the required budget to achieve a desired fidelity, independent of the specific query design at the taskmaster or the decoding algorithm at the crowdsourcer. They then analyze a concrete query design—k-ary incidence coding—and compare its performance to the bound under a specific channel model.

Strengths
- The connection between crowdsourcing with unreliable workers and the rate–distortion/joint source–channel coding framework is elegant and insightful. It yields clean, scheme-agnostic lower bounds and may stimulate further use of information-theoretic tools in this area.
- The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete instantiation, allowing for empirical comparison to the theoretical limits.

Concerns and questions
1) Worker heterogeneity and allocation policy. The model appears to conflate worker reliability and skill into a single channel and assumes the taskmaster issues the same number of queries to all workers regardless of their ability. In many real systems, workers have ratings or histories that guide adaptive allocation (more informative queries to high-skill workers, redundancy for low-skill workers, etc.). How would the theory and bounds change if the transmitter had partial side information about worker skill, or could adaptively allocate budget across workers?

2) Channel realism and adversaries. The channel models used to capture unreliability seem limited to random errors. In practice, some workers may be strategic or adversarial, intentionally aiming to degrade fidelity, possibly with partial knowledge of the task. Can the framework incorporate adversarial/noisy-or mixtures, worker-dependent confusion matrices, or worst-case guarantees (e.g., bounds that hold under a fraction of Byzantine workers)? How sensitive are the results to these modeling choices?

Clarity of numerical results
- The explanation of the experiments is hard to follow, and the labeling in Fig. 2 should be improved. Please clarify what each curve represents, the exact channel parameters used, and how they map to the theoretical quantities.
- The case k=1 is unclear. With k=1, incidence queries that compare pairs are not possible. Does k=1 reduce to direct labeling of single items? If so, what is the precise query/answer format and error model?

Assumptions and placement
- On page 6, line 246, you assume knowledge of P(B(X)). This is a strong modeling assumption that should be stated earlier, alongside the problem setup and notations. Please discuss how P(B(X)) would be estimated or learned in practice, and how misspecification affects the bounds and algorithms.

Editorial and presentation issues
- Page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a → Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.b → Figure 1.c,” and “Figure 1.b → Figure 1.c” should be “Figure 1.a → Figure 1.b” (please verify and correct the cross-references).
- Throughout the paper, “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” are used without accompanying numbers. When not tied to specific numbered statements, please use lowercase (“theorem,” “lemma,” “corollary”) or, preferably, ensure every such reference points to a numbered result.

Overall assessment
The paper’s core idea—framing crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding to obtain budget–fidelity trade-offs—is compelling and of potential broad interest. The scheme-agnostic lower bounds are valuable, and the analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides a useful benchmark. However, the current modeling choices seem far from many real crowdsourcing deployments, especially regarding worker heterogeneity, adaptive allocation, and adversarial behavior. Strengthening the discussion and, where possible, extending the theory to cover partial worker side information, richer channel models, and robustness to adversaries would significantly increase the practical impact. Improving the clarity of the numerical section, explicitly defining the k=1 setting, and surfacing key assumptions earlier will also enhance readability and reproducibility.
