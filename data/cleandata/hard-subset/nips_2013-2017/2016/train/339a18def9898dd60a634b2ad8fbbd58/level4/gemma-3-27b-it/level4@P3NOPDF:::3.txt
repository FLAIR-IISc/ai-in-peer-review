## Review of NIPS 2016 Paper: [Paper Title - Please fill in]

This paper tackles the challenging problem of optimally aggregating responses from workers in a crowdsourcing setting, leveraging the powerful tools of information theory to establish fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. While I found the core ideas and proposed model to be genuinely interesting and potentially valuable for designing more effective crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I also found the paper difficult to fully grasp due to its heavy reliance on information-theoretic terminology.

The paper’s strength lies in its formalization of the crowdsourcing problem within an information-theoretic framework. Deriving fundamental limits on performance provides a valuable theoretical foundation for understanding the inherent constraints in crowdsourcing systems. The insights presented, particularly those discussed at the end of Section 3 regarding the relationship between budget, worker accuracy, and overall system fidelity, are promising. However, these key takeaways are currently somewhat buried within the technical details and would benefit from more prominent articulation. A concluding section explicitly outlining the practical implications of these results – what a practitioner *should do* differently based on these findings – would greatly enhance the paper’s impact.

My primary concern is accessibility. Despite the authors’ commendable efforts to bridge the gap for readers less familiar with information theory, the constant translation between the crowdsourcing application and the language of channels, codes, and distortion made the paper challenging to follow. I strongly recommend restating the core results and theorems in terms of the crowdsourcing problem itself. This would significantly improve clarity and allow a broader audience to appreciate the contributions. For instance, expressing the budget-fidelity tradeoff directly in terms of worker costs and achievable accuracy, rather than through the lens of rate-distortion theory, would be more intuitive.

The related work section is thorough but could benefit from acknowledging the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers in crowdsourcing, often framed as “crowdsourcing mechanism design.” This area frequently addresses varying worker abilities and, increasingly, investigates the synergy between incentive structures and sophisticated aggregation methods. Integrating this perspective would provide a more complete picture of the current research landscape.

Specific comments:

* **Line 84:** “is reviewed in” should likely be “are reviewed in.”
* **Line 106 & earlier:** The distinction between “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” was unclear. The term “crowdsourcing” itself, being a combination of “crowd” and “outsourcing,” might lead readers to assume the taskmaster’s role precedes worker involvement. Clarifying this relationship early on would be helpful.
* **Line 122:** The expression “A A(X1,X2)” appears to be a typographical error.
* **Lines 275-276:** The phrase “In case…” was difficult to parse and could be reworded for greater clarity.
* **Figure 2:** This figure did not provide significant insight. A more detailed caption explaining the axes and the information conveyed would greatly improve its usefulness. Perhaps visualizing the budget-fidelity tradeoff directly, even with simplified data, would be more impactful.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and potentially important application of information theory to crowdsourcing. The theoretical results are valuable and the identified tradeoffs are likely fundamental. However, the paper's accessibility is currently limited by its technical language and lack of clear articulation of practical implications. Addressing these issues through a more application-focused presentation and a dedicated concluding section would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its appeal. I believe with revisions, this work could make a substantial contribution to the field.
