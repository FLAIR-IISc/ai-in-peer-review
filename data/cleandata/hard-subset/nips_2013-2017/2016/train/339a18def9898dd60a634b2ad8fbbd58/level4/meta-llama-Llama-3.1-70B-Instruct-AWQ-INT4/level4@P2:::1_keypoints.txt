- Novel information-theoretic modeling of crowdsourcing by treating workers as noisy channels (symmetric memoryless and spammer/hammer).
- Reduces labeling from crowdsourcing to a coding problem, enabling analysis of fundamental limits.
- Studies the budget–fidelity trade-off by mapping budget to code rate and deriving information-theoretic bounds.
- Provides bounds for both unknown worker skill (SL-UK) and known skill (SL-CS) scenarios as ultimate performance limits.
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where workers label subsets of k items simultaneously.
- Analyzes kIC under the spammer-hammer worker pool and derives a lower bound on its performance.
- Numerical results show kIC performance improves with larger k and higher worker reliability q.
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and accessible to readers with basic information-theory background.
- Thorough related-work review and clear explanation of context and significance.
- Analysis is technically sound and results are presented clearly.
- Assumes a simplified worker behavior model that may not reflect real-world complexity.
- Analysis is limited to two channel models, raising questions about generalizability to other models/scenarios.
- Lacks discussion on practical implications and guidance for real-world crowdsourcing system design.
- Original contributions: novel information-theoretic framing and the introduction/analysis of k-ary incidence coding.
- Significance: advances fundamental understanding of crowdsourcing limits and informs query-scheme design.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty of approach, theoretical bounds, and kIC contribution with thorough analysis.
- Arguments for rejection: oversimplified worker models, limited model scope, and insufficient practical applicability discussion.
