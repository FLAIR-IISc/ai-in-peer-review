The authors provide a novel information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, deriving fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic sense. By modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, the authors leverage existing results on coding rates to establish these limits. In this model, item labels are represented as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, worker skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder. The authors also provide a lower bound on the error probability of the "k-ary incidence coding" scheme under a specific crowdsourcing model.

This well-written paper may be a first step towards JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing. However, several technical aspects warrant further consideration. Firstly, the authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem to obtain their results (Theorem 2 and 3). Nevertheless, they should explicitly argue that this theorem remains valid when the channel has a random state unknown at the encoder but potentially known at the receiver. Although the separation theorem likely holds in such situations, the authors should either provide a proof or cite an existing one.

Secondly, the current model may be overly restrictive, as it assumes the taskmaster (encoder) only sees item labels, and the crowdsourcer (decoder) only sees worker-produced labels. In reality, both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer may have access to the original data (items), which could serve as side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem. Incorporating side information, particularly when present at both the encoder and decoder or at the decoder alone, could enhance the model's realism and convincingness.

Thirdly, in Section 4.2, the authors comment on the curves in Figure 2, stating that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) improve as k and q grow. However, this statement seems to imply that the lower bound becomes smaller, potentially indicating better code performance. Nevertheless, without an upper bound for comparison, it is more accurate to say that the lower bound decreases rather than improves.

In response to the authors' feedback, it is worth noting that while the application of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is intriguing, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practical. The authors may find it beneficial to explore recent results by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, as presented in their 2013 IEEE Transactions on Information Theory paper, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime."
