- Paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system and studies budget–fidelity trade-offs via an information-theoretic rate–distortion framework.  
- Derives minimum query rates (budgets) to achieve target error under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker-skill scenarios.  
- Provides theorems establishing limits for M-ary symmetric channels and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as an error-correcting query scheme.  
- Claims to extend prior aggregation-focused crowdsourcing work by offering fundamental performance bounds instead of heuristic schemes.  
- Strength: original application of rate–distortion theory to crowdsourcing, filling a gap in theoretical CS foundations.  
- Strength: kIC is a concrete scheme with provable advantages over standard approaches.  
- Strength: Theorems are technically sound with rigorous proofs and supported by numerical results (Fig. 2).  
- Weakness: Heavy use of channel-coding jargon (“encoder,” “decoder,” “distortion”) obscures practical crowdsourcing interpretation.  
- Weakness: Confusing introduction and inconsistent use of roles (“taskmaster” vs. “crowdsourcer”) needing clear definitions.  
- Weakness: Key results (e.g., Theorem 4) are stated in coding-theoretic terms rather than actionable CS metrics (queries per item, error probability).  
- Weakness: Practical takeaways are buried; the paper lacks a concise summary of implications for real CS system design.  
- Weakness: Figure 2 lacks clear axis labels, context, and an explanatory caption comparing kIC to theoretical bounds.  
- Weakness: Related work omits mechanism-design literature on worker incentives/skill estimation (e.g., Abraham et al. 2013, Zhang et al. 2015).  
- Minor issues: several grammatical/typo errors noted (p.84, p.122, p.275–276).  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong, original contribution; clarity issues are fixable with minor revisions.  
- Revision suggestions: replace coding terms with CS equivalents, add a Practical Implications paragraph, clarify roles, cite mechanism-design work, and improve Figure 2 caption.  
- Pro/Con summary: Pros — first to set fundamental CS limits, kIC practical, rigorous theorems; Cons — jargon obscures practice, missing real-world guidance, omitted related work, minor clarity/figure issues.
