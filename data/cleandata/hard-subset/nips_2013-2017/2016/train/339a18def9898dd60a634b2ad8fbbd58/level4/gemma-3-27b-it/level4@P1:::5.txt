The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information theoretic framework to establish fundamental limits on the number of queries required per task to achieve a specified error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent channels, utilizing both symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to represent worker errors and a spammer-hammer channel model (SHC(q)) to account for malicious or consistently accurate contributors. The analysis assumes non-adaptive coding schemes and considers two scenarios: one where the crowdsourcer knows each worker’s skill level, and another where this information is unavailable. In both scenarios, the taskmaster is assumed to be unaware of individual worker skills, assigning the same number of tasks to each. The paper concludes by providing a necessary condition for the minimum query rate required for k-ary incidence coding, a scheme where workers are presented with *k* items and asked to identify those belonging to the same category, again under the SHC(q) model. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the lack of empirical validation or simulations demonstrating the gap between current crowdsourcing practices (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) and these theoretical limits is a notable omission. Section 2.4 could be streamlined for conciseness, and Section 3.1, while providing context, feels somewhat lengthy or could be abbreviated. Prioritizing proof sketches within the main body of the paper, rather than relegating them to the appendix, would likely enhance readability and impact for a broader audience. 

This work appears novel; to the best of my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, there hasn’t been a prior analysis of crowdsourcing through an information theoretic lens. The results are potentially very valuable for designers of crowdsourcing systems, providing insights into the minimum query rates needed to achieve desired error probabilities and aiding in project pricing. While I have not meticulously reviewed the appendices, the presented arguments appear logically sound.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The framework appears mathematically rigorous and the derivations seem well-motivated.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results provide fundamental limits that can guide the design and optimization of crowdsourcing systems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experiments or simulations limits the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   **Conciseness:** Some sections (2.4 and 3.1) could be more concise.
*   **Appendix Dependency:** Important details are relegated to the appendices, potentially hindering comprehension.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. While the lack of empirical validation is a weakness, the novelty, soundness, and potential impact of the work are substantial. I believe the paper is suitable for acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the empirical validation point in future work.
