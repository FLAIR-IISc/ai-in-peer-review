

This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as joint source-channel coding (JSCC) with a random channel state (representing worker skill levels). The authors establish asymptotic fundamental limits on the number of queries per item, leveraging established JSCC results. The modeling is clear: items' labels are an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster acts as an encoder observing only labels, crowd workers constitute channel uses with skill-dependent states, and the crowdsourcer functions as the decoder. The work is well-structured and represents a promising first step toward applying JSCC to crowdsourcing. However, several technical concerns require clarification and strengthening to enhance the paper’s contribution and rigor.

First, the authors invoke the source-channel separation theorem (Theorems 2 and 3) without justification for the specific scenario where the channel state is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the decoder. While the separation theorem likely holds in this setting, the absence of a proof or reference to an existing proof weakens the theoretical foundation. The authors should explicitly address whether separation is valid here, citing relevant literature (e.g., works on channel state information in JSCC) or providing a concise proof. Without this, the asymptotic limits derived remain contingent on an unverified assumption.

Second, the model restricts the taskmaster (encoder) and crowdsourcer (decoder) to observing only item labels, ignoring potential access to the original item data. This omission is significant, as real-world taskmasters and crowdsourcers typically have knowledge of the input data (e.g., images, text), which constitutes valuable side information. Crucially, side information at the decoder (or both encoder and decoder) can substantially reduce the required query complexity in JSCC. The authors should discuss how incorporating such side information—particularly at the decoder—would refine their model and improve its practical relevance. A model that neglects this aspect may overestimate the query burden, limiting the applicability of their results.

Third, Section 4.2 states that "the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) improve as k and q grow." This is misleading: lower bounds on error probability *decrease* (become tighter) as k and q increase, indicating better achievable performance, not an improvement in the bound itself. The authors should clarify this terminology to avoid confusion. A lower bound decreasing with larger k/q suggests the coding scheme’s performance approaches the theoretical limit, but the bound’s *value* is not "improving" in an absolute sense. Precision here is essential for readers to interpret the results correctly.

Finally, while the asymptotic analysis is theoretically interesting, the paper would benefit from a nonasymptotic perspective. As noted by another reviewer, practical crowdsourcing systems operate with finite data, where asymptotic guarantees are less directly applicable. The authors should consider recent nonasymptotic results for JSCC, such as Kostina and Verdu’s finite-blocklength converse (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, 2013), to characterize the trade-offs between query complexity, error probability, and dataset size. This would significantly strengthen the paper’s relevance to real-world applications and align it with current trends in information theory for finite-data problems.

In summary, the paper offers a compelling conceptual bridge between information theory and crowdsourcing, with a clear and well-executed asymptotic analysis. However, the technical contributions would be substantially enhanced by: (1) justifying the use of source-channel separation for the given channel model; (2) incorporating side information from original item data to improve model realism; (3) clarifying the interpretation of lower bounds; and (4) addressing nonasymptotic limits via recent advances. Addressing these points would elevate the work from a promising theoretical exploration to a more robust and impactful foundation for future crowdsourcing research. The writing is generally clear, but these revisions are necessary for the paper to fully realize its potential.
