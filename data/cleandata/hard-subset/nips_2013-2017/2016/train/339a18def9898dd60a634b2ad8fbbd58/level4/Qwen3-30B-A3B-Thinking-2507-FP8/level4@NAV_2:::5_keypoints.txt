- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as parallel noisy channels (MSC and SHC) to derive lower bounds on query rates for target error probabilities.  
- Establishes two scenarios: skill levels unknown to crowdsourcer (SL-UK) achieving average worker capacity, and skill levels known (SL-CS) leveraging average pool skill.  
- Proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme and provides theoretical performance bounds under the SHC model.  
- Extends prior NIPS crowdsourcing work by focusing on fundamental performance limits rather than algorithmic inference.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with well-justified theorems (2–4) and clear distortion-rate derivations.  
- Framework unifies source/channel coding principles for crowdsourcing and the SHC worker model is well-motivated.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-organized; kIC description and figures enhance readability.  
- The information-theoretic angle is a clear and novel contribution relative to prior inference-focused work.  
- Derived bounds have practical significance, offering guidance on minimum query rates and pricing for platforms like AMT.  
- Lacks empirical validation against real-world crowdsourcing systems or comparison with existing inference methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene).  
- Section 3.1 is overly long; a concise reference to the source-channel separation theorem would suffice.  
- kIC analysis should be more tightly integrated with the main bounds (Theorems 2–3) to clarify how it approaches the limits.  
- Overall verdict: strong theoretical contribution, well-suited for NIPS, with minor revisions suggested to enhance impact.
