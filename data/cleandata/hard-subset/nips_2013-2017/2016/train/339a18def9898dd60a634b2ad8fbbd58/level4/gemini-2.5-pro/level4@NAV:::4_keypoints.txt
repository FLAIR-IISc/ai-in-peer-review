- Studies a crowdsourcing framework that decomposes a large inference task into micro-tasks, distributes them to heterogeneous unreliable workers, and aggregates responses to guarantee a target fidelity under a query budget.  
- Maps the crowdsourcing problem to joint source-channel coding (taskmaster = transmitter, crowdsourcer = receiver) to analyze budget–fidelity trade-offs and derives an information-theoretic lower bound independent of specific query/aggregation schemes.  
- Proposes and analyzes a concrete query design called k-ary incidence coding (KIC) and compares its performance to the derived lower bound under a specified channel model.  
- The rate-distortion analogy is novel and compelling, with potential to spur further research at the intersection of information theory and crowdsourcing.  
- Concern that the model is tailored to classic point-to-point communication, which may reduce realism and applicability to practical crowdsourcing settings.  
- Worker skill and reliability are conflated into a single channel model, and the framework assumes uniform query allocation rather than exploiting worker ratings or past performance.  
- Question about realism of the channel models: they do not appear to account for adversarial workers who intentionally submit malicious responses.  
- Numerical-results presentation needs clarification: curve labeling in Figure 2 is hard to interpret and should be improved.  
- Clarify physical interpretation of the k=1 case (does it mean directly asking a worker for a single item’s label?).  
- The assumption that the prior P(B(X)) is known is critical and should be stated much earlier in the manuscript.  
- Fix incorrect figure references on page 4 (likely Figure 1.a → 1.b and Figure 1.b → 1.c).  
- Stylistic fix: use lowercase 'theorem', 'lemma', 'corollary' when not referencing specific numbered results.
