Summary and contributions
The paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity trade-off for label crowdsourcing by leveraging rate–distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivations appear technically sound, and, if this is the first systematic application of these tools to crowdsourcing, the conceptual framing could be of moderate significance.

Strengths
- Clear connection drawn between desired labeling accuracy (distortion) and required annotation budget via information-theoretic quantities.
- The converse-style reasoning is well motivated and, at a high level, aligns with established principles in coding and compression.
- The paper could help unify disparate results on redundancy, error rates, and worker noise within a single information-theoretic lens.

Main concerns
1) Gap between channel coding freedom and crowdsourcing constraints. In coding, one has substantial freedom to design codebooks (repetition, Hamming, convolutional, RS, LDPC, etc.), enabling tight capacity-based conclusions. In crowdsourcing, the “encoder” is the task allocation and query design. It is not clear that the mapping in Eq. (3) from B(X)^n to U^{∑ m_i} admits the same breadth as channel encoders. Many mappings that are admissible in coding are not implementable in crowdsourcing due to:
- Per-worker constraints: assigning the same query multiple times to the same worker breaks the i.i.d. response assumption (Eqs. (1)–(2)) and induces dependence across “channel uses.”
- Unknown and heterogeneous worker reliabilities: the “channel law” may be only partially known or must be learned, which alters achievability.
- Allocation and interaction constraints: limited queries per worker, platform rules, costs that vary across workers/tasks, and ethical/practical constraints on repeated or adversarial probing.
- Dependence via shared latent item difficulty: even if worker responses are i.i.d. conditional on the true label, item difficulty or context can induce correlations not captured by a memoryless channel.

Because of these constraints, the capacity-style converse may substantially overestimate what is achievable with realistic encoders, risking a very loose bound.

2) Limited design degrees of freedom beyond repetition. Repetition across different workers with majority voting is a natural analogue of a simple code. Beyond this, it is unclear how to construct structured redundancy or “codewords” that improve error-correction in a principled, implementable way. Without demonstrating feasible encoder constructions that approach the bound (even in stylized models), the practical value of the bound is uncertain.

3) Novelty relative to prior art. The contribution is more compelling if this is indeed the first application of rate–distortion and channel coding to crowdsourcing. Otherwise, the derivations—while correct—feel relatively straightforward extensions of standard converses, and the paper’s novelty would hinge on tightness, scope, or new achievability insights. This is currently underdeveloped.

Questions and requests for clarification
- What exact class of encoders is allowed in Eq. (3)? Is the mapping arbitrary, or is it constrained to crowd-implementable protocols (e.g., no repeating the same item to the same worker; limited per-worker budget; no adaptation across items for the same worker)? Please formalize these constraints and discuss their effect on the bound.
- Are assignments adaptive (based on interim responses), and are worker reliabilities known, partially known, or learned online? How do these choices alter the capacity analogy and the stated converse?
- Do you assume conditional independence of responses given the true label and worker parameters? Are item difficulty or context effects modeled?
- Is there any achievability result (even a simple one) that demonstrates a nontrivial gap to the converse under realistic constraints?

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Tightness and empirical validation: Compare the derived bound to empirical budget–error curves obtained by state-of-the-art algorithms (e.g., Dawid–Skene EM, GLAD, KOS, spectral methods, Bayesian methods, IWMV) on synthetic data under canonical worker models (symmetric noise, spammer–hammer, skill distributions) and on real datasets. Quantify gaps and identify regimes where the bound is informative versus vacuous.
- Constrained converse: Explicitly encode practical constraints (e.g., assignment matrix constraints, no intra-worker repetition, bounded per-worker load, unknown reliabilities) and derive a constrained capacity/converse. Even partial results or bounds for special cases would improve credibility and relevance.
- Achievability baselines: Provide simple, implementable schemes (e.g., stratified majority voting, skill-aware allocation, adaptive querying) with performance guarantees to bracket the converse and illuminate tightness.
- Interpretability: Offer concrete numeric examples translating the bound into budgets for target error rates under common worker models, to illustrate planning value.

Minor comments and nits
- Eq. (8): Please specify that the minimum is over C, C′ subject to E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.
- Theorem 4: The proof would benefit from a more detailed derivation, including explicit handling of terms where ln(1 − q) < 0. As written, it is unclear whether ε_S is larger than ẑε; please clarify the direction of the inequality and any conditions needed for monotonicity.
- Eq. (17): Explain why H_M(E(ε)) appears, or cite a prior result establishing this step. If this comes from a known extremal property (e.g., maximum entropy at fixed error), please reference it.
- Notation: Clearly define B(X), U, u^n, and ∑ m_i when first introduced; ensure domains/codomains in Eq. (3) are correct and consistent with later use.
- Assumptions: Explicitly state any independence or stationarity assumptions and when they are violated by realistic protocols (e.g., re-querying the same worker on the same item).

Overall assessment
The paper offers a clean and potentially useful information-theoretic perspective on crowdsourced labeling. However, without a careful treatment of crowdsourcing-specific constraints and evidence of tightness or near-tight achievability, the current bound risks being overly loose and of limited practical utility. Addressing the implementability of encoders, clarifying assumptions, and benchmarking against strong baselines would significantly strengthen the contribution.
