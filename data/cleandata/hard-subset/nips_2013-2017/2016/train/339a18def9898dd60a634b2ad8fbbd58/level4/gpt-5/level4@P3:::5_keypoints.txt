- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem, deriving budget–fidelity tradeoffs.  
- Views dataset labels as a discrete source and worker responses as outputs of parallel noisy channels.  
- Considers two worker models: M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel SHC(q).  
- Assumes non-adaptive querying (no feedback) and analyzes two cases: SL-UK (unknown skills) and SL-CS (skills known).  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries per item (rate) for target Hamming distortion.  
- Studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and gives necessary rate conditions under SHC(q).  
- Discusses implications for query pricing.  
- Contrasts with prior aggregation/inference work by taking a rate–distortion perspective to establish fundamental limits independent of specific algorithms.  
- Novel or rare contribution in framing crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel problem in this community.  
- Strength: clear, principled formulation separating source complexity, channel noise, and budget using information-theoretic tools.  
- Strength: general bounds that clarify the value of estimating worker skills (average capacity vs average of capacities).  
- Strength: kIC analysis gives intuition on structured queries, error detection, and rate scaling with k and q.  
- Strength: potential practical guidance for budget planning and pricing independent of decoding algorithm.  
- Weakness: no empirical or simulation evidence comparing common schemes (majority vote, DS-EM, spectral/EM) to the derived limits.  
- Weakness: Section 3.1 overrecaps information theory; space should be used for proof sketches of main theorems.  
- Weakness: cost model and rate normalization for kIC need concise main-text mapping of queries per item across k values.  
- Weakness: strong modeling assumptions (worker/task independence, stationarity, non-adaptivity) warrant discussion of how relaxations affect bounds.  
- Weakness: notation unclear regarding roles of N and M near main theorems.  
- Weakness: SL-CS (perfect skill knowledge) is idealized; robustness to imperfect skill estimates should be discussed.  
- Quality: technically sound use of rate–distortion and separation; SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction is insightful.  
- Quality: completeness limited by lack of empirical evaluation and limited discussion of bound tightness.  
- Evaluation: absent — adding simulations would strengthen the paper.  
- Clarity: generally well written; recommend shortening background (Section 3.1) and expanding intuitive proof sketches and examples.  
- Originality: framing in rate–distortion terms and kIC analysis under SHC(q) is novel and informative.  
- Significance: provides principled baselines for designers and can inform task design and pricing if complemented by simulations.  
- Questions for authors: empirical tightness of bounds and comparisons to practical algorithms?  
- Questions for authors: formalization/intuition of SL-UK as capacity of the mixture channel vs SL-CS averaging entropies?  
- Questions for authors: explicit expressions/monotonicity for the kIC spammer error term and informative regimes in k?  
- Questions for authors: impact of limited adaptivity (e.g., re-querying) on the rate?  
- Questions for authors: extensions to non-binary labels when M ≠ N?  
- Pros (for acceptance): principled novel framing, highlights value of skill estimation and structured queries, potentially broad impact.  
- Cons (against acceptance): no empirical validation, exposition could focus more on proofs, and idealized assumptions limit applicability.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and promising; would benefit from simulations, clearer proofs, and cost normalization—leaning toward acceptance if major concerns are addressed.
