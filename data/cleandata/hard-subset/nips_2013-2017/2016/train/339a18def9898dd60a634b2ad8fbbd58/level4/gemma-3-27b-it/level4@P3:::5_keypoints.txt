- Models digital crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation and analyzes fundamental query limits via information theory.  
- Represents workers as parallel noisy channels (symmetric discrete memoryless channels and spammer-hammer channels).  
- Considers scenarios where worker skill is known vs. unknown to the crowdsourcer.  
- Focuses on non-adaptive coding schemes and derives lower bounds on required queries for target fidelity.  
- Provides specific bounds for a k-ary incidence coding scheme (workers identify same-class items among k items).  
- Novel contribution: reframes crowdsourcing as a communication problem using source and channel coding concepts.  
- Theoretical rigor: establishes well-defined information-theoretic limits and budget–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Modeling is intuitive and flexible, covering multiple worker behaviors (MSC and SHC).  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and makes complex concepts accessible.  
- Potential impact: offers guidance on minimum resources and pricing strategies for crowdsourcing system designers.  
- Major weakness: lacks empirical validation or simulations demonstrating tightness and real-world relevance.  
- Some sections (notably 2.4 and 3.1) are overly long and could be streamlined; move full proofs to the appendix.  
- Limited discussion of practical issues such as query-type costs and worker incentives.  
- The non-adaptive coding assumption may be unrealistic; adaptive coding merits exploration.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper with potential impact; recommended as Weak Accept, contingent on encouraging empirical validation.
