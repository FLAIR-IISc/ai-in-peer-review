This paper studies a crowdsourcing setting in which a taskmaster decomposes a large inference problem into small tasks (queries) for a pool of workers. Workers have heterogeneous skills and may report unreliably. The crowdsourcer aggregates their responses to solve the original inference problem.

The goal is to guarantee a target fidelity (quality) at the crowdsourcer despite some incorrect worker responses, while minimizing the total number of queries (the budget). The paper frames the budget–fidelity trade-off by mapping the system to a joint source–channel coding problem: the taskmaster is the transmitter/encoder, the workers’ response process forms a noisy channel, and the crowdsourcer is the receiver/decoder. Under this mapping, a lower bound on the required budget for any desired fidelity follows from standard information-theoretic results; this bound is independent of the specific query design at the taskmaster and the decoding algorithm at the crowdsourcer. The authors then analyze a particular query design, k-ary incidence coding, and compare its performance to the information-theoretic lower bound under a specific channel model.

I find the connection between the rate–distortion framework and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers interesting and potentially influential; it may encourage further use of information-theoretic tools for crowdsourcing. However, I am not convinced that the model and results capture real systems with sufficient realism. In particular, the paper appears to prioritize an exact mapping to point-to-point communication so that existing theory can be applied with minimal adaptation.

Major questions and concerns:
1) Worker heterogeneity: Reliability and skill seem to be conflated and modeled as a single channel. The transmitter (taskmaster) is assumed to allocate the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their skill level. In many real platforms, workers carry ratings that reflect skill, and tasks are allocated non-uniformly. Please discuss how the framework could incorporate worker-specific channels or adaptive allocation.
2) Channel realism and adversaries: How realistic are the assumed channel models for worker unreliability? What about workers who possess task knowledge and deliberately degrade fidelity in an adversarial manner? Please clarify whether the framework can handle adversarial or strategic behavior, and under what assumptions.
3) Numerical results: The explanation is not very clear, and the labeling of the curves in Fig. 2 can be improved. What does the k=1 case correspond to? If k=1, a worker simply provides the label of a single item; one cannot present a pair and ask whether they share the same label. Please clarify the interpretation and how it is instantiated in the experiments.

Presentation and clarity:
- Page 6, line 246: You assume knowledge of P(B(X)). This assumption should be stated much earlier, as it is central to the analysis.
- Page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b.”
- Page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c.”
- The manuscript repeatedly uses “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without accompanying numbers. When not referring to a specific numbered result, please use lowercase “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary.”
- Throughout, “queries” and “budget” are defined informally at first; consider defining them precisely at the outset to avoid ambiguity.

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a neat theoretical lens and a clean lower bound that is agnostic to specific schemes, along with an analysis of k-ary incidence coding compared to the bound under a particular channel model. To strengthen the paper and its relevance to practice, I encourage the authors to (i) better separate worker skill and reliability and allow non-uniform or adaptive query allocation, (ii) justify and/or broaden the channel models to include adversarial behavior, and (iii) clarify the numerical section and figure labeling, including the meaning of k=1 and the early statement of assumptions such as knowledge of P(B(X)).
