- Novel rate-distortion/information-theoretic framing mapping crowdsourcing to a communication system (source, noisy channels, encoder, decoder).  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on budget required for a target fidelity in two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
- Uses a symmetric memoryless channel worker model for theoretical analysis.  
- Proposes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a practical query scheme and analyzes its performance under a spammer-hammer model.  
- Significant conceptual contribution: establishes fundamental performance benchmarks for all crowdsourcing strategies.  
- Theoretical approach is sound—application of joint source-channel coding yields meaningful insights about skill knowledge.  
- Paper is well written, clearly organized, and effectively illustrates the communication analogy.  
- Bridges theory and practice by providing a concrete example (kIC) that connects coding concepts to query design.  
- Relies on idealized assumptions (worker models, asymptotic regime); needs discussion of applicability to finite, realistic settings.  
- Does not fully account for cognitive cost differences of queries (k>2 increases worker load), which affects budget comparisons.  
- Lacks explicit empirical comparison to standard algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene/EM) to contextualize how close practical methods are to the bounds.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and inspiring contribution suitable for NIPS, with practical caveats to address.
