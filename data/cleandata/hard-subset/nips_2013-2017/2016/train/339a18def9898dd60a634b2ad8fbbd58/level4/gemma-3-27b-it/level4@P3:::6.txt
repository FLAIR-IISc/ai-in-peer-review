This work investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity tradeoffs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem within an information-theoretic framework using rate-distortion theory and channel coding principles. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on achievable performance, a potentially valuable contribution if it represents the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing. However, the novelty and practical implications of the work remain questionable, as the derivation of the bound appears relatively straightforward and risks being loose due to inherent differences between classical channel coding and the crowdsourcing setting.

The paper begins by framing crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and establishes a communication system model. It then leverages joint source-channel coding theory to identify fundamental limits on fidelity achievable with a given budget (number of queries). Specific query schemes, such as k-ary incidence coding, are explored, and an associated query pricing strategy is briefly discussed. The presentation of the information-theoretic background is concise and relevant.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** Applying information-theoretic tools – specifically rate-distortion theory and channel coding – to crowdsourcing is a potentially novel approach. While the tools themselves are well-established, their application to this domain offers a fresh perspective.
*   **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The problem is clearly formulated, and the derivation of the information-theoretic bounds appears logically sound, building on established results in coding theory. Theorems 2 and 3 provide explicit bounds under different scenarios (unknown vs. known worker skill levels).
*   **Exploration of a Specific Coding Scheme:** The analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) provides a concrete example and illustrates how the theoretical framework can be applied to a specific query design.
*   **Consideration of Worker Skill Levels:** The paper appropriately differentiates between scenarios where worker skill levels are known or unknown, reflecting a realistic aspect of crowdsourcing.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Looseness of the Bound:** The primary concern is the potential looseness of the derived bound. Unlike traditional channel coding, the constraints on designing valid encoding schemes (equation 3) in crowdsourcing are significant. The authors rightly point out the difficulty in replicating the freedom available in designing codebooks for conventional communication channels. The assumption of i.i.d. responses becomes problematic when the same worker receives similar queries. This substantially restricts the achievable rates compared to the theoretical bound.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** While the theoretical bounds are interesting, the paper lacks a clear connection to practical crowdsourcing systems. It doesn't provide much guidance on how to translate these bounds into concrete design choices for real-world applications.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** Crucially, the paper does *not* compare the derived information-theoretic bound to the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting, more recent probabilistic models). Such a comparison is essential to assess the tightness of the bound and demonstrate its practical relevance. The authors should include simulations or experiments to evaluate the performance gap.
*   **Query Pricing Discussion is Brief:** The discussion of query pricing based on the kIC scheme is underdeveloped and feels tacked on.
*   **Minor Technical Points:**
    *   In equation (8), the minimization is over both C and C' such that E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D<sup>t</sup>.
    *   The proof for Theorem 4 could benefit from further elaboration.
    *   In Theorem 4, since ln(1-q) < 0, the relationship between ϵ<sub>S</sub> and  ̂ needs clarification.
    *   Equation (17) and the justification for including H<sub>M</sub>(E(ϵ)) require further explanation or citation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Provides a clear mathematical framework for analyzing budget-fidelity tradeoffs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The derived bound may be too loose to be practically useful due to the inherent constraints of crowdsourcing.
*   Lacks empirical validation and comparison to existing crowdsourcing algorithms.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications and guidance for system design.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical investigation, but falls short of making a compelling contribution to the field. The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a worthwhile exploration, but the paper doesn't adequately address the practical challenges and constraints that limit the applicability of the derived bound. The lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns regarding the tightness of the bound and inclusion of empirical validation. The authors should focus on bridging the gap between the theoretical results and practical crowdsourcing systems.  Specifically, including a comparison to existing algorithms would strengthen the paper considerably.
