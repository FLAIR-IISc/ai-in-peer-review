This paper proposes a novel information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing. The authors model digital crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a given probability of error. The framework considers two scenarios: when the crowdsourcer knows the skill level of all workers and when it does not.

The paper is well-organized and well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning. The use of information-theoretic tools to analyze crowdsourcing is innovative and provides a fresh perspective on the problem. The authors' approach is rigorous, and they provide a thorough analysis of the problem, including the derivation of lower bounds on the number of queries per task.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a fundamental limit on the performance of crowdsourcing systems. This limit can be used as a benchmark to evaluate the performance of existing crowdsourcing algorithms and to design new algorithms that achieve this limit. The paper also discusses the implications of the results on pricing crowdsourcing projects, which is a crucial aspect of the problem.

However, there are some areas that could be improved. Firstly, the paper lacks experimental or simulation results to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework. It would be useful to include some results to show how far current crowdsourcing schemes are from the limits derived in the paper. Secondly, some sections, such as Section 2.4 and Section 3.1, could be shortened or removed as they do not add significant value to the paper. Instead, the authors could provide proof sketches for the theorems, which would be more useful for readers.

Overall, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and provides a new perspective on the problem. The results are novel and have significant implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems. With some minor revisions, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel information-theoretic framework to analyze crowdsourcing.
* The framework provides a fundamental limit on the performance of crowdsourcing systems.
* The results have significant implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems and pricing crowdsourcing projects.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks experimental or simulation results to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
* Some sections could be shortened or removed to improve the paper's clarity and focus.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
