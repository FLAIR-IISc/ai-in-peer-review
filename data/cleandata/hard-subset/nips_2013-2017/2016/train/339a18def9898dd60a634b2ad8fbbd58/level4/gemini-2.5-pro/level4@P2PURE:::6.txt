This paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing by applying principles from rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of the bound itself appears to be sound. If this is the first work to establish such a connection, it could represent a reasonably important contribution to the field.

However, the paper's novelty and practical value are questionable. The derivation of the information-theoretic bound is relatively straightforward, and more importantly, the bound is at risk of being too loose to be meaningful in practice.

The central issue lies in the direct analogy drawn with channel coding. The tightness of a channel capacity bound relies on the freedom to design a wide variety of powerful codebooks (e.g., repetition, Hamming, LDPC codes). It is not clear that a similar degree of freedom exists when designing the sequence of worker queries, u^n, in the crowdsourcing setting. For the bound to be tight, almost all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(∑ mi) in equation (3) would need to correspond to valid, implementable "codebooks." While simple schemes like repetition codes (with majority voting as the decoder) are possible, it is unclear how more complex error-correcting schemes could be implemented.

A striking difference highlights this limitation: in crowdsourcing, one generally cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times, as this would violate the i.i.d. assumption in equation (1) or (2); a worker is likely to provide the same answer if asked repeatedly. In contrast, a communication channel is typically assumed to be i.i.d., allowing for the repeated transmission of the same symbol to leverage channel properties. This is a key example of the fundamental constraints on the set of possible encoding schemes in equation (3) that do not exist in classical channel coding. Due to these inherent constraints, the proposed information-theoretic bound is likely unachievable and therefore may be very loose.

To assess the practical relevance of the bound, the authors should compare it against the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms. This would provide valuable insight into the gap between the theoretical limit and current practice.
