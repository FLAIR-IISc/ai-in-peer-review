

The paper models digital crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to derive lower bounds on the required number of queries per task for achieving a specified probability of error. Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, with symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) representing worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel model (SHC(q)) capturing scenarios where a worker acts as a spammer with probability 1−q. The coding scheme is assumed nonadaptive. The analysis considers two distinct cases: (1) when the crowdsourcer (who aggregates worker responses to determine task values) knows all workers' skill levels, and (2) when the crowdsourcer knows nothing about worker skills. In both cases, the taskmaster (who assigns tasks to workers) is assumed to have no knowledge of worker skills, resulting in each worker being assigned an equal number of tasks. The paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum required rate—defined as the number of queries per task—for k-ary incidence coding (where a worker is shown k items and asked whether they belong to similar or different breeds) under an SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, it lacks experimental validation or simulations to demonstrate how current crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) compare to the derived theoretical limits. Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 appears unnecessary or could be condensed; the authors should instead prioritize including proof sketches for the theorems. The work appears novel, as the paper correctly claims no prior information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing exists. The results are highly useful, enabling designers to determine the minimum query rate required to achieve a target error probability, which can inform pricing strategies for crowdsourcing projects. The appendix was not reviewed, so no assessment of the proofs is provided.
