This paper tackles the problem of optimally aggregating responses in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework, aiming to establish fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system with noisy channels representing workers, and derive bounds on achievable performance. While the paper presented a challenging read, the core idea of applying information theory to crowdsourcing design is novel and potentially impactful.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in crowdsourcing, particularly in aggregation techniques like majority voting [5] and the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5, 9, 12, 10, 13]. It differentiates itself by moving beyond algorithmic design and analyzing *fundamental limits* – what's theoretically possible with any querying and decoding strategy. The paper also explores a specific querying scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and considers optimized query pricing. However, related work would benefit from acknowledging the growing research area of robust economic incentives for workers, often framed as "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing," which also addresses worker heterogeneity and combinations with advanced aggregation methods.

The paper's strength lies in its attempt to provide a theoretical foundation for crowdsourcing system design. Theorems 2 and 3 establish lower bounds on the required query rate based on worker skill levels and desired accuracy. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is also a positive contribution, offering a potentially more efficient querying strategy. However, the presentation hinders full appreciation of these results. The constant reliance on information-theoretic terminology (channels, codes, distortion) makes it difficult to translate the findings into practical implications for crowdsourcing practitioners. A more intuitive explanation of the results, framed directly within the context of crowdsourcing, is needed. For instance, more emphasis could be placed on the insights at the end of Section 3, and a concluding section summarizing key takeaways would be valuable.

Several aspects could be clarified. The distinction between “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” (lines 106-110) is unclear and requires better definition. In the description of k-ary incidence coding (line 275-276), the phrasing is convoluted and should be revised for readability. The notation "A A(X1,X2)" (line 122) appears to be a typographical error. Figure 2 lacks sufficient detail in its caption, making it difficult to interpret the results presented. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of information theory to fundamentally limit crowdsourcing performance is a new and promising approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides mathematically grounded bounds on achievable performance.
*   **Potential Impact:** Understanding these fundamental limits could guide the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to understand for readers unfamiliar with information theory. The heavy reliance on technical jargon obscures the practical implications of the results.
*   **Accessibility:** The results are not presented in a way that is readily usable by crowdsourcing practitioners.
*   **Limited Practical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical validation or simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. However, its lack of clarity and limited practical focus weaken its impact. While the mathematical analysis is sound, the paper would benefit significantly from a more accessible presentation and a stronger connection to real-world crowdsourcing applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focusing on improved clarity, a more intuitive explanation of the results, and a more comprehensive discussion of related work.
