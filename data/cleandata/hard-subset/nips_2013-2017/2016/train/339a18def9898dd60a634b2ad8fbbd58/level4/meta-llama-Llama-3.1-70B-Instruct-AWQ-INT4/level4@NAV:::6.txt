This paper presents an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff for label crowdsourcing, leveraging rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The derivation of this bound is technically sound. If this represents the first application of rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem to the crowdsourcing problem, it could be considered a significant contribution to the field. However, the originality and practical implications of this work remain uncertain, as the derivation of the information-theoretic bound is relatively straightforward and the bound may be overly loose. 

The primary concern lies in the differences between the channel coding problem and the crowdsourcing problem. Unlike channel coding, where there is considerable freedom in designing codebooks or codewords, it is unclear whether similar flexibility exists in designing the sequence of channel input u^n. In equation (3), most mappings from B(X)^n to U^(\sum mi) should be valid or implementable codebooks, otherwise, the channel capacity may be a loose bound. While simple techniques like repetition code are feasible, where majority voting serves as the natural decoding method, it is unclear how to design u^n to enhance error-correcting capabilities.

A notable distinction between the channel coding problem and the crowdsourcing problem is the inability to assign the same query to the same worker multiple times, as this would compromise the iid assumption in equation (1) or (2). In contrast, communication channels can be assumed to be iid, and the same query can be reused for the same channel. This highlights the fundamental constraints present in the crowdsourcing problem, which are not accounted for in the proposed information-theoretic bound, potentially leading to a loose bound.

Comparing the performance of state-of-the-art methods with the derived information-theoretic bound would be beneficial. Minor comments include:

- The proof for Theorem 4 could be more detailed. 
- In Theorem 4, ln(1-q) < 0. It is unclear whether \epsilonS is larger than \hat{\epsilon}. 
- In equation (17), the rationale behind H_M(E(\epsilon)) should be explained or supported with a citation from previous work.
