

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. The authors establish fundamental limits on the query budget required to achieve a given fidelity in crowdsourced labeling tasks. Specifically, they characterize the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown to the taskmaster (SL-UK) and when they are known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The framework leverages the source-channel separation theorem to derive these limits, and also analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), establishing a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer-hammer channel model.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by framing crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic problem, which is a novel perspective compared to the existing literature that primarily focuses on heuristic aggregation algorithms. The authors effectively relate their work to prior NIPS contributions on crowdsourcing (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13]), demonstrating how their information-theoretic approach provides a complementary perspective to the algorithmic studies. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the results represent a significant step toward understanding fundamental limits in crowdsourcing systems.

Regarding quality, the paper establishes rigorous theoretical bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) that provide asymptotic performance limits for crowdsourcing systems. The derivations follow standard information-theoretic approaches, and the results are well-supported by mathematical analysis. The authors also provide a concrete analysis of kIC (Theorem 4), which demonstrates how specific query designs relate to the fundamental limits. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the theoretical limits translate to practical crowdsourcing systems. The asymptotic nature of the results (L → ∞) is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed in terms of practical relevance.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-structured sections and clear mathematical formulations. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within the broader crowdsourcing literature, and the model is presented logically. The description of kIC in Section 2.3 is particularly helpful for understanding the query scheme. However, the paper would be clearer if it more explicitly stated the assumptions underlying the information-theoretic model (e.g., the iid assumption for the source, the memoryless channel assumption for workers).

The originality of the paper lies in its application of information theory to crowdsourcing, which represents a fresh perspective on the problem. While the technical approach (JSCC with random channel states) builds on established information theory, the specific application to crowdsourcing is novel. The authors adequately distinguish their work from previous contributions, particularly from the algorithmic perspective of the crowdsourcing literature.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it establishes fundamental performance limits that can guide the design of future crowdsourcing systems. The framework provides a theoretical benchmark against which practical algorithms can be evaluated, and the analysis of kIC offers concrete insights into query design. However, the paper would be strengthened by more discussion of how the asymptotic results translate to practical systems with finite datasets.

I have two main concerns about the paper:

First, the authors rely on the source-channel separation theorem without justification for the case when the channel state (worker skill levels) is unknown at the encoder but may be known at the decoder. While I believe the separation theorem still holds in this scenario, the authors should either provide a proof or cite an existing reference that establishes this result for random channel states with encoder ignorance.

Second, the model is somewhat restrictive in that it assumes the taskmaster and crowdsourcer only have access to the labels (B(X)) rather than the original items (X). This ignores the potential value of side information from the original data, which could significantly improve performance. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more thorough discussion of how side information would affect their bounds would strengthen the paper.

The paper also lacks a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits. As another reviewer noted, recent work by Kostina and Verdu (IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, 2013) provides non-asymptotic converses for JSCC that would be highly relevant to this problem. Including such an analysis would make the paper more practically useful for real-world crowdsourcing applications.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution by applying information theory to crowdsourcing. The main strengths are the novel framing of the problem, the rigorous theoretical bounds, and the analysis of kIC. The main weaknesses are the lack of justification for the source-channel separation theorem in their specific setting, the restrictive model that ignores side information, and the absence of non-asymptotic analysis. While the paper provides an interesting first step toward a theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing, it would benefit from addressing these limitations.

Pro: Novel information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, rigorous asymptotic bounds, analysis of kIC query scheme, clear relation to prior work.

Con: Reliance on source-channel separation without justification for random channel states, restrictive model ignoring side information, asymptotic results without practical non-asymptotic characterization.
