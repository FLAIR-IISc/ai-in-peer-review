Summary
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental trade-off between budget (queries per item) and fidelity (distortion) using rate–distortion theory and channel capacity. Items’ labels are treated as the source B(X); each worker is a noisy channel, with two models considered: an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). Two scenarios are analyzed: (i) worker skill levels unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and (ii) skill levels perfectly inferred by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error, essentially via a separation-style argument. They also introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query design that adds combinatorial redundancy, provide a bound for kIC under an SHC model with an oracle decoder, and discuss a simple pricing rule-of-thumb.

Relation to prior work
Most prior crowdsourcing work focuses on inference under redundancy: majority vote, Dawid–Skene (EM) and its variants, spectral/graphical methods, and allocation/bandit strategies (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Whitehill et al.; Zhang et al.; Vinayak–Oymak–Hassibi; Abraham et al.). This paper differs by framing the entire pipeline in information-theoretic terms to obtain achievability/converse-style limits, rather than proposing a new inference or allocation algorithm. To my knowledge, the explicit use of rate–distortion and capacity to bound budget–fidelity in crowdsourcing is uncommon and could be novel if no close antecedent exists.

Strengths
- Clear, unified information-theoretic formulation that separates source uncertainty from worker noise.
- Closed-form expressions for Rmin in MSC settings that cleanly highlight the gap between SL-UK and SL-CS (average worker vs average of per-worker channel qualities).
- kIC is a concrete, structured query design that induces error-correction via invalid response patterns; the SHC analysis provides intuition and a simple pricing heuristic.
- The framework helps reason about ultimate performance ceilings independent of specific inference algorithms.

Main concerns and weaknesses
- Practical tightness of the bounds: Unlike communication channels, the feasible encoding space in crowdsourcing is heavily constrained. Mapping (3) implicitly treats almost all mappings B(X)n → UΣmi as admissible, but many are not implementable under realistic constraints (human comprehensibility, per-worker budget limits, no repeated identical queries to the same worker, limited adaptivity, and dependence structures). As a result, separation-based bounds may be quite loose.
- Limited codebook design freedom: In channel coding we can design rich codes (repetition, Hamming, RS, LDPC, etc.) and reuse the same channel many times in an iid manner. In crowdsourcing, we typically cannot assign the exact same query to the same worker repeatedly (memory/strategic behavior breaks the iid assumption in (1)–(2)), and queries must be meaningful to humans. Beyond simple redundancy (e.g., repetition with majority vote), it is unclear how to construct sequences u^n with strong error-correction while respecting constraints. This gap undermines the operational relevance of the capacity-style bounds unless the admissible code class is explicitly restricted and analyzed.
- Assumptions in kIC analysis: Theorem 4 relies on an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers vs hammers and assumes non-adaptive, uniform query assignment. The SHC model is stylized; in practice worker behavior is more nuanced. This further limits the predictive value of the bound.
- Lack of empirical validation: No comparison is provided between the information-theoretic limits and the performance of state-of-the-art inference/assignment schemes (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral–EM, convex clustering) under MSC/SHC-like conditions or on real AMT data. Such comparisons would calibrate tightness.
- Clarity/notation: M (response choices) and N (number of classes) are easy to conflate; some equations and assumptions would benefit from more precise statements.

Quality
- The derivations appear technically sound given the assumptions and follow standard rate–distortion and capacity arguments. However, the paper should more explicitly justify the step from worker heterogeneity to HM(E(ε)) in (12)/(17) (i.e., expectation of per-worker channel entropies/capacities over the skill distribution), with a citation (e.g., separation with channel state known at the decoder; Cover–Thomas; El Gamal–Kim).
- Theorem 4’s inequality needs careful sign handling because ln(1−q) < 0; clarify the direction of the bound and the role of ε̄S versus ε̂.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, but several definitions and assumptions (feedback/feedforward, independence, item sampling) should be stated once and referenced.
- Tighten the proof sketches (especially Theorem 4) to make all steps explicit.
- Fix minor typos and standardize notation (budget vs budge; Wiley; conference names).

Originality
- If this is the first explicit application of rate–distortion/capacity to crowdsourcing budget–fidelity limits, the conceptual contribution is novel. The kIC perspective is known in related clustering-by-queries work, but its connection to information-theoretic lower bounds and pricing here is a useful synthesis.

Significance
- The work can inform the design space by indicating what might be achievable in idealized regimes. Its significance depends on how tight the bounds are under realistic encoding constraints and how well they predict or bound modern algorithms’ performance. Without that calibration, practical impact is limited.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clean information-theoretic framework and closed-form bounds.
- Useful insight into effects of known vs unknown skills.
- Concrete analysis of kIC and an interpretable pricing heuristic.

Cons:
- Potentially loose due to unrealistic encoding assumptions and oracle elements.
- No empirical calibration against strong baselines.
- Some technical and clarity issues in proofs and notation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly characterize the admissible encoding class (e.g., non-adaptive, per-worker distinct queries, no reuse to the same worker, bounded query arity/cost) and derive converses under these constraints. If possible, provide achievability via an implementable scheme (e.g., randomized incidence designs) to narrow gaps.
- Compare derived limits to the performance of majority vote, Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral–EM, and convex clustering on synthetic MSC/SHC data and at least one real labeling task.
- Clarify the role of M vs N and the conditions under which HN(·) and HM(·) appear; justify HM(E(ε)) in (17) with a clear argument or citation.
- Expand the proof of Theorem 4, define ε̄S precisely, and resolve the sign issue with ln(1−q).
- Discuss adaptivity and feedback: do separation-style results extend, or do we expect gains?

Minor comments
- In (8), the minimization is over C, C′ subject to E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ Dt.
- Theorem 4: given ln(1−q) < 0, check the inequality direction and whether ε̄S ≥ ε̂ is assumed.
- Explain why HM(E(ε)) arises in (17) or cite prior work on mixtures of MSCs with state known at the decoder.
- Several typographical issues in references and text (publisher names, conference labels).
