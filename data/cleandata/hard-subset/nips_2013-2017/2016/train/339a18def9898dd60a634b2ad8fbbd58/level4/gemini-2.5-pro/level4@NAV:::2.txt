This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the crowdsourcing problem and derives the fundamental, asymptotic limits on the required number of queries per item. The core of the approach is to model crowdsourcing as a problem of joint source-channel coding (JSCC) with a random channel state, where the state is unknown to the encoder but may be known to the decoder. In this formulation, item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster functions as the encoder, the crowd workers represent multiple uses of a channel, their varying skill levels correspond to the random states of this channel, and the crowdsourcer acts as the decoder. Leveraging this model, the paper applies established results on coding rates for the JSCC problem to determine the fundamental limits for crowdsourcing. Additionally, the authors derive a lower bound for the error probability of a "k-ary incidence coding" scheme within a specific instance of their model. In summary, this is a well-articulated paper that pioneers the application of a JSCC framework to the crowdsourcing problem.

Some technical comments:
1. The proposed model relies on the source-channel separation theorem to derive its main results (Theorems 2 and 3). However, a crucial point that needs to be addressed is the validity of this theorem in the specific context of a channel with a random state that is unknown to the encoder. While it is plausible that the separation theorem remains valid under these conditions, the paper would be strengthened by either providing a proof or citing existing literature that establishes this.

2. The current model defines the taskmaster (encoder) and crowdsourcer (decoder) as having access only to the item labels and worker-generated labels, respectively. This formulation may be overly restrictive, as it overlooks a practical scenario where both parties have access to the original items themselves. This access constitutes side information for the JSCC problem. Although side information available only at the encoder might not offer benefits, its presence at the decoder, or at both encoder and decoder, could significantly improve performance. Incorporating side information would make the model more realistic and its conclusions more compelling.

3. In Section 4.2, the discussion of Figure 2 states that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) "improve" with increasing k and q. This phrasing is ambiguous. It is presumed that "improve" means the lower bound becomes numerically smaller (i.e., tighter), suggesting that the code's potential performance is better. However, describing this as an "improvement" to the bound itself is imprecise, especially in the absence of a corresponding upper bound for comparison. The authors should clarify this statement.

Edited following authors' response:
While the application of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is a novel and interesting direction, a characterization of the fundamental limits in the non-asymptotic (finite blocklength) regime would be of greater practical utility. To this end, the authors are strongly encouraged to consider the recent work by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, specifically: V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013.
