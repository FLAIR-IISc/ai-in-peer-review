- Frames crowdsourcing as a rate–distortion problem and analyzes budget (queries/item) vs fidelity (error rate) trade-offs using information theory.  
- Models workers as independent noisy channels: M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer model SHC(q), focusing on non-adaptive querying.  
- Studies two scenarios: SL-UK (unknown worker skill levels) and SL-CS (known/perfectly inferred skills), with uniform task assignment.  
- Derives separation-based information-theoretic lower bounds on minimum query rate for a target Hamming distortion.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), showing invalid response patterns give it an error-correcting flavor.  
- Provides a necessary rate condition for kIC under SHC(q) and a pricing rule-of-thumb relating effort across k.  
- Complements existing aggregation literature by providing fundamental limits any algorithm must respect.  
- Strength: clear, principled joint source–channel coding model with interpretable separation-based bounds for SL-UK and SL-CS.  
- Strength: explicit comparison of known vs unknown worker skills clarifies budget impact of skill estimation.  
- Strength: concrete, practically relevant kIC analysis and useful pricing guidance for practitioners.  
- Strength: well organized and generally well written.  
- Weakness: no experiments or simulations to evaluate bound tightness or relate common methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral) to limits.  
- Weakness: simplifying assumptions (non-adaptive querying, MSC/SHC models, iid items) limit realism; missing discussion of difficulty-dependent noise and inter-worker correlations.  
- Weakness: excessive general IT background (Sec. 3.1, Sec. 2.4) at expense of problem-specific intuition and proofs.  
- Weakness: lack of proof sketches in main text; full proofs relegated to appendix reduce accessibility.  
- Weakness: kIC pricing model needs clearer justification (time/effort vs monetary cost) and domain of validity for the rule-of-thumb.  
- Weakness: minor presentation issues — inconsistent notation, incomplete references, and some grammar slips.  
- Quality: theoretical development appears sound and separation approach appropriate, but practical relevance is unclear without experiments and finite-block analysis.  
- Clarity: mostly clear and well structured; recommend trimming background and adding proof sketches and a small illustrative example of SL-UK vs SL-CS gap.  
- Originality: novel framing of crowdsourcing as rate–distortion and separation-based lower bounds distinct from prior inference-focused work.  
- Significance: provides valuable benchmarks and actionable insights for redundancy and pricing, but practical impact depends on empirical validation and modeling discussion.  
- For acceptance: conceptually clean, novel, bridges theory and practice via kIC analysis and pricing, likely to stimulate research.  
- Against acceptance: lacks empirical validation, relies on strong simplifying assumptions, and misallocates space to generic background instead of proofs/insights.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution recommend acceptance if authors add basic simulations and streamline background to include proof sketches; otherwise borderline due to missing empirical support.
