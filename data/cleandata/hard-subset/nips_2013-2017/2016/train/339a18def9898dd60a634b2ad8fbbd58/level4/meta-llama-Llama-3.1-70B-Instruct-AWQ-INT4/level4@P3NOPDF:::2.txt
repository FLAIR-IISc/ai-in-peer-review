The authors provide an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, deriving fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic sense. By modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, they leverage existing results on coding rates to establish these limits. Specifically, the labels of items are represented as an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) source, the taskmaster as an encoder, the crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, the workers' skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder.

The paper is well-written and may be a first step towards JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing. The authors also provide a lower bound on the error probability of the "k-ary incidence coding" scheme under a specific crowdsourcing model. However, there are several technical aspects that warrant further consideration.

Firstly, the authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem in information theory to obtain their results (Theorem 2 and 3). However, they should explicitly argue that this theorem remains valid when the channel has a random state, unknown at the encoder but potentially known at the receiver. While it is likely that the separation theorem still holds in such situations, the authors should provide a proof or reference an existing one to solidify their claims.

Secondly, the current model may be overly restrictive, as it assumes the taskmaster (encoder) and crowdsourcer (decoder) only have access to the labels of the items, ignoring the potential availability of original data. This original data could serve as side information for both the encoder and decoder, potentially improving the performance of the JSCC problem. Incorporating side information into the model would make it more convincing and realistic.

Lastly, in Section 4.2, the authors comment on the curves in Figure 2, stating that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) improve as k and q grow. However, this statement is somewhat misleading, as the lower bound itself does not improve, but rather becomes smaller, indicating potential better performance of the code. To clarify, the authors should rephrase this statement to avoid confusion.

In response to the authors' comments, it is worth noting that the use of JSCC to model crowdsourcing is indeed interesting. Nevertheless, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more useful. The authors may want to explore recent results by Kostina and Verdu on non-asymptotic converses for JSCC, which could provide valuable insights for future work.
