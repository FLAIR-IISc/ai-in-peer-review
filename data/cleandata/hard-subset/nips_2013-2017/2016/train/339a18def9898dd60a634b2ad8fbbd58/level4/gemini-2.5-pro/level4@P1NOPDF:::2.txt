The authors present an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, deriving fundamental, asymptotic limits on the number of queries required per item. The crowdsourcing process is modeled as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with a random channel state, where the state is unknown to the encoder but may be known to the decoder. In this framework, the item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster acts as the encoder, the crowd workers represent multiple uses of a channel, their varying skill levels are modeled as the channel's random state, and the crowdsourcer serves as the decoder.

Leveraging this model, the authors apply existing results on coding rates for JSCC to establish fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability for a specific scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," under a particular crowdsourcing model. Overall, this is a well-written paper that takes a promising first step toward modeling crowdsourcing using a JSCC framework.

Some technical comments:
1.  The results in Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 rely on the standard source-channel separation theorem. However, the authors should justify the application of this theorem to their specific setting: a channel with a random state that is unknown to the encoder but may or may not be known to the decoder. While I believe the separation theorem likely holds under these conditions, the authors should either provide a proof or cite an existing one to substantiate this claim.

2.  The proposed model defines the taskmaster (encoder) as having access only to the true item labels, and the crowdsourcer (decoder) as having access only to the worker-provided labels. This model seems overly restrictive, as it overlooks a key aspect of practical crowdsourcing: both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer typically have access to the items themselves. This access to the original data constitutes side information that could be exploited by the encoder and decoder. While side information at the encoder alone may not improve the capacity, its presence at the decoder, or at both encoder and decoder, can be very helpful. Incorporating side information would make the model more convincing and realistic.

3.  In Section 4.2, when discussing Figure 2, the authors state that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) "improve" as k and q increase. Presumably, this means the lower bound on the error probability decreases, suggesting better potential performance for the code. However, describing a lower bound as "improving" is ambiguous. A lower bound becoming smaller does not necessarily mean the bound itself is better (e.g., tighter), especially without an upper bound for comparison. The phrasing should be more precise.

*Edited following authors' response:*
The application of joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing is interesting. However, as another reviewer also pointed out, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be of greater practical value. The authors are encouraged to consider recent results on non-asymptotic analysis, such as the work by Kostina and Verdu on JSCC in the finite blocklength regime:

V. Kostina and S. Verdu, "Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime", IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013.
