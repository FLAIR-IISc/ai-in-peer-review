- Presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving lower bounds on queries per task to achieve a target error probability.  
- Models workers as parallel, independent channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) for errors and spammer-hammer channel SHC(q) for random responders.  
- Assumes non-adaptive coding: queries are fixed in advance and do not depend on prior responses.  
- Considers two knowledge scenarios: crowdsourcer knows worker skills vs. does not; taskmaster is assumed to have no skill knowledge and assigns equal tasks per worker.  
- Derives a necessary condition for the minimum required rate (queries/task) when using k-ary incidence coding with an SHC(q) worker pool.  
- k-ary incidence coding posed as asking workers whether k presented items belong to the same or different categories.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and claims novelty as the first information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing.  
- Potentially high impact: theoretical lower bounds can guide query budgeting, pricing, and system design.  
- Major weakness: no empirical validation (experiments or simulations) comparing real-world schemes to theoretical limits.  
- Presentation could be improved: Section 2.4 and Section 3.1 are overly lengthy and should be condensed; prioritize proof sketches over exhaustive detail in the main text.  
- Reviewer did not evaluate the appendix, so completeness/correctness of proofs there is not assessed.  
- Overall recommendation: a valuable, novel contribution worth acceptance pending better empirical validation and streamlined exposition.
