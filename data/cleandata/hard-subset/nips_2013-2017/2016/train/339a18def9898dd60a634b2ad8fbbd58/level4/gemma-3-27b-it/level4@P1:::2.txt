The authors provide an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic setting. Specifically, the crowdsourcing problem is modeled as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The channel state represents worker skill levels, assumed unknown at the encoder (taskmaster) but potentially known at the decoder (crowdsourcer). The item labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, with the taskmaster as the encoder, crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder. Using established results from information theory, the authors derive fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item required for crowdsourcing. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability of a coding scheme called “k-ary incidence coding” under a specific crowdsourcing model. Overall, this is a well-written paper and represents a first step towards modeling the crowdsourcing problem within a JSCC framework. 

Some technical comments: 1. The crowdsourcing problem is modeled as JSCC with a random channel state. The authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem to obtain their results (Theorems 2 and 3). However, they should first justify the applicability of the separation theorem when the channel has a random state unknown at the encoder, but potentially known at the receiver. While the separation theorem likely still holds in these cases, a proof or citation would strengthen the argument. 2. The model assumes the taskmaster only observes item labels, and the crowdsourcer only observes worker responses. This may be overly restrictive, as both the taskmaster and crowdsourcer often have access to the original data (the items themselves). This side information could be leveraged by the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem. Side information available at both encoder and decoder, or even at the decoder alone, could significantly improve performance. Considering side information would make the model more realistic and convincing. 3. In Section 4.2, the authors state that the lower bounds for kIC improve as *k* and *q* grow. This likely means the lower bound decreases as *k* and *q* increase, potentially indicating better code performance. However, this doesn’t necessarily mean the lower bound *itself* improves without an upper bound for comparison.

Following discussions with the authors, it's clear that a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more valuable. The authors should consider recent results from Kostina and Verdu concerning non-asymptotic converses for JSCC: V. Kostina and S. Verdu, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” *IEEE Transactions on Information Theory*, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
