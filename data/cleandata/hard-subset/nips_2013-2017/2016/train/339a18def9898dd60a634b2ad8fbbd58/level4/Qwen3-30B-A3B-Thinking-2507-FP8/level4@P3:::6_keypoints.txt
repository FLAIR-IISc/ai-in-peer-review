- Models crowdsourcing as a communication system and studies budget-fidelity trade-offs via rate-distortion and channel coding frameworks.  
- Derives theoretical bounds for unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS) with formal theorems and proofs.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme with error-correcting properties and provides numerical comparisons to bounds.  
- Theoretical derivations (Theorems 2 and 3) are technically sound and rigorously proved in appendices.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; figures effectively convey the communication-system model and kIC intuition.  
- Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, filling a theoretical gap beyond algorithmic approaches like Dawid-Skene and majority voting.  
- kIC represents a novel, actionable query-design idea that leverages error correction in crowdsourcing.  
- Main weakness: information-theoretic bounds may be loose because crowdsourcing coding constraints (no reuse of identical queries with same worker) differ from standard channel coding assumptions.  
- Authors should clarify whether derived bounds are actually achievable with practical coding schemes or only optimistic fundamental limits.  
- Lacks direct, quantitative comparison between theoretical bounds and state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods (e.g., DS, majority voting); a comparison table of error rates is recommended.  
- Theorem 4 needs clearer exposition (handling of ln(1-q)<0 and the relationship between ε̄S and ε̂).  
- Expression H_M(E(ε)) in Theorem 3 requires better explanation or citation, as it extends standard information-theoretic notation.  
- Originality could be better qualified by explicitly comparing kIC and the approach to related work (e.g., Korlakai Vinayak et al. NIPS 2014) to strengthen novelty claims.  
- Recommendation: weak accept contingent on addressing achievability/looseness of bounds, adding empirical comparisons to existing methods, and clarifying Theorem 4 and H_M(E(ε)).
