- Introduces an information-theoretic JSCC framework for crowdsourcing with random channel states, characterizing query budget limits for target fidelity.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and known skill levels (SL-CS).  
- Derives theoretical bounds on the minimum number of queries per item using the source-channel separation theorem.  
- Provides analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and a lower bound on its error under the spammer-hammer model (Theorem 4).  
- Novelty: brings an information-theoretic perspective and JSCC connection to crowdsourcing, which is uncommon in prior work.  
- Technical strength: Theorems 2 and 3 elucidate how worker skill affects performance; kIC gives a concrete coding-theoretic example.  
- Well-written and organized with a thorough literature review.  
- Major concern: reliance on source-channel separation without proving or citing its validity for random channel states where state is unknown at encoder.  
- Model limitation: assumes taskmaster/crowdsourcer only have labels (not original data), ignoring useful side information present in real-world tasks.  
- Ambiguity: Section 4.2’s phrase that "lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow" is misleading and needs clarification (rate decreases vs. bound tightness).  
- Missing finite-blocklength/non-asymptotic analysis; should consider recent finite-blocklength JSCC results (e.g., Kostina & Verdú).  
- Limited practical impact: asymptotic focus with no empirical validation or implementation guidance reduces immediate utility for practitioners.  
- Presentation issues: some technical parts and figures (notably Figure 2) need clearer labeling and more accessible exposition.  
- Recommendation: major revision to address theoretical gaps, relax/model side information, clarify interpretations, and better connect to practical systems.
