This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the problem of labeling data from crowdsourcing, utilizing an information-theoretic framework. The authors model each worker as a noisy channel, considering two types of channels: the symmetric memoryless channel without feedback and the spammer/hammer channel model. By transforming the problem into a coding problem for the channel, they investigate the trade-offs between distortion and budget. A key contribution of the paper is the introduction of a basic coding scheme, termed k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which involves requiring crowdsourced tasks to label a subset of k items at a time.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, making it accessible to readers with a basic understanding of information theory. The use of theoretical models and proofs adds rigor to the analysis, and the numerical results provide a useful benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed coding scheme.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity, considering scenarios where the workers' skill levels are unknown or perfectly known. These bounds serve as a useful benchmark for evaluating the performance of different coding schemes.

The k-ary incidence coding scheme is an interesting contribution, as it provides a simple yet effective way to improve the overall performance of crowdsourcing. The authors demonstrate that kIC can achieve better performance than traditional coding schemes, especially when the number of queries per item is limited.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The use of figures and tables helps to illustrate the key concepts and results. The references provided are relevant and up-to-date, demonstrating the authors' awareness of the current state of research in the field.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. One potential limitation is that the analysis is largely theoretical, and it would be useful to see more experimental results or real-world applications of the proposed approach. Additionally, the authors could provide more discussion on the practical implications of their results, such as how to design and optimize crowdsourcing systems to achieve better performance.

Overall, I found the paper to be well-written and interesting, with a clear and concise explanation of the authors' approach. The use of theoretical models and proofs adds rigor to the analysis, and the numerical results provide a useful benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed coding scheme.
