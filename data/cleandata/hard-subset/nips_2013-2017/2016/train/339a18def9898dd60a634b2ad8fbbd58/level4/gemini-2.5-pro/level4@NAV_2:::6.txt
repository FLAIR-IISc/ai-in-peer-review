This paper frames the problem of label crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The central idea is to treat the true labels of a dataset as a source, the crowd workers as a set of parallel noisy channels, and the query/inference mechanisms as an encoding/decoding scheme. Leveraging rate-distortion theory, the authors derive fundamental limits on the trade-off between the budget (queries per item, or "rate") and the achievable fidelity (error probability, or "distortion"). The analysis is presented for two scenarios: one where worker skill levels are unknown, and another where they are known to the aggregator. The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC).

**Strengths of the paper:**
*   The primary strength of this work is its novel application of rate-distortion theory to establish fundamental performance limits for crowdsourcing. This provides a new and principled theoretical lens for understanding the budget-fidelity trade-off, connecting the field to the well-established domain of information theory.
*   The theoretical derivations of the information-theoretic bounds under the proposed model appear to be technically sound and are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses of the paper:**
*   A major concern is the practical validity of the analogy between crowdsourcing query schemes and channel codes. In communication theory, the channel capacity bounds are meaningful because there is immense freedom in designing powerful codes (e.g., LDPC, Turbo codes) to approach these limits. It is unclear if a similarly rich family of "codes" (i.e., practical query schemes) exists for crowdsourcing. The set of meaningful queries one can pose to a human worker is likely far more constrained than the set of possible binary sequences for a channel code.
*   Due to the above-mentioned constraints on query design, the derived information-theoretic bounds risk being too loose to be practically informative. Beyond simple repetition codes (which correspond to asking multiple workers the same question, with majority voting as a decoder), the paper does not convincingly argue that more complex, capacity-approaching schemes are feasible in the crowdsourcing context.
*   The assumption of workers as memoryless channels may not hold in practice. For instance, assigning the same query to the same worker multiple times is fundamentally different from sending the same symbol over a memoryless communication channel. A worker would likely provide the same (or a highly correlated) answer, violating the i.i.d. error assumption that underpins the channel model. This potential mismatch between the model and reality calls into question the direct applicability of the bounds.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
To demonstrate the tightness and practical relevance of the derived bounds, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a comparison with the empirical performance of existing state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., those based on Dawid-Skene or spectral methods). Plotting the performance of these methods on the rate-distortion plane alongside the theoretical bounds would help ground the contribution and show how close (or far) current methods are from the theoretical optimum.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
*   In Equation (8), the definition of the rate-distortion function is typically a minimization over all conditional distributions `P(Z_hat|Z)` that satisfy the distortion constraint, rather than over specific codes `C, C'`. Please clarify the notation for consistency with standard information theory literature.
*   The proof for Theorem 4 could be expanded with additional details to improve its clarity and self-containment.
*   In Theorem 4, the term `ln(1-q)` is negative. For the bound to be meaningful, does this imply a condition such that `epsilon_S > epsilon_hat`? Some clarification on the interpretation of this inequality would be helpful.
*   In the denominator of Equation (11) in Theorem 2, the term `H_M(E(epsilon))` arises. This corresponds to the capacity of an "average" channel. A brief justification for this step, perhaps citing Jensen's inequality for the concave entropy function, would strengthen the argument.
