- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a communication system, mapping budget to rate and inference quality to distortion.  
- Main contribution: applies rate-distortion theory to derive algorithm-agnostic budget–fidelity trade-off bounds.  
- Derives bounds for two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
- Proposes and analyzes a practical k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) scheme and compares it to the theoretical limits.  
- Strength: novel, elegant information-theoretic framing that is original in the crowdsourcing literature.  
- Strength: establishes fundamental benchmarks that can guide system design and future research.  
- Concern: model makes simplifying assumptions and may be tailored to point-to-point communication, limiting real-world applicability.  
- Question: workers modeled by a single skill parameter with equal query allocation—how do bounds change if the encoder can use prior worker ratings for adaptive allocation?  
- Question: channel models (MSC, SHC) assume random, non-malicious errors—how robust is the framework to strategic or adversarial workers and correlated/memory effects?  
- Question: analysis assumes known source distribution P(B(X))—how would limits change if this distribution is unknown and must be learned, and should this assumption be stated earlier?  
- Presentation issue: Figure 2 labels/curves are hard to distinguish and the meaning of k=1 for kIC is unclear; caption/text need clarification.  
- Minor corrections: incorrect figure references on page 4 (line 135) and avoid capitalizing 'Theorem/Lemma/Corollary' when not citing numbered items.  
- Overall: thought-provoking and conceptually strong, but assumptions require more discussion to assess practical relevance.
