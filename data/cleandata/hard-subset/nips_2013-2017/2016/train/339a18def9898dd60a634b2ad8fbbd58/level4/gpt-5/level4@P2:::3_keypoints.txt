- Models crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem, deriving budget–fidelity fundamental limits via information theory.
- Considers two scenarios: unknown worker skills to crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS), showing known skills increase effective capacity.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where workers group k items, deriving necessary rate conditions under a spammer–hammer model and a pricing heuristic across k.
- Connects bounds to existing aggregation methods, focusing on ultimate performance limits rather than algorithms.

- Strength: Presents a clear principled framework yielding capacity-like and rate–distortion-like limits for crowdsourcing.
- Strength: Novel explicit budget–fidelity bounds under MSC and SHC worker models and the SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast.
- Strength: kIC analysis gives practical insight into constrained responses creating error-correction and a simple cost comparison across k.
- Strength: Relevant unifying lens for designing redundancy, worker assignment, and query formats.

- Weakness: Heavy information-theoretic notation reduces accessibility for many NIPS readers; results should be restated in crowdsourcing terms.
- Weakness: Modeling assumptions (iid items, symmetric MSC errors, non-adaptive queries, oracle SL-CS) limit practical applicability.
- Weakness: kIC bounds are limited to SHC, binary labels, and an oracle decoder; little discussion of achievable performance with practical decoders.
- Weakness: No empirical evaluation or comparisons to show tightness of bounds or real-world benefits of kIC.
- Weakness: Presentation issues—unclear “taskmaster” vs “crowdsourcer,” awkward sentences, and Figure 2 lacks informative caption and interpretation.

- Quality: Technical claims appear sound with proofs in appendices, but paper needs more intuition, worked numerical examples, and proof sketches for key theorems.
- Clarity: Organization is good but readability suffers from jargon; recommend restating theorems with concrete numeric examples and a concise “Design Takeaways” section.
- Originality: The information-theoretic approach and kIC analysis are novel contributions relative to EM/spectral/graph inference literature.
- Significance: Results can guide redundancy, skill estimation investment, and query format design, though practical impact requires bridging to algorithms and adaptive strategies.

- Suggestion: Restate key results in application terms with small numerical examples and add a “Design Takeaways” summary.
- Suggestion: Clarify or consolidate the roles “taskmaster” vs “crowdsourcer” early and consistently.
- Suggestion: Expand related work to include incentive/mechanism design literature addressing heterogeneous abilities and effort elicitation.
- Suggestion: Discuss limitations (iid, MSC/SHC idealizations, non-adaptivity) and outline extensions to adaptive querying or difficulty-dependent noise.
- Suggestion: Improve Figure 2 with axis labels, parameter descriptions, curve explanations, and annotated achievable/impossible regions.
- Suggestion: Add short intuitions for SL-UK vs SL-CS differences and for the kIC error-correction effect.

- Acceptance argument: Novel theoretical framework with clear NIPS relevance that bridges information theory and crowdsourcing, offering guidance for system design.
- Acceptance argument: Insightful bounds that can catalyze new research connecting theory to practical algorithms.

- Rejection argument: Accessibility and presentation issues limit impact for the NIPS audience.
- Rejection argument: Practical implications are abstract without experiments or links to implementable aggregation schemes.
- Rejection argument: kIC results are narrow in scope (binary labels, oracle decoder).
