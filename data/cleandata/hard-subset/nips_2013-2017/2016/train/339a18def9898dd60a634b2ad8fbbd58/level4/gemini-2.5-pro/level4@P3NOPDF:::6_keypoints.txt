- Derives an information-theoretic bound on budget–fidelity tradeoff for crowdsourcing via rate-distortion and channel coding; technical derivation is clear and well-executed.  
- Potentially significant if this is the first application of these information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing.  
- Primary concern: the channel-coding analogy may be fundamentally flawed because coding theory assumes freedom to design large codebooks, unlike constrained task-assignment strategies.  
- The i.i.d. worker-response assumption is invalid in practice—repeating a task to the same worker yields correlated answers, undermining “repetition code” analogies.  
- Because implementable task-assignments form a small subset of theoretical codebooks, the derived bound may be unachievable or very loose, limiting practical utility.  
- Secondary concern: the technical contribution is largely a straightforward application of standard information-theoretic results, so novelty rests mainly on the conceptual mapping.  
- Recommendation: empirically compare the theoretical bound to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., EM, belief propagation) on benchmark datasets to test tightness.  
- Recommendation: include a dedicated discussion of practical constraints of the crowdsourcing “channel” and how it differs from classical communication channels.  
- Minor: in Eq. (8) explicitly state the domain of minimization (e.g., over all p(c,c') satisfying E[d(Z,Ż)] ≤ D^t).  
- Minor: expand the proof of Theorem 4 with intermediate steps for clarity.  
- Minor: clarify Theorem 4’s inequality involving ln(1−q) (negative for q∈(0,1)) and its implications for ε_S vs. ĥε.  
- Minor: in Eq. (17) justify the term H_M(E(ε)) or provide a citation for its origin.
