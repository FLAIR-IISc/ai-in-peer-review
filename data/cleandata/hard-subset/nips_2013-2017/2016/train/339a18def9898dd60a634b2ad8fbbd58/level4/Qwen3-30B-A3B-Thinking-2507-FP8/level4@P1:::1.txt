

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing systems. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication problem where workers are represented as noisy channels, and the taskmaster's query design and the crowdsourcer's inference algorithm constitute a joint source-channel coding scheme. The paper establishes fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget (measured as queries per item) and fidelity (measured as error probability) for crowdsourcing systems. The authors introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a novel query scheme that asks workers to label subsets of k items simultaneously, and analyze its performance within their framework. The work is positioned as a significant departure from previous crowdsourcing research that primarily focused on aggregation algorithms (e.g., majority voting, Dawid-Skene) without considering fundamental performance limits.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it establishes a rigorous information-theoretic foundation for crowdsourcing, providing the first fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors derive two key theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) that characterize the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error probability under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). These results are significant because they provide ultimate performance bounds that no crowdsourcing system can exceed, regardless of the specific query or inference scheme employed. Second, the paper introduces k-ary incidence coding as a novel query scheme that leverages error-correcting properties to improve performance. The authors prove theoretical bounds on kIC performance (Theorem 4) and provide numerical results that demonstrate how performance improves with larger k and higher hammer worker probability q.

The paper is technically sound, with well-justified theorems and rigorous proofs provided in the appendices. The information-theoretic approach is novel and well-motivated, providing a fundamental perspective that complements previous algorithmic work. The kIC scheme is particularly interesting as it represents a concrete example of how query design can exploit error-correcting capabilities to improve crowdsourcing performance. The paper also provides a clear discussion of how the results relate to practical considerations, such as query pricing (Appendix E).

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-organized and well-written, with helpful figures (1b and 1c) that illustrate the kIC concept. The authors clearly define their problem setting, channel models, and coding schemes. However, the connection to recent NIPS work on crowdsourcing could be strengthened. While the paper cites relevant works (e.g., [8], [11]), it doesn't explicitly discuss how its information-theoretic approach relates to other NIPS papers on crowdsourcing that have appeared in recent years (e.g., Zhang et al. 2014, Whitehill et al. 2009, Karger et al. 2013). A more comprehensive comparison with these recent works would better establish the paper's novelty.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a fundamental question in crowdsourcing that has important practical implications for system design. The derived performance bounds provide a theoretical foundation for evaluating crowdsourcing systems, while the kIC scheme offers a concrete approach to improving efficiency. The work is likely to be valuable for both researchers developing new crowdsourcing algorithms and practitioners designing real-world crowdsourcing systems.

The main limitations of the paper include: (1) the assumption of independent channels and no feedback, which may be restrictive for practical crowdsourcing applications where worker skill levels might be correlated or where adaptive query design could improve performance; (2) the limited numerical results section, which would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with existing algorithms; and (3) the lack of discussion on how the theoretical bounds translate to practical systems, particularly regarding the implementation of the kIC scheme.

The paper is original in its approach, clearly distinguishing itself from previous work by providing fundamental limits rather than proposing new algorithms. The information-theoretic perspective is a valuable addition to the crowdsourcing literature, and the kIC scheme represents a novel contribution.

Strengths: Novel information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, rigorous derivation of fundamental limits, introduction of k-ary incidence coding with error-correcting properties, clear theoretical analysis.

Weaknesses: Limited connection to recent NIPS crowdsourcing work, restrictive channel assumptions, limited numerical results, lack of practical implementation considerations.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the crowdsourcing literature by establishing fundamental performance limits. The information-theoretic approach provides a new perspective that complements existing algorithmic work. While some practical considerations could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are significant and well-executed. The paper is technically sound, original, and addresses a fundamental question in crowdsourcing with clear implications for system design. I recommend acceptance.
