The paper tackles aggregation of crowd workers’ outputs using information-theoretic tools and presents fundamental limits for the budget–fidelity trade-off. Although I did not fully grasp all of the details (see below), I found the modeling and resulting insights to be interesting and potentially useful for designing crowdsourcing aggregation methods. I would have liked the high-level intuition behind the results (e.g., the takeaways at the end of Section 3) to be emphasized more clearly, as well as a brief conclusion highlighting the main practical lessons (I realize space is limited). While I appreciated the authors’ efforts to make the work accessible to readers without a deep background in information theory, it was still quite hard for me to follow. I suggest restating many of the results directly in crowdsourcing terms rather than primarily using the terminology of channels, codes, and distortion. In related work, please also cite the parallel (and increasingly convergent) literature on robust incentive design for workers (often under “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing”). Some of this line addresses heterogeneous worker abilities and, more recently, how to combine incentives with more sophisticated aggregation methods.
