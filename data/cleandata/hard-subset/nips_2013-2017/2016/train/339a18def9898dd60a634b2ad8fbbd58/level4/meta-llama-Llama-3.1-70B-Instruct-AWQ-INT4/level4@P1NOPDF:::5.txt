The paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, providing lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a given probability of error. The workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, specifically symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMC) to capture errors made by workers, and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to account for the scenario where a worker is a spammer with probability 1-q. The code is assumed to be non-adaptive. The analysis considers two cases: one where the crowdsourcer, responsible for determining the value of each task after collecting worker responses, has knowledge of all workers' skill levels, and another where no such knowledge is available. In both cases, the taskmaster, who distributes tasks to workers, is assumed to be unaware of the workers' skills, resulting in each worker being assigned an equal number of tasks.

The paper is well-organized and well-written. However, it lacks experimental or simulation-based results to demonstrate how current schemes, such as those used in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT), compare to the established limits. For instance, it would be beneficial to show how far existing schemes are from the theoretical limits. Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be condensed or even omitted, as they do not significantly contribute to the overall understanding of the paper. Instead, providing proof sketches for the theorems would be more valuable. The paper's novelty lies in its information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem, which has not been explored previously. This work is particularly useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, as it enables them to determine the minimum rate required to accomplish a task with a given probability of error, ultimately aiding in the pricing of such projects. Unfortunately, I did not review the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the proof.
