- Paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity tradeoff for crowdsourced labels using rate-distortion theory and channel coding; derivation is rigorous and could be significant if this is the first such application.

- Novelty and practical value are questionable: the derivation appears straightforward and the resulting bound may be very loose.

- It's unclear whether the crowdsourcing problem admits the same freedom in designing input sequences u^n as channel coding admits for codebooks, which undermines the applicability of channel-capacity-based bounds.

- If most mappings from B(X)^n to U^{Σ m_i} are not implementable, channel capacity may be a loose or irrelevant bound for crowdsourcing.

- Simple encoding schemes like repetition codes correspond to majority voting in crowdsourcing, but it's unclear how to design u^n to obtain stronger error-correction than repetition.

- A key difference: you cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times without violating the iid assumptions, unlike repeated uses of an iid communication channel.

- Such fundamental constraints (e.g., equation (3)) in crowdsourcing likely make the information-theoretic bound less tight than in channel coding, where encoding mappings have few constraints.

- To strengthen the paper, compare the derived information-theoretic bound empirically against state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods.
