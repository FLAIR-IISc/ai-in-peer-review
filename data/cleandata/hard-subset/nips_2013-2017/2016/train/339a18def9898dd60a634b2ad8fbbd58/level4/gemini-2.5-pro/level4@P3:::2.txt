This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The central idea is to model the crowdsourcing process as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. In this model, the true labels of a dataset are treated as a random source, the taskmaster's query design is the source encoder, the collective responses of the crowd workers are modeled as transmissions over a set of parallel noisy channels, and the final inference or aggregation step is the decoder. The workers' varying skill levels are cleverly captured as random states of these channels.

Using this JSCC analogy, the authors apply classical information-theoretic results to derive asymptotic lower bounds on the required "budget" (defined as the average number of queries per item) to achieve a desired "fidelity" (the maximum allowable error probability). The analysis considers two scenarios: one where worker skill levels are unknown to the aggregator/decoder (SL-UK), and another where they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC) within a spammer-hammer worker model, deriving a lower bound on its performance. This work distinguishes itself from prior art, which has largely focused on specific algorithmic approaches like majority voting or EM-based methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene), by instead seeking to establish ultimate performance benchmarks for *any* possible crowdsourcing scheme.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and elegant formulation of the crowdsourcing problem from a fundamental information-theoretic perspective. Framing the budget-fidelity trade-off as a rate-distortion problem is an insightful approach that provides a new lens through which to view a well-studied area in machine learning.
2.  **Significance:** Establishing fundamental limits is a valuable scientific contribution. If technically sound, these results could serve as an important benchmark for practitioners and researchers to compare the efficiency of existing and future crowdsourcing algorithms. The concept of analyzing query structures (like kIC) as explicit channel codes is particularly compelling and could inspire new, more efficient query designs.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mapping from the components of a crowdsourcing system to the elements of a communication system is explained clearly, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Technical Justification:** The main theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on the source-channel separation theorem. However, the paper applies this theorem to a scenario with a random channel state (the worker skill level) that is unknown at the encoder. While the separation principle may indeed hold in this setting, this is not a standard textbook case and requires explicit justification. The authors should either provide a proof that the theorem extends to this scenario or cite a specific result from the information theory literature (e.g., in texts like El Gamal & Kim) that covers it.
2.  **Model Abstraction and Realism:** The JSCC model, while elegant, abstracts away a crucial component of most crowdsourcing systems: the data items themselves. Both the taskmaster (encoder) and the aggregator (decoder) have access to the items being labeled (e.g., the images). This item content can be viewed as side information. The presence of side information, particularly at the decoder or at both ends, can fundamentally change the rate-distortion limits. By ignoring this, the model may be overly restrictive and the derived bounds may not accurately reflect the true limits of practical systems where item features (e.g., difficulty, visual similarity) can be exploited to improve querying and inference.
3.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Results:** The derived limits are asymptotic, assuming an infinitely large number of items (block length n → ∞). While this is standard in classical information theory, its practical relevance to real-world crowdsourcing projects with finite datasets is limited. A non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be significantly more impactful. The authors are encouraged to explore recent advances in finite-blocklength information theory, such as the work by Kostina and Verdu ("Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE T-IT, 2013), which provides tools for a more practical, non-asymptotic analysis.
4.  **Minor Clarity Issue:** In Section 4.2, the text states that the performance bounds for kIC "improve as k and q grow." This phrasing is ambiguous. It would be clearer to state that the lower bound on the required rate *decreases* (or the achievable error rate for a given rate decreases), suggesting that the kIC scheme becomes more efficient with larger k or higher-quality workers.

**Summary**

Overall, this paper introduces a thought-provoking and original perspective on the crowdsourcing problem. The application of joint source-channel coding theory is a promising direction for understanding the fundamental trade-offs involved. However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The theoretical claims need stronger justification, and the model's practical significance is constrained by its simplifying assumptions—most notably, the omission of item content as side information and the reliance on an asymptotic regime.

While the novelty is high, the concerns about technical completeness and the model's realism currently temper its potential impact. The paper represents a strong first step, but it would need to address the weaknesses outlined above to constitute a complete and compelling contribution.
