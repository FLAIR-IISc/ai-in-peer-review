Summary
The paper derives an information-theoretic bound on the budget–fidelity trade-off for label crowdsourcing using rate–distortion theory and the channel-coding theorem. The derivation is careful and technically sound. If this is indeed the first application of rate–distortion and channel-coding principles to crowdsourcing, it could represent a meaningful conceptual contribution.

Strengths
- Clear and well-executed derivation of an information-theoretic lower bound on achievable fidelity given a labeling budget.
- A principled framing that connects crowdsourcing to classical results in information theory, which could help structure future analyses.

Major concerns
- Novelty and practical impact: While the framework is elegant, the derivation appears relatively straightforward given existing information-theoretic tools. More importantly, the resulting bound risks being very loose in practical crowdsourcing settings.
- Limited design freedom vs. channel coding: In classical channel coding, we have substantial freedom to design codebooks and codewords (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, convolutional codes, Reed–Solomon, LDPC), which underpins the tightness of capacity-based arguments. In the crowdsourcing setting, it is unclear that a comparable freedom exists for designing the sequence of channel inputs u^n. For the bound in equation (3) to be tight, almost all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σ m_i) (except degenerate ones) would need to be valid and implementable “codebooks.” This seems doubtful in crowdsourcing due to task and worker constraints, raising the possibility that the channel-capacity-based bound substantially overestimates what is achievable.
- Constraints unique to crowdsourcing: Simple “repetition coding” is possible (with majority voting as a natural decoder), but beyond that, it is unclear how to design u^n to increase error-correction capability. A striking difference from communication channels is that we cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times without likely violating the i.i.d. assumptions used in equations (1) and (2): the same worker will probably produce the same answer to the same query. In contrast, channel models often assume i.i.d. uses of the same channel for repeated symbols. This is just one illustration; more generally, crowdsourcing appears to impose many fundamental constraints on the admissible encoders in equation (3), unlike the relatively unconstrained mapping {0,1}^{nR} -> {0,1}^n in channel coding. These constraints suggest the proposed information-theoretic bound may be quite loose in practice.
- Empirical relevance: The paper would be stronger with an empirical comparison showing how close state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods come to the derived bound. Without such evidence, it is difficult to judge the practical utility of the result.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly characterize the implementability constraints on the mapping in equation (3). What structural restrictions does crowdsourcing (worker models, task allocation, non-adaptivity/adaptivity, no repeated queries to the same worker, etc.) impose on admissible encoders? Which classes of mappings are actually realizable?
- Discuss how these constraints affect the tightness of the bound. If only a restricted family of encoders is implementable, can the analysis be adapted to yield a correspondingly tighter bound?
- Provide a comparison between the bound and the performance of state-of-the-art algorithms on representative datasets or synthetic regimes. Even a gap analysis would help quantify looseness.
- Clarify whether any nontrivial “coding gains” beyond repetition/majority voting are possible under realistic worker and task models, and, if so, how u^n would be designed.

Minor comments
- In equation (8), the minimization should be over C, C′ subject to E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.
- The proof of Theorem 4 could be elaborated for clarity.
- In Theorem 4, since ln(1 − q) < 0, is ε_S larger than ẑε? Please clarify the inequality directions and any thresholds that depend on the sign.
- In equation (17), please justify the appearance of H_M(E(ε)) or provide a citation to prior work that establishes this step.
