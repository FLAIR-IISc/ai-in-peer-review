- Frames crowdsourcing as a transmitter–noisy channel–receiver problem and studies the budget–fidelity trade-off via an information-theoretic joint source–channel coding viewpoint.  
- Derives a scheme-independent lower bound on the number of queries needed to attain a target fidelity using rate–distortion theory.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and compares its performance to the information-theoretic bound for a specific worker model.  
- Strength: novel and interesting application of rate–distortion theory to crowdsourcing, opening new directions for research.  
- Concern: mapping crowdsourcing to a point-to-point communication model may oversimplify practical system complexities.  
- Worker-skill modeling issue: reliability and skill are merged into a single channel characterization and all workers are assumed to receive equal queries—how would known reputations or unequal allocations change the bounds?  
- Channel-model realism: unclear whether the MSC and SHC realistically capture human error and whether the framework can handle adversarial or more complex worker behaviors.  
- Numerical presentation: Figure 2 and the numerical results lack clear labeling and explanation, reducing readability.  
- Clarify k=1 case in kIC: is k=1 equivalent to asking workers for direct labels?  
- Modeling assumption: the paper assumes the source distribution P(B(X)) is known—this important assumption should be stated earlier.  
- Typos: Figure references on page 4 (line 135) appear incorrect (Figure 1a/1b likely swapped).  
- Style: avoid capitalizing generic terms like 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' in running text when not citing numbered items.
