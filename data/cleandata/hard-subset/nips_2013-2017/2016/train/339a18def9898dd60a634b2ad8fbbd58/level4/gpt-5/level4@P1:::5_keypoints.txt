- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives lower bounds on queries per task for a target error probability.  
- Workers modeled as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer SHC(q); coding is non-adaptive and taskmaster lacks skill knowledge.  
- Analyzes two decoder knowledge scenarios (decoder knows skills vs. decoder does not) and provides minimum-rate bounds for both.  
- Provides a necessary condition on minimum rate for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under SHC(q).  
- kIC analysis suggests error-correction benefits from restricting invalid responses and has pricing implications.  
- Strength: clear, natural modeling with interpretable, clean lower bounds and useful guidance for practitioners.  
- Strength: well organized and well written; results appear practically useful for budgeting/pricing.  
- Weakness: no experiments or simulations to assess tightness or practical gap to the theoretical limits.  
- Weakness: Sections 2.4 and 3.1 are overly long or unnecessary; main text lacks proof sketches for key theorems.  
- Quality: technical claims are plausible and assumptions clearly stated, but proofs were not verified by the reviewer.  
- Clarity: generally clear, but would benefit from shorter background and inclusion of brief proof sketches in the main text.  
- Originality: novel and timely — first explicit information-theoretic rate–distortion perspective on crowdsourcing.  
- Significance: results can inform query rates and pricing; kIC bound is especially relevant for structured queries.  
- Suggestion: add simulations or case studies (e.g., AMT binary labeling with majority vote or EM/Dawid–Skene) to illustrate practical gaps.  
- Suggestion: shorten Section 2.4, move/condense Section 3.1 to appendix, and include proof sketches for central theorems.  
- Suggestion: for kIC, discuss how query cost scales with k and translate the necessary condition into realistic pricing.  
- Pros: novel formulation, useful budgeting/pricing guidance, insightful kIC analysis, good writing and organization.  
- Cons: lacks empirical validation, has excess background material, missing main-text proof sketches, and limited discussion of robustness to model assumptions.
