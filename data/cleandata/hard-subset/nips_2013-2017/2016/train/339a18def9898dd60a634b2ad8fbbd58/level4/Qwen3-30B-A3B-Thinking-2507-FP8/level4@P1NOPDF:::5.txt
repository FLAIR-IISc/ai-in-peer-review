

This paper analyzes digital crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, deriving lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per task required to achieve a specified error probability. Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, with symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMCs) capturing typical worker errors and the spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) modeling spammers (who provide correct answers with probability q). The coding scheme is assumed nonadaptive. The analysis considers two distinct scenarios for the taskmaster (who assigns tasks): one where the taskmaster possesses full knowledge of all workers' skill levels, and another where the taskmaster has no prior knowledge of worker skills (thus assigning each worker an identical number of tasks). For the latter case, the paper establishes a necessary condition on the minimum required rate—specifically, for k-ary incidence coding where workers are shown k items and asked to judge breed similarity—under a pool of workers following SHC(q).  

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. However, it lacks experimental validation or simulations demonstrating how existing crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., on Amazon Mechanical Turk) compare to the derived theoretical limits. This omission weakens the practical impact of the results. Additionally, Section 2.4 appears overly detailed and could be condensed. Section 3.1 is largely redundant and could be shortened or removed; the authors would better serve readers by including concise proof sketches for the main theorems instead.  

The paper’s core contribution appears novel. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing problems, as claimed. The results are highly valuable: they provide designers of crowdsourcing systems with a precise lower bound on the query rate needed to achieve a target error probability, which can directly inform task pricing and resource allocation. The absence of empirical comparisons, however, leaves an important gap in demonstrating the practical relevance of these bounds.  

I did not review the appendix and therefore cannot comment on the completeness or rigor of the proofs.
