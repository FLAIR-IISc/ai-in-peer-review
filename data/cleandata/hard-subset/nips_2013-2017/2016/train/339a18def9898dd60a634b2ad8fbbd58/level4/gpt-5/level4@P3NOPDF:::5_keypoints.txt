- Clear, well-organized exposition framing crowdsourcing as parallel noisy channels.
- Novel contribution: first converse-style information-theoretic lower bounds for crowdsourcing with MSC and SHC(q) models.
- Potential impact: bounds can guide design and costing of large-scale annotation by quantifying minimal query rates.
- Modeling choices (MSC and SHC(q)) are interpretable and align with existing noisy-label literature.

- Major weakness: no empirical or simulation evidence comparing practical aggregation schemes to the theoretical lower bounds.
- Main-text proofs largely deferred to the appendix; include brief proof sketches to improve readability and trust.
- Paper organization could be tightened (shorten Sections 2.4 and 3.1) to make room for proofs, intuition, or simulations.
- Restriction to nonadaptive codes may be conservative; discuss extensions or contrasts with adaptive task assignment.
- Equal-task allocation assumption ignores worker heterogeneity and known-worker settings; comment on unequal allocations and partial knowledge.
- Modeling details need early clarification: precise MSC definition, independence assumptions across workers/tasks, and assumptions on label priors/task difficulty.

- Question on tightness: are there achievable schemes that meet the lower bounds (up to constants/log factors) or match upper bounds in special cases?
- Question on parameter knowledge: what exact worker-skill knowledge is assumed and how sensitive are bounds to misspecification?
- Question on estimating q in SHC(q): can the analysis accommodate estimation error or provide robustness bounds?
- Question on k-ary incidence coding: is the necessary condition order-tight and are there constructions close to it?

- Clarity: generally well written; streamline background and add proof intuition for expert readers.
- Suggest adding a concise “takeaways” subsection mapping theoretical results to practical design guidelines.
- Overall: a novel, well-crafted theoretical contribution with practical relevance, but acceptance should depend on including proof sketches or simulations to contextualize bounds.
