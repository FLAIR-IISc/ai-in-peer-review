This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in digital crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding problem. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve a desired fidelity, considering workers as noisy channels with potentially unknown skill levels. They then analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and compare its performance to these bounds. The core idea of framing crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens is compelling and could inspire further research utilizing these tools.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the challenges of crowdsourcing due to worker unreliability and unknown skill levels. It correctly identifies connections to established concepts in information theory, such as rate-distortion theory and joint source-channel coding, providing a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing the problem. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding offers a concrete example of a query scheme and its analysis adds practical value. Theorems 2 and 3 provide useful theoretical benchmarks, and the analysis of k-ary incidence coding (Theorems 4 and Corollary 1) is a significant contribution.

However, the model's simplification of real-world crowdsourcing raises some concerns. The assumption of a direct mapping to a point-to-point communication problem, while facilitating the application of existing theory, might overlook crucial aspects of crowdsourcing dynamics. Specifically, the paper treats worker reliability and skill level as a single channel parameter, and assigns the same number of queries to all workers regardless of their demonstrated expertise. Real-world systems often incorporate worker ratings or reputation scores to prioritize tasks. Furthermore, the channel models considered (MSC and SHC) may not fully capture the complexities of worker behavior, particularly the possibility of adversarial actions or strategic misreporting. The discussion of query pricing, while briefly touched upon, could be expanded. 

Here's a list of arguments pro and con acceptance:

**Pro:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel and insightful approach.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with well-defined bounds.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The k-ary incidence coding scheme and its analysis offer a concrete example and performance evaluation.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework has the potential to inspire new designs and analyses of crowdsourcing systems.

**Con:**

*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The model's simplifying assumptions may limit its realism and applicability to complex crowdsourcing scenarios.
*   **Limited Consideration of Worker Heterogeneity:** The uniform treatment of workers ignores the importance of skill levels and reputation systems.
*   **Channel Model Limitations:** The chosen channel models might not fully capture the nuances of worker behavior.
*   **Clarity of Numerical Results:** The explanation of Figure 2 could be improved.



**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

*   **Address the limitations of the model:** Acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the simplifying assumptions, and suggest potential avenues for future work to address these limitations.
*   **Discuss worker heterogeneity:** Elaborate on how the framework could be extended to incorporate worker skill levels and reputation systems.
*   **Explore more realistic channel models:** Consider alternative channel models that can capture more complex worker behaviors, including adversarial strategies.
*   **Clarify numerical results:** Provide a more detailed explanation of Figure 2, clearly labeling the curves and explaining the significance of the k=1 case. Specifically, explain why k=1 corresponds to directly asking the worker for a label rather than a pair comparison.
*   **State assumptions earlier:** The assumption of knowing P(B(X)) (page 6, line 246) should be stated earlier in the paper, ideally in Section 2.1.
*   **Correct typographical errors:**
    *   Page 4, line 135: Change "Figure 1.a" to "Figure 1(b)".
    *   Page 4, line 135: Change "Figure 1.b" to "Figure 1(c)".
*   **Refine terminology:** Use “theorem”, “lemma”, and “corollary” as descriptive terms instead of formal designations when not referencing a numbered result. For example, change "As discussed in Lemma 1 of [8]" to "As discussed in a lemma presented in [8]".




In summary, this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. While the simplifying assumptions warrant consideration, the novelty of the approach and the rigor of the analysis make it a worthwhile contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
