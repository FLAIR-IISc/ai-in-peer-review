This paper investigates fundamental limits in digital crowdsourcing, modeling it as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyzing it through an information-theoretic lens. The core idea is to establish the ultimate fidelity achievable given a budget (number of queries) by considering query schemes and inference algorithms. This is framed as a joint source-channel coding problem, where workers are modeled as noisy channels. The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off, and then analyze a specific query scheme – k-ary incidence coding – comparing its performance to these bounds.

The work is valuable in drawing parallels between crowdsourcing and well-established concepts in information theory. This perspective may inspire future research employing information-theoretic tools to optimize crowdsourcing systems. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between query cost and achievable fidelity as a central challenge. Their approach of establishing fundamental limits provides a benchmark for evaluating practical crowdsourcing algorithms.

However, the assumptions made to fit the crowdsourcing problem into a standard communication model may limit the applicability of the results to real-world scenarios. The model treats worker reliability and skill level as a single channel characteristic, and assumes uniform query allocation regardless of worker expertise. This simplification, while facilitating the application of information-theoretic results, potentially overlooks important aspects of realistic crowdsourcing platforms where worker ratings and varying skill levels are common. The analysis currently doesn't explicitly account for adversarial workers attempting to deliberately degrade fidelity.

Specifically, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the following points: 

1. **Worker Skill and Reliability:** The current model conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel characteristic. Real-world systems often incorporate worker ratings or reputation scores, allowing for non-uniform query allocation.  Discussing how the model could be extended to accommodate such features would strengthen the analysis.

2. **Adversarial Workers:** The current channel models may not fully capture the behavior of malicious or deliberately unhelpful workers.  Acknowledging this limitation and potentially sketching approaches to address adversarial behavior would be valuable.

3. **Clarity of Numerical Results:** The explanation of the numerical results (Figure 2) is somewhat unclear.  Labels on curves should be improved for better readability. The meaning of the *k*=1 case requires clarification – presenting a pair and asking if they share the same label is not directly analogous to the described k-ary incidence coding. 

4. **Assumption Placement:** The assumption of known *P(B(X))* (page 6, line 246) should be stated earlier in the paper for clarity.

5. **Figure References:**  There are minor errors in figure references (page 4, lines 135-136). Figure 1a should refer to 1b, and vice versa.

6. **Terminology:** The authors frequently use “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without specific numbering. Using these terms more generally, as descriptive statements, would be more appropriate.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information-theoretic concepts to crowdsourcing.
*   Clear articulation of fundamental limits and a useful benchmark for comparison.
*   Well-defined problem statement and a rigorous mathematical analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Simplifying assumptions may limit the realism and applicability of the results.
*   Lack of consideration for adversarial worker behavior.
*   Areas for improvement in the presentation and clarity of the numerical results.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing. While the simplifying assumptions may limit its direct applicability to all real-world scenarios, the insights gained from this information-theoretic perspective are significant.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the discussion of worker skill, adversarial behavior, and clarification of the numerical results.
