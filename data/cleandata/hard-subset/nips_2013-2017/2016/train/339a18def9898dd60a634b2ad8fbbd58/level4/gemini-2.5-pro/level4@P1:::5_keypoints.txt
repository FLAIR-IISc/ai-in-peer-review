- Proposes an information-theoretic framework treating crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem mapping items to source, workers to noisy channels, and query+inference to code.  
- Aims to derive lower bounds on queries per item (rate) needed to achieve target accuracy (fidelity).  
- Analyzes two worker error models: M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) for general errors and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) for spammers mixed with perfect workers.  
- Provides theoretical limits for two scenarios: worker skill levels unknown to the aggregator and perfectly known.  
- Assumes the taskmaster is unaware of worker skills and thus assigns tasks uniformly.  
- Introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) and derives a necessary condition on minimum rate under the SHC model to reach a target error probability.  
- Strength: Novel application of rate-distortion theory to establish fundamental crowdsourcing performance limits.  
- Strength: Theoretical bounds offer practical and theoretical benchmarks for system design, query schemes, and pricing.  
- Strength: Paper is well-organized and clearly explains the communication-system analogy, making the formulation intuitive.  
- Weakness: Lacks empirical validation or simulations to show how practical aggregation methods compare to the theoretical limits.  
- Weakness: Presentation could be improved by shortening background (Section 3.1) and condensing Section 2.4 to include proof sketches in the main text.  
- Weakness: Technical soundness depends on appendix proofs, which the reviewer did not verify in detail.
