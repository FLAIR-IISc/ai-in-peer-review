This paper investigates the fundamental limits of digital crowdsourcing by modeling it as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyzing it through an information-theoretic lens. The authors aim to determine the minimum number of queries needed to achieve a target fidelity, given a budget, and establish lower bounds on performance. They model workers as parallel noisy channels – specifically, symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channels (SHC) – and consider two scenarios: when worker skill levels are known versus unknown to the crowdsourcer. The analysis focuses on non-adaptive coding schemes and culminates in bounds for a *k*-ary incidence coding scheme, where workers are presented with *k* items and asked to identify those belonging to the same class.

This work builds upon prior research in crowdsourcing that focuses on optimized design of aggregation and inference schemes (e.g., Dawid & Skene [5], majority voting [8], and EM-based approaches [9, 12, 10]). However, unlike these works which primarily focus on *algorithms* for inference, this paper takes a fundamentally different approach, establishing *information-theoretic limits* on performance. It also distinguishes itself from work on bandit approaches to worker selection [1] and techniques for identifying low-skill workers [6], by focusing on the achievable performance given imperfect worker skill and a fixed budget. The framing of crowdsourcing as a communication problem, leveraging concepts from source and channel coding, is a novel contribution. Related work in network information theory [7] provides the theoretical foundation for this approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of information theory to analyze crowdsourcing performance is a significant and original contribution. The paper provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper establishes well-defined theoretical bounds on achievable performance, offering a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between budget and fidelity.
*   **Clear Modeling:** The use of parallel noisy channels to represent workers is intuitive and allows for a rigorous analysis.  The consideration of both MSC and SHC models provides flexibility in capturing different worker behaviors.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, making the complex concepts accessible.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results offer valuable insights for designers of crowdsourcing systems, enabling them to better understand the minimum resources required to achieve desired performance levels and inform pricing strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks experimental results or simulations to demonstrate the tightness of the derived bounds and their relevance to real-world crowdsourcing platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk. While theoretical limits are valuable, connecting them to practical performance would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Section Length & Focus:** Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could benefit from streamlining. While providing context, they feel somewhat lengthy and divert attention from the core theoretical contributions. Proof sketches, rather than detailed derivations, could be included in the main body of the paper, relegating complete proofs to the appendix.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the paper touches upon query pricing, it could further explore practical considerations such as the cost of different query types and the impact of worker incentives.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of non-adaptive coding, while simplifying the analysis, may not be realistic in many scenarios. Exploring the potential benefits of adaptive coding could be a valuable direction for future work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to analyzing crowdsourcing performance.
*   The derived information-theoretic bounds provide fundamental insights into the limitations of crowdsourcing systems.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the design and optimization of future crowdsourcing platforms.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation limits the ability to assess the practical relevance of the derived bounds.
*   The paper’s theoretical focus may not appeal to all members of the NIPS community.
*   Some sections could be streamlined to focus more on the core contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. The lack of empirical validation is a significant drawback, but the theoretical rigor and potential for future work outweigh this concern. I believe the paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS 2016, with the caveat that the authors be encouraged to include experimental results in future iterations of this work.



**Rating:** Accept (Weak Accept – Encouraging empirical validation would strengthen the paper considerably)
