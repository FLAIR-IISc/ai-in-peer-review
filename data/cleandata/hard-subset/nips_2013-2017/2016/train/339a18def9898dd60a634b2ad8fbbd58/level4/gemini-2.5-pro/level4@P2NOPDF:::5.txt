This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing digital crowdsourcing. The primary contribution is the derivation of lower bounds on the required number of queries per task (i.e., the rate) to achieve a specified probability of error.

In this model, workers are treated as parallel, independent communication channels. The authors consider two specific channel types: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) to model random worker errors, and a spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) to account for adversarial workers (spammers) who are present with a probability of 1-q. The analysis assumes a non-adaptive query scheme.

The framework is applied to two distinct scenarios concerning the aggregator (the entity making the final decision on a task's label): one where the aggregator has full knowledge of each worker's skill level, and another where it is entirely uninformed about their abilities. A key assumption throughout is that the taskmaster, responsible for task assignment, is unaware of worker skills, leading to a uniform assignment strategy where each worker receives the same number of tasks. Finally, the paper derives a necessary condition on the minimum rate for a specific problem of k-ary incidence coding (e.g., a worker is shown k items and asked if they belong to the same class) under the spammer-hammer worker model.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Its main strength lies in its novelty. To my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors, this is the first work to rigorously analyze the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing from an information-theoretic perspective. This approach is highly valuable, as it provides theoretical benchmarks that can inform the design and even the pricing of practical crowdsourcing projects. By establishing the minimum rate needed to accomplish a task with a given reliability, the framework offers a principled foundation for a field that is often guided by heuristics.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
A significant weakness is the absence of experiments or simulations. The theoretical bounds would be far more impactful if contextualized by comparing them to the performance of existing, practical crowdsourcing algorithms, perhaps using data from a platform like Amazon Mechanical Turk. This would help quantify the gap between the derived theoretical limits and current practice.

On a minor note, the presentation could be improved by shortening Section 2.4 and making Section 3.1 more concise. The space saved could be more effectively used to include proof sketches for the main theorems directly in the body of the paper. This would enhance the paper's self-containedness and make the theoretical contributions more accessible to readers before they consult the appendix.

**Conclusion:**
This is a novel and potentially impactful paper that introduces a new theoretical lens for understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The information-theoretic approach is sound and provides valuable insights. The main limitations are the lack of empirical validation and some areas where the presentation could be streamlined.

Please note that I have not verified the proofs in the appendix, so this review is based on the claims and results presented in the main body of the paper.
