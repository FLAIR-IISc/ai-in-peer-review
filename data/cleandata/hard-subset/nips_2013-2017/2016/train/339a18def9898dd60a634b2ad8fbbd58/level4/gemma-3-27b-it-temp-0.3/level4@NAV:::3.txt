This paper tackles the problem of aggregating responses in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. Despite finding the core insights and modeling approach to be both interesting and potentially valuable for designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques, I admit to struggling with full comprehension of the technical details. 

To enhance impact, I suggest more prominently highlighting the key, high-level takeaways from the results – particularly those presented at the end of section 3 – and including a concise concluding section summarizing the practical implications of this work. While the authors made commendable efforts to improve accessibility for readers less familiar with information theory, the paper remained challenging to follow. I recommend re-expressing the results primarily in the context of the crowdsourcing application itself, moving away from the terminology of channels, codes, and distortion.

The related work section would benefit from acknowledging the growing body of research on robust economic incentives for workers in crowdsourcing, often referred to as “crowdsourcing mechanisms” or “mechanism design for crowdsourcing.” This related work frequently addresses varying worker abilities and, increasingly, integrates incentive structures with advanced aggregation methods. 

Finally, there are a few minor points to address: on line 84, “is reviewed in” should likely be “are reviewed in”; line 106 and earlier explanations were unclear regarding the distinction between the “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” – perhaps clarifying this relationship would be helpful, given the etymology of “crowdsourcing”; on line 122, please include the missing space in “A A(X1,X2)”; and lines 275-276 contain a sentence (“In case…”) that is difficult to parse. Figure 2 would be more informative with a more detailed caption explaining the presented information.
