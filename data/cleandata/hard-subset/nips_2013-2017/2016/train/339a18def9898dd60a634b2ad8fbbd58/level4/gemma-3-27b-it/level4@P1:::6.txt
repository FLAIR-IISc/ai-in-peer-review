This work provides an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff for label crowdsourcing, utilizing rate-distortion theory and channel coding theorem. The information-theoretic bound is well-derived and, if this is the first attempt to apply these tools to the crowdsourcing problem, represents a potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the novelty and practical value of this paper remain questionable, as the derivation of the bound is relatively straightforward and may be loose in practice. The core challenge lies in the differences between the crowdsourcing problem and traditional channel coding. While channel coding offers significant freedom in codebook design (repetition codes, Hamming codes, LDPC codes, etc.), it’s unclear whether similar flexibility exists in designing the sequence of channel inputs *u<sup>n</sup>* in equation (3). In other words, the set of implementable encoding schemes may be highly constrained. Almost all mappings from *B(X)<sup>n</sup>* to *U<sup>∑mi</sup>* (excluding degenerate cases) *should* be valid codebooks; otherwise, the resulting channel capacity may be a loose bound.

Consider, for example, repetition coding, which aligns with the natural decoding method of majority voting. Beyond this, however, it's not clear how we can design *u<sup>n</sup>* to improve error correction. Critically, we cannot re-query the same worker with the same question multiple times, as this violates the independence assumption in equations (1) and (2). The same worker is likely to provide the same answer, while channel coding assumes an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) channel where repeated queries are valid. This is just one example, but, in general, the crowdsourcing problem likely possesses significant fundamental constraints on equation (3) that are absent in standard channel coding, where an encoding mapping from {0,1}<sup>nR</sup> to {0,1}<sup>n</sup> has few inherent restrictions. Consequently, there likely exist important constraints on the set of possible encoding schemes, leading to the potential looseness of the proposed information-theoretic bound. 

Finally, it would strengthen the paper to compare the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods with the derived information-theoretic bound.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel application of information-theoretic tools (rate-distortion theory and channel coding) to the crowdsourcing problem.
*   The bound is well-derived and provides a theoretical limit on performance.
*   Introduces the concept of k-ary incidence coding and analyzes its performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty may be limited, given the relatively straightforward derivation of the bound.
*   The practical value is uncertain, as the bound may be loose due to the inherent constraints of the crowdsourcing problem.
*   Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods.
*   The discussion regarding the limitations and tightness of the bound could be expanded.
