This paper provides an information-theoretic bound on the budget-fidelity tradeoff for label crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem. The information-theoretic bound is well-derived, and if this is the first attempt to apply rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem to the crowdsourcing problem, it can be a reasonably important contribution to the field.

However, the novelty and practical value of this paper are still questionable. The derivation of the information-theoretic bound is relatively straightforward, and the bound has a risk of being very loose. The main problem is that, unlike the channel coding problem, where we have a large amount of freedom in designing a codebook or codewords (e.g., repetition code, Hamming code, convolutional code, RS code, low-density parity-check (LDPC) code), it is unclear whether we have such freedom in designing the sequence of channel input $u^n$. In equation (3), almost all mappings from $\mathcal{B}(X)^n$ to $U^{\sum m_i}$ (except degenerate ones) should be valid or implementable codebooks; otherwise, the channel capacity may be a loose bound.

For crowdsourcing, simple schemes like repetition code are possible, where the natural decoding method is majority voting. However, beyond this, it is unclear how to design $u^n$ to improve error-correcting capability. There is a striking difference between the channel coding problem and the crowdsourcing problem in that we cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times, as this would ruin the iid assumption in equation (1) or (2). In communication channels, we can assume the channel is iid, and the same query can be used multiple times for the same channel. This is just one example, and in general, it is very likely that the crowdsourcing problem has lots of fundamental constraints in equation (3), unlike channel coding, where an encoding mapping $\{0,1\}^{nR} \rightarrow \{0,1\}^n$ has little constraint. Therefore, there should be important constraints on the set of possible encoding schemes in equation (3), and due to such constraints, the proposed information-theoretic bound has a risk of being very loose.

To strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial to compare the performances of state-of-the-art methods with the derived information-theoretic bound. This would provide a better understanding of the bound's tightness and its practical value.

Some minor comments:

* In equation (8), the minimization is over $C$ and $C'$ with $E[d(Z,\hat{Z})] \leq D^t$. It would be helpful to clarify this in the text.
* The proof for Theorem 4 could be more elaborated to improve its readability and understanding.
* In Theorem 4, $\ln(1-q) < 0$. It is unclear whether $\epsilon_S$ is larger than $\hat{\epsilon}$. A clarification on this point would be appreciated.
* In equation (17), the reason why we have $H_M(E(\epsilon))$ should be explained or supported with a citation of previous work.

Overall, while the paper provides a well-derived information-theoretic bound, its novelty and practical value are still uncertain due to the potential looseness of the bound and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
