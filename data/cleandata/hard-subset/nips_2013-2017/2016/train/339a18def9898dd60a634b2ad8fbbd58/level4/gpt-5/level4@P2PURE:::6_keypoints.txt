- Paper derives an information-theoretic budget–fidelity bound for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem; derivation is careful.  
- If first to apply these theories to crowdsourcing, the work could be an important contribution.  
- Novelty and practical value are questionable; derivation seems straightforward and the bound may be very loose.  
- Major concern: unlike channel coding, it is unclear whether one has freedom to design the sequence of channel inputs u^n (i.e., valid/implementable “codebooks”) in crowdsourcing.  
- Equation (3) may implicitly allow mappings that are not implementable in practice, so using channel capacity could give an overly loose bound.  
- Repetition coding (majority voting) is plausible in crowdsourcing, but designing u^n to improve error-correction beyond that is unclear.  
- Critical difference: cannot assign the same query to the same worker repeatedly without breaking the i.i.d. assumptions, unlike repeated uses of an i.i.d. communication channel.  
- More broadly, crowdsourcing imposes substantive constraints on feasible encoding schemes in (3), unlike the lightly constrained encoding maps in channel coding, which may weaken the bound.  
- Paper would be strengthened by empirical comparison of the derived bound to state-of-the-art methods.  
- Minor: in (8) the minimization should be stated over C, C' with E[d(Z, \hat{Z})] <= D^t.  
- Minor: proof of Theorem 4 needs more elaboration.  
- Minor: in Theorem 4, ln(1 - q) < 0 raises the question whether ε_S is larger than \hat{ε}.  
- Minor: in (17), the term H_M(E(ε)) should be justified or cited to prior work.
