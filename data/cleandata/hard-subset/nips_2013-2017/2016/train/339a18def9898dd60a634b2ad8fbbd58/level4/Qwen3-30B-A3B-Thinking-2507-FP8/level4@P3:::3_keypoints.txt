- Presents an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding over noisy worker channels, deriving fundamental budget–fidelity limits.  
- Extends prior work by providing information-theoretic limits (not heuristics), deriving distortion–rate functions for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios.  
- Key theoretical insight: SL-UK performance depends on the average worker quality, whereas SL-CS depends on the weighted average of worker qualities.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme with inherent error-correction and gives a principled query-pricing rule (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2).  
- Theorems 2–3 establish tight bounds; results are rigorously derived and validated numerically.  
- Novelty: first fundamental-limits perspective in crowdsourcing, unifying tasks like labeling and clustering under one information-theoretic framework.  
- Weakness (clarity): heavy channel-coding terminology obscures crowdsourcing relevance; needs CS-centric framing and high-level intuition for key results.  
- Weakness (related work): omits discussion of incentive-compatible crowdsourcing literature and how bounds interact with payment/incentive design.  
- Minor issues: clarify roles of "taskmaster" vs "crowdsourcer"; improve Figure 2 caption (axes and why kIC improves with k); fix grammar and clarify SHC(q) parameter meaning.  
- Pros: significant theoretical contribution, kIC offers a theoretically grounded error-correcting query design, and theorems are technically strong.  
- Cons: dense exposition may limit practical impact; missing incentive-design discussion and insufficient figure interpretation.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions—rewrite key results in CS terms, add a "Practical Implications" subsection with concrete examples, and discuss connections to incentive design.
