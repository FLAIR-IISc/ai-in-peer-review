- Paper casts crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion / joint source-channel coding problem, mapping taskmaster to transmitter, workers to channel, and crowdsourcer to decoder.
- Derives a universal information-theoretic lower bound on the number of queries (budget) needed to achieve a target fidelity.
- Proposes and analyzes a concrete query design, "k-ary incidence coding," and compares its performance to the lower bound for a specific channel model.
- Core contribution (framing crowdsourcing in an information-theoretic framework) is novel and potentially impactful.
- Model unrealistically conflates worker skill and reliability into a single uniform channel parameter.
- Assumes equal query allocation to all workers, ignoring real-world non-uniform allocation based on reputations or skills.
- Channel models do not account for strategic or adversarial workers who may intentionally sabotage results.
- Numerical results are unclear: Figure 2 curve labeling needs improvement and the physical meaning of k=1 is ambiguous.
- Critical assumption that P(B(X)) is known is stated too late and should appear earlier in the problem formulation.
- Typos in figure references (e.g., "Figure 1.a" and "Figure 1.b" appear incorrect) need correction.
- Inconsistent capitalization of theorem-like terms; should use lowercase when not referring to numbered statements.
