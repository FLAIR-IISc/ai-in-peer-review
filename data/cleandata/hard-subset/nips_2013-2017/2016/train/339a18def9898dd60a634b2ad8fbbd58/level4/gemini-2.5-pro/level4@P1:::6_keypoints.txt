- Paper models crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels and uses rate-distortion theory to derive bounds on queries-per-item vs error rate; introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC).  
- Strength: novel, principled application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing with technically sound derivations.  
- Main weakness: practical relevance and tightness of bounds are questionable because the crowdsourcing "code" space is far more constrained than coding in communications.  
- For bounds to be tight the encoding mapping (Eq. 3) must permit a rich set of query schemes; the paper does not show such schemes beyond simple repetition/majority voting.  
- Fundamental issue: the i.i.d. noise assumption fails in crowdsourcing since repeating the same query to the same worker yields correlated (same) answers, unlike repeated channel uses.  
- Because of such constraints, capacity-based bounds may be loose and represent unachievable theoretical ideals for practical crowdsourcing.  
- Recommendation: compare the theoretical bounds against state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithm performance to quantify the gap and calibrate significance.  
- Comment: In Eq. (8) the rate-distortion minimization should be explicitly stated as over all codes (C, C') satisfying E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D_t.  
- Comment: The proof of Theorem 4 is terse and needs more detailed steps for clarity.  
- Question: In Theorem 4, since ln(1-q) < 0, ln(ε̂/ε̄_S) must also be negative (implying ε̄_S > ε̂); clarify whether this is an implicit or necessary assumption.  
- Question: In Eq. (11) the denominator uses H_M(E(ε)) (entropy of average error); please justify using capacity of the average worker instead of average of workers' capacities E[H_M(ε)] as appears in Theorem 3 (Eq. 12), with brief reasoning or citation.
