- Proposes an information-theoretic JSCC framework for crowdsourcing and derives asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item.  
- Models labels as an i.i.d. source, taskmaster as encoder, workers as a channel with random state, and aggregator as decoder.  
- Leverages JSCC coding rates to characterize query complexity and analyzes "k-ary incidence coding" with a lower bound on its error probability.  
- Contribution: a novel, principled, and well-written information-theoretic formulation that clarifies trade-offs in noisy label aggregation.  
- Major concern: relies on source-channel separation theorem for a channel with random state unknown at the encoder/decoder—needs formal proof or literature citation.  
- Major concern: analysis is asymptotic only; lacks finite-blocklength (non-asymptotic) characterization needed for practical crowdsourcing settings.  
- Major concern: model omits item features/side information at encoder/decoder, which in practice can substantially change achievable performance (e.g., Wyner–Ziv effects).  
- Minor comment: wording about Figure 2 ("improve as k and q grow") is ambiguous—clarify that the lower bound decreases and note absence of a matching upper bound.  
- Overall: promising theoretical direction but weakened by an unjustified key assumption, restrictive modeling of side information, and limited practical relevance due to asymptotic focus.
