- Paper models crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies budget–fidelity tradeoffs using rate–distortion and separation.
- Workers are modeled as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channels (SHC) under two scenarios: skill unknown to decoder (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS).
- Theorems derive lower bounds on queries per item to achieve target error; k-ary incidence coding (kIC) is introduced as a practical query type that enforces invalid-pattern redundancy.
- Numerical comparisons show gaps between information-theoretic limits and kIC lower bounds.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual framing: source–channel abstraction, channel models, and SL-UK vs SL-CS scenarios are well presented.
- Explicit, interpretable limits: Theorems 2 and 3 relate minimal rate to source entropy, target error, and worker reliability.
- kIC analysis: Formalizes relative queries and shows how invalid responses provide redundancy; SHC analysis and pricing heuristic are useful starting points.

Main weaknesses / concerns:
- Separation-based bounds may be loose because crowdsourcing imposes encoder constraints (no repeated identical queries to same worker, platform/interface limits, per-worker load fairness, kIC coupling).
- IID and memoryless worker assumptions ignore real human response dependencies and kIC-induced correlations, which could invalidate bounds.
- Some achievability results rely on oracle decoders knowing worker types; practical schemes approaching these limits are not provided.
- Lack of empirical validation comparing standard aggregation methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM) to the theoretical limits.
- kIC cost model is insufficiently explicit; per-query cognitive/time costs and justification of pricing heuristic are missing.

Quality:
- Theoretical development is mostly sound under assumptions, but some proofs (e.g., Theorem 4) are terse and the mapping from communication-theoretic assumptions to crowdsourcing constraints is not fully justified.

Clarity:
- Generally readable but notation and some statements need tightening; several equations require clearer conditions and citations.

Originality:
- Moderately novel: applying rate–distortion/separation to crowdsourcing is interesting and kIC under SHC appears new, though parts follow naturally from the abstraction.

Significance:
- Potentially useful as a conceptual baseline; practical impact depends on formalizing realistic encoder constraints and providing algorithms that approach the limits.

Key suggestions:
- Explicitly define admissible encoders reflecting no repeated identical queries, per-worker caps, and kIC coupling, and revisit converses under these constraints.
- Discuss dependence across channel uses and identify mixing conditions under which bounds still hold.
- Present a concrete, implementable scheme (e.g., randomized kIC with simple aggregation) and evaluate its gap to the limits.
- Empirically compare majority vote, Dawid–Skene, and spectral/EM methods against the derived bounds under synthetic MSC/SHC models.
- State and justify a per-query cost model for kIC in the main text and empirically support the pricing rule.

Minor comments / corrections:
- Eq. (8): clarify minimization over P(Ẑ|Z) and cite appropriate rate–distortion results.
- Theorem 4: check sign/monotonicity issues (ln(1−q) < 0) and expand proof sketch.
- Eq. (12)/(17): justify E[HM(ε)] appearance via channels with receiver-known states and cite standard references.
- Clarify notation (define pmax, consistent use of M vs N, define Hb and HM).
- Fix editorial issues and incomplete references.

Pros (summary):
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing.
- Interpretable SL-UK vs SL-CS limits and insightful comparisons.
- Concrete kIC formulation with SHC analysis.

Cons (summary):
- Bounds may be loose under realistic encoder/dependence constraints.
- Oracle assumptions and limited achievability/empirical validation.
- Clarity issues in proofs, notation, and citations.

Recommendation:
- Borderline reject; would be stronger with clarified encoder models, dependence discussion, and empirical validation.
