This paper presents a novel framework for analyzing crowdsourced data labeling by modeling the problem from a coding theory perspective. Each worker is conceptualized as a noisy channel, and the authors explore two specific models: the symmetric memoryless channel without feedback and the spammer/hammer model.

This formulation transforms the label aggregation task into a coding problem, where the financial budget for crowdsourcing is mapped to the rate of the code used for the analysis. The central investigation of the paper revolves around the inherent tradeoff between distortion (labeling error) and budget. Furthermore, the authors propose and study a concrete coding scheme, termed "k-incidence coding," which involves assigning workers to label subsets of k items at a time.

Overall, I found this to be an interesting paper that introduces a valuable connection between crowdsourcing and information theory. The formulation is compelling, and the analysis of the distortion-budget tradeoff is a key contribution. As this work falls somewhat outside my core area of expertise, I cannot comment in detail on the technical novelty of the coding theory aspects. However, the application and framework appear sound and well-motivated.
