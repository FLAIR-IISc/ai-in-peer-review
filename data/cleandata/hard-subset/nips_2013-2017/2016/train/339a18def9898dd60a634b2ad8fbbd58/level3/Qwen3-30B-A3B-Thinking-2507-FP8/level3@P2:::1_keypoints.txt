- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as noisy channels and frames crowdsourcing as a rate–distortion optimization problem.
- Establishes fundamental budget–fidelity limits with rigorous bounds for unknown and known worker-skill scenarios (Theorems 2 and 3; SL-UK and SL-CS).
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a practical query scheme and analyzes its performance under the spammer–hammer model (Theorem 4).
- Connects theoretical results to practical concerns by analyzing query pricing (Appendix E).
- Provides rigorous proofs and clear exposition; the communication-system model (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the framework.
- Novelty: first application of information-theoretic tools to establish fundamental limits in crowdsourcing; kIC is an innovative practical instantiation.
- Significance: offers benchmarks to evaluate algorithms and guidance for designing more cost-effective, accurate crowdsourcing systems.
- Limitation: discrete channel and iid data assumptions may oversimplify real worker behavior and task correlations.
- Limitation: limited empirical validation beyond theoretical analysis.
- Limitation: practical aspects of implementing kIC (e.g., presenting k items to workers) are not fully addressed.
- Could improve connections to recent NIPS/related work on crowdsourcing (e.g., newer Dawid–Skene improvements).
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution with practical relevance; recommends acceptance.
