This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The authors model the problem of label crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. In this model, the true labels of a set of items are treated as an i.i.d. source. A taskmaster acts as an encoder, designing queries for workers. The workers themselves are modeled as parallel noisy channels, where the channel state (representing a worker's skill level) is random. Finally, the crowdsourcer acts as a decoder, inferring the true labels from the noisy responses. This approach moves beyond the analysis of specific algorithms (like majority voting or Dawid-Skene) to establish ultimate performance bounds on the trade-off between the query budget (rate) and the achievable fidelity (distortion). The main theoretical contributions, Theorems 2 and 3, apply the classic source-channel separation theorem to derive asymptotic lower bounds on the required queries per item for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown to the decoder, and when they are known. The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," providing a lower bound on its error probability for a spammer-hammer worker model.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and elegant formulation of the crowdsourcing problem from a fundamental information-theoretic perspective. Framing crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem is a powerful conceptual leap that allows for the derivation of ultimate performance limits, providing a much-needed benchmark for the field. This approach has the potential to unify various aspects of crowdsourcing system design, from query formulation to response aggregation, under a single theoretical lens. The results are significant because they quantify the best possible performance any system can achieve, irrespective of the specific algorithms used.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The analogy between the components of a crowdsourcing system and a communication system is explained well, making the information-theoretic model intuitive and accessible. The problem statement and the main results are presented precisely.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Technical Justification of Source-Channel Separation:** The paper's central results (Theorems 2 and 3) hinge on the direct application of the source-channel separation theorem. However, the channel model here has a crucial feature: the channel state (worker skill level) is random and, importantly, unknown to the encoder (taskmaster). Standard proofs of the separation theorem assume a channel that is memoryless and whose statistics are known. It is not immediately obvious that separation holds for channels with a random state unknown to the transmitter. While this result may indeed hold (particularly in the SL-CS case where the state is known to the decoder), this is a non-standard extension that requires either a formal proof or, at a minimum, a citation to existing information theory literature that establishes separation under these specific conditions. Without this justification, the technical soundness of the main theorems is in question.

2.  **Modeling Assumptions (Lack of Side Information):** The model assumes that the encoder and decoder operate only on the abstract labels `B(X)`, ignoring the items `X` themselves. In many practical crowdsourcing tasks, the item content provides critical side information. For example, an encoder (taskmaster) could adapt its query strategy based on the intrinsic difficulty of an item (e.g., asking more questions about an ambiguous image). Similarly, a decoder (crowdsourcer) could use features from the items to intelligently weigh or aggregate worker responses. By ignoring this side information, the model may overlook important practical aspects of crowdsourcing and the derived bounds might not be the tightest possible for systems that can leverage this information. The authors should at least acknowledge and discuss this limitation.

3.  **Clarity of Wording in Section 4.2:** The statement in Section 4.2 and Figure 2, that the "lower bounds improve as k and q grow," is potentially misleading. This phrasing suggests that the bounds themselves become analytically tighter. A more accurate description is that the value of the lower bound on the probability of error *decreases*, implying that better performance is theoretically achievable with larger `k` (more complex queries) and `q` (better workers). Since no corresponding upper bound is provided, one cannot claim the bound itself has "improved" in tightness. The authors should rephrase this to avoid ambiguity, for instance, by stating that the performance bound indicates lower error probabilities are achievable.

4.  **Asymptotic Focus:** The analysis is purely asymptotic, relying on the block length `n → ∞`. While this is standard for classical information theory, practical crowdsourcing involves finite datasets. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the nonasymptotic regime. Recent work on finite-blocklength information theory provides tools for deriving converses (impossibility bounds) that are more relevant for practical system sizes. Citing and perhaps briefly discussing the implications of work on finite-blocklength JSCC converses, such as Kostina & Verdú (2013, IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory), would place the results in a more modern context and point toward important future work.

In summary, this paper introduces a highly original and promising framework for studying the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The core idea is elegant and significant. However, the paper's main theoretical claims require stronger technical justification regarding the applicability of the source-channel separation theorem to their specific channel model. Addressing this critical point and discussing the limitations of the model would substantially strengthen the contribution. Despite these concerns, the paper is a valuable first step in an interesting and potentially fruitful research direction.
