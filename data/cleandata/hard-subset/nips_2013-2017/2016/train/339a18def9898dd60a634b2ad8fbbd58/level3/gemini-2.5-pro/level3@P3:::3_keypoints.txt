- Proposes a novel information-theoretic framework mapping crowdsourcing to source-channel coding and derives algorithm-agnostic lower bounds on the budget–fidelity trade-off.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: worker skills unknown to aggregator (SL-UK) and skills known (SL-CS), showing different fundamental limits.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a specific query scheme within the framework.  
- Positions contribution as complementary to prior work that focuses on specific aggregation algorithms (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.).  
- Strength: highly original formulation providing theoretical “gold standard” benchmarks for crowdsourcing systems.  
- Strength: technically rigorous application of joint source-channel coding and rate-distortion theory with solid derivations.  
- Strength: yields valuable insights, notably formalizing why knowing worker skills improves efficiency (average capacity vs capacity of average worker).  
- Weakness: heavy information-theory jargon reduces accessibility for non-expert ML audiences, limiting impact.  
- Weakness: insufficient practical guidance on how bounds translate into real-world design decisions (budget allocation, when to use complex queries).  
- Weakness: literature review omits relevant work on economic incentives and mechanism design tied to budget considerations.  
- Suggestion: add a clear “Conclusion and Practical Takeaways” section translating theorems into intuitive, actionable guidance (e.g., stopping criteria, budget returns).  
- Suggestion: rephrase technical terms into crowdsourcing language (rate → queries/item, distortion → error rate, channel capacity → worker-pool reliability).  
- Suggestion: expand related work to include incentive/mechanism-design literature and discuss implications for economic models.  
- Suggestion: clarify roles/terminology for “crowdsourcer” vs “taskmaster” and use consistent naming.  
- Suggestion: improve Figure 2 caption and/or figure clarity to explain curves, axes, and key conclusions (e.g., kIC performance vs theoretical limit).
