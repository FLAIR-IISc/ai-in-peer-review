- Paper frames crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity (rate–distortion) limits.  
- Worker populations modeled as M-ary symmetric channels and spammer–hammer channels to analyze labeling performance.  
- Contrasts scenarios with unknown worker skills (SL-UK) versus skills estimated at decoder (SL-CS), showing skill knowledge changes achievable budgets.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), provides lower bounds and a pricing heuristic, and compares numerically to the information-theoretic limits.  
- Situates work relative to aggregation and worker-model literature (Dawid–Skene EM, majority vote, spectral/EM hybrids, Karger–Oh–Shah), offering fundamental limits complementary to algorithmic prior work.  
- Theoretical claims are carefully stated with clear theorems and assumptions; bounds align with intuition and known special cases.  
- Empirical illustrations are limited but acceptable for a primarily theoretical contribution.  
- Presentation is difficult for readers without information-theory background; key results often in channel/coding language rather than application terms.  
- Paper is original in applying rate–distortion framing to crowdsourcing and in its treatment of kIC and pricing.  
- Results are significant and potentially actionable for platform designers (budget vs. error, value of skill estimation, query arity/pricing).  
- Suggestion: emphasize high-level intuition and practical takeaways (add a “Design Takeaways” section with rules-of-thumb).  
- Suggestion: restate main theorems in crowdsourcing units (queries per item, fraction of reliable workers, target error).  
- Suggestion: clarify roles of “crowdsourcer” vs “taskmaster” early to avoid confusion.  
- Suggestion: expand related work to include robust incentives/mechanism design and peer-prediction literature (several cited works).  
- Suggestion: improve Figure 2 (annotate axes/regimes, explain the gap, add detailed caption).  
- Suggestion: fix specific editing issues and unclear sentences (line edits flagged).  
- Pros: clear fundamental framing, useful theoretical limits, interesting kIC analysis, potential design guidance.  
- Cons: accessibility problems, limited practitioner-facing exposition, and some presentation/editing issues.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on improving clarity and expanding the related-work discussion.
