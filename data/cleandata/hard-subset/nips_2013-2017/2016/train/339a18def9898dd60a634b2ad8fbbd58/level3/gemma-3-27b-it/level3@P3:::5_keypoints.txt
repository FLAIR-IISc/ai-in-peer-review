- Studies budget–fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework.
- Models workers as parallel, independent noisy channels (MSC and SHC for spammers).
- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem with full/no worker-skill knowledge and non-adaptive coding.
- Derives lower bounds on queries per task required to achieve a target error probability.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a structured query scheme.
- Novel contribution shifting from algorithmic heuristics to fundamental, information-theoretic limits.
- Well-motivated connection to rate–distortion theory and channel capacity applied to crowdsourcing.
- Mathematical derivations appear rigorous; full proofs are in the appendix (not verified by reviewer).
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- Includes discussion of query pricing implications based on derived bounds.
- Major weakness: no experimental validation on real-world data or platforms (e.g., AMT).
- Practical relevance and implementation complexity (especially for k-ary coding) remain untested.
- Sections 2.4 and 3.1 are verbose and should be streamlined.
- Main theorems need high-level proof sketches in the main text to improve accessibility.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance conditional on addressing conciseness, adding proof sketches, and acknowledging lack of empirical evaluation.
- Future work should prioritize experimental validation to assess practical impact of the theoretical limits and coding schemes.
