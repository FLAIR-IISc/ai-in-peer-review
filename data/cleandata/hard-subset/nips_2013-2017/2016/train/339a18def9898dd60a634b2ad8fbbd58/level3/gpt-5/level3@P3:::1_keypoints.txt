- Models crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels and studies budget–fidelity tradeoffs using rate–distortion and capacity concepts.
- Uses two worker channel models: M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel (SHC).
- Reformulates labeling as joint source–channel coding: dataset labels = source, queries = encoder, worker responses = channels, inference = decoder.
- Maps budget to code rate (queries per item) and derives lower bounds on queries needed for target Hamming distortion.
- Treats two settings: SL-UK (unknown skills) and SL-CS (skills known to crowdsourcer), showing how skill knowledge affects effective capacity.
- Proposes k-incidence coding (kIC): query k-item subsets and restrict answers to consistent clusterings to embed error correction.
- Analyzes kIC under SHC and provides a converse-style lower bound plus a heuristic pricing rule accounting for per-query burden.
- Connects to Dawid–Skene, majority voting, bandit-survey allocation, spectral/EM methods, and prior information-theoretic crowdsourcing work.
- Strength: principled information-theoretic formulation linking budget and fidelity with clear SL-UK vs SL-CS insight.
- Strength: general, interpretable lower bounds for MSC that separate source uncertainty and channel terms.
- Strength: kIC is simple, implementable, and analyzed under SHC, illustrating implicit error-correcting structure.
- Strength: offers a unifying lens and useful baselines independent of specific aggregation algorithms.
- Strength: practical discussion of pricing for different k values.
- Weakness: worker models (MSC, SHC) are simplistic and ignore item difficulty, correlations, and adversaries.
- Weakness: SL-CS assumption of perfect skill knowledge is strong; lacks finite-sample skill-estimation analysis.
- Weakness: only non-adaptive codes and asymptotic large-blocklength results; finite-budget regimes are not characterized.
- Weakness: limited empirical validation and no simulations comparing kIC to standard baselines under matched/mismatched models.
- Weakness: pricing rule is heuristic and ignores heterogeneous costs, dropout, and learning effects.
- Quality: theoretical development appears sound and closely tied to rate–distortion/capacity results; some modeling assumptions need clearer scope.
- Clarity: well written and organized; notation could be tightened and a small worked example (kIC k=3, N>2) would help.
- Originality: novel application of rate–distortion/source–channel separation to crowdsourcing and a fresh SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison; kIC framing is original.
- Significance: bounds guide feasibility of accuracy under budget and worker quality; kIC is a practical idea with potential robustness gains.
- Suggestions: add finite-sample bounds/experiments comparing practical decoders to IT limits.
- Suggestions: relax worker models to include item difficulty, structured confusions, or IRT.
- Suggestions: analyze partial skill estimation (SL-EST) to bridge SL-UK and SL-CS.
- Suggestions: explore adaptive codes and extend kIC theory to multi-class N>2 with empirical k tradeoffs and cost measurements.
- Suggestions: clarify feedforward effects in SHC+kIC and scope of the converse; add intuition for key lemmas.
- Pros for acceptance: clean rigorous framework, bridges information theory and crowdsourcing, and proposes a practical coding idea.
- Cons for acceptance: simplified worker models, strong assumptions, limited experiments, non-adaptive focus, and lack of algorithms approaching the bounds.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept—compelling theoretical contribution but needs stronger empirical validation and relaxed modeling assumptions.
