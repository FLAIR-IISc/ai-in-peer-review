

# Review for "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework to model crowdsourcing as a communication problem, where the source is the data to be labeled, workers are noisy channels, and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. The authors derive fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off using rate-distortion theory, considering two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). They introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme and analyze its performance. The work extends prior algorithmic approaches to crowdsourcing (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting) by providing theoretical limits rather than proposing new aggregation algorithms.

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs provided in the appendix. The derivations of fundamental limits using rate-distortion theory are well-executed. The authors carefully distinguish between different worker models (MSC and SHC) and scenarios (SL-UK vs. SL-CS), and they thoughtfully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The information-theoretic framework provides a clean mathematical foundation that complements existing algorithmic approaches. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying their models and their potential limitations in real-world crowdsourcing settings.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized but suffers from poor accessibility for non-information-theory readers. The heavy reliance on channel coding terminology (e.g., "rate-distortion function," "joint source-channel coding") without clear connection to crowdsourcing concepts makes the paper difficult to follow for most NIPS readers. The distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" is confusing and needs clarification. Several sections (e.g., lines 275-276) are hard to parse and require rephrasing. Figure 2 lacks a clear caption and is difficult to interpret without additional explanation. The paper would be significantly improved by restating key results in crowdsourcing terms rather than channel coding language.

**Originality**: This is a novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing aggregation, which distinguishes it from prior work focused on algorithmic approaches. The k-ary incidence coding scheme represents a meaningful contribution to query design. While some information-theoretic work on crowdsourcing exists (e.g., [8]), the paper's focus on fundamental limits rather than specific algorithms is a fresh perspective. However, the paper could better position itself against recent work combining incentives with aggregation (e.g., [1], [6]), which would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance**: The theoretical insights provide valuable benchmarks for evaluating crowdsourcing systems. Understanding the fundamental budget-fidelity trade-off is important for optimizing crowdsourcing projects. However, the paper does not sufficiently articulate how these theoretical limits translate into practical improvements for real-world crowdsourcing systems. The significance would be stronger if the authors provided concrete examples of how these limits could guide the design of specific crowdsourcing applications. The paper would benefit from expanding the discussion of practical takeaways, particularly in the conclusion.

**Specific Suggestions**:
1. Clarify the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" throughout the paper
2. Rewrite key results in crowdsourcing terms (e.g., "minimum number of queries per item" instead of "rate")
3. Add a clear, detailed caption for Figure 2
4. Address the typo at line 122 ("A A(X1,X2)")
5. Expand the discussion of practical implications in the conclusion
6. Include related work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing (e.g., [1], [6])
7. Consider adding a high-level intuition section that explains the main insights without technical details

**Overall Assessment**: This paper presents a theoretically sound and novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing. The fundamental limits derived are valuable for the field, but the paper's accessibility and connection to practical crowdsourcing applications need significant improvement. The theoretical results are promising but require better presentation for the NIPS audience.

**Arguments for acceptance**: Novel theoretical framework, rigorous derivations, valuable fundamental limits, useful k-ary incidence coding scheme.

**Arguments against acceptance**: Poor accessibility for most NIPS readers, insufficient practical relevance, unclear terminology, weak connection between theory and application.

The paper makes an important contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, but would benefit from significant revision to improve clarity and accessibility. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the clarity issues and strengthen the practical relevance.
