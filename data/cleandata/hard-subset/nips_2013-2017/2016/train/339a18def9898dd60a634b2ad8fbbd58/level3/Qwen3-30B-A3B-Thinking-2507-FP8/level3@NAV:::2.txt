

This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem, deriving fundamental asymptotic limits on queries per item. The authors frame crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework, modeling labels as i.i.d. source, taskmaster as encoder, workers as channel uses with random skill-level states, and crowdsourcer as decoder. They apply standard source-channel separation results to establish Theorems 2 and 3, providing asymptotic query-rate limits for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) or perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The paper also presents a lower bound on error probability for "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific channel model.

The paper is well-written and represents a promising first step in applying JSCC to crowdsourcing, addressing an important gap in the literature. It correctly situates its work within the broader context of crowdsourcing research, acknowledging prior approaches like majority voting and Dawid-Skene algorithms.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that must be addressed. The most critical issue is the lack of justification for applying source-channel separation when the channel has random state unknown to the encoder (though possibly known to the decoder). This is a fundamental technical gap that undermines the validity of the main results. The authors should cite and justify this assumption, referencing relevant information theory literature.

Additionally, the modeling ignores original items as side information, which could be crucial for practical crowdsourcing systems and should be considered. The wording in Section 4.2 and Figure 2 is misleading—"lower bounds improve as k and q grow" should clarify that the lower bound value decreases (implying potential performance gain), not that the bound itself tightens without comparison to an upper bound.

The authors should also include nonasymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses, citing Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses, to strengthen the theoretical foundation. While the asymptotic analysis provides valuable insights, practical crowdsourcing systems operate with finite resources, making this extension essential.

Overall, this is a promising but technically incomplete contribution that requires significant revisions to address the fundamental theoretical gap before it can be considered for publication.
