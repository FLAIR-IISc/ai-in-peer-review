- Paper models label-aggregation as joint source–channel coding and uses rate–distortion theory to derive budget–fidelity limits.
- Core mapping: labels = source, worker responses = noisy channels, query/inference = code.
- Main theoretical result: lower bounds on queries per item needed to achieve target average error (distortion).
- Two worker-skill variants: SL-UK (skills unknown) and SL-CS (skills known), with SL-CS giving strictly better limits.
- SL-UK behaves like a single channel with average confusion; SL-CS harvests average of channel capacities.
- Concrete scheme analyzed: k-ary incidence coding (kIC) offering built-in error correction and necessary budget conditions under spammer–hammer model.
- Paper situates results as an information-theoretic envelope for existing aggregation/inference algorithms (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral methods, etc.).
- kIC analysis connects to graph-based labeling/clustering and offers principled query-design reasoning.
- Strength: conceptual clarity—casting crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding yields clean, interpretable limits.
- Strength: generality—results apply regardless of specific aggregation algorithms under stated worker models.
- Strength: concrete instantiation—kIC provides tangible bounds and pricing guidance.
- Strength: potential impact—guides redundancy, trade-offs between query complexity, budget, and accuracy.
- Reviewer requests more high-level intuition and a practitioner-takeaway summary (e.g., H(B) − HN(ε) over per-query info).
- Suggest including simple back-of-the-envelope rules for common binary cases and numerical examples.
- Recommend practitioner guidance for kIC: trade-offs between k, hammer fraction q, target ε, budget R, and pricing.
- Accessibility concern: restate theorems in crowdsourcing language and reduce notation for non–information-theory readers.
- Clarify terminology: distinguish taskmaster (encoder) vs. crowdsourcer (decoder) and state when they coincide.
- Related-work gap: add literature on incentives/peer prediction and works combining incentives with aggregation/task allocation.
- Quality: technical claims seem sound; clarify scope of kIC bounds (non-adaptive querying, model assumptions) and robustness to assumption violations.
- Clarity suggestions: provide a running binary-labeling example, restate Theorems 2–3 in plain language, and include one numerical worked example.
- Specific actionable edits: add a concluding takeaway section, clarify Figure 2 axes/legend, and rephrase ambiguous lines (e.g., 275–276).
- Minor copyedits: fix typos (Line 84, Line 122) and maintain consistent notation for N, M, HN(·), HM(·), pmax, and ε constraints.
- Originality: while related ideas exist, the SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast and kIC necessary conditions are novel and useful.
- Significance: provides theoretical benchmarks for aggregation schemes and motivates query designs with structural error-correction.
- Acceptance pros: elegant, general framework with clear design implications and a concrete analyzable query code.
- Acceptance cons: heavy information-theory formalism limiting accessibility and limited empirical validation or adaptive-query treatment.
