- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem.  
- Models workers as parallel noisy channels and separates query design (source coding) from inference (channel decoding).  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly known skills (SL-CS).  
- Derives distortion-rate functions that give minimum queries per item to achieve a target error probability.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a structured multi-item query scheme with built-in error-correction.  
- Shows via source-channel separation that when skills are unknown capacity corresponds to average worker quality.  
- Shows that when skills are known capacity corresponds to the average quality of the worker pool.  
- Demonstrates kIC can approach the theoretical limits as k increases without requiring worker skill estimation.  
- Theorems 2 and 3 provide strong lower bounds that apply to any query scheme.  
- Numerical results (Figure 2) compare kIC performance with information-theoretic limits, showing close approach for larger k.  
- Paper’s main contribution is establishing fundamental limits as benchmarks, distinct from heuristic aggregation work.  
- Presentation is heavily information-theoretic and may be inaccessible to non-specialists.  
- The roles “taskmaster” and “crowdsourcer” are confusingly distinguished and need clearer definition.  
- Key results are not restated in intuitive crowdsourcing terms (e.g., concrete queries-per-error-rate examples).  
- Missing discussion/citations on incentives and mechanism-design related crowdsourcing work (e.g., Whitehill et al. 2009; Karger et al. 2011).  
- Provides a practical query pricing rule (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) as a tangible takeaway for platform designers.  
- Strengths: novel theoretical framing, rigorous bounds, kIC scheme, and meaningful numerical validation.  
- Weaknesses: poor accessibility, ambiguous terminology, insufficient incentive-literature connection, and Figure 2 lacks interpretability.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions to improve clarity, practical interpretation, and related-work coverage.
