This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing label crowdsourcing. The authors model the problem of querying unreliable workers as a joint source-channel coding task, allowing them to derive fundamental lower bounds on the trade-off between the budget (number of queries) and the fidelity (accuracy) of the final labels. This rate-distortion perspective is a departure from common algorithmic approaches that focus on inference models like Dawid-Skene or EM algorithms. The paper also presents and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), comparing its performance against the derived theoretical limits for a spammer-hammer channel model.

The main strength of this work lies in its originality and potential significance. Framing crowdsourcing in the language of rate-distortion theory is an elegant and interesting approach that provides a new lens through which to understand the problem's fundamental limits. This perspective could serve as a valuable theoretical benchmark and inspire future research on optimal query and aggregation strategies.

However, the paper's contributions are currently undermined by several weaknesses, primarily concerning the realism of the underlying assumptions. A major concern is that the worker model conflates skill and reliability into a single noisy channel. This loses the important distinction between a worker's ability to discern the correct label and their diligence in reporting it. Furthermore, the model assumes the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, which is unrealistic for platforms where workers have established reputations and heterogeneous capabilities. The chosen spammer-hammer and symmetric channel models may also be too simplistic to capture the behavior of strategic or informed malicious workers. The authors should provide a stronger justification for these modeling choices or explicitly discuss their limitations.

The paper also suffers from clarity issues. The numerical results in Figure 2 are difficult to interpret due to unclear labeling of the curves, and the paper should explicitly state what the `k=1` baseline represents. A key methodological assumption—knowledge of the source distribution P(B(X))—is not declared until late in the paper (page 6) and should be stated upfront in the problem formulation. Minor errors, such as incorrect figure references on page 4, should also be corrected. Finally, the use of un-numbered headings like "Theorem" and "Lemma" is unconventional; these should be properly numbered for clarity and readability.
