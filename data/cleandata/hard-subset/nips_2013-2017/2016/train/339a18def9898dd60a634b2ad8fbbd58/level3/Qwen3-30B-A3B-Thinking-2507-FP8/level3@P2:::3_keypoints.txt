- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication problem and derives budget-fidelity limits via rate-distortion theory.  
- Considers two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS), and analyzes worker models (MSC and SHC).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query/query-design scheme and analyzes its performance.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical derivations and proofs for the fundamental limits.  
- Paper is technically sound but would benefit from more discussion of modeling assumptions and real-world limitations.  
- Presentation is dense and inaccessible to non-information-theory readers due to heavy channel-coding terminology.  
- Terminology issues (e.g., distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster") and unclear passages (e.g., lines 275–276) need clarification.  
- Figure 2 lacks a clear caption and is hard to interpret.  
- Original and novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, distinct from algorithmic aggregation work.  
- Needs better positioning against recent work on incentives and mechanism design to strengthen novelty claims.  
- Theoretical limits offer valuable benchmarks but the paper does not sufficiently translate them into practical guidance or examples.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions to improve clarity, accessibility, and practical relevance (includes specific fixes: rewrite results in crowdsourcing terms, fix typo at line 122, add intuition section, expand conclusion).
