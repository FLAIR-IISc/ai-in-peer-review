## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents a compelling initial exploration of crowdsourcing through the lens of joint source-channel coding (JSCC). The authors model the crowdsourcing process – taskmaster, workers, and crowdsourcer – as an encoder, noisy channel, and decoder, respectively, establishing fundamental limits on the number of queries needed to achieve a desired fidelity. This is a novel and promising approach, moving beyond empirical analyses to a more rigorous information-theoretic foundation. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and methodology. The connection to established concepts in coding theory is well-defined, and the derived theorems (2 and 3) provide valuable asymptotic bounds. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a concrete example that demonstrates the practical implications of the theoretical framework.

A key strength of the paper lies in its attempt to apply established information-theoretic results to a complex, human-in-the-loop system. The modeling choices, while simplifying, are reasonable and allow for a tractable analysis. The presentation of the results is clear, and the numerical examples help to illustrate the theoretical findings. However, a significant technical concern arises regarding the justification of source-channel separation. The authors apply standard results assuming separation, but do not adequately address whether this holds when the channel state (worker skill) is random and unknown to the encoder. This is a critical assumption that needs further justification or citation of relevant work addressing this specific scenario. 

Furthermore, the modeling choice to only consider labels as input to the encoder/decoder, ignoring the original items, is a potential limitation. The original items likely contain valuable side information that could improve performance, and excluding them may lead to overly conservative bounds. Exploring the inclusion of item information would strengthen the analysis. 

Finally, the wording in Section 4.2 and Figure 2 is misleading. Stating that the lower bounds “improve as k and q grow” is inaccurate; the *value* of the lower bound decreases, indicating potential performance gains, but the bound itself doesn’t become “tighter” without a corresponding upper bound for comparison. This should be clarified for better understanding. The authors should also consider including non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses, potentially citing Kostina & Verdú (2013) for relevant techniques in JSCC.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of JSCC to crowdsourcing.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Derivation of fundamental limits with theoretical rigor.
*   Potential to significantly impact the design of crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for source-channel separation with random, unknown channel state.
*   Potential limitation of excluding original items from the encoding process.
*   Misleading wording in Section 4.2/Figure 2.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong first step towards a more theoretically grounded understanding of crowdsourcing. While the technical concerns need to be addressed, the paper’s originality, clarity, and potential significance make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
