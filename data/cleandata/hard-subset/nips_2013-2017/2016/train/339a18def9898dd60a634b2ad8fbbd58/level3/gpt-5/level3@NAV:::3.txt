Summary and relation to prior work: The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits (rate–distortion style) under worker populations modeled as M-ary symmetric channels and spammer–hammer channels. It contrasts scenarios where worker skill is unknown (SL-UK) versus estimated at the decoder (SL-CS), showing how knowledge of skills shifts the achievable budget. The work also analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides lower bounds and a pricing heuristic, with numerical comparisons to information-theoretic limits. This framing complements a rich literature on aggregation and worker modeling (Dawid–Skene EM, majority vote, spectral/EM hybrids, and allocation/budget analyses such as Karger–Oh–Shah), providing fundamental limits that prior NIPS work typically approached algorithmically.

Quality: The theoretical claims appear carefully stated with clear theorems and assumptions; the bounds match intuition and align with known special cases. While I cannot fully verify every technical step, the results seem internally consistent and potentially impactful. Empirical illustrations are limited but adequate for a theory paper.

Clarity: Accessibility efforts (motivating examples, kIC narrative) are appreciated, yet the presentation remains difficult for readers without information theory background. Key results are often presented in channel/coding language instead of application terms, and several figures/text passages are hard to parse.

Originality: Framing CS aggregation via rate–distortion and presenting budget–fidelity limits across SL-UK/SL-CS, plus the treatment of kIC and pricing, is novel and differentiates the paper from prior aggregation-centric work.

Significance: The bounds offer actionable guidance for platform designers (e.g., target error vs. budget, benefits of skill estimation, query arity/pricing). I expect researchers to build on these limits and practitioners to use them as design baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Emphasize high-level intuition and practical takeaways (expand end of Section 3; add a short “Design Takeaways” conclusion with concrete rules-of-thumb, e.g., plug-in examples for q, ε, M).
- Restate the main theorems directly in crowdsourcing units (queries per item, fraction of reliable workers, target error) before or alongside channel/capacity forms.
- Clarify the roles “crowdsourcer” vs “taskmaster” early; I found this confusing.
- Expand related work to include robust incentives/mechanism design and combinations with aggregation (e.g., Prelec 2004; Miller–Resnick–Zeckhauser 2005; Dasgupta–Ghosh 2013; Shnayder et al. NIPS 2016; Witkowski–Parkes; Ho–Wei; Shah/Wainwright; peer-prediction and truth serum variants).
- Improve Figure 2: annotate axes, regimes, and explain how to read the gap; add a detailed caption.
- Editing: line 84 “is reviewed in” → “are reviewed in”; line 122 “A A(X1,X2)” → “A(X1,X2)”; lines 275–276 (“In case...”) are hard to parse—please rephrase.

Pros: clear fundamental framing; useful limits; interesting kIC analysis; potential design guidance. Cons: accessibility; limited practitioner-facing exposition; some presentation issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarity and related-work revisions.
