- Novel approach: model crowdsourcing workers as noisy communication channels and reformulate labeling as a coding problem.
- Main contribution: information-theoretic analysis of the fundamental trade-off between budget (queries) and fidelity (accuracy).
- System model: crowd represented as parallel noisy channels; two channel models introduced—M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel (SHC).
- Query scheme: propose k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a query asks workers to identify same-labeled items among k items.
- Theoretical results: derive lower bounds on minimum queries per item to achieve target fidelity for unknown skill levels (SL-UK) and known skill levels (SL-CS).
- kIC analysis: study performance of kIC with an SHC worker pool and derive a lower bound plus numerical comparisons to info-theoretic bounds.
- Practical suggestion: propose a pricing rule of thumb that query price should scale with number of items in the query.
- Strength: novel modeling and coding perspective on crowdsourcing problems.
- Strength: rigorous, thorough information-theoretic derivations of budget–fidelity limits.
- Strength: analytical bounds and kIC performance study provide design insights for coding schemes.
- Weakness: simplistic crowd model that may not capture real-world worker heterogeneity, expertise, and biases.
- Weakness: focus limited to labeling tasks, reducing generality to other crowdsourcing task types.
- Weakness: lack of clear practical evaluation of kIC’s real-world performance.
- Ratings: Originality 8/10, Quality 9/10, Clarity 9/10, Significance 8/10.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—address crowd model limitations, broaden task scope discussion, and provide clearer practical evaluation of kIC.
