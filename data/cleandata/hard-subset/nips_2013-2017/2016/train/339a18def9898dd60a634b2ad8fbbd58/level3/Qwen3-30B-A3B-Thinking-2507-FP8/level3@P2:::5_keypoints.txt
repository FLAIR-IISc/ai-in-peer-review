- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding and derives fundamental limits on query budget for target error rates.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and perfectly known (SL-CS).  
- Derives closed-form minimum-rate (budget) expressions via rate-distortion theory using discrete memoryless channel models (MSC and SHC).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides theoretical performance bounds under the SHC(q) model.  
- Paper is well-motivated and connects clearly to prior crowdsourcing literature (e.g., Dawid–Skene).  
- Originality: novel information-theoretic lens offering fundamental limits rather than heuristic algorithms.  
- Significance: bounds yield actionable design/pricing insights (e.g., π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1k2) and clarify when skill estimation matters.  
- Clarity: well-written with clear definitions, logical flow, and accessible technical exposition for a theory audience.  
- Main weakness: lacks empirical validation or simulations comparing kIC and theoretical bounds to practical methods/real datasets (e.g., AMT, Dawid–Skene).  
- Secondary weakness: Section 3.1 is overly detailed on standard coding theorems and should be condensed.  
- Additional weakness: kIC is not compared to existing coding-inspired or clustering approaches and lacks implementation details.  
- Acceptance summary — Pro: first fundamental limits and useful theoretical contributions; Con: insufficient empirical grounding and comparisons for a ML venue.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong and novel but empirically under-supported; borderline recommendation (weak accept, 6.5/10) with advised addition of experiments.
