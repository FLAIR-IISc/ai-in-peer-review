This paper models crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem. The dataset labels B(X) are treated as an i.i.d. discrete source; the taskmaster’s query scheme is the encoder; workers are parallel discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) with random state reflecting skill; and the crowdsourcer’s inference is the decoder. Two channel families are considered: M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with error parameter ε and the spammer–hammer channel (SHC(q)), where a worker is perfect with probability q and otherwise answers uniformly at random. Using source–channel separation, the authors derive asymptotic lower bounds on the required query rate (queries per item) to achieve a target error under Hamming loss, for two regimes: (i) worker skill unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK); (ii) skill known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The expressions differ in whether the “channel entropy” term averages inside or outside the entropy (HM(E[⋅]) vs E[HM(⋅)]), matching the intuition that CSIR increases effective capacity.

Beyond the general bounds, the paper analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), in which a query presents k items and asks a worker to indicate which belong to the same cluster; invalid partitions are excluded, providing implicit error correction. For binary labels and SHC(q), Lemma 1 gives the spammer’s error probability as a function of k, and Theorem 4 provides a converse on the rate: if R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln ε̄S, no decoder can achieve error ≤ ε. A specialization of the information-theoretic lower bound (Corollary 1) yields an asymptotic benchmark for any coding scheme under SHC with CSIR. The paper also sketches a pricing rule of thumb that scales approximately with k.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself against aggregation methods such as majority vote and Dawid–Skene EM, spectral/graphical models, and bandit-based task assignment. The move to an information-theoretic formulation (Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim) provides fundamental limits rather than specific algorithms. This complements works like Karger–Oh–Shah on budget-optimal allocation and Zhang et al. on provably optimal inference, by abstracting the query/inference mechanism via JSCC.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: casting crowdsourcing as JSCC yields clean, interpretable lower bounds on query budgets versus fidelity, separating skill uncertainty from coding/inference design.
- Clear asymptotic limits: Theorems 2–3 crisply distinguish SL-UK vs SL-CS regimes and connect to channel capacity intuition; the kIC converse (Theorem 4) is tailored to a widely used query primitive.
- Practical design insights: The dependence of the required rate on response alphabet size M and on k and q provides guidance for query design and pricing.

Weaknesses and questions
- Separation with random state: The main bounds rely on source–channel separation for channels with random state unknown to the encoder and possibly known to the decoder. Please justify this rigorously and cite appropriate results (e.g., El Gamal & Kim, Ch. 7–9 on channels with state and JSCC). Specify precisely the CSI model (no CSI, CSIR, or both), ergodicity, and how capacity C is defined in each case; then show that the separation inequality R∗(D) < C indeed yields Theorems 2–3.
- Side information and source–channel coupling: The encoder/decoder are assumed to operate only on B(X), ignoring the raw items X. In many CS tasks, worker reliability depends on item difficulty, and kIC’s effective channel law depends on the specific tuple of items shown. This is a channel-with-state influenced by the source (or a Gelfand–Pinsker/WDP-type coupling), where separation can fail. Please clarify the scope: When item-dependent states or adaptive assignment are allowed, what are the limits? Even a discussion or bounds for the “source-dependent state” scenario would strengthen the paper.
- Finite-blocklength relevance: Real deployments operate with modest L and non-asymptotic budgets. Including finite-blocklength converses/approximations would improve practical value (see Kostina & Verdú, IEEE TIT 2012/2013, on finite-blocklength lossy compression and JSCC converses).
- Clarity around kIC bounds: In Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2, the phrasing “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is potentially misleading. The lower bound on the required rate decreases as k (and q) increase, implying that larger k yields potential efficiency gains. Consider rewording.
- Modeling details: Define M explicitly for each query type (e.g., for kIC, number of valid partitions as a function of k and N). Clarify sampling of items into kIC queries, coverage uniformity, whether R′ is integer, and independence assumptions across queries sharing items. Discuss how violations (e.g., worker fatigue, correlated errors) would affect the bounds.
- Empirical/algorithmic complement: It would help to show the gap between the kIC converse and achievable performance with a standard decoder (e.g., DS/EM or ML with oracle CSIR) under MSC/SHC, and to compare kIC to unary or pairwise queries at equal worker effort.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: The asymptotic bounds and the kIC converse are technically sound under the stated assumptions, but the separation step with random state requires a precise statement and citation. Proof sketches in the main text (with full details in the appendix) would aid readability.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Some notation (HM, HN, M under different schemes) could be summarized in a small glossary or table, and the roles of SL-UK vs SL-CS should be emphasized when presenting corollaries.
- Originality: Framing CS as JSCC with explicit rate–distortion limits and analyzing kIC in this lens is novel and timely.
- Significance: Establishes a principled baseline for what is achievable with given budgets, potentially influencing the design of query schemes and pricing. Including non-asymptotic results or empirical demonstrations would broaden impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a clean, foundational framework for CS with interpretable lower bounds.
- Bridges two communities (crowdsourcing and information theory) and can guide practical design.
- The kIC analysis offers actionable insights on query structure and effort.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on separation with random state is not fully justified; scope of validity is unclear.
- Ignores item-dependent and adaptive phenomena that are central in practice, where separation may break.
- Lacks finite-blocklength results and concrete achievability benchmarks to assess the tightness of the converses.

Suggestions to improve the manuscript
- Add a precise theorem and citations for separation under the stated CSI model(s), and state all assumptions (memoryless, i.i.d. worker selection, ergodicity).
- Discuss extensions to source-dependent channel state and adaptivity; flag where separation is known to fail or remains open.
- Include finite-blocklength converse/approximation references (Kostina & Verdú) and, if possible, a short non-asymptotic bound.
- Clarify M for different query types; rephrase Sec. 4.2 language around “improvement.”
- Provide experiments or simulations showing achievable performance vs the derived limits for kIC and simpler baselines.
- Justify the pricing rule with a behavioral effort model; consider nonlinearity of cognitive load with k.
