Summary
This paper models budget-fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourced labeling through the lens of joint source–channel coding (JSCC). The dataset labels are treated as an i.i.d. discrete source; the taskmaster is an encoder that designs queries; workers are modeled as parallel channel uses with random skill-level “states”; and the crowdsourcer is a decoder. Leveraging standard rate–distortion and channel capacity concepts, the authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits on the minimum queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability.

Two key asymptotic results are presented. Theorem 2 addresses the case where worker states are unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK); the effective channel is the state-averaged M-ary symmetric channel, leading to a query-rate lower bound of the form [H(B(X)) − HN(ε̂)] / [log M − HM(E(ε))]. Theorem 3 handles worker states known at the decoder (SL-CS), yielding the corresponding denominator log M − E[HM(ε)], i.e., the expected capacity with decoder side information. These follow a source–channel separation argument: R(D) must be less than the appropriate channel capacity.

The paper also studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a concrete querying scheme that groups k items per query. Under a spammer–hammer channel (SHC(q)) and an oracle decoder (that knows the worker state), the authors provide a converse-style necessary condition (Theorem 4) on the required query rate to achieve a target error, via a bound that depends on k and q; they also give a lemma for the spammer error probability induced by kIC. A corollary instantiates the information-theoretic limit under SHC(q) with decoder side information. Overall, the work connects information-theoretic limits to practical query design, and relates to NIPS literature on crowdsourcing (Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral/EM methods) by providing fundamental budget limits that are decoder-agnostic.

Quality
- The asymptotic limits in Theorems 2 and 3 are clean and intuitive, correctly reflecting the capacity of (i) a state-averaged MSC (state unknown to decoder) and (ii) a state-known-at-decoder MSC, respectively, combined with the source R(D) via separation. The kIC SHC(q) lower bound is carefully derived for a concrete model and clarifies how grouping affects spammer-induced errors.
- The main technical gap is the lack of a precise justification and citation for applying source–channel separation with random channel state that is unknown to the encoder (and possibly known to the decoder). In particular, the SL-UK case reduces to a DMC with transition probabilities averaged over the state; the SL-CS case to a DMC with decoder side information and capacity C = maxP(x) I(X;Y|S). The authors should explicitly state the independence assumptions (i.i.d. state, independent of the source and input) and cite standard results showing that separation holds in these settings (e.g., Cover & Thomas, Elements of Information Theory, Thms. 7.14.1/7.14.2; El Gamal & Kim, Network Information Theory, chs. on channels with random parameters). If any feedforward/input-dependent state is present (as hinted), separation generally may not hold and the assumptions should be clarified.
- Beyond asymptotics, the paper would be strengthened by nonasymptotic converses and achievability bounds. Please consider citing and leveraging finite-blocklength JSCC converses, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy joint source-channel coding in the finite blocklength regime,” IEEE Trans. Info. Theory, 2013, as well as Polyanskiy–Poor–Verdú for channel coding, to provide practically relevant bounds for finite datasets.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The mapping from CS components to JSCC is clear. Notation for alphabets (N for labels, M for response options) is mostly consistent; please ensure it is uniform throughout and specify log base explicitly.
- Clarification requested for Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2: the sentence “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is misleading. The bound is a necessary condition; what improves with larger k or q is the achievable performance (lower required rate), i.e., the lower bound value decreases. Please revise the wording accordingly and avoid implying any tightening of a bound in the absence of a matching upper bound.

Originality
- Framing crowdsourcing as JSCC with random worker states and deriving explicit asymptotic query-rate limits via separation is a novel and insightful contribution relative to prior NIPS work focused on inference/aggregation algorithms (Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM, budget-optimal allocation). The kIC analysis under SHC(q) provides a fresh converse under a widely used worker model.

Significance
- The results offer principled targets for any algorithm designer: a benchmark on the minimum budget to achieve a desired fidelity. The kIC analysis yields design intuition on how increasing k combats spammers. With the suggested additions (state/separation justification and finite-blocklength bounds), the impact for both theoreticians and practitioners would be substantial.

Major comments
- Justify source–channel separation with random channel state: state precise assumptions (i.i.d. state, independence from source/input), and cite appropriate theorems (Cover–Thomas; El Gamal–Kim; Gallager). Clarify that SL-UK reduces to the averaged DMC and SL-CS to capacity with decoder side information C = maxP(x) I(X;Y|S).
- Modeling concern: The encoder/decoder observe only B(X) and ignore X as side information. In practice, item features and difficulty can aid both query design and decoding (e.g., adaptive or item-aware coding, correlation between item difficulty and worker accuracy). Please discuss this limitation and potential extensions (e.g., side information at encoder/decoder; Wyner–Ziv/Heegard–Berger settings; input-dependent state where separation may fail).
- Add finite-blocklength treatment: include nonasymptotic converses/achievability (Kostina & Verdú, and Polyanskiy–Poor–Verdú) to quantify penalties at realistic dataset sizes.

Minor comments
- Be explicit about independence assumptions between the source labels and worker states; if violated (e.g., hard items reduce accuracy), the capacity expressions and separation need revision.
- In Sec. 4.2, clearly distinguish information-theoretic limits (applying to any code) from kIC-specific lower bounds with an oracle decoder; avoid overinterpreting “improvement” without matching achievability.
- The pricing rule-of-thumb could be caveated as model-dependent (SHC(q), uniform labels).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean JSCC formulation of crowdsourcing with random worker states.
- Clear asymptotic limits that align with known capacities; useful benchmarks.
- Concrete analysis of kIC under SHC(q) providing actionable design insights.
- Generally well written and positioned relative to prior NIPS work.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing rigorous justification/citations for separation with random state; assumptions not fully explicit.
- Asymptotic focus; lacks finite-blocklength bounds and empirical validation.
- Modeling omits item-side information and potential source–state coupling.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on adding the separation-with-state justification, clarifying assumptions and wording in Sec. 4.2, and, if possible, including finite-blocklength bounds or at least discussing them with proper citations (Kostina & Verdú, 2013).
