- Presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling crowdsourcing and budget–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Models crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation with a taskmaster dividing projects into batches assigned to imperfect workers.  
- Framework is practically useful for system designers and pricing by quantifying budget vs. fidelity trade-offs.  
- Provides lower bounds on the number of queries per task needed to reach a target error probability.  
- Models workers as parallel independent channels, using symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel SHC(q).  
- Analyzes two knowledge regimes for the crowdsourcer: full knowledge of worker skills and no knowledge.  
- Derives a necessary condition on minimum rate for k-ary incidence coding with SHC(q) worker pools.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.  
- Technically sound with thorough, well-motivated arguments and contributions.  
- Original approach with significant potential impact on crowdsourcing research and practice.  
- Lacks experiments or simulations comparing theoretical limits to practical schemes (e.g., AMT).  
- Does not include proof sketches in the main text; proofs are only in the appendix.  
- Recommends shortening or removing background sections (Section 2.4 and Section 3.1) to improve conciseness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, practical usefulness, clear exposition, and strong analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing empirical validation, absent in-text proof sketches, and some overly long background sections.
