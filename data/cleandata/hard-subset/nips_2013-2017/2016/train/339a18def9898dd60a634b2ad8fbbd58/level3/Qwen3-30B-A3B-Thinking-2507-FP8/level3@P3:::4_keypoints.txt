- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem and applies rate-distortion theory to derive budget-fidelity limits.  
- Novel theoretical contribution: lower bounds on queries needed for target fidelity using information-theoretic foundations.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query design with error-correction capabilities and analyzes its performance.  
- Strong connection to information theory and complements prior EM/aggregation-focused crowdsourcing work.  
- Theoretical analysis is sound within the paper’s assumptions and offers a principled mathematical framework.  
- Main model flaw: merges worker skill and reporting noise into a single parameter, obscuring important practical distinctions.  
- Unrealistic assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, ignoring worker heterogeneity.  
- Worker unreliability models fail to capture adversarial or malicious behavior beyond spammer-hammer and random errors.  
- Key assumption of knowing P(B(X)) is stated late and should be declared earlier, as it materially affects results.  
- Numerical results are unclear: k=1 not explicitly defined and curves/labels do not clearly separate bounds from kIC performance.  
- Presentation issues: incorrect figure references (Figure 1.a→1.b, 1.b→1.c) and inconsistent/missing theorem/lemma/corollary numbering.  
- Practical significance is limited by modeling simplifications despite the compelling theoretical framework.  
- Originality is strong in framing; kIC is a reasonable but not highly novel extension of coding ideas.  
- Recommendation: justify and refine worker/channel models, clarify assumptions up front, and improve figures and writing for clarity.  
- Overall: promising theoretical work that needs better model realism and presentation improvements before strong acceptance.
