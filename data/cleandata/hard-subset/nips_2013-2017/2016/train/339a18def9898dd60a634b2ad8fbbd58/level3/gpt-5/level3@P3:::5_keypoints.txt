- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and uses rate–distortion to quantify budget–fidelity tradeoffs.  
- Models workers as parallel independent discrete memoryless channels (MSC and SHC) and analyzes SL-UK vs SL-CS regimes with lower bounds on queries per item.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a natural code family with built-in error-correction and analyzes its rate behavior for SHC and binary labels.  
- Novel viewpoint: applying information-theoretic (capacity/rate–distortion) tools to crowdsourcing is original and insightful.  
- Clear modeling: distinctions between MSC/SHC and SL-UK vs SL-CS are well articulated and useful.  
- Concrete code family: kIC is practically relevant (pairwise/ternary/group queries) and its budget scaling is quantified.  
- Practical relevance: provides guidance for budgeting/pricing (kIC cost roughly scales with k) and highlights limits of nonadaptive designs.  
- Writing and organization: generally well written and organized.  
- Main weakness: no experiments or simulations to compare practical algorithms (majority, DS/EM, spectral) to theoretical bounds.  
- Scope limitation: analysis restricted to nonadaptive coding and equal load across workers, excluding adaptive assignment benefits.  
- Overlong background: Sections 3.1 and 2.4 contain standard material that displaces space for proofs, intuition, and experiments.  
- Modeling simplifications: assumes worker independence and memorylessness, ignoring correlated task difficulty and learning/fatigue effects.  
- kIC limitation: necessary condition derived only for N=2 and oracle decoding; extensions to general N and realistic decoders are not explored.  
- Theoretical claims are sound and derivations coherent, but absence of empirical validation limits assessment of tightness.  
- Clarity suggestions: shorten textbook background, add proof sketches, and provide more intuition on priors (pmax) and alphabet size M.  
- Specific suggestions: add simulations comparing practical methods to SL-UK/SL-CS bounds; discuss adaptive assignment implications; formalize kIC cost model and validate pricing rule-of-thumb.  
- Recommend broadening kIC discussion to N>2 and examining robustness to nonuniform priors and heterogeneous response options.  
- Pros for acceptance: conceptually novel, yields clear converse-style bounds, and offers practical budget/pricing insights likely to spur follow-up work.  
- Cons for acceptance: no empirical validation, narrow nonadaptive focus, and some space wasted on standard background.  
- Open questions for authors: How tight are the SL-UK/SL-CS bounds for moderate L under simple decoders? Can kIC results extend to N>2 or non-oracle decoders? How sensitive are results to correlated task difficulty or worker dependence?  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept contingent on adding a minimal experimental section for practical validation.  
- Reviewer confidence: Moderate (did not verify appendix proofs line-by-line).
