This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity tradeoffs in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework. The authors model the crowdsourcing process as a communication system with workers represented as parallel noisy channels and establish lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve a target error probability. The work considers both scenarios where worker skill levels are known and unknown to the crowdsourcer, analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a specific query scheme, and briefly touches upon optimized query pricing.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and places it within the context of human-in-the-loop computation and existing crowdsourcing approaches, referencing relevant work such as Dawid and Skene’s algorithm [5], bandit survey problems [1], and work on clustering with convex optimization [11]. The modeling in Section 2 is thorough, providing a solid foundation for the subsequent analysis. The presentation of the information-theoretic bounds in Section 3 builds logically upon the model and is generally accessible.  The exploration of k-ary incidence coding offers a concrete example and an initial investigation into practical query scheme design.

A key strength of this work lies in its novel approach to a challenging problem. While existing crowdsourcing research often focuses on algorithmic improvements for aggregation and inference, this paper steps back to examine the ultimate theoretical limits of performance. This provides valuable insights for designers of crowdsourcing systems and potentially informs pricing strategies, as highlighted in the paper. The distinction between the known and unknown worker skill level scenarios is also a valuable contribution, offering a nuanced understanding of the problem.  The analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a good example of applying the theoretical framework to a specific scheme.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of empirical validation. The theoretical bounds are interesting, but their practical relevance is difficult to assess without comparison to real-world crowdsourcing platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). Experiments or simulations, even simplified ones, demonstrating how the theoretical limits relate to performance on AMT or similar platforms would significantly strengthen the paper.  

Section 2.4 feels somewhat lengthy and could benefit from streamlining. The discussion of distortion and the design problem, while technically sound, could be more concise. Similarly, Section 3.1, providing a background on joint source-channel coding, feels somewhat extensive for a NIPS audience likely familiar with these concepts. While helpful for completeness, shortening this section would improve the paper's flow. The proofs of the theorems, while presumably correct (I did not verify them), are relegated to appendices, which is standard, but including short proof sketches within the main text would enhance understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The information-theoretic approach to analyzing crowdsourcing performance is original and provides a valuable perspective.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical results and establishes fundamental limits on achievable performance.
*   **Potential Impact:** The findings could be beneficial for designing more efficient crowdsourcing systems and developing optimal pricing strategies.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experiments or simulations limits the assessment of the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   **Section Length:** Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be shortened without losing critical information.
*   **Proof Detail:**  Including proof sketches would improve readability and accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the lack of empirical validation is a notable weakness, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and potential impact of the work are significant. The paper addresses an important problem with a rigorous and insightful approach.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Consider adding some experimental results or simulations to demonstrate the connection between the theoretical limits and practical crowdsourcing systems.
2.  Shorten Sections 2.4 and 3.1 for improved conciseness.
3.  Include brief proof sketches for the key theorems within the main text.
