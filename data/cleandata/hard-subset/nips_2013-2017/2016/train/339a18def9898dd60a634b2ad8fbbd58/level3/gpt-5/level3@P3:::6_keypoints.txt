- Paper frames crowdsourcing labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives rate–distortion and capacity-based budget–fidelity bounds for unknown vs known worker skills.  
- Workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels (MSC/SHC); central results (Theorems 2–3) give minimum queries per item for target Hamming distortion in SL-UK and SL-CS.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides an oracle-decoder lower bound for SHC (Theorem 4) plus a simple pricing heuristic.  
- Conceptual contribution: connects classical information-theoretic separation results to crowdsourcing budget–fidelity tradeoffs.  

Strengths
- Clear, technically sound derivation of separation-based bounds with interpretable formulas depending on label entropy, noise, and alphabet size.  
- Clean comparison of SL-UK vs SL-CS highlighting the value of knowing worker reliabilities.  
- kIC is a natural query class; SHC analysis and oracle converse give intuition on redundancy (k) vs hammer fraction (q) tradeoffs.  
- Well written and grounded in standard information-theoretic tools.  

Main concerns / weaknesses
- Implementability: converses assume arbitrary encoders C: B(X)^n→U^Σ m_i, but microtasks must be human-comprehensible local queries, so encoder class is unrealistically rich.  
- Reuse and iid assumptions: repeated iid channel uses may fail in practice due to worker learning/fatigue/dependence, and constraints on reuse aren’t captured in the converses.  
- Codebook feasibility: unclear which error-correcting-like mappings are realizable as microtasks beyond repetition codes, so capacity-style bounds may overestimate achievable performance.  
- Novelty: derivations follow standard JSCC/separation arguments; practical novelty depends on linking bounds to realistic crowdsourcing constraints.  
- No empirical/algorithmic comparison to practical methods (DS/EM, spectral-EM, adaptive allocation), making tightness and practical value unclear.  

Quality
- Technical correctness: converses and kIC/SHC oracle bound are derived soundly.  
- Completeness: missing constrained-encoder converses and empirical validation leaves a gap to practice.  

Clarity
- Generally clear with helpful background; some proofs (e.g., Theorem 4) are terse and notation/optimization domains need clarification.  

Originality
- Moderate: neat framing of rate–distortion/channel coding for crowdsourcing, but tools and proofs are standard and lack crowdsourcing-specific tight converses.  

Significance
- Conceptually useful baseline; practical impact limited without converses under implementability constraints and empirical comparisons.  

Suggestions for improvement
- Derive constrained-encoder converses for realistic microtask classes (per-item labels, pairwise/triplet queries, limited incidence, bounded reuse).  
- Model worker reuse/dependence (channels with memory/state) or justify iid assumptions with platform evidence.  
- Provide algorithmic/empirical comparisons simulating realistic worker pools and comparing DS/EM/spectral methods and kIC decoders to bounds.  
- Give achievability sketches or explicit constructions within feasible query classes or clarify infeasibility.  

Questions for authors
- Which subclasses of mappings C in Eq. (3) are implementable on standard platforms and can you formalize them?  
- Can you design u^n beyond repetition while keeping queries human-interpretable and per-worker load reasonable?  
- How sensitive are the bounds to mild dependence across repeated queries to the same worker?  
- In SL-CS, how are skill estimates obtained and how do estimation errors affect the bounds?  

Pros
- Sound information-theoretic framework, clear SL-UK vs SL-CS insight, and useful kIC analysis/pricing heuristic.  

Cons
- Bounds likely loose due to unrealistic encoder freedom and iid assumptions; limited novelty and no empirical benchmarking.  

Minor/comments to fix
- Eq. (8): clarify minimization domain over source codes or conditional distributions satisfying distortion constraint.  
- Theorem 4: expand proof steps relating R to hammer counts, union bound details, and even/odd k handling via Lemma 1.  
- Theorem 4 signs/parameters: clarify inequality directions given ln(1−q)<0 to avoid confusion.  
- Eq. (17): explain HM(E(ε)) or cite prior work on mixtures of symmetric channels and define E(ε).  
- Units/notation: ensure log base 2, define all symbols at first use, and clarify R as queries per item.  
- Related work: discuss links to task-allocation/budget-optimal results and practical aggregation algorithms.
