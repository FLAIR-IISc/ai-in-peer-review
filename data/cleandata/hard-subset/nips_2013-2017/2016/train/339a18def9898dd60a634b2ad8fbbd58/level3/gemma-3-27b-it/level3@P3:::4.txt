This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, framing the problem within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework. This is a compelling approach, connecting crowdsourcing to well-established information theory concepts and offering potentially inspiring avenues for future work. The authors derive lower bounds on achievable fidelity given a budget and analyze a specific query scheme – k-ary incidence coding – in relation to these bounds. While the overall idea is strong, several concerns regarding model assumptions, clarity of presentation, and methodological details need addressing.

The core strength of the paper lies in its theoretical framing. Applying rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing is a novel and insightful perspective, providing a rigorous basis for understanding the inherent limitations of the system. The identification of fundamental limits, even under simplifying assumptions, is a valuable contribution. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding offers a concrete example and allows for comparison with the theoretical bounds, though the practical implications of this specific scheme require further scrutiny.

However, a major weakness is the simplification of worker characteristics. The model merges worker skill and reliability into a single channel, obscuring the distinction between inherent ability (potentially quantifiable through a rating system) and reporting noise. A worker might be highly skilled but occasionally make errors due to momentary lapses in concentration, or a worker might consistently provide low-quality responses due to lack of expertise. Treating these as the same phenomenon limits the model’s ability to capture the nuances of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.  

Furthermore, the assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers is unrealistic. In practice, taskmasters leverage worker ratings and demonstrated capabilities to intelligently assign tasks, directing more queries to reliable workers and fewer to those with lower confidence scores.  Ignoring this heterogeneity undermines the practical relevance of the derived bounds and the proposed schemes.

The chosen channel models for worker unreliability also raise concerns. While the MSC and SHC models provide a starting point, they may not adequately capture the behavior of adversarial or informed malicious workers who intentionally provide incorrect responses. In real-world scenarios, spam and strategically misleading contributions are potential issues that need consideration. The authors should provide a more thorough justification for the realism and limitations of the adopted channel models and discuss how the results might change with more sophisticated models.

Several issues impact clarity and rigor. The assumption that P(B(X)) is known is stated late in the paper (page 6, line 246) and should be explicitly declared much earlier, preferably in Section 2. This knowledge significantly impacts the applicability of the derived bounds and should be clearly stated from the outset.  The numerical results and explanations in Figure 2 are unclear. The curves require improved labeling, and the meaning of k=1 needs to be explicitly stated.  Finally, the authors misuse the terms 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' without numbering. Using lowercase or adding numbers (e.g., Theorem 1, Lemma 2) would greatly enhance readability and avoid ambiguity.

Minor corrections are also needed: The figure references on page 4 are incorrect ("Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c").



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, providing a rigorous theoretical framework.
*   Derivation of fundamental limits on budget-fidelity trade-offs.
*   Insightful analysis of k-ary incidence coding.
*   Potential to inspire future research in more sophisticated models and algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Oversimplification of worker characteristics by conflating skill and reliability.
*   Unrealistic assumption of uniform query allocation.
*   Limited realism of channel models for worker unreliability.
*   Lack of clarity in presentation and numerical results.
*   Methodological issues related to the late declaration of P(B(X)).
*   Inconsistent use of theorem/lemma/corollary terminology.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful contribution. However, the significant limitations in model assumptions and clarity of presentation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined above. Specifically, the authors should consider more realistic worker models, explore adaptive task allocation strategies, and clarify the presentation of their results. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its value to the NIPS community.
