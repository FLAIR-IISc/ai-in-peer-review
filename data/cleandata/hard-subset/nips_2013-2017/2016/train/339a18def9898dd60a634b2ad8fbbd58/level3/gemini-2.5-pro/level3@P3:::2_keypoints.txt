- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem, modeling true labels as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as encoder, workers as parallel noisy channels with random skill states, and the crowdsourcer as decoder.
- Main theoretical contributions (Theorems 2 & 3) use source-channel separation to derive asymptotic lower bounds on queries-per-item for cases when worker skill is unknown or known to the decoder.
- The paper also introduces "k-ary incidence coding" and provides a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer-hammer worker model.
- Strength — originality/significance: novel, elegant information-theoretic formulation that yields fundamental performance limits and a unifying benchmark for crowdsourcing systems.
- Strength — clarity: generally well-written, with an intuitive analogy between crowdsourcing components and communication-system components.
- Weakness — separation justification: applying source-channel separation with a random channel state unknown to the encoder is nonstandard and needs a formal proof or citations to validate the central theorems.
- Weakness — modeling omission: the model ignores item-side information (works only on B(X)), whereas practical systems can use item content to adapt queries and aggregate responses, possibly tightening bounds.
- Weakness — wording in Sec. 4.2: saying "lower bounds improve as k and q grow" is misleading; the authors should state that achievable error decreases rather than claiming improved bound tightness.
- Weakness — asymptotic focus: results are purely asymptotic (n → ∞); the paper should discuss finite-blocklength implications and cite nonasymptotic JSCC converse work (e.g., Kostina & Verdú).
