Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem and derives asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a target fidelity (average error probability) under two worker-state scenarios. The data labels B(X) are modeled as an i.i.d. finite-alphabet source; the taskmaster acts as an encoder that maps items to queries; workers are parallel noisy channels with random skill-level states; and the crowdsourcer is a decoder that reconstructs labels. Using source–channel separation, the paper provides lower bounds on the minimal query rate (Theorems 2 and 3) under an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) worker model when (i) worker skill levels are unknown to the decoder (SL-UK) and (ii) perfectly known to the decoder (SL-CS). The bounds match intuitive capacities: for SL-UK, the effective channel is averaged by E[ε]; for SL-CS, the capacity is the expectation of per-state capacities. The paper also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC)—a query that presents k items and asks for their incidence (same/different label)—and analyzes its error-correcting effect by eliminating invalid answer patterns. For a spammer–hammer channel (SHC), the authors derive a lower bound on the achievable error with kIC assuming an oracle decoder, and compare it to an information-theoretic lower bound derived from Theorem 3. They briefly discuss implications for query pricing.

The approach is distinct from aggregation/inference algorithms such as DS-EM and spectral/graphical models [5, 8–13], and complements budget–accuracy analyses (e.g., [6]) by framing crowdsourcing limits via rate–distortion and capacity.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main limits are obtained by applying the JSCC separation theorem to a mixture of MSCs (SL-UK) and to channels with decoder side information (SL-CS). This is plausible, but the paper should explicitly justify separation under channel state: specify the stationarity/ergodicity assumptions and cite standard results for DMCs with i.i.d. state unknown to the encoder and known/unknown to the decoder (e.g., Cover–Thomas, ch. 7; El Gamal–Kim). Clarify that your non-adaptive setting with no input-dependent state preserves separation.
- The kIC analysis under SHC is carefully derived for binary, uniform labels, though it relies on an oracle decoder (thus a converse-style lower bound). It would help to quantify the gap to the information-theoretic limit and discuss tightness.
- Assumptions: i.i.d. labels, random assignment, non-adaptive queries, and workers’ errors independent of item content are strong. The paper acknowledges potential feedforward (item difficulty) but defers it. Given the prevalence of instance-dependent difficulty and the availability of the raw items X as side information to both encoder and decoder, discussing how these break/modify separation would strengthen the contribution.
- Missing nonasymptotic analysis: Providing finite-blocklength converses/approximations (e.g., Kostina & Verdú, 2013, for lossy JSCC) would make the limits more actionable.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The communication analogy in Sec. 2 is clear, and the definitions of SL-UK/SL-CS are helpful.
- Please standardize notation: N (#classes) vs M (response alphabet size); in labeling tasks these often coincide—make the mapping explicit.
- Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2: The statement “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is potentially misleading. What improves is the achievable performance region; the plotted necessary bound on rate decreases with k and q. Consider rephrasing accordingly and, if possible, include upper bounds or actual kIC performance to illustrate tightness.
- Minor: fix typos and incomplete references (years/publishers), e.g., “Wiely,” missing years/issues; ensure consistency with NIPS bibliographic style.

Originality
- Framing crowdsourcing as JSCC with explicit rate–distortion formulas under MSC/SHC models is, to my knowledge, new in the NIPS literature and complements prior algorithmic/graphical approaches. The kIC perspective (invalid-response-induced redundancy) is a neat coding-theoretic angle that connects to pairwise constraints in clustering [11].
- The technical ingredients (separation, MSC capacity) are classical; the novelty lies in the modeling instantiation and the kIC analysis.

Significance
- Asymptotic limits provide valuable baselines for system design and can guide budget allocation and query design choices (e.g., when to prefer higher-arity incidence queries). The SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison clarifies the value of estimating worker reliability.
- Practical impact depends on extending the framework to non-i.i.d. sources, instance-dependent difficulty, partial side information, and finite budgets. The pricing heuristic for kIC is intriguing but would benefit from validation.

Specific suggestions and questions
- Justify source–channel separation with channel state: clearly state assumptions (i.i.d. state independent of inputs, no feedback, stationarity/ergodicity) and cite a precise theorem (e.g., Cover–Thomas, Thm. 7.7.1; El Gamal–Kim, ch. 7). For SL-CS, point to capacity with decoder side information C = max_{P(U)} I(U;V|S).
- Discuss side information X at encoder/decoder and instance-dependent state (difficulty). What changes if the channel state depends on X (breaking the memoryless assumption)? Even a brief remark or a bound under mismatched/compound channels would help scope the results.
- Provide finite-blocklength insights (normal approximation or converses) for JSCC (Kostina & Verdú, 2013), and comment on expected gaps at practical dataset sizes.
- For kIC, show empirical or analytical upper bounds (achievability) under simple decoders to gauge tightness of Theorem 4, and extend beyond N=2 if possible.
- Clarify the pricing rule-of-thumb π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 (typesetting currently ambiguous) and provide sensitivity to q and target error.
- Add experiments or simulations that compare kIC against pairwise (2IC) with DS/spectral inference under SHC/MSC to demonstrate practical gains.

Pros
- Clear JSCC formulation of crowdsourcing; informative SL-UK vs SL-CS limits.
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding with an intuitive error-correction effect.
- Potentially impactful guidance on budget vs fidelity and query design/pricing.

Cons
- Reliance on asymptotic separation with limited discussion of state and side-information assumptions.
- Oracle-based kIC bound without corresponding achievability results; tightness unclear.
- Strong i.i.d./non-adaptive assumptions limit immediate applicability.
- Some clarity/notation/reference issues.

Overall: A well-written and promising first step toward information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing. With stronger justification of separation under state, clearer treatment of assumptions, and some finite-blocklength/achievability evidence for kIC, this could be a solid contribution.
