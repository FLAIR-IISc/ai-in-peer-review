- Paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling crowdsourcing and deriving lower bounds on queries per task for a target error probability.
- Workers are modeled as noisy channels, specifically symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channels (SHC(q)).
- Analysis covers both known and unknown worker skill levels to the crowdsourcer.
- Task assignment is uniform and non-adaptive (taskmaster has no prior on worker skills).
- Provides a necessary condition for minimum query rate for k-ary incidence coding in SHC(q) worker pools.
- Claims practical utility for designing and pricing crowdsourcing by computing minimum query rates for desired error levels.
- Strength: original application of information theory to crowdsourcing, offering fundamental query-budget vs. fidelity trade-offs.
- Strength: theoretical results are well-motivated, practically relevant, and the paper is well-written and organized.
- Strength: inclusion and analysis of k-ary incidence coding under SHC(q) is a valuable contribution.
- Weakness: no experimental validation or simulations to compare theoretical bounds to real-world schemes (e.g., AMT).
- Weakness: absence of empirical evidence limits assessment of practical applicability.
- Weakness: Sections 2.4 and 3.1 are overly detailed and could be shortened or replaced with proof sketches.
- Reviewer did not evaluate the appendix, so proof rigor is unverified.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; authors should add simulations/experiments in future work and improve clarity by trimming verbose sections.
