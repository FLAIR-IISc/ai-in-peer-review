- Models workers as noisy communication channels and reformulates labeling as a channel coding problem.
- Establishes fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoffs by mapping crowdsourcing budget to code rate and analyzing distortion vs. budget.
- Proposes k-incidence coding (kIC) for labeling subsets of k items and analyzes its performance.
- Analyzes performance under symmetric memoryless channel and spammer–hammer channel models.
- Theorems 2 and 3 provide information-theoretic limits for unknown and known worker skill levels.
- Novel theoretical contribution connecting crowdsourcing to information theory, not extensively explored in prior NIPS work.
- Effectively relates and contrasts the framework with prior crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, EM).
- Strong technical quality and rigorous analysis (reviewer notes limited personal expertise in information theory).
- Writing is clear, well-structured, and properly referenced.
- Significant impact: provides fundamental limits that can guide query design and budget allocation in crowdsourcing.
- Main weakness: limited experimental validation beyond theoretical bounds.
- Practical limitation: theory may not directly address real-world implementation challenges on crowdsourcing platforms.
