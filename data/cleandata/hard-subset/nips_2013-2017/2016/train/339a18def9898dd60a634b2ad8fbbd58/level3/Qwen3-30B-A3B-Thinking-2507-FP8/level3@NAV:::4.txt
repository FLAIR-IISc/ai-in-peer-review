

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing by modeling it as joint source-channel coding to derive fundamental budget-fidelity trade-offs. The authors establish rate-distortion bounds for crowdsourcing systems under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). They analyze a k-ary incidence coding scheme and compare its performance against the derived information-theoretic lower bounds.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel application of information-theoretic principles to crowdsourcing, establishing fundamental limits that could inspire future work. The rate-distortion framing is particularly promising and connects well to recent advances in crowdsourcing literature (e.g., [8, 11, 12]). The k-ary incidence coding analysis provides concrete insights into query design.

However, several concerns significantly impact the paper's quality and realism. First, the model conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel parameter, failing to distinguish between skill/rating and reporting noise - a critical limitation for practical crowdsourcing systems. Second, the assumption that the taskmaster allocates identical query volumes to all workers is unrealistic, given that workers typically have known ratings and heterogeneous capabilities. Third, the channel models do not adequately capture adversarial or informed malicious workers, which are prevalent in real crowdsourcing platforms.

The paper would be substantially strengthened by clarifying and justifying the realism of the assumed worker/channel models. The numerical results (Fig. 2) are unclear without proper curve labeling and explicit explanation of what k=1 corresponds to. Additionally, the assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is stated late (page 6, line 246) and should be declared earlier. Minor issues include incorrect figure references (page 4) and inconsistent use of 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' without numbering.

While the theoretical framework is interesting, these methodological concerns and lack of clarity on model realism weaken the paper's contribution. The authors should address these issues before publication to demonstrate the practical relevance of their theoretical findings.
