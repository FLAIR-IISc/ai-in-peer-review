

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" (NIPS 2016)

This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state (worker skill levels), deriving asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item. It represents a significant conceptual shift from the predominant algorithmic approaches in crowdsourcing literature (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting, and EM-based inference) toward an information-theoretic framework. The authors frame crowdsourcing as a communication system where labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as encoder, workers as channel uses with random skill-level states, and the crowdsourcer as decoder. This approach builds on classical information theory results but applies them to a novel domain.

The paper's main contributions include establishing asymptotic query-rate limits for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown to the taskmaster (SL-UK) and when they are known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS), as formalized in Theorems 2 and 3. The authors also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and derive a lower bound on its error probability under the spammer-hammer channel model (Theorem 4). The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a promising first step toward a fundamental understanding of crowdsourcing.

**Strengths:**
- The JSCC modeling framework is novel and well-executed, offering a fresh perspective on crowdsourcing that connects to established information theory.
- The asymptotic results (Theorems 2 and 3) provide valuable fundamental limits that could guide future algorithm design.
- The paper's clear exposition and well-structured presentation make it accessible to both information theorists and machine learning researchers.
- The introduction of k-ary incidence coding and its analysis provides concrete insights into query scheme design.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Major technical gap:** The authors apply source-channel separation results without justifying why this holds when the channel state (worker skill levels) is unknown to the encoder (taskmaster) but possibly known to the decoder (crowdsourcer). This is a critical assumption that requires justification or citation (e.g., to the literature on channel coding with state information).

2. **Modeling concern:** The encoder and decoder are assumed to only see labels, ignoring the original items as side information. In many practical crowdsourcing scenarios (e.g., image labeling), the original items could provide valuable side information that could significantly improve performance.

3. **Misleading wording:** The statement in Section 4.2/Figure 2 that "lower bounds improve as k and q grow" is incorrect. The lower bound value actually decreases (implying better potential performance), not that the bound itself tightens. This should be clarified.

4. **Missing analysis:** The paper lacks nonasymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses, which would be valuable for practical applications. The authors should cite Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses to strengthen their analysis.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its derivations but has a critical gap in justifying the key assumption of source-channel separation with random channel state. The main theorems are properly derived, but the foundational assumption requires stronger justification.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The main concepts are explained thoroughly, though the misleading wording in Section 4.2 needs correction.

**Originality:** This is a highly original contribution that applies information theory to crowdsourcing in a way that hasn't been done before. The JSCC framing represents a novel conceptual contribution to the field.

**Significance:** While the asymptotic results provide fundamental insights, the missing justification for source-channel separation limits the paper's immediate practical significance. However, this work opens a promising research direction that could influence future crowdsourcing design.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel information-theoretic approach to crowdsourcing
- Well-executed and clearly presented framework
- Provides valuable asymptotic fundamental limits
- Establishes a promising foundation for future work

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Critical gap in justifying source-channel separation with random channel state
- Misleading wording that needs correction
- Lacks finite-blocklength analysis that would strengthen the results

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable conceptual contribution to crowdsourcing research. The major technical gap in justifying source-channel separation with random channel state is significant but could be addressed in a revision. The paper is well-positioned to spark new research directions in information-theoretic crowdsourcing, though it requires addressing the technical concerns raised. With revisions to address these points, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
