This paper, "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing", presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing with an information-theoretic framework. The authors aim to provide lower bounds on the number of queries per task for a target error probability, which is a crucial aspect of crowdsourcing systems.

The paper is well-organized and well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The use of a communication system model for crowdsourcing is a clever way to analyze the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing. The authors model workers as parallel independent channels, which is a reasonable assumption given the nature of crowdsourcing.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a unified framework for analyzing different crowdsourcing scenarios. The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They also analyze the performance of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and provide a necessary condition on the minimum rate for this scheme.

The paper's contribution to the field of crowdsourcing is significant, as it provides a new perspective on how to analyze and design crowdsourcing systems. The authors' use of information-theoretic tools is novel and provides a fresh insight into the problem.

However, the paper lacks experiments or simulations to compare practical schemes (e.g., Amazon Mechanical Turk) to the theoretical limits provided. This is a significant limitation, as it would be useful to see how the theoretical results translate to real-world scenarios.

Additionally, some sections of the paper could be shortened or removed. For example, Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 could be removed or shortened. The paper could also benefit from adding proof sketches for the theorems, as this would make the paper more self-contained.

Overall, I believe that the approach presented in this paper is novel and practically useful for designers and pricing. While the paper has some limitations, it provides a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and is well-written and well-organized.
