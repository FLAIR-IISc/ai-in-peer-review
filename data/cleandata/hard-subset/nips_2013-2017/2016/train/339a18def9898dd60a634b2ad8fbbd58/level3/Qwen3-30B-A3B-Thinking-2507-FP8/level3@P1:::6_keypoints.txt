- Presents a novel information-theoretic framework applying rate-distortion and channel coding to crowdsourcing, modeling workers as noisy channels.
- Derives technically sound theoretical bounds (distortion-rate function) for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios; Theorems 2 and 3 are valuable benchmarks.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and shows how query design can leverage error-correction to improve performance.
- Main novelty is mostly the application of established information theory tools rather than fundamentally new theory.
- Practical significance is questionable because the model assumes arbitrary encoder mappings that are often infeasible in real crowdsourcing.
- Fails to address critical differences from channel coding: severe constraints on query design and distribution in crowdsourcing.
- Ignores dependence introduced by querying the same worker multiple times, undermining the independence assumption used in channel coding arguments.
- Does not demonstrate that derived bounds are tight or applicable in realistic crowdsourcing settings.
- Insufficient empirical or theoretical comparison to practical methods like majority voting and Dawid–Skene.
- Implementation feasibility of kIC for k > 2 (showing multiple items concurrently to humans) is not explained and may be impractical.
- Makes an interesting but under-analyzed observation on query pricing (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2).
- Several technical/clarity issues: Theorem 4 proof needs elaboration on ε_S vs. ε̂, H_M(E(ε)) in Eq. (12) lacks justification/citation, and Eq. (8) should clarify minimization constraints.
- Main recommendation: address query-design constraints, compare theoretical bounds with practical methods, and explain how results can guide real-world system design.
- Overall verdict: technically interesting and borderline; accept only if authors close the gap between theory and practical crowdsourcing constraints.
