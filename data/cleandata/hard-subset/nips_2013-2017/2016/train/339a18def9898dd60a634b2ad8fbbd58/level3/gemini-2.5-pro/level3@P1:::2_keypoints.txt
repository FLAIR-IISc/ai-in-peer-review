- Novel formulation: models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem (labels = i.i.d. source, query design = encoder, crowd = parallel noisy channels with random states, inference = decoder) to derive rate–distortion trade-offs.  
- Main theoretical results: Theorems 2 and 3 give asymptotic lower bounds on queries-per-item needed for a target error, for unknown and known worker skills respectively, derived via source–channel separation.  
- Practical scheme analyzed: introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides a lower bound on its performance under a spammer–hammer worker model.  
- Contribution: offers fundamental performance benchmarks as a conceptual alternative to algorithmic inference/task-assignment work.  
- Strength — originality: novel, elegant rate-distortion perspective that is a significant conceptual contribution.  
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and the communication-system analogy is accessible.  
- Strength — direction: represents a promising theoretical first step and applies framework to a concrete query strategy (kIC).  
- Major technical weakness: application of the standard source–channel separation theorem to channels with random/unknown worker states is not justified and is critical to the validity of Theorems 2–3.  
- Required fix: authors must prove separation holds for this random-state setting or cite appropriate information-theoretic results (e.g., compound/AVC literature).  
- Modeling limitation: assumes labels are i.i.d. and independent of item features (no side information at encoder/decoder), potentially yielding overly pessimistic bounds for real tasks.  
- Practicality limitation: results are asymptotic (L → ∞); lacks non-asymptotic/finite-blocklength converse discussion (e.g., Kostina & Verdú).  
- Minor clarity issue: ambiguous phrasing in Section 4.2/Figure 2 about "improve as k and q grow" should specify that required rate decreases.  
- Overall verdict: interesting and promising framework but technically incomplete until separation justification and model/finite-blocklength limitations are addressed.
