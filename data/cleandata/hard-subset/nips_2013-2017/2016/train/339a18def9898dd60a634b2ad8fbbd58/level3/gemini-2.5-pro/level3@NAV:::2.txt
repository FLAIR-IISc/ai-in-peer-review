This paper models the label crowdsourcing problem using an information-theoretic joint source-channel coding (JSCC) framework to derive fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors conceptualize labels as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, workers as parallel channel uses with random skill-level states, and the final aggregator as a decoder. The primary theoretical contribution is the derivation of asymptotic limits on the required queries per item (the rate) to achieve a target error probability. This is achieved by applying standard source-channel separation results to derive the main limits in Theorems 2 and 3. The work also analyzes a specific query scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," providing a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer-hammer worker model.

The main strength of this paper is its originality and potential significance. Framing crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem provides a novel and powerful lens for understanding the ultimate performance limits, moving beyond the analysis of specific aggregation algorithms prevalent in the literature. The work is well-written, clearly organized, and represents a promising first step toward a rigorous information-theoretic treatment of this domain.

Despite these strengths, there is a major technical concern regarding the paper's quality that must be addressed. The central claims in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on the source-channel separation theorem. However, it is not obvious that separation holds for a channel with a random state that is unknown to the encoder (the taskmaster). This is a non-standard setting, and the authors must either provide a proof justifying the application of separation or cite relevant work that extends the theorem to this specific scenario.

Furthermore, the model's practicality is limited by the assumption that the encoder and decoder operate only on labels, ignoring the items themselves, which often provide critical side information for both querying and inference. A minor clarification is also needed in Section 4.2, where the text "lower bounds improve as k and q grow" is misleading. The value of the bound decreases, suggesting better potential performance, but the bound itself is not necessarily tighter without an upper bound for comparison. To increase the impact, the authors could consider nonasymptotic (finite-blocklength) analysis, as the asymptotic regime may not apply to many practical crowdsourcing projects. Citing and discussing the work of Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses would be a valuable addition.
