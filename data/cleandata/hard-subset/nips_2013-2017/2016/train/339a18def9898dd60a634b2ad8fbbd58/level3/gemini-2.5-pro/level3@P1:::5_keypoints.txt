- Proposes a novel information-theoretic framework mapping crowdsourcing to a communication system (source, noisy channels, encoder/decoder).  
- Derives fundamental lower bounds on budget (queries per item / rate) needed for target accuracy (fidelity/distortion).  
- Models worker errors with two channels: M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) for general misclassification and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) for perfect vs random workers.  
- Focuses on non-adaptive query strategies with uniform task assignment because the taskmaster is assumed unaware of individual skills.  
- Analyzes two decoder-side scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly known skill levels (SL-CS).  
- Contrasts with prior work that analyzes specific aggregation algorithms by providing algorithm-agnostic performance limits.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), giving a lower bound for its performance under SHC.  
- Theoretical development appears technically sound and applies standard information-theoretic tools appropriately.  
- Highlights the practical value of bounds for system design, budgeting, and pricing decisions.  
- Major weakness: entirely theoretical and asymptotic—no experiments or simulations to assess finite-sample tightness or practical relevance.  
- Lacks empirical comparison to practical algorithms (e.g., EM on Mechanical Turk), leaving a gap between theory and practice.  
- Some main-text sections are background-heavy (information theory, distortion) and could be condensed or moved to appendix.  
- Suggests including brief proof sketches for main theorems in the main text to improve intuition and self-containedness.  
- Overall recommendation: accept for its novel conceptual contribution, but strongly recommend adding discussion of practical implications and (if possible) simulation results in the final version.
