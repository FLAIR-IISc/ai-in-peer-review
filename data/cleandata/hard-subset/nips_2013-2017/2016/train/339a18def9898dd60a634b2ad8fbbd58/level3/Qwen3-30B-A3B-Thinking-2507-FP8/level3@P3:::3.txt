

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors frame crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, where the source is the dataset to be labeled, the channel models workers' skill levels, and the coding scheme represents query design and inference. The main contribution is deriving the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity, which depends on the source entropy, worker skill levels, and distortion measure. The authors introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme with error correction capabilities and analyze its performance.

The paper builds on previous crowdsourcing research at NIPS, particularly the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5] and majority voting approaches [8], but takes a fundamentally different theoretical approach by establishing performance limits rather than proposing specific aggregation algorithms. This information-theoretic perspective is novel in the crowdsourcing literature.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis. The proofs are provided in the appendices, and the main results are rigorously derived. The authors carefully distinguish between cases where worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) or known (SL-CS) to the crowdsourcer. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed experimental validation to complement the theoretical results.

**Clarity:** While the authors have made accessibility efforts, the paper remains difficult to follow for non-information-theory readers. The heavy use of channel/code/distortion terminology makes it challenging for crowdsourcing practitioners. The distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" is confusing and should be clarified. Several sections (e.g., lines 275-276) are hard to parse and need rephrasing. Figure 2 requires a more detailed caption. Minor copyediting issues include "is reviewed in" (line 84, should be "are reviewed in") and "A A(X1,X2)" (line 122).

**Originality:** This work is original in framing crowdsourcing as a source-channel coding problem to establish fundamental limits. While previous work has studied crowdsourcing aggregation algorithms, this paper provides the first information-theoretic analysis of the fundamental performance limits. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding as a query scheme with error correction capabilities is also novel.

**Significance:** The results have potential significance for designing optimized crowdsourcing systems. The fundamental limits provide a benchmark for evaluating existing crowdsourcing algorithms. However, the paper would be more impactful if the insights were translated into practical takeaways for crowdsourcing practitioners. The authors should highlight high-level intuition and practical implications more prominently, particularly in the conclusion.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. **Reframe results in crowdsourcing terms:** Translate information-theoretic results into crowdsourcing language. For example, instead of discussing "rate-distortion function," explain how many queries per item are needed to achieve a certain level of accuracy.

2. **Expand practical insights:** The authors should expand the end of Section 3 and add a concluding takeaway section that summarizes the practical implications of the findings for crowdsourcing practitioners. This would help readers understand the real-world relevance of the theoretical results.

3. **Clarify terminology:** Consistently define and use "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" (currently used interchangeably in confusing ways).

4. **Include relevant related work:** The paper should cite and discuss work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing, particularly research that combines incentives with aggregation (e.g., [1,6,13]).

5. **Fix minor issues:** Correct all the mentioned copyediting errors and rephrase the hard-to-parse sections.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*For Acceptance:* The paper presents a novel information-theoretic approach to crowdsourcing, establishes fundamental performance limits, and introduces a promising query scheme (kIC) with error correction capabilities. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported.

*Against Acceptance:* The current presentation makes it difficult for the broader NIPS audience to appreciate the significance of the results. The heavy use of technical information theory terminology without translation to crowdsourcing terms limits accessibility and practical impact.

**Overall Recommendation:** The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to crowdsourcing research. With the suggested revisions to improve clarity, translate results into practical terms, and include relevant related work, this would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper is technically sound but would benefit from greater accessibility for the NIPS community. I recommend a weak accept with major revisions.
