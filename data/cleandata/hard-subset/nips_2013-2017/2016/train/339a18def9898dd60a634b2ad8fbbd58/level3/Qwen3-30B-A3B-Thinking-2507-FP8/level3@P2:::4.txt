

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the trade-off between budget (number of queries per item) and fidelity (error probability). The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, where the source is the data to be labeled (or clustered), and workers are modeled as parallel noisy channels. The paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds for the budget-fidelity trade-off in two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). The authors also analyze a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and compare its performance to the theoretical bounds.

The paper builds on previous work in crowdsourcing, such as the Dawid and Skene algorithm for probabilistic inference [5], Zhang et al.'s spectral methods for crowdsourcing [12], and Karger et al.'s budget-optimal task allocation [6]. However, it distinguishes itself by taking an information-theoretic approach to establish fundamental limits rather than focusing on practical algorithms. This framing is novel and potentially inspiring for future theoretical work in crowdsourcing.

**Quality:** The paper presents theoretically sound derivations of fundamental limits using information theory. The joint source-channel coding framework is well-motivated and the proofs for Theorems 2 and 3 appear rigorous. However, the model merges worker skill and reliability into a single channel parameter, which obscures the distinction between worker expertise (skill) and reporting noise. This is a significant limitation as real crowdsourcing systems often have workers with known skill ratings. Additionally, the assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers is unrealistic, as platforms typically allocate more queries to higher-rated workers. The channel models also fail to account for adversarial or informed malicious workers, which are common in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper's main ideas are clearly presented, but there are several issues that reduce clarity. The assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is stated late (page 6, line 246) and should be declared earlier in the paper. The paper misuses "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" without numbering, which is confusing. Figure 1 references are incorrect (1.a → 1.b, 1.b → 1.c). The numerical results in Figure 2 are unclear without better labeling and explicit explanation of what k=1 corresponds to (which the authors note is equivalent to the analysis in [8]).

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution is original in applying joint source-channel coding to crowdsourcing to derive fundamental limits. This represents a significant theoretical advance beyond previous crowdsourcing work that focused on practical algorithms. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding as a concrete example that approaches the theoretical bounds is also novel. However, the information-theoretic approach to crowdsourcing is a natural extension of the field, and the paper should more explicitly distinguish its contribution from related work on rate-distortion in machine learning.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in crowdsourcing - the budget-fidelity trade-off - with theoretical significance. Establishing fundamental limits is valuable for understanding the boundaries of what is achievable in crowdsourcing systems. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the unrealistic assumptions about worker behavior and task allocation. The results could be more impactful if the authors had addressed how their theoretical bounds might inform practical crowdsourcing system design, given the limitations of their model.

**Strengths:**
- Novel information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem
- Derivation of fundamental budget-fidelity trade-off limits for crowdsourcing
- Analysis of k-ary incidence coding as a query scheme that approaches theoretical bounds
- Clear theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing

**Weaknesses:**
- Unjustified assumption that taskmaster allocates equal queries to all workers
- Merged skill and reliability into a single channel parameter, losing important distinctions
- Channel models do not realistically capture adversarial or informed malicious workers
- Unclear numerical results and insufficient explanation of Figure 2
- Late introduction of key assumption (knowledge of P(B(X)))
- Misuse of mathematical terminology without proper numbering

**Recommendation:** The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to understanding crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens. However, the unrealistic assumptions about worker behavior and task allocation significantly limit its impact. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Justify or revise the assumptions about worker skill levels and query allocation
2. Address how their theoretical bounds might inform practical crowdsourcing systems
3. Clarify the numerical results and improve figure labeling
4. Introduce key assumptions earlier in the paper
5. Properly number theorems, lemmas, and corollaries

I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing these concerns. The theoretical framework is promising but requires more realistic modeling of crowdsourcing systems to have broader significance.
