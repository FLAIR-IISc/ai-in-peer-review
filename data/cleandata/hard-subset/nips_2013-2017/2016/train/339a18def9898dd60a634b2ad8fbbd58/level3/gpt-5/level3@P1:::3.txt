Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Items’ latent labels B(X) constitute the source; workers are modeled as noisy channels (MSC or spammer–hammer SHC). The key results are single-letter lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error rate under two regimes: skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) vs known/estimated perfectly (SL-CS). The bounds mirror rate–distortion over parallel channels: the numerator reflects the information needed about the labels (H(B(X)) minus what error tolerance allows), and the denominator reflects the effective per-query information that can be extracted from the crowd. The paper then studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), showing that grouping items into k-wise queries prunes invalid response patterns, providing an implicit error-correction effect. Under SHC, it derives a necessary condition on rate for a target error and offers a pricing rule-of-thumb that scales with k.

The work connects to established aggregation methods (majority vote; Dawid–Skene EM; spectral/graphical methods) and theoretical analyses of crowd labeling (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah on budget-optimal allocation and reliability). Its novelty is to cast the entire pipeline in information-theoretic terms and to analyze kIC as a code that shapes workers’ error profiles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level structure is coherent: a separation-style argument yields fundamental limits; Theorems 2–3 specialize to SL-UK vs SL-CS and agree with intuition (unknown skills behave like an “average worker,” known skills yield “average of capacities”). The kIC analysis under SHC is a clean necessary condition. However:
  - Assumptions should be stated more crisply: iid items, non-adaptive assignment, large-blocklength, independence across workers and items, and identical response alphabets. Clarify where separation is tight and when finite-length or adaptive designs could improve constants.
  - Notation is sometimes heavy and occasionally ambiguous (M vs N vs k, source vs response alphabets).
  - Figure 2 needs clearer explanation of axes, operating points, and how k and q affect the gap to the information-theoretic bound.

Clarity
- The exposition will be challenging for readers without an information theory background. Sections 2–3 are dense in channel/source terminology. The paper would benefit from restating the main theorems in crowdsourcing terms immediately after each formal statement. For example: “To achieve error ≤ ε on a balanced binary labeling task, the minimum queries per item is at least (1 − Hb(ε)) divided by the effective per-question information conveyed by workers. With known skills this denominator is the expected channel capacity; with unknown skills it is the capacity of the mixture channel.”
- Expand the high-level intuition at the end of Section 3 and add a short concluding section that summarizes actionable takeaways (e.g., when it is worth investing in estimating worker skills; how k affects price and reliability; when majority vote is fundamentally insufficient).
- Clarify roles: throughout, distinguish “taskmaster” (assigns queries) vs “crowdsourcer/decoder” (aggregates and infers). State explicitly which entity knows/does not know worker skills in SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- Minor places are hard to parse (see detailed comments).

Originality
- Casting budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion and separation brings a clean, principled perspective that complements probabilistic/graphical models and bandit approaches. The kIC view of multi-item queries as codes with invalid responses providing error-correction is insightful. While related works analyze optimal allocation, EM, and spectral methods, the explicit information-theoretic limits and the SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison appear novel.

Significance
- The results offer baseline limits that any practical algorithm should be measured against, guiding system design: (i) quantify value of estimating worker skills; (ii) reason about when richer query types (kIC) pay off; (iii) inform pricing as a function of k and target accuracy. If clarified and connected more directly to application metrics (e.g., expected spend for ε-accuracy under realistic worker pools), the impact could be substantial for both research and practice.

Specific suggestions
- Rephrase key results in application language. After Theorems 2–3, include a paragraph translating the bounds into “queries per item ≈ (bits needed about labels)/(bits reliably obtained per query).”
- Provide a short design guide: given target ε and prior over skills, how to decide between (a) spending queries to estimate skills (SL-CS), (b) using kIC with larger k, or (c) allocating more redundancy.
- kIC: add intuition for Lemma 1 and Theorem 4. A small table or examples for k ∈ {2,3,4} showing number of valid response patterns, spammer error rates, and implied price scaling would help. Clarify the pricing rule: as written π(k1) π(k2) ≈ k1k2 is ambiguous; likely intended π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 (price proportional to k).
- Figure 2: enlarge labels, define all curves in the caption, state parameters (q, M, ε), and explain the gap between the information-theoretic bound and kIC lower bounds.
- Discuss scope and limitations: independence assumptions, item difficulty (input-dependent channels), adversarial or colluding workers, and adaptivity. Briefly relate to adaptive/bandit task assignment (e.g., Abraham et al.; Ho–Slivkins–Vaughan) and note whether adaptivity can change the asymptotic limits vs constants.
- Related work on incentives/mechanism design: please cite and position against works that combine incentives with aggregation/selection, e.g., Prelec (Bayesian Truth Serum), Witkowski & Parkes (peer prediction), Radanovic & Faltings (multi-task peer prediction), Slivkins/Singer/Vaughan on pricing and incentives in crowdsourcing markets. This will help connect your pricing comments and the assumption that workers’ behavior is stationary.
- Experimental/numerical section: beyond Figure 2, consider a small simulation comparing majority vote, DS, and a kIC-aware decoder under MSC/SHC, reporting empirical error vs budget to illustrate how close practical schemes get to your bounds.

Minor issues and copyedits
- Line 84: “are reviewed in” (plural).
- Clarify “crowdsourcer” vs “taskmaster” early (Section 2.1 or 2.3).
- Line 122: remove duplicated “A” in “A A(X1,X2)”.
- Lines 275–276: the sentence starting “In case...” is hard to parse; please rephrase to explicitly state the valid range of ε and the role of pmax.
- Notation: ensure consistent use of N (number of classes), M (response alphabet size), and k (query arity).
- Figure 2: clearer caption and axis labels.

Pros
- Clear, principled framework yielding fundamental limits.
- Insightful comparison of SL-UK vs SL-CS; actionable message about valuing skill estimation.
- kIC interpretation as error-correcting queries; initial bounds and pricing guidance.

Cons
- Accessibility: dense information-theoretic presentation; needs stronger application-level exposition.
- Some assumptions and notation are under-specified; figure and minor clarity issues.
- Limited empirical illustration; related work on incentives/mechanisms is missing.

Overall: a strong and original theoretical contribution with practical implications. With improved clarity, broader related-work coverage, and more application-oriented discussion, it would be a valuable addition.
