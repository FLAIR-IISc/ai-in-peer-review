Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an information-theoretic formulation of crowdsourcing (CS) as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion theory. Items X are mapped to a discrete “ground-truth” function B(X) (e.g., labels/clusters). Workers are modeled as noisy channels: either M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with error parameter ε, or spammer–hammer channels (SHC) with hammer probability q. Two cases are analyzed: (i) worker skill levels unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) worker skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). Using separation-style arguments, the paper derives lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item to achieve target Hamming distortion (Theorems 2 and 3). It then introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query jointly involves k items, analyzes the spammer error within kIC, and provides a converse-style lower bound (Theorem 4) on the rate needed to achieve a target error under SHC, along with a rule-of-thumb for pricing different k.

The work connects to classic aggregation/inference methods in CS (majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM, spectral/EM hybrids, and assignment/pricing/bandit formulations). Prior NIPS and related work has focused on statistical estimation algorithms and assignment policies under various worker models; this paper instead asks for information-theoretic limits regardless of the specific inference scheme. To my knowledge, this is among the first papers to explicitly cast CS budget–fidelity trade-offs in a rate–distortion framework, though the derivations closely follow standard separation theorems and basic channel capacity bounds (e.g., Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim).

Strengths
- Clear and principled information-theoretic perspective on CS, yielding compact, interpretable lower bounds on achievable fidelity under budget constraints (Theorems 2 and 3).
- Useful decomposition of SL-UK vs SL-CS; the comparison clarifies how knowing worker skills at the decoder impacts the “effective capacity.”
- kIC is a natural and practically motivated coding scheme; the spammer error analysis and Theorem 4 provide intuition on how k and q trade off with budget, and the pricing rule-of-thumb is appealing.
- The paper situates the work among popular aggregation methods and touches on practical issues (non-adaptivity, cost per query, pricing).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Main concerns about modeling and tightness of bounds
- Implementability constraints on encoding C: The separation-based bounds assume one can realize arbitrary mappings C: B(X)^n → U^{Σ m_i} and design long block codes across items. In CS, feasible encodings are severely constrained: a worker can only be shown a few small, human-readable queries; codebooks cannot be arbitrarily complex; and query formats are limited. The paper should clarify which classes of encoders are implementable and how that restriction affects the achievable rate–distortion region. Without this, the derived limits are likely loose for real CS systems.
- Independence and channel model assumptions: Unlike repeated uses of an iid DMC, repeated queries to the same worker break memorylessness (learning, fatigue, anchoring), while avoiding re-using workers reduces opportunities for coding across channel uses. The paper samples random workers per query but does not discuss finite-pool effects, sampling without replacement, or dependence induced by kIC overlap across items. Please articulate when the DMC approximation is justified and how violations affect the results.
- Input design freedom: Information-theoretic bounds are most meaningful when one can approach them with explicit codes. Beyond repetition (majority voting), it is unclear how to design queries u^n that realize strong error-correction under practical constraints. The paper would benefit from proposing implementable code constructions (e.g., expander-based incidence designs, parity-check-like hypergraph queries) and demonstrating performance relative to the limits.

Theoretical details needing clarification
- Theorem 2/3 denominators: In (11)/(12), the terms log2 M − H_M(·) (or E[H_M(ε)]) appear. Please justify these via a precise capacity or mutual information calculation for the effective channel seen by the decoder in SL-UK vs SL-CS, with citations. If SL-UK induces a mixture channel, capacity is generally not simply log M − H_M(E[ε]); the derivation should specify the exact random channel state model and whether the encoder/decoder know the state distribution or realizations.
- Equation (8): Clarify that the minimization is over conditional pmfs P(Ẑ|Z) (or, equivalently, over codes C,C′) subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.
- Theorem 4: Since ln(1−q) < 0, please check the inequality direction and the role of ε̄_S versus ĥε. Define all symbols explicitly, relate R to the integer R′ = number of kIC incidences per item (ceiling/floor), and elaborate the steps that lead from the oracle-decoder argument to the final bound.
- Lemma 1: Provide a concise derivation or intuition for the combinatorial sums, and specify whether the dataset is balanced (P(B=0)=P(B=1)=1/2).
- Notation consistency: M (answer alphabet size) vs N (number of classes) vs k (incidence arity) should be consistently tracked; in kIC for binary labeling N=2, what is M?

Empirical/practical evaluation
- No empirical comparison to state-of-the-art methods (DS, spectral-EM, Karger–Oh–Shah) is given. Even small-scale simulations under MSC/SHC showing how close kIC (with a concrete decoder) can get to the information-theoretic limits would strengthen the paper and demonstrate practical significance.
- Source distribution P(B(X)) and N are often unknown. Discuss robustness of the bounds to misspecification and how to estimate these quantities in practice.
- Pricing: The rule-of-thumb π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 relies on the lower bound; include sensitivity analyses and discuss platform constraints (worker time, UI complexity).

Quality
- The derivations appear technically sound at a high level, but several steps need more careful justification (mixture channel capacity, Theorem 4 detail/sign). The results are lower bounds; without constructive schemes approaching them, practical impact is limited.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Adding precise definitions, fixing notation, and expanding proofs (Theorem 4, Lemma 1) would improve readability. Please correct minor issues listed above.

Originality
- Framing CS budget–fidelity via rate–distortion is a nice conceptual contribution, but much of the development is a direct application of standard separation theorems. Novelty is moderate; kIC itself has been explored in related forms (pairwise/ternary queries) in prior work.

Significance
- The conceptual lens could influence future design of query schemes. However, in its current form—without implementable constructions and empirical validation—the impact on practice is uncertain.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean, principled information-theoretic framing of CS.
- Explicit, interpretable lower bounds; interesting comparison of SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- kIC analysis and pricing insight are potentially useful.

Arguments against acceptance
- Bounds likely loose under realistic implementability constraints; key assumptions (iid DMC, arbitrary codebooks) are not well justified for CS.
- No empirical evaluation or constructive schemes approaching the bounds.
- Several theoretical details and notational issues need clarification.

Recommendation
Leaning to reject. The paper offers a compelling perspective but requires stronger connection to implementable encoders/decoders, more careful treatment of the effective channel in SL-UK, clearer proofs, and empirical comparisons to demonstrate significance.
