- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as noisy channels to characterize fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.  
- Frames crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion trade-off and derives bounds on achievable fidelity given query budget and vice-versa.  
- Analyzes both unknown and perfectly known worker skill scenarios, with key results in Theorems 2 and 3.  
- Proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a concrete query scheme and provides performance analysis (Theorem 4).  
- Connects crowdsourcing to joint source-channel coding, highlighting the need for query design and error correction.  
- Provides a clear, rigorous mathematical formulation accessible to readers with information-theory background.  
- Builds on and contrasts with prior algorithmic work (e.g., majority voting, Dawid–Skene) by focusing on fundamental limits.  
- Assumes independent, memoryless worker channels, which simplifies reality and ignores temporal/contextual correlations.  
- Offers limited empirical validation; numerical kIC results are present but broader practical comparisons are lacking.  
- Often assumes uniformly distributed data, and sensitivity to other data distributions is not examined.  
- Practical benefits of kIC for non-binary tasks are unclear and may be outweighed by increased query complexity.  
- Does not address worker incentives or mechanism design for truthful reporting beyond modeling spammers/hammers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically rigorous, insightful bounds, and potential to shape future research.  
- Arguments against acceptance: simplifying assumptions limit real-world applicability, insufficient empirical support, and possible impracticality of kIC.  
- Overall assessment: strong and significant contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions to address empirical validation and practical considerations.
