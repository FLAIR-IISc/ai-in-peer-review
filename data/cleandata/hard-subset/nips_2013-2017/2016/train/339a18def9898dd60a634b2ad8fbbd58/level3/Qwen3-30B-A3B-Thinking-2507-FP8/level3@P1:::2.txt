

This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states (worker skill levels), deriving fundamental asymptotic limits on query rates for achieving target fidelity. The authors frame the crowdsourcer as a decoder, taskmaster as an encoder, and workers as parallel noisy channels with skill-dependent error rates. They leverage information-theoretic source-channel separation theorems to establish Theorems 2 and 3, which characterize the minimum query rates required for achieving target error probabilities under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). The paper also analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), establishing a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer-hammer channel model.

The paper makes a promising contribution by applying information theory to crowdsourcing, which has previously been dominated by heuristic aggregation methods (majority voting, Dawid-Skene) and optimization approaches. The authors correctly identify the need for fundamental limits, noting that prior work has focused on practical algorithms without establishing theoretical boundaries. This aligns with NIPS' interest in theoretical foundations, though the authors could better connect to recent theoretical crowdsourcing work like Zhang et al. (NIPS 2014) on spectral methods.

Strengths include clear writing, well-structured arguments, and a novel information-theoretic framework that unifies previous approaches. The paper successfully demonstrates how standard JSCC theory applies to crowdsourcing, providing rigorous bounds that previous heuristic approaches lacked. The kIC analysis is particularly valuable as it demonstrates how coding theory can inform practical query design. The writing is crisp and accessible to both theory and applied researchers, with helpful figures illustrating the kIC scheme.

However, the paper has significant technical concerns. The most critical is the unjustified application of source-channel separation when the channel state (worker skill) is unknown to the encoder (taskmaster) but potentially known to the decoder (crowdsourcer). The authors cite Theorem 1 from Cover & Thomas, but this theorem assumes a known channel, not a random channel state unknown to the encoder. The authors should either provide a justification for the separation theorem in this setting or cite relevant literature (e.g., El Gamal & Kim's work on channels with state). Without this, the asymptotic bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 lack rigorous foundation.

Another modeling concern is the assumption that the encoder and decoder only see labels (B(X)) and ignore the original items (X) as side information. In many crowdsourcing applications (e.g., image labeling), the original items contain crucial information that could be leveraged to design better queries and inference. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and discuss how it might affect their bounds.

The paper's Figure 2 and associated text are misleading in stating that "lower bounds improve as k and q grow." The lower bound values actually decrease (meaning better performance), but the phrasing suggests the bounds themselves are tightening, which is confusing without an upper bound for comparison. This should be clarified.

The authors could strengthen their work by addressing finite-blocklength effects. While asymptotic bounds are valuable, the paper could cite Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses to provide more practical insights for real-world crowdsourcing with limited data. The current analysis assumes large L (dataset size), but practical crowdsourcing projects often have finite, moderate-sized datasets.

The significance of this work is substantial. Establishing fundamental limits provides a benchmark against which to evaluate existing crowdsourcing algorithms, and the JSCC framework opens new avenues for algorithm design. The kIC scheme they propose could lead to more efficient query designs, potentially reducing costs while maintaining fidelity. This is particularly relevant for platforms like AMT where query costs are significant.

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable first step toward theoretical foundations for crowdsourcing. The core contribution of applying JSCC to crowdsourcing is novel and significant, though the technical execution requires strengthening. The main weakness is the unaddressed issue of channel state unknown to the encoder, which undermines the theoretical foundation of the key results. The modeling limitation regarding side information is also noteworthy.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing; clear presentation; establishes fundamental limits previously absent in the literature; provides a concrete coding scheme (kIC) with theoretical analysis.

Con arguments against acceptance: Unjustified application of source-channel separation theorem for random channel state; modeling assumption ignoring side information; misleading interpretation of bounds in Figure 2; lacks finite-blocklength analysis.

Overall, this paper makes a promising contribution that would be significantly strengthened with the technical clarifications noted. The core insight is valuable and aligns with NIPS' focus on theoretical foundations. Given the importance of establishing fundamental limits in crowdsourcing, and the paper's generally strong execution aside from the technical gap, I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the channel state issue and clarify the bounds. The authors should cite relevant work on channels with state unknown to encoder (e.g., [El Gamal & Kim, 2011]) and provide a justification for the source-channel separation in their setting.
