Summary and relation to prior work: This paper casts crowdsourcing with unreliable workers as a joint source–channel coding problem and uses rate–distortion theory to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the budget–fidelity trade-off. It gives bounds for two scenarios (unknown worker skills vs. skills known to the decoder), and analyzes a concrete querying scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), comparing its performance to the lower bounds under a spammer–hammer channel. The mapping to source–channel separation and the use of R(D) as a proxy for budget are conceptually appealing and relate to earlier theoretical analyses of crowdsourcing reliability and sample complexity (e.g., Dawid–Skene; Karger–Oh–Shah; spectral/EM methods at NIPS), but here expressed via a clean information-theoretic lens.

Strengths:
- Original and insightful framing via rate–distortion; the separation between SL-UK and SL-CS yields clear, interpretable limits.
- kIC offers a concrete code-like scheme that can be compared to the bounds, potentially inspiring future code-design-style query schemes.
- The oracle-vs.-limit comparisons help position achievable schemes against fundamental limits.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Modeling: The worker is abstracted as a single memoryless channel, merging competence (confusion matrix over labels) and reporting noise/intent. This conflates skill with reliability; many practical settings require separate modeling of ability (systematic biases) and honesty/strategic behavior. Consider models with per-worker confusion matrices (as in Dawid–Skene) plus additional reporting noise, and adversarial/informed malicious workers beyond MSC/SHC.
- Allocation: The taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, despite known heterogeneity and often-available ratings. Adaptive or bandit-based allocations (e.g., Abraham et al., Karger–Oh–Shah) are more realistic and could materially change the limits.
- Realism/justification: Please clarify why MSC and SHC capture real worker behaviors; discuss adversarial/strategic settings and whether the bounds extend or how they would change under Byzantine models.
- Methodology/clarity: The assumption that P(B(X)) is known appears late (p.6, l.246); state this prominently in the problem setup and discuss robustness if it is misspecified or learned online. Numerical results: improve Fig. 2 by labeling curves clearly and explicitly stating what k=1 corresponds to (direct per-item labeling). Minor: fix figure references on p.4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b, 1.b → 1.c). Theorems/lemmas/corollaries should be numbered consistently (or use lowercase if informal).
- Scope: Non-adaptive coding and equal rates limit practical applicability; a brief discussion of adaptive/sequential designs would strengthen significance.

Quality: Sound under assumptions; clarity issues and modeling choices limit applicability. Originality: Moderate-to-high for the JSCC/R(D) perspective. Significance: Conceptually valuable; practical impact depends on addressing heterogeneity and adversaries.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant theoretical reframing with clean limits.
- Could stimulate cross-fertilization between information theory and crowdsourcing.

Cons for acceptance:
- Unrealistic worker/channel and allocation assumptions.
- Clarity/presentation issues and limited empirical interpretability.

Recommendation: Borderline; encourage revision addressing modeling realism, adaptive allocation, clearer numerics, and presentation fixes.
