- Presents an information-theoretic framework for budget–fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing.
- Derives novel bounds on minimum queries per item for target fidelity under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills.
- Introduces and analyzes a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC).
- Uses a joint source–channel coding model to represent queries and inference over parallel noisy worker channels.
- Shows bounds depend on source distribution, number of workers, and worker quality.
- Technical contributions are sound with proofs provided in appendices.
- Includes numerical results and comparisons demonstrating the approach's effectiveness.
- Paper is well-organized and explains high-level intuition and practical takeaways accessibly.
- Some technical details remain challenging for readers without information-theory background.
- Suggest restating results in crowdsourcing/application terms rather than channel/code/distortion language.
- Suggest highlighting high-level intuition and practical takeaways more prominently (e.g., expand Section 3 end).
- Recommend adding related work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing.
- Ask to clarify the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster."
- Request clearer figure/caption for Figure 2.
- Minor copyedits: Line 84 pluralization and Line 122 typo "A A(X1,X2)."
- Other edits: rephrase hard-to-parse lines 275–276 and provide more numerical comparisons with existing methods.
- Overall: technically sound, original, and significant for crowdsourcing system design.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, new bounds for SL-UK and SL-CS, and practical implications.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited accessibility for non-information-theory readers and need for more numerical comparisons.
