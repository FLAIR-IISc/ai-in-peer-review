This paper attempts to model crowdsourcing as a communication problem and derive fundamental limits on budget-fidelity trade-offs using information theory, specifically rate-distortion theory and channel coding. While the application of these tools to crowdsourcing is novel, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily stemming from the mismatch between the assumptions of classical information theory and the practical realities of crowdsourcing.

The core idea – framing crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem – is interesting and potentially valuable. The derivation of bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) is sound, given the model’s assumptions. If this *is* the first application of these information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing, it represents a conceptual contribution. However, the novelty is somewhat diminished by the relatively straightforward nature of the derivation, and the resulting bounds appear likely to be loose in practical scenarios. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) is a worthwhile addition, though its practical relevance remains unclear without comparison to existing methods.

The major concern is the applicability of channel coding-style bounds to crowdsourcing. Unlike standard communication channels, the crucial assumption of being able to freely design the input sequence *u<sup>n</sup>* (the encoding) doesn’t readily translate. In crowdsourcing, one cannot arbitrarily query the same worker multiple times without violating the implicit assumption of i.i.d. worker responses. Repeated queries to the same worker introduce correlation that the model doesn't account for, invalidating the theoretical foundation. This fundamental difference undermines the tightness of the proposed bound. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn’t fully explore its implications.

Further complicating matters is the question of implementable codebooks. The paper doesn’t adequately address *which* mappings from B(X)<sup>n</sup> to U<sup>Σ m<sub>i</sub></sup> are practically realizable as query schemes in a crowdsourcing context. The proposed kIC provides an example, but the general problem remains open.  While repetition codes (akin to majority voting) are conceivable, designing *u<sup>n</sup>* to achieve stronger error correction beyond simple repetition is unclear given the constraints outlined above.

The paper lacks a crucial empirical validation. The derived information-theoretic bound is presented in isolation. There is no comparison to the performance of state-of-the-art practical methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene, other EM-based algorithms, or even simple majority voting) under comparable conditions.  Without such a comparison, it is impossible to assess the practical utility or tightness of the theoretical bounds. Is the bound orders of magnitude away from achievable performance? Does it offer any actionable insights for system design? These questions remain unanswered.

**Minor Points:**

*   In equation (8), it should be explicitly stated that the minimization is over all codes C and C' such that E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D<sup>t</sup>.
*   The proof of Theorem 4 requires further elaboration. The jump in logic from the error probabilities to the overall bound isn’t fully clear.
*   In Theorem 4, since ln(1−q) < 0, it needs to be clarified whether ε<sub>S</sub> is larger or smaller than  ε̂. The relationship between the two should be explicitly stated.
*   In equation (17), the origin of H<sub>M</sub>(E(ε)) is not explained. Either provide a justification for its inclusion or cite prior work where this term appears.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing.
*   Sound mathematical derivation of the proposed bounds.
*   Introduction of the k-ary incidence coding scheme.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The applicability of the theoretical framework to practical crowdsourcing is questionable due to the violation of key assumptions (e.g., independent queries, freely designable input sequence).
*   Lack of empirical validation and comparison to existing methods.
*   The resulting bounds are likely to be loose and lack practical utility without further refinement and validation.
*   Insufficient discussion of the implementability of the proposed codebooks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting but ultimately flawed attempt to apply information theory to crowdsourcing. While the mathematical derivations are correct, the underlying assumptions are too restrictive to provide meaningful insights into the performance of real-world crowdsourcing systems. The lack of empirical validation and comparison to existing methods further weakens the paper.  I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should either significantly revise the model to better reflect the constraints of crowdsourcing or focus on providing a rigorous empirical evaluation of the bounds’ tightness in realistic scenarios.
