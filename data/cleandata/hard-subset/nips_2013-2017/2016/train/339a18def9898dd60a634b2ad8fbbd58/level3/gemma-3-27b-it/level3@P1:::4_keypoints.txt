- Paper models budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and treats workers as noisy channels.
- Authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on queries needed to reach a target fidelity.
- Paper introduces and analyzes a practical query scheme: k-ary incidence coding, and compares its performance to the bounds.
- Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, offering a fresh, rigorous perspective.
- Provides fundamental limits that serve as benchmarks for practical aggregation algorithms.
- Clear core modeling of crowdsourcing as a communication system with workers-as-channels.
- k-ary incidence coding demonstrates a concrete use of the theoretical framework.
- Worker model conflates skill and reliability, obscuring the effect of true worker expertise versus reporting noise.
- Assumes uniform query allocation to all workers, which is unrealistic given pre-rated and heterogeneous workers.
- Channel models (MSC and SHC) may not capture malicious, adversarial, or strategically noisy worker behaviors.
- Numerical results and Fig. 2 are unclear: curves unlabeled and the role/significance of k=1 is not explained.
- Key assumption that P(B(X)) is known is declared late and should be stated earlier.
- Minor editorial issues and inconsistent theorem/figure labeling reduce clarity.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous framework; useful information-theoretic bounds; practical demonstration via k-ary coding.
- Arguments against acceptance: oversimplified worker and allocation assumptions, limited channel model realism, and unclear numerical presentation.
- Overall assessment: technically sound and interesting but practical relevance is weakened by oversimplifications and clarity issues.
- Recommendation: weak accept with major revisions to address worker modeling, query allocation, channel models, numerical clarity, and earlier statement of assumptions.
