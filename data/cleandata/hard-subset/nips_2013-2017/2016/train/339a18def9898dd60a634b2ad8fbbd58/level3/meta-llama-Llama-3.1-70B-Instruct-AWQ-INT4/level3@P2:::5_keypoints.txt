- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication system to derive fundamental budget–fidelity limits.  
- Taskmaster splits projects into small batches assigned to imperfect-skilled workers; crowdsourcer aggregates results to infer labels.  
- Novel compared to prior work by deriving lower bounds on queries-per-task needed for a target error probability.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and perfectly known skills (SL-CS), deriving bounds for both.  
- Bounds are code-agnostic and provide fundamental minimum queries per task for a given error target.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): queries ask a set of k items and workers identify items with the same label.  
- Derives a necessary condition on minimum rate for kIC under a spammer–hammer worker pool and gives numerical illustrations.  
- Strengths: novel modeling, derivation of fundamental limits, new query scheme, well-organized and well-written.  
- Weaknesses: lacks experiments/simulations comparing practical platforms (e.g., AMT) to theoretical limits.  
- Presentation issues: some sections (e.g., 2.4, 3.1) are overly long and could be shortened or removed.  
- Suggestion: include proof sketches in the main text rather than relegating all proofs to the appendix.  
- Overall: significant, practically useful contribution for designers/pricing; scores — Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, fundamental limits, kIC contribution, clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing empirical validation, verbosity in parts, lack of main-text proof sketches.
