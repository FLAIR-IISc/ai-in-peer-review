Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models label-aggregation crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding and uses rate–distortion theory to derive fundamental budget–fidelity limits. The core mapping is: (i) the ground-truth labels (or other functions of interest) are the “source,” (ii) worker responses are noisy channels, and (iii) a query/inference scheme is a code. Using this abstraction, the paper derives lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (budget) needed to achieve a target average error rate (distortion), with two variants: (a) worker skill levels unknown to the system (SL-UK) and (b) worker skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The latter enjoys a strictly better limit (average of capacities vs. capacity under average channel). The paper also analyzes a concrete “k-ary incidence coding” (kIC) scheme, which embeds basic error-correction by ruling out logically inconsistent responses, and gives a necessary condition on budget for kIC under a spammer–hammer worker model.

The work connects to a large crowdsourcing literature on aggregation and inference (majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM and variants; Karger–Oh–Shah spectral/graph-based methods; Whitehill et al.; Raykar et al.; Zhang et al.) by providing an information-theoretic envelope within which these algorithms must operate. The kIC analysis relates to graph-based labeling and clustering with pairwise/tuplewise queries (e.g., convex relaxations for clustering) and offers a principled way to reason about query design.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding is elegant and yields clean, interpretable limits. The SL-UK vs. SL-CS contrast is insightful and practically relevant.
- Generality: Results are oblivious to specific aggregation algorithms; they apply to any querying and decoding scheme under the stated worker models.
- Concrete instantiation: kIC gives a tangible example that exhibits “built-in error correction,” with informative bounds and rule-of-thumb pricing guidance.
- Potential impact: These limits can guide practitioners on how much redundancy is needed and how to trade query complexity (k) against budget and accuracy.

Weaknesses and suggestions
High-level intuition and practical takeaways
- Please foreground intuition. A short “practitioner takeaway” box at the end of Section 3 and a concluding section would help. Suggested points to include:
  - Budget ≈ queries per item. Fidelity ≈ expected mislabeling rate. Each query contributes mutual information about the label; the minimum budget is roughly H(B) − HN(ε_target) divided by average per-query information (log M − HM(noise)).
  - SL-UK vs. SL-CS: When worker skills are unknown, the system behaves like a single channel with the average confusion rate; when known, you effectively route information over parallel channels and harvest the average of their capacities—this strictly reduces the budget.
  - For binary, balanced labels and symmetric worker error p, a back-of-the-envelope rule: Rmin ≈ [1 − Hb(ε)] / [E[1 − Hb(p_worker)]]. Give a numerical example for common p and ε.
  - For kIC: Larger k reduces logical inconsistency options (more error-correction) but increases per-query effort/cost; include a simple guidance curve/table translating q (fraction hammers), k, and target ε into R and approximate price.

Accessibility and terminology
- The paper makes commendable efforts, but it will still be difficult for non–information-theory readers. Consider restating the main theorems alongside “crowdsourcing-language” paraphrases (e.g., “If labels are roughly balanced and workers make p% mistakes, you need at least R queries per item to keep the average labeling error below ε”). Throughout, consider using both vocabularies: source/labels; channel/worker; rate/budget; distortion/error rate; capacity/average information per query.
- Clarify the roles of “taskmaster” vs. “crowdsourcer.” The current usage is confusing. Suggested clarification: taskmaster = entity that designs and issues queries (encoder); crowdsourcer = entity that collects responses and runs aggregation/inference (decoder). If the same party plays both roles, say so explicitly early on.

Related work to include
- Mechanism design and robust incentives for truthful/reliable labeling and their interplay with aggregation: Prelec (Bayesian Truth Serum, Science 2004); Miller, Resnick, Zeckhauser (JME 2005); Jurca & Faltings; Witkowski & Parkes (peer prediction variants); Radanovic & Faltings (robust peer prediction); Shnayder et al. (peer prediction with heterogeneous signals); Gao, Wright, & Leyton-Brown/Zhou (incentivizing reliable evaluations); Shah & Zhou (iterative/double-or-nothing peer prediction). Also work combining incentive design with quality-estimation/aggregation or task allocation (e.g., Ho & Vaughan; Kamar et al.; Frongillo & Witkowski). Position your results as orthogonal limits that apply regardless of incentive scheme, and discuss how incentives that improve effective worker reliability shift the budget–fidelity frontier.

Quality
- The technical claims appear sound and well-grounded in standard separation theorems. The comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS is illuminating. For kIC, clearly state the scope: non-adaptive querying with SHC and oracle skill detection in the bound. It would help to briefly justify modeling choices (e.g., independence across workers/tasks; MSC vs. SHC) and to discuss robustness if these are violated.

Clarity
- Reduce notation load where possible and provide a running example (binary labeling on AMT) alongside formal statements.
- Restate Theorems 2–3 in plain language and give one numerical worked example.
- Expand the end of Section 3 with a bulleted “what this means for practitioners,” and add a short concluding section summarizing design rules.

Originality
- While rate–distortion for human computation has been contemplated, this paper provides a clean and explicit set of budget–fidelity limits for commonly used worker models and a concrete query code (kIC). The SL-UK vs. SL-CS contrast and the kIC necessary conditions are novel and useful.

Significance
- Provides a theoretical envelope to benchmark existing and future aggregation schemes, supports principled budget planning, and motivates query design with structural error-correction (e.g., kIC). Likely to influence both theory and practice.

Specific actionable comments
- Restate results in application terms, as noted above; add a concluding takeaway section.
- Related work: add citations on incentives/peer prediction and works combining incentives with aggregation/task allocation.
- Clarify terminology: “crowdsourcer” vs. “taskmaster” (see suggestion above).
- Figure 2: The caption should fully specify axes (x: queries per item R; y: target error ε), worker model parameters (q, M), and which curves correspond to Corollary 1 vs. Theorem 4 for each k. Consider separate panels per k or use distinct linestyles and a legend; annotate the feasible/infeasible regions.
- Lines 275–276 (“In case...”) are hard to parse. Suggested rephrase: “For rates above the bound in Corollary 1, there exists some coding scheme achieving error at most ε. For rates below the bound in Theorem 4, no non-adaptive kIC can achieve ε. Between these bounds, feasibility depends on the specific code and worker mix.”
- Minor copyedits:
  - Line 84: “is reviewed in” → “are reviewed in.”
  - Line 122: “A A(X1,X2)” → “A(X1, X2).”
- Small consistency checks: define N (labels) vs. M (response options) once and stick to it; briefly explain HN(·) and HM(·) on first use; ensure that pmax and constraints on ε are explained.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, general theoretical framework with clear design implications.
- Useful limits that can guide practice and benchmark algorithms.
- Concrete, analyzable query design (kIC) illustrating principles.

Arguments against acceptance
- Accessibility: heavy reliance on information-theoretic formalism; practical takeaways need to be more prominent.
- Limited empirical validation; only partial treatment of adaptive querying and richer worker/task models.

Overall, I find the model and insights interesting and potentially useful, even though some technical details are dense. With improvements to accessibility, clearer practitioner guidance, and expanded related work on incentives, this would be a strong contribution.
