**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems. The authors model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and use rate-distortion theory and channel coding to derive bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off. They consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The paper also introduces a novel query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyzes its performance.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work in crowdsourcing, information theory, and channel coding. The authors draw inspiration from the work of Dawid and Skene (1980) on maximum likelihood estimation of observer error-rates using the EM algorithm. They also reference recent work on crowdsourcing, such as the paper by Zhang et al. (2015) on spectral methods meet EM. The paper's use of rate-distortion theory and channel coding is novel in the context of crowdsourcing.

**Strengths**

1. **Sound derivation**: The paper's derivation of the information-theoretic budget-fidelity bound is sound and well-explained.
2. **Novel application**: The application of rate-distortion theory and channel coding to crowdsourcing is novel and potentially meaningful.
3. **Introduction of k-ary incidence coding**: The paper introduces a new query scheme, kIC, which is analyzed in detail.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Loose bound**: The derived bound may be very loose, which limits its practical value.
2. **Inappropriateness of channel-coding-style bounds**: The paper's use of channel-coding-style bounds may be inappropriate for crowdsourcing, as it does not account for the constraints imposed by the crowdsourcing platform.
3. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper does not compare the derived information-theoretic bound to state-of-the-art practical methods, which makes it difficult to assess its practical value.
4. **Minor errors and lack of elaboration**: There are minor errors and lack of elaboration in some parts of the paper, such as the proof of Theorem 4.

**Originality**

The paper's application of rate-distortion theory and channel coding to crowdsourcing is original, but the derivation is straightforward. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is also novel.

**Significance**

The paper's contribution to the field of crowdsourcing is potentially significant, as it provides a new framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems. However, the practical value of the derived bound is questionable due to its potential looseness.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, but some parts of the proof could be elaborated further.

**Quality**

The paper's quality is good, but it could be improved by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above.
