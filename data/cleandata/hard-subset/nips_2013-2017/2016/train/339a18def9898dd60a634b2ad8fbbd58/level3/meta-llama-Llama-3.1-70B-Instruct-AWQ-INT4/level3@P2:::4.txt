This paper explores the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing by mapping the problem to joint source-channel coding and deriving information-theoretic lower bounds. The authors' use of rate-distortion framing is interesting and potentially inspiring for future work. However, there are several concerns and areas for improvement.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a communication system model for crowdsourcing, which enables the analysis of fundamental performance limits. The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity in both scenarios.

One of the concerns is that the model merges worker skill and reliability into a single channel, losing the distinction between skill/rating and reporting noise. This oversimplification may not accurately capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. Additionally, the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, which is unrealistic given that workers often have known ratings and heterogeneous capabilities.

The channel models for worker unreliability, such as the M-ary symmetric channel model (MSC) and the spammer-hammer channel model (SHC), may not realistically capture adversarial or informed malicious workers. The authors should clarify and justify the realism of the assumed worker/channel models.

The paper also analyzes a specific scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compares it to the information-theoretic lower bound for a particular channel model. However, the numerical results and explanations are unclear. The labeling of curves in Figure 2 should be improved, and the authors should explicitly state what k=1 corresponds to.

From a methodological perspective, the assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is stated late in the paper (page 6, line 246) and should be declared earlier. Additionally, the authors misuse 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' without numbering; they should use lowercase or add numbers for clarity.

In terms of the paper's strengths, the authors provide a comprehensive overview of the related work in crowdsourcing and information theory. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. The use of figures and tables is helpful in illustrating the concepts and results.

To improve the paper, the authors should address the concerns mentioned above and provide more clarity on the numerical results. They should also consider adding more realistic worker/channel models and exploring the implications of their results on real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel approach to analyzing the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing.
* The use of rate-distortion framing is interesting and potentially inspiring for future work.
* The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model oversimplifies the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios by merging worker skill and reliability into a single channel.
* The channel models for worker unreliability may not realistically capture adversarial or informed malicious workers.
* The numerical results and explanations are unclear.
* The assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is stated late in the paper and should be declared earlier.

Rating: 6/10

Recommendation: The paper has some interesting ideas, but it needs significant revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. The authors should provide more clarity on the numerical results, add more realistic worker/channel models, and explore the implications of their results on real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
