- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem to derive fundamental budget (queries/item) vs. fidelity (distortion) limits.  
- Worker channels modeled as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel (SHC); query design = encoding, inference = decoding.  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds (converses) on minimum rate for target error, distinguishing unknown (SL-UK) vs. known (SL-CS) worker skills.  
- Proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) that queries k items jointly and rejects logically inconsistent responses, giving inherent error-correction.  
- For SHC, derives a necessary rate condition to achieve given error and a simple pricing heuristic across k.  
- Connects to crowdsourcing/aggregation literature (majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM, spectral methods, Karger–Oh–Shah sample-complexity) and frames limits independently of specific algorithms.  
- Strength: principled information-theoretic framework mapping budget to rate and fidelity to distortion with interpretable lower bounds.  
- Strength: clear quantification of value of skill knowledge via SL-UK vs. SL-CS distinction (average channel vs. average of capacities).  
- Strength: kIC is intuitive and formalizes error-correction via invalid-response elimination.  
- Strength: results serve as benchmarks and inform pricing/effort tradeoffs by query arity.  
- Weakness: unrealistic assumptions (iid items, known source distribution, MSC symmetry, non-adaptivity, and oracle SL-CS regime).  
- Weakness: mainly converses; lacks constructive/achievability schemes and finite-length analyses.  
- Weakness: limited empirical validation; no simulations comparing kIC to DS, spectral-EM, majority voting under matched budgets.  
- Weakness: pricing heuristic derived under restrictive SHC ignores cognitive load and platform-specific costs.  
- Weakness: clarity and notation issues plus missing reference details hinder assessment and reproducibility.  
- Suggestion 1: compare derived limits to sample-complexity bounds (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah) and discuss scaling differences with error and worker quality.  
- Suggestion 2: provide at least one achievability result for kIC (e.g., concrete decoder) to show gap to converses and practical utility.  
- Suggestion 3: include simulations comparing kIC (k=2,3,4) to single-item queries with DS/spectral-EM across MSC and SHC, varying class imbalance and N>2.  
- Suggestion 4: discuss adaptivity effects (e.g., requerying uncertain items or selecting hammers) on rate–distortion limits, even qualitatively.  
- Suggestion 5: clarify modeling choices and notation (distinguish N vs. M, handle cluster-label permutation, state assumptions on P(B(X))).  
- Suggestion 6: comment on worker-model robustness (adversarial spammers, class-dependent confusion matrices) and extensions to worst/average-case analyses.  
- Suggestion 7: strengthen pricing by referencing HCI literature or user studies on cognitive cost scaling with k and regime where larger k hurts.  
- Quality: technical development is sound using standard information-theoretic tools, but deferred proofs and composite-channel nuances need care; lack of achievability/empirics limits completeness.  
- Clarity: generally organized with helpful diagrams, but notation overload, typos, and missing details warrant a notation table and explicit assumptions before theorems.  
- Originality: novel rate–distortion framing for crowdsourcing and an interesting coding view of multi-item queries (kIC), though related to existing incidence-clustering work.  
- Significance: conceptually useful as a benchmark for aggregation and query design; practical impact requires constructive schemes and empirical validation.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled novel perspective, bridges information theory and crowdsourcing, and provides actionable insights via kIC analysis.  
- Cons for acceptance: lacks empirical/constructive evidence, realism/generalizability of assumptions is limited, and presentation/related-work ties need improvement.  
- Reviewer confidence: low–medium (outside primary area).
