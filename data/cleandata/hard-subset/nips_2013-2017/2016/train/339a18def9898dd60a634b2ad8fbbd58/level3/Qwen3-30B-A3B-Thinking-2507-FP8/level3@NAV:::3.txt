

This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing aggregation, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding problem to derive fundamental budget-fidelity limits. The authors establish two key theorems (2 and 3) that characterize the minimum query rate required to achieve a target error probability under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill levels, respectively. The paper further introduces k-ary incidence coding as a query scheme and analyzes its performance with spammer-hammer channel models.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretically rigorous approach to crowdsourcing, which provides fundamental performance bounds previously unattained in the literature. The information-theoretic perspective offers valuable insights into the trade-offs between budget constraints and fidelity, potentially guiding future crowdsourcing system design. The k-ary incidence coding analysis is particularly interesting as it demonstrates how query structure can improve performance through error correction capabilities.

However, the paper's accessibility is a significant weakness. The heavy reliance on information-theoretic terminology (channel coding, rate-distortion, etc.) makes it difficult for non-specialists to grasp the practical implications. The authors should restate key results in crowdsourcing terms (e.g., "minimum number of workers per item" instead of "rate-distortion function") and substantially expand Section 3 to highlight high-level intuition and practical takeaways. The current conclusion lacks a clear summary of actionable insights for practitioners.

The manuscript also has several issues requiring attention: 1) The distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" is confusing and needs clarification; 2) Minor copyediting errors (line 84, line 122 typo); 3) Lines 275-276 require rephrasing for clarity; 4) Figure 2 is unclear without a detailed caption; 5) The related work section should be expanded to include recent advances in robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing, particularly work combining incentives with aggregation (e.g., [1, 6, 10]).

While the theoretical contribution is strong and original, the paper's value would be significantly enhanced by making the results more accessible to the broader crowdsourcing community. The authors should focus on translating their elegant information-theoretic results into practical design principles for crowdsourcing practitioners. With these revisions, this work could make an important contribution to the field.
