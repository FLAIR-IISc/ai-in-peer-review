This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework to model crowdsourcing and derive fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget and fidelity. The authors consider a communication system model for crowdsourcing, where the taskmaster breaks down the project into small batches of tasks and assigns them to workers with imperfect skill levels. The crowdsourcer then collects and analyzes the results to infer the correct label for each task.

The paper relates to previous work on crowdsourcing, including optimized design and analysis of aggregation and inference schemes, and redundant task assignment. However, the authors take a novel approach by modeling the crowdsourcing problem in an information-theoretic setting, which allows them to derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a target error probability.

The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). They derive information-theoretic bounds for both scenarios, which serve as ultimate performance bounds for any crowdsourcing system. The bounds are oblivious to the type of code used and provide a fundamental limit on the minimum number of queries per task required to achieve a target error probability.

The paper also introduces a novel query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which poses a query as a set of k items and inquires the workers to identify those with the same label. The authors derive a necessary condition on the minimum rate for kIC with a spammer-hammer worker pool and provide numerical results to illustrate the performance of kIC.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing, its derivation of fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget and fidelity, and its introduction of a new query scheme. The paper is well-organized and well-written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with information theory.

However, the paper lacks experiments or simulations to compare practical schemes (e.g., Amazon Mechanical Turk) to the theoretical limits. Additionally, some sections, such as Section 2.4 and Section 3.1, could be shortened or removed to make the paper more concise. The reviewer also recommends adding proof sketches for the theorems instead of relegating them to the appendix.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing by providing a fundamental understanding of the trade-off between budget and fidelity. The approach is novel and practically useful for designers and pricing, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.
