This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies fundamental budget–fidelity limits. Items to be labeled are treated as a source B(X); workers are modeled as noisy channels, primarily via an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). The crowdsourcing budget is mapped to the code rate (queries per item), allowing the use of rate–distortion and capacity tools. Two scenarios are analyzed: (i) unknown worker skill levels to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). For iid sources, Hamming distortion, and non-adaptive schemes, the authors obtain lower bounds on the rate needed to achieve target error, with separate expressions for SL-UK and SL-CS that cleanly show the penalty of not knowing worker skills. They further introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query asks workers to cluster k items; invalid response patterns are disallowed, imparting error-correction structure. For SHC, they derive a lower bound on the error under an oracle decoder and relate k, rate, error, and hammer probability q. They also provide a simple pricing rule-of-thumb comparing kIC variants.

Relation to prior work: Most NIPS work on crowdsourcing emphasizes aggregation/inference (majority voting vs Dawid–Skene EM, spectral-EM hybrids, convex formulations for pairwise comparisons, budget-optimal assignment, and bandit survey models). This paper differs by asking for information-theoretic limits independent of a specific algorithm, akin to the “separation” perspective in communication. The kIC proposal connects to pairwise/triplet comparisons used in clustering and graph-based methods but is analyzed here through channel coding lenses. To my knowledge, NIPS has had few papers giving general-rate lower bounds for crowdsourcing under such worker channel models; the explicit comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS is particularly insightful.

Quality:
- The mapping to joint source–channel coding is sound, and the use of separation to derive R(D) vs C-type conditions is appropriate for iid sources and memoryless channels. Theorems 2 and 3 crisply quantify rate requirements under MSC mixtures in SL-UK vs SL-CS, correctly distinguishing average-channel capacity vs average of capacities. The kIC analysis under SHC provides a meaningful, oracle-based necessary condition that improves with k and q.
- Assumptions are strong: iid sources, memoryless independent workers, non-adaptive assignment, and either completely unknown or perfectly known skill levels. The SL-CS assumption subsumes perfect skill estimation, which is idealized; still, it makes a useful benchmark.
- The kIC results are restricted to binary labels, SHC, and non-adaptive schemes; the numerical section compares to the information-theoretic bound but lacks empirical validation on finite datasets or against standard inference methods.
- Proofs are relegated to appendices; the main text sketches seem consistent with standard arguments. Some notation could be tightened (see clarity).

Clarity:
- Generally well organized with a clear modeling section and a clean transition to bounds and kIC. A few concrete suggestions:
  - Unify notation: N is the number of labels; M is the number of response options in the channel. Make early and explicit that in kIC, the effective channel alphabet differs from raw 2^{k choose 2} by invalidating impossible configurations.
  - Define H_N(·) and H_M(·) inline when first used; state p_max explicitly and explain the regime where the bound becomes vacuous.
  - The mapping from budget to rate is central; Section 2.3’s caution about heterogeneous query costs is useful—summarize the key pricing implications in the main text rather than in an appendix only.
  - The statement that kIC recovers clusters “up to label permutations” deserves an explicit distortion definition consistent with clustering equivalence classes.
  - Minor editorial issues (typos and figure references) can be cleaned up.

Originality:
- The information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing budget–fidelity tradeoffs and the explicit R_min expressions under MSC with SL-UK vs SL-CS appear novel and insightful. The kIC coding view extends known pairwise/triplet query paradigms by emphasizing redundancy from invalid patterns and tying it to rate lower bounds under SHC. While elements like DS/EM and pairwise clustering are known, the unifying rate–distortion perspective and the derived limits are original and useful.

Significance:
- The results offer benchmarks for any algorithm, clarifying what budgets are fundamentally necessary to achieve target accuracy under specified worker models. The SL-UK vs SL-CS gap quantifies the value of skill estimation and motivates investing in better worker modeling. The kIC analysis offers design guidance on choosing k and a simple pricing heuristic, which could be practical. Impact will depend on how closely real platforms fit the assumptions; nonetheless, the paper can influence both theoretical and system design work.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical contribution establishing budget–fidelity limits under standard worker channel models.
- Insightful comparison between unknown and known skill scenarios.
- kIC provides a concrete coding scheme with intuitive error-correcting structure and interpretable bounds.
- Bridges disparate crowdsourcing lines (aggregation, pairwise comparisons) under an information-theoretic umbrella.

Cons:
- Strong assumptions (iid items, memoryless independent workers, non-adaptive assignment, perfect skill knowledge in SL-CS).
- Limited scope of kIC analysis (binary labels, SHC, oracle decoding) and absence of empirical validation.
- Practical aspects like variable query costs and task difficulty are largely deferred; feedforward/adaptive designs are noted but not developed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add experiments comparing observed error vs rate for DS/EM, majority voting, and kIC on synthetic and real datasets; examine finite-sample deviations from asymptotic bounds.
- Relax assumptions: include models with task difficulty (input-dependent channels) and partial/estimated skill levels; quantify the SL-UK to SL-CS interpolation.
- Extend kIC beyond N=2 and beyond SHC; discuss decoding complexity and assignment strategies for non-adaptive vs adaptive variants.
- Clarify the distortion notion for clustering up to permutations and discuss how the bounds translate under that metric.

Overall assessment: A theoretically strong and original paper that frames crowdsourcing through rate–distortion and provides clean lower bounds and design insights. While the assumptions limit immediate practicality and the empirical component is light, the contribution is valuable as a benchmark and may spur further work on adaptive designs and more realistic worker models. I lean toward acceptance if the committee values foundational theory; otherwise, a borderline accept pending strengthened empirical validation and clarifications.
