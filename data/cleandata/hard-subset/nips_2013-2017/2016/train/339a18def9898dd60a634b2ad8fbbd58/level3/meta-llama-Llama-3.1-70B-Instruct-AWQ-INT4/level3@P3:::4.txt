This paper presents a novel approach to crowdsourcing by mapping it to joint source-channel coding and deriving a budget-fidelity trade-off via information-theoretic lower bounds. The authors model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyze it within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework. The goal is to identify the ultimate fidelity that can be achieved by any form of query from the crowd and any decoding (inference) algorithm with a given budget.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of fundamental limits on the crowdsourcing rate for a given fidelity. The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They show that in the SL-UK case, the crowdsourcing system provides an overall work quality (capacity) of an average worker, whereas in the SL-CS case, the system provides an overall work quality that pertains to the average of the work quality of the workers.

The authors also present a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and analyze its performance. They derive a lower bound on the performance of kIC with a spammer-hammer worker pool and show that the coding theoretic lower bounds for kIC depend on k, q, and fidelity, and improve as k and q grow.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to crowdsourcing, its rigorous analysis, and its derivation of fundamental limits on the crowdsourcing rate. The authors' use of information-theoretic tools to analyze crowdsourcing is particularly interesting and potentially inspiring for future work.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. One concern is that the model merges worker skill and reliability into a single channel, losing the distinction between skill/rating and reporting noise. This simplification may not accurately capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. Another concern is that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, which is unrealistic given that workers often have known ratings and heterogeneous capabilities.

Additionally, the channel models for worker unreliability may not realistically capture adversarial or informed malicious workers. The authors should clarify and justify the realism of the assumed worker/channel models.

The numerical results and explanations are unclear, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 should be improved. The authors should also explicitly state what k=1 corresponds to.

A methodological issue is the assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)), which is stated late in the paper (page 6, line 246) and should be declared earlier.

Minor corrections include fixing figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b, 1.b → 1.c).

The writing and style could be improved by using lowercase or adding numbers for clarity when referring to theorems, lemmas, and corollaries.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. However, the authors could be more careful in evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper's clarity could be improved by reorganizing some sections and providing more explanations for the technical terms and concepts.

The originality of the paper is high, as it presents a novel approach to crowdsourcing and derives new results. The related work is adequately referenced, and the authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to crowdsourcing and derives new results.
* The authors use information-theoretic tools to analyze crowdsourcing, which is a fresh and interesting perspective.
* The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model simplifications may not accurately capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
* The paper could benefit from more explanations and justifications for the assumed worker/channel models.
* The numerical results and explanations are unclear, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 should be improved.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
