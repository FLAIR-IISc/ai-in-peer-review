- Novel JSCC framing: models crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding with worker skill as random channel state, a significant conceptual shift from algorithmic approaches.
- Main contributions: derives asymptotic query-rate limits for unknown/known skill scenarios (Theorems 2 and 3) and analyzes k-ary incidence coding with a lower bound under the spammer-hammer model (Theorem 4).
- Strength — conceptual novelty: connects crowdsourcing to established information-theoretic results in a well-executed framework.
- Strength — theoretical limits: provides valuable asymptotic fundamental limits that can guide future algorithm design.
- Strength — exposition: paper is clearly written and accessible to both information theorists and ML researchers.
- Strength — concrete scheme analysis: introduction and analysis of k-ary incidence coding give practical insights into query design.
- Major weakness — technical gap: authors apply source-channel separation without justifying it when channel state is unknown to the encoder, a critical assumption needing proof or citation.
- Weakness — modeling omission: encoder/decoder ignore original items as side information, which in practice (e.g., image labeling) could materially improve performance.
- Weakness — misleading wording: statement that “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is misleading and should clarify that the bound value decreases (implying better performance).
- Weakness — missing finite-blocklength analysis: lacks nonasymptotic converses; should cite and relate to Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC.
- Quality assessment: derivations are largely sound but foundational separation assumption undermines immediate validity.
- Clarity assessment: exceptionally clear and well-organized, apart from the noted misleading phrasing.
- Originality assessment: highly original application of information theory to crowdsourcing, opening a new research direction.
- Significance assessment: asymptotic insights are important but practical impact limited until the separation assumption and finite-blocklength issues are addressed.
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, solid presentation, useful asymptotic limits, promising foundation for future work.
- Rejection arguments: critical unaddressed separation assumption, misleading phrasing, and absent finite-blocklength analysis.
- Overall: strong and promising paper that requires revision to justify source-channel separation and address modeling/finite-blocklength concerns before publication.
