This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The central idea is to model the crowdsourcing process as a communication system: the true labels are the source, the workers are noisy channels (specifically, symmetric memoryless or spammer/hammer channels), and the query/inference mechanism is a joint source-channel coding scheme. This formulation allows the authors to map the crowdsourcing budget to the code rate and analyze the ultimate trade-off between labeling fidelity (distortion) and budget. While prior work at NIPS has focused on probabilistic models and iterative algorithms for label aggregation (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.), this work stands out by using rate-distortion theory to derive fundamental performance bounds, shifting the focus from algorithm design to theoretical limits.

The primary strength of this paper is its originality and potential significance. By framing crowdsourcing as a coding problem, the authors provide a powerful and principled lens for understanding the budget-fidelity trade-off, independent of any specific aggregation algorithm. The derived theorems on the minimum required queries offer clear, actionable insights into system design, particularly on the value of estimating worker skill levels. The proposed "k-incidence coding" (kIC) is a well-motivated and concrete example of a practical query scheme that fits neatly into their framework, and its analysis effectively grounds the abstract theory. The paper is technically sound and the theoretical claims appear well-supported by the proofs.

The main weaknesses stem from the simplifying assumptions required by the theoretical model. The worker models (MSC and SHC), while standard in coding theory, may not fully capture the nuances of human annotator behavior, such as task-dependent error rates or more complex adversarial strategies. The analysis is also asymptotic, and its applicability to finite, practical crowdsourcing scenarios could be further explored. While this is a theoretical contribution, the paper would be strengthened by simulations or a small-scale empirical study to demonstrate how the theoretical bounds relate to the performance of existing algorithms in practice. Furthermore, while I found the paper's approach highly interesting and the presentation clear, it is somewhat outside my core area of expertise, so I cannot comment deeply on the novelty of the specific information-theoretic techniques used.

In summary, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that introduces a valuable new perspective to the crowdsourcing literature. Despite the limitations of its theoretical assumptions, it successfully establishes fundamental performance limits that can guide future research and system design.
