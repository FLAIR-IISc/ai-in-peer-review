- Models crowdsourcing with an information-theoretic rate–distortion framework to derive lower bounds on queries-per-item for target error probability.
- Treats workers as independent parallel channels, using M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel SHC(q) error models.
- Focuses on nonadaptive task assignment and analyzes two knowledge regimes: SL-UK (unknown worker skills) and SL-CS (crowdsourcer knows skills; taskmaster assigns equal load).
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), giving a necessary minimum-rate condition for SHC(q) pools and a practical pricing rule-of-thumb.
- Provides converse-style fundamental limits complementary to prior algorithmic work (majority voting, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM, adaptive/bandit methods).
- Theoretical development is sound, with clean lower-bound statements clarifying the impact of skill knowledge on capacity.
- Assumptions (independent, memoryless workers; nonadaptive assignments; equal load) are clearly stated but restrictive.
- Paper is generally well organized and well written.
- Sec. 2.4 and especially Sec. 3.1 are overly long for the venue; recommend shortening and replacing with proof sketches in the main text.
- The information-theoretic formulation and MSC/SHC specialization are novel and distinct from prior algorithmic lines.
- Results are significant and actionable for system designers and pricing, showing dependence on q, k, and M.
- Lacks empirical validation or simulations comparing practical schemes (majority vote, DS, spectral/EM) to the derived limits.
- Limited discussion of adaptivity and how adaptive schemes relate to nonadaptive bounds.
- Pros: clear principled limits for SL-UK/SL-CS, insightful kIC bounds, novel practical framing.
- Cons: no experiments/simulations, restrictive nonadaptive/equal-assignment assumptions, main text lacks proof sketches.
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance if authors add proof sketches and focused simulations illustrating gap-to-limit.
