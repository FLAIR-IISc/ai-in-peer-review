- Paper models crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding (JSCC), mapping labels to a source, taskmaster to encoder, workers to noisy channels with skill as channel state, and crowdsourcer to decoder.
- Main contributions: asymptotic budget-fidelity fundamental limits via source-channel separation (Theorems 2–3) for unknown and known worker skills, and analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) with a spammer-hammer model.
- Major technical weakness: application of the source-channel separation theorem is unjustified because the channel has random states (worker skills) unknown to the encoder; separation may not hold without proof or citation.
- Authors must justify using average-channel capacities (SL-UK) or averaged capacities (SL-CS) by citing/deriving results for channels with state information or compound channels.
- kIC analysis is a solid, technically sound contribution that provides a concrete performance bound for a practical query scheme.
- Results are purely asymptotic (L → ∞); lack of finite-blocklength analysis limits practical relevance and tightness for real datasets.
- Recommend discussing or citing finite-blocklength JSCC results (e.g., Kostina & Verdú) to address practical performance.
- Minor clarity issue: wording in Section 4.2/Fig.2 misleading about “bounds improving” with k and q; should clarify that achievable performance (not bound tightness) improves.
- Originality is high: the JSCC framing of crowdsourcing and the kIC scheme appear novel and provide a new research direction.
- Significance: potentially important for establishing absolute limits and guiding algorithm design, but currently constrained by modeling assumptions and technical gaps.
- Key modeling limitation: ignoring item features X and only modeling labels B(X) omits useful side information (e.g., item difficulty) and may render bounds less applicable to real scenarios.
- Paper is well-written and organized, with a clear analogy between crowdsourcing and communication systems.
- Arguments for acceptance: originality, clear exposition, and concrete kIC analysis.
- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved technical flaw in applying separation theorem, omission of item-side information, and lack of finite-blocklength discussion.
- Conclusion: promising and creative work but a critical technical issue (separation theorem application) must be resolved before the main results can be considered sound.
