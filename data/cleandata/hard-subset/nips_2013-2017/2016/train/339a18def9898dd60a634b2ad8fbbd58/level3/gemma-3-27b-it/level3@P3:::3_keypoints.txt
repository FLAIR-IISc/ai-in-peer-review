- Novel approach: Models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, offering a fresh information-theoretic perspective.  
- Theoretical contributions: Derives fundamental limits on fidelity given budget constraints with mathematically rigorous results.  
- Novel scheme: Proposes k-ary incidence coding and associated pricing strategies as an interesting query mechanism.  
- Potential impact: Insights could improve efficiency and reliability of crowdsourcing systems.  
- Accessibility problem: Heavy use of information-theoretic terminology makes the paper hard to follow for non-experts.  
- Translate results: Many statements should be restated in crowdsourcing/application terms (e.g., tasks needed for target accuracy).  
- Practical implications: Sections (notably end of Sec. 3) need expansion to explain how Theorems 2 and 3 inform practitioner design choices.  
- Concluding takeaway: Add a clear summary of key insights for non-information-theory audiences.  
- Terminology confusion: Clarify the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" and use terms consistently.  
- Minor technical issues: Fix typos and dense phrasing (e.g., line 84, line 122, lines 275–276) and rephrase hard-to-parse sentences.  
- Figure clarity: Improve Figure 2 caption and visualization to explain axes, curves, and the tradeoffs being illustrated.  
- Related work gap: Discuss literature on incentives and mechanism design (worker motivation, spam, quality control) and combine with aggregation techniques.  
- Add references: Include relevant works (e.g., bandit-based task allocation, incentivizing truthful reporting such as Abraham et al., COLT 2016).  
- Acceptance pros: Novel framing, theoretical rigor, potential significance, and an interesting query scheme support acceptance.  
- Acceptance cons: Poor accessibility, limited practical guidance, incomplete related work, and clarity issues argue against acceptance without revision.  
- Recommendation: Promising paper but requires major revisions on clarity, accessibility, and related work before strong acceptance.
