- Introduces an information-theoretic framework to model and analyze crowdsourcing, applying rate-distortion and channel coding concepts.  
- Derives a fundamental budget-fidelity bound for label crowdsourcing as the paper's main contribution.  
- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding system with encoding = query design and decoding = inference.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and known skill levels (SL-CS), with Theorems 2 and 3 giving minimum queries per item.  
- Studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and shows it has error-correction capability; provides a lower bound under a spammer-hammer worker pool.  
- Provides numerical results illustrating the information-theoretic limit.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and accessible to a broad audience.  
- Novelty: first application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing, making a significant conceptual contribution.  
- Concern: the derived budget-fidelity bound may be straightforward and potentially very loose.  
- Concern: no comparison with state-of-the-art practical crowdsourcing methods to assess empirical significance.  
- Limitation: assumes the taskmaster can freely design input sequence u^n, which may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Limitation: assumes iid worker responses, an assumption that may not hold in real crowdsourcing settings.  
- Suggestion: include comparisons with practical methods and deeper discussion of related work to clarify novelty.  
- Suggestion: discuss limitations more clearly and provide more detailed explanation of the information-theoretic tools used.
