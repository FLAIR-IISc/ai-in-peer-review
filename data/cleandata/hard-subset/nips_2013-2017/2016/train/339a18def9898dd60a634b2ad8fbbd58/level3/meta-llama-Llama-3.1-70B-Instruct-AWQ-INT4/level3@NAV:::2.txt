This paper presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item, providing a promising first step towards understanding the theoretical bounds of crowdsourcing.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a communication system model for crowdsourcing, where labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, workers as channel uses with random skill-level states, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. The authors apply standard source-channel separation results to obtain Theorems 2 and 3, which provide asymptotic query-rate limits for two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS).

The paper also presents a lower bound on the error probability of "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model. The results show that the coding theoretic lower bounds for kIC depend on k, q, and fidelity, and improve as k and q grow.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and results. The use of information-theoretic tools to analyze crowdsourcing is a novel and promising approach.

However, there are some technical concerns that need to be addressed. Specifically, the authors need to justify or cite that source-channel separation holds when the channel has a random state unknown to the encoder (and possibly known to the decoder). Additionally, the assumption that the encoder/decoder only see labels and ignore original items as side information may not be realistic, and the authors should consider the impact of this assumption on their results.

Furthermore, the wording in Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2 is misleading, and the authors should clarify that the lower bound value decreases (implying potential performance gain), not that the bound itself is tightened without an upper bound for comparison.

Overall, this paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory. With some revisions to address the technical concerns, this paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. Novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem.
2. Derivation of asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item.
3. Well-written and clear explanation of the model and results.
4. Promising first step towards understanding the theoretical bounds of crowdsourcing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. Technical concerns regarding source-channel separation and the assumption of ignoring original items as side information.
2. Misleading wording in Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2.
3. Lack of consideration of non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses.
