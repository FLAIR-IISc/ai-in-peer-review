- Paper models crowdsourcing as a communication problem using rate-distortion and channel coding, deriving budget-vs-fidelity bounds and analyzing k-ary incidence coding (kIC).
- Theorems 2 and 3 characterize bounds for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill scenarios.
- Strength: Theoretical framework is well-motivated and connects crowdsourcing to information theory.
- Strength: Derivations of main theorems are technically sound and correctly relate to rate-distortion theory.
- Strength: Analysis of kIC provides a concrete query scheme that may outperform simple repetition.
- Strength: Correctly emphasizes the human-in-the-loop challenge that workers cannot be reused like communication channels.
- Weakness: Applying channel coding is questionable because reusing the same worker violates iid assumptions, undermining bound tightness.
- Weakness: No comparison to state-of-the-art practical methods (Dawid-Skene, majority voting, recent EM approaches), so practical relevance is unclear.
- Weakness: Implementation constraints are unaddressed—paper doesn’t show which encoder mappings are implementable beyond repetition coding.
- Weakness: Minor technical issues—Theorem 4 needs more detail, relationship between ε_S and ̂ unclear, and H_M(E(ε)) notation is not well-explained or cited.
- Originality: Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing but conceptually straightforward and not a major theoretical advance.
- Significance: Identifies an important problem but significance is reduced by questionable applicability and unclear practical value.
- Quality: Technically sound derivations but weakened by failure to address fundamental differences between communication channels and workers.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, but key technical/implementation points lack sufficient clarification.
- Arguments for acceptance: Establishes an interesting information-theoretic connection, offers a conceptual foundation, and presents a concrete kIC example.
- Arguments against acceptance: Framework misapplied to crowdsourcing, bounds likely not reflective of practice, lacks empirical comparison, and offers limited novelty.
- Recommendation: Reject—major revisions needed to address worker-versus-channel differences, provide comparisons to existing methods, and demonstrate practical relevance.
