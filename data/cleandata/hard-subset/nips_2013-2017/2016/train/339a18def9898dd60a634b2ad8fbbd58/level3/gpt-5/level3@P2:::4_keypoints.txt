- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and uses rate–distortion theory to derive budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Models workers as noisy channels (M-ary symmetric and spammer–hammer) and maps items to a discrete source B(X).
- Theorems 2–3 provide lower bounds on queries per item for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills.
- Proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a non-adaptive query scheme with built-in consistency checks and error-correction effects.
- Analyzes kIC under a SHC(q) worker model and derives necessary rate conditions, comparing to an information-theoretic benchmark.
- Strength: elegant rate–distortion framing that clarifies budget–fidelity vocabulary.
- Strength: general lower bounds linking minimum rate to source entropy and channel quality, showing benefit of knowing skills.
- Strength: concrete code family (kIC) connecting coding ideas to practical query design and pricing.
- Strength: potential impact as an IT benchmark guiding algorithm and task-allocation design.
- Weakness: worker model conflates skill and response noise, ignoring per-worker confusion matrices and systematic errors.
- Weakness: assumes homogeneous query allocation, unrealistic for platforms with reputation and adaptive routing.
- Weakness: SHC(q) models random spammers but not adversarial or Byzantine behavior; realism of channel choice needs justification.
- Technical clarity issue: assumption that crowdsourcer knows P(B(X)) is stated late and should be moved earlier with discussion on estimation and misspecification sensitivity.
- Notation issue: inconsistent use of N (source labels) and M (response options); need to standardize and state M≠N cases.
- Proof clarity: achievability relies on separation and converse uses averaging/conditioning—sketches belong in main text and finite-blocklength effects should be discussed.
- kIC clarity: explicitly state k=1 corresponds to single-item labeling to aid interpretation of figures.
- Figure 2 needs better labeling and explanation of the gap between kIC performance and the IT limit (suboptimality vs. bound looseness).
- Discuss realism of SHC's k-dependence (task fatigue, UI complexity) and validate the pricing heuristic π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 empirically.
- Quality note: results are information-theoretically sound but assumptions are strong and kIC bounds are necessary conditions under an oracle decoder.
- Clarity improvements needed: state distributional assumptions earlier, tighten notation, fix figure labels, and include short proof sketches.
- Originality: novel explicit rate–distortion formulation for crowdsourcing and comparison of SL-UK vs. SL-CS; kIC contributes a coding viewpoint.
- Significance: offers conceptual benchmarks with potential influence, but practical impact requires relaxing assumptions and empirical validation.
- Reasons to accept: elegant IT formalization, insight on value of skill knowledge, and concrete kIC analysis with pricing implications.
- Reasons to reject: strong/simplistic modeling assumptions, limited empirical validation, and notation/organization issues.
- Key questions: sensitivity of Theorems 2–3 to P(B(X)) misspecification, handling per-worker confusion matrices or adversaries, and empirical gap between kIC and the IT limit.
- Minor fixes: correct figure references, complete citation metadata, fix typos, and explicitly state k=1 in text and figures.
- Overall recommendation: borderline—solid theoretical contribution but needs stronger model justification, clearer exposition, and better empirical support.
