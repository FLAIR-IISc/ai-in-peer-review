- Paper models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem to derive budget–fidelity limits.  
- Main contribution: minimum queries per item needed for target fidelity based on source entropy, worker skills, and distortion.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme with error-correction analysis.  
- Builds on Dawid–Skene and majority voting literature but takes a fundamentally different, theoretical limits approach.  
- Technical quality: rigorous analysis with proofs in appendices and clear distinction between known/unknown worker-skill cases.  
- Experimental validation is limited; more empirical results are needed to complement theory.  
- Clarity issues: heavy information-theory jargon makes the paper hard to follow for crowdsourcing practitioners.  
- Terminology confusion: "crowdsourcer" vs. "taskmaster" used inconsistently and should be clarified.  
- Presentation problems: several sentences/sections are hard to parse, Figure 2 needs a better caption, and there are minor copyediting errors.  
- Originality: novel framing of crowdsourcing as source–channel coding and the introduction of kIC are original contributions.  
- Significance: provides useful fundamental benchmarks but lacks explicit practical takeaways for system designers.  
- Recommendation to authors: reframe results in crowdsourcing language and state queries-per-accuracy directly.  
- Recommendation to authors: expand practical insights, add a takeaway/conclusion section, and include related work on incentives/mechanism design.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound and interesting but needs clearer presentation and practical translation — weak accept with major revisions.
