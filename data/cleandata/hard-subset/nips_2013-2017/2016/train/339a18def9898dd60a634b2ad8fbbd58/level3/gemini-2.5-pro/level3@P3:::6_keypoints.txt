- Paper proposes an information-theoretic framework mapping crowdsourcing to a communication problem and derives rate-distortion bounds on queries versus fidelity.  
- Analysis covers worker-skill-unknown (SL-UK) and skill-known (SL-CS) scenarios and introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a practical encoding example.

- Novel theoretical contribution: applies rate-distortion and joint source-channel coding to crowdsourcing, offering first-principles performance limits.  
- Technical soundness: main bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) appear correctly derived using established information-theoretic tools.  
- Strong conceptual insight: elegant analogy mapping workers to noisy channels and query design to encoding.

- Encoding assumption is questionable: practical query designs for humans are far more constrained than channel codes, so channel-coding bounds may be loose or inapplicable.  
- Weak connection to practical codes: paper gives little guidance on implementable codebooks or how to design codes beyond the single kIC example.  
- Channel-use constraints ignored: re-querying the same worker or relying on many independent worker uses violates i.i.d. assumptions and differs from classic block-code settings.  
- No empirical comparison: lacks experiments against practical aggregation methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene, spectral methods) to quantify the gap between theory and practice.

- Suggest explicitly discussing practical limitations on allowable "codes" / query schemes for humans.  
- Recommend adding experimental evaluation comparing theoretical limits to state-of-the-art algorithms on benchmark or synthetic data.

- Minor clarification: in (8) specify minimization over P(Ẑ|Z) satisfying E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D_t.  
- Minor clarification: expand the brief proof of Theorem 4 in the appendix for readability.  
- Minor clarification: clarify sign/assumption in Theorem 4 regarding ln(1−q) and the relation between ε_S and ε̂.  
- Minor clarification: justify the H_M(E(ε)) term in (11)’s denominator (e.g., cite Jensen’s inequality or a standard channel-with-state result).

- Overall: original and promising theoretical work, but its practical significance is limited without addressing encoding constraints and providing empirical validation.
