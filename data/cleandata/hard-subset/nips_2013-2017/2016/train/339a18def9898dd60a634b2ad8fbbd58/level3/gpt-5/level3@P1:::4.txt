The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives fundamental budget–fidelity limits via an information-theoretic rate–distortion framing. The dataset labels B(X) are the “source”; worker responses are modeled as outputs of parallel noisy channels (workers), with two concrete channel families: an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). The main results give lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target average labeling error under two scenarios: unknown worker skill at the taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and worker skill known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The bounds follow from a source–channel separation argument: the required rate is R ≥ R*(D)/C, where R*(D) is the source rate–distortion and C is the effective per-query capacity of the crowd under the assumed worker channel model. The paper then introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), in which each query shows k items and asks for their incidence structure; because many incidence patterns are invalid, kIC implicitly has error-correcting capability. For SHC, the paper derives a necessary condition on the rate for kIC to achieve a given error and specializes the information-theoretic bound for comparison, along with a pricing “rule of thumb.”

Relation to prior work: The work is complementary to much of the NIPS literature focused on inference/aggregation algorithms under reliability heterogeneity (e.g., majority vote; Dawid–Skene EM; spectral/EM hybrids; Karger–Oh–Shah budget allocation; convex clustering with pairwise queries). Rather than proposing a new inference algorithm, the paper provides limits that any scheme must respect, akin to rate–distortion bounds in communications. This perspective is relatively rare in crowdsourcing and can inform the design of adaptive allocation and pricing mechanisms studied in the ML community.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: a principled mapping of crowdsourcing to rate–distortion with explicit roles for query rate (budget), worker reliability (channel), and task prior P(B(X)). The separation-based lower bounds are standard but useful benchmarks for the community.
- Two informative scenarios (SL-UK vs SL-CS) clarify how knowing worker skills at the decoder increases effective capacity; the comparison is insightful.
- kIC is a natural and interesting query design whose “structural redundancy” yields error correction; the SHC analysis cleanly illustrates this.
- Practical angle: the pricing heuristic π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 gives an interpretable link between query arity and cost.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Worker model realism: Modeling worker “skill” solely as a memoryless symmetric channel merges perceptual/knowledge limitations with reporting noise and ignores systematic biases, adversarial behavior, or instance-dependent difficulty. Please justify the MSC and SHC choices and discuss their limits. For example:
  - Distinguish competence (confusion across classes) from response noise (e.g., random guessing under time pressure).
  - Consider adversarial or informed malicious workers (who are not uniform “spammers”) and instance-dependent channels (hard/easy items).
  - Clarify whether “M” (response alphabet) equals “N” (number of true classes) in all settings; much of the analysis seems to assume M = N, but this should be stated explicitly where used.
- Allocation assumption: The taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers (mi = m′), which is unrealistic when worker ratings or historical reliabilities are often available (even partially) on platforms. While your limits target non-adaptive coding, please (i) emphasize this scope early, (ii) discuss how adaptive selection or per-worker budgeting would change the capacity term (e.g., moving from average worker capacity to a weighted average or best-worker selection), and (iii) relate to bandit-style adaptive allocation results.
- Prior P(B(X)): The assumption of known P(B(X)) is crucial for R*(D) but is only stated late. Please declare it upfront (in Sec. 2.1 or 3.2), explain how it might be estimated in practice, and discuss sensitivity to misspecification or the worst-case (minimax) setting.
- Numerical presentation: The comparison in Fig. 2 is hard to parse.
  - Label each curve with the corresponding k and q; specify axes units (queries per item) and whether rates are per item or per k-tuple.
  - Explicitly state what “k = 1” corresponds to in your framework (single-item direct labeling vs 2IC baseline), and align the legend.
  - Clearly indicate which curves are information-theoretic limits (unachievable below) versus kIC necessary conditions.
- Scope of results: Many bounds are necessary (not tight). Please make the “oracle decoder” assumption in the kIC analysis prominent and discuss how realistic decoders (e.g., DS, spectral-EM) compare empirically.
- Notation/organization: Some figure references and theorem labeling are inconsistent.
  - Fix figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b; 1.b → 1.c).
  - Ensure all Theorem/Lemma/Corollary statements are numbered and cross-referenced consistently.
  - Define M, N, and their relationship once and use consistently.

Quality
- The information-theoretic derivations are standard and technically sound given the assumptions. The kIC analysis under SHC is careful and yields interpretable necessary conditions. The main concerns are modeling realism and clarity of assumptions, which can be addressed with revisions.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but several assumptions should be stated earlier and figures improved as above. Add an up-front “Modeling assumptions” subsection summarizing worker channels, prior knowledge, non-adaptivity, and independence assumptions.

Originality
- The rate–distortion framing applied to crowdsourcing is a novel and valuable viewpoint for NIPS, even if the underlying theorems are classical. kIC as a coding lens on pairwise/multiway incidence queries adds a nice design angle.

Significance
- As benchmarks, the bounds can guide budgeting, query design, and pricing. Practical impact depends on extending beyond symmetric, non-adaptive, iid assumptions to more realistic settings (adaptive allocation, adversaries, instance difficulty). Even so, the conceptual contribution is likely to influence future work.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel perspective connecting crowdsourcing and rate–distortion; clear, interpretable lower bounds.
- Insightful comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS; useful guidance for when estimating worker reliability pays off.
- kIC analysis highlights how query structure can provide error correction; potential to inform practical query design and pricing.

Arguments against acceptance
- Worker/channel models and uniform allocation assumption limit realism; lack of adaptive or adversarial analysis.
- Numerical section and figure labeling need clarification; strength of empirical validation is limited.
- Key assumptions (e.g., known P(B(X))) are deferred and insufficiently discussed.

Minor comments
- Move the statement that P(B(X)) is known to the beginning of Sec. 2.1/3.2.
- Clarify the relationship between N (classes) and M (answer alphabet) where used in Theorems 2–3 and Corollary 1.
- Improve Fig. 2 labels and legends; specify what “k = 1” denotes.
- Number all Theorems/Lemmas/Corollaries and reference them consistently.
- Fix figure references on page 4 as noted above.
