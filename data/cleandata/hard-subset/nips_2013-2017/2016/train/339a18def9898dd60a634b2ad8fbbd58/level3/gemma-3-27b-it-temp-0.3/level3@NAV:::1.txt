## Review of “Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing”

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, framing the labeling problem as a coding problem over noisy channels representing individual workers. The core idea is compelling: modeling workers as symmetric memoryless channels or spammer/hammer channels allows for a rigorous analysis of the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (labeling accuracy). The authors map the crowdsourcing budget to a code rate and analyze this relationship, proposing and studying a basic coding scheme – k-incidence coding – as a concrete example. 

The work builds upon existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly those focusing on aggregation schemes like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm, by shifting the focus to fundamental limits rather than algorithmic optimization. It connects to broader information theory concepts like rate-distortion theory and channel coding, providing a theoretical underpinning for understanding the inherent constraints of crowdsourcing systems. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem and highlights its relevance to various applications.

A key strength of the paper lies in its attempt to provide a theoretical lower bound on the required budget for achieving a certain fidelity. Theorems 2 and 3, establishing these bounds for unknown and known worker skill levels respectively, are significant contributions. The analysis of k-incidence coding, while basic, provides a concrete example of how these theoretical bounds can be applied and offers insights into the potential benefits of structured querying. 

However, the paper’s strength is also its limitation. My expertise lies outside the specific intersection of information theory and crowdsourcing. While the mathematical framework appears sound, a detailed assessment of its technical correctness is beyond my capabilities. The numerical results, while presented, feel somewhat limited in scope and could benefit from a more extensive exploration of parameter space and comparison with existing empirical results. Furthermore, the connection between the theoretical bounds and practical crowdsourcing systems could be strengthened by discussing the implications of channel assumptions (MSC, SHC) in real-world scenarios. The discussion of query pricing, while interesting, feels somewhat tacked on and could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding fundamental limits.
*   Clear formulation of the problem and well-defined theoretical results (Theorems 2 & 3).
*   Potential for guiding the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Technical details are difficult to fully assess due to my limited expertise in the area.
*   Numerical results are somewhat limited.
*   Practical implications and connection to real-world systems could be further elaborated.



Overall, this is an interesting and potentially impactful paper. While I cannot provide a fully detailed technical evaluation, the novelty of the approach and the potential for theoretical advancement justify consideration for acceptance, perhaps with a request for minor revisions to expand the numerical results and discussion of practical implications.
