This paper proposes a novel framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing by modeling it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The core idea is to treat the true labels of a dataset as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster's query design as an encoder, the pool of crowd workers as a set of parallel noisy channels, and the final inference mechanism as a decoder. The workers' varying skill levels are modeled as random states of the channels. Within this information-theoretic framework, the paper derives fundamental limits on the trade-off between the query budget (rate) and the achievable fidelity (distortion).

The main results are asymptotic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain error probability, presented in Theorem 2 (for the case where worker skills are unknown to the crowdsourcer) and Theorem 3 (where skills are known to the crowdsourcer). These results are derived by applying the classic source-channel separation theorem. The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC), where workers are asked to compare k items at a time, and provides a lower bound on its performance under a spammer-hammer worker model. This information-theoretic approach is a departure from prior work which has largely focused on specific algorithmic designs for inference (e.g., Dawid-Skene, spectral methods) or task assignment. By focusing on fundamental limits, this work aims to provide ultimate performance benchmarks against which any practical crowdsourcing system can be measured.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel and elegant formulation of the crowdsourcing problem within a rate-distortion framework. This is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a new lens through which to understand the inherent trade-offs in crowdsourcing. Establishing fundamental performance limits is a valuable endeavor, as it provides a theoretical gold standard for the field.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The analogy between the components of a crowdsourcing system and a communication system is clearly explained, making the information-theoretic modeling accessible.

3.  **Promising Direction:** This work represents a promising first step toward a more principled, theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a good example of how the framework can be used to evaluate concrete query strategies.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Major Technical Justification Needed (Quality):** The central theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on the source-channel separation theorem (Theorem 1). However, the standard separation theorem applies to a single, known discrete memoryless channel. In this paper's model, the communication channel is effectively a random mixture of channels, where the state (worker skill level) is unknown to the encoder (taskmaster) and may or may not be known to the decoder (crowdsourcer). The validity of applying the standard separation theorem in this scenario is not immediately obvious and is a critical point that needs to be addressed. The authors must either provide a proof that separation holds for channels with random state unknown to the encoder, or cite relevant results from the information theory literature that justify this step (e.g., on compound channels or arbitrarily varying channels). Without this justification, the technical soundness of the main theorems is in question.

2.  **Modeling Assumptions (Significance):** The model assumes that the labels B(X) are an abstract i.i.d. source, completely independent of the items X themselves. In many practical crowdsourcing applications (e.g., image classification), the items X contain significant side information that a sophisticated taskmaster or crowdsourcer could leverage. For instance, a taskmaster could adapt its queries based on the difficulty of an item, or a crowdsourcer could use item features to help resolve ambiguous or conflicting worker responses. By ignoring this side information, the model may provide limits that are overly pessimistic for real-world scenarios. The authors should at least acknowledge this limitation and ideally discuss how their framework might be extended to incorporate side information at the encoder and/or decoder.

3.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The derived limits are asymptotic, assuming an infinitely large dataset (L → ∞). While standard in information theory, this limits the direct practical relevance for finite crowdsourcing projects. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include a discussion of non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) performance. Non-asymptotic converse bounds, which provide limits for any finite number of items, are particularly relevant here. The authors may find the work on finite-blocklength joint source-channel coding converses, such as in Kostina & Verdú (IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, 2013), to be relevant.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issue:** In Section 4.2 and Figure 2, the wording "the lower bounds for kIC ... improve as k and q grow" is slightly ambiguous. "Improve" could mean the bound becomes tighter or that the performance it implies is better. It appears the authors mean the latter: the bound on the required rate *decreases*, suggesting better performance is achievable. This should be rephrased for precision, for example: "The performance bound for kIC indicates that a lower query rate is sufficient (i.e., the bound value decreases) as k and q increase..."

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and highly original information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing.
    *   Addresses the important problem of establishing fundamental performance limits.
    *   The paper is well-written and the core ideas are communicated clearly.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The technical foundation rests on an application of the source-channel separation theorem to a channel with random state, which is not justified in the text and is a critical missing step.
    *   The model makes simplifying assumptions (e.g., ignoring item side information) that may limit the relevance of the derived bounds.
    *   The results are purely asymptotic, and a discussion of more practical finite-blocklength results is absent.

Overall, this paper introduces a very interesting and promising research direction. However, the lack of justification for the application of the separation theorem is a major technical weakness that must be addressed for the main results to be considered sound. If the authors can convincingly address this point and acknowledge the limitations of their model, the paper would be a strong contribution.
