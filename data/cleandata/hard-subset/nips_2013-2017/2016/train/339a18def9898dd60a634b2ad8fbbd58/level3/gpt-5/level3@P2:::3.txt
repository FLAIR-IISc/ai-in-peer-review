Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic limits that relate budget (queries per item) to fidelity (error/distortion). The dataset labels are treated as a discrete source; workers are modeled as noisy channels (MSC and spammer–hammer), and the taskmaster’s query scheme plus the crowdsourcer’s inference are treated as an encoder–decoder pair. Using rate–distortion and source–channel separation, the authors give lower bounds on the minimum query rate needed to achieve a target error, under two scenarios: (i) SL-UK: worker skill/quality unknown; (ii) SL-CS: worker skill known to the decoder (or perfectly inferred). They also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC), showing its implicit error-correction via invalid-response pruning, derive a lower bound for kIC under SHC workers (oracle decoding), and discuss a rule-of-thumb pricing relation across different k.

The work connects to NIPS literature on aggregation/inference under noisy labels (Dawid–Skene EM; majority vote; Karger–Oh–Shah; Whitehill et al.; Zhang et al. spectral+EM; convex methods for clustering), and to adaptive selection/bandit-survey models (Abraham et al.). The main novelty is a clean separation-style budget–fidelity limit and a coding view of query design (kIC), rather than proposing another inference algorithm.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding yields intuitive, general lower bounds that are independent of specific aggregation schemes.
- Two regimes (unknown vs known skills) clearly delineate how knowledge of worker quality shifts the effective capacity.
- kIC formalization highlights an inherent error-correcting mechanism from structural invalid answers, offering a principled lens on “smart queries.”
- Pricing heuristic links query arity to cost, which is practically relevant.
- Results are potentially reusable as baselines for future algorithmic innovations.

Weaknesses
- Accessibility: the presentation is heavy on information-theoretic notation; many readers in crowdsourcing/ML may struggle to extract takeaways without rephrasing in application terms.
- Assumptions limit realism: iid items, large-block asymptotics, non-adaptive querying, and especially oracle/perfect skill identification for some bounds. The implications for finite datasets and practical decoders are not empirically validated.
- Limited empirical or simulation support; Figure 2 is hard to interpret and does not convincingly bridge theory to practice.
- Related work on incentives/mechanism design is missing, despite the discussion of spammers/hammers and pricing.

Quality
The technical development is sound as far as I can tell, though some details were difficult to parse. Theorems 2–3 cleanly instantiate separation: minimum queries per item ≈ (source rate under target distortion) divided by effective worker-channel capacity; the difference between SL-UK and SL-CS is intuitive (system behaves like an average worker vs the average of worker qualities). The kIC bound under SHC with oracle decoding is a necessary condition; the paper is careful to label it as such. However, the paper would benefit from:
- Numerical examples instantiating Theorems 2–3 for simple binary/uniform cases, comparing to majority vote/DS baselines.
- Finite-length effects or concentration bounds (even heuristic) to guide practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized but difficult for non–information theorists. Suggestions:
- Expand the high-level intuition and practical takeaways at the end of Section 3 and add a brief concluding section summarizing what a practitioner should do: e.g., “Given class balance p, target error epsilon, and estimated worker confusion/noise, here is a back-of-the-envelope query budget.”
- Restate key results in crowdsourcing terms (labels/classes, error rate, per-item queries, proportion of reliable workers) alongside the information-theoretic versions.
- Clarify terminology: define and consistently distinguish “taskmaster” (query design/assignment) vs “crowdsourcer” (decoder/inference). This was confusing in multiple places.

Originality
Applying a rate–distortion/separation analysis to derive budget–fidelity limits for crowdsourced labeling and formalizing kIC’s error-correction are, to my knowledge, novel in the NIPS crowdsourcing literature. The pricing rule-of-thumb is also a nice byproduct. The modeling of workers as channels is not new, but the explicit use of separation-style limits is.

Significance
If presented accessibly, the bounds could become standard reference baselines for aggregation methods and query design, and could inform the choice of k in kIC and budget planning. The work bridges communities (IT and crowdsourcing), which is valuable. Stronger empirical validation would raise the impact.

Pros
- General theoretical framework with clear limits.
- Insightful comparison between unknown vs known worker qualities.
- kIC analysis and pricing heuristic offer actionable guidance.
- Connects to well-cited NIPS aggregation literature.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on asymptotic, idealized assumptions; limited discussion of finite-sample effects.
- Presentation is hard to follow for the target audience; key insights are buried.
- Missing related work on incentive-compatible mechanisms and robust economic incentives (e.g., peer-prediction and effort elicitation: Prelec 2004; Radanovic & Faltings, NIPS 2014/2016; Waggoner & Chen, EC 2014; Ho, Slivkins, Vaughan, EC 2015), and work integrating incentives with aggregation.
- Figure 2 unclear; minimal empirical/experimental support.

Requested revisions and specific suggestions
- Expand practitioner-facing intuition: summarize Theorems 2–3 and Corollary 1 in plain language with a small numeric example (binary, uniform labels; show how q, M, epsilon map to queries per item).
- Emphasize in the main text that kIC lower bounds assume oracle skill identification; discuss how performance might degrade with realistic decoders, and include a small simulation comparing kIC vs pairwise vs direct voting under SHC/MSC.
- Clarify costs: bring Appendix E’s pricing/cost model into the main text and connect to real platform friction (time-to-complete, abandonment).
- Related work: add a short subsection on incentives/mechanism design and how your limits might interact with truthful/effort-eliciting payment rules.
- Terminology: clearly define “crowdsourcer” vs “taskmaster” early and enforce consistency.
- Improve Figure 2: larger fonts, axes labeled with units (queries per item; error rate), legend mapping curves to k and q; add a caption explaining which curves are information-theoretic limits vs kIC bounds.
- Minor edits:
  - Line 84: “are reviewed in,” not “is reviewed in.”
  - Line 122: remove duplicated “A” in “A A(X1,X2).”
  - Lines 275–276: the sentence beginning “In case...” is hard to parse; please rephrase for readability.

Overall assessment
A theoretically interesting and potentially useful contribution that could meaningfully influence how we plan crowdsourcing budgets and design queries. However, accessibility and practical grounding need improvement. With revisions addressing clarity and added empirical illustration, I would support acceptance.
