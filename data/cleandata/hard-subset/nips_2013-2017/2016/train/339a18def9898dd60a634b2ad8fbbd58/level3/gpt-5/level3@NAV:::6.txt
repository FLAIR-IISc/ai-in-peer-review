This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel problem and derives an information-theoretic budget–fidelity trade-off using rate–distortion theory (for the source) and channel capacity (for worker noise), under MSC and SHC worker models. It presents bounds for two regimes—unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and skills known to the decoder (SL-CS)—and analyzes a concrete query design, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), including an oracle lower bound and a pricing heuristic. The exposition connects to classical information theory (Cover–Thomas; El Gamal–Kim) and to crowdsourcing aggregation methods (Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM, convex methods). If this is indeed the first explicit rate–distortion/capacity formulation for crowdsourcing, the conceptual bridge could be meaningful.

Quality: The derivations appear sound and the mapping to separation-type bounds is correct. However, the main bound is likely quite loose for real crowdsourcing because it inherits the freedom to design u^n and random codebooks that typical channel-coding converses assume. In practice, crowdsourcing imposes strong constraints on encodings: workers can only be queried a few times, items receive limited and heterogeneous exposures, and one cannot repeatedly query the same worker without violating i.i.d. assumptions. It is unclear which mappings C: B(X)^n → U^(Σ m_i) are implementable as crowdsourcing codebooks; repetition codes (majority voting) are feasible, but how to realize stronger error-correcting encodings is not addressed. These constraints likely undermine the tightness of the capacity-based lower bounds and Theorem 4’s usefulness beyond order-of-magnitude guidance.

Clarity: Generally clear, but some statements need precision and proof details. See minor comments below.

Originality: The formal rate–distortion framing for CS could be novel, but the derivations themselves are standard once the mapping is fixed. The kIC discussion is interesting but incremental.

Significance: Without modeling implementability constraints and without empirical or theoretical comparisons to state-of-the-art practical methods (DS, spectral EM, Karger–Oh–Shah sample complexity), it is hard to assess practical value or tightness.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean, correct information-theoretic formulation; potentially first application to CS.
- Provides a unifying vocabulary and baseline bounds.
- kIC analysis may stimulate query design work.

Cons for acceptance:
- Likely loose bounds due to unrealistic encoding freedom; crowdsourcing constraints not modeled.
- Limited novelty in derivation; unclear practical impact.
- Missing comparisons to modern algorithms and datasets.

Suggestions:
- Formalize admissible encodings (per-worker/item constraints, no repeat queries to the same worker) and derive constrained bounds.
- Compare predicted sample complexity to DS/spectral/KOS methods, both theoretically and via simulation, to gauge tightness.
- Discuss how to construct implementable encoders beyond repetition (e.g., combinatorial designs for kIC).

Minor comments:
- In (8), clarify that the minimization is over P(Ẑ|Z) (equivalently C,C′) subject to E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.
- Theorem 4: since ln(1−q) < 0, specify sign conditions on ln(ε̄_S) and whether ε_S ≥ ẑε or vice versa.
- Expand the proof of Theorem 4; several steps are terse.
- In (17), justify the appearance of H_M(E(ε)) or cite prior work that establishes this term.
