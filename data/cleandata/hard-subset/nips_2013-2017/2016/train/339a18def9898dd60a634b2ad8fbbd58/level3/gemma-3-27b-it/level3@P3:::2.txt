This paper presents a novel and promising information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling them as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. The core idea—treating the taskmaster as an encoder, workers as noisy channels, and the crowdsourcer as a decoder—is insightful and offers a strong foundation for understanding fundamental limits on query efficiency and accuracy. The derivation of asymptotic query-rate limits (Theorems 2 and 3) is a significant contribution. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding and the exploration of query pricing strategies further enrich the work. Overall, the paper is well-written and represents a valuable first step towards a more rigorous, information-theoretic understanding of crowdsourcing.

However, several points require attention. The most critical technical concern relates to the applicability of source-channel separation. The authors invoke this theorem (Theorem 1) to justify their results, but crucially, the channel (representing the workers) has a random state unknown to the encoder (taskmaster). Standard source-channel separation theorems require the channel state to be known to the encoder or, at least, independent of the source. The authors need to either justify why separation holds in this scenario (perhaps by citing relevant results for channels with unknown states) or acknowledge this limitation and frame the results directly within a JSCC framework without relying on separation. This is a fundamental issue that impacts the theoretical validity of the derived bounds.

A modeling concern revolves around the exclusive focus on labels (B(X)) by the encoder and decoder, neglecting the original items (X) as potential side information. In many realistic crowdsourcing scenarios, the items themselves carry valuable information—e.g., image features, text content—that could significantly improve inference accuracy and reduce the required query rate. Ignoring this side information is a simplification that may limit the practical relevance of the results. A discussion of the impact of incorporating item information would strengthen the analysis.

Clarity is needed in Section 4.2 and Figure 2. The statement “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is misleading. Lower bounds, by definition, *decrease* in value as performance improves. The current wording suggests the bound itself is becoming “tighter” without a corresponding upper bound for comparison, leading to confusion. It should be stated that the lower bound *value* decreases, indicating potential for better performance with increasing k and q.

Finally, while the asymptotic analysis is valuable, the inclusion of nonasymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses would be highly beneficial. This would provide more practical insights into the performance of crowdsourcing systems with limited data. The authors should consider citing Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses and exploring how those results could be adapted to the crowdsourcing context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful modeling of crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem.
*   Derivation of fundamental limits on query rate (Theorems 2 and 3).
*   Analysis of a practical query scheme (k-ary incidence coding).
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Addresses a significant problem with potential real-world impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for applying source-channel separation theorem given the channel state is unknown to the encoder.
*   Simplification of ignoring item information as side information.
*   Misleading wording in Section 4.2/Figure 2.
*   Absence of nonasymptotic analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the core ideas are strong and the theoretical analysis is sound *given the assumptions*, the critical issue regarding source-channel separation and the other points mentioned above need to be addressed. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I believe the potential of this research direction is substantial, and with revisions, this paper could be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
