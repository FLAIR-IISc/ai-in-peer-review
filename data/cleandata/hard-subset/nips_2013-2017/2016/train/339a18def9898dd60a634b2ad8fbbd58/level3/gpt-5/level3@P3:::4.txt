Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models crowdsourcing with unreliable workers as joint source–channel coding and analyzes the fundamental budget–fidelity trade-off via rate–distortion style information-theoretic (IT) lower bounds. The authors treat workers as noisy channels (MSC and SHC models), define budget as query rate, and fidelity as distortion (Hamming error), and derive converse-style bounds for two cases: unknown worker skill at the taskmaster (SL-UK) and known skill at the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They further analyze a concrete non-adaptive query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compare its converse-type performance bound to an IT lower bound specialized to SHC. The work sits alongside prior NIPS research on aggregation/inference (Dawid–Skene EM; majority voting), improved inference (e.g., Zhang et al.; Whitehill et al.), and task allocation/budget optimization (Karger–Oh–Shah; Abraham et al. bandit survey). Unlike these, the paper seeks fundamental limits using a JSCC framing, which is original in this literature and could inspire principled design.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The rate–distortion/joint source–channel perspective on crowdsourcing provides a clear language for limits and trade-offs and helps unify query design and inference.
- Clean converse bounds: Theorems 2–3, under the MSC pool, are intuitive and technically sound given the assumptions; they clarify how knowledge of worker skill at the decoder improves rates.
- Concrete code instance: The kIC scheme is interesting for clustering-style queries; the analysis under SHC and the pricing “rule of thumb” are useful sanity checks and may guide practice.
- Potential for impact: The separation-style view could catalyze future work on code design for human-in-the-loop systems, adaptive strategies, and robust modeling.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling conflates skill and reporting reliability: Collapsing a worker’s perceptual/labeling skill and reporting noise into one channel obscures realistic distinctions (e.g., a skilled but occasionally careless worker vs. an unskilled but honest one). This matters for both limits and mechanisms (e.g., incentives).
- Unrealistic uniform allocation: The taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers. Many platforms maintain worker ratings or quality priors; budget-optimal task allocation and active selection (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; bandit-survey) show heterogeneous, adaptive allocation is crucial. The current assumptions limit applicability of the bounds.
- Worker-channel realism: MSC/SHC do not capture adversarial or informed malicious behavior (e.g., anti-hammers who preferentially pick wrong but consistent answers, or strategic manipulation). They also do not capture label- or item-difficulty-dependent noise (e.g., GLAD/Whitehill et al.).
- Assumption clarity: The paper assumes knowledge of P(B(X)) and states this late (p.6, line 246). This is central to the bounds and should be declared up front, with discussion of robustness if P(B(X)) is misspecified.
- Presentation issues: Figure 2 labeling is unclear; the meaning of k=1 is unstated in text; several figure references and theorem/lemma usages are inconsistent.

Quality
The theoretical framing is correct and the derivations appear sound given the assumptions. However, realism of the channel models and the uniform-allocation constraint limit the external validity of the conclusions. The kIC analysis is careful for SHC, but empirical validation or stress tests under more realistic noise/adversarial models are missing.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but key assumptions are revealed late; some figures and references are confusing; and theorem/lemma/corollary usage should be standardized and numbered. The narrative would benefit from a clearer separation of modeling assumptions, what is known to whom (taskmaster vs crowdsourcer), and how rates map to budgets under differing query types.

Originality
High on conceptual framing. While kIC builds on incidence-style queries known in clustering, the joint IT treatment and the link between kIC and information-theoretic converses are novel in crowdsourcing.

Significance
Moderate. The results could influence how the community thinks about limits and guide query/inference co-design. Practical significance depends on extending the models to heterogeneous, adversarial, and adaptive settings.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Modeling: Separate worker skill (confusion matrix over true labels; possibly item-difficulty dependent) from reporting noise/adversarial behavior by modeling a two-stage channel (source → perceptual label via skill; perceptual label → reported label via reporting mechanism). Discuss how bounds change under this richer model and whether separation still holds.
- Allocation: Justify the equal-rate assumption; at minimum, add a section discussing optimal heterogeneous allocation with known/learned quality priors and how the bounds might extend (e.g., replacing average channel quality with a mixture-capacity or waterfilling-like allocation), and cite Karger–Oh–Shah and bandit-survey work more explicitly.
- Adversarial workers: Add models beyond SHC/MSC, e.g., biased or worst-case confusions, informed adversaries, or channels with state correlated with queries. Even a discussion and a sensitivity plot would strengthen the case for realism.
- Assumption placement: Move the assumption of known P(B(X)) to Section 2.1. Clarify who knows P(B(X)) (encoder, decoder, both) and discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Mapping k and budget: Explicitly define what k=1 corresponds to (direct per-item labeling with no incidence constraints) and how queries-per-item R scales with k in non-adaptive deployments.
- Numerical section: Improve Figure 2: add a legend identifying each curve with k and q; label axes with units; annotate the IT lower bound vs. kIC bound; and explain in caption what k=1 is and how R is computed. Explain the parameter M in Corollary 1 in the plotted setting.
- Minor corrections: Fix figure references on p.4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b; 1.b → 1.c). Number all Theorems, Lemmas, Corollaries (or use lowercase if informal).
- Scope/limitations: State explicitly that all bounds are converse-style and non-adaptive; discuss how feedback/adaptivity (task routing) could tighten achievable performance.

Pros
- Elegant and potentially influential JSCC framing for crowdsourcing
- Clear converse bounds highlighting the role of label entropy and worker reliability
- Concrete kIC analysis and connection to bounds; pricing insight

Cons
- Worker/channel models are oversimplified; adversarial and difficulty effects not captured
- Uniform allocation assumption is unrealistic for modern platforms
- Some clarity/presentation issues and late statement of key assumptions

Overall evaluation
Borderline accept. The paper’s conceptual contribution is strong and relevant to NIPS, but the current modeling choices limit practical relevance. With clearer assumption statements, improved figures, and a more compelling discussion of model realism (or first steps toward heterogeneous/adversarial extensions), this could be a valuable contribution.
