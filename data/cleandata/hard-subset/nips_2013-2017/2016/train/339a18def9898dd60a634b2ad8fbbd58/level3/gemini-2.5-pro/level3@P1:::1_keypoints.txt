- Introduces a novel information-theoretic framework that models crowdsourcing as an end-to-end communication system (source, encoder, noisy channels, decoder).  
- Maps crowdsourcing budget to rate and labeling accuracy to fidelity (rate–distortion analogy) and derives lower bounds on budget needed for given fidelity.  
- Analyzes two worker noise models: M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC).  
- Considers two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known worker skills (SL-CS), quantifying the performance gain from knowing skills.  
- Provides rigorous, formally derived theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) using joint source–channel coding principles; derivations appear correct.  
- Proposes and analyzes a practical query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), and derives performance bounds compared to information-theoretic limits.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with consistent notation and helpful figures explaining the analogy and models.  
- Original contribution: reframes crowdsourcing via rate–distortion theory, offering fundamental performance limits rather than algorithmic heuristics.  
- Significant impact: supplies theoretical benchmarks for budget–fidelity trade-offs and insights for optimal query design and worker-skill effects.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical results give tight formal bounds under stated assumptions.  
- Strength: clear model and insightful distinction between average worker capacity vs. average of capacities when skills are known.  
- Strength: bridges theory and practice by linking coding rate to concrete query design (k) and suggesting pricing heuristics.  
- Weakness: results are asymptotic (L, n -> ∞); applicability and tightness in finite, practical settings are unclear.  
- Weakness: worker models (MSC, SHC) are simplified; real-world behaviors (adversarial, task-dependent, non-stationary) are not handled.  
- Weakness: kIC analysis uses an Oracle decoder that identifies hammers, so practical decoder performance and true costs are not fully evaluated.  
- Conclusion: excellent, original work that opens a promising direction; recommended for acceptance despite standard theoretical limitations.
