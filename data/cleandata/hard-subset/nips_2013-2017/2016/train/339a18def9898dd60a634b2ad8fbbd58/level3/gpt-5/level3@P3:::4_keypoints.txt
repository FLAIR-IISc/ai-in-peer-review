- Paper frames crowdsourcing with unreliable workers as joint source–channel coding and analyzes budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion-style IT lower bounds.  
- Workers are modeled as noisy channels (MSC and SHC); budget = query rate, fidelity = Hamming distortion.  
- Derives converse-style bounds for unknown-skill-at-taskmaster (SL-UK) and known-skill-at-crowdsourcer (SL-CS) cases.  
- Analyzes a concrete non-adaptive query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and compares it to an IT lower bound specialized to SHC.  
- Conceptual novelty: JSCC/rate–distortion perspective provides a unifying language for limits and co-design of queries and inference.  
- Clean converse bounds (Theorems 2–3) under MSC are intuitive and technically sound given assumptions, showing decoder knowledge improves rates.  
- kIC is an interesting, carefully analyzed concrete scheme with useful “pricing” rules of thumb.  
- Framing could spur future work on code design, adaptive strategies, and robust modeling for human-in-the-loop systems.  
- Main modeling flaw: conflates perceptual/skill errors and reporting noise into one channel, obscuring realistic distinctions important for limits and incentives.  
- Unrealistic uniform allocation: assuming equal queries per worker ignores platform ratings and adaptive/heterogeneous allocation literature (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah, bandit-survey).  
- Worker-channel realism is limited: MSC/SHC omit adversarial, biased, informed, or item-difficulty-dependent behaviors (e.g., GLAD-style models).  
- Key assumption P(B(X)) is stated late; knowing this distribution is central and should be declared up front with robustness discussion.  
- Presentation issues: unclear Figure 2 labeling, undefined k=1, inconsistent figure references and theorem/lemma numbering.  
- Theoretical derivations are correct under assumptions, but external validity is limited by simplistic channel models and uniform allocation.  
- Empirical validation or stress tests under realistic/adversarial noise models are missing.  
- Clarity could be improved by stating assumptions early, clarifying who knows what (taskmaster vs crowdsourcer), and mapping rates to budgets for query types.  
- Originality is high for applying joint IT treatment to crowdsourcing; kIC builds on incidence queries but the JSCC link is novel.  
- Significance is moderate: conceptually influential but practical impact requires extensions to heterogeneous, adversarial, and adaptive settings.  
- Suggested modeling fix: separate worker skill (confusion matrix) from reporting/adversarial noise via a two-stage channel and analyze bound changes.  
- Suggested allocation fix: justify equal-rate assumption or discuss extensions to heterogeneous allocation (mixture-capacity or waterfilling-like approaches).  
- Suggested adversary fix: include models or sensitivity analysis for biased, worst-case, or informed adversaries and label/item-dependent noise.  
- Suggested presentation fixes: move P(B(X)) assumption to Section 2.1, define k=1 and R scaling, and improve Figure 2 (legend, axis labels, annotations).  
- Minor corrections: fix figure references, standardize numbering/formatting of Theorems/Lemmas/Corollaries.  
- Scope limitation: emphasize that bounds are converse-style and non-adaptive; discuss how adaptivity/task routing could change achievable performance.  
- Overall evaluation: borderline accept — strong conceptual contribution but limited practical relevance without clearer assumptions, better figures, and extensions to realistic/heterogeneous/adversarial settings.
