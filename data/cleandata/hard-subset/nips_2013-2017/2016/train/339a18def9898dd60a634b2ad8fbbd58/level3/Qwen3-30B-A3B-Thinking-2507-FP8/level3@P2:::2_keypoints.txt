- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem with random channel states and derives asymptotic bounds on the minimum query-rate for a target error probability.
- Extends prior crowdsourcing and information-theoretic work (e.g., Dawid–Skene, Vox Populi) by incorporating unknown worker skill levels and focusing on fundamental limits rather than algorithms.
- Novelty: Presents a new theoretical formulation that opens fresh research directions in crowdsourcing.
- Clarity: Exceptionally well-written with clear definitions, logical flow, helpful figures, and detailed mathematical derivations.
- Significance: Provides theoretical benchmarks to evaluate crowdsourcing systems and assess proximity to fundamental limits.
- Main technical weakness: No justification for applying source–channel separation when channel states are unknown to the encoder; needs citation/argument (e.g., Cover & Thomas or related state-dependent channel results).
- Modeling limitation: Assumes only labels are available and ignores original items as side information, which is often informative in practice.
- Presentation issue: Section 4.2 and Figure 2 are misleading — should clarify that lower bound values decrease (better performance) as k and q grow.
- Practicality gap: Lacks finite-blocklength (nonasymptotic) converses; should cite and discuss Kostina & Verdú (2013) for practical guidance.
- Pro: Establishes the first fundamental limits for crowdsourcing with random worker skill levels.
- Pro: Offers a clean, unifying information-theoretic framework and a concrete k-ary incidence coding example.
- Con: Technical gap on source–channel separation, omission of side information, misleading wording, and missing finite-blocklength analysis weaken practical impact.
- Overall assessment: Promising and important theoretical contribution that merits acceptance pending a minor revision to address the technical justification and presentation issues.
