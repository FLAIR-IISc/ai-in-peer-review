This paper presents a novel approach to analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing with unreliable workers by mapping it to joint source-channel coding. The authors derive a budget-fidelity trade-off via information-theoretic lower bounds, which is an interesting and potentially inspiring contribution to the field. However, there are several concerns and methodological issues that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the model used in the paper merges worker skill and reliability into a single channel, losing the distinction between skill/rating and reporting noise. This oversimplification may not accurately capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The authors should clarify and justify the realism of the assumed worker/channel models.

Secondly, the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, which is unrealistic given that workers often have known ratings and heterogeneous capabilities. This assumption may not be practical and could be improved by incorporating more realistic worker allocation strategies.

Thirdly, the channel models for worker unreliability may not realistically capture adversarial or informed malicious workers. The authors should consider more sophisticated models that account for these types of workers.

On the other hand, the specific scheme of k-ary incidence coding is analyzed and compared to the information-theoretic lower bound for a particular channel model. This is a valuable contribution, but the numerical results and explanations could be improved. Specifically, the labeling of curves in Figure 2 should be clarified, and the authors should explicitly state what k=1 corresponds to.

Furthermore, there is a methodological issue with the assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)), which is stated late in the paper (page 6, line 246). This assumption should be declared earlier in the paper to provide a clear understanding of the framework.

Minor corrections include fixing figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b, 1.b → 1.c). Additionally, the authors misuse 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' without numbering; they should use lowercase or add numbers for clarity.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting approach to analyzing crowdsourcing, there are several concerns and methodological issues that need to be addressed. With revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to analyzing crowdsourcing with unreliable workers using joint source-channel coding.
2. The authors derive a budget-fidelity trade-off via information-theoretic lower bounds, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
3. The specific scheme of k-ary incidence coding is analyzed and compared to the information-theoretic lower bound for a particular channel model.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The model used in the paper oversimplifies the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios by merging worker skill and reliability into a single channel.
2. The taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, which is unrealistic given that workers often have known ratings and heterogeneous capabilities.
3. The channel models for worker unreliability may not realistically capture adversarial or informed malicious workers.
4. The numerical results and explanations could be improved, and the labeling of curves in Figure 2 should be clarified.
5. The assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is stated late in the paper and should be declared earlier.
6. Minor corrections are needed, including fixing figure references and using consistent notation for theorems, lemmas, and corollaries.
