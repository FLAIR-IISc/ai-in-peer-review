- Paper models crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives budget–fidelity trade-offs using rate–distortion and channel capacity.  
- Workers are modeled as independent discrete memoryless channels; two families analyzed: M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel (SHC(q)).  
- Taskmaster uses non-adaptive query assignment; two knowledge regimes: skills unknown to crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and skills known (SL-CS).  
- Theorems 2–3 provide information-theoretic lower bounds on queries per item for MSC, highlighting value of skill knowledge (capacity differences).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a practical query design that prunes invalid patterns and provides built-in error correction.  
- For SHC(q), Lemma 1 computes spammer-induced error under kIC and Theorem 4 gives necessary rate conditions for any decoder (including oracle).  
- Corollary gives an information-theoretic benchmark for SHC with known skills; paper also proposes a pricing heuristic for kIC.  
- Work complements aggregation/inference and worker-selection literature by focusing on fundamental limits rather than algorithms.  
- Strength: principled, information-theoretic modeling linking Shannon-style tools to crowdsourcing budget–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Strength: separate analysis of SL-UK vs SL-CS clarifies benefit of skill estimation.  
- Strength: kIC is concrete and practically relevant; SHC analysis and necessary conditions are novel.  
- Strength: pricing discussion offers actionable guidance for practitioners.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation — no simulations comparing standard aggregation methods or kIC decoders to theoretical limits.  
- Weakness: strong modeling assumptions restrict scope (non-adaptive assignment, iid items, independent channels, MSC/SHC only, some uniform priors and oracle skill knowledge).  
- Weakness: main text delegates proofs to appendix; limited in-text sketches of new converses/achievability and Section 3.1 is largely textbook review.  
- Weakness: notation inconsistencies and unclear relation between number of classes and answer choices (M vs N, pmax, HN).  
- Weakness: some modeling choices need discussion (difficulty-dependent error, label imbalance, adversarial behaviors beyond SHC).  
- Quality: theoretical claims are well-motivated and appear sound; need clarity that Theorems 2–3 are converse/necessary conditions unless achievability is shown.  
- Quality: kIC vs SHC analysis gives interpretable necessary conditions; more discussion of tightness and achievable schemes would strengthen contribution.  
- Clarity: paper is readable but should shorten background, include proof sketches for main theorems, and unify notation (N vs M, state assumptions when M=N).  
- Originality: framing budget–fidelity via rate–distortion and separation is novel; kIC necessary condition under SHC with oracle seems new.  
- Significance: bounds can guide system design and pricing, but impact would increase with empirical comparisons showing practical gaps to limits.  
- Suggestions: add simulations comparing majority vote, Dawid–Skene, and kIC decoders to bounds; include finite-sample effects.  
- Suggestions: provide proof sketches or key lemmas in main text and move more background to appendix.  
- Suggestions: clarify achievability vs converse and when separation is tight (alphabets, adaptive vs non-adaptive).  
- Suggestions: discuss non-uniform priors, class imbalance, difficulty-dependent errors, and adaptivity/worker selection links.  
- Minor fix: check pricing ratio in Appendix E; likely scales as k1/k2 not k1k2.  
- Questions: how tight are kIC necessary conditions and are there simple achievable schemes approaching the bound?  
- Questions: how sensitive are results to deviations from independence (correlated worker errors or item difficulty)?  
- Questions: can framework be extended to adaptive querying without losing tractability?  
- Pros for acceptance: principled theoretical framing, practical query/pricing insights, clear exposition relevant to the community.  
- Cons for acceptance: no empirical validation, limited discussion of tightness, strong assumptions, and redundant background without main-text proofs.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — valuable theoretical perspective but needs simulations and tighter presentation.  
- Reviewer confidence: medium; appendices not verified in detail.
