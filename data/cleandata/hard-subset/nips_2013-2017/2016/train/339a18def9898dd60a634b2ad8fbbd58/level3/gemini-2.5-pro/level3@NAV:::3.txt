This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of label crowdsourcing. The authors model the problem as a communication system: true labels represent a source, workers are noisy channels, and the query/aggregation process is a joint source-channel code. Using this analogy, the paper derives the budget-fidelity function, establishing the ultimate theoretical trade-off between the number of queries (budget) and the achievable accuracy (fidelity). This analysis is applied to scenarios with known and unknown worker skill levels and is complemented by an evaluation of a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding.

The primary strength of this work lies in its originality and potential significance. I found the model and insights interesting and potentially very useful. Framing crowdsourcing in rate-distortion terms provides a powerful new perspective, moving beyond the predominantly algorithmic focus of prior work (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.) to establish fundamental performance benchmarks. Deriving these limits is a significant contribution that can help researchers gauge the efficiency of practical algorithms.

However, the paper's main weakness is its accessibility. Despite the authors' efforts, the heavy reliance on information theory jargon makes the core technical arguments difficult to parse for a general machine learning audience. The practical implications are often obscured by the terminology. A second weakness is the omission of a relevant body of literature on the economic aspects of crowdsourcing, such as mechanism design and robust incentive schemes, which also study budget-quality trade-offs.

To improve the paper and broaden its impact, I strongly recommend the following revisions:
1.  **Enhance Clarity and Intuition:** Please restate key results in concrete crowdsourcing terms rather than channel/code/distortion language. This would make the work's value clearer. Expanding the end of Section 3 and adding a concluding takeaway section summarizing the high-level intuition would be highly effective.
2.  **Broaden Related Work:** Please include a discussion of related work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design, and how your information-theoretic approach complements or differs from this line of inquiry.
3.  **Address Specifics:** Several smaller issues hinder readability. Please clarify the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster," as this was confusing. Figure 2 is unclear and requires a more detailed caption or a clearer visualization. The manuscript would also benefit from a copyedit to fix minor issues (e.g., the typo "A A(X1,X2)" on line 122; the sentence on lines 275-276 is hard to parse; "is reviewed in" on line 84 should be "are reviewed in").

In summary, this is a promising paper with a strong theoretical contribution. Addressing these clarity and positioning issues would significantly increase its value and impact for the NIPS community.
