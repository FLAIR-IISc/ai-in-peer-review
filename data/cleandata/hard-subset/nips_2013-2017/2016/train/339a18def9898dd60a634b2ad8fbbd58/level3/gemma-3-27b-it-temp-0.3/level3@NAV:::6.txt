## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic approach, specifically rate-distortion theory and channel coding concepts. The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel decoding problem, deriving bounds on achievable fidelity given a budget constraint (number of queries). They also analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and discuss query pricing.

The application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing is a novel direction, and if successful, could provide valuable insights into the inherent limitations of such systems. The derivation of the bounds appears sound, building upon established results in source and channel coding. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a concrete attempt to translate theoretical concepts into a practical scheme. 

However, the paper’s novelty and practical value are questionable. While the derivation is mathematically correct, it feels relatively straightforward given the existing literature in information theory. The derived bounds, particularly those in Theorems 2 and 3, may be very loose in practice. A significant concern is the applicability of channel coding-style bounds to crowdsourcing. Unlike traditional communication channels, the i.i.d. assumption is easily violated when querying workers multiple times, and the freedom to design the input sequence *u<sup>n</sup>* (encoding) is severely limited. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t fully address how these constraints impact the tightness of the bounds. The discussion of designing *u<sup>n</sup>* to achieve stronger error correction beyond repetition codes (majority voting) is also underdeveloped. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a crucial comparison to state-of-the-art practical crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene). Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the derived bounds. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding, while interesting, feels somewhat isolated without a benchmark against existing techniques. Several minor points require clarification: the min operation in (8), elaboration on the proof of Theorem 4, clarification of the relationship between ε<sub>S</sub> and  ̂ε in Theorem 4, and justification for the appearance of H<sub>M</sub>(E(ε)) in (17).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Sound mathematical derivation of bounds.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Questionable novelty and practical value due to loose bounds and limited comparison to existing methods.
*   Concerns about the applicability of channel coding concepts to the constraints of crowdsourcing.
*   Lack of empirical validation or simulation results.
*   Minor technical clarifications needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents an interesting theoretical exploration, but its limited practical relevance and lack of comparison to existing methods prevent it from being a strong contribution at this time. Addressing the concerns regarding the applicability of channel coding concepts and including a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing techniques would significantly strengthen the paper.
