This paper presents a novel approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing systems using an information-theoretic framework. The authors derive a fundamental limit on the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing, leveraging rate-distortion theory and channel coding. The paper is well-structured, and the ideas are clearly explained, making it easy to follow.

The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the crowdsourcing problem, highlighting the challenges of dealing with imperfect worker responses and unknown skill levels. They then introduce a communication system model for crowdsourcing, which enables the analysis of fundamental performance limits. The model is based on a joint source-channel coding scheme, where the query scheme and inference are represented as encoding and decoding mappings.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of an information-theoretic budget-fidelity bound for crowdsourcing. The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They provide theoretical results, including Theorems 2 and 3, which identify the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity in each scenario.

The paper also explores the performance of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which is shown to have an error correction capability. The authors derive a lower bound on the performance of kIC with a spammer-hammer worker pool and provide numerical results to illustrate the information-theoretic limit.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing and its ability to provide a fundamental limit on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly explained, making it accessible to a broad audience.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty and practical value of the paper. The derivation of the budget-fidelity bound is straightforward, and the bound may be very loose. Additionally, the paper does not provide a comparison with state-of-the-art practical methods, which would be helpful in assessing the significance of the results.

Furthermore, the paper assumes that the taskmaster can design the input sequence u^n (encoding) with wide freedom, which may not be the case in crowdsourcing. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a clear solution. The paper also assumes that the workers' responses are iid, which may not hold in practice.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is significant, as it applies information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing for the first time. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work and a clearer explanation of how the results differ from previous contributions.

In conclusion, this paper provides a novel approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing systems using an information-theoretic framework. While the paper has some limitations, its contribution to the field is significant, and the results have the potential to impact the design of crowdsourcing systems.
