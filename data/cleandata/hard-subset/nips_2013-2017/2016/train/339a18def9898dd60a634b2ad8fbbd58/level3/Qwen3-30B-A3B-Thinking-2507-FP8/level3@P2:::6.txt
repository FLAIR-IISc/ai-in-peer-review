

# Review for "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper applies information-theoretic rate-distortion theory and channel coding to analyze the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing systems. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication problem where workers are represented as noisy channels, and derive bounds on the minimum budget (number of queries per item) required to achieve a target fidelity (error probability). The paper presents two key theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) that characterize these bounds for scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) or known (SL-CS), and analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

The paper builds on a growing line of work on crowdsourcing, including the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5] and recent EM-based approaches [9, 10, 12]. It extends this line of research by adopting an information-theoretic perspective that has not been previously applied to crowdsourcing. The authors properly contextualize their work against related NIPS papers such as [8, 11, 13] and acknowledge the limitations of existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and the paper makes a clear attempt to establish fundamental limits for crowdsourcing.
2. The derivation of the main theorems (2 and 3) is technically sound, and the connection to rate-distortion theory is properly established.
3. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) provides a concrete example of a query scheme that can potentially achieve better performance than simple repetition coding.
4. The paper correctly identifies the key challenge of crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop system where workers cannot be reused in the same way as communication channels.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Questionable applicability of channel coding framework:** The paper's main contribution relies on applying channel coding theory to crowdsourcing, but fails to address a fundamental difference between communication channels and crowdsourcing workers: in communication, the same channel can be used repeatedly without violating iid assumptions, while in crowdsourcing, querying the same worker multiple times would violate the iid assumption (as the worker's skill level is fixed). This undermines the tightness of the proposed bounds.
   
2. **Lack of practical relevance:** The paper fails to demonstrate how the derived bounds compare to state-of-the-art practical crowdsourcing methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting, or recent approaches like [11, 13]). Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the theoretical bounds are meaningful for real-world applications.

3. **Implementation constraints unaddressed:** The paper presents a coding framework (equation 3) but does not adequately address which mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σ m_i) are actually implementable in practice. For instance, it's unclear how to design the encoding to achieve stronger error-correction beyond simple repetition coding (which is the only clearly implementable scheme).

4. **Minor technical issues:** The proof of Theorem 4 needs more elaboration, and the paper should clarify the relationship between ε_S and ̂ in Theorem 4. The notation H_M(E(ε)) in Theorem 3 is not well-explained, and the paper should cite prior work justifying this form.

**Originality:** The application of rate-distortion theory and channel coding to crowdsourcing is novel, but the derivation itself is straightforward and does not introduce significant new theoretical machinery. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from previous work on crowdsourcing performance bounds.

**Significance:** The paper identifies an important problem (fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs), but the significance is diminished by the questionable applicability of the theoretical framework to real crowdsourcing systems. The practical value is unclear without comparison to existing methods.

**Quality:** The technical derivation is sound, but the paper fails to address the critical difference between communication channels and crowdsourcing workers, which weakens the theoretical contribution. The paper would benefit from more careful analysis of the implementation constraints in crowdsourcing.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but some key technical points (e.g., the implementation of the coding scheme) are not sufficiently clarified.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper establishes a theoretically interesting connection between information theory and crowdsourcing.
- The framework provides a useful conceptual foundation for understanding crowdsourcing limits.
- The kIC analysis offers a concrete example of a potentially improved query scheme.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The channel coding framework is not appropriately applied to crowdsourcing due to fundamental differences in the system model.
- The derived bounds likely do not reflect practical performance limits due to implementation constraints.
- The paper lacks comparison to existing crowdsourcing methods, making it difficult to assess practical relevance.
- The main contribution appears to be a straightforward application of known information theory concepts rather than a novel theoretical advance.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem, the theoretical framework is misapplied to crowdsourcing, and the resulting bounds are unlikely to be tight or practically useful. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address the fundamental differences between communication channels and crowdsourcing workers, provide a meaningful comparison to existing methods, and demonstrate the practical relevance of their bounds. Without these revisions, the paper does not make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field.
