This paper explores the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing by modeling the problem in an information theoretic rate-distortion framework. The authors analyze the ultimate performance bounds that can be achieved by any form of query from the workers and any inference algorithm with a given budget. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach and results.

The main contribution of this paper is the derivation of an information-theoretic budget-fidelity bound for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and channel coding. The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They derive the minimum number of queries per item required to obtain an overall error probability of at most ε, and they analyze the performance of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear introduction to the problem, a thorough explanation of their approach, and a detailed analysis of their results. The paper includes several theorems and lemmas that provide a solid theoretical foundation for the authors' claims.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to provide a new perspective on the problem of crowdsourcing. By modeling the problem in an information theoretic framework, the authors are able to derive fundamental limits on the performance of crowdsourcing systems. This is a significant contribution, as it provides a theoretical foundation for the design and analysis of crowdsourcing systems.

However, there are some concerns about the novelty and practical value of the paper. The derivation of the information-theoretic bound is straightforward, and it is unclear whether this bound is tight. The authors acknowledge that the bound may be loose, but they do not provide a clear explanation of why this is the case.

Another concern is that the paper does not provide a clear comparison with state-of-the-art practical methods. The authors should compare their derived information-theoretic bound to existing methods to demonstrate its value and relevance.

There are also some minor issues with the paper. In equation (8), the authors should clarify that the minimum is over C, C' with E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t. The proof of Theorem 4 needs more elaboration, and the authors should clarify whether ε_S is larger than ε. In equation (17), the authors should explain why H_M(E(ε)) appears or cite prior work justifying it.

In conclusion, this paper provides a new perspective on the problem of crowdsourcing by modeling it in an information theoretic framework. While the derivation of the information-theoretic bound is straightforward, the paper raises important questions about the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems. However, the authors should address the concerns about novelty, practical value, and comparison with state-of-the-art methods to strengthen their contribution.
