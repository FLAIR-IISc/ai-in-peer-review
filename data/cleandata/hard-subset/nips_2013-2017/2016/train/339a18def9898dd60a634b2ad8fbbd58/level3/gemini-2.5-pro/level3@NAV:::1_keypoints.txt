- Proposes a novel information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication system (labels = source, workers = noisy channels, query/inference = coding).  
- Uses rate-distortion theory to map crowdsourcing budget to code rate and analyze the trade-off between labeling fidelity and budget.  
- Shifts focus from algorithm design to fundamental performance limits, complementing prior probabilistic/iterative aggregation work.  
- Derives theorems giving minimum required queries and actionable system-design insights, notably on the value of estimating worker skills.  
- Introduces k-incidence coding (kIC) as a concrete, well-motivated query scheme that grounds the theory.  
- Theoretical claims are technically sound and supported by proofs.  
- Main limitation: worker models (MSC and SHC) are simplifying and may not capture complex, task-dependent, or adversarial annotator behaviors.  
- Analysis is asymptotic, so applicability to finite, practical crowdsourcing settings is uncertain.  
- Lacks simulations or empirical validation to relate theoretical bounds to real-world algorithm performance.  
- Reviewer notes interest and clear presentation but acknowledges limited ability to judge novelty of specific information-theoretic techniques.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original, and significant contribution that establishes useful fundamental limits despite theoretical assumptions.
