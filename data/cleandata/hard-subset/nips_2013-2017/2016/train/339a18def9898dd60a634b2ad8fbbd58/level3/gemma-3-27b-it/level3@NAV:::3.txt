## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents an interesting and novel framework for analyzing crowdsourcing through the lens of information theory, deriving fundamental limits on the achievable fidelity given a specific budget. The core idea of modeling the crowdsourcing problem as a joint source-channel coding problem is promising and provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding the inherent trade-offs involved. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding and the derived bounds are particularly noteworthy. While the technical details were, at times, challenging to fully grasp, the potential impact of this work is significant, offering valuable insights into the design of more efficient and reliable crowdsourcing systems.

The paper’s strength lies in its formalization of a complex problem. Framing crowdsourcing in information-theoretic terms allows for the application of well-established tools and concepts, potentially leading to optimized algorithms and resource allocation strategies. However, accessibility is a significant weakness. While the authors acknowledge and attempt to address this with introductory explanations, the paper remains heavily rooted in information theory jargon, which may limit its reach to a broader audience within the machine learning community. Many results are presented using channel/code/distortion language rather than directly relating back to practical crowdsourcing scenarios. 

To improve clarity, I recommend expanding the discussion at the end of Section 3 to provide more intuitive takeaways for practitioners. A concluding section summarizing the key insights and their practical implications would also be extremely beneficial.  Specifically, restating the core findings in terms of crowdsourcing – “with this budget, you can expect this level of accuracy” – would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. 

The paper would also benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the areas of robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing, and research combining incentive structures with aggregation techniques.  Furthermore, the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" is unclear and should be clarified. A few minor edits are also needed: line 84 should read “are reviewed in,” and the typo on line 122, “A A(X1,X2),” should be corrected. Lines 275–276 are difficult to parse and require rephrasing. Finally, Figure 2 lacks sufficient detail in its caption and could benefit from a clearer visual representation of the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel framing of crowdsourcing, rigorous theoretical analysis, potentially impactful insights.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited accessibility, reliance on information theory jargon, insufficient connection to practical applications, lack of discussion of related incentive mechanisms.

Overall, despite the accessibility challenges, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the suggested improvements would significantly enhance its clarity and impact, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
