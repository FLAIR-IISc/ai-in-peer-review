**Paper Review: Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing**

This paper presents a novel approach to analyzing the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing by mapping it to a joint source-channel coding problem. The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off, providing a framework for understanding the ultimate performance limits of crowdsourcing systems.

**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system, where a taskmaster assigns tasks to workers with imperfect skill levels, and the crowdsourcer collects and analyzes the results to infer the correct labels. The authors use a joint source-channel coding framework to derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity. They also present a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper presents a fresh perspective on analyzing crowdsourcing systems by mapping it to a joint source-channel coding problem.
2. **Information-theoretic bounds**: The authors derive rigorous lower bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off, providing a fundamental limit on the performance of crowdsourcing systems.
3. **k-ary incidence coding**: The paper presents a specific query scheme and analyzes its performance, providing insights into the design of efficient crowdsourcing systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Model assumptions**: The paper assumes a simplistic model of worker reliability, merging worker skill and reliability into a single channel. This may not accurately capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing systems.
2. **Taskmaster allocation**: The paper assumes that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, which may not be realistic in practice.
3. **Channel models**: The paper uses a discrete channel model, which may not capture the nuances of real-world crowdsourcing systems, such as adversarial or informed malicious workers.
4. **Numerical results**: The paper presents numerical results, but the explanations and labeling of curves in Figure 2 are unclear.
5. **Methodological issues**: The paper assumes knowledge of P(B(X)) without explicitly stating it earlier in the paper.
6. **Minor corrections**: The paper has minor errors, such as incorrect figure references.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel approach to analyzing crowdsourcing systems, and the information-theoretic bounds derived are original and significant. However, the k-ary incidence coding scheme and its analysis are not entirely new, as similar schemes have been proposed in previous work.

**Significance**

The paper provides a fundamental limit on the performance of crowdsourcing systems, which is significant for understanding the ultimate limits of these systems. The results can be used to design more efficient crowdsourcing systems and to optimize the budget-fidelity trade-off.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and numerical results. However, the paper could benefit from more explicit explanations and justifications of the model assumptions and methodological choices.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-organized, but some sections, such as the numerical results, could be improved for clarity. The writing style is clear, but some sentences are long and convoluted.

**Recommendations**

1. **Clarify model assumptions**: The authors should clarify and justify the realism of the assumed worker/channel models.
2. **Improve numerical results**: The authors should improve the labeling of curves in Figure 2 and explicitly state what k=1 corresponds to.
3. **Address methodological issues**: The authors should explicitly state the assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) earlier in the paper.
4. **Minor corrections**: The authors should correct minor errors, such as incorrect figure references.

**Acceptance**

Based on the review, I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above. The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory, and the results have the potential to impact the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems.
