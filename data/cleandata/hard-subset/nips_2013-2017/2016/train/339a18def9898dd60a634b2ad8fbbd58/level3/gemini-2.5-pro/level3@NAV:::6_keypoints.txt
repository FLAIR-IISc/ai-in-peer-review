- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as parallel noisy channels and uses rate-distortion theory to bound minimum queries per item for a target fidelity.  
- Frames query design and response aggregation as a joint source-channel coding problem to derive budget-fidelity limits.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior work by aiming to define fundamental performance benchmarks instead of proposing practical aggregation algorithms.  
- Strength: novel and elegant application of a well-established theoretical framework; derivation appears technically sound and could set a useful theoretical ceiling.  
- Potential significance: if first to connect rate-distortion theory with crowdsourcing limits, represents a notable conceptual advance.  
- Major weakness: questionable assumption that channel-coding-style encoding freedom applies in crowdsourcing—practical query design constraints (e.g., non-i.i.d. repeated queries) may invalidate tightness of the bound.  
- Practicality concern: unclear what powerful error-correcting codes beyond repetition are implementable in crowdsourcing, so the bound may be loose and of limited practical relevance.  
- Recommendation: compare the theoretical bound to performance of state-of-the-art practical methods to demonstrate tightness.  
- Minor clarification: in Eq. (8), specify the minimum is over all joint distributions P(Z, Ẑ) with E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor clarification: expand the proof of Theorem 4 with more detail.  
- Minor clarification: in Theorem 4, since ln(1−q) is negative, clarify the relationship between ε_S and ĥε to make the inequality meaningful.
