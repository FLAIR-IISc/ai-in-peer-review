- Proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, modeling it as joint source-channel coding to derive budget–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Derives rate–distortion bounds for two scenarios: worker skill unknown (SL-UK) and worker skill perfectly known (SL-CS).  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding and compares its performance against the information-theoretic lower bounds.  
- Strength: novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing that could inspire future work.  
- Strength: rate–distortion framing connects well to recent crowdsourcing literature and is promising.  
- Strength: k-ary incidence coding analysis provides concrete, practical insights into query design.  
- Major concern: model conflates worker skill and reporting noise into a single channel parameter, failing to distinguish skill/rating from reliability.  
- Major concern: assumption that the taskmaster assigns identical query volumes to all workers is unrealistic given heterogeneous worker ratings.  
- Major concern: channel models do not capture adversarial or informed malicious workers common in real platforms.  
- Recommendation: clarify and justify the realism of the assumed worker/channel models to improve practical relevance.  
- Numerical issues: Fig. 2 is unclear without proper curve labeling and explanation of what k=1 represents.  
- Presentation issues: assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is stated too late and should be declared earlier.  
- Minor issues: incorrect figure references and inconsistent use/numbering of Theorem/Lemma/Corollary.  
- Overall: interesting theoretical framework, but methodological and realism concerns weaken the contribution; authors should address these before publication.
