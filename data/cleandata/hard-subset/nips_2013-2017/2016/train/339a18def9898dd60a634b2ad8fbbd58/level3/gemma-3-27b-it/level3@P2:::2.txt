This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. The authors derive asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item required to achieve a given fidelity, considering scenarios with both unknown and known worker skill levels. They also analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and provide a lower bound on its performance under a spammer-hammer channel model.

The work is well-motivated, as crowdsourcing is a prevalent technique in modern machine learning, particularly for data labeling. The framing of crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem is a novel and potentially insightful approach, allowing the application of information-theoretic tools to analyze the inherent limitations of the system. The paper clearly lays out the model, defining the data source, workers as noisy channels, the query scheme as encoding, and the inference process as decoding. The analysis, building on standard source-channel separation results, yields Theorems 2 and 3 which provide asymptotic query-rate limits – a valuable contribution for understanding the theoretical performance boundaries. The inclusion of the analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete example and demonstrates the applicability of the framework.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical rigor and the clarity of its presentation. The mathematical derivations are generally well-explained, and the notation is consistent. The connection to existing literature in crowdsourcing and information theory is also apparent, with relevant references cited throughout. The paper is well-organized, progressing logically from problem formulation to theoretical analysis and practical considerations like query pricing.

However, there are several weaknesses that need to be addressed. A major technical concern revolves around the application of source-channel separation. The authors implicitly assume that source-channel separation holds even when the channel state (worker skill level) is random and *unknown* to the encoder. This assumption is not standard and requires justification or a citation to support its validity in this specific setting. While the results are presented as fundamental limits, the applicability is limited if this core assumption is questionable.

Another modeling concern relates to the limited information available to the encoder and decoder. The model assumes that the encoder (taskmaster) only observes the labels returned by workers and does *not* have access to the original items as side information during the encoding phase. In many practical scenarios, the taskmaster has access to the original items (e.g., images) and can leverage this information to design more efficient queries. Ignoring this side information may lead to overly conservative bounds on the required query budget. Exploring the impact of incorporating item features into the encoding process could significantly strengthen the analysis.

Regarding the analysis of k-ary incidence coding, the wording in Section 4.2 and Figure 2 is potentially misleading. Stating that "the lower bounds improve as k and q grow" is imprecise. The lower bound *value* decreases as k and q grow, indicating a potential performance gain, but the bound itself isn’t “improving” in the sense of becoming tighter. A clearer phrasing would be “the lower bound decreases as k and q grow, suggesting improved potential performance.”

Finally, the analysis focuses on asymptotic limits. While valuable, it would be beneficial to explore non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) performance. Acknowledging the gap between theoretical limits and practical performance is crucial. The authors could consider citing relevant work on finite-blocklength JSCC converses, such as Kostina & Verdú (2013), to provide context and suggest potential avenues for future research.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Clear and rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Well-motivated problem and potential for significant impact.
*   Provides fundamental limits on performance that are valuable for understanding the trade-offs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Questionable assumption regarding source-channel separation with unknown channel state.
*   Oversimplified model by neglecting side information available to the taskmaster.
*   Imprecise wording in the description of k-ary incidence coding results.
*   Lack of consideration of finite-blocklength performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising first step towards a more rigorous, information-theoretic understanding of crowdsourcing. The model is well-defined, and the analysis is generally sound. However, the concerns raised above, particularly regarding the validity of the source-channel separation assumption and the limitations of the model, need to be addressed before the paper can be considered a major contribution. I recommend a **weak reject** with the expectation that the authors will address the reviewers' comments and resubmit a revised version. Addressing the concerns regarding source-channel separation and the potential for incorporating side information are crucial for elevating the quality and impact of the work.
