- Novel modeling: treats crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem (taskmaster=encoder, workers=noisy channels, crowdsourcer=decoder), offering an insightful foundation.  
- Theorems 2 and 3: derivation of asymptotic query-rate limits is a significant contribution.  
- Practical schemes: analysis of k-ary incidence coding and exploration of query pricing enrich the work.  
- Presentation: paper is well-written and represents a valuable first step toward rigorous, information-theoretic crowdsourcing analysis.  
- Major technical concern: applicability of source-channel separation is unjustified because the worker channel has a random state unknown to the encoder; authors must justify separation or reframe within JSCC.  
- Modeling limitation: encoder/decoder only use labels B(X) and ignore item features X as side information, which may reduce practical relevance.  
- Clarity issue: wording in Section 4.2/Figure 2 (“lower bounds improve as k and q grow”) is misleading; should state the lower-bound value decreases with k and q.  
- Missing finite-blocklength analysis: absence of nonasymptotic converses limits practical insight; cite/adapt Kostina & Verdú (2013).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel JSCC modeling, fundamental limits derivation, practical scheme analysis, clear writing, and real-world relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unjustified source-channel separation, omission of item side information, misleading wording in Section 4.2, and lack of nonasymptotic results.  
- Overall assessment: rated weak accept conditional on addressing the separation issue and other concerns to strengthen theoretical validity and practical relevance.
