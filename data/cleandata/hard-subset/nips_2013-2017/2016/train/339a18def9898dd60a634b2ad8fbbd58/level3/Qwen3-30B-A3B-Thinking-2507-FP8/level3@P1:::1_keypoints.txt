- Presents an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as noisy channels (M-ary symmetric and spammer-hammer) and reframes labeling as a source-channel coding problem.  
- Establishes fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoffs by mapping crowdsourcing budget to code rate and analyzing distortion versus budget.  
- Derives distortion-rate functions for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- Proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) that queries subsets of k items to provide combinatorial error-correction capabilities.  
- Provides performance bounds for kIC (Theorem 4) and positions it as a concrete coding-theory application to crowdsourcing.  
- Builds on prior crowdsourcing models (e.g., Dawid & Skene, majority voting) but differs by focusing on fundamental limits rather than aggregation algorithms.  
- Mathematical presentation is clear, with consistent notation and well-structured proofs.  
- Addresses practical considerations such as query pricing in an appendix.  
- Novelty: introduces an elegant information-theoretic perspective and a novel kIC querying scheme.  
- Strength: technically sound derivations and careful distinction between skill-known and skill-unknown cases.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation comparing theoretical bounds and kIC to practical algorithms (e.g., DS, majority voting).  
- Weakness: relies on assumptions (iid workers, large datasets, specific channel models) that may not hold on platforms like MTurk.  
- Weakness: limited exploration of how assumption violations affect applicability in real-world scenarios.  
- Weakness: insufficient guidance on how the theoretical limits translate to actionable crowdsourcing system design.  
- Accessibility concern: heavy information-theory content may be hard for non-specialist readers.  
- Recommendation: valuable theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better articulate practical significance and address limitations.
