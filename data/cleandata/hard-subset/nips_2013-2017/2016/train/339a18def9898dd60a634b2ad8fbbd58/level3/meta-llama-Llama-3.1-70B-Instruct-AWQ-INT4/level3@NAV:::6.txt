This paper presents a novel application of information-theoretic tools to the problem of crowdsourcing, specifically to derive a budget-fidelity bound using rate-distortion theory and channel coding. The authors model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyze the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of an information-theoretic budget-fidelity bound for crowdsourcing, which provides a fundamental limit on the achievable fidelity of crowdsourced tasks given a certain budget. The authors also investigate a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance.

The paper's strengths include its novel application of information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing, its clear and well-organized presentation, and its thorough analysis of the k-ary incidence coding scheme. The authors also provide a useful comparison between the derived information-theoretic bound and state-of-the-art practical methods.

However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's novelty and practical value. The derivation of the budget-fidelity bound is straightforward, and the bound may be very loose. Additionally, the paper's assumption that the taskmaster has wide freedom to design the input sequence u^n (encoding) may not be realistic in crowdsourcing scenarios, where workers may not be able to respond to queries in a way that is independent of their skill levels.

Furthermore, the paper's analysis of k-ary incidence coding is limited to a specific scenario, and it is unclear how to design u^n to achieve stronger error-correction in crowdsourcing. The paper also assumes that the workers' skill levels are unknown or perfectly known, which may not be realistic in many crowdsourcing scenarios.

To strengthen the paper, the authors could provide more discussion on the practical implications of their results, including how to design query schemes that achieve stronger error-correction in crowdsourcing. They could also investigate more realistic scenarios, such as workers with varying skill levels or tasks with different levels of difficulty.
