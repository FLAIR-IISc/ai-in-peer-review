- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem using rate–distortion and capacity tools.  
- Workers modeled primarily via M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel (SHC).  
- Maps crowdsourcing budget to code rate (queries per item) enabling information-theoretic analysis.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and skills known to crowdsourcer (SL-CS).  
- For iid sources, Hamming distortion, and non-adaptive schemes, derives lower bounds on rate to achieve target error.  
- Provides separate R(D)/capacity expressions for SL-UK vs SL-CS, highlighting the penalty of unknown skills.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): queries ask workers to cluster k items, disallowing invalid response patterns for redundancy.  
- For SHC, derives a lower bound on error under an oracle decoder and relates k, rate, error, and hammer probability q.  
- Offers a simple pricing rule-of-thumb comparing kIC variants.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior NIPS crowdsourcing work by focusing on information-theoretic limits rather than specific inference algorithms.  
- Connects kIC to pairwise/triplet comparison literature but analyzes it through channel coding lenses.  
- Novelty: explicit general-rate lower bounds under worker channel models and clear SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison.  
- Theoretical mapping and use of separation (R(D) vs C) are sound for iid sources and memoryless channels.  
- Theorems 2 and 3 cleanly quantify rate requirements and correctly differentiate average-channel capacity vs average of capacities.  
- kIC analysis under SHC gives meaningful oracle-based necessary conditions that improve with k and q.  
- Strong assumptions: iid items, memoryless independent workers, non-adaptive assignments, and idealized perfect skill knowledge in SL-CS.  
- kIC results restricted to binary labels, SHC, non-adaptive schemes, and lack empirical validation on finite datasets or against standard methods.  
- Proofs relegated to appendices but sketches in main text are consistent with standard arguments.  
- Notation could be tightened and some definitions (e.g., H_N, H_M, p_max) should be introduced earlier.  
- Suggest clarifying the budget-to-rate mapping and summarizing pricing implications in the main text rather than appendices.  
- Request explicit distortion definition for clustering "up to label permutations."  
- Minor editorial fixes needed (typos, figure references).  
- Significance: provides benchmarks for required budgets, quantifies value of skill estimation, and offers kIC design guidance; impact depends on fit to real platforms.  
- Pros: clear theoretical contribution, insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, concrete kIC scheme, and unifying information-theoretic perspective.  
- Cons: strong/idealized assumptions, limited kIC scope and oracle decoding, and minimal empirical work; practical factors like variable query costs and task difficulty are deferred.  
- Suggestions: add experiments comparing DS/EM, majority voting, and kIC; relax models to include task difficulty and partial skill estimates; extend kIC beyond binary/SHC and address decoding/assignment complexity; clarify clustering distortion metric.  
- Overall: theoretically strong and original benchmark paper; recommend accept if committee values foundational theory, but borderline if empirical validation and clarifications are required.
