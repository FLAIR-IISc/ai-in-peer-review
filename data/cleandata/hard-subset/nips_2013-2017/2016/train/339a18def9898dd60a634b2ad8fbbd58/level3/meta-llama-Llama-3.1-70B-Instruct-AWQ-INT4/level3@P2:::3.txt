This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing. The authors model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and derive bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and their approach.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing. The authors consider two scenarios: one where the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and one where they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They show that in the SL-UK scenario, the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity is given by Theorem 2, which depends on the entropy of the source, the number of classes, and the maximum probability of a class. In the SL-CS scenario, the minimum number of queries per item is given by Theorem 3, which depends on the entropy of the source, the number of classes, and the expected entropy of the workers' skill levels.

The authors also introduce a new query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which poses a query as a set of k items and inquires the workers to identify those with the same label. They derive a lower bound on the performance of kIC with a spammer-hammer worker pool and show that the bound improves as k and the probability of a hammer increase.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing, its clear and concise presentation, and its derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors also provide a good discussion of the related work and highlight the differences between their approach and previous work.

However, the paper could benefit from some improvements. The reviewer found the model and insights interesting and potentially useful but had difficulty fully understanding the technical details. The authors could provide more high-level intuition and practical takeaways to make the paper more accessible to non-information-theory readers. Additionally, the reviewer recommends restating many results in crowdsourcing/application terms instead of channel/code/distortion language.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results. For example, how do the bounds on the minimum number of queries per item translate to practical crowdsourcing scenarios? What are the implications of the results for the design of crowdsourcing systems?

Finally, the reviewer notes that the paper could benefit from some minor copyedits and clarifications. For example, the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" is not clear, and the reviewer recommends clarifying this distinction.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores high on quality, originality, and significance. The authors present a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing and derive bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off that are relevant to practical crowdsourcing scenarios. However, the paper could benefit from some improvements in clarity and accessibility.
