- Reformulates labeling in crowdsourcing as a coding problem over noisy channels representing worker skill, deriving budget–fidelity tradeoff limits.
- Introduces two worker channel models: symmetric memoryless channel and spammer/hammer channel.
- Proposes k‑incidence coding, a basic scheme that labels subsets of k items.
- Derives information-theoretic bounds on minimum queries per item for given fidelity, with theorems for unknown and perfectly known worker skills.
- Analyzes k‑incidence coding performance with a spammer‑hammer worker pool and provides numerical results.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and explains framework and results clearly.
- Novel use of information theory to analyze fundamental limits in crowdsourcing.
- Thorough review of related research demonstrating strong understanding of background work.
- Strength: derivation of rigorous bounds on queries required for target fidelity.
- Weakness: worker response models are relatively simple and may not capture real-world complexity.
- Weakness: analysis limited to labeling tasks, unclear extensibility to other crowdsourcing types.
- Weakness: numerical results are specific and may not generalize to other scenarios.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, solid theoretical contribution, thorough literature grounding.
- Arguments against acceptance: simplistic assumptions, limited scope, potential lack of practical generalizability.
