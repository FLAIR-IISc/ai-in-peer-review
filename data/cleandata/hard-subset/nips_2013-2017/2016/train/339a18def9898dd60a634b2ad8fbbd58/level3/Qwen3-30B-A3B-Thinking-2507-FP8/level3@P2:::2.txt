

# Review for "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states, establishing fundamental limits on the query-rate (budget) required to achieve a target fidelity (error probability). The authors consider a crowdsourcing setting where the taskmaster acts as an encoder, workers as parallel noisy channels with unknown skill levels (channel states), and the crowdsourcer as a decoder. The paper extends standard information-theoretic results to derive asymptotic bounds on the minimum query-rate required for different scenarios.

The work relates to previous NIPS research on crowdsourcing, such as the Dawid-Skene algorithm (NIPS 2008) and recent EM-based inference approaches (NIPS 2013, 2015), by shifting the focus from practical inference algorithms to fundamental performance limits. It builds upon the information-theoretic approach to human computation introduced in "Vox Populi" (NIPS 2013) but extends it to include random channel states (worker skill levels) and formalizes the budget-fidelity trade-off.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by framing crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem with random channel states, establishing fundamental limits that previous practical approaches (majority voting, EM, etc.) could not address. This represents a significant theoretical advance that opens new research directions.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and effective use of figures (particularly Figure 1). The mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail to be understandable to experts in information theory.
- **Significance:** The results provide a theoretical benchmark for evaluating crowdsourcing systems, which is valuable for both practitioners designing crowdsourcing platforms and researchers developing new algorithms. The derived bounds help identify when a system is operating near fundamental limits.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Quality:** The key technical gap is the lack of justification for applying source-channel separation when the channel state (worker skill levels) is unknown to the encoder (taskmaster) but possibly known to the decoder (crowdsourcer). The paper cites standard JSCC results but fails to address whether source-channel separation holds in this specific setting with random channel states. The authors should cite and justify their use of this result (e.g., reference to Cover & Thomas or more recent work on state-dependent channels).
- **Quality:** The modeling assumes the encoder/decoder have access only to labels and ignore the original items as side information. In many practical crowdsourcing scenarios (e.g., image labeling), the original items could provide valuable side information that could improve performance. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed.
- **Clarity:** The wording in Section 4.2 and Figure 2 is misleading. The statement "lower bounds improve as k and q grow" should clarify that the lower bound value decreases (meaning better performance is achievable), not that the bound itself tightens without reference to an upper bound.
- **Significance:** The paper would be strengthened by including nonasymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses, which would provide more practical guidance for real-world crowdsourcing applications. The authors should cite Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pros:*
- Establishes the first fundamental limits for crowdsourcing with random worker skill levels
- Provides a clean information-theoretic framework that unifies previous work
- Well-written with clear exposition of complex concepts
- The k-ary incidence coding analysis offers a concrete example of a practical query scheme

*Cons:*
- Technical gap in justifying source-channel separation with unknown channel states
- Modeling omission of side information (original items)
- Misleading presentation of results in Section 4.2
- Lacks finite-blocklength analysis for more practical guidance

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a promising theoretical contribution to crowdsourcing by establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The information-theoretic framework provides valuable insights that can guide the design of future crowdsourcing systems. While the technical justification for source-channel separation with random channel states is a significant gap, the paper's core contribution remains novel and important for the field.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental theoretical question in a growing area of machine learning research. The authors have made a solid first step toward a more complete information-theoretic understanding of crowdsourcing systems. With the technical gap addressed (by adding a citation or brief justification), this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper presents an important theoretical advance that will inform future crowdsourcing research. The technical gap is significant but can be addressed with a minor revision. The paper's novelty, clarity, and significance outweigh the identified weaknesses.
