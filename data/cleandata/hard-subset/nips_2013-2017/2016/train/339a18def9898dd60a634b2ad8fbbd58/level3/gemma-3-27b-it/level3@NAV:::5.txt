## Review of “Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing”

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, establishing lower bounds on the number of queries required per task to achieve a desired error probability. The core idea is modeling the crowdsourcing process as a joint source-channel coding problem, where workers are treated as parallel, independent noisy channels, specifically utilizing symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and a spammer-hammer channel (SHC) for spammers. The analysis considers two scenarios: full knowledge of worker skills and no knowledge, with a taskmaster assumed to have no prior skill information. The paper further analyzes a k-ary incidence coding scheme and touches upon query pricing implications.

The approach presented is novel and valuable. Framing crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework provides a rigorous foundation for understanding fundamental limitations, which could be highly impactful for system designers and pricing strategies. The modeling of workers as noisy channels, incorporating both standard errors and malicious spammer behavior via the SHC model, is a particularly strong point. The exploration of both full and no-knowledge scenarios regarding worker skills adds nuance and practical relevance. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a promising direction, offering potential for error correction through query structure. 

However, the paper’s main weakness is the lack of experimental validation. While the theoretical bounds are insightful, their practical significance remains unclear without comparison to existing crowdsourcing platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) or simulations with more realistic worker models.  The paper would significantly benefit from demonstrating the tightness of these bounds in real-world or simulated scenarios. 

Regarding manuscript structure, Section 2.4 feels somewhat lengthy and could be condensed. Similarly, the detailed derivations in Section 3.1, while rigorous, could be streamlined by including proof sketches instead of full proofs within the main body of the paper. This would improve readability and focus the discussion on the core contributions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a critical problem in crowdsourcing. The information-theoretic framework offers a powerful tool for understanding fundamental limits and informing system design.
**Arguments against acceptance:** The absence of experimental validation limits the immediate impact of the work. Some sections could be shortened for clarity and conciseness.

**Overall:** Despite the lack of experimental validation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are significant. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the length/clarity suggestions and considering including at least some preliminary experimental results or simulations in future work. The potential for this work to influence the design and pricing of crowdsourcing systems is substantial.
