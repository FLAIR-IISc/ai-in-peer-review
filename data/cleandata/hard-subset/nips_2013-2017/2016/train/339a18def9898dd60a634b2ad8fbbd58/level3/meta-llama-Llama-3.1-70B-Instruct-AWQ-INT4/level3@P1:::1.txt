This paper presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing workers as noisy channels and reformulating the labeling problem as a coding problem. The authors use an information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the trade-off between budget and fidelity.

The paper begins by introducing a communication system model for crowdsourcing, where the crowd is represented by a set of parallel noisy channels, each modeling a worker with imperfect skill levels. The authors then present two channel models: the M-ary symmetric channel model (MSC) and the spammer-hammer channel model (SHC). They also introduce a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which poses a query as a set of k items and inquires the workers to identify those with the same label.

The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity in crowdsourcing. They present two theorems, one for the case where the worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and another for the case where the skill levels are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The theorems provide a lower bound on the number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity.

The paper also presents a study on the performance of kIC with a SHC worker pool. The authors derive a lower-bound on the performance of kIC and present numerical results along with the information-theoretic lower bounds. They also propose an approximate rule of thumb for pricing, which suggests that the query price should be set proportional to the number of items in the query.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors present a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing workers as noisy channels and reformulating the labeling problem as a coding problem.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the trade-off between budget and fidelity.
* The authors derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity in crowdsourcing.
* The paper presents a study on the performance of kIC with a SHC worker pool, which provides insights into the design of coding schemes for crowdsourcing.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes a simplistic model of the crowd, where each worker is represented by a noisy channel. In practice, the crowd may be more complex, with workers having varying levels of expertise and biases.
* The paper focuses on the labeling problem, which may not be representative of all crowdsourcing tasks.
* The authors do not provide a clear evaluation of the proposed coding scheme, kIC, in terms of its practical performance.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing workers as noisy channels and reformulating the labeling problem as a coding problem. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the trade-off between budget and fidelity. However, the paper has some limitations, including the assumption of a simplistic model of the crowd and the focus on the labeling problem.
