Summary
This paper models crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels and studies fundamental tradeoffs between labeling fidelity and budget. Workers are modeled as memoryless channels, with two canonical models: (i) an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) that captures uniform confusion among labels, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) that mixes perfect workers with random spammers. The labeling pipeline is reformulated as a joint source–channel coding problem: the dataset labels form the source, queries correspond to an encoder, worker responses form parallel channels, and inference is the decoder. The budget is mapped to code rate (queries per item), enabling the use of rate–distortion and capacity concepts to derive lower bounds on the budget needed to achieve a target distortion (Hamming risk). Two scenarios are treated: SL-UK (unknown skill levels) and SL-CS (skill levels known to the crowdsourcer). The paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum queries per item Rmin as functions of the source entropy and channel parameters, clarifying how knowing worker skills shifts the effective capacity. It also proposes k-incidence coding (kIC), which queries subsets of k items and restricts answer choices to internally consistent clusterings, yielding implicit error-correcting structure. For SHC workers, the paper analyzes kIC and gives a converse-style lower bound on the budget needed to reach a target error, along with a pricing rule-of-thumb that accounts for the differing per-query burden as k varies.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to classical Dawid–Skene EM-style aggregation and majority voting, to bandit-survey budget allocation (Abraham et al.), and to spectral/EM methods with theoretical guarantees (Zhang et al.). It is closest in spirit to information-theoretic analyses of crowdsourcing reliability and budget-optimal task allocation (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah) but takes a more explicit source–channel separation route with general rate–distortion formulations and channel models. The kIC idea aligns with pairwise/tuplewise querying used in clustering with missing data (Vinayak et al.), but here is framed as a code with built-in redundancy.

Strengths
- Clear, principled information-theoretic formulation that maps budget to rate and fidelity to distortion; the SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is insightful.
- Derivation of general lower bounds for MSC worker pools that isolate source uncertainty H(B(X)) and channel “capacity-like” terms; easy to interpret.
- k-incidence coding is a simple, implementable scheme that naturally embeds error correction by disallowing inconsistent answers; analysis under SHC is novel and illuminating.
- The work offers a unifying lens for disparate crowdsourcing designs and provides useful baselines for what is fundamentally achievable independent of specific algorithms.
- Discussion of pricing for different k is practical and helps translate theory into platform design choices.

Weaknesses
- Worker models are simplistic. MSC ignores item difficulty and systematic confusions; SHC is an extreme bimodal abstraction. Many real platforms exhibit instance-dependent noise, correlations, and adversaries.
- The SL-CS assumption of perfectly known skill at the decoder is strong; the gap between SL-UK and SL-CS bounds would be more actionable with finite-sample skill-estimation analysis.
- Non-adaptive codes only are considered, and large-blocklength asymptotics underlie the bounds; finite-budget regimes typical of crowdsourcing are not characterized.
- Limited empirical validation. Aside from the kIC converse vs IT bound plot, there is no simulation comparing kIC to standard baselines (majority vote, DS, spectral/EM) under matched/mismatched worker models.
- The pricing rule is heuristic; cost heterogeneity across k (cognitive load, time) and worker dropout/learning effects are not modeled.

Quality
- The theoretical development appears sound and carefully tied to classic rate–distortion and capacity results. Theorems 2–3 transparently explain how H(B), HN(.), and channel entropies determine Rmin. The kIC SHC bound is conservative (oracle decoder) and clearly stated as a necessary condition.
- Some modeling choices (e.g., feedforward effects of k on SHC) are acknowledged but not fully integrated into the theorems; clarifying assumptions and the scope of the converse for kIC would help.
- The lack of experiments limits assessment of practical relevance and tightness of bounds in finite regimes.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with an accessible high-level narrative for ML audiences. The mapping from crowdsourcing primitives to coding constructs is clear.
- Notation could be tightened: M vs N (number of choices vs number of classes), and the use of HN(.)/HM(.) might benefit from brief intuition inline.
- A small worked example showing how kIC restricts answer sets for k=3, N>2 would aid intuition.

Originality
- Applying rate–distortion and source–channel separation to crowdsourcing in this general way is a fresh angle compared to prior minimax/statistical analyses. The SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is novel and clarifies the role of skill knowledge.
- The kIC framing as an error-correcting code and the SHC converse are original, though tuplewise queries have appeared before in clustering.

Significance
- The bounds can guide task designers on whether a target accuracy is even feasible under a given budget and worker pool quality, independent of specific algorithms.
- kIC offers a simple, implementable idea with potential robustness gains and a pathway to principled pricing.
- Broader impact depends on empirical follow-up; as-is, the paper sets benchmarks and ideas more than ready-to-use algorithms.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample, non-asymptotic bounds or experiments that show how close practical decoders (majority vote, DS, spectral/EM) get to the IT limits under MSC/SHC.
- Relax worker models: include instance difficulty (Dawid–Skene with item difficulty), structured confusions, or Item Response Theory; discuss how this affects the channel abstraction.
- Analyze partial skill estimation (e.g., SL-EST with estimation error) to bridge SL-UK and SL-CS and quantify achievable gaps with realistic calibration.
- Explore adaptive codes: show whether adaptivity (query selection based on prior answers) can improve rates relative to the separation baseline.
- Extend kIC theory to multi-class N>2 and compare k tradeoffs empirically, including measured per-query costs and worker completion times.
- Clarify the feedforward issue in SHC+kIC and whether the converse accounts for it fully; add intuition for Lemma 1.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and rigorous framework; advances theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing fundamentals.
- Bridges information theory and crowdsourcing with concrete, interpretable bounds and a practical coding idea (kIC).
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work on code design for human computation and better worker/noise models.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplified worker models and strong assumptions (perfect skill knowledge in SL-CS).
- Limited empirical validation; unclear finite-budget tightness and practical gains.
- Non-adaptive focus; no constructive algorithms shown to approach the bounds.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a compelling theoretical lens and useful lower bounds with an implementable coding idea. Strengthening empirical validation and relaxing modeling assumptions would make it a clear accept.
