- Paper reframes crowdsourcing with unreliable workers as a joint source–channel coding problem using rate–distortion to derive budget–fidelity lower bounds.
- Provides bounds for two scenarios (worker skills unknown vs. known to decoder) and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a spammer–hammer channel.
- Conceptually appealing mapping to source–channel separation and use of R(D), connecting to prior theoretical crowdsourcing work.
- Strength: original and insightful use of rate–distortion with interpretable limits from the SL-UK vs SL-CS separation.
- Strength: Presents kIC as a concrete, code-like querying scheme that can be compared to the bounds.
- Strength: Oracle-versus-limit comparisons help position achievable schemes relative to fundamental limits.
- Weakness: Worker modeled as a single memoryless channel, conflating competence (confusion matrix) and reporting noise/intent—misses per-worker systematic biases.
- Weakness: Equal query allocation to all workers ignores heterogeneity; adaptive or bandit-based allocations could change limits significantly.
- Weakness: MSC/SHC worker models (spammer–hammer) need stronger justification and discussion of adversarial/Byzantine behaviors.
- Methodology/clarity: The assumption that P(B(X)) is known is introduced late—should be stated up front and robustness to misspecification or online learning discussed.
- Presentation: Numerical plots need clearer labeling (Fig.2 curves, meaning of k=1) and fix figure references; theorems/lemmas/corollaries should be numbered/ formatted consistently.
- Scope: Restriction to non-adaptive coding and equal rates limits practical applicability; adaptive/sequential designs should be discussed.
- Quality/Originality/Significance: Analysis appears sound under assumptions; originality is moderate-to-high for the JSCC/R(D) perspective; conceptual value high but practical impact depends on addressing heterogeneity and adversaries.
- Pros for acceptance: Elegant theoretical reframing and potential to stimulate cross-fertilization between information theory and crowdsourcing.
- Cons for acceptance: Unrealistic worker/channel and allocation assumptions, clarity/presentation issues, and limited empirical interpretability.
- Recommendation: Borderline—encourage revision to address modeling realism, adaptive allocation, clearer numerics, and presentation fixes.
