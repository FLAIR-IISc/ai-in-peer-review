This paper develops an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing that formalizes the budget–fidelity trade-off and derives lower bounds on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability. The crowd is modeled as a bank of parallel discrete memoryless channels (one per worker), with two error models: (i) M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and (ii) spammer–hammer channel SHC(q). The taskmaster uses non-adaptive coding (fixed task assignment independent of answers). Two knowledge regimes are analyzed: SL-UK (unknown worker skills to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer) and SL-CS (worker skills perfectly known to the crowdsourcer).

Main technical contributions:
- Rate–distortion view of crowdsourcing: The authors cast the design goal as minimizing average distortion (with Hamming loss for labeled/clustering tasks) subject to a query budget, and connect feasibility to source–channel separation. This yields information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (Rmin) to meet a target error rate ε.
- Fundamental limits under MSC:
  • Theorem 2 (SL-UK): For an N-ary source with prior P(B(X)), the minimum queries per item to achieve error ≤ ε is Rmin = [H(B(X)) − HN(ε)] / [log2 M − HM(E(·))], under ε ≤ min{1 − pmax, 1 − 1/N}, where E(·) is the distribution over worker error parameters. Intuitively, the system behaves as if served by an “average worker.”
  • Theorem 3 (SL-CS): With perfect knowledge of individual worker skills at the decoder, Rmin = [H(B(X)) − HN(ε)] / [log2 M − E(HM(·))], reflecting “average of qualities” rather than “quality of the average.” The comparison clarifies the benefit of estimating worker reliabilities, consistent with the crowdsourcing literature on EM/Dawid–Skene and related models.
- k-ary incidence coding (kIC): The paper formalizes kIC (querying k items jointly and asking for within-query equivalence classes), highlighting its built-in error-correction via invalid response elimination. For SHC(q) workers and binary labels, Lemma 1 gives the error probability for a spammer under kIC, and Theorem 4 provides a necessary condition on the per-item rate R to achieve error ≤ ε: R must exceed a threshold that scales as 1/k, reflecting the efficiency of higher-arity queries. A corollary specializing the information-theoretic bound to SHC(q) with SL-CS gives Rmin ≥ [1 − Hb(ε)] / [q log2 M], which serves as a universal benchmark. The pricing discussion in Appendix E suggests query cost should scale approximately linearly with k, offering a practical guideline.

Relation to prior work: The work complements existing aggregation/inference approaches (majority voting; Dawid–Skene EM and variants; spectral/EM hybrids [e.g., Zhang et al.]; convex methods for pairwise clustering [e.g., Vinayak et al.]; and budget-aware task allocation [Karger–Oh–Shah; bandit survey formulations]). Unlike these, which optimize algorithms or assignments under specific worker models, this paper focuses on fundamental lower bounds and a coding-theoretic lens (joint source–channel coding), offering system-level guidance independent of a specific inference algorithm.

Quality:
- The modeling choices are clear, and the separation-based arguments are appropriate for the non-adaptive, memoryless setting. The distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS is insightful and technically well-motivated.
- The kIC analysis under SHC(q) is carefully set up and yields interpretable scaling laws. The oracle-decoder assumption is conservative for lower bounds.
- I did not verify the appendices in detail; including brief proof sketches in the main text would strengthen confidence.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and clearly written. The exposition of the coding perspective for non-information-theorists is helpful. Sections 2.1–2.3 articulate the mapping from crowdsourcing to channel coding cleanly.
- Suggestions: (i) Shorten Section 3.1 (textbook background) and move some to appendix; (ii) streamline Section 2.4; (iii) include succinct proof sketches and a clear statement of assumptions (independence, memorylessness, non-adaptivity, i.i.d. priors, known M vs N, when feedforward/state dependence is or isn’t allowed).

Originality:
- Framing crowdsourcing’s budget–fidelity trade-off via rate–distortion and deriving explicit Rmin bounds under MSC/SHC seems novel for NIPS. The kIC characterization as a code with inherent error-correction is a fresh perspective, and the pricing heuristic derived from the bounds is practically appealing.

Significance:
- The bounds can guide system designers: e.g., when it is beneficial to estimate worker reliabilities, how k trades off against budget, and how to price multi-item queries. The results provide an interpretable benchmark for evaluating algorithmic advances and task-allocation strategies. That said, the lack of empirical validation limits immediate impact.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- No experiments or simulations to show how close practical schemes (e.g., DS-EM, spectral methods, majority) come to the bounds under MSC/SHC, or how kIC performs in practice versus 2IC/standard labeling under a fixed budget.
- The analysis of kIC is restricted to N = 2; extension to multi-class would markedly increase applicability.
- Non-adaptive assignments only; many systems are adaptive, and separation may not hold with input-dependent difficulty/state. The “static feedforward” note for kIC deserves a more formal treatment of when the separation-based converse still applies.
- Worker models are simplified (MSC/SHC) and assume independence; adversarial or correlated errors, item difficulty, and class imbalance beyond pmax deserve discussion.
- Some notation could be tightened (e.g., clarify M vs N vs k consistently).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation of fundamental limits for crowdsourcing.
- Insightful comparison between unknown vs known worker skills.
- Novel view of k-ary queries as codes with error-correction; useful scaling laws and pricing guidance.
- Good connections to, and differentiation from, prior NIPS literature.

Cons for acceptance:
- No empirical validation or simulations.
- kIC results limited to binary labeling.
- Background section length vs space for proof sketches.
- Practical impact would be higher with concrete algorithmic/assignment schemes approaching the bounds.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add simulations on synthetic MSC/SHC crowds comparing majority, DS-EM, and a simple kIC decoder against the derived bounds; report gaps.
- Provide proof sketches of Theorems 2–4 in the main paper and a succinct statement of technical conditions under which the separation argument holds here.
- Extend kIC analysis to N > 2, or at least discuss the challenges and expected scaling.
- Discuss adaptive task allocation and whether similar converse-style bounds can be obtained.
- Clarify the pricing rule of thumb and its dependence on worker mix and ε; present a simple numerical example.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical framing and limits are novel and valuable; adding even modest empirical evidence and trimming background would make this a stronger contribution.
