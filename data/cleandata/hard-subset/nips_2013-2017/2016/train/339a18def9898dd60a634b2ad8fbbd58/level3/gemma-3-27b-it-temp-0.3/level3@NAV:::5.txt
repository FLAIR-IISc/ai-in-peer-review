## Review of “Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing”

This paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, framing it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The authors leverage information theory to derive lower bounds on the number of queries required per task to achieve a target error probability, modeling workers as parallel independent channels, and considering both symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channels (SHC). The analysis is performed under both full and no knowledge of worker skills, with a focus on non-adaptive coding schemes. The work is well-organized and clearly written, presenting a complex topic in a digestible manner. 

The core contribution lies in establishing these theoretical limits, offering a valuable benchmark for evaluating practical crowdsourcing systems like Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). The introduction effectively motivates the problem and connects it to existing applications. The modeling choices, particularly the use of information theory, are well-justified and provide a rigorous framework for analysis. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a thoughtful addition, and the analysis of its performance with a SHC worker pool provides concrete insights. The paper builds upon existing work in crowdsourcing, such as the Dawid-Skene algorithm and majority voting, by providing a fundamental limit against which these heuristics can be compared.

However, a significant weakness is the complete lack of experimental validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, their practical relevance remains unclear without comparison to real-world performance on platforms like AMT. The paper would be significantly strengthened by simulations or experiments demonstrating how closely practical schemes approach these theoretical bounds. Furthermore, while well-written, Section 2.4 on distortion and the design problem feels somewhat lengthy and could be streamlined. Similarly, Section 3.1 providing background on joint source-channel coding, while relevant, could be condensed.  Adding proof sketches for the key theorems would improve accessibility without sacrificing rigor. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Provides fundamental limits and benchmarks for practical systems.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Potentially useful for designing and pricing crowdsourcing tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of experimental validation.
*   Some sections could be shortened for conciseness.
*   Missing proof sketches for key theorems.



**Overall:** Despite the lack of experimental validation, I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The approach is novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the results have the potential to inform the design of more efficient and reliable crowdsourcing systems. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the need for experimental validation in future work.
