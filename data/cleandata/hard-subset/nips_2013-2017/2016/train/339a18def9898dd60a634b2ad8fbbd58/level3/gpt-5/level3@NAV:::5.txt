This paper models crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic rate–distortion lens to derive fundamental lower bounds on the budget (queries per item) required to achieve a target error probability. Workers are treated as independent parallel channels, with errors modeled by M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and a spammer–hammer channel SHC(q). The analysis focuses on nonadaptive task assignment and considers two knowledge regimes: SL-UK (no knowledge of worker skills) and SL-CS (perfect knowledge at the crowdsourcer but not the taskmaster, which assigns equal load to all workers). The paper also analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), giving a necessary condition on the minimum rate with an SHC(q) worker pool and a useful rule-of-thumb for pricing. Relative to prior NIPS work—majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM and its variants, spectral/graphical methods, and bandit-style adaptive assignment—the paper takes a complementary “converse-style” approach, providing limits rather than new algorithms.

Quality: The theoretical development appears sound and carefully positioned, with clean statements of lower bounds for both SL-UK and SL-CS (clarifying how knowledge of skills affects capacity). I did not verify the appendices line-by-line. The assumptions (independent, memoryless workers; nonadaptive assignments; equal load) are clearly stated but restrictive.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and well written. However, Sec. 2.4 and especially Sec. 3.1 are longer than needed for a NIPS audience; I recommend shortening Sec. 2.4 and removing or sharply compressing Sec. 3.1, replacing the space with brief proof sketches for the main theorems to aid readability.

Originality: The information-theoretic formulation of budget–fidelity trade-offs in CS, and the specialization to MSC/SHC with explicit rate lower bounds and kIC analysis, are novel and distinct from prior algorithmic lines of work.

Significance: These limits can guide system designers and platform pricing, offering actionable insights (e.g., dependence on q, k, M). The lack of experiments/simulations is a missed opportunity: comparisons with majority vote, DS, and spectral/EM on synthetic or AMT-style data would contextualize the achievable gap to the limits, and a discussion of adaptivity (e.g., bandit survey) versus nonadaptive bounds would broaden impact.

Pros:
- Clear, principled limits for SL-UK and SL-CS; insightful kIC lower bounds.
- Novel and practically relevant framing; pricing implications.
- Well written and organized.

Cons:
- No empirical validation or simulation against practical schemes (AMT, DS, spectral).
- Restrictive nonadaptive, equal-assignment assumption; limited discussion of adaptivity.
- Overlong background; lacks proof sketches in the main text.

Overall: A strong theoretical contribution; I lean toward acceptance if the authors add proof sketches and at least a focused simulation section illustrating gap-to-limit under MSC/SHC and kIC.
