Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and uses rate–distortion theory to derive fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Items are mapped to a discrete source B(X) with distribution P(B(X)); workers are modeled as noisy channels (primarily M-ary symmetric channels and a spammer–hammer channel). Theorems 2–3 give lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error under two regimes: (i) skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The paper also analyzes a specific non-adaptive query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which asks workers to partition k items, thereby implicitly encoding multiple pairwise constraints and excluding invalid responses (an error-correction effect). For a SHC(q) worker model, it derives a necessary condition on the rate R to achieve target accuracy and compares this to an information-theoretic benchmark specialized from Theorem 3. The work is positioned among classical aggregation methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene EM) and more recent inference/graph-based approaches [e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Whitehill et al.; Smyth et al.; Vinayak–Oymak–Hassibi], as well as adaptive worker-selection/bandit formulations.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The rate–distortion framing is elegant and provides a clean vocabulary for budget–fidelity limits in crowdsourcing.
- General lower bounds: Theorems 2–3 articulate how the minimum rate scales with source entropy and “channel quality,” and they clarify the benefit of knowing worker skill.
- Concrete code family: kIC is a natural scheme for multi-item queries with built-in consistency checks; the analysis under SHC connects coding ideas to practical query design and pricing.
- Potential impact: Establishing IT-type benchmarks can guide the design/evaluation of inference algorithms and task allocation in future work.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Modeling assumptions:
- Worker model conflates skill and response noise. In many settings, “skill” determines a confusion structure over labels (systematic errors), while response noise models lapses or spamming. The MSC/SHC abstractions collapse these effects into one parameter. Please clarify what phenomena are captured by “skill” and discuss alternative models (e.g., per-worker confusion matrices) and whether the main conclusions extend.
- Homogeneous query allocation. The analysis assumes the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers. This is unrealistic when platforms expose reputation or when one can adaptively route tasks (as in bandit survey/active task assignment). The paper should acknowledge this limitation and discuss how the bounds might change with adaptive or heterogeneous allocations. Even a discussion of separation-type arguments in the adaptive case would be useful.
- Adversarial/strategic behavior. The SHC(q) model captures random spammers, not informed adversaries. Some real platforms face targeted, label-dependent manipulation. Please justify the realism of the channel models used and discuss whether the framework can incorporate adversarial channels or Byzantine noise.

Technical clarity:
- Assumption on P(B(X)). The assumption that the crowdsourcer knows P(B(X)) appears late (p.6, line ~246). It should be stated upfront in the model section, with discussion of how one might estimate P(B(X)) in practice and the sensitivity of bounds to misspecification.
- N vs. M. Throughout, N is the number of source labels and M the number of response options. In many labeling tasks M=N; with kIC these can differ. Please standardize notation and explicitly state when M≠N and how that affects Theorems 2–3.
- Achievability/converse. The bounds are presented as minimum rates. The achievability relies on separation; the converse depends on averaging over worker skills (SL-UK) vs. conditioning (SL-CS). It would help to briefly sketch these arguments in the main text (not only appendices) and to clarify any finite-blocklength effects relevant to realistic dataset sizes.

kIC analysis and experiments:
- Interpretability. Explain explicitly that k=1 corresponds to single-item direct labeling (and connect to Lemma 1 of [8]) so readers can interpret Fig. 2 and the progression with k.
- Figure 2. Improve curve labeling (legend, axis titles, parameter values for q, k, target error) and explain the gap between the kIC lower bound and the information-theoretic limit. Is the gap due to suboptimality of kIC or to looseness of the bound?
- Realism of SHC dependence on k. Under SHC, the spammer error depends on k; please discuss whether this dependence matches observed worker behavior (task fatigue, UI complexity).
- Pricing rule. The pricing heuristic π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 is interesting; please derive it more explicitly and validate empirically (even via small-scale simulation measuring worker time/accuracy).

Quality
- Generally sound and well-grounded in information theory; assumptions are strong and should be better justified. The kIC bounds are necessary conditions under an oracle decoder; the paper should emphasize this and qualify claims about “fundamental limits” accordingly.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear but needs improved exposition: state distributional assumptions earlier; tighten notation (N vs. M); add missing figure labels; and include short proof sketches for main theorems.
- Number Theorems, Lemmas, and Corollaries consistently (Theorem 1, 2, 3, …) and refer to them accordingly.

Originality
- The explicit rate–distortion formulation for crowdsourcing and the comparison of SL-UK vs. SL-CS within an IT framework appear novel for NIPS. kIC as a coding viewpoint is a nice contribution, though related in spirit to prior incidence/graph-based querying.

Significance
- Provides useful conceptual benchmarks and could influence the design and evaluation of crowdsourcing systems. Practical impact will depend on relaxing key assumptions (heterogeneous/adaptive assignment, richer worker models) and on empirical validation.

Reasons to accept
- Elegant information-theoretic formalization of budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Clear theoretical insights into the value of worker-skill knowledge.
- Concrete analysis of a natural query family (kIC) with implications for pricing.

Reasons to reject
- Strong modeling assumptions (homogeneous allocation; simplistic worker channels) limit practical relevance.
- Limited empirical validation; figures and explanations need clarification.
- Some notation/organization issues hinder readability.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are Theorems 2–3 to misspecification of P(B(X))? Can you provide robust variants?
- Can your framework handle per-worker confusion matrices or adversarial workers, and what changes in the bounds?
- What is the empirical gap between kIC and the IT limit across k and q? Any prospects for codes approaching the limit?

Minor comments
- Fix figure references on p.4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b; 1.b → 1.c).
- Ensure complete citation metadata and correct typos (e.g., publisher names, years).
- Explicitly state what k=1 means in text and figures.

Overall recommendation: Borderline. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution with clear potential, but it needs stronger justification of modeling choices, clearer exposition, and more convincing empirical illustrations to reach the NIPS bar. Confidence: medium.
