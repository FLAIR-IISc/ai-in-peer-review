This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in digital crowdsourcing. The authors model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem, where the data to be labeled is the source, workers are noisy channels with potentially unknown skill levels, and the taskmaster/crowdsourcer acts as the encoder and decoder respectively. The primary contribution lies in deriving asymptotic query-rate limits—the minimum number of queries per item—required to achieve a certain fidelity in labeling, under different scenarios of worker skill level knowledge. The paper also analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and provides a performance bound for it.

The work builds upon established concepts in information theory, specifically rate-distortion theory and channel coding. It extends these concepts to a human-in-the-loop computation setting, which is a novel application. Previous work in crowdsourcing, such as Dawid and Skene’s algorithm [5] and more recent EM-based approaches [9, 12, 10], primarily focus on algorithmic design and inference schemes. This paper takes a different approach by establishing *fundamental limits* achievable by *any* query and decoding algorithm, providing a benchmark for the performance of existing and future methods.  Relatedly, works considering crowdsourcing as a bandit problem [1] differ in their objective - optimizing worker selection rather than characterizing fundamental performance bounds.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Modeling:** The JSCC framework provides a valuable, theoretically grounded perspective on crowdsourcing. This allows the authors to leverage existing information-theoretic tools to analyze the problem.
* **Clear Theoretical Results:** Theorems 2 and 3 provide clear, albeit asymptotic, bounds on the minimum query rate. These results offer insight into the trade-offs between budget and fidelity.
* **Analysis of k-ary Incidence Coding:** The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete example and illustrates how the theoretical framework can be applied to a specific query scheme.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the main ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Source-Channel Separation Assumption:** A crucial assumption underpinning Theorems 2 and 3 is the applicability of source-channel separation when the channel state (worker skill level) is unknown to the encoder. The authors do not provide justification or citation for this assumption. Source-channel separation theorems generally require the channel state to be known or at least statistically characterized at the encoder. This is a significant technical concern.  
* **Limited Use of Side Information:** The model assumes the encoder only observes the labels and does not have access to the original items (e.g., images) as side information. In many crowdsourcing scenarios, this side information can be leveraged to design more efficient queries and improve labeling accuracy. Ignoring this can lead to overly conservative bounds.
* **Misleading Wording in Section 4.2:** The statement in Section 4.2 / Figure 2 that “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is misleading. Improvement implies the bound is becoming *tighter* (i.e., closer to the achievable rate), but this statement merely indicates that the *value* of the bound decreases (implying better potential performance). This should be rephrased to reflect that the lower bound suggests lower rate requirements are achievable as k and q increase.
* **Asymptotic Results:** The presented results are asymptotic, relying on large block lengths.  While fundamental, they may not be directly applicable to practical crowdsourcing scenarios with limited budgets and dataset sizes.  Exploring finite-blocklength performance bounds would enhance the paper's practical relevance.  Relevant work by Kostina and Verdú [Kostina & Verdú, 2013] provides finite-blocklength converses for JSCC and could be cited.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful modeling framework for crowdsourcing based on information theory.
* The theoretical results provide fundamental limits that can guide the design and evaluation of crowdsourcing systems.
* The analysis of k-ary incidence coding demonstrates the practical application of the framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The questionable assumption regarding source-channel separation with unknown channel state raises significant technical concerns.
* The limited use of side information and asymptotic nature of the results may limit the practical applicability of the findings.
* The misleading wording in Section 4.2 detracts from the clarity of the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a promising first step toward a rigorous information-theoretic understanding of crowdsourcing. While the modeling approach is insightful and the theoretical results are interesting, the lack of justification for the source-channel separation assumption and the limitations in practical applicability raise concerns.  I recommend **weak acceptance** contingent upon the authors addressing the technical concerns regarding source-channel separation (either by providing a justification or acknowledging its limitations) and clarifying the wording in Section 4.2.  A discussion of finite-blocklength performance and potential avenues for incorporating side information would also strengthen the paper.

**References:**

Kostina, V., & Verdú, V. (2013). Limited-Length Analysis of Joint Source-Channel Coding. *IEEE Transactions on Information Theory*, *59*(6), 3882–3901.
