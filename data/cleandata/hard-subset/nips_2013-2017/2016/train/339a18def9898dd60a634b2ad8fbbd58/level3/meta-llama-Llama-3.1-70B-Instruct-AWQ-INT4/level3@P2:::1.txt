This paper presents a novel approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing systems, specifically those involving labeling tasks, using an information-theoretic framework. The authors reformulate the labeling problem as a coding problem over noisy channels, where the channels represent the workers' imperfect skill levels. This framework allows them to derive fundamental limits on the tradeoff between budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing systems.

The paper builds on previous work in information theory and crowdsourcing, and the authors provide a thorough review of related research in the field. They introduce two channel models, the symmetric memoryless channel and the spammer/hammer channel, to represent the workers' responses. The authors also propose a basic coding scheme called k-incidence coding, which involves labeling subsets of k items.

The main contribution of the paper is the derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity. The authors provide two theorems, one for the case where the workers' skill levels are unknown and another for the case where the skill levels are perfectly known. They also analyze the performance of k-incidence coding with a spammer-hammer worker pool and provide numerical results.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their framework and results. The use of information theory to analyze crowdsourcing systems is a novel and interesting approach, and the authors demonstrate its potential for providing insights into the fundamental limits of these systems.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing systems using information theory
* Clear and concise explanation of the framework and results
* Thorough review of related research in the field
* Derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes a relatively simple model of the workers' responses, which may not capture the complexity of real-world crowdsourcing systems
* The analysis is limited to labeling tasks, and it is unclear how the framework can be extended to other types of crowdsourcing tasks
* The numerical results are limited to a specific scenario, and it is unclear how the results generalize to other scenarios
