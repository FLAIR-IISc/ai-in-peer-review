- Paper frames crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic communication problem, modeling true labels as a source and workers as noisy channels.  
- Derives rate–fidelity (queries per item vs. accuracy) bounds for crowdsourcing under this framework.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS), quantifying the value of skill information.  
- Shows system capacity shifts from being limited by the "average worker" to the "average of worker qualities" when skills are known.  
- Proposes and analyzes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), a practical query scheme asking workers to compare k items.  
- Provides performance bounds for kIC under a spammer–hammer worker model.  
- Strength: original and significant information-theoretic framing offering algorithm-agnostic fundamental limits.  
- Strength: theoretical results give intuitive, high-level insights useful as benchmarks for algorithms.  
- Strength: kIC grounds the abstract framework with a concrete coding/querying example.  
- Main weakness: paper is hard to follow for non–information-theory experts, limiting conference impact.  
- Suggestion: consistently restate information-theory terms in crowdsourcing language (e.g., rate → budget, distortion → error).  
- Suggestion: expand high-level intuition throughout the paper and add practitioner-focused takeaways (e.g., in intro or a discussion).  
- Missing connection: no discussion of incentives/mechanism-design literature tying budget to payments, effort, and honesty.  
- Clarity issues to fix: define taskmaster vs. crowdsourcer earlier and use consistently; rephrase a confusing sentence on lines 275–276.  
- Figure 2 needs a much clearer caption explaining curves, what is plotted, and why kIC improves with k.  
- Minor typos to correct (e.g., line 84: "is reviewed in" → "are reviewed in"; line 122: duplicate "A").  
- Summary pros: novel framing, fundamental algorithm-agnostic limits, clear insights on worker-skill value.  
- Summary cons: accessibility problems for general audience, missing incentive literature connection, several clarity and presentation issues.  
- Reviewer stance: borderline leaning toward acceptance conditioned on substantial revisions addressing clarity and positioning.
