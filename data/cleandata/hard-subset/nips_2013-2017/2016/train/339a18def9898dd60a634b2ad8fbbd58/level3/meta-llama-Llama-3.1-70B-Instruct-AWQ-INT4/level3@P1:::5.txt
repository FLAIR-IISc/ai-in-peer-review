This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling crowdsourcing, with a focus on the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as a human-in-the-loop computation problem, where a taskmaster breaks down a project into small batches of tasks and assigns them to workers with imperfect skill levels. The crowdsourcer then collects and analyzes the results for inference and serving the purpose of the project.

The paper is well-organized and well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and arguments. The use of an information-theoretic framework to analyze the crowdsourcing problem is novel and provides a fresh perspective on the field. The authors' approach is practically useful for designers and pricing, as it provides a way to quantify the trade-offs between budget and fidelity.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a target error probability. The authors model workers as parallel independent channels and consider two types of channels: symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) for worker errors and spammer-hammer channel SHC(q) for spammers. They also analyze two crowdsourcer knowledge cases: full knowledge of worker skills and no knowledge.

The paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum rate for k-ary incidence coding with SHC(q) worker pool, which is a useful result for designers. However, the paper lacks experiments or simulations to compare practical schemes (e.g., AMT) to the theoretical limits. This is a limitation of the paper, as it would be useful to see how the theoretical results compare to real-world systems.

Another limitation of the paper is the lack of proof sketches for the theorems. While the proofs are provided in the appendix, it would be helpful to include a brief summary of the proof ideas in the main text. This would make the paper more self-contained and easier to follow.

In terms of suggestions for improvement, the reviewer recommends shortening Section 2.4 and removing or shortening Section 3.1. These sections provide some background information on information theory, but they are not essential to the main results of the paper.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. The authors' approach is novel and practically useful, and the paper is well-written and well-organized.
