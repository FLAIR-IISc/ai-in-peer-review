- Novel application of rate-distortion/information-theoretic framework to crowdsourcing, providing a rigorous theoretical basis.  
- Derivation of lower bounds on achievable fidelity given a budget, identifying fundamental limits.  
- Analysis of k-ary incidence coding as a concrete scheme and comparison to theoretical bounds, though practical implications need more scrutiny.  
- Major modeling weakness: conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel, losing important behavioral distinctions.  
- Unrealistic assumption that the taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers; ignores adaptive allocation by worker quality.  
- Chosen channel models (MSC, SHC) may not capture adversarial, spammy, or strategically misleading workers; require stronger justification.  
- Key assumption that P(B(X)) is known is stated late and should be declared early (Section 2), as it affects applicability of bounds.  
- Figure 2 and numerical results are unclear: curves need improved labeling and the meaning of k=1 must be explained.  
- Improper or inconsistent use/numbering of "Theorem", "Lemma", and "Corollary"; numbering or lowercase would improve readability.  
- Minor corrections needed: incorrect figure references on page 4 ("Figure 1.a"→"Figure 1.b", "Figure 1.b"→"Figure 1.c").  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framing, derivation of fundamental limits, k-ary coding analysis, and potential to inspire future work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: oversimplified worker model, uniform query allocation assumption, limited realism of channel models, and clarity/methodological issues.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and potentially impactful but significant modeling and presentation issues justify rejection now; recommend revision to address realism, adaptive allocation, and clarity.
