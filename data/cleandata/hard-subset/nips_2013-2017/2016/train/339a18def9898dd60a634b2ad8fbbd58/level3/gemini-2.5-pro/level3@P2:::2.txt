### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The authors model the crowdsourcing process as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. In this analogy, the set of true labels for a dataset is treated as an i.i.d. information source. The taskmaster, who designs the queries, acts as a source-channel encoder. The crowd of workers is modeled as a set of parallel noisy channels, where each worker's skill level corresponds to a random channel state. Finally, the crowdsourcer, who aggregates the responses to infer the true labels, acts as the decoder.

The main contributions are twofold. First, the paper applies the source-channel separation theorem to derive asymptotic fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off, expressed as the minimum number of queries per item (rate) required to achieve a target error probability (distortion). These limits are established for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown to all parties (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known to the crowdsourcer/decoder (SL-CS). Second, the authors propose and analyze a specific query scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC), where workers are asked to compare k items at a time. They derive a lower bound on the performance of kIC under a spammer-hammer worker model. This work distinguishes itself from prior research (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.), which largely focuses on specific aggregation algorithms, by instead aiming to establish algorithm-agnostic performance bounds.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound in its analysis of the k-ary incidence coding scheme. However, there appears to be a critical gap in the justification for the main theoretical results presented in Theorems 2 and 3.

*   **Major Weakness:** The derivation of the fundamental limits hinges on the application of the source-channel separation theorem (Theorem 1). However, the standard version of this theorem applies to a single, known discrete memoryless channel. The crowdsourcing model presented here involves a channel with a random state (the worker's skill level) which is unknown to the encoder (taskmaster). This corresponds to a more complex channel model (e.g., a compound channel or a channel with state information). It is not immediately obvious that source-channel separation holds in this setting. The authors must provide a proof or cite appropriate results from the information theory literature (e.g., on channels with state information unknown at the encoder) to justify that the capacity of the average channel (for SL-UK) or the average of capacities (for SL-CS) are the correct quantities to use. Without this justification, the technical soundness of the main claims is in question.

*   **Strength:** The analysis of kIC is a solid contribution that provides a concrete performance bound for a non-trivial query scheme.

*   **Weakness:** The results are purely asymptotic (i.e., for a dataset of infinite size, L→∞). For practical crowdsourcing applications where L is finite, these bounds may not be tight. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) limits. For instance, citing or incorporating ideas from recent work on finite-blocklength converses for JSCC (e.g., Kostina & Verdú, 2013) could provide a more practically relevant perspective.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The analogy between crowdsourcing and a communication system is clearly articulated and serves as an effective vehicle for the analysis.

*   **Minor Weakness:** The wording in Section 4.2 and Figure 2 is slightly misleading. The authors state that "the lower bounds for kIC ... improve as k and q grow." In the context of bounds, "improving" typically means the bound becomes tighter (i.e., closer to the true value). Here, the authors seem to mean that the performance limit itself is better (the achievable error rate for a given budget is lower). This should be rephrased for clarity, for example: "The performance bound demonstrates that a lower query rate is achievable as k and q grow."

**Originality**

The central idea of modeling crowdsourcing from a JSCC perspective is highly original and provides a novel and powerful lens through which to view the problem. While information-theoretic ideas have appeared in machine learning, this specific and comprehensive formulation for crowdsourcing appears to be new. The introduction and analysis of k-ary incidence coding as a practical JSCC scheme is also a novel contribution.

**Significance**

The paper is significant as it opens a new and promising research direction for understanding the absolute limits of what can be achieved with crowdsourcing. Establishing fundamental bounds can guide the development of more efficient practical algorithms.

*   **Weakness:** The significance is currently limited by a key modeling assumption. The framework models the source as only the labels `B(X)` and ignores the items `X` themselves. In many real-world scenarios, the items contain valuable side information. For example, the difficulty of an image can affect worker accuracy, and item features can be used by the decoder to improve inference. By abstracting the items away, the derived bounds might not reflect the true limits in settings where this side information is available. Discussing this limitation and how side information could be incorporated into the model would be a valuable addition.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly original and elegant information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, which has the potential to open up a new line of research.
*   The paper is very well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly.
*   The analysis of k-ary incidence coding is a concrete and novel contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The main theoretical claims (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on an application of the source-channel separation theorem to a channel with a random state, which is not justified in the paper. This is a significant technical gap that undermines the core results.
*   The modeling assumption that ignores the features of the items being labeled may limit the practical relevance of the derived bounds.
*   The asymptotic nature of the results, without discussion of finite-blocklength performance, also tempers the immediate practical significance.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a creative and promising approach to understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The originality of the core idea is a major strength. However, the current manuscript contains a critical technical flaw in its application of the source-channel separation theorem, which must be addressed for the main results to be considered sound. While the paper has high potential, this unresolved technical issue is a major barrier to acceptance in its current form.
