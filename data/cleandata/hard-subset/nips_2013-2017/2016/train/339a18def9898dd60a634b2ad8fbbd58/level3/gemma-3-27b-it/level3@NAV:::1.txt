## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, framing the labeling problem as a coding challenge over noisy channels representing crowd workers. The authors model workers as either symmetric memoryless channels or as exhibiting spammer/hammer behavior, mapping the crowdsourcing budget to a code rate and analyzing the resulting distortion-budget trade-offs. A specific coding scheme, k-incidence coding, is proposed and analyzed, alongside considerations for optimized query pricing.

The work builds upon existing research in crowdsourcing, acknowledging prior efforts focused on aggregation schemes like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm. It diverges by moving away from algorithmic improvements and instead seeks fundamental performance bounds, establishing a theoretical limit on achievable fidelity given a budget. This approach is novel in the context of crowdsourcing, traditionally dominated by practical algorithm design. The connection to classical coding theory – drawing parallels to source and channel coding – provides a fresh perspective.

A key strength of the paper is its rigorous, information-theoretic treatment of the problem. The derivation of bounds on the required query rate for achieving a desired fidelity is valuable.  The introduction of k-incidence coding is also interesting, offering a potential practical scheme grounded in the theoretical framework. The effort to connect coding parameters to query pricing demonstrates an attempt to bridge the gap between theory and application.

However, while the theoretical framework appears sound, the paper’s connection to the practical realities of crowdsourcing feels somewhat tenuous. The assumptions of channel models (MSC, SHC) are simplifications of complex human behavior.  The analysis, while mathematically elegant, may not directly translate into easily implementable solutions. Furthermore, the numerical results presented are limited, and more extensive simulations evaluating the performance of k-incidence coding in realistic scenarios would strengthen the findings. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, providing fundamental limits.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and derivation of performance bounds.
*   Introduction of a new coding scheme, k-incidence coding, with potential practical value.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Simplified channel models may not accurately reflect real-world worker behavior.
*   Limited connection to practical implementation and validation through simulations.
*   Numerical results are limited and could be expanded.



Despite these weaknesses, I believe the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. While the results might not immediately translate into concrete improvements in crowdsourcing systems, the framework established and the bounds derived provide a useful benchmark and a new way of thinking about the problem. Given the theoretical nature of the work and my limited expertise in this specific area, I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that authors consider expanding the experimental validation in future work.
