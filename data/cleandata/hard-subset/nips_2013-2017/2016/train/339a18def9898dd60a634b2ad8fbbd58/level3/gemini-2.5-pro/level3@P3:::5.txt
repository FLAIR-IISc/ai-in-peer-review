This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The central idea is to model the entire crowdsourcing process as a communication system: the true labels are the source, the query design is the encoder, the workers are a set of parallel noisy channels, and the inference algorithm is the decoder. Using this model, the authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries per task (i.e., the budget) required to achieve a target error probability (i.e., fidelity). This approach stands in contrast to much of the existing literature, which tends to focus on developing and analyzing specific aggregation algorithms like majority voting or EM-based methods (e.g., Dawid & Skene, 1979). By focusing on fundamental limits, this work provides a valuable theoretical benchmark against which any practical crowdsourcing scheme can be measured.

The authors model workers using two types of discrete memoryless channels: the M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) to represent general fallible workers, and the Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) to model a mix of perfect workers and random spammers. The analysis considers two scenarios regarding the crowdsourcer's knowledge of worker skills: one where skills are perfectly known to the decoder and another where they are unknown. A key assumption is that the taskmaster, who assigns the queries, is ignorant of worker skills and thus assigns tasks uniformly.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel application of information theory to the crowdsourcing problem. Framing crowdsourcing in terms of a joint source-channel coding problem provides a powerful and principled way to reason about the inherent trade-offs between budget and accuracy. The derived lower bounds are not tied to a specific algorithm but apply to any possible query and inference scheme under the model assumptions, making the results fundamental and broadly applicable.

2.  **Practical Implications:** The theoretical results have significant practical utility. For designers of crowdsourcing systems (e.g., on platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk), these bounds offer guidance on the minimum budget required to reach a desired level of quality. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding also provides insights into how the design of the query itself impacts performance and can inform pricing strategies for different types of tasks.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The communication system analogy is well-explained and consistently used throughout the paper, which helps in understanding the theoretical development. The problem setup, models, and main theorems are presented logically.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the absence of any experiments or simulations. While the paper provides a strong theoretical contribution, its impact would be greatly enhanced by connecting the derived bounds to the performance of practical, state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms. A simulation study comparing the performance of, for instance, an EM-based algorithm with the theoretical limits derived in Theorems 2 and 3 would provide a valuable sense of how large the gap is between theory and practice. This would help answer the question: how close are existing methods to being optimal?

2.  **Paper Structure and Content:** The paper could be made more concise and impactful with some restructuring.
    *   Section 3.1 ("Background") provides a standard overview of rate-distortion and channel coding theorems. While useful for context, this material is likely familiar to the NIPS audience and could be significantly shortened or removed, perhaps with a citation to a standard text like Cover & Thomas.
    *   Similarly, Section 2.4 ("Distortion and the Design Problem") could be streamlined.
    *   The space saved could be used to provide more intuition about the main results. Instead of relegating all proofs to the appendix, including brief proof sketches for the main theorems in the body of the paper would be very beneficial. This would give the reader insight into *why* the bounds take the form they do (e.g., the difference between E[H(ε)] and H(E[ε]) in the two main theorems) without requiring a deep dive into the appendix.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical work appears to be sound, establishing a solid foundation for this line of inquiry. The provided theorems are clear and the approach is rigorous. (Note: I have not checked the proofs in the appendix in detail). The paper is a complete theoretical contribution but lacks the empirical component that would make it a more comprehensive work.
*   **Clarity:** As noted, the paper is very clear. The analogy to communication systems is effective. The suggested structural changes would further improve its clarity and focus for the target audience.
*   **Originality:** The approach is highly original. While information-theoretic concepts have been used in machine learning, their application to establish fundamental budget-fidelity limits for the entire crowdsourcing pipeline is a novel and welcome contribution.
*   **Significance:** The work is significant because it shifts the perspective from algorithm-specific analysis to fundamental limitations. These results have the potential to influence how researchers and practitioners design, budget for, and price crowdsourcing tasks.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that introduces a novel and valuable perspective on the crowdsourcing problem. Its main limitation is the lack of connection to practice through experiments. With some revisions to the structure and the potential addition of simulation results, this could be an excellent contribution to the field.
