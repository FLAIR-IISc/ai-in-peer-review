- Novel JSCC framework: paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem with random channel states, offering a fresh information-theoretic perspective on crowdsourcing limits.  
- Theoretical results: leverages existing JSCC results to derive asymptotic bounds on queries per item and error probability.  
- k-ary incidence coding (kIC): provides a lower bound on error probability for kIC under specific crowdsourcing models, analyzing its error-correction properties.  
- Worker modeling: models workers as noisy channels (M-ary symmetric channels and spammer-hammer channels), which is well-motivated and realistic.  
- Practical implications: discusses query pricing strategies and presents numerical results (Figure 2) with practical takeaways for system design.  
- Use of separation theorem: relies on the separation theorem for analysis, but applicability to channels with encoder-unknown random states is assumed without rigorous proof.  
- Gap in rigor: lack of proof for separation theorem applicability weakens the theoretical robustness of the results.  
- Unrealistic assumptions: assumes taskmasters/crowdsourcers have no access to original data or side information, which may not hold in practice.  
- Interpretation issues: lower-bound results (Figure 2) are unclear and hard to assess due to absence of matching upper bounds.  
- Asymptotic focus: results are asymptotic; absence of non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) analysis limits practical applicability.  
- Related work: literature review could be more comprehensive, especially regarding recent probabilistic modeling and inference work in crowdsourcing.  
- Recommendation: accept conditionally if authors justify the separation theorem’s applicability, provide non-asymptotic bounds or discussion, clarify Figure 2, and expand related-work coverage.
