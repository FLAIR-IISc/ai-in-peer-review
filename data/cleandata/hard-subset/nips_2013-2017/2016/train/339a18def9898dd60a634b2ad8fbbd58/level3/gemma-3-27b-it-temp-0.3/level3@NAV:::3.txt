## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents an interesting and novel approach to analyzing crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, deriving fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The framing of crowdsourcing as a noisy channel problem is a compelling perspective, and the derived bounds offer valuable theoretical insights. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding as a specific query scheme is also a positive contribution. While the technical details were challenging to fully grasp for this reviewer (lacking deep expertise in information theory), the core ideas are intuitively appealing and potentially impactful.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to establish fundamental limits, moving beyond the more common algorithmic and incentive-based approaches to crowdsourcing. The theorems presented (2 & 3) are significant in identifying the theoretical minimum query requirements for achieving a desired fidelity. However, the paper would benefit greatly from a more accessible presentation of these results. The current reliance on channel/code/distortion language makes it difficult to immediately translate the findings into practical takeaways for a broader audience. Expanding the discussion at the end of Section 3, and adding a concluding section summarizing the key insights in crowdsourcing terms (e.g., how many workers are *needed* for a given accuracy, rather than just a rate), would significantly improve its impact. 

Several areas require attention. The distinction between “crowdsourcer” and “taskmaster” is unclear and should be explicitly defined. There’s a typo on line 122 (“A A(X1,X2).”). Lines 275-276 are difficult to parse and require rephrasing. Figure 2 lacks a detailed caption and could be made clearer to convey its message. Additionally, the paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive review of related work, specifically including research on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing, and work that combines incentive structures with aggregation techniques. Finally, line 84 should read “are reviewed in.”

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Derivation of fundamental budget-fidelity limits.
*   Introduction of a potentially useful query scheme (k-ary incidence coding).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Accessibility issues for readers without a strong information theory background.
*   Lack of clarity in certain definitions and explanations.
*   Limited discussion of related work in crowdsourcing incentives and mechanisms.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but requires revisions to improve its clarity and accessibility. Addressing the points above would significantly enhance its impact and make it more appealing to a wider audience at NIPS.
