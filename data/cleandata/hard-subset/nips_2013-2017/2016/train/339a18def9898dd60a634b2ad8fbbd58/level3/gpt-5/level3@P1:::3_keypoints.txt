- Models crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding and derives single-letter lower bounds on queries per item for target error under SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- Interprets bounds as (bits needed about labels) / (effective per-query information), mirroring rate–distortion over parallel channels.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC): grouping k items prunes invalid responses, giving implicit error-correction and a necessary rate condition under SHC.
- Provides a pricing rule-of-thumb for kIC that scales with k (current wording ambiguous).
- Novelty: reframes crowd labeling limits in information-theoretic terms and contrasts unknown vs known skill regimes; treats multi-item queries as coding.
- Technical soundness: separation-style argument is coherent; Theorems 2–3 and kIC analysis align with intuition.
- Key assumptions (iid items, non-adaptive assignment, large blocklengths, independence across workers/items, identical response alphabets) need to be stated crisply.
- Notation is sometimes heavy and ambiguous (M vs N vs k; source vs response alphabets); require consistency and clarification.
- Must clarify when separation is tight and whether finite-length or adaptive designs can improve constants.
- Figure 2 needs clearer axes, labels, defined curves, parameter settings (q, M, ε), and explanation of how k and q affect gaps to bounds.
- Exposition is dense for non-information-theory readers; restate main theorems in crowdsourcing terms and add high-level intuition after formal statements.
- Distinguish roles (taskmaster vs crowdsourcer/decoder) and explicitly state which entity knows worker skills in SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- Recommend a short design guide: when to invest in skill estimation, when to use larger kIC, or when to increase redundancy for a given ε.
- kIC sections need more intuition (Lemma 1, Theorem 4) and a small table/examples for k∈{2,3,4} showing valid patterns, spammer error rates, and price scaling.
- Clarify the pricing rule (likely π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) rather than the current ambiguous expression.
- Expand related work to include incentives/mechanism-design literature (Prelec; Witkowski & Parkes; Radanovic & Faltings) and adaptive/bandit assignment work (e.g., Slivkins/Singer/Vaughan).
- Add simulations comparing majority vote, Dawid–Skene, and a kIC-aware decoder under MSC/SHC to show empirical error vs budget and gap to bounds.
- Discuss scope and limitations: item difficulty (input-dependent channels), adversarial/colluding workers, adaptivity, and how these affect asymptotic vs constant factors.
- Minor copyedits and notational fixes needed (e.g., pluralization, duplicated words, consistent use of N/M/k, clarifying specific sentences).
- Pros: principled framework with fundamental limits; clear SL-UK vs SL-CS insights; kIC viewed as error-correcting queries.
- Cons: accessibility issues, under-specified assumptions/notation, limited empirical results, and missing incentives literature.
- Overall: strong, original theoretical contribution with practical implications that will benefit from clearer exposition, broader related-work coverage, and more application-oriented discussion.
