- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication system (source, encoder, noisy channels, decoder).
- Applies rate-distortion theory and source-channel separation to derive a budget (rate) vs. fidelity (distortion) trade-off.
- Provides theoretical bounds for two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known worker skills (SL-CS).
- Analyzes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a concrete query/encoding scheme within the framework.
- Novel perspective shifting focus from algorithm design to fundamental performance limits; originality is a key strength.
- Technical derivations (Theorems 2 and 3) appear sound and the noisy-channel worker models (MSC, SHC) are reasonable simplifications.
- Major modeling weakness: i.i.d. memoryless channel assumption is unrealistic because workers’ responses can show dependencies (learning, fatigue, anchoring).
- Core theoretical reliance on source-channel separation assumes freedom to design arbitrary encodings, which is not available in crowdsourcing.
- Human-understandable queries constrain allowable encodings, making capacity-achieving codes likely infeasible in practice.
- kIC is a useful structured example but unlikely to bridge the gap to the general capacity limits; bounds may be overly optimistic.
- Without empirical validation or comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, the practical significance of the bounds is unclear.
- The derived bounds may serve as loose upper bounds rather than true fundamental limits for practical crowdsourcing.
- Acceptance arguments: introduces an elegant, technically sound framework that could open new research directions.
- Rejection arguments: questionable applicability of unconstrained coding model, lack of experiments, and novelty mainly from applying standard theorems.
- Minor suggestions: clarify minimization over P(Ẑ|Z) in Eq.(8); elaborate Theorem 4 proof linking R to unseen items; clarify sign/conditions in the ln(1-q) inequality; provide intuition or citation for H_M(E(ε)) in Theorem 2.
