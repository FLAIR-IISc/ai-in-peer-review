This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework. The authors model the crowdsourcing process as a joint source-channel coding problem, representing workers as parallel, independent noisy channels (specifically, MSCs and SHCs for spammers). They analyze scenarios with both full and no knowledge of worker skills, assuming non-adaptive coding. A key contribution is the derivation of lower bounds on the queries per task needed to achieve a target error probability, and the analysis of k-ary incidence coding as a query scheme.

The approach is novel and represents a valuable shift toward a rigorous, information-theoretic understanding of crowdsourcing performance. Prior work, as the authors correctly point out, largely focuses on algorithmic improvements to aggregation and inference schemes (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting) and often lacks fundamental limits. This paper fills a crucial gap by providing theoretical benchmarks for comparison. The connection to rate-distortion theory and channel capacity is well-motivated and the application to crowdsourcing is insightful. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is also a potentially practical contribution, offering a structured approach to query design. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and the mathematical derivations appear sound (though, as the reviewer notes, the appendix proofs were not checked). The discussion of query pricing based on the derived bounds is a promising direction.

However, the most significant weakness of the paper is the complete lack of experimental validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, their practical relevance is difficult to assess without comparison to existing crowdsourcing schemes (like those implemented on AMT) and real-world data. The authors acknowledge this, but it severely limits the impact of the work. How well do these theoretical limits hold up in practice? Are the gains from k-ary incidence coding significant enough to offset the complexity of implementation? These questions remain unanswered.

Several specific suggestions for improvement would strengthen the paper. The authors are correct to identify Sec. 2.4 (Distortion and the Design Problem) as somewhat verbose and could benefit from streamlining. Similarly, Sec. 3.1 (Background) presents a fairly standard review of information theory concepts and could be condensed. The core contribution lies in the derived bounds (Theorems 2, 3, and 4), and focusing on these would improve clarity. More importantly, providing *proof sketches* for the theorems, as suggested, would significantly enhance understanding and build confidence in the results. While full proofs are appropriate for the appendix, a high-level overview of the key steps within the main body of the paper would be very valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The information-theoretic approach to crowdsourcing is a significant departure from existing work.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous and the results are logically consistent.
*   **Potential Practical Impact:** The derived bounds offer a benchmark for evaluating existing crowdsourcing schemes and designing new ones, and the k-ary incidence coding scheme shows promise.
*   **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Experimental Validation:** The absence of experiments severely limits the practical relevance of the results.
*   **Verbosity:** Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be more concise.
*   **Proof Accessibility:** Full proofs in the appendix without sketches in the main text hinder understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of experimental validation, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical framework is sound, the results are interesting, and the potential impact is significant. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding conciseness and proof accessibility, and acknowledging the limitations due to the lack of empirical evaluation. Future work should prioritize experimental validation to assess the practical relevance of the derived bounds and proposed schemes. The reviewer’s assessment that the work is both novel and practically useful is accurate, and this paper holds promise for guiding the design and pricing of crowdsourcing systems.
