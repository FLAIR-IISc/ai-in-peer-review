## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, framing the problem as a joint source-channel coding task. The authors aim to establish a budget-fidelity trade-off, analyzing the ultimate achievable performance given a limited budget for querying workers. This is a compelling approach, and the application of rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing is a potentially fruitful avenue for future research. The exploration of k-ary incidence coding as a concrete scheme is also a valuable contribution.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel framing of crowdsourcing and the derivation of theoretical bounds. The authors successfully translate the practical challenges of crowdsourcing – unreliable workers, cost limitations – into a rigorous mathematical framework. The derived bounds, particularly Theorems 2 and 3, offer fundamental limitations on achievable fidelity, independent of specific algorithms. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete example and insight into the potential gains from structured queries. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant concern is the model's simplification of worker characteristics. Merging worker skill and reliability into a single channel loses the crucial distinction between inherent ability and noisy reporting. Realistically, workers possess varying skill levels *and* exhibit different levels of noise in their responses. Furthermore, the assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers is unrealistic; systems typically leverage worker ratings and capabilities for adaptive task assignment. The channel models used (MSC, SHC) may also not fully capture adversarial or strategically malicious workers who could intentionally provide misleading responses. The authors need to more thoroughly justify the realism of their simplified worker/channel models and discuss the limitations.

The presentation of numerical results, specifically Figure 2, is unclear. The curves are poorly labeled, and it’s not explicitly stated what k=1 corresponds to, hindering interpretation. A methodological issue is the late introduction of the assumption that P(B(X)) is known (page 6, line 246); this should be stated much earlier in the paper. Finally, there are minor issues with notation: 'Theorem,' 'Lemma,' and 'Corollary' are misused without numbering – using lowercase or adding numbers would improve clarity.  Additionally, figure references on page 4 are incorrect (Figure 1.a → 1.b, 1.b → 1.c).



**Arguments for acceptance:**  Novel framing of crowdsourcing using information theory; derivation of fundamental limits; exploration of k-ary incidence coding.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Simplistic worker model; unrealistic task allocation assumption; potentially inadequate channel models; unclear numerical results; notational inconsistencies. 

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a potentially impactful idea, the simplifying assumptions and presentation issues significantly detract from its overall quality.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the concerns outlined above. Addressing the modeling limitations and clarifying the presentation would substantially strengthen the contribution.
