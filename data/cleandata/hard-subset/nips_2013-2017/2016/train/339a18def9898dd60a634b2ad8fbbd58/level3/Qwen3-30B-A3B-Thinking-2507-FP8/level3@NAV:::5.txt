

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling workers as parallel noisy channels (MSC and SHC(q)) to establish fundamental lower bounds on queries per task for target error probability. The authors derive theoretical limits for two key scenarios: when the crowdsourcer has full knowledge of worker skills (SL-CS) versus no knowledge (SL-UK), and analyze k-ary incidence coding as a practical query scheme. The work relates to prior NIPS/ML research on crowdsourcing (e.g., [5, 8, 11]) but offers a novel theoretical foundation rather than algorithmic proposals.

The paper excels in clarity and organization, with well-structured theoretical development. The information-theoretic approach is original and provides valuable insights for system designers regarding budget-fidelity trade-offs. The results are theoretically sound, with the framework offering a rigorous foundation for understanding crowdsourcing limits. The practical relevance for pricing strategies (e.g., the kIC pricing rule π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁k₂) is particularly noteworthy.

However, the paper suffers from a significant weakness: it lacks any experimental validation or simulations to compare theoretical bounds with practical crowdsourcing systems (e.g., AMT). Without empirical results, it's difficult to assess how tight these bounds are in real-world settings or how the theoretical framework translates to actual system design. The reviewer recommends shortening Section 2.4 and Section 3.1 while adding proof sketches for key theorems to improve readability.

While the theoretical contribution is strong and the paper is well-written, the absence of experimental validation limits its practical significance. The work is technically sound but would benefit from empirical validation to strengthen its claims about real-world applicability. The novelty and theoretical importance justify acceptance, though the lack of experiments is a notable limitation. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field but would be significantly stronger with empirical validation to demonstrate the practical relevance of its bounds.
