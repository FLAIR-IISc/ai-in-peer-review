- Models crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion problem: dataset as source, workers as noisy channels, queries as encoding.  
- Main contribution: information-theoretic bounds on budget (queries/item) vs fidelity (error) using rate-distortion and channel coding; Theorems 2 & 3 cover unknown/known skill levels and analyze k-ary incidence coding.  
- Strength: theoretically sound derivations that correctly apply information-theory principles.  
- Strength: conceptually novel connection between crowdsourcing and communication theory that could inspire future work.  
- Strength: k-ary incidence coding is well-motivated and numerical results demonstrate some practical relevance.  
- Weakness: novelty and practical significance are questionable—application of rate-distortion is conceptually straightforward and bounds may be loose.  
- Major concern: fundamental mismatch between channel coding assumptions and crowdsourcing—cannot freely design input sequences or reuse workers, violating iid and codebook assumptions.  
- Specific issue: infeasibility of querying the same worker multiple times and unclear which mappings B(X)^n → U^(Σ m_i) are implementable as codebooks.  
- Specific issue: paper does not show how to design u^n to achieve error-correction beyond simple repetition/majority voting.  
- Implication: Theorem 2’s bound on minimum queries/item may be invalid or overly loose because assumed encoding schemes may not be implementable.  
- Weakness: no comparison to state-of-the-art practical methods (e.g., Dawid-Skene, spectral methods), so tightness and practical usefulness are unclear.  
- Minor issues: equation (8) should minimize over C,C' with E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t; Theorem 4’s proof needs clearer relation between ε_S and ε̂; H_M(E(ε)) in eq.(17) needs explanation/citation.  
- Overall assessment: conceptually interesting and technically correct but practically limited due to unrealistic assumptions and lack of connection to practical methods.  
- Recommendation: reject; authors should (1) characterize implementable codebooks in crowdsourcing, (2) demonstrate bound tightness via comparisons to practical algorithms, and (3) address worker-reuse constraints.
