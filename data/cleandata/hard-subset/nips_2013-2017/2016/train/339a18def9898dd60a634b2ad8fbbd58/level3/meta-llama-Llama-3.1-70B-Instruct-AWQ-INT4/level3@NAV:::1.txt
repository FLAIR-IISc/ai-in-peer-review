This paper presents a novel approach to understanding the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing. By modeling crowdsourcing workers as noisy channels and reformulating the labeling problem as a coding problem, the authors provide a new perspective on the design of optimized crowdsourcing systems.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have done an excellent job of explaining the technical details of their approach. The use of information-theoretic tools to analyze the crowdsourcing problem is a significant contribution to the field. The authors' analysis of the k-ary incidence coding scheme is particularly interesting, as it provides a new insight into the performance of this coding scheme.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas and relate them to previous work in the field. The authors have also done a good job of discussing the strengths and weaknesses of their approach and providing a list of arguments for and against acceptance.

From a technical standpoint, the paper is sound, and the authors have provided a thorough analysis of their approach. The use of theorems and lemmas to establish the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing is a significant contribution to the field. The numerical results presented in the paper are also interesting, as they provide a comparison between the information-theoretic bounds and the performance of the k-ary incidence coding scheme.

However, one potential weakness of the paper is that it may be challenging for readers who are not familiar with information theory to follow the technical details of the analysis. The authors could consider providing more background information on the technical tools they use to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
