- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states (worker skill levels) and derives asymptotic query-rate limits for target fidelity.  
- Frames crowdsourcer as decoder, taskmaster as encoder, and workers as parallel noisy channels with skill-dependent error rates.  
- Leverages source-channel separation theorems to produce Theorems 2 and 3 for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill scenarios.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer-hammer channel model.  
- Novel contribution: applies information theory to crowdsourcing, offering fundamental limits where prior work was largely heuristic.  
- Strength: clear writing, well-structured arguments, and accessible presentation with helpful figures.  
- Strength: unifies previous approaches under an information-theoretic framework and shows how coding theory can guide practical query design (kIC).  
- Critical flaw: unjustified application of source-channel separation when channel state is unknown to the encoder; cited theorem assumes a known channel.  
- Requires citation/justification from channels-with-state literature (e.g., El Gamal & Kim) or a new proof; without this Theorems 2 and 3 lack rigorous foundation.  
- Modeling limitation: encoder/decoder only see labels B(X) and ignore original items X as side information, which is unrealistic for many tasks and can affect bounds.  
- Figure 2/text misleadingly state "lower bounds improve as k and q grow"—phrasing should clarify that bound values decrease (better performance) and explain lack of an upper bound.  
- Paper should address finite-blocklength effects (cite Kostina & Verdú 2013) because real crowdsourcing datasets are often finite/moderate in size.  
- Significance: establishes benchmarks for evaluating crowdsourcing algorithms and could lead to more cost-efficient query designs relevant to platforms like AMT.  
- Recommendation: promising and novel but accept only with major revisions to fix the channel-state justification, address side-information modeling, clarify Figure 2, and discuss finite-blocklength issues.
