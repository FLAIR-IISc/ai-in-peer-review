- Paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem using rate-distortion theory to derive budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Key contribution: a scheme-independent lower bound on the number of queries required for a target fidelity.
- Analysis of a specific query scheme (k-ary incidence coding, kIC) and comparison to the theoretical lower bound under a spammer-hammer worker model.
- Novel conceptual connection between crowdsourcing and rate-distortion theory with potential to inspire further information-theoretic work on human-in-the-loop systems.
- Theoretical limits provide a strong analytical foundation and practical insights into query design.
- Results are well-contextualized with prior crowdsourcing aggregation literature (e.g., majority voting, Dawid–Skene).
- Model oversimplifies human behavior by merging worker reliability and skill into a single channel parameter.
- Assumption of uniform query allocation may not reflect real-world task assignment practices and needs justification or alternatives.
- The spammer-hammer channel model is limited, especially for adversarial workers who intentionally degrade performance.
- Numerical results and figure labeling (e.g., Fig. 2) are unclear and hinder comprehension.
- Critical assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)) is not stated early enough and should be clarified.
- Typographical errors and inconsistent use of terms (Theorem/Lemma/Corollary) reduce polish and readability.
- Recommendation split: pro acceptance for novelty and theory; con acceptance due to limited real-world applicability and presentation issues.
- Suggested improvements: separate reliability and skill in the model, justify or explore non-uniform allocation, address adversarial worker models, clarify numerical results and figures, and fix typographical/terminology inconsistencies.
