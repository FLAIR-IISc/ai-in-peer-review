## Review of “Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing”

This paper presents an intriguing application of information theory to the problem of crowdsourcing, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem. The authors establish fundamental limits on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a certain fidelity, considering workers as noisy channels with potentially varying skill levels. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and represents a promising first step toward a more rigorous theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing systems. The connection to JSCC is novel and offers a powerful framework for analysis.

The core strength lies in applying established information-theoretic results to a practically relevant problem. Theorems 2 and 3 provide asymptotic query-rate limits under different scenarios regarding worker skill level knowledge, offering valuable insights into the inherent trade-offs between budget and accuracy. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding and the subsequent analysis, including a lower bound on its error probability, adds a concrete example to illustrate the framework and potentially inform practical design choices. The paper effectively connects the theoretical framework to real-world considerations like spammer/hammer worker models.

However, the paper has some limitations. The central assumption that source-channel separation holds with a random, unknown channel state (worker skill) requires justification or citation. This is a crucial point as the standard separation theorem relies on the encoder having knowledge of the channel statistics. Additionally, the modeling choice of ignoring the original item information (e.g., the image itself) at the encoder/decoder is potentially restrictive.  The item could provide valuable side information aiding in more efficient querying and inference. 

Furthermore, the wording in Section 4.2 and Figure 2 regarding the k-ary incidence coding bounds is misleading. It states the "lower bounds improve as k and q grow," which suggests tightening of the bounds, but actually refers to a *decrease* in the bound’s value—indicating potential performance improvement. This should be clarified. Finally, while the asymptotic results are valuable, exploring non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses, perhaps referencing Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses, would strengthen the analysis and offer more practical guidance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the framework and results.
*   Establishment of fundamental limits on query rate and fidelity.
*   Provides a strong starting point for further research in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for source-channel separation with unknown channel state.
*   Potentially restrictive modeling assumption ignoring item side information.
*   Misleading wording regarding k-ary incidence coding bounds.




Overall, this is a solid contribution with significant potential. Addressing the technical comment and clarification request would substantially improve the paper and strengthen its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
