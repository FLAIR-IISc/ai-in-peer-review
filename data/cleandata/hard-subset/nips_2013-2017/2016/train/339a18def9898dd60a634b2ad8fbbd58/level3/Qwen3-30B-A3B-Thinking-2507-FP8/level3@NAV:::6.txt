

This paper models crowdsourcing as a communication problem using rate-distortion theory and channel coding to derive fundamental budget-fidelity trade-off bounds. The authors establish information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing in two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The derivation of Theorems 2 and 3 follows standard information-theoretic approaches and is technically sound, with proper application of source-channel separation principles.

The paper's main strength lies in its rigorous mathematical framework, which provides a theoretical foundation for crowdsourcing performance limits. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) in Section 4 also offers interesting insights into query design. However, the contribution is significantly undermined by a fundamental mismatch between channel coding theory and crowdsourcing constraints. Unlike communication channels where the encoder can freely design the input sequence, crowdsourcing imposes severe limitations: workers cannot be queried multiple times without violating iid assumptions, and the taskmaster has minimal control over the "channel input" design. This makes the channel-coding-style bounds potentially loose and practically irrelevant, as the paper fails to address which mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σ m_i) are actually implementable in crowdsourcing.

The paper also neglects to compare the theoretical bounds with state-of-the-art practical methods, which is essential for assessing significance. Without such comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the bounds are tight or useful for real-world applications. The minor issues noted (clarification of min in (8), elaboration of Theorem 4's proof, and clarification of ε_S vs. \hat{ε}) further detract from the paper's quality.

While the technical derivation is sound, the inappropriate application of channel coding theory to crowdsourcing—without addressing the fundamental differences between the two domains—significantly limits the paper's novelty and practical value. The contribution would be substantially stronger if the authors addressed these constraints and demonstrated how their bounds relate to existing practical methods. As presented, the paper's theoretical contribution lacks sufficient significance for publication at this venue.
