- Proposes an information-theoretic (rate-distortion) framework modeling crowdsourcing as source-encoder-channel-decoder to study budget (rate) vs. fidelity (distortion) limits.  
- Derives algorithm-independent lower bounds on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability.  
- Analyzes two worker error models: M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC).  
- Considers two crowdsourcer knowledge scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).  
- Introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a specific query scheme and derives a performance lower bound under SHC.  
- Assumptions: non-adaptive coding, iid source data, large datasets and worker pools; framework and theorems are clearly stated.  
- Paper is technically sound and provides concrete, computable bounds via rigorous rate-distortion analysis.  
- Highly original: first work to establish fundamental, algorithm-independent budget-fidelity limits for crowdsourcing.  
- Significant potential impact: bounds can benchmark algorithms, guide query/pricing design, and show value of skill estimation.  
- Major weakness: no experiments or simulations to relate theoretical bounds to practical algorithm performance.  
- Writing and organization are strong; the communication-systems analogy is effective and the problem formulation is clear.  
- Suggested improvements: condense standard source-channel background, reallocate space to include proof sketches for main theorems.  
- Recommendation: accept, but authors should add at least small-scale simulations (e.g., compare majority voting/EM to bounds) for the final version.
