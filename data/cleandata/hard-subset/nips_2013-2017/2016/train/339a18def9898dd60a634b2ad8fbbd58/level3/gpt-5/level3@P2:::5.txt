Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models labeling crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Workers are modeled as parallel independent discrete memoryless channels. Two channel families are analyzed: (i) the M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) to capture random labeling noise, and (ii) the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), where a worker is a perfect hammer with probability q and otherwise responds uniformly at random. The taskmaster uses non-adaptive coding (query design and assignment fixed prior to seeing any answers). Two knowledge regimes are considered: (SL-UK) unknown worker skill levels to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer, and (SL-CS) worker skills perfectly known at the crowdsourcer (e.g., after ideal inference).

Using rate–distortion and channel capacity arguments, the authors present lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target average Hamming error. Theorems 2–3 give information-theoretic limits for MSC pools under SL-UK vs. SL-CS, highlighting the value of knowing skills (capacity of the “average worker” vs. average of capacities). The paper further introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): queries present k items and ask for within-query partitioning, which prunes invalid answer patterns and yields “built-in” error correction. For SHC(q), Lemma 1 computes the spammer’s induced error probability under kIC, leading to Theorem 4: a necessary condition on rate for any decoder (including an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers) to meet a target error. A corollary gives an information-theoretic benchmark for SHC with known skills. The paper also discusses a pricing heuristic for kIC based on rate lower bounds.

The work is complementary to aggregation/inference methods such as majority vote and Dawid–Skene EM [5] and to budget-optimal assignment and worker selection (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Abraham et al.) by focusing on fundamental limits rather than algorithms. It connects crowdsourcing to Shannon’s separation theorem and joint source–channel coding.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling that bridges information theory and crowdsourcing, with explicit rate–distortion formulations of budget vs. fidelity.
- Distinct analyses for SL-UK and SL-CS provide insight into the benefit of estimating worker skills.
- kIC is a concrete, practically relevant query design with error-correcting structure; the SHC analysis and necessary conditions are novel and useful.
- The pricing discussion provides actionable guidance for practitioners.
- Overall well-organized and clearly written.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is missing: no simulations comparing standard aggregation methods (majority, DS, spectral/EM) or practical kIC decoders against the theoretical limits.
- Strong assumptions limit scope: non-adaptive assignment, iid items, independent channels, MSC/SHC only, and in places uniform priors (N=2) and oracle skill knowledge.
- The main text outsources all proofs; Section 3.1 is largely textbook background while sketches of the new converses/achievability are absent in the main body.
- Notation could be tightened (M vs. N; pmax and HN definitions; relation between number of classes and number of answer choices).
- Some modeling choices merit discussion: e.g., when queries influence difficulty-dependent error rates (feedforward), label imbalance, and adversarial behaviors beyond SHC.

Quality
- The theoretical claims are well-motivated, grounded in standard information-theoretic tools, and appear sound (I did not check the appendix proofs line-by-line). Theorems 2–3 are (converse) lower bounds on rate; clarify in the text that they are necessary conditions unless achievability is explicitly established under the crowdsourcing constraints (non-adaptive querying, particular alphabets).
- The kIC analysis under SHC provides interpretable necessary conditions and a clean dependence on k and q. More discussion of tightness (e.g., simple achievable schemes) would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
- The paper reads well and is generally self-contained. To improve: (i) shorten Section 2.4 and the textbook review in Section 3.1; move some to appendix and include brief proof sketches for the new theorems; (ii) unify notation for class cardinality (N) and answer alphabet size (M), and state assumptions when M=N; (iii) explicitly flag when results assume uniform label priors and N=2.

Originality
- Casting budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing via rate–distortion and separation is fresh and different from algorithm-focused prior work. The kIC necessary condition under SHC with an oracle decoder appears new.

Significance
- The bounds can guide system design (e.g., required redundancy, value of skill estimation) and query/pricing strategies, especially for non-adaptive labeling tasks. Significance would be higher with empirical comparisons showing gaps between practice and limits.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add simulations: compare majority vote, DS, and a simple kIC decoder against the lower bounds under MSC/SHC across q, k, and class priors; include finite-sample effects.
- Provide proof sketches or key lemmas in the main text for Theorems 2–4; move some of Section 3.1 to appendix.
- Clarify achievability vs. converse. Under what conditions (alphabets, adaptive vs. non-adaptive) is separation tight here?
- Discuss non-uniform priors, class imbalance, and difficulty-dependent error models; comment on adaptivity and worker selection (relating to [1], [6]).
- Minor: check the pricing ratio in Appendix E; π(k1)/π(k2) likely scales as k1/k2 (the text shows k1k2, which looks like a typo).

Questions for authors
- How tight are the kIC necessary conditions? Can you provide a simple achievable scheme that approaches the bound for moderate k and q?
- How sensitive are results to deviations from independence (e.g., correlated worker errors or item difficulty)?
- Can the framework be extended to adaptive querying without losing tractability?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel theoretical framing and bounds.
- Practical insights on query design (kIC) and pricing.
- Clear exposition; relevance to NIPS communities in learning from noisy labels.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical validation; limited discussion of tightness.
- Strong assumptions (non-adaptive, simple channel models).
- Some redundancy in background; proofs not sketched in main text.

Overall recommendation
- Weak accept. The theoretical perspective is valuable and likely to spur follow-up work, but the paper would benefit from simulations and tighter presentation.

Reviewer confidence
- Medium. I did not verify appendices in detail.
