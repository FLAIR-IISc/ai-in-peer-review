- Presents an information-theoretic analysis of budget–fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing, deriving lower bounds on queries needed for a target error probability.  
- Novel framing: treats crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to establish fundamental performance limits independent of specific algorithms.  
- Models the crowd as parallel noisy channels and studies two scenarios: worker skill levels unknown to the taskmaster (SL-UK) and perfectly estimated during inference (SL-CS).  
- Uses two worker error models—Markov symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel (SHC)—to capture different worker behaviors.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a specific query scheme and provides insights into optimized query pricing.  
- Produces rigorous theorems giving lower bounds on required query rates (budget) for given fidelity; paper is well-organized and clearly written.  
- Strength: theoretical soundness and potential practical impact as benchmarks for evaluating and designing crowdsourcing algorithms.  
- Major weakness: no empirical validation or simulations to demonstrate practicality or compare with real-world systems (e.g., AMT).  
- Presentation issues: Section 2.4 (distortion/design) and Section 3.1 (background) are verbose and could be more concise.  
- Accessibility issue: proofs are relegated to appendices; providing proof sketches in the main text would improve readability.  
- Recommendation: overall a strong theoretical contribution; accept contingent on addressing conciseness and proof-accessibility concerns.
