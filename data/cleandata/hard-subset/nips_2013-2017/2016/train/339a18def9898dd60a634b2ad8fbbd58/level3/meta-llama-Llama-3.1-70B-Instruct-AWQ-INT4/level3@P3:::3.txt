This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for modeling and analyzing the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing. The authors derive novel bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity, considering both scenarios where the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and perfectly known (SL-CS) to the crowdsourcer. The paper also introduces a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyzes its performance.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the crowdsourcing rate, which serves as a fundamental limit on the performance of any crowdsourcing system. The authors show that these bounds depend on the distribution of the source data, the number of workers, and the quality of the workers. The results are established using a joint source-channel coding scheme, which represents the query scheme and inference, over parallel noisy channels, modeling workers with imperfect skill levels.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the information-theoretic framework and the crowdsourcing model. The technical details are sound, and the proofs are provided in the appendices. The paper also includes numerical results and comparisons with existing methods, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a high-level intuition and practical takeaways. The authors do a good job of explaining the key insights and results in a way that is accessible to non-experts. However, some of the technical details may still be challenging for non-information-theory readers to follow.

To improve the paper's clarity and accessibility, I suggest the following:

1. Restate many results in crowdsourcing/application terms instead of channel/code/distortion language. This would help to make the paper more accessible to readers who are not familiar with information theory.
2. Highlight the high-level intuition and practical takeaways more prominently, for example, by expanding the end of Section 3 and adding a concluding takeaway.
3. Consider including related work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing, and work combining incentives with aggregation. This would help to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the crowdsourcing problem and the proposed solution.
4. Clarify the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster," which confused me as a reviewer.
5. Provide a more detailed caption or clearer figure for Figure 2, which is unclear.
