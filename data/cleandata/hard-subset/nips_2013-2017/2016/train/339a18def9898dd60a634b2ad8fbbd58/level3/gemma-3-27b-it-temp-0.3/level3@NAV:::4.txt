## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, framing the problem as a joint source-channel coding scenario. The core idea of mapping crowdsourcing to this framework and deriving budget-fidelity trade-offs via lower bounds is a compelling and potentially fruitful approach. The rate-distortion framing is particularly interesting and could inspire future work exploring more sophisticated coding schemes tailored for crowdsourcing environments.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing, moving beyond purely algorithmic or system-level optimizations. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding offers a concrete example and a starting point for practical implementations, and the discussion of query pricing is a welcome addition.

However, the paper suffers from several limitations. A key concern is the simplification of worker characteristics. Merging worker skill and reliability into a single channel model obscures the distinction between inherent ability/rating and transient reporting noise. This simplification may limit the model’s realism and applicability to scenarios where worker quality is a primary concern. Furthermore, the assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers is unrealistic. In practice, task allocation is often informed by known worker ratings and heterogeneous capabilities, and a more nuanced allocation strategy would likely improve performance.

The channel models used to represent worker unreliability (MSC and SHC) may also be overly simplistic. They may not adequately capture the behavior of adversarial or strategically malicious workers who intentionally provide misleading responses. The authors need to more thoroughly justify the realism of their assumed worker/channel models and discuss the potential impact of these simplifications.

Several methodological issues require attention. The assumption that P(B(X)) is known is stated late in the paper (page 6) and should be explicitly declared earlier. The numerical results and explanations are unclear; the curves in Fig. 2 require improved labeling, and the meaning of k=1 should be explicitly stated. Finally, the authors misuse the terms ‘Theorem’, ‘Lemma’, and ‘Corollary’ without numbering; using lowercase or adding numbers would improve clarity. Minor corrections are also needed, specifically fixing the figure references on page 4 (Figure 1.a → 1.b, 1.b → 1.c).



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem.
*   Derivation of information-theoretic lower bounds on budget-fidelity trade-offs.
*   Potential to inspire new coding schemes and system designs for crowdsourcing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Oversimplification of worker characteristics and task allocation.
*   Potentially unrealistic channel models for worker unreliability.
*   Methodological issues and lack of clarity in numerical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable theoretical framework, the limitations and methodological issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and address the concerns outlined above. A more realistic model of worker behavior and a clearer presentation of the numerical results would significantly strengthen the contribution.
