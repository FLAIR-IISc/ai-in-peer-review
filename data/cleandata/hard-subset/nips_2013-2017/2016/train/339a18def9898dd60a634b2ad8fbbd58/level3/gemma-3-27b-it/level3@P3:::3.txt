This paper tackles the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework. The core idea – modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem – is intriguing and potentially impactful, offering a novel perspective on a widely used practice. The authors derive fundamental limits on achievable fidelity given a budget constraint, and investigate a specific query scheme (k-ary incidence coding) along with associated pricing strategies. While the technical details were challenging to fully grasp, the overall approach and potential insights are compelling.

However, the paper suffers from accessibility issues. Despite the authors' efforts, the heavy reliance on information-theoretic terminology (rate, distortion, capacity, etc.) makes it difficult for readers unfamiliar with the field to appreciate the core contributions. Many results are presented in channel/code/distortion language when restating them in crowdsourcing/application terms would significantly improve understanding. For example, discussing the implications of the derived rates in terms of the number of tasks needed for a desired accuracy would be more concrete. The end of Section 3 feels abrupt; expanding on the practical implications of Theorems 2 and 3 – what do these bounds *mean* for a practitioner designing a crowdsourcing system? – would be invaluable. A concluding takeaway summarizing the key insights for a non-information-theory audience would also be highly beneficial.

The distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster" is unclear and should be clarified. Throughout the paper, these terms seem to be used interchangeably, causing confusion. A precise definition of each role would improve clarity. Additionally, there are minor technical issues: line 84 should read “are reviewed in,” and a typo exists at line 122 (“A A(X1,X2).”). Lines 275–276 (“In case…”) are particularly dense and difficult to parse; rephrasing is strongly recommended. 

Figure 2 is currently unclear. The caption needs to be significantly more detailed, explaining the axes, curves, and relationships being illustrated. A clearer figure visualizing the tradeoffs would be preferable.

Regarding related work, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of relevant literature. Specifically, the authors should acknowledge and discuss work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing. This field directly addresses the issues of worker motivation, spam, and quality control, complementing the information-theoretic approach. Including work that *combines* incentive mechanisms with aggregation techniques would be particularly relevant, as it bridges the gap between theoretical limits and practical system design. References like [Abraham et al., COLT 2016] on bandit-based task allocation, as well as work on incentivizing truthful reporting in crowdsourcing, would be valuable additions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing is a fresh and potentially impactful approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents mathematically sound results establishing fundamental limits.
*   **Potential Significance:** The insights could lead to more efficient and reliable crowdsourcing systems.
*   **Interesting Query Scheme:** The k-ary incidence coding scheme is a novel contribution with potential benefits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Accessibility:** The paper is difficult to understand for readers without a strong background in information theory.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** The connection between the theoretical results and practical system design is not fully developed.
*   **Incomplete Related Work:** The discussion of related work is lacking, particularly regarding economic incentives and mechanism design.
*   **Clarity Issues:** The unclear terminology and dense writing hinder understanding.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising and novel contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. However, significant improvements in accessibility, clarity, and related work discussion are needed before it can be considered a strong addition to NIPS. Addressing the reviewer's comments, particularly those relating to clarifying the practical implications and restating results in crowdsourcing terms, would substantially strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance contingent upon major revisions addressing these concerns.
