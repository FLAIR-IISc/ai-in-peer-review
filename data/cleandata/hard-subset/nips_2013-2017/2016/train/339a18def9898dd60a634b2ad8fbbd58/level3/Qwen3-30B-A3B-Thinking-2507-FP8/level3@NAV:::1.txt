

This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing by modeling workers as noisy communication channels and reformulating the labeling problem as a channel coding problem. The authors establish fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in crowdsourcing, mapping the crowdsourcing budget to code rate and analyzing distortion versus budget tradeoffs. They propose a novel coding scheme called k-incidence coding (kIC) for labeling subsets of k items and analyze its performance under different channel models (symmetric memoryless channel and spammer-hammer channel).

The paper makes a strong contribution by connecting crowdsourcing to information theory, which has not been extensively explored in previous NIPS work. While prior research (e.g., Dawid-Skene algorithm, EM-based approaches) has focused on inference algorithms, this work establishes fundamental performance bounds that any query scheme can achieve. The authors effectively relate their framework to existing crowdsourcing literature and provide clear theoretical results with theorems 2 and 3 establishing information-theoretic limits for both unknown and known worker skill levels.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis, though I must note my limited expertise in information theory prevents me from fully evaluating the mathematical depth. The writing is clear and well-structured, with appropriate references to related work in crowdsourcing and information theory. The kIC scheme is well-motivated and the analysis of its performance provides valuable insights.

The significance of this work is substantial as it establishes fundamental limits that can guide the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems. By framing the problem in information-theoretic terms, the authors provide a new lens through which to view crowdsourcing optimization, potentially enabling more principled approaches to query design and budget allocation.

Pro: Novel theoretical framework connecting crowdsourcing to information theory, clear establishment of fundamental limits, well-motivated kIC scheme, strong connection to prior work.

Con: Limited experimental validation beyond theoretical bounds, the information-theoretic approach may not directly address practical implementation challenges in real-world crowdsourcing platforms.

Overall, this paper offers a valuable theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of crowdsourcing fundamentals. While the technical depth is beyond my expertise, the paper appears to make a significant contribution to the field by establishing fundamental performance limits that will guide future work. The paper is well-written, clearly positioned in the literature, and addresses an important problem from a novel perspective.
