This paper presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework. The authors aim to identify the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the ultimate fidelity that can be achieved with a given budget. They model crowdsourcing workers as noisy channels, employing both symmetric memoryless channels and spammer/hammer channels to capture the imperfections in worker responses.

The paper reformulates the labeling problem as a coding problem for these channels, mapping the crowdsourcing budget to the code rate for analytical purposes. This allows the authors to analyze distortion versus budget tradeoffs, providing valuable insights into the design of optimized crowdsourcing systems.

One of the key contributions of the paper is the proposal and analysis of a basic coding scheme called k-incidence coding, which involves labeling subsets of k items. The authors derive a lower bound on the performance of k-incidence coding with a spammer-hammer worker pool and present numerical results to demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The use of information-theoretic concepts to model crowdsourcing is innovative and provides a fresh perspective on the problem. The analysis of distortion versus budget tradeoffs is thorough, and the results provide valuable insights into the design of optimized crowdsourcing systems.

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The paper is original, and the approach is novel. The authors combine familiar techniques from information theory and coding theory to tackle the problem of crowdsourcing. The related work is adequately referenced, and the authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

The results are significant, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors provide a unique theoretical framework for analyzing crowdsourcing, which can be applied to various problems in this domain. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a unique theoretical approach to crowdsourcing.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of distortion versus budget tradeoffs, which is valuable for the design of optimized crowdsourcing systems.
* The proposal and analysis of k-incidence coding is a key contribution of the paper.
* The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper assumes a specific model for the workers' skill levels, which may not be accurate in all scenarios.
* The analysis is limited to a specific type of crowdsourcing problem, namely labeling.
* The paper does not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach, and the numerical results are limited.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing. The results are significant, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. While there are some limitations to the paper, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper is a valuable contribution to the field.
