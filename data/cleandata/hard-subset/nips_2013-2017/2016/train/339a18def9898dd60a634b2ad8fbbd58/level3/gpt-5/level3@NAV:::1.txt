This paper models crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels, treating workers either as M-ary symmetric memoryless channels (MSC) or as spammer/hammer channels (SHC). It reformulates labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem, mapping budget to code rate and fidelity to distortion, and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on achievable error for both unknown and known (to the decoder) worker skill scenarios. The paper also proposes k-incidence coding (kIC), where each query jointly labels k items, quantifies its implicit error-correction capability via the reduction of invalid response patterns, and analyzes its distortion–rate tradeoff under SHC, including an oracle lower bound and a pricing rule-of-thumb. This connects to prior NIPS work on aggregation and worker modeling (Dawid–Skene; GLAD/Whitehill et al.; Karger–Oh–Shah; spectral/EM methods by Zhang et al.) by shifting emphasis from algorithmic inference to fundamental limits.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound at a high level, with clear use of separation and capacity/rate–distortion tools and explicit statements (Theorems 2–4, Corollary 1). However, several assumptions are strong (iid items; MSC/SHC; large-block asymptotics; non-adaptive coding), the achievability side is limited (bounds are mostly necessary; constructive codes beyond kIC are sparse), and empirical validation is minimal. Clarifications would help on finite-sample effects, tightness of bounds, and how kIC compares empirically to majority vote/DS under equalized cost.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and readable for an information-theory audience. Adding more intuition (e.g., diagrams quantifying valid kIC patterns as k,N vary), a consolidated notation table, and a more explicit mapping from platform pricing to rate would improve accessibility to the NIPS audience.

Originality: Recasting crowdsourcing through joint source–channel coding and deriving rate–distortion-style limits is a fresh perspective. kIC generalizes pairwise/ternary incidence queries with a principled analysis; to my knowledge, the oracle lower bounds under SHC and the budget–pricing linkage are novel.

Significance: Establishing limits is valuable for the design space (e.g., adaptive assignment, query arity, pricing). Practical impact depends on relaxing assumptions (difficulty/item-response models, adversaries beyond SHC, adaptivity) and on demonstrations that approach the bounds.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled framework linking budget to fidelity via information theory.
- Novel perspective with potentially broad influence on CS system design.
- Concrete analysis of kIC and actionable pricing heuristic.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong modeling assumptions; limited empirical/constructive evidence.
- Little comparison to state-of-the-art inference under realistic costs.
- Non-adaptive focus; limited treatment of item difficulty and heterogeneity.

Note: I find the paper interesting, though this is somewhat outside my core expertise; my comments are high-level.
