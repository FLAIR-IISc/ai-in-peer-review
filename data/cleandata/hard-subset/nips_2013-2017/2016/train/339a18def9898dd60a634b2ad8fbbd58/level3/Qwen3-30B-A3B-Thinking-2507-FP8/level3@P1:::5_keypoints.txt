- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion problem to find minimum queries per item for a target error probability.
- Models workers as parallel noisy channels using M-ary symmetric channel and spammer-hammer channel models.
- Analyzes two scenarios: skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and skill levels known (SL-CS).
- Derives fundamental limits (Theorems 2 and 3) for both SL-UK and SL-CS settings.
- Proposes and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), showing structured queries can exploit error-correcting properties to improve performance.
- Theorem 4 provides a necessary condition for kIC performance with spammer-hammer workers.
- Novel contribution: applies classical information theory to crowdsourcing, establishing theoretical benchmarks that prior NIPS work lacked.
- Strong technical quality: clear problem definition, rigorous communication model, and well-organized presentation with helpful figures.
- Provides practical design guidance including a pricing insight for queries (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2).
- Significant potential impact for both theoretical and practical crowdsourcing communities and other human-in-the-loop systems.
- Major weakness: lacks empirical validation or simulations comparing to practical methods like Dawid-Skene.
- Presentation issues: Section 3.1 is overly detailed, Section 2.4 could be shortened, and main-theorem proof sketches should be moved into the text.
- Appendix issues: discussion of query pricing and other practical points should be integrated into the main paper.
- Overall assessment: technically strong and novel but limited by absence of experiments, making it a borderline accept pending empirical validation.
