Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel communication problem and uses an information-theoretic (rate–distortion) lens to quantify the fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoff. Workers are modeled as parallel, independent discrete memoryless channels. Two channel families are analyzed: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) to capture symmetric worker noise, and (ii) the spammer–hammer channel SHC(q), where a worker is a perfect “hammer” with probability q and otherwise answers uniformly at random. The authors focus on nonadaptive coding (task assignments fixed upfront), consider two knowledge regimes—skill-level unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK) versus perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS)—and derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error probability.

Within this framework, they also study k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and asks for a partition into equal/different bins. Because not all k-wise incidence patterns are valid, kIC has built-in error-correction. For a SHC(q) worker pool and binary labels (N=2), the paper derives a necessary condition (lower bound) on the rate for kIC under an oracle decoder, and provides a pricing rule-of-thumb that scales roughly linearly with k.

The paper is well positioned relative to classic aggregation methods (majority voting, Dawid–Skene EM) and more recent probabilistic/graph-based approaches, as well as budget-reliability tradeoffs and worker-selection/bandit formulations. Its contribution differs from prior NIPS work by aiming at information-theoretic limits for nonadaptive crowdsourcing, rather than proposing a new estimator or assignment algorithm.

Strengths
- Novel viewpoint: Casting crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding is original and insightful, yielding clean converse-style lower bounds on queries per task for a target error probability.
- Clear modeling: Workers as parallel independent channels (MSC and SHC) and the SL-UK vs SL-CS regimes are clearly delineated. The contrast between “capacity of the average worker” (SL-UK) and “average capacity across workers” (SL-CS) is crisp and useful.
- Concrete code family: The kIC family is natural in practice (pairwise/ternary/group comparisons are common) and the paper quantifies its inherent error-correction and budget scaling.
- Practical relevance: The results give designers guidance for budgeting and pricing (e.g., kIC price approximately proportional to k) and highlight limits nonadaptive designs cannot beat.
- Writing and organization: The paper is well written and generally well organized.

Weaknesses
- Lack of empirical validation: No experiments or simulations compare practical schemes (e.g., majority, DS/EM, spectral, or real AMT workflows) against the theoretical limits, even on synthetic data. This makes it hard to assess the tightness and real-world relevance of the bounds.
- Scope limitations: The analysis assumes nonadaptive coding and equal load across workers because the taskmaster is ignorant of skills. While clean theoretically, many systems use adaptive assignment and worker modeling; a discussion or preliminary bounds for adaptive schemes would strengthen the impact.
- Overlong background: Section 3.1 reproduces standard source/channel coding background; Section 2.4 is also longer than needed. These displace space that could be used for intuition, proof sketches, and experiments.
- Modeling simplifications: Independence across workers and memorylessness within workers may be violated by correlated task difficulties and learning/fatigue effects. The paper notes possible feedforward but does not analyze it.
- kIC constraints: The kIC necessary condition is derived for binary labels (N=2) and oracle decoding; general-N or realistic decoders are not explored.

Quality
- The theoretical claims are sound and well supported by derivations. The converse-style lower bounds for SL-UK and SL-CS with MSC and the necessary condition for kIC with SHC(q) are coherent and useful. I did not check the appendix proofs in detail.
- The absence of experiments is the main quality gap. Even small-scale simulations would illuminate tightness (e.g., plotting majority/DS/spectral kIC performance vs the SL-CS lower bound).

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. However, Sec. 3.1 (textbook recap) could be shortened or removed; Sec. 2.4 can be tightened. Replace this space with proof sketches for theorems and additional intuition for the role of priors (pmax) and alphabet size (M).
- For kIC, clearly state the number of valid response patterns as a function of k and N, and provide intuition for Lemma 1 (spammer error) and Theorem 4 (rate condition R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln(ȳS)).

Originality
- The approach is novel in the crowdsourcing literature and distinct from prior aggregation/assignment algorithms. Bringing rate–distortion and capacity-style converse bounds to this domain is original.

Significance
- The results provide actionable lower bounds and design guidance, especially for budgeting, pricing, and understanding the value of worker-skill knowledge. With experimental support, this could influence practical system design.

Specific suggestions
- Shorten Sec. 2.4 and remove or substantially shorten Sec. 3.1; add brief proof sketches and intuition for the main theorems instead.
- Add simulations: compare majority, DS/EM, and a simple spectral method against the SL-UK and SL-CS bounds for MSC; for kIC with SHC(q), plot empirical error vs the necessary condition across k and q.
- Discuss adaptive assignment: even without proofs, articulate expected gains and which pieces of the analysis might extend (e.g., effective capacity with active learning/worker selection).
- Clarify cost model: different kIC queries have different cognitive/time costs; formalize the pricing rule-of-thumb and validate it empirically.
- Broaden kIC: comment on extensions to N>2 and the impact on valid pattern counts and error-correction.
- Discuss robustness to nonuniform priors P(B(X)) and to heterogeneous M (number of response options).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and elegant information-theoretic framework with clear, interpretable bounds.
- Practical insights for budgeting and pricing, including kIC design.
- Well written; likely to stimulate follow-up work on adaptive limits and empirics.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical validation; unclear tightness of bounds in practice.
- Narrow focus on nonadaptive, equal-load regimes.
- Some space used for standard background rather than contributions.

Questions for authors
- How tight are the SL-UK/SL-CS bounds for moderate L under simple decoders (majority, DS)? Any finite-length corrections?
- Can the kIC necessary condition be extended to N>2 or to non-oracle decoders?
- How sensitive are the conclusions to correlated task difficulties or worker dependence?

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (assuming addition of a minimal experimental section would be feasible in the camera-ready). The theoretical framing is novel and valuable; empirical comparisons would substantially strengthen the paper.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate (did not check appendix proofs line-by-line).
