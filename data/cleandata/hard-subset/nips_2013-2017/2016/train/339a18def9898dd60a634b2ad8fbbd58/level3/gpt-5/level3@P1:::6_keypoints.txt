- Models crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity (rate–distortion) tradeoffs.
- Main results: lower bounds on queries per item R to achieve average labeling error ≤ ε for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills.
- Key insight: SL-UK uses “average worker” capacity, SL-CS uses “average of capacities.”
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), deriving spammer error rates and converse-style lower bounds under SHC(q).
- Provides a heuristic pricing rule linking query arity k to cost.
- Situates work relative to DS, spectral/EM hybrids, bandit selection, and graph-clustering; frames novelty as a Shannon-limit abstraction.
- Strength: clear formalization via separation theorems and interpretable limits (average worker vs average of capacities).
- Strength: unifying lens across worker models and query schemes for benchmarking.
- Strength: kIC analysis shows enumeration-induced error-correction and that larger k reduces spammer-induced error.
- Strength: offers a simple, actionable pricing heuristic.
- Weakness: implementability of general codebooks is not discussed; arbitrary encoders likely infeasible in human-in-the-loop settings.
- Weakness: unrealistic iid channel/use and freely designable input alphabet assumptions (worker memory, presentation effects, item difficulty violate model).
- Weakness: all results are converse-style bounds with no constructive achievability schemes beyond repetition/kIC; gap-to-bound unknown.
- Weakness: limited empirical validation and no comparison to practical aggregators (DS, spectral-EM, KOS) on realistic models.
- Technical/clarity issues: Theorem 4 proof sketch too brief; monotonicity/sign handling (e.g., ln(1−q) < 0) needs explicit care.
- Technical/clarity issues: Equation (8) minimization over encoders/decoders should be clarified; Equations (12)/(17) need derivation/citation for H_M(E(ε)).
- Notational issue: reconcile N (classes) vs M (answer choices) and define symbols at first use.
- Clarity: move key appendices into main text, state which code families are implementable vs abstract, and declare worker reuse/independence assumptions.
- Originality: fresh framing for NIPS but largely a direct application of classical information theory; kIC under SHC is modestly novel.
- Significance: valuable as a conceptual benchmark, but limited practical impact without constructive codes or empirical evidence.
- Suggestions: propose implementable code families (kIC/hypergraph/LDPC-like), provide achievability analyses and simulations, and compare to SOTA aggregators.
- Suggestions: discuss worker reuse constraints, item-difficulty (state) effects, adaptive assignment, and quantify capacity with state information.
- Acceptance arguments: principled benchmark linking IT and crowdsourcing; could inspire constructive designs; interesting kIC insights.
- Rejection arguments: limited novelty, loose implementability, no nontrivial achievability or empirical backing, and technical/notation gaps.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject — appealing conceptual framing but needs constructive schemes, empirical validation, and clearer treatment of assumptions to merit acceptance.
