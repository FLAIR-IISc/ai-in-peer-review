- Novel framing: Treats crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem using rate-distortion and channel coding tools.  
- Core idea: Framing crowdsourcing as a communication problem is interesting and potentially valuable.  
- Theorems: Derivations of bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are mathematically sound under the paper’s assumptions.  
- Novelty caveat: Contribution may be conceptually new but derivations are straightforward and novelty is limited.  
- Practical looseness: Resulting bounds are likely loose and may not reflect real-world performance.  
- k-ary incidence coding: Analysis of kIC is a useful addition but its practical relevance is unclear without comparison.  
- Key mismatch: Channel-coding assumptions (freely designable input u^n) don’t map well to crowdsourcing constraints.  
- Correlation issue: Re-querying the same worker breaks i.i.d. assumptions and introduces correlations not modeled, undermining bounds.  
- Implementability gap: Paper doesn’t specify which mappings from B(X)^n to U^{Σ m_i} are achievable as real query schemes.  
- Error-correction limits: Beyond repetition codes, it’s unclear how to design u^n to achieve stronger error correction in practice.  
- Empirical validation missing: No experiments comparing the bound to Dawid–Skene, EM methods, or majority voting.  
- Unclear utility: Without empirical comparison, it’s unknown whether the bound is far from achievable or offers actionable design guidance.  
- Minor: Equation (8) should state minimization is over codes C and C' satisfying E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor: Proof of Theorem 4 needs more detail; the step from error probabilities to the overall bound is unclear.  
- Minor: Theorem 4 should clarify whether ε_S is larger or smaller than ε̂ given ln(1−q) < 0.  
- Minor: In equation (17), the origin of H_M(E(ε)) is unexplained and needs justification or citation.  
- For acceptance: Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, sound mathematics, and introduction of kIC.  
- Against acceptance: Framework applicability is questionable, lacks empirical validation, bounds likely loose, and implementability of codebooks is insufficiently discussed.  
- Recommendation: Reject as-is; requires model revision to reflect crowdsourcing constraints or rigorous empirical evaluation of bound tightness.
