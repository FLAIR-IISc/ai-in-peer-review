This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems. The authors model crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and derive bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The key contribution of this work is the derivation of the distortion-rate function, which characterizes the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear overview of the problem, the proposed framework, and the main results. The technical details are sound, and the proofs are provided in the appendices. The authors also provide numerical results and comparisons with existing methods, which help to illustrate the effectiveness of their approach.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. Novel framework: The authors propose a new information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, which provides a fresh perspective on the problem.
2. Rigorous analysis: The authors provide a thorough analysis of the budget-fidelity trade-off and derive bounds on the distortion-rate function.
3. Practical relevance: The results have practical implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems and can be used to optimize the budget-fidelity trade-off.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. Accessibility: While the authors provide a clear overview of the problem and the proposed framework, the technical details may be challenging for non-experts to follow.
2. Limited scope: The paper focuses on a specific aspect of crowdsourcing systems, namely the budget-fidelity trade-off. Future work could explore other aspects of crowdsourcing systems, such as the impact of worker skill levels and motivations.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. Highlight high-level intuition and practical takeaways more prominently: While the authors provide a clear overview of the problem and the proposed framework, it would be helpful to highlight the key insights and practical implications of the results more prominently.
2. Restate results in crowdsourcing/application terms: To make the paper more accessible to non-experts, it would be helpful to restate the results in terms of crowdsourcing and applications, rather than using information-theoretic terminology.
3. Include related work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design: The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive review of related work on robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing.
4. Clarify the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster": The reviewer found the distinction between these two terms confusing, and it would be helpful to clarify their roles and responsibilities in the context of crowdsourcing systems.

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. With some revisions to improve accessibility and clarity, it has the potential to be a highly influential paper in the field.
