- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives budget–fidelity limits via a rate–distortion perspective.  
- Worker responses modeled as parallel noisy channels with two families: M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel (SHC).  
- Main theoretical bound: required query rate R ≥ R*(D)/C, where R*(D) is source rate–distortion and C is effective per-query crowd capacity.  
- Two decoder-knowledge scenarios analyzed: unknown worker skill (SL-UK) and crowdsourcer-known skill (SL-CS), showing capacity gains when skills are known.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): queries show k items and ask for incidence structure, giving implicit error-correcting redundancy.  
- For SHC, derives necessary conditions on kIC rate to achieve target error and compares these to information-theoretic limits; includes a pricing heuristic.  
- Pricing heuristic links query arity and cost via π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 for interpretability.  
- Work complements prior ML crowdsourcing literature by providing fundamental limits rather than new inference algorithms.  
- Clear conceptual contribution: principled mapping to rate–distortion with useful separation-based lower bounds as community benchmarks.  
- Insightful comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS that quantifies the value of knowing worker reliabilities.  
- kIC is a natural, interesting query design whose structural redundancy is well-motivated and illustrated under SHC.  
- Technical derivations are standard but sound; kIC analysis under SHC is careful and interpretable.  
- Major modeling concern: worker “skill” as a memoryless symmetric channel ignores systematic biases, adversaries, and instance-dependent difficulty—need justification and discussion.  
- Suggest distinguishing competence (confusion across classes) from response noise, consider adversarial/informed spammers, and model instance-dependent difficulty.  
- Clarify relationship between M (response alphabet) and N (true classes); many results seem to assume M = N and this should be stated.  
- Allocation assumption unrealistic: taskmaster assigns equal queries to all workers—need to emphasize non-adaptivity scope and discuss adaptive/per-worker budgeting or bandit-style allocation effects.  
- Prior P(B(X)) is assumed known late in the paper; state it upfront, discuss practical estimation and sensitivity to misspecification or minimax alternatives.  
- Numerical/figures need improvement: Fig. 2 curves should be labeled with k and q, axes units and per-item vs per-k-tuple rates clarified, and legend aligned (explain “k = 1”).  
- Distinguish clearly which curves are information-theoretic (unachievable) limits versus kIC necessary conditions in plots.  
- Scope/realism: many bounds are necessary but not tight—make the oracle-decoder assumption in kIC explicit and discuss realistic decoders (DS, spectral-EM) and empirical comparisons.  
- Notation and organization issues: fix figure cross-references, number and consistently reference Theorems/Lemmas/Corollaries, and define M and N once for consistent use.  
- Recommend adding an up-front “Modeling assumptions” subsection summarizing worker channels, prior knowledge, non-adaptivity, and independence assumptions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel rate–distortion perspective, useful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, and kIC’s design insight for query structure and pricing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited realism of worker/channel and allocation models, unclear numerical presentation, deferred key assumptions, and limited empirical validation.  
- Minor fixes requested: move P(B(X)) statement earlier, clarify N vs M in theorems/corollary, improve Fig. 2 labels/legend, number all formal results, and correct figure references on page 4.
