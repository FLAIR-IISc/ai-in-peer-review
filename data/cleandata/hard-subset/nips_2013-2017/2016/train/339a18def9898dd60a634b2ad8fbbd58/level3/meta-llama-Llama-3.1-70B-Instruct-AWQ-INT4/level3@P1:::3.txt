This paper presents a novel approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing problems in an information-theoretic framework. The authors derive fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding the performance of crowdsourcing systems. The paper's main contributions include:

1. **Modeling Crowdsourcing as a Communication System**: The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system, where a taskmaster (transmitter) sends queries to a crowd of workers (channels) and a crowdsourcer (receiver) collects and analyzes the responses. This framework allows for the application of information-theoretic tools to analyze the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.

2. **Derivation of Budget-Fidelity Limits**: The authors derive two theorems that provide lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity in crowdsourcing. The first theorem assumes that the skill levels of workers are unknown, while the second theorem assumes that the skill levels are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer.

3. **Analysis of k-ary Incidence Coding**: The authors analyze the performance of k-ary incidence coding, a query scheme that poses a query as a set of k items and inquires the workers to identify those with the same label. They derive a lower bound on the performance of k-ary incidence coding with a spammer-hammer worker pool and provide numerical results.

The paper's strengths include:

* **Novel Approach**: The authors' information-theoretic approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing problems is novel and provides a fresh perspective on the field.
* **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the performance of crowdsourcing systems, which can inform the design of practical systems.
* **Insights into Crowdsourcing**: The paper provides insights into the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, including the trade-off between budget and fidelity.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

* **Accessibility**: The paper is written in a technical style that may be difficult for non-experts in information theory to follow. The authors could benefit from providing more high-level intuition and practical takeaways.
* **Related Work**: The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, including robust economic incentives and mechanism design for crowdsourcing.
* **Clarity**: Some sections of the paper, such as the distinction between "crowdsourcer" and "taskmaster," could be clarified for better understanding.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. With some revisions to improve accessibility and clarity, the paper has the potential to be even more impactful.
