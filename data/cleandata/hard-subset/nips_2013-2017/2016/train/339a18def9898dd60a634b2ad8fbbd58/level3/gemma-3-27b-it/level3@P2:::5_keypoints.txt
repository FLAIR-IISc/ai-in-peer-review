- Investigates fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework.
- Models crowdsourcing as a communication system with workers as parallel noisy channels.
- Establishes lower bounds on the number of queries required to reach a target error probability.
- Considers both known and unknown worker skill scenarios, providing nuanced analysis for each.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a concrete query scheme application of the framework.
- Briefly discusses optimized query pricing and its relation to theoretical limits.
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and effectively motivated in the introduction.
- Modeling in Section 2 is thorough and provides a solid foundation for analysis.
- Presentation of information-theoretic bounds in Section 3 is logical and generally accessible.
- Novel approach: applying information theory to crowdsourcing limits is original and valuable.
- Theoretical results are rigorous and establish fundamental performance limits.
- Potential practical impact for designing crowdsourcing systems and pricing strategies.
- Major weakness: lack of empirical validation or simulations to assess practical relevance.
- Sections 2.4 and 3.1 are overly lengthy and could be streamlined for conciseness.
- Proofs are relegated to appendices; including short proof sketches in the main text would aid understanding.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add experiments/simulations, shorten specified sections, and include brief proof sketches.
