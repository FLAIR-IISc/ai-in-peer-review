- Novel approach: models crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop computation problem within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework.
- Worker modeling: treats workers as noisy channels, using symmetric memoryless channels and spammer/hammer channels.
- Reformulation: casts labeling as a coding problem and maps crowdsourcing budget to code rate for analysis.
- Main analysis: derives distortion versus budget tradeoffs to inform optimized crowdsourcing design.
- Contribution: proposes k‑incidence coding (labeling subsets of k items) and analyzes its performance.
- Theoretical result: provides a lower bound on k‑incidence coding performance for a spammer-hammer worker pool.
- Empirical results: presents numerical results demonstrating effectiveness, though limited in scope.
- Writing and organization: paper is well-written, clear, and concise.
- Technical soundness: analysis and claims are well-supported by theory and experiments.
- Originality: combines information theory and coding theory to offer a novel perspective on crowdsourcing.
- Significance: advances the state of the art and provides a reusable theoretical framework.
- Pros for acceptance: novelty, thorough distortion-budget analysis, k‑incidence contribution, technical rigor.
- Cons for acceptance: specific worker-skill model may not generalize to all scenarios.
- Scope limitation: analysis is focused on labeling problems and may not extend to other crowdsourcing tasks.
- Evaluation limitation: numerical evaluation is not comprehensive.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions (rating 8/10).
- Minor revisions suggested: broaden numerical evaluation, discuss limitations and future work, add more background on information-theoretic concepts for non-expert readers.
