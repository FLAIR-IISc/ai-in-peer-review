- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling workers as noisy channels and reformulating labeling as a coding problem.  
- Maps crowdsourcing budget to code rate to analyze budget-fidelity tradeoffs.  
- Derives distortion-rate functions for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios; Theorems 2–3 give bounds on queries per item.  
- Proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a structured query design that exploits error-correction to improve performance.  
- Models workers using symmetric memoryless and spammer-hammer channels, with the latter providing a more nuanced worker model.  
- Strong technical quality: rigorous, well-structured proofs using standard information-theoretic methods.  
- Elegant and insightful connection between coding theory and crowdsourcing that yields clear theoretical performance bounds.  
- Good clarity and organization; figures (notably Fig.1) effectively illustrate kIC.  
- Assumes familiarity with information theory; mathematical notation could be harder for non-specialists.  
- High originality: first to establish fundamental information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing and to introduce kIC.  
- Significant impact: provides a theoretical foundation for designing efficient crowdsourcing systems and opens new research directions.  
- Weaknesses: limited experimental validation beyond theoretical bounds.  
- Weaknesses: practical implementation details of kIC are not fully explored.  
- Weaknesses: does not address the case of unknown number of clusters (N).  
- Recommendation: technically sound and significant; reviewer recommends acceptance but notes limited empirical validation and accessibility concerns.
