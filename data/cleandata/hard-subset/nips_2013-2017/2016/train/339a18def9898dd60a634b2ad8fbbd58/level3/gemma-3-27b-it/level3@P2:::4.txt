This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, framing the problem as a joint source-channel coding problem and deriving information-theoretic lower bounds on achievable performance. The core idea of modeling crowdsourcing tasks as communication over noisy channels, where workers represent the channels and their skill/reliability dictate channel characteristics, is an interesting and potentially inspiring perspective. The paper establishes lower bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve a given fidelity, considering both scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown and known to the crowdsourcer. A specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, is analyzed and its performance compared against the derived lower bounds.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting crowdsourcing challenges to fundamental information-theoretic concepts. The rate-distortion framework provides a rigorous lens through which to analyze the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing systems. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding, while limited to specific channel models, offers a concrete example of how these theoretical bounds can be applied and potentially guide practical system design. The results have the potential to be significant, offering insights into optimal query strategies and resource allocation in crowdsourcing platforms.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A primary concern is the conflation of worker skill and reliability into a single channel model. While simplifying the analysis, this obscures a crucial distinction. Workers may possess high skill (understanding the task well) but be prone to errors due to carelessness or lack of attention, or they may lack the necessary skills altogether. Separating these factors could lead to more realistic and nuanced models. Furthermore, the assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers, regardless of their known ratings or heterogeneous capabilities, seems unrealistic. In practice, informed taskmasters will prioritize querying more reliable workers, and the model should account for this. The channel models employed (MSC and SHC) also raise concerns about realism. While SHC captures the presence of spammers, it doesn't fully represent more subtle forms of malicious or informed adversarial behavior that could be present in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.

The authors should clarify and strongly justify the realism of their chosen worker/channel models. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of these models and potential avenues for incorporating more sophisticated representations of worker behavior would strengthen the paper. The numerical results and their explanation are also unclear. The labeling of curves in Figure 2 is insufficient, and it isn’t explicitly stated what k=1 corresponds to (presumably a baseline query scheme). Providing a clearer interpretation of the numerical results would enhance their impact. 

A methodological issue concerns the assumption of knowledge of P(B(X)), the distribution of the true labels. This assumption is stated late in the paper (page 6, line 246) and should be declared earlier, as it significantly impacts the applicability of the derived bounds. The assumption should be discussed regarding its practical implications and limitations. Minor corrections are also needed: the figure references on page 4 are incorrect (Figure 1.a should be 1.b, and 1.b should be 1.c). Finally, the authors misuse the terms 'Theorem,' 'Lemma,' and 'Corollary' without numbering. Consistent numbering or using lowercase labels would improve clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of crowdsourcing as a communication problem with inherent limitations.
*   Rigorous information-theoretic analysis providing lower bounds on achievable performance.
*   Potential for inspiring future work on optimal query strategies and resource allocation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Oversimplification of worker characteristics by conflating skill and reliability.
*   Unrealistic assumption of uniform query allocation regardless of worker quality.
*   Limited realism of channel models for worker unreliability.
*   Unclear presentation of numerical results.
*   Methodological concerns regarding the assumption of knowing P(B(X)).



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and derives potentially valuable insights, the simplifying assumptions and lack of realism in the model significantly limit its practical relevance. Addressing the concerns raised above, particularly regarding worker modeling and the justification of assumptions, could substantially improve the paper’s quality and increase its potential impact. Currently, the limitations outweigh the contributions.
