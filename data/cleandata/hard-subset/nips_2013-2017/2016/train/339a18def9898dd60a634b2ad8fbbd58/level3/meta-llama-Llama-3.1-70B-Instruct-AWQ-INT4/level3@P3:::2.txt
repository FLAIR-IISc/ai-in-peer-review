This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item, providing valuable insights into the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing.

The paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly explained. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the crowdsourcing problem, including its applications, challenges, and existing solutions. The proposed JSCC model is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, including the derivation of fundamental limits on queries per item.

The paper's main contributions are the derivation of Theorems 2 and 3, which provide asymptotic query-rate limits for crowdsourcing with unknown and known worker skill levels, respectively. The authors also present a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model.

The paper's strengths include its originality, technical soundness, and significance. The authors tackle a challenging problem and provide a novel solution that sheds light on the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. One major technical comment is that the authors need to justify or cite that source-channel separation holds when the channel has a random state unknown to the encoder (and possibly known to the decoder). This assumption is crucial to the derivation of Theorems 2 and 3, and the authors should provide more evidence to support this claim.

Another modeling concern is that the encoder/decoder are assumed to only see labels and ignore original items as side information, which can be important and should be considered. This assumption may not be realistic in many crowdsourcing scenarios, and the authors should discuss the implications of this assumption and potential extensions to the model.

Additionally, the wording in Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2 is misleading, as "lower bounds improve as k and q grow" should state that the lower bound value decreases (implying potential performance gain), not that the bound itself is tightened without an upper bound for comparison.

Finally, the authors could benefit from including non-asymptotic (finite-blocklength) converses, citing Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.

In conclusion, this paper is a promising first step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing. While there are some areas that require improvement, the paper's originality, technical soundness, and significance make it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Originality: The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem.
* Technical soundness: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem, including the derivation of fundamental limits on queries per item.
* Significance: The paper sheds light on the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing and provides valuable insights into the budget-fidelity trade-off.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lack of justification for source-channel separation assumption.
* Ignoring original items as side information may not be realistic.
* Misleading wording in Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2.
* Limited to asymptotic analysis; non-asymptotic converses could provide a more comprehensive understanding.

**Rating:** 8/10

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
