- Introduces a novel information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication problem (labels = source, workers = noisy channels, query-inference = joint source-channel code).  
- Derives lower bounds on queries-per-task (budget) required to achieve target error probability (fidelity).  
- Analyzes worker models including symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channels (SHC).  
- Considers scenarios with worker skills known and unknown to the crowdsourcer.  
- Provides algorithm-independent, fundamental performance limits serving as benchmarks for practical systems.  
- Offers a new perspective compared to prior algorithm-focused work (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.).  
- Strength: highly original application of rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing.  
- Strength: results are significant for system design and pricing strategies.  
- Strength: paper is well-organized and clearly written.  
- Main weakness: no experiments or simulations to demonstrate practical relevance.  
- Recommendation: include comparisons to standard algorithms (majority voting, EM) on real or synthetic data to quantify the theory-practice gap.  
- Suggestion: shorten background sections and add proof sketches in the main body to improve intuition.  
- Overall verdict: strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance but urge inclusion of simulation results in the final version.
