This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling label crowdsourcing to derive fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors model the system as a communication problem: true labels are a source, workers are parallel noisy channels, and the query-inference mechanism is a joint source-channel code. Using this model, they establish lower bounds on the queries per task (budget) required to achieve a target error probability (fidelity). The analysis considers workers modeled as symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) or spammer-hammer channels (SHC), and examines scenarios where worker skills are either known or unknown to the crowdsourcer. This approach provides a new perspective compared to prior work in this area, which has largely focused on designing specific aggregation algorithms (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al.) rather than establishing algorithm-independent performance bounds.

The primary strength of this work is its originality and potential significance. By framing crowdsourcing in the language of rate-distortion theory, the paper provides a powerful new lens for a well-studied problem. The resulting performance bounds are independent of any specific algorithm and thus serve as a fundamental benchmark against which practical systems can be measured. This is a valuable contribution for both researchers and practitioners, offering insights into the ultimate efficiency of crowdsourcing systems and providing a theoretical basis for system design and pricing strategies. The paper is also well-organized and clearly written.

The main weakness is the complete absence of experiments or simulations. The theoretical contributions are compelling, but their practical relevance is not demonstrated. The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing the derived bounds to the performance of existing, widely-used algorithms (e.g., majority voting, EM-based methods) on either real or synthetic data. This would ground the theory and quantify the gap between the fundamental limits and current practice. Furthermore, while the proofs are deferred to an appendix (which I did not check in detail), the main body would benefit from more intuition. I suggest shortening the background review in Sections 2.4 and 3.1 and using the space to include proof sketches for the main theorems.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper introducing a novel and important perspective. Despite the lack of empirical validation, its originality and significance are high.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Provides fundamental, algorithm-agnostic performance limits.
*   Well-written and methodologically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Lacks empirical comparison, making it difficult to assess the gap between theory and practice.

I recommend acceptance, but strongly urge the authors to include simulation results in the final version to maximize the paper’s impact.
