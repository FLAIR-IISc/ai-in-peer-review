- Novel application of rate-distortion and joint source-channel coding to model crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic communication system.  
- Well-defined model using parallel noisy channels to represent workers and clear problem framing (Section 2).  
- Introduction and preliminary analysis of k-ary incidence coding as a potentially interesting query scheme with error-correction properties.  
- Rigorous derivation of information-theoretic bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) with sound mathematical treatment.  
- Major concern about practical relevance: unclear how tight or achievable the derived bounds are in real-world crowdsourcing.  
- Questionable applicability of standard channel-coding bounds because crowdsourcing encoding (query design) is heavily constrained compared to free channel input design.  
- IID worker and query-independence assumptions are oversimplified; repeated queries to the same worker violate IID.  
- Lack of empirical validation and no comparison to practical methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, majority voting) on real datasets.  
- Insufficient discussion of implementability and practical challenges of k-ary incidence coding (worker cognitive load, decoding cost).  
- Proof of Theorem 4 needs more detail; relationship between ε_S and the estimator is unclear and the term H_M(E(ε)) in (17) is not well justified.  
- Originality lies in framing crowdsourcing with information theory, though core techniques (rate-distortion, channel coding) are established.  
- Significance depends on bound tightness and empirical evidence; current lack of validation limits practical impact.  
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical contribution but practical relevance is questionable without stronger justification and experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel information-theoretic framing, clear mathematical model, potential of k-ary incidence coding.  
- Arguments against acceptance: questionable practical relevance, no empirical validation, limited implementability discussion, and concerns about applying standard coding bounds.  
- Recommendation: Reject, with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing empirical validation, bound tightness, and implementation limitations.
