- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication system (labels as source, workers as noisy channels, queries as codes).
- Derives rate–distortion style bounds relating budget (queries per item) to fidelity under two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).
- Proposes and analyzes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) with performance bounds under a spammer–hammer worker model.
- Claimed novelty: first formal application of rate–distortion theory to crowdsourcing budget–fidelity limits.
- Strength: mathematically sound application of classical joint source–channel coding theorems (Theorems 2 and 3).
- Main weakness: the crowdsourcing–channel analogy is questionable because practical query “encoding” is far more constrained than communication encoders.
- Practical gap: unclear which capacity-achieving codes/mappings are realizable in crowdsourcing beyond trivial repetition codes.
- Problematic i.i.d. and large-block-length assumptions: worker responses cannot realistically provide the repeated independent channel uses assumed.
- Clarity issues: Eq.8 minimization over P(Z,Ẑ) should be explicit; proof of Theorem 4 is too brief and needs expansion.
- Technical concern: inequality in Theorem 4 involves ln(1−q) and ln(ε_S) signs and the relation between ε̂ and ε_S needs clarification.
- Non-standard step: appearance of H_M(E(ε)) in SL-UK rate expression (average vs worst-case channel) requires justification or citation.
- Originality caveat: idea is novel but largely a direct application of known information-theory results without deep adaptation to crowdsourcing constraints.
- Significance limited: bounds may be very loose and the paper lacks empirical comparisons to practical algorithms to demonstrate relevance.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel perspective, technically sound, and conceptual contribution formalizing budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Arguments against acceptance: flawed model assumptions, potentially unachievable loose bounds, no empirical grounding, and limited methodological novelty.
- Conclusion: interesting theoretical perspective but weakened by disconnect between theory and practical crowdsourcing; needs either feasibility arguments for complex codes or empirical benchmarking.
