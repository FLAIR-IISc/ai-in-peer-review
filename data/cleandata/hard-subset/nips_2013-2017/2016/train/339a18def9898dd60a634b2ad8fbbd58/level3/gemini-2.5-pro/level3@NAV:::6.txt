This paper introduces an information-theoretic framework to establish fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing. The authors model workers as parallel noisy channels and treat the process of designing queries and aggregating responses as a joint source-channel coding problem. Using rate-distortion theory, they derive a bound on the minimum budget (queries per item) required to achieve a target fidelity (e.g., error rate). This work distinguishes itself from the majority of crowdsourcing literature, which typically focuses on the design and analysis of practical aggregation algorithms like majority voting or the Dawid-Skene EM algorithm, by instead aiming to define the ultimate performance benchmarks for any possible system.

The primary strength of this paper is the novel application of a well-established theoretical framework to the crowdsourcing domain. The formulation is elegant, and the derivation of the information-theoretic bound appears technically sound. Establishing such fundamental limits is a valuable scientific contribution, as it could provide a theoretical ceiling against which all practical methods are measured. If this is the first work to formally connect rate-distortion theory with crowdsourcing limits, it represents a significant conceptual advance.

However, the paper's main weakness lies in the questionable assumption that the full power of channel coding is available in the crowdsourcing setting. The tightness of channel-coding-style bounds relies on the freedom to design optimal input sequences (encodings) that can achieve channel capacity. In crowdsourcing, this "encoding" corresponds to the design of queries, which faces fundamental constraints not present in communication systems. For instance, querying the same worker repeatedly on an item to mimic repeated channel use violates standard i.i.d. assumptions. It is unclear what class of powerful error-correcting codes, beyond simple repetition (majority voting), is practically implementable. This core disconnect may render the derived bound very loose and thus of limited practical significance. The paper would be much stronger if it compared the theoretical bound to the performance of state-of-the-art practical methods to demonstrate its tightness.

Finally, a few minor points require clarification:
*   In equation (8), it should be clarified that the minimum is over all joint distributions `P(Z, Ẑ)` such that `E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ D^t`.
*   The proof of Theorem 4 would benefit from more detailed elaboration.
*   In Theorem 4, since `ln(1−q)` is negative, the relationship between `ε_S` and `hat{ε}` needs clarification to make sense of the inequality.
