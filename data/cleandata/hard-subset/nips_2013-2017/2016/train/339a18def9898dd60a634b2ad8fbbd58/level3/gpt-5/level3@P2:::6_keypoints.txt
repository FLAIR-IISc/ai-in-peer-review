- Proposes an information-theoretic formulation of crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion theory.  
- Models workers as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) or spammer–hammer channels (SHC) and analyzes two cases: skills unknown (SL-UK) and skills known (SL-CS).  
- Derives separation-style lower bounds on queries per item to meet target Hamming distortion (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), analyzes spammer error within kIC, and gives a converse-style lower bound (Theorem 4) plus a pricing rule-of-thumb.  

Strengths:
- Offers a clear, principled information-theoretic lens yielding compact, interpretable lower bounds.  
- Useful decomposition and comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS showing impact of decoder knowledge.  
- kIC is practical-sounding, with intuition on how k and q trade off with budget and a simple pricing insight.  
- Situates work relative to standard aggregation/inference methods and touches on practical issues like pricing and non-adaptivity.  

Main concerns about modeling and tightness:
- Separation-based bounds assume arbitrary, long block encoders C that are likely infeasible for human-readable, small queries used in CS.  
- IID DMC assumption ignores worker memory effects (learning, fatigue) and finite-pool sampling without replacement; kIC overlap induces dependence not discussed.  
- Lack of concrete, implementable code constructions beyond repetition; unclear how to realize strong error-correction in practice.  

Theoretical details needing clarification:
- Justify the denominators in Theorems 2/3 (log M − H_M(·)) with precise capacity/mutual-information calculations and citations.  
- Clarify minimization in Eq. (8) over conditional pmfs P(Ẑ|Z) or equivalent code mappings subject to distortion.  
- Check inequality directions and definitions in Theorem 4, define all symbols, and relate R to integer incidence counts for kIC.  
- Provide derivation/intuition for Lemma 1 combinatorial sums and state dataset balance assumptions.  
- Ensure consistent notation for M (answer alphabet size), N (classes), and k (incidence arity); clarify M in binary kIC.  

Empirical/practical evaluation lacking:
- No empirical comparison to DS, spectral-EM, Karger–Oh–Shah; small-scale simulations with concrete decoders would strengthen claims.  
- Robustness to unknown/misspecified source distribution P(B(X)) and class count N is not addressed; need practical estimation discussion.  
- Pricing rule-of-thumb lacks sensitivity analysis and discussion of platform/UI constraints and worker time.  

Quality and clarity:
- Derivations seem plausible at a high level but require more careful justification on mixture channel capacity and some proof details.  
- Generally well written but needs precise definitions, fixed notation, and expanded proofs for Theorem 4 and Lemma 1.  

Originality and significance:
- Framing CS budget–fidelity via rate–distortion is a nice conceptual contribution but largely applies standard separation theorems; novelty is moderate.  
- Potentially influential conceptually, but practical impact is limited without implementable constructions and empirical validation.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clean information-theoretic framing, explicit lower bounds, and useful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison; kIC/pricing insight is interesting.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Bounds likely loose under realistic implementability constraints, key assumptions (iid DMC, arbitrary codebooks) are unjustified, and no constructive schemes or empirical validation are provided.  

Recommendation:
- Leaning to reject pending stronger connections to implementable encoders/decoders, clearer treatment of SL-UK channel model, corrected proofs/notation, and empirical comparisons.
