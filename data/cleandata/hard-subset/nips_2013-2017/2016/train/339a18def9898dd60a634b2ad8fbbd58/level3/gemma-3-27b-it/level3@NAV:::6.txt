## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework, specifically using rate-distortion theory and channel coding concepts. The core idea is to model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel decoding problem, deriving bounds on achievable fidelity given a budget constraint (number of queries). The authors explore this bound under scenarios with unknown and known worker skill levels, and also analyze a specific query scheme – k-ary incidence coding – offering insights into optimized query pricing.

This work attempts a novel application of established information-theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing domain, which, if successful, could provide valuable theoretical insights. Previous work in crowdsourcing, as outlined in the introduction, largely focuses on practical algorithms like Dawid-Skene or majority voting, and doesn’t typically employ this level of theoretical analysis. The paper appropriately references related work and contextualizes its contribution within the existing literature. 

However, the main weakness lies in the applicability and tightness of the derived bounds. While the derivation itself appears sound, the core assumption that crowdsourcing behaves like a traditional communication channel is questionable. Unlike channel coding, the i.i.d. assumption is significantly challenged in crowdsourcing—repeatedly querying the same worker violates this assumption. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but don't fully address how it impacts the bound’s usefulness. Specifically, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how to map the theoretical encoding scheme *u<sup>n</sup>* to practical query assignments in a crowdsourcing setting, particularly with the i.i.d. constraint. The discussion of error correction beyond repetition codes feels underdeveloped.  The comparison to state-of-the-art practical methods, as suggested by the guidelines, is missing, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical bounds.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Sound theoretical derivation of the bounds (given the underlying assumptions).
*   Exploration of a potentially useful query scheme (k-ary incidence coding).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Questionable applicability of channel coding framework to crowdsourcing due to the i.i.d. assumption.
*   Potential looseness of the derived bounds and lack of comparison to existing practical methods.
*   Underdeveloped discussion of how to implement the theoretical encoding scheme in a real-world setting.



**Minor Comments:**

*   In (8), explicitly state that the minimization is over all valid codes C and C' satisfying E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ D<sup>t</sup>.
*   The proof of Theorem 4 would benefit from more elaboration and motivation.
*   In Theorem 4, clarify the relationship between ε<sub>S</sub> and  ̂, given ln(1−q) < 0.
*   In (17), provide a citation or justification for the appearance of H<sub>M</sub>(E(ε)).



**Overall:** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical exploration, the questionable applicability of the underlying assumptions and the lack of practical validation raise significant concerns. I would lean towards **rejection** at this time, but encourage the authors to address these concerns – specifically, by either demonstrating a practical instantiation of the theoretical framework or acknowledging its limitations more explicitly.
