**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, where labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as encoder, workers as channel uses with random skill-level states, and the crowdsourcer as decoder. The authors apply standard source-channel separation results to obtain asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item. They also derive a lower bound on the error probability of "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work in crowdsourcing and information theory, including the work of Dawid and Skene (1979) on maximum likelihood estimation of observer error-rates using the EM algorithm, and the work of Kostina and Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses. The authors also mention related work on crowdsourcing for clustering, such as the paper by Korlakai Vinayak et al. (2014) on graph clustering with missing data.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-written and well-organized**: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and logical structure.
2. **Promising first step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing**: The paper provides a promising first step toward modeling crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem, which has the potential to lead to new insights and results in the field.
3. **Asymptotic fundamental limits**: The authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item, which provides a useful benchmark for evaluating the performance of crowdsourcing systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of justification for source-channel separation**: The authors assume that source-channel separation holds when the channel has random state unknown to the encoder (and possibly known to the decoder), but they do not provide a clear justification or citation for this assumption.
2. **Modeling concern**: The authors assume that the encoder/decoder only see labels and ignore original items as side information, which can be important and should be considered in future work.
3. **Misleading wording**: The wording in Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2 is misleading, as it suggests that the lower bound value improves as k and q grow, when in fact the lower bound value decreases (implying potential performance gain), but without an upper bound for comparison.

**Originality**

The paper provides a new and original contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory, by modeling crowdsourcing as a JSCC problem and deriving asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item.

**Significance**

The paper has the potential to be significant, as it provides a new framework for evaluating the performance of crowdsourcing systems and could lead to new insights and results in the field.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear and concise language.
