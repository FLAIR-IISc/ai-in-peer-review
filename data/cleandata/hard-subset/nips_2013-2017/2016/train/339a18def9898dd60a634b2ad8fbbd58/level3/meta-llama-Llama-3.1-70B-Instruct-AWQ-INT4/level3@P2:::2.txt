This paper proposes a novel approach to model crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The authors derive asymptotic fundamental limits on queries per item and provide a lower bound on the error probability of "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model. The paper is well-written, and the approach is a promising first step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing.

The main idea of the paper is to model crowdsourcing as a communication system where labels are the source, the taskmaster is the encoder, workers are the channel uses with random skill-level states, and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. The authors apply standard source-channel separation results to obtain Theorems 2 and 3, which give asymptotic query-rate limits. The paper also provides a lower bound on the error probability of "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowdsourcing/channel model.

The paper is related to previous work on crowdsourcing, but the JSCC approach is new and provides a fresh perspective on the problem. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the related work and clearly explain how their approach differs from previous methods.

The technical quality of the paper is high, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. However, there are a few technical comments that need to be addressed. Firstly, the authors must justify or cite that source-channel separation holds when the channel has random state unknown to the encoder (and possibly known to the decoder). This is a critical assumption in the paper, and it needs to be clearly justified.

Additionally, the authors assume that the encoder and decoder only see labels and ignore original items as side information. This assumption may not be realistic in practice, and the authors should consider the impact of side information on their results.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. However, there are a few minor issues with the wording. For example, in Sec.4.2/Fig.2, the authors state that the lower bounds improve as k and q grow. This wording is misleading, and the authors should clarify that the lower bound value decreases (implying potential performance gain), not that the bound itself is tightened without an upper bound for comparison.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a new perspective on crowdsourcing and provides fundamental limits on queries per item. The results are important for understanding the limits of crowdsourcing and can be used to design more efficient crowdsourcing systems.

Overall, the paper is a promising first step toward JSCC modeling of crowdsourcing. However, the authors need to address the technical comments mentioned above and provide more clarification on some of the assumptions and results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a new perspective on crowdsourcing and provides fundamental limits on queries per item.

* The paper is well-written, and the technical quality is high.

* The authors need to justify or cite that source-channel separation holds when the channel has random state unknown to the encoder (and possibly known to the decoder).
