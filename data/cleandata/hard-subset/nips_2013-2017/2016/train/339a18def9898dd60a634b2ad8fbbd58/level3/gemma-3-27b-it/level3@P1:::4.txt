This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in digital crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding scenario. The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve a given fidelity, considering workers as noisy channels. The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and presents results relating its performance to the derived bounds. This is a novel application of information theory to a practical problem, and the framing offers a potentially insightful perspective for designing and analyzing crowdsourcing systems.

The work builds on existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly aggregation schemes like majority voting [8] and the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5], and extends them by providing fundamental limits rather than focusing solely on algorithmic optimization. It connects to broader information-theoretic concepts such as rate-distortion theory [4] and channel capacity [7], presenting a rigorous analysis within this established framework. The consideration of workers as parallel noisy channels is a reasonable abstraction, and the derived bounds offer valuable benchmarks for evaluating practical crowdsourcing systems.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framework:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing provides a fresh and mathematically rigorous perspective on the problem.
* **Fundamental Limits:**  Deriving information-theoretic lower bounds establishes ultimate performance limits and offers a benchmark for practical algorithms.
* **Clear Modeling:** The core modeling of crowdsourcing as a communication system, with workers as channels, is well-defined and facilitates analysis.
* **k-ary Incidence Coding:**  The introduction and analysis of a specific query scheme (k-ary incidence coding) demonstrate a practical application of the theoretical framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Oversimplified Worker Model:** A significant concern is the merging of worker skill and reliability into a single channel model. This conflates inherent ability with noise in reporting, potentially obscuring the impact of worker expertise.  Distinguishing between a worker’s true skill level (rating) and the probability of inaccurate responses is crucial for realistic system design.
* **Uniform Query Allocation:** The assumption that the taskmaster assigns the same number of queries to all workers is unrealistic. In practice, workers are often pre-rated, and task allocation is typically adjusted based on these ratings and heterogeneous worker capabilities. Ignoring this prior knowledge seems limiting.
* **Channel Model Limitations:** The reliance on MSC and SHC channel models may not adequately capture the behavior of more sophisticated workers, including those who are deliberately malicious or strategically providing noisy responses. The models lack the flexibility to represent, for example, adversarial attacks or informed spamming behavior.
* **Clarity of Numerical Results:** The numerical results and explanations (particularly around Fig. 2) are unclear. The curves are not adequately labeled, and the significance of k=1 is not explicitly stated.  A clearer explanation of the relationship between the theoretical bounds and the performance of k-ary incidence coding is needed.
* **Late Declaration of Assumption:** The assumption that the distribution P(B(X)) is known is stated relatively late in the paper (page 6, line 246). This assumption should be explicitly stated much earlier, as it is fundamental to the analysis.
* **Minor Editorial Issues:** There are minor inconsistencies in figure references (page 4: "Figure 1.a" should be "Figure 1b", etc.).  The authors should also consistently use lowercase or numbered references for Theorems, Lemmas, and Corollaries to improve clarity (e.g., “theorem 2” or “Theorem 2”).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous framework for analyzing crowdsourcing, offering a valuable contribution to the field.
* The derived information-theoretic bounds provide fundamental limits that can guide the design of future crowdsourcing algorithms.
* The introduction of k-ary incidence coding demonstrates a practical application of the theoretical framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The oversimplified worker model and unrealistic query allocation assumption limit the practical applicability of the results.
* The channel models may not adequately capture real-world worker behavior, particularly in the presence of adversarial or informed malicious actors.
* The clarity of the numerical results needs significant improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and technically sound application of information theory to the problem of crowdsourcing.  However, the limitations of the underlying assumptions and the lack of clarity in certain areas raise concerns.  While the paper provides valuable theoretical insights, its practical relevance is somewhat diminished by the oversimplifications.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with a request for major revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the worker model, query allocation, and channel models.  They should also clarify the numerical results and explicitly state key assumptions earlier in the paper. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field.
