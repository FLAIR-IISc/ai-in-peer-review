- Models crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels (MSC and SHC) and reframes labeling as joint source–channel coding mapping budget→code rate and fidelity→distortion.  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on achievable error for both unknown and decoder-known worker skill scenarios (Theorems 2–4, Corollary 1).  
- Proposes k‑incidence coding (kIC): queries that jointly label k items and reduce invalid response patterns, giving implicit error‑correction.  
- Analyzes kIC’s distortion–rate tradeoff under SHC and provides an oracle lower bound plus a pricing rule‑of‑thumb.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically sound, with clear use of separation and capacity/rate–distortion tools.  
- Strong assumptions: iid items, MSC/SHC worker models, large‑block asymptotics, and non‑adaptive coding.  
- Achievability side is limited—mostly necessary bounds and few constructive codes beyond kIC.  
- Empirical validation is minimal; needs finite‑sample analysis and empirical comparisons (kIC vs majority vote / Dawid–Skene) under equalized cost.  
- Clarity is good for an information‑theory audience but could use more intuition, diagrams (valid kIC patterns vs k,N), a notation table, and clearer mapping from platform pricing to rate.  
- Originality: fresh recasting of crowdsourcing as source–channel coding; kIC generalizes incidence queries and oracle bounds/pricing linkage appear novel.  
- Significance: establishes useful fundamental limits for system design (adaptivity, query arity, pricing), but practical impact depends on relaxing assumptions and demonstrating proximity to bounds.  
- Pros: principled budget–fidelity framework, novel perspective with potential broad influence, concrete kIC analysis and actionable pricing heuristic.  
- Cons: strong modeling assumptions, limited empirical/constructive evidence, little treatment of adaptivity or item difficulty/heterogeneity.  
- Reviewer qualifiedness: finds the paper interesting but comments are high‑level and somewhat outside their core expertise.
