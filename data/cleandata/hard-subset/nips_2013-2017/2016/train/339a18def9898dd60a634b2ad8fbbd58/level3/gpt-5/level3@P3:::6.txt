Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models labeling crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits using rate–distortion theory and channel capacity. The dataset labels B(X) form the source; workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels (MSC or SHC). The central bounds (Theorems 2–3) give the minimum queries per item to achieve target Hamming distortion (error rate) for two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and perfectly known skills at the decoder (SL-CS). The paper then studies a concrete “k-ary incidence coding” (kIC) scheme and derives a lower bound for SHC via an oracle-decoder analysis (Theorem 4), plus a simple pricing rule-of-thumb. The work connects to classical results (Shannon separation, Cover–Thomas; El Gamal–Kim) and to crowdsourcing literature (majority vote, Dawid–Skene; Karger–Oh–Shah). If this is indeed the first explicit use of rate–distortion and channel coding to bound budget–fidelity in crowdsourcing, the conceptual bridge is valuable.

Strengths
- Clear and technically sound derivation of separation-based bounds; formulas are interpretable and recover sensible dependences on label entropy, channel noise, and response alphabet size.
- Nice comparison of SL-UK vs SL-CS that cleanly isolates the value of knowing worker reliabilities.
- kIC is a natural query class; the SHC analysis and oracle converse provide intuition about how redundancy (k) and hammer fraction (q) trade off with budget.
- The paper is clearly written overall and grounded in standard information-theoretic tools.

Main concerns and weaknesses
- Implementability of channel-coding encoders. Unlike communication, a crowdsourcer cannot freely design an arbitrary u^n across items. Microtasks must be human-comprehensible local queries (e.g., label, pairwise/triplet comparisons), and the mapping C: B(X)^n → U^(Σ m_i) is severely constrained. The separation-based converse thus applies to an idealized encoder class much richer than what human-task constraints permit, making bounds potentially very loose for realistic systems.
- Reuse and iid assumptions. Channel coding relies on repeated iid channel uses. In crowdsourcing, repeatedly querying the same worker (to realize codeword structure) can break iid assumptions due to learning, fatigue, or dependence; avoiding reuse limits diversity and code design. These constraints are not reflected in the converses, again risking looseness.
- Codebook feasibility. It is unclear which mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σ m_i) are implementable given platform constraints, worker heterogeneity, and limited adaptivity. For example, beyond repetition codes (majority voting), how would one realize stronger “error-correcting” encoders in the microtask format? Without a characterization of feasible encoders, the capacity-style bounds may substantially overestimate achievable performance.
- Novelty. The derivations largely follow standard JSCC/separation arguments; while appropriate, the conceptual step is straightforward. The practical novelty hinges on connecting those bounds to realistic crowdsourcing constraints, which is currently limited.
- Empirical/algorithmic comparison. There is no quantitative comparison of the information-theoretic lower bounds against state-of-the-art practical methods (e.g., DS/EM variants, spectral-EM, adaptive task allocation). This makes it hard to judge tightness and practical value.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The JSCC-based converses and the kIC/SHC oracle bound are derived soundly.
- Completeness: The lack of constrained-encoder converses and empirical validation leaves a gap between the idealized bounds and practice.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with a helpful background section. Some proofs (Theorem 4) are terse and could be expanded as suggested below. Clarify notation and optimization domains in several equations.

Originality
- Moderate. Applying rate–distortion and channel-coding style converses to crowdsourcing is a neat framing, and kIC is a natural formalization of multi-item queries. However, the proofs and tools are standard, and the work does not yet show new tight converses under crowdsourcing-specific constraints.

Significance
- Conceptually useful as a baseline. Practical impact is limited without (i) converses under implementability constraints (local queries, limited worker reuse/adaptivity), and (ii) empirical comparisons to modern algorithms.

Suggestions for improvement
- Constrained encoder converses: Formulate and analyze bounds under a restricted class of encoders corresponding to implementable microtasks (e.g., per-item label queries; pairwise/triplet-only queries; limited item–worker incidence; bounded reuse of workers). Fano-style lower bounds under these constraints would be much more informative.
- Worker-reuse and dependence: Either justify the iid assumption with platform evidence or model memory/fatigue with channels with state/memory and give robust converses.
- Algorithmic/empirical comparison: Simulate realistic worker pools (MSC or SHC) and compare DS/EM/spectral methods and kIC decoders to the derived bounds; quantify the gap across budgets.
- Achievability sketches: Provide explicit constructions that approach the bounds within the feasible query classes (beyond repetition), or clarify why such constructions are infeasible.

Questions for the authors
- Which subclasses of mappings C in (3) are implementable on standard platforms? Can you formalize such constraints and re-derive converses?
- Can you design u^n beyond repetition while maintaining human interpretability and reasonable per-worker load?
- How sensitive are the bounds to mild dependence across repeated queries to the same worker?
- In SL-CS, how are skill estimates obtained and how do estimation errors affect the bounds?

Pros
- Sound, clean information-theoretic framework and bounds.
- Insightful comparison of SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- Natural analysis of k-ary incidence coding; useful pricing heuristic.

Cons
- Bounds likely loose due to unrealistic encoder freedom and iid assumptions.
- Limited novelty beyond applying standard JSCC converses.
- No empirical comparison to state-of-the-art practical methods.

Minor comments
- Eq. (8): Please clarify that the minimization is over source codes satisfying E[d(Z, Ẑ)] ≤ Dt; equivalently over conditional distributions P(Ẑ|Z) (or over codebooks C, C′) that meet the distortion constraint.
- Theorem 4: Expand the proof. Explicitly (i) relate R to the distribution of the number of times an item is seen by hammers, (ii) show the union bound/tight steps, and (iii) handle the even/odd k cases using Lemma 1.
- Theorem 4 sign/parameters: Since ln(1−q) < 0, clarify the direction of the inequality and whether the bound implies εS ≥ ε̂ or vice versa in your parameterization. A short remark will prevent confusion.
- Eq. (17): Explain the appearance of HM(E(ε)) (entropy of the effective channel mixture) or cite prior work on mixtures of symmetric channels; specify whether E(ε) denotes averaging over skill levels.
- Units/notation: Ensure consistent use of log base 2 for rates; define all symbols on first use; clarify that R is queries per item.
- Related work: Consider discussing how your bounds relate to task-allocation/budget-optimal results (already cited) and to practical aggregation algorithms, highlighting similarities and differences.

Overall
Interesting conceptual framing with solid derivations, but limited novelty and questionable practical tightness due to strong encoder assumptions. Strengthen with constrained converses and empirical comparisons.
