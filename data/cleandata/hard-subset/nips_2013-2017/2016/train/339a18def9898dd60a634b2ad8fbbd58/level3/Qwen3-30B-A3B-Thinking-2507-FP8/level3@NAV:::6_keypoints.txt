- Paper models crowdsourcing with rate-distortion theory and channel coding, deriving budget–fidelity trade-off bounds.
- Establishes information-theoretic limits for two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS).
- Derivations of Theorems 2 and 3 follow standard information-theoretic methods and are technically sound.
- Main strength is a rigorous mathematical framework that provides theoretical foundations for crowdsourcing limits.
- Analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) in Section 4 offers useful insights into query design.
- Fundamental mismatch: channel coding assumes freedom to design inputs, but crowdsourcing restricts repeated queries and input design.
- Workers cannot be queried multiple times without violating i.i.d. assumptions, limiting applicability of channel-coding results.
- Paper fails to specify which mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σ m_i) are implementable in real crowdsourcing, making bounds potentially loose and irrelevant.
- Neglects empirical comparison with state-of-the-art practical methods, so tightness and real-world usefulness of bounds are unknown.
- Minor clarity issues: clarify min in (8), expand proof of Theorem 4, and distinguish ε_S versus \hat{ε}.
- Overall, inappropriate application of channel coding to crowdsourcing without addressing domain differences reduces novelty and practical value.
- Contribution would be stronger if authors addressed constraints and demonstrated connections to existing practical methods; as is, not sufficient for publication.
