- Frames crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem and models workers as noisy channels.
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries needed to achieve a target labeling fidelity.
- Considers both unknown and known worker-skill scenarios in the bound derivations.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding as a concrete query scheme and compares it to the bounds.
- Well-motivated use of rate-distortion framework offering a rigorous lens on crowdsourcing limits.
- Results could guide optimal query strategies and resource allocation in crowdsourcing systems.
- Major weakness: conflates worker skill and reliability into a single channel parameter, obscuring important distinctions (skill vs. attention/carelessness).
- Unrealistic assumption that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers regardless of known ratings or heterogeneity.
- Channel models (MSC and SHC) are limited in realism and do not capture nuanced adversarial or informed malicious behaviors.
- Authors should better justify and discuss the realism and limitations of their worker/channel models and possible extensions.
- Numerical results are unclear: Figure 2 curves are insufficiently labeled and k=1 is not explicitly defined.
- Critical methodological issue: assumption of knowing P(B(X)) is only stated late and must be declared earlier with discussion of practical implications.
- Minor corrections needed: wrong figure references (Fig.1.a→1.b, 1.b→1.c) and inconsistent use/numbering of Theorem/Lemma/Corollary.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framing, rigorous analysis, and potential to inspire future work.
- Arguments against acceptance: oversimplified worker modeling, unrealistic query allocation, limited channel realism, unclear results, and problematic P(B(X)) assumption.
- Overall assessment: weak reject — interesting theoretical contributions but simplifying assumptions and realism issues limit practical relevance.
