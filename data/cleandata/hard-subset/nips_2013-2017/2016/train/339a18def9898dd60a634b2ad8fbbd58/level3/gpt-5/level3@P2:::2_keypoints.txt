- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem and derives asymptotic query-rate limits under two worker-state scenarios (SL-UK and SL-CS).  
- Models: labels B(X) iid finite-alphabet source, taskmaster as encoder, workers as parallel noisy channels (MSC/SHC), crowdsourcer as decoder.  
- Main results: lower bounds on minimal queries per item (Theorems 2 and 3); SL-UK uses averaged error E[ε], SL-CS uses expectation of per-state capacities.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyzes its error-reduction via invalid-response elimination.  
- For the spammer–hammer channel (SHC), derives a converse-style lower bound for kIC assuming an oracle decoder and compares it to the information-theoretic lower bound.  
- Novelty: JSCC framing and kIC perspective are new in this literature and complement algorithmic aggregation/graphical models.  
- Technical soundness: applying separation to mixtures of MSCs and channels with decoder side information is plausible but needs explicit justification and citations (stationarity/ergodicity, DMC with i.i.d. state).  
- Must clarify that non-adaptive queries and input-independent state preserve separation and cite precise theorems (e.g., Cover–Thomas, El Gamal–Kim).  
- kIC analysis is carefully derived for binary uniform labels but relies on an oracle decoder; the gap to information-theoretic limits and tightness need quantification.  
- Strong modeling assumptions (iid labels, random assignment, non-adaptive queries, worker errors independent of item content) limit applicability; instance-dependent difficulty and side information X should be discussed.  
- Need to discuss how channel state depending on X (breaking memoryless assumption) would change results or require compound/mismatched-channel analysis.  
- Missing nonasymptotic/finite-blocklength analysis — recommend citing/using Kostina & Verdú (2013) for practical approximations.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well organized and definitions (SL-UK/SL-CS) are clear.  
- Notation should be standardized (e.g., N vs M for classes/response alphabet) and mappings made explicit.  
- Rephrase potentially misleading statement in Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2 about “lower bounds improve as k and q grow”; clarify what is improving and consider plotting achievability to show tightness.  
- Fix minor issues: typos, incomplete references (publisher/years), and ensure consistent bibliographic style.  
- Specific suggestions: explicitly justify separation with channel state, discuss encoder/decoder side information X, provide finite-blocklength insights, give kIC achievability or empirical upper bounds, extend beyond N=2, and clarify pricing rule π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2.  
- Recommend adding experiments/simulations comparing kIC to 2IC with DS/spectral inference under SHC/MSC to show practical gains.  
- Pros: clear JSCC formulation, informative SL-UK vs SL-CS limits, novel kIC coding-theoretic angle, useful asymptotic baselines for budget/fidelity tradeoffs.  
- Cons: relies on asymptotic separation with limited state-side information discussion, oracle-based kIC converse without achievability, strong iid/non-adaptive assumptions, and some clarity/notation/reference problems.  
- Overall: promising and well-written first step toward information-theoretic crowdsourcing limits that would be strengthened by separation justification, assumption scope, finite-blocklength analysis, and kIC achievability evidence.
