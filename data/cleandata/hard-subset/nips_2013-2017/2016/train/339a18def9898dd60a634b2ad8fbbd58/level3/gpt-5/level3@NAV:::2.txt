This paper models crowdsourcing (CS) as joint source–channel coding (JSCC) with random channel state, treating labels as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as encoder, workers as parallel channel uses with random skill-level states, and the crowdsourcer as decoder. Using source–channel separation, the authors derive asymptotic limits on queries per item (Theorems 2 and 3) for two regimes: worker skill unknown to both encoder/decoder vs. known to the decoder. They also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a spammer–hammer model, providing a lower bound on achievable error and a pricing heuristic. The framing connects to prior NIPS work on inference/aggregation (Dawid–Skene EM and its variants, spectral/convex approaches, and budget-optimal assignment by Karger–Oh–Shah) by shifting focus from algorithm design to information-theoretic limits.

Quality: Theorems are plausible and clearly stated, and the kIC analysis offers interpretable insights. However, a major technical gap is the justification of separation with random channel state unknown to the encoder (and possibly known at the decoder). Please either prove or cite precise conditions under which separation holds in your setting (e.g., stationary memoryless channel with i.i.d. state; decoder side information), and align your capacity expressions accordingly. References such as Cover–Thomas (channels with receiver side information), El Gamal–Kim (channels with state), and Csiszár–Körner would help. Beyond asymptotics, including nonasymptotic converses would strengthen practical relevance; see Kostina & Verdú (2013) on finite-blocklength JSCC converses (also Polyanskiy–Poor–Verdú, 2010, for channel coding).

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Please clarify Sec. 4.2/Fig. 2: “lower bounds improve as k and q grow” is potentially misleading—your plotted lower bound decreases with larger k and q, indicating potential performance gains, not a tightening of the converse in the absence of an upper bound. Minor notational reminders relating N (labels) and M (response alphabet) would aid readability.

Modeling: The encoder/decoder are assumed to only see labels, ignoring original items as side information (e.g., features, difficulty), which is often crucial in CS. Please discuss implications and potential extensions (e.g., Wyner–Ziv-like source side information; adaptive/feedback schemes; state-dependent channels).

Originality and significance: Framing CS within JSCC with random state is novel for this community and provides a useful benchmark for query-complexity vs. fidelity. The kIC lower bound is a clean first step, though predicated on an oracle decoder.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framing with new asymptotic limits
- Useful comparison of SL-UK vs. SL-CS regimes
- Concrete analysis of kIC and pricing heuristic
- Well written and relevant to NIPS audiences

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing justification/citations for separation with random state
- No finite-blocklength converses or achievability constructions
- Modeling ignores item/feature side information and adaptivity
