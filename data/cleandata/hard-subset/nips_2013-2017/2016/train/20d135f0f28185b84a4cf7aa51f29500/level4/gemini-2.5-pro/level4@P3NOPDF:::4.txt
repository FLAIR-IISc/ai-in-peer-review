This paper proposes a stochastic gradient descent method integrated with Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The authors claim that this framework offers advantages across a variety of computer vision tasks, including classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. While the application to diverse problems is appreciated, the paper has several major weaknesses in its current form.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution. The proposed sMCL method appears to be a direct adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning framework presented in [8] for neural network-based models. While the motivation inherited from MCL is sound, it cannot be considered a novel contribution of this work. The paper is essentially an application of a pre-existing method to a new domain with some adjustments, which significantly limits its impact.

2.  **Insufficient Technical Detail and Clarity:** The paper is not well-written and lacks the necessary technical depth. The core technical description of the proposed method in Section 3 is confined to a single page, which is insufficient for readers to fully grasp the implementation details and theoretical underpinnings. Furthermore, the authors fail to adequately differentiate their framework from established multi-task or alternative training strategies. It is unclear how the proposed method compares to or improves upon these widely adopted techniques for jointly training on multiple objectives. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true contribution and positioning of the work.

3.  **Weak Experimental Validation:** The empirical evidence provided is not strong enough to support the authors' claims. Although experiments are conducted on several computer vision tasks, they suffer from two key issues:
    *   **Lack of Strong Baselines:** The baselines chosen for comparison are not state-of-the-art or are otherwise weak, making it difficult to gauge the true effectiveness of the sMCL framework.
    *   **Marginal Performance Gains:** The reported improvements over the selected baselines are small and may not be statistically significant.

To build a more convincing case for their framework's efficacy, the authors should conduct a more rigorous experimental evaluation. For instance, a comparison on a standard benchmark like the PASCAL VOC 2012 segmentation challenge against stronger, well-established baselines would provide a much clearer picture of the method's practical benefits.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, while the idea of applying MCL to deep learning is interesting, this paper falls short due to its limited novelty, lack of technical depth, and unconvincing experimental results. The contribution is incremental, and the claims of superiority are not well-substantiated. I recommend rejecting the paper in its current state but would be open to reconsidering a substantially revised version that addresses the aforementioned issues, particularly with a more thorough experimental comparison against strong, relevant baselines.
