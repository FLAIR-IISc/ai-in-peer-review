This paper proposes an ensemble training algorithm for deep models in which, for each training example, only a single ensemble member is updated: the one with the lowest loss with respect to the true label. This winner-takes-update scheme is simple, computationally efficient per example, and yields strong empirical results. The approach addresses limitations of conventional deep and classical ensembles, and the experiments are surprisingly compelling. I recommend a weak accept.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive training rule: select the lowest-loss member per example and update only that model.
- Strong empirical performance and apparent robustness relative to standard ensemble training.
- Conceptually simple, with minimal overhead compared to training multiple models independently.

Questions and concerns
1) Test-time procedure is unclear. Line 159 on page 5 appears to imply selecting the “best” member using ground-truth information, which is not available at inference. Please clarify precisely how predictions are produced at test time (e.g., selection rule, aggregation method, and computational cost), and ensure this matches what was used in reported results.

2) Baseline definitions need clarification. The figures and text compare against “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (Indp.; see line 181 on page 5), but I could not find a precise description of these baselines. Please specify:
- How each baseline is trained (data splits, seeds, optimization settings).
- Whether parameters, training budgets, and model counts are matched to your method.
- The inference rule used for each baseline (averaging, majority vote, etc.).

3) Specialization claims require stronger evidence or explicit modeling. The paper frequently attributes class-wise specialization to ensemble members, yet the reassignment mechanism operates per example, not per class. Please provide quantitative analyses supporting specialization (e.g., class distribution per member, member-wise confusion matrices, entropy/calibration of per-member class assignments). Additionally, consider incorporating specialization or diversity directly into the objective so the claim is grounded in the loss, not only in post hoc observations.

4) Related work on diversity regularization is under-discussed. Prior work (e.g., Chen et al.) formalizes diversity among predictors via explicit penalties. This seems closely related to your motivation. Please discuss similarities and differences, whether your per-example selection implicitly promotes diversity, and how your method compares conceptually and empirically to diversity-regularized training in neural networks. Even if such methods are less explored in deep settings, a careful positioning would strengthen the paper.

Overall assessment
A neat and practical idea with promising results. Addressing the test-time procedure, clarifying baselines, substantiating specialization, and situating the method within diversity-regularization literature would significantly improve clarity and impact. With these revisions, I believe the contribution would be solid. Weak accept.
