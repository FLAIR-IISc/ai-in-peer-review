

The paper proposes a method for generating multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize the oracle loss, which is particularly useful in interactive settings. The authors integrate deep networks into the MCL framework and introduce a stochastic block-coordinate descent optimization approach to minimize the oracle loss. This method can be seamlessly implemented within standard backpropagation and enables end-to-end training of the entire model. The oracle experiments across three tasks demonstrate the method's effectiveness.  

However, the paper lacks sufficient discussion on initialization sensitivity. Specifically, it is unclear how critical the choice of initialization is. What performance degradation occurs if models are initialized randomly rather than using a pre-trained network? The authors do not clarify whether baseline methods used the same pre-trained network as MCL for initialization. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements stem from the proposed optimization or merely from a shared initialization strategy.  

Additionally, the evaluation relies solely on oracle accuracy as the primary metric. While this metric is well-motivated, the paper does not analyze the diversity of the ensemble members—a critical factor for multi-output methods. The authors should provide deeper analysis on how the proposed approach balances accuracy and diversity, such as through pairwise disagreement metrics or coverage analysis. Without this, it remains unclear whether the ensemble genuinely produces complementary predictions or simply duplicates similar outputs.  

Finally, the authors should compare their work against a closely related prior study: Stefan Lee et al.'s "Why M Heads Are Better Than One" (CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015), which also explores training diverse deep network ensembles. While the technical approaches differ (Lee et al. use multi-head architectures with diversity regularization, whereas this paper uses block-coordinate optimization), the core objective of improving ensemble diversity through joint training is highly analogous. A comparison with this work would strengthen the paper's positioning and clarify its novelty.  

Overall, the method is well-motivated and technically sound, but the missing analysis of initialization sensitivity, ensemble diversity, and relevant prior work weakens the contribution. Addressing these points would significantly improve the paper's impact.
