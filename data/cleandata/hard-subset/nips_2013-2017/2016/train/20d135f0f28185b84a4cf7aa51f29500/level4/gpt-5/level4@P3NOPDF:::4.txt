Summary
The paper proposes a stochastic variant of Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) for training neural networks, aiming to produce diverse predictors whose best output among M choices achieves improved performance. The authors claim broad applicability across classification, segmentation, and captioning.

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation for multiple-choice predictions and diversity in model outputs.
- Simple, easy-to-implement training scheme that could be practical across tasks.
- Empirical evaluation spans several computer vision problems, suggesting generality.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The method appears to be a neural-network instantiation of MCL (e.g., [8]) with relatively minor adjustments to the optimization procedure. The paper does not convincingly articulate what is fundamentally new in the objective or algorithm beyond prior MCL formulations.
- Insufficient technical detail: The core method (Section 3) is only about one page, which is inadequate. Crucial aspects—objective, optimization details, stability considerations, and theoretical grounding—are either missing or too terse.
- Missing strong and appropriate baselines: Comparisons to standard alternatives (e.g., training multiple heads jointly with averaged loss, alternating training schemes, mixture-of-experts with gating, and standard ensembles) are absent or weak. Reported gains over the provided baselines are small.
- Incomplete evaluation: Results are given across several tasks but do not include widely recognized strong benchmarks and comparisons. For segmentation in particular, evaluation on PASCAL VOC 2012 with competitive baselines is expected.
- Lack of ablations and analysis: No systematic study of the number of choices M, assignment strategy (hard vs. soft), diversity metrics, parameter sharing, or initialization sensitivity. Computational overhead and convergence behavior are not discussed.
- Writing and organization: The paper is difficult to follow in places, with grammatical issues and insufficient detail in the technical section.

Detailed comments and questions
- Novelty and relation to prior work:
  - Please make explicit how sMCL differs from [8]. Is the objective function the same with a different optimizer, or is there a substantively different formulation? A formal statement and comparison would help.
  - Situate the approach relative to mixture-of-experts (with learned gating), standard ensembles, diversity-promoting ensemble methods (e.g., negative correlation learning), and recent multi-head architectures.

- Algorithmic clarity:
  - Precisely define the loss and assignment rule: For each sample or mini-batch, do you backprop only through the head with the lowest loss? How are ties handled?
  - Discuss whether you use hard assignments (argmin) or a soft weighting (e.g., softmin). If hard, what steps are taken to avoid instability and head collapse?
  - Describe any regularization to encourage diversity or load balancing among heads.
  - Provide pseudocode and complexity analysis, including computational and memory costs versus (i) M independent models and (ii) a shared trunk with M heads trained on the averaged loss.

- Baselines and comparisons:
  - Include strong baselines: (a) multi-head network trained with an averaged loss across heads, (b) round-robin/alternative training across heads, (c) standard ensembles of independently trained networks with the same total parameter budget, and (d) mixture-of-experts with a gating network.
  - For each task, compare against widely used competitive methods. For segmentation, evaluate on PASCAL VOC 2012 and compare to standard baselines; for captioning, include COCO metrics (e.g., CIDEr); for classification, include CIFAR-100 or ImageNet with standard architectures.

- Evaluation protocol:
  - Clarify whether reported performance is oracle (best-of-M) or includes a practical selection mechanism at test time. If the oracle is used, also report performance with a learned or heuristic selector to demonstrate usefulness without an oracle.
  - Provide ablations varying M, capacity per head, degree of parameter sharing, and initialization seeds. Report diversity metrics among heads and how they correlate with oracle and non-oracle gains.
  - Report statistical significance and confidence intervals where feasible.

- Practical aspects:
  - Discuss convergence properties, sensitivity to hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size), and runtime overhead.
  - Analyze failure modes: when do heads collapse to similar solutions, and how does the method mitigate this?

- Writing and presentation:
  - Expand and clarify Section 3 with full derivations and algorithmic details.
  - Improve grammar and organization; add a concise problem statement, clear notation, and a limitations subsection.
  - Strengthen the related work section to more completely cover MCL variants, ensemble diversity methods, and mixture-of-experts.

Overall assessment
The paper’s motivation is sound and the idea is practically appealing, but the technical contribution appears incremental relative to prior MCL work, and the current exposition and empirical evaluation are not yet at the standard expected for NIPS. Substantially stronger baselines, clearer methodological distinctions from [8] and related approaches, and more thorough analyses are necessary to establish significance.

Recommendation: Reject (weak reject), pending major revisions to clarify novelty and strengthen experiments.

Confidence: Medium.
