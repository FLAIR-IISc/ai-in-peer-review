- Proposal: sMCL extends Multiple Choice Learning to deep networks via an SGD-based, parameter-free, architecture-agnostic training that minimizes oracle loss and trains ensemble members concurrently.  
- Empirical results: Demonstrated across classification, segmentation, and captioning with consistent oracle-metric improvements over independent ensembles, original MCL, and Dey et al.  
- Practical significance: Addresses real-world need for multiple hypotheses (e.g., autonomous vehicles, accessibility), with clear motivating examples.  
- Technical simplicity: Winner-take-gradient mechanism is simple to implement and requires no architectural changes.  
- Interpretable specialization: Ensemble members naturally specialize (class- or segmentation-specific), providing useful insights into model behavior.  
- Limited novelty: Core idea closely builds on MCL [8]; claim of being first to formalize for deep networks is overstated.  
- Insufficient technical depth: Section 3 is brief and lacks detail on differences from standard multi-task learning and theoretical guarantees vs alternating optimization.  
- Modest gains: Improvements (6–10%) are consistent but relatively small compared to state-of-the-art in these domains.  
- Incomplete baselines: Missing comparisons to stronger modern methods (e.g., recent ensemble/diversity techniques and SOTA segmentation on PASCAL VOC 2012).  
- Clarity/reproducibility: Paper is well written overall but lacks sufficient technical detail for replication and should explicitly contrast with multi-head multi-task approaches.  
- Originality assessment: Practical adaptation to deep learning is useful but not highly novel; SGD-based optimization is the primary new element but under-justified.  
- Significance assessment: Problem and applicability are important, but limited novelty and modest improvements reduce overall impact.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on clarifying technical details, comparing to stronger baselines, and better situating the work relative to prior multi-task and ensemble methods.
