

This paper addresses Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep ensembles, where the goal is to train multiple predictors whose collective outputs minimize the oracle loss (i.e., the loss of the best prediction in the set). The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based method that trains ensemble members concurrently by backpropagating gradients only to the lowest-error predictor per example during each batch update. This avoids the computationally expensive retraining required by prior MCL approaches [8, 9], while achieving state-of-the-art oracle performance across diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning). The work generalizes Guzman-Rivera et al.’s MCL framework to deep networks, demonstrating that specialization emerges naturally under oracle loss minimization without explicit diversity constraints.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical approach is sound, with a clear derivation of the stochastic optimization strategy (Eq. 2) and rigorous empirical validation. The method is parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and significantly faster than MCL (5× speedup), as shown in thorough experiments across three distinct domains. The analysis of emergent specialization (e.g., Figs. 4c, 5b, 7) provides strong evidence that the approach achieves the stated goal of diverse, high-quality outputs.  
*Originality:* While MCL [8, 9] established the paradigm, this is the first practical method for training deep ensembles under oracle loss. The sMCL algorithm is novel in its simplicity (no retraining, no explicit diversity terms) and directly addresses a key gap in prior work for deep learning. The paper adequately distinguishes itself from ensemble diversity literature [2, 21, 27] and multiple-solution generation [3, 5, 15, 23, 24].  
*Significance:* The problem is highly relevant to real-world perception systems (e.g., autonomous vehicles, assistive tools), where providing multiple hypotheses improves robustness to ambiguity. The method’s broad applicability (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs) and consistent 6–10% oracle accuracy gains over baselines (Fig. 5a, Table 1) demonstrate tangible impact. The interpretable specialization (e.g., "segmentation specialists" in Fig. 6) offers new insights into ensemble behavior.  
*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation (Sec. 1), formalization (Sec. 3), and experimental analysis (Sec. 4) are logically structured. Key terms (e.g., "oracle loss," "emergent specialization") are consistently defined. Minor phrasing issues (e.g., "of enough importance" → "of sufficient importance") could be refined, but the core exposition is strong.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Originality:* The title and abstract overstate "diversity" as a primary contribution, while the method achieves diversity *implicitly* via oracle loss minimization (not through explicit diversity terms or constraints). The paper should clarify that diversity is a *byproduct* of specialization under the oracle objective, not an explicit design goal. This misalignment risks misleading readers about the method’s novelty.  
*Quality:* The comparison to MCL [8] requires more detail. The review notes that MCL’s performance drops on segmentation/captioning due to "infeasible retraining," but the paper does not specify how the authors ensured MCL ensemble members converged reasonably (e.g., number of meta-iterations, convergence criteria). This undermines the fairness of the comparison.  
*Clarity:* The abstract’s first sentence is grammatically awkward ("Many practical perception systems exist within larger processes that include interactions..."). Rewording to "Practical perception systems often operate within larger workflows containing components capable of evaluating predicted solutions" would improve fluency. The phrase "diversity" should be tempered throughout (e.g., "diverse outputs" → "outputs with emergent diversity").

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept.** The paper makes a strong contribution by enabling practical MCL for deep networks via a simple, efficient algorithm. The core technical idea is novel, well-validated, and broadly applicable. While the "diversity" emphasis needs refinement, the method’s effectiveness and clarity outweigh this limitation. The work advances the state of the art in ensemble learning for ambiguous prediction tasks and aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, rigorous machine learning research. The minor clarity issues are easily addressed in revision.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Simple, efficient, architecture-agnostic; consistent oracle performance gains (6–10%) across 3 tasks; emergent specialization demonstrated; addresses a practical gap in real-world perception systems.  
*Con:* Overstates "diversity" as a design goal (it is emergent, not explicit); MCL comparison lacks convergence details; title/abstract overemphasize "diversity."
