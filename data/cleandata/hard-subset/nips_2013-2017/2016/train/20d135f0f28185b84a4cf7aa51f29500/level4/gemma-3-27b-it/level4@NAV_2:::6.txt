The paper introduces a novel approach, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), for training diverse deep ensembles. The central idea is to frame ensemble learning as a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem and to address the resulting optimization challenge with a stochastic gradient descent-based method. Unlike previous MCL approaches and traditional ensemble techniques, sMCL updates only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each training example, leading to a computationally efficient and effective training procedure. The method is broadly applicable, being agnostic to network architecture and loss function, and requires no additional hyperparameters. The authors demonstrate strong performance gains across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, outperforming baseline ensembles and related methods like MCL and Dey et al. The analysis reveals an interesting emergent behavior where ensemble members develop specialized expertise, aligning with the intuitive notion of diverse solutions.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical need for diverse predictions in real-world applications where oracle feedback is available. The proposed algorithm is conceptually simple yet technically sound, and the experimental results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines on a variety of tasks. The inclusion of qualitative examples further strengthens the analysis, illustrating the benefits of diversity in terms of interpretable representations of task ambiguity. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and discuss potential future directions.

However, a few points could be clarified. The testing phase isn't fully detailed; it would be helpful to explicitly state how predictions are generated during inference (e.g., selecting the best member per input, averaging outputs). The meaning of “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (denoted as ‘Indp.’ in figures) is unclear and requires more explicit definition. While the authors argue for specialization based on per-example reassignment, the link between this process and true specialization isn’t fully established, and exploring incorporating explicit diversity regularization into the loss function might strengthen this claim. Finally, related work on diversity regularization techniques in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.) is not discussed, and a comparison to these approaches would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and novel approach to ensemble learning.
*   Strong empirical results across diverse tasks and architectures.
*   Computational efficiency compared to existing MCL methods.
*   Emergent specialization of ensemble members is a valuable observation.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity regarding the testing phase.
*   Unclear definition of “independent” and “regular” ensembles.
*   Insufficient justification for the claim of specialization.
*   Missing discussion of related work on diversity regularization.

**Overall:**

Despite minor areas for improvement, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm offers a practical and effective solution for training diverse deep ensembles, and the experimental results demonstrate its broad applicability and efficacy. I recommend weak acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above.
