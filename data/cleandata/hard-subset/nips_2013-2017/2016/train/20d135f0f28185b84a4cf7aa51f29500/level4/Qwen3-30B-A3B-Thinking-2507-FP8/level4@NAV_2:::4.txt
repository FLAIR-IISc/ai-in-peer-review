

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss through a stochastic gradient descent approach. The authors demonstrate its application across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

Quality: While technically sound, the contribution appears incremental rather than fundamentally novel. The method is an adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning framework from Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] to deep networks, with minimal theoretical innovation. The approach essentially extends [8] with a stochastic optimization strategy rather than introducing a substantially new methodology. The technical section (Section 3) is too brief (only one page) to adequately explain how sMCL differs from standard ensemble training approaches where multiple networks are trained jointly to minimize a combined loss. The paper does not sufficiently contrast sMCL with existing techniques for training multiple models simultaneously.

Clarity: The paper suffers from poor organization and insufficient technical detail. Section 3, which contains the core methodological contribution, is too concise to fully explain the proposed approach and its distinction from alternative training strategies. The authors do not clearly articulate how sMCL differs from standard ensemble training where multiple networks are trained in parallel on the same data. The writing is often vague, making it difficult to assess the novelty and technical contributions.

Originality: The main contribution appears to be applying an existing Multiple Choice Learning framework [8] to deep networks rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. While the authors claim to be the first to formalize MCL for deep networks, the core algorithm is an adaptation of prior work rather than a novel contribution. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from the prior work in [8] or other ensemble learning methods.

Significance: The performance improvements demonstrated are marginal (6-10% relative improvements over baselines), and the experimental results lack strong baselines for comparison. The paper does not compare against recent, strong baselines for image segmentation (e.g., on PASCAL VOC 2012) that would better demonstrate the significance of the proposed approach. The claim that sMCL "significantly outperforms" baselines is not strongly supported by the current experimental setup, particularly as the improvements are small and the baselines are not state-of-the-art for the tasks. The paper would benefit from more extensive comparisons with recent strong baselines to better establish the significance of the approach.
