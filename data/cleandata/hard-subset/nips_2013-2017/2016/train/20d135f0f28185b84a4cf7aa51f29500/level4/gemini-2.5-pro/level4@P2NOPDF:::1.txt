This paper introduces a stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training ensemble models. The authors build upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which defines the ensemble loss as the loss of the single best-performing classifier within the ensemble for a given example. This approach is designed to yield a diverse set of classifiers. The central contribution of this work is the adaptation of this framework to stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which critically enables its application to large-scale problems and deep neural networks.

The paper is interesting and clearly written. The motivation is well-established, and the proposed method is a logical extension of prior art.

My primary initial concern was whether the novelty of the algorithm was merely incremental over the work presented in [8]. The core conceptual change—substituting a batch optimization method with a stochastic one—can be seen as a straightforward adaptation. However, the authors successfully argue that this modification has significant practical implications. By enabling mini-batch training, their method transforms the ensemble technique from one limited to smaller datasets into a scalable algorithm suitable for the current deep learning landscape. This enhancement of applicability and utility is a valuable contribution. Given this, I am in favor of accepting the paper.

For potential improvements, I would suggest the following:

1.  **Ablation Study on Batch MCL:** An interesting and valuable comparison would be to evaluate the original Mixture of Conditional Likelihoods (MCL) algorithm from [8] when trained with mini-batches instead of the entire dataset. This would create a more direct baseline and help to precisely isolate the benefits derived from the authors' proposed stochastic optimization scheme, as opposed to the benefits that simply come from using mini-batches.

2.  **Deeper Analysis of Diversity:** The paper posits that the chosen loss function promotes diversity among the ensemble members, a key motivation inherited from [8]. While the empirical results support the method's effectiveness, the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit analysis of this diversity effect. The authors could include quantitative metrics (e.g., disagreement rates between classifiers, Q-statistics) or qualitative analyses (e.g., visualizing learned features or decision boundaries) to concretely demonstrate that the individual models in the ensemble are learning distinct and complementary representations. This would provide stronger evidence for the underlying mechanism behind the method's success.
