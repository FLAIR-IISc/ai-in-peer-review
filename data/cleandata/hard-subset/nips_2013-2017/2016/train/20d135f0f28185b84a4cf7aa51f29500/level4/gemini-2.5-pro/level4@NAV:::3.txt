This work introduces a straightforward approach to the multiple-choice learning (MCL) problem for deep neural networks. The authors correctly note that existing MCL frameworks do not easily translate to deep learning settings. They demonstrate that a "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer implemented during backpropagation effectively unifies the training of individual learners with the data assignment process in an ensemble context. The method's efficacy is validated with strong experimental results across a diverse set of applications: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses the important and timely problem of adapting multiple-choice learning for deep models, a topic of significant interest to the NeurIPS community.
- The proposed method is commendable for its simplicity and elegance.
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, with positive results demonstrated across three distinct and challenging tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The primary concern is the significant disconnect between the paper's motivation and its technical execution. The concept of "diversity" is heavily emphasized throughout the manuscript, including in the title, yet the model itself does not contain any explicit mechanism to enforce or promote it. This creates a mismatch of expectations, as the reader is led to anticipate a novel diversity-promoting component that is ultimately absent from the method.
- The contribution appears to be an incremental step when viewed in the context of the relaxation technique proposed by Guzman et al.

**Minor Suggestions:**
- The first sentence of the abstract should be revised for clarity.
- The emphasis on "diversity" should be toned down throughout the paper to better reflect the method's actual contribution.
- Line 108: In the expression "we fixed the form of...", the first "f" appears to be a typo and should be "g".
- Line 115: There is an extraneous period in the middle of the sentence.

**One Question:**
- For the deep learning MCL baseline, what procedure was followed to ensure that each individual network had converged to a satisfactory state? The ensemble's performance could be substantially impacted if the training of individual learners is terminated prematurely, which could unfairly favor the proposed method.
