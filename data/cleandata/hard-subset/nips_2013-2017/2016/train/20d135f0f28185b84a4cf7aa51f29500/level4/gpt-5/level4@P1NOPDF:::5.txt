This paper proposes a method for generating multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize the oracle loss, which is particularly useful in interactive settings. The authors integrate deep networks into the MCL paradigm and introduce a stochastic block gradient descent procedure to optimize the MCL oracle loss. The approach is straightforward to implement within standard backpropagation and supports end-to-end training. Oracle-based experiments on three tasks provide evidence of effectiveness.

While the method is appealing, I have several concerns about experimental setup and analysis:

- Sensitivity to initialization: How critical is the choice of initialization? What happens if the ensemble members are initialized randomly rather than from a pre-trained network? The paper should quantify performance differences under these regimes, as the reliance on strong initialization could limit practicality or inflate gains.

- Fairness and clarity of comparisons: The paper does not sufficiently describe the initialization and training settings used for baselines. Do the baseline methods use the same pre-trained backbone used for sMCL, with identical fine-tuning schedules and data augmentations? If not, the comparisons may be confounded. Please provide a clear, controlled protocol for all methods.

- Evaluation metrics beyond oracle accuracy: The paper focuses solely on oracle accuracy. However, the value of an ensemble under an oracle hinges on diversity among members. I would like to see a more thorough analysis of diversity (e.g., pairwise disagreement, error overlap, or other standard diversity metrics), and its relationship to oracle performance. Reporting the trade-off between per-model accuracy and diversity would also strengthen the claims.

- Related work: There is closely related prior work that should be discussed and compared against, both conceptually and empirically: Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks,” CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015). Please position your contributions relative to this approach and, if possible, include a direct comparison under matched settings.

Overall, the paper presents a simple and practical optimization scheme for MCL with deep networks and shows encouraging oracle results on multiple tasks. Addressing the concerns about initialization, ensuring fair comparisons, and providing a principled analysis of diversity would significantly improve the rigor and clarity of the empirical evaluation.
