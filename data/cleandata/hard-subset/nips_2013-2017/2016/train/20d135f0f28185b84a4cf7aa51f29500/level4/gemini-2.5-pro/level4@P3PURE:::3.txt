This paper addresses the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) with deep network learners, noting that existing MCL solutions do not readily extend to this setting. The authors propose a simple yet effective solution: a 'winner-takes-the-gradient' layer that allows for the joint training of the individual learners and the assignment of examples within the ensemble via backpropagation. The efficacy of this method is demonstrated through experimental results across a diverse range of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and image captioning.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem of adapting Multiple Choice Learning for deep learning is highly relevant and of significant interest to the NIPS community.
*   The proposed method is appealing in its simplicity.
*   The experimental validation is thorough, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on three distinct and challenging tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   My primary concern is the significant disconnect between the paper's motivation and the proposed model. The paper, including its title, extensively emphasizes the importance of "diversity" among the learners. This sets an expectation that the model will explicitly enforce or encourage diversity. However, the proposed method does not contain any explicit mechanism for this, which is a notable omission.
*   The proposed solution appears to be an incremental contribution over the relaxation method proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   The first sentence of the abstract is unclear and should be rewritten.
*   Given the lack of an explicit diversity mechanism, the claims regarding "diversity" throughout the paper should be toned down to more accurately reflect the method's contributions.
*   Line 108: In "we fixed the form of f...", the first 'f' should likely be 'g'.
*   Line 115: There is a superfluous period in the middle of the sentence.

**Question:**

*   Regarding the baseline MCL implementation for deep networks: What measures were taken to ensure that each individual network had converged to a reasonable solution before evaluating the ensemble? Terminating the training of the learners prematurely could significantly and unfairly handicap the baseline's performance, so clarification on the convergence criteria is needed.
