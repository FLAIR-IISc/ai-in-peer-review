The paper proposes an ensemble training algorithm for deep networks. Its key novelty is that, for each training example, only a single ensemble member is active—unlike prior deep or classical ensembles. During training, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is selected and updated via its gradient. The idea is simple yet yields surprisingly strong empirical results and appears to address some shortcomings of existing methods. I therefore recommend a weak accept.

1. The test-time procedure is not clearly described. From line 159 on page 5, I suspect you select the best member using ground-truth labels. Please clarify how inference is performed without access to ground truth at test time.

2. The figures and text refer to “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (abbreviated “Indp.” in the figures and at line 181 on page 5). I could not find a clear description of this baseline; please define it explicitly.

3. The paper repeatedly claims that ensemble members “specialize” to different output classes. However, the reassignment during training is done on a per-example basis, which does not directly encourage class-level specialization. This claim is insufficiently justified. Can you incorporate specialization explicitly into the loss (e.g., through class-based assignment or regularization) to substantiate this argument?

4. Relatedly, the paper does not discuss closely related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.). This line of work is more rigorous in formalizing diversity, though it has not yet been extensively examined in deep settings. Please compare and contrast with these approaches.
