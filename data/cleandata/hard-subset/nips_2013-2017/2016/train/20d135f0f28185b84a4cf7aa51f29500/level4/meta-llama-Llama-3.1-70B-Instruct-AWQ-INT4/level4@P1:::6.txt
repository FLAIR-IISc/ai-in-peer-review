This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach for training diverse deep ensembles, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The method is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on various tasks and deep architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve. The proposed method is intuitive and easy to understand, and the experimental results are impressive. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of their method and compare it to existing baselines.

However, there are a few areas that need clarification and improvement. Firstly, the testing phase is not clearly explained. It is assumed that the authors use the ground truth to find the best member of the ensemble, but this needs to be explicitly stated. Secondly, the authors repeatedly refer to 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' (denoted as 'Indp.' in figures) without providing a clear explanation of what these baselines entail. A brief description of these baselines would be helpful.

Furthermore, the authors' argument about the specialization of ensemble members towards different output classes is not entirely convincing. The reassignment of examples to ensemble members during training is done on a per-example basis, which makes it unclear how this leads to specialization. It would be beneficial to incorporate specialization into the loss function to provide a more rigorous justification for this claim.

Additionally, the authors do not discuss the related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, which is a more rigorous approach to promoting diversity in ensembles. While the authors' method is more practical and efficient, a discussion of the trade-offs between these approaches would be valuable.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is promising. With some clarifications and improvements, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of deep learning.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve and propose a novel solution.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the language is clear and concise. However, there are a few areas that need clarification, as mentioned above.

Originality: The proposed method is novel and unique in that it only updates the ensemble member with the lowest loss during training. This approach is different from existing deep and classical ensembles.

Significance: The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, particularly in the area of ensemble methods. The proposed method is simple to implement and can be applied to various tasks and architectures.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and unique approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results are impressive, and the method outperforms existing baselines.
* The paper is well-written, and the language is clear and concise.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The testing phase is not clearly explained.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of the 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' baselines.
* The argument about specialization of ensemble members is not entirely convincing.
* The authors do not discuss the related work on diversity regularization in neural networks.
