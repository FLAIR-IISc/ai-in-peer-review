This paper presents a straightforward approach to addressing the multiple choice learning problem in deep networks, a challenge that existing solutions do not readily accommodate. By incorporating a winner-takes-the-gradient layer during back propagation, the authors effectively merge the training of individual learners with the assignment problem in an ensemble setting. Experimental evaluations demonstrate the method's efficacy across a diverse range of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

Strengths:
- The problem of multiple choice learning in deep learning is a significant concern relevant to the NIPS community.
- The proposed method's simplicity is a notable advantage.
- The experiments provide results on three distinct tasks, showcasing the approach's versatility.

Weaknesses:
- A major concern is that despite emphasizing "diversity" throughout the paper, including in the title, the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. This omission is disappointing, as it was expected that the authors would incorporate a diversity term into their model.
- The proposed solution can be seen as an incremental improvement building upon the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract requires rephrasing for clarity.
- The emphasis on "diversity" should be toned down to align with the method's actual capabilities.
- In line 108, "f" should be corrected to "g" in the phrase "we fixed the form of."
- An extraneous period in the middle of a sentence in line 115 should be removed.

One question remains: How did the authors ensure that each network in the baseline MCL with deep learning converged to reasonable results? Prematurely terminating the learners could significantly impact the ensemble's performance.
