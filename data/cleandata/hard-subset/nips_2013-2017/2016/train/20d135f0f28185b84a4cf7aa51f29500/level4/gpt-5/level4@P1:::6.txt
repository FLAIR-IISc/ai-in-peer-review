Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based algorithm for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. For each training example in a minibatch, the method computes the loss under each ensemble member and backpropagates only through the member with the lowest loss (“winner-take-gradient”). This provides a practical alternative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches that require repeated retraining or sequential addition of models. The authors evaluate on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM), and report consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles and recent diversity/boosting-style methods, while being much faster than classical MCL.

The work is closely related to: (i) MCL and related oracle-loss formulations for structured prediction (Guzman-Rivera et al.); (ii) approaches for generating multiple diverse solutions (M-best/diverse in MRFs, diverse N-best decoding); and (iii) ensemble diversity techniques (e.g., resampling, negative correlation learning, and diversity regularization). The main novelty is an SGD-friendly, parameter-free training procedure that makes MCL practical for modern deep networks.

Strengths:
- Simple, elegant training rule that is architecture- and loss-agnostic, and easy to integrate into existing SGD pipelines.
- Significant oracle performance gains across three distinct tasks and model families, with clear efficiency advantages over alternating/retraining-based MCL.
- Empirical analyses suggest interpretable specialization emerges among ensemble members.
- Parameter-free and concurrent training are practical advantages in deep learning settings where repeated retraining is prohibitively costly.

Weaknesses and questions:
1) Test-time procedure and evaluation clarity: The testing phase is not clearly explained. From line 159 (p.5), I infer that the best member is selected using ground truth during evaluation (oracle metrics). Please clarify explicitly:
- At test time, do you output all M predictions and compute oracle metrics using ground truth solely for evaluation?
- How is the method intended to be used in deployment when no oracle is available? Is there any selection or re-ranking module, or do you envision integration with external verification components?
- It would be helpful to report non-oracle metrics (e.g., single-prediction accuracy/IoU/caption scores after a simple selection heuristic) to contextualize the practical impact.

2) Baseline specification (“independent”/“regular” ensembles): The manuscript refers to “independent” or “regular” ensembles (denoted “Indp.”) but I could not find a precise definition. Please detail:
- How these models are trained (identical architectures/hyperparameters, random seeds only).
- Whether any data resampling or augmentation differs across members.
- For captioning, whether the CNN is shared/frozen across ensemble members (as memory constraints forced in sMCL) and how this affects fairness.

3) Specialization claims vs. training signal: The paper repeatedly emphasizes specialization of ensemble members to different classes or modes, yet the training rule assigns the gradient on a per-example basis. This does not by itself enforce class- or mode-level specialization. The qualitative and assignment histograms are suggestive but not conclusive. Consider:
- Providing stronger quantitative evidence (e.g., mutual information between assignments and class labels; concentration of assignments per member; per-class confusion and coverage).
- Incorporating specialization/diversity directly into the objective, e.g., via a regularizer that encourages orthogonality/low correlation of predictions, encourages high-entropy coverage across members, or constrains per-class coverage. This could also help align the empirical “emergent specialization” with an explicit design principle.

4) Related work on diversity regularization in neural networks: The paper does not discuss closely related approaches that explicitly regularize ensemble diversity in neural nets (e.g., diversity regularization by Chen et al.; negative correlation learning). These techniques provide a more principled treatment of diversity and error decorrelation, though they are less explored at deep scale. Please include and contrast these methods conceptually and, if feasible, empirically.

Additional suggestions:
- Fairness to MCL baseline: Results are shown after only five meta-iterations; given MCL’s known convergence behavior, please show convergence curves or justify that more iterations are infeasible and unlikely to close the gap.
- Stability and sensitivity: How sensitive is sMCL to initialization, tie-breaking, batch size, and ensemble size M? Assignments can be brittle early in training—do you use any warm-up or smoothing (e.g., k-winners, soft assignments)? A brief ablation (your k-winner variant) is mentioned; more systematic analysis would strengthen the case.
- Captioning setup: Because the CNN is shared/frozen for memory reasons, please discuss how this constraint affects diversity and whether fine-tuning jointly through the shared CNN biases members toward similar features.
- Computational cost: Report absolute training times for all methods, and per-epoch overhead of computing and routing per-example losses.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The core idea is sound and empirically supported on three tasks using oracle metrics. Some methodological details (test-time procedure, baselines) need clarification, and additional analysis would better substantiate specialization claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but the testing/evaluation protocol and baseline definitions should be made explicit for reproducibility.
- Originality: While grounded in prior MCL work, the winner-take-gradient formulation for deep SGD training is novel and practical.
- Significance: High. A drop-in method to train diverse deep ensembles with consistent oracle gains is valuable and likely to be adopted.

Pros:
- Practical, parameter-free, and easy to implement.
- Strong oracle performance across diverse tasks and architectures.
- Concurrent training with substantial speedup over MCL.
- Insightful qualitative analyses of diversity.

Cons:
- Ambiguity around test-time/oracle evaluation and deployment.
- Incomplete description of baselines.
- Specialization claims exceed the direct implications of the loss; missing diversity-regularization comparison.
- Limited analysis of stability/sensitivity and fairness for MCL baseline.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is simple and impactful with solid empirical gains. Addressing the clarity issues, adding related work on diversity regularization, and strengthening the specialization analysis would further improve the paper.
