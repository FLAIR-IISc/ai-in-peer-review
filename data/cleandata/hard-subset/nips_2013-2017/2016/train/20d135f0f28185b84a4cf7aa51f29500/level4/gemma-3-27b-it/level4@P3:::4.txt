This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by framing the problem as Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and employing a stochastic gradient descent approach. The core idea is to minimize oracle loss, where an oracle selects the best prediction from an ensemble of networks. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, reporting improvements over baseline ensemble methods.

The motivation for exploring MCL is well-established – providing multiple hypotheses is beneficial in real-world applications where downstream processes or human operators evaluate solutions. However, the novelty of this work is incremental. While the authors correctly position sMCL as an adaptation of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] to deep networks, the core contribution lies in the efficient implementation rather than a fundamentally new approach to MCL. The method’s advantage – avoiding the costly retraining required by the original MCL formulation – is significant, but not a breakthrough in conceptual terms. 

The paper’s clarity could be improved. While the introduction clearly articulates the problem, Section 3, which details the technical contribution of sMCL, is relatively brief. The explanation of how sMCL differs from, and improves upon, standard multi-task learning or other ensemble training strategies (e.g., those employing regularization or data resampling) is insufficient. A more detailed discussion of the gradient computation and its properties would strengthen the technical argument. The notation is generally clear, but could benefit from more consistent use of subscripts.

The experimental evaluation is a strength, covering a diverse set of tasks and architectures. However, the presented performance gains are often modest. The comparison to baseline ensembles is valuable, but the lack of stronger, more recent baselines is a weakness. For instance, in semantic segmentation, comparison to more recent fully convolutional network architectures or state-of-the-art methods on PASCAL VOC 2012 would provide a more compelling benchmark. The authors mention the challenges of implementing and running the original MCL method as justification for not including it in the experiments. However, reporting performance against a limited number of iterations of MCL would be helpful. Furthermore, more detailed ablation studies—for example, varying the ensemble size or the loss function—would help to better understand the method's sensitivity and optimal configuration. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practical problem (generating diverse predictions) with a sound motivation.
*   The proposed sMCL method offers a computationally efficient solution for training diverse ensembles compared to existing MCL approaches [8].
*   The experiments demonstrate the method’s applicability across multiple vision tasks.
*   The interpretable emergence of specialization within ensemble members is an interesting observation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the work is incremental.
*   The performance improvements over strong baselines are often small.
*   The technical explanation lacks depth and a clear comparison to relevant existing techniques (e.g. multi-task learning).
*   The experimental evaluation could benefit from more rigorous baselines and ablation studies.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a practical and reasonably well-executed approach to training diverse deep ensembles. While the core idea builds upon existing work in MCL, the efficient implementation and demonstration of its applicability across different tasks are valuable contributions. However, the limited novelty and modest performance gains, combined with the areas for improvement in clarity and experimental rigor, lead me to recommend a **weak accept**. I believe that with revisions addressing the comments above, particularly a more detailed technical discussion and a stronger baseline comparison, this paper could be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
