

This paper addresses the multiple-choice learning (MCL) problem for deep networks, a significant challenge where existing MCL solutions fail to generalize. The authors propose a simple yet effective modification: inserting a winner-takes-all layer during backpropagation to integrate learner training with the assignment problem within an ensemble framework. Experimental validation demonstrates strong performance across diverse domains—image classification, segmentation, and captioning.  

**Strengths:**  
The problem’s relevance to deep learning research warrants attention within the NIPS community. The proposed method is notably elegant and conceptually straightforward. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation spans three distinct tasks, providing robust evidence of the approach’s versatility.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The most significant concern lies in the paper’s emphasis on *diversity*—evident in both the title and extensive motivation—yet the method contains no explicit mechanism to enforce it. The authors frame diversity as a core contribution, but the approach merely aggregates predictions without any structural or optimization-driven diversity induction. This misalignment between motivation and implementation is particularly disappointing, as the title and narrative strongly suggest a novel diversity-enforcing technique.  

Additionally, the solution represents a minor incremental advance over Guzman et al.’s relaxation of the MCL problem. While the technique is practical, its theoretical novelty is limited.  

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- The abstract’s opening sentence requires rephrasing for clarity.  
- The term "diversity" should be de-emphasized throughout to align with the method’s actual contribution.  
- Line 108: Correct "f" to "g" in "we fixed the form of...".  
- Line 115: Remove the extraneous period mid-sentence.  

**Key Question:**  
For the deep-learning MCL baselines, how did the authors ensure each individual network had converged to a reasonable solution? Early termination of learners could substantially compromise ensemble performance, yet the paper does not address this critical aspect of experimental setup.
