- Novelty is overstated: the method is an incremental neural-network adaptation of prior MCL work rather than a fundamentally new multi-task learning framework.  
- No theoretical justification is provided for why MCL should outperform standard multi-task training.  
- Technical exposition is inadequate: Section 3 is too short and leaves critical methodological details unspecified.  
- The paper fails to clearly distinguish MCL from standard multi-task paradigms (shared layers, task-specific heads, loss weighting).  
- Lacks rigorous comparisons to common baselines in terms of computational overhead, convergence, and architectural flexibility.  
- Experimental gains are marginal and not shown to be statistically significant.  
- Baselines are weak: the paper does not compare against state-of-the-art multi-task methods or strong task-specific models (e.g., DeepLab, PSPNet).  
- Missing key benchmark evaluations (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012) and stronger baselines like DeepLabv3+ or HRNet to validate claims.  
- Writing is inconsistent and imprecise; terminology is confusing (mixing “multiple choice” with “multiple tasks”).  
- Figures and tables lack clarity and statistical information (e.g., missing error bars).  
- Ablation study is incomplete, omitting sensitivity analyses to key hyperparameters.  
- Recommendation: clarify distinction from existing methods, expand technical details, and run robust baseline experiments to substantiate claims.
