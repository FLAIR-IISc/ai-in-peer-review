Summary
This paper proposes a simple and effective way to train an ensemble of deep networks to minimize the oracle loss, producing multiple plausible outputs that can benefit user-interactive scenarios. The method integrates deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm via a stochastic block gradient descent procedure (sMCL) that is compatible with standard backpropagation, enabling end-to-end training. Experiments on three tasks report strong improvements on oracle metrics.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated objective: directly optimizing oracle loss for multi-hypothesis prediction.
- Practical training procedure that fits neatly into standard deep learning toolchains; conceptually simple and easy to implement.
- Empirical results show consistent oracle gains across multiple tasks.
- End-to-end formulation without bespoke inference machinery.

Weaknesses and questions
- Sensitivity to initialization: How crucial is pretraining? The paper hints at using a pre-trained network for initialization. Please report ablations that start from random initialization and from different pre-trained checkpoints, with multiple seeds. Do the gains persist without warm starts?
- Fair comparison and setup clarity: Do baseline methods receive the same pre-trained initialization and training schedule as sMCL? Are model capacities, data augmentation, learning rate schedules, and early stopping policies matched? The paper should clearly document these settings to rule out confounds.
- Diversity analysis is missing: Oracle accuracy alone does not reveal whether ensemble members are truly diverse or simply redundant with occasional lucky hits. Please quantify diversity using, for example:
  - Pairwise prediction disagreement or error correlation.
  - Pairwise KL divergence between predictive distributions.
  - Specialization metrics such as mutual information between expert assignment and ground-truth attributes or classes.
  - Coverage of distinct modes or qualitative specialization analysis.
  - Diversity–accuracy trade-offs and calibration of individual members.
- Evaluation metrics for user-interaction: If the motivation is interactive systems, consider reporting top-K accuracy, expected user regret, or success rate under a fixed number of user queries. Oracle metrics can overstate practical utility if diversity is not aligned with likely user intents.
- Relation to closely related work: The paper should compare empirically and discuss in depth the similarities and differences with:
  - Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks,” CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
    Please clarify whether sMCL differs in using separate networks vs shared trunk with multiple heads, the loss formulation, training stability, and computational cost, and include a head-to-head comparison.
- Optimization details: The stochastic block gradient descent procedure needs more specifics:
  - How are assignments to ensemble members performed (per-sample vs per-mini-batch), how frequently are they updated, and how are ties broken?
  - Is there any temperature or smoothing in assignment to prevent early collapse?
  - What is the computational overhead vs independently trained ensembles?
  - Any convergence issues or instability, and how are they mitigated?
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How does performance vary with the number of ensemble members M, batch size, learning rate, and regularization? Is there a point of diminishing returns for M?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a thorough initialization ablation:
  - Random initialization vs pretraining, matched across all methods.
  - Multiple seeds with mean and variance.
- Ensure fair and transparent baselines:
  - Same architectures, initializations, data pipelines, and training budgets for all methods.
  - Report total parameter counts and training time.
- Add explicit diversity analyses and visualizations:
  - Quantitative diversity metrics and error correlation matrices.
  - Qualitative specialization (e.g., which inputs each member handles best).
  - Diversity–oracle performance curves.
- Expand evaluation metrics:
  - Top-K accuracy, calibration of individual members, and expected gain for a user given K options.
- Strengthen related work positioning:
  - Direct comparisons to multi-head and diverse ensemble methods, especially Lee et al. (2015), and a discussion of mixture-of-experts–style alternatives.
- Clarify technical details:
  - Formal definition of oracle loss on first use, including task-specific losses.
  - Exact training algorithm, including assignment-update schedule and any regularizers to encourage diversity.
  - Reproducibility details and, if possible, code or pseudocode.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important and timely problem—training deep ensembles to directly optimize oracle performance—and offers a simple, backprop-compatible solution with encouraging empirical results. However, the current presentation leaves key questions open about initialization sensitivity, fairness of comparisons, and the actual diversity learned by the ensemble. A direct comparison with closely related multi-head ensemble work is also necessary. With these issues addressed, the contribution would be compelling; as it stands, I am borderline positive.
