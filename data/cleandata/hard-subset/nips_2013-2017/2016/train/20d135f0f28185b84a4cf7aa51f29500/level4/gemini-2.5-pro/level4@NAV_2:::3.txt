This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep networks for Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where the goal is to produce a set of plausible outputs to minimize an oracle loss. The authors observe that prior methods for MCL, such as the alternating block coordinate descent approach by Guzman-Rivera et al., are computationally infeasible for modern deep learning due to the requirement of training models to convergence multiple times. To overcome this, the paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and efficient algorithm based on stochastic gradient descent. The core mechanism is a "winner-take-gradient" strategy, where for each training example, the loss is backpropagated only to the single ensemble member that currently produces the best prediction (i.e., lowest loss). This approach elegantly interleaves the data assignment and model update steps, allowing all ensemble members to be trained concurrently. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and effectiveness of sMCL through extensive experiments on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing superior oracle performance compared to several baselines.

**Strengths:**
*   The problem of generating multiple high-quality hypotheses from deep models is of significant and growing importance for the NIPS community, particularly for tasks where ambiguity is inherent and for systems that involve a downstream selection process or a human in the loop.
*   The proposed sMCL method is laudable for its simplicity and elegance. The "winner-take-gradient" approach is intuitive, straightforward to implement in existing deep learning frameworks, and is notably parameter-free and agnostic to the specific loss function or network architecture.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. By demonstrating strong results across three distinct and challenging domains (classification, segmentation, and captioning) with appropriate modern architectures, the authors provide compelling evidence for the versatility and effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
*   My primary concern is the potential disconnect between the paper's framing, which heavily emphasizes "diversity" (including in the title), and the technical formulation of the method. The sMCL algorithm directly optimizes the oracle loss, which encourages different models to specialize on different subsets of the data, thereby leading to diverse outputs as an emergent property. However, the objective function does not contain an explicit term to model or enforce diversity by, for instance, penalizing similarity between the outputs of different ensemble members. While the resulting specialization is interesting, framing the paper around diversity without modeling it directly could be seen as a limitation.
*   The contribution, while practical and effective, could be viewed as an incremental step from a conceptual standpoint. The method is essentially a stochastic, online adaptation of the "hard-EM" objective function proposed in the original MCL work [8]. The main novelty lies in the efficient optimization strategy for deep networks, rather than in a new theoretical formulation of the problem.

**Minor Suggestions and Questions:**
*   The opening sentence of the abstract is quite general. A more direct statement that immediately introduces the core problem (e.g., the intractability of training deep ensembles for oracle loss minimization) might be more impactful.
*   Considering the point above, the authors might consider slightly toning down the emphasis on "diversity" and framing the contribution more directly as an efficient and effective algorithm for minimizing oracle loss in deep ensembles, from which diverse specializations naturally emerge.
*   Regarding the baseline MCL implementation with deep networks: Could the authors clarify how they ensured that each network had converged sufficiently within each meta-iteration of the alternating optimization? Stopping the training of an individual learner prematurely before data re-assignment could significantly hinder the performance of this baseline, potentially inflating the reported gains of sMCL.
