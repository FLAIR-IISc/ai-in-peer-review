### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for training an ensemble of deep networks to generate multiple diverse outputs, a scenario particularly useful for applications involving user interaction. The authors adapt the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm to deep networks, proposing a joint training procedure that directly minimizes the oracle loss of the ensemble. To achieve this, a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method is introduced, which integrates seamlessly into the standard back-propagation framework and allows for end-to-end training. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated through experiments on three different tasks, showing improvements in oracle accuracy over baseline approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Practical Contribution:** The primary contribution is the successful integration of the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm with deep neural networks. This addresses the important and practical problem of generating a diverse set of high-quality predictions to interact with a user or a downstream system.

2.  **Elegant Optimization Method:** The proposed stochastic block gradient descent optimization is well-suited for the problem. It is a practical solution that allows the entire ensemble model to be trained efficiently in an end-to-end fashion within a standard back-propagation framework, which should make it easy for others to implement and build upon.

3.  **Clear Empirical Gains:** The experimental results presented on three distinct tasks clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The method consistently improves oracle accuracy compared to the evaluated baselines.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed to strengthen its claims and contribution.

1.  **Unclear Role of Initialization:** The role and sensitivity of model initialization are not sufficiently explored. The authors use a pre-trained network for initialization, but it is unclear how critical this is to the method's success. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study that compares performance when starting from a pre-trained network versus initializing the models with random weights. This would clarify whether the method requires a strong starting point or if it can learn diverse and effective solutions from scratch.

2.  **Insufficient Detail on Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental setup for the baseline methods lacks detail, making a fair assessment of the results difficult. A crucial missing piece of information is whether the baseline methods were also initialized using the same pre-trained network as the proposed sMCL. For a rigorous and fair comparison, all methods should share the same initialization protocol. The authors should clarify this point in the experimental section.

3.  **Limited Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation relies solely on oracle accuracy. While this metric is appropriate for the stated goal, it does not directly measure the **diversity** of the predictions generated by the ensemble members. High oracle accuracy could potentially be achieved by an ensemble whose members produce very similar outputs. The paper would be much more convincing if it included analysis on the diversity of the ensemble's predictions (e.g., by measuring the disagreement rate, the average distance between predictions, or visualizing the differences in outputs). Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into *how* the method achieves its performance and whether it is truly learning a diverse set of predictors.

4.  **Missing Comparison to Highly Relevant Work:** The paper fails to discuss or compare against a highly relevant and similar piece of prior work:
    *   Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks," CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).

    This paper shares a very similar motivation and technical approach—training an ensemble of deep networks to produce diverse outputs by encouraging different "heads" to explore different modes of the data. A discussion of the conceptual similarities and differences, along with an empirical comparison to this method, is essential for positioning the current work within the literature. Its absence is a significant omission.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents an interesting and well-motivated method for training ensembles of deep networks. The proposed optimization technique is practical and the results are promising. However, the paper is weakened by a lack of clarity in the experimental setup, a limited evaluation that neglects to measure diversity, and the omission of a key related work. Addressing these points would substantially improve the quality and impact of the paper.
