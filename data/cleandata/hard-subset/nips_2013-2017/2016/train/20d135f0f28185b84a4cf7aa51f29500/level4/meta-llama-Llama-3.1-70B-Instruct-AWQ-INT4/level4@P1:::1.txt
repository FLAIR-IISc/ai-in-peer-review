This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm to train ensemble models, building upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The authors' key contribution is to modify the original Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) algorithm to make it more suitable for deep neural networks. The proposed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) algorithm enables the training of diverse deep ensembles by minimizing the oracle loss directly.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the sMCL algorithm, including its strengths and weaknesses, and demonstrate its effectiveness on various tasks and architectures.

The main idea of the paper is to train an ensemble of deep networks to produce multiple outputs that minimize the oracle loss. The authors achieve this by introducing a stochastic gradient descent-based approach that avoids the need for costly retraining. The sMCL algorithm is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The experimental results show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the original MCL algorithm. The authors also demonstrate that sMCL can be applied to various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

One potential concern is whether the algorithmic novelty of the paper is merely incremental to that of [8]. However, the authors convincingly argue that the modification they propose makes the algorithm much more useful and practical for deep neural networks.

To further strengthen the paper, it would be interesting to see a comparison of the MCL algorithm when batches are used (rather than the entire dataset). Additionally, more analysis regarding the "diversity" effect of the sMCL algorithm would be beneficial.

Overall, I am in favor of acceptance, as the paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training ensemble models.
2. The sMCL algorithm is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.
3. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on various tasks and architectures.
4. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the sMCL algorithm, including its strengths and weaknesses.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The algorithmic novelty of the paper may be seen as incremental to that of [8].
2. More analysis regarding the "diversity" effect of the sMCL algorithm would be beneficial.
3. A comparison of the MCL algorithm when batches are used (rather than the entire dataset) would be interesting.
