This paper proposes a novel approach to the multiple choice learning problem, specifically tailored for deep networks. The authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based method, dubbed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which enables the training of diverse deep ensembles. By interleaving the assignment step with batch updates in stochastic gradient descent, sMCL overcomes the limitations of existing methods, such as costly retraining or sequential training.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of multiple choice learning for deep networks is of significant importance to the NIPS community. The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, making it a valuable contribution to the field. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the efficacy of sMCL across a wide range of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method, making it accessible to a broad audience. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, highlighting the benefits of sMCL in terms of oracle performance and interpretability.

However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the paper's emphasis on "diversity" is not entirely justified, as the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. While the results do show some level of specialization among ensemble members, it is not clear whether this is a direct result of the sMCL algorithm or an artifact of the experimental setup.

Another concern is that the proposed solution is an incremental step, building upon the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al. While the authors do provide some insights into the differences between their approach and existing methods, it would be beneficial to have a more detailed discussion on the novelty and significance of their contribution.

Minor suggestions include rephrasing the first sentence of the abstract, toning down the emphasis on diversity, and correcting some minor errors in the text (e.g., line 108, the first "f" should be "g" in "we fixed the form of ..").

One question that remains unanswered is how the authors ensured that each of the networks in the baseline MCL ensemble converged to a reasonable result. Cutting the learners early on might significantly affect the ensemble performance, and it would be beneficial to have more information on this aspect.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multiple choice learning for deep networks. While some concerns and minor suggestions have been raised, the paper's strengths and comprehensive experimental results make it a strong candidate for publication.
