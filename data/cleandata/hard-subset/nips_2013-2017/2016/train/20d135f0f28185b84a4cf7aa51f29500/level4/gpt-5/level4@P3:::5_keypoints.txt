- Proposes sMCL: an SGD-friendly "winner-take-gradient" scheme that backpropagates only through the ensemble member with lowest loss per example (ties broken arbitrarily).  
- Casts objective as minimizing oracle loss over an ensemble, approximating MCL via stochastic block-coordinate descent without repeated retraining.  
- Demonstrates improvements in oracle metrics and training-time savings on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning versus independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.  
- Method is simple, general, parameter-free, agnostic to architecture and loss, and easy to implement with standard backprop.  
- Shows emergent specialist behavior among ensemble members and provides k-winner ablation illustrating specialization vs oracle trade-offs.  
- Related to prior M-best, submodular, ensemble diversity, hard-EM/K-means and mixture-of-experts literature; main novelty is a practical SGD-compatible training scheme for deep nets.  
- Strong empirical evaluation across diverse tasks and architectures with sensible baselines.  
- Needs clarification on initialization and fairness: whether all baselines used identical initializations, pretraining, training budgets, and data order.  
- Requests experiments showing sMCL robustness to random initialization and whether sMCL benefits more from warm-starts than baselines.  
- Asks for quantitative diversity metrics beyond oracle performance (classification disagreement, ambiguity coverage, segmentation IoU overlaps, caption self-BLEU/distinct-n/SPICE).  
- Raises concern about load balancing and specialization dynamics: request assignment histograms, per-member utilization over epochs, effect of M and batch size, and whether regularization/annealing is needed.  
- Suggests brief theoretical discussion on non-differentiable min/hard assignments: convergence, stability, relation to hard gating, and bias in the gradient estimator.  
- Requests additional baselines/ablations: learned gating mixture-of-experts, shared-trunk multi-head vs independent models, and beam/top-k evaluation trade-offs.  
- Recommends reporting top-1/top-k (non-oracle) metrics to show potential degradation of single-prediction performance.  
- Asks to clarify relationship to "M Heads are Better than One" (Lee et al.) and explicitly state novel contributions over that preprint.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; Algorithm 2 is easy to follow.  
- Suggests moving implementation details (tie-breaking, k-winner variant, assignment tracking, training schedules) to an appendix and specifying all training schedules.  
- Originality assessed as moderate-to-good: practical adaptation of MCL to deep nets, but conceptually related to existing hard-EM and gating approaches.  
- High practical significance: simple, general recipe likely to be adopted due to consistent gains across tasks.  
- Pros: simplicity, generality, parameter-free, strong oracle improvements, faster training, emergent interpretable specialization, informative ablation.  
- Cons: missing comprehensive diversity/load-balance analyses, unclear fairness/initialization across baselines, limited theoretical discussion, and unclear comparison to related preprints and MoE baselines.  
- Recommendation: lean accept conditional on clarifying experimental details, adding quantitative diversity and specialization analyses, and strengthening related-work and training-dynamics discussion.
