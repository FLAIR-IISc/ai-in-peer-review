## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission

This paper proposes a stochastic gradient descent method incorporating Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and claims advantages across diverse tasks including classification, segmentation, and captioning. While the motivation for employing MCL is understandable, the presented work offers incremental novelty and lacks sufficient clarity and compelling experimental validation to warrant strong acceptance.

**1. Novelty and Related Work:**

The core idea behind MCL – learning from a discrete set of options – is not novel in itself. The paper correctly identifies the connection to previous work [8], and the presented method can be largely characterized as a neural network implementation of this earlier approach with some modifications. The contribution of these adjustments, however, remains unclear. The paper doesn't adequately articulate *how* these changes build upon or significantly improve the original formulation, nor does it justify why a neural network implementation is crucial for realizing the benefits of MCL in the context of modern deep learning. The motivation for applying MCL to multi-task learning is clearly stated, but framing this motivation as a contribution feels overstated. 

**2. Clarity and Technical Presentation:**

The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and detail, particularly in its technical presentation. Section 3, which details the proposed method, is surprisingly concise, occupying only one page. This brevity hinders understanding. A more thorough explanation of the MCL implementation within the proposed framework is necessary. Crucially, the paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from established multi-task learning techniques. Many existing methods implicitly perform a form of task weighting or selection during joint training. The authors need to explicitly address how their framework differs from, and potentially improves upon, these widely-adopted alternatives. What specific mechanism within the MCL framework provides an advantage over simpler multi-task training strategies? A detailed comparison, both conceptually and potentially through ablation studies, is essential. 

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

The experimental evaluation, while broad in its task coverage (classification, image segmentation, and captioning), is ultimately unconvincing. The reported performance improvements are consistently small, and the absence of strong, state-of-the-art baselines significantly weakens the conclusions. Simply demonstrating *some* improvement is insufficient; the authors must demonstrate a *substantial* and statistically significant advantage over competing methods. 

Specifically, the evaluation lacks a comparison against methods specifically designed for multi-task learning. For the image segmentation task, performance should be benchmarked against approaches like those utilizing shared representations with task-specific heads, or methods employing gradient surgery to prevent negative transfer.  For the captioning task, comparison to models leveraging attention mechanisms or reinforcement learning strategies would be crucial. 

Furthermore, the authors should consider evaluating their framework on the PASCAL VOC 2012 dataset, a standard benchmark for semantic segmentation.  This would allow for a more direct and meaningful comparison to established techniques and provide a stronger basis for claiming the effectiveness of their method. Providing error analysis and discussing failure cases would also strengthen the evaluation.



**Overall:**

This paper presents an idea with intuitive appeal, but its execution falls short of expectations. The lack of significant novelty, combined with the unclear presentation and limited experimental validation, make it difficult to recommend acceptance in its current form. The authors need to substantially revise the paper by: 

*   Clearly articulating the specific contributions of their approach beyond simply implementing MCL in a neural network.
*   Providing a more detailed and rigorous technical explanation, with a clear differentiation from existing multi-task learning methods.
*   Conducting a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including stronger baselines and potentially on standard benchmark datasets like PASCAL VOC 2012.
