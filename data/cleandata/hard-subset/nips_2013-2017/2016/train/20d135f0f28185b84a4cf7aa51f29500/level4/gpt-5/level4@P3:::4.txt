Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based procedure for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. For each training example, only the member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates (“winner-take-gradient”); other members receive zero gradient. This is cast as a stochastic block gradient descent approximation to the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) objective of Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS’12, AISTATS’14), which alternates between assigning examples to predictors and retraining models on those assignments. The authors argue that sMCL makes MCL practical for deep networks by avoiding costly re-training, is architecture- and loss-agnostic, and is parameter-free. Experiments span CIFAR-10 classification (Caffe “Quick” CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MS COCO image captioning (VGG+LSTM). sMCL improves oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles and over baselines inspired by [5, 8, 9], while being faster than alternating MCL. The paper also reports emergent specialization across ensemble members.

The paper builds squarely on the MCL paradigm [8, 9] and related “M-best/diverse” inference work [2, 15, 16, 23, 24], moving it into end-to-end deep learning with an SGD-compatible training rule. It is also closely related to hard-EM/mixture-of-experts style assignments and joint losses for ensemble learning [19, 26], and to specialist architectures informed by a generalist [2, 12], though here specialization emerges without an explicit gating network.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: the “winner-take-gradient” rule is easy to implement in any SGD-trained architecture and applies to arbitrary per-example losses.
- Practical contribution: concurrent training avoids the multiple full re-trainings required by alternating MCL, yielding substantial speedups.
- Breadth of empirical domains: classification, dense prediction, and sequence generation, with qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization.
- Oracle-centric evaluation is appropriate for multiple-hypothesis settings and aligns with established “top-k” evaluation practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Incremental novelty: The main idea is a straightforward stochasticization of the MCL objective with hard assignments; it is very close to hard-EM/mixture-of-experts without a gating network. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning relative to these established paradigms, including what is genuinely new beyond the practical SGD formulation.
- Limited technical depth in Section 3: The core method is described very briefly. Important details are missing or deserve analysis: tie-breaking strategy and its effect on convergence; potential “starvation” of members early in training; load-balancing or regularization to avoid degenerate assignments; stability across random seeds; and convergence guarantees or bounds (even informal) for the stochastic block updates.
- Baselines and fairness: 
  - For captioning, the comparison to beam search is helpful; analogous multi-hypothesis baselines are missing for classification and segmentation (e.g., top-k from a single model via sampling, MC-dropout, or test-time augmentation). 
  - The alternating MCL baseline is reported after only five meta-iterations; this may be insufficient for a fair comparison, though the authors argue additional iterations are infeasible. A more thorough discussion of computational budgets and wall-clock time per method would help.
  - The segmentation experiments use a VOC 2011 validation subset; comparing on the more standard VOC 2012 split and to stronger FCN variants would strengthen claims.
- Scope of evaluation: Results focus almost exclusively on oracle metrics. While that is the target objective, practitioners may care about the trade-off with single-prediction performance, calibration, and diversity measures. It would be useful to report: 
  - single-model accuracy/IoU/CIDEr per member and ensemble-averaged; 
  - diversity metrics (e.g., disagreement, n-gram diversity for captioning) versus oracle gains; 
  - sensitivity to ensemble size M and to the “top-k winners” relaxation the authors briefly explore.
- Clarity and presentation: The method is clear at a high level, but the exposition is terse. Adding pseudocode (Algorithm 2), specifying batch-wise operations precisely, and detailing optimizer and schedule parity between sMCL and baselines would improve reproducibility.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound at the level presented, and the empirical results support the main claims about oracle performance and speed. However, the lack of analysis on assignment dynamics, stability, and convergence is a gap. Baseline coverage is incomplete for some tasks, and dataset/split choices are not the strongest available.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable, but Section 3 is too short for the central contribution. Please expand the algorithmic description (forward/backward pass details, tie handling, initialization, and any regularizers), include pseudocode, and state training-time and memory overheads relative to independent ensembles.

Originality
- The contribution is primarily practical: adapting MCL to deep networks via SGD-friendly hard assignments. Conceptually it is close to known methods (hard-EM, mixture-of-experts without gating). The paper should articulate sharper distinctions to prior art and, if possible, provide theoretical or empirical insights unique to the proposed formulation.

Significance
- Providing a simple, practical way to train diverse deep ensembles for oracle metrics is useful, and the breadth of applicability is attractive. Demonstrated gains are modest to moderate and would be more compelling with stronger baselines and standardized benchmarks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines: 
  - Classification/segmentation: compare to top-k or multi-hypothesis outputs from a single model (e.g., sampling, test-time augmentation), and to specialist/generalist setups [2, 12].
  - Segmentation: evaluate on PASCAL VOC 2012 with standard splits; consider more competitive FCN variants.
- Analysis and ablations: 
  - Report assignment distributions over training, member “starvation” incidents, and any load-balancing techniques; study the “top-k winners” variant more systematically.
  - Provide sensitivity to M, initialization, and tie-breaking; include multiple random seeds.
  - Report trade-offs with single-prediction metrics and calibration.
- Method details: provide explicit pseudocode for sMCL, computational cost per iteration, memory overhead, and training curves for both oracle and standard losses.

Pros
- Simple, general, and easy to adopt.
- Concurrent training yields large speedups over alternating MCL.
- Demonstrated across diverse tasks with interpretable specialization.

Cons
- Incremental novelty relative to MCL/hard-EM/mixture-of-experts.
- Limited technical depth and analysis of training dynamics.
- Baselines and benchmarks could be stronger; reliance on oracle metrics alone.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is practical and promising, but the current submission feels incremental and underdeveloped in analysis and evaluation. Addressing the baseline and clarity issues above could move this to a strong, impactful contribution.
