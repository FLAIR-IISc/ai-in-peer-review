This paper presents a method for generating multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks with the objective of minimizing the oracle loss, a useful paradigm for interactive applications. The effectiveness of the proposed approach is validated through oracle experiments on three different tasks. The core contributions are the integration of deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework and the development of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization algorithm to minimize the MCL oracle loss. A key advantage of this optimization technique is its compatibility with standard back-propagation, allowing the entire model to be trained in an end-to-end manner.

However, I have a few concerns. The manuscript would be strengthened by an analysis of the sensitivity to initialization. For example, how does the model perform if its components are initialized from random values rather than with a pre-trained network? Furthermore, the experimental settings for the comparative analysis lack sufficient detail. For a fair comparison, it should be clarified whether the baseline methods use the same pre-trained network for initialization as the proposed sMCL method.

The evaluation relies exclusively on oracle accuracy. Given that a primary goal of ensemble methods is to produce varied outputs, I would expect to see an analysis of the diversity among the predictions of the ensemble members.

Finally, the paper omits a highly relevant piece of prior work with a very similar concept. The authors should discuss and compare their method to the following paper:
Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks." CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
