This paper introduces a straightforward solution to the multiple-choice learning (MCL) problem when the learners are deep networks. Existing approaches to MCL do not readily transfer to deep architectures. The authors demonstrate that adding a winner-takes-the-gradient layer into backpropagation enables joint optimization of learner training alongside the assignment problem in an ensemble setting. Empirical results indicate good performance across a broad set of tasks: image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

Strengths:
- The MCL problem in deep learning is clearly relevant to the NIPS community.
- The proposed approach is admirably simple.
- The experiments cover three different tasks.

Weaknesses:
- Primary concern: the paper strongly emphasizes “diversity” (including in the title), yet the method does not explicitly enforce diversity. I expected to see an explicit diversity term integrated into the model but found none.
- Relative to the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al., the contribution appears incremental.

Minor suggestions:
- Rewrite the first sentence of the abstract.
- Tone down the emphasis on diversity.
- Line 108: in “we fixed the form of …,” the first “f” should be “g.”
- Line 115: there is an extra period in the middle of a sentence.

One Question:
For the deep-learning MCL baseline, how did you ensure that each network converged to a reasonable solution? Stopping some learners early could substantially affect the ensemble’s performance.
