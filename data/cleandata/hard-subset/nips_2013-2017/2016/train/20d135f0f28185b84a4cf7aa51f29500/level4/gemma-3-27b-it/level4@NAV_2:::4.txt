The authors propose a stochastic gradient descent approach, termed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), for training ensembles of deep networks within a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework. The core idea is to minimize oracle loss by allowing ensemble members to specialize, providing a set of diverse and plausible hypotheses. The method is demonstrated across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

This paper presents an incremental contribution to the field. While the motivation for MCL – providing multiple hypotheses for downstream processes – is well-established, the work largely adapts the existing MCL paradigm [8] to deep neural networks. The novelty lies in the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization suitable for this context, avoiding the costly retraining required by previous MCL methods. However, the technical depth dedicated to explaining this adaptation (Section 3) is relatively brief. A more thorough discussion of the differences between sMCL and alternative multi-task learning strategies, which also involve concurrent training and gradient-based optimization, would strengthen the presentation.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering a variety of computer vision tasks. However, the reported performance improvements are modest, and the comparison to baselines is not exhaustive. To further substantiate the effectiveness of sMCL, it would be valuable to include results on a more competitive benchmark, such as PASCAL VOC 2012, and to compare against stronger, more recent baselines in each task. The authors should also consider providing ablation studies to analyze the contribution of specific components of sMCL.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practical problem – generating diverse predictions for systems interacting with oracles or human users.
*   sMCL offers a computationally efficient approach to training diverse ensembles, addressing a key limitation of prior work [8].
*   The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over independent ensembles across multiple tasks.
*   The qualitative results suggest that the diverse solutions produced by sMCL can provide interpretable representations of task ambiguity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the work is incremental; it's primarily an application of existing concepts to a new setting.
*   The technical explanation of the proposed method is somewhat concise, lacking a detailed comparison to related multi-task learning techniques.
*   The performance improvements are modest, and the comparison to baselines could be more rigorous.
*   The writing style could be improved for clarity and conciseness.
