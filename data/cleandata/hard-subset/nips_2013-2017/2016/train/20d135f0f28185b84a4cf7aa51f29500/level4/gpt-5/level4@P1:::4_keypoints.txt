- Proposes an SGD variant of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss, evaluated on classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Conceptually extends prior MCL [8] by interleaving assignment and training within SGD for neural networks.
- Core contribution appears incremental—a neural-network instantiation of [8] with minor adjustments rather than a new paradigm.
- Targets a practical problem: producing diverse ensemble outputs optimized for oracle selection.
- Method is simple and evaluated across multiple vision tasks, demonstrating some generality.
- Incremental novelty: technical delta over prior MCL work is small.
- Paper is poorly written; Section 3 (technical method) is short and lacks detail.
- Unclear distinction from standard alternating/block-coordinate training; needs precise conceptual and algorithmic comparison.
- Experimental evaluation uses weak baselines and shows only small gains.
- Missing stronger segmentation benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012) and comparisons to stronger methods.
- Quality concerns: limited technical detail and insufficient analysis versus alternative/alternating training.
- Clarity concerns: core method under-explained; needs formal derivations, algorithmic details, and training dynamics.
- Originality limited: essentially a “winner-take-gradient” SGD adaptation of MCL, novelty not convincingly established.
- Significance uncertain due to small improvements and lack of competitive baselines on standard benchmarks.
- Suggested improvements: expand technical section with derivations and clarify differences from alternating training.
- Suggested improvements: include stronger baselines across tasks and add PASCAL VOC 2012 segmentation experiments.
- Suggested improvements: provide ablations (assignment strategies, tie-breaking, ensemble size) and detailed training analysis.
- Pros: addresses a practical problem with a simple, broadly applicable approach.
- Cons: incremental over [8], poor clarity, minimal technical exposition, weak baselines, and missing stronger benchmark evaluations.
