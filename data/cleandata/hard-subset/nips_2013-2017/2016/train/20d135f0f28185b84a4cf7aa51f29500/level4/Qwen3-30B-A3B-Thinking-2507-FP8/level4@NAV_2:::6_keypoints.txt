- Introduces sMCL: trains diverse deep ensembles by updating only the lowest-loss member per example, enabling concurrent SGD-based training without costly retraining.  
- Builds on Multiple Choice Learning but adapts it for deep networks, applicable to classification and structured prediction tasks.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains across CIFAR10, PASCAL VOC, and MSCOCO, outperforming independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.  
- Method is parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and 5× faster than MCL, showing practical utility.  
- sMCL achieves substantial oracle performance improvements (6–10%) and yields interpretable member specialization.  
- Paper properly motivates the need for diverse hypotheses in ambiguous real-world tasks (e.g., autonomous vehicles, assistive tech).  
- Testing/inference description is insufficiently detailed—unclear how the oracle selection operates at test time and whether ground truth is assumed.  
- Definition and justification of the "independent ensembles" baseline are unclear, hindering fair comparison.  
- Evidence for class-specific specialization is weak—reassignments occur per example, so class-level specialization claim is not fully supported.  
- Omits discussion of related diversity-regularization work in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), missing relevant prior art.  
- Technical approach is sound with clear theoretical motivation and strong empirical validation.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized but needs clearer experimental/testing procedure for reproducibility.  
- Contribution is original by adapting MCL to deep networks via stochastic optimization and has significant potential impact.  
- Overall assessment: strong technical contribution with practical value; recommend weak acceptance pending clarification of methodological details.
