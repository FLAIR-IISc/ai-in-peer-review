The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep learners, which is unique in that only one learner of the ensemble is active for each training example. Unlike previous deep and classical ensembles, during training, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is identified and updated with the gradient. This approach leads to surprisingly good experimental results and fixes some deficiencies of existing methods.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and its relevance to real-world applications. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, including ensemble learning and generating multiple solutions. However, there are a few areas that could be improved.

Firstly, the testing phase is not clearly explained. It appears that the authors use the ground truth to find the best member of the ensemble, as explained in line 159 on page 5. However, this could be clarified further.

Secondly, the authors refer to "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" (denoted as "Indp." in figures and line 181 on page 5) without providing a clear explanation for this baseline. It would be helpful to provide more context or a reference to understand what these terms mean.

Thirdly, the authors repeatedly point to the "specialization" of ensemble members towards different output classes. However, the reassignment in training is on a per-example basis. This argument is not justified sufficiently, and it is unclear whether this specialization is a direct result of the proposed algorithm or an artifact of the experimental setup. One possible way to address this concern is to incorporate specialization in the loss function.

Lastly, the authors did not discuss the very related work of diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.). This technique is more rigorous but yet to be studied in the deep setting. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on how the proposed algorithm relates to this existing body of work.

Overall, the paper provides a simple yet effective idea for training diverse deep ensembles. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field. However, addressing the above concerns would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the broad applicability and efficacy of the proposed algorithm.
* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of Multiple Choice Learning and its relevance to real-world applications.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The testing phase is not clearly explained.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation for the "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" baseline.
* The argument for specialization of ensemble members towards different output classes is not justified sufficiently.
* The authors did not discuss the very related work of diversity regularization in neural networks.

Recommendation:

I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with revisions to address the above concerns. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of the testing phase, clarify the "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" baseline, and discuss the related work on diversity regularization in neural networks. Additionally, the authors should consider incorporating specialization in the loss function to strengthen their argument.
