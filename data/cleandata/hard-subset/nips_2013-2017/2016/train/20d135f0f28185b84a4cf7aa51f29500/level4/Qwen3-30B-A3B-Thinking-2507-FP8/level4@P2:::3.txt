

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple plausible hypotheses rather than single predictions. The authors address a significant limitation in existing Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods, which require costly retraining of models for deep networks. They propose a simple yet effective modification to standard backpropagation where gradients are only backpropagated to the lowest-error predictor for each example during training, eliminating the need for iterative model retraining. The method is applicable to any deep learning architecture trained with stochastic gradient descent, making it broadly useful across various computer vision and natural language processing tasks.

The work builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL framework (NIPS 2015), which formalized the problem of producing multiple outputs to minimize oracle loss. While their approach requires alternating between model training and example assignment, sMCL integrates these steps into a single stochastic training process. The authors also relate their work to ensemble learning approaches (e.g., Ahmed et al., NIPS 2016) and methods for generating multiple diverse solutions (e.g., Dey et al., ICML 2016), highlighting how sMCL overcomes their limitations for deep networks.

Quality: The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness with thorough experiments across three distinct tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning). The authors provide both quantitative results (showing consistent 6-10% improvements over baselines) and qualitative analysis of how ensemble members develop specialized expertise. The method is clearly well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The paper is complete as a technical contribution, with appropriate attention to both strengths and weaknesses (e.g., noting that specialization is less evident in segmentation than in classification).

Clarity: The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its relationship to prior work. The figures effectively illustrate the concept of ensemble specialization and the qualitative benefits of the approach. The technical description of sMCL is particularly accessible, with Algorithm 2 providing a clear implementation guide. Some minor grammatical issues exist (e.g., "we fixed the form of .." should be "g" instead of "f" on line 108), but these do not impede understanding.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending MCL to deep learning architectures in a practical way. While the core idea of using gradient-based optimization for MCL is related to Guzman-Rivera et al., the authors' specific solution (sMCL) is novel in its simplicity and efficiency. The method's parameter-free nature and applicability across diverse architectures represent meaningful advances over prior work. The paper adequately distinguishes its contribution from related NIPS papers, particularly those addressing ensemble diversity (e.g., Ahmed et al., NIPS 2016).

Significance: The work addresses a practical problem in AI systems where ambiguity is common (e.g., object classification with overlapping categories, ambiguous image content). The ability to produce multiple plausible hypotheses rather than a single prediction has clear implications for real-world applications, particularly in systems involving human-in-the-loop evaluation. The paper demonstrates this significance through convincing experiments across multiple domains, showing consistent improvements in oracle performance. The method's simplicity and broad applicability further enhance its significance.

Strengths:
- The proposed method is simple, elegant, and directly applicable to existing deep learning frameworks
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across three distinct tasks
- Provides insightful qualitative analysis of ensemble specialization
- Avoids the computational burden of prior MCL methods (5x faster than MCL)
- Well-motivated by practical AI applications where ambiguity is common

Weaknesses:
- The title and some portions of the paper overemphasize "diversity" as a primary goal, when the method does not explicitly enforce diversity but rather discovers it as a consequence of minimizing oracle loss
- The paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of the relationship between specialization and diversity (e.g., how the method ensures that members specialize on different aspects of the data distribution)
- The baseline comparison with MCL requires clarification on how convergence was handled (as noted in the draft review), as the authors acknowledge that MCL's performance drops substantially on segmentation and captioning tasks due to the retraining requirement
- While the method is parameter-free, it would be useful to discuss how the number of ensemble members (M) affects performance

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a meaningful contribution to a relevant problem in deep learning, with a simple yet effective solution that overcomes a significant limitation in prior work. The technical soundness is strong, with thorough experiments across multiple domains, and the method's simplicity and broad applicability enhance its value to the community.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper's emphasis on "diversity" is somewhat misleading, as the method does not explicitly address diversity but rather discovers it as a byproduct of the training objective. This could potentially mislead readers about the method's primary contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a practical solution to an important problem. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear advantages over prior approaches. The minor concerns regarding the emphasis on diversity can be addressed with minor revisions to the paper. I believe this work is well-suited for NIPS and would be of significant interest to the community.
