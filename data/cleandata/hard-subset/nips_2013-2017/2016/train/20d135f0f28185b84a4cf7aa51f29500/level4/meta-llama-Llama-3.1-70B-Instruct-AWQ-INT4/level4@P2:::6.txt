The paper proposes a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep learners, which is unique in that only one learner of the ensemble is active for each training example, unlike previous deep and classical ensembles. During training, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is identified and updated with the gradient. This approach is simple yet leads to surprisingly good experimental results, addressing some deficiencies of existing methods.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), and demonstrates its broad applicability and efficacy in training diverse deep ensembles. The experiments show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure. The authors also provide an analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

However, there are a few areas that require clarification or further discussion. Firstly, the testing phase is not clearly explained, and it is suspected that the authors use the ground truth to find the best member, as explained in line 159 on page 5. A clearer explanation of the testing phase would be beneficial.

Secondly, the authors refer to 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' (denoted as 'Indp.' in figures and line 181 on page 5) without providing a clear explanation for this baseline. A more detailed description of these baselines would help to better understand the experimental results.

Thirdly, the authors repeatedly point to the `specialization` of ensemble members towards different output classes. However, the reassignment in training is on a per-example basis, which raises questions about the justification of this argument. It would be beneficial to discuss whether incorporating specialization in the loss function could further improve the results.

Lastly, the authors do not discuss the related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which is a more rigorous technique that has yet to be studied in the deep setting. A discussion on how sMCL relates to this technique and its potential applications would be valuable.

Overall, the paper presents a unique and effective approach to training deep ensembles, and with some clarifications and additional discussions, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. Therefore, I recommend weak acceptance.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective ensemble training algorithm for deep learners.
* The experiments demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL in training diverse deep ensembles.
* The authors provide an analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles.

Weaknesses:

* The testing phase is not clearly explained.
* The 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' baselines are not clearly explained.
* The argument for `specialization` of ensemble members towards different output classes is not fully justified.
* The related work on diversity regularization in neural networks is not discussed.
