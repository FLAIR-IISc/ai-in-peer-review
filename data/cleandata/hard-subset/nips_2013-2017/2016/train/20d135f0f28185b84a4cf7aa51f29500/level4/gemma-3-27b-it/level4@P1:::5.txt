The authors propose a method to generate multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks that minimizes the oracle loss, which is beneficial in scenarios involving interactions with users or downstream verification components. Experiments on three tasks – image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning – demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. This paper integrates deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm and introduces a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to minimize the oracle loss. Importantly, this optimization method can be easily implemented within existing back-propagation learning frameworks, allowing for end-to-end training of the entire model. 

A key question is the sensitivity of the method to initialization. Would performance be maintained if models were initialized with random weights instead of pre-trained networks? The paper doesn’t explore this, and clarification would strengthen the results. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks detail regarding the baselines. Specifically, do the baseline methods utilize the same pre-trained networks as sMCL for initialization? Providing this information is crucial for a fair comparison. While the authors evaluate performance using oracle accuracy, a more thorough analysis of the diversity of ensemble members would be valuable. Quantifying the dissimilarity between generated outputs, perhaps using metrics like cosine similarity or n-gram overlap, would provide a more complete picture. 

Finally, the authors should consider and compare to related work, specifically Stefan Lee et al. (2015) – “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks.” This work presents a similar idea of training diverse ensembles and a comparison would be insightful.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses a practical problem – generating diverse and plausible hypotheses for downstream processes.
*   The proposed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) method is a novel and theoretically sound approach to training diverse ensembles.
*   The method is computationally efficient and integrates well with existing deep learning frameworks.
*   The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across a variety of tasks and network architectures.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   The sensitivity to initialization is not explored.
*   The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding baseline initialization.
*   The diversity of ensemble members is not thoroughly analyzed.
*   The paper does not adequately address related work, particularly Lee et al. (2015).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The method is practical and easy to implement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of analysis regarding initialization sensitivity and baseline comparison weakens the conclusions.
*   The lack of quantitative analysis of ensemble diversity limits the understanding of the method's behavior.
*   The omission of relevant related work detracts from the paper’s contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL method is a promising approach to generating diverse outputs for tasks where multiple plausible solutions exist. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding experimental detail and analysis of diversity, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above. The authors should provide more details about the experimental setup, explore the sensitivity to initialization, analyze the diversity of ensemble members, and discuss related work more thoroughly.
