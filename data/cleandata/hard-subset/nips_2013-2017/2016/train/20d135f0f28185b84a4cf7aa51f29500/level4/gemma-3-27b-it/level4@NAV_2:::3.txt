This paper proposes a straightforward approach to multiple choice learning (MCL) within the context of deep neural networks. Existing methods for MCL do not readily scale to deep learning architectures. This work demonstrates that a ‘winner-take-gradient’ strategy during backpropagation enables joint training of learners and addresses the assignment problem in an ensemble setting. Experimental results demonstrate strong performance across diverse tasks including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. 

**Strengths:**
- The problem of applying MCL to deep learning is relevant and of interest to the NIPS community.
- The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to implement.
- The experimental evaluation covers a broad range of tasks and architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
- A primary concern is the emphasis on “diversity” throughout the paper (reflected even in the title), while the method does not explicitly enforce diversity. The approach relies on oracle loss minimization to implicitly encourage specialization, but lacks a direct diversity objective.
- The proposed solution represents an incremental improvement over existing work, particularly the relaxation of the optimization problem presented by Guzman et al. [8, 9]. While effective, the novelty may be somewhat limited. 

**Minor Suggestions:**
- The first sentence of the abstract could be rephrased for clarity.
- The emphasis on “diversity” should be toned down to more accurately reflect the method’s mechanism.
- Line 108, replace the first instance of "f" with "g" in the sentence "we fixed the form of…".
- Remove the extraneous period in the middle of the sentence on line 115.

**Question to Authors:**
For the baseline MCL implementation with deep networks, how was convergence of individual networks ensured? Prematurely halting training of the learners could negatively impact the overall ensemble performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a practically relevant problem – training diverse ensembles for scenarios requiring multiple plausible hypotheses.
- Presents a simple and effective algorithm that avoids the computational costs of existing MCL methods.
- Demonstrates strong empirical results across a variety of tasks and architectures.
- The method is generally applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The novelty of the proposed approach may be considered incremental, building upon the foundations laid by Guzman et al. [8, 9].
- The method does not explicitly enforce diversity, potentially limiting its ability to explore the full space of possible solutions.
