Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based algorithm for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss—i.e., the loss of the best prediction among M outputs. Building on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which alternates between assigning examples to predictors and retraining to convergence, sMCL replaces the costly alternating optimization with a “winner-take-gradient” strategy: for each example in a minibatch, only the lowest-loss model in the ensemble receives gradient updates. This makes the approach compatible with modern deep networks and agnostic to both architecture and loss. Experiments on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCNs), and image captioning (CNN+LSTM on MSCOCO) show consistent gains in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, the original MCL procedure, and the boosting-style method of Dey et al. [5]. The authors also document emergent specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class clusters in classification, structural variations in segmentation, and n-gram specialization in captioning).

Relation to prior work
The work directly extends MCL [8, 9] by providing a stochastic optimization scheme that interleaves assignment and updating within standard SGD, avoiding repeated full retraining. It is complementary to diverse M-best inference methods for probabilistic models [3, 15, 16, 23, 24], which are not readily applicable to general deep architectures, and to ensemble diversity techniques such as resampling, artificial data, or negative correlation learning [21, 27, 18]. Compared to the sequential boosting-like approach of Dey et al. [5], sMCL trains ensemble members concurrently and scales to deep models.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective follows the MCL upper bound on oracle loss. The gradient routing is correct for hard assignments, and the algorithm is straightforward to implement in any SGD-based framework. Empirical results are convincing across tasks, with clear oracle gains and qualitative diversity.
- Completeness: The paper provides ablations (e.g., k-winners) and qualitative analysis of specialization. More analysis of diversity metrics and sensitivity would strengthen it (see suggestions).
- Baselines: Comparisons include independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al., and beam search for captioning. Reporting wall-clock speedups over MCL is useful.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for oracle metrics, the algorithmic description, and the interpretation of emergent expertise are clear. Minor improvements could include more explicit pseudocode details (e.g., tie-breaking policy) and clearer reporting of compute budgets.

Originality
The core idea—replacing alternating block optimization with a stochastic, per-batch hard assignment and winner-only gradient updates—is incremental relative to MCL but practically significant. The contribution lies in making MCL usable for deep networks, not in redefining the objective. The paper’s strength is operationalizing a known idea at scale.

Significance
The method is impactful for applications where multiple plausible outputs are desired and an oracle (human or downstream module) can select among them. sMCL’s simplicity, architectural and loss-function agnosticism, and empirical gains make it a practical tool likely to see adoption.

Strengths
- Simple, parameter-free algorithm compatible with standard SGD pipelines.
- Strong and consistent improvements in oracle metrics across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Concurrent training with substantial speedup over MCL (no meta-iterations).
- Emergent, interpretable specialization among ensemble members.
- Clear exposition and broad applicability.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty is primarily a practical optimization reformulation of [8]; limited theoretical analysis (e.g., convergence under hard assignments, comparison to soft variants).
- Diversity analysis is mostly qualitative or task-specific. Limited use of explicit diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise disagreement, MI, n-gram diversity beyond counts).
- Limited discussion of trade-offs with non-oracle metrics (e.g., single-prediction accuracy or calibration) and potential mode collapse within members.
- Sensitivity to M (ensemble size), tie-breaking strategy, and batch size is not fully explored.
- MCL baseline may be disadvantaged if not using minibatches/early stopping; fairness of meta-iteration budget should be clarified.
- Compute reporting could include FLOPs/epoch and hardware to contextualize speedups.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against a minibatch variant of MCL (batch-wise assignments with partial training) to isolate the benefit of interleaving vs. full retraining.
- Report explicit diversity measures: classifier disagreement, coverage of class/mode space, mutual information between predictions, and for captioning, distinct-n metrics alongside CIDEr.
- Evaluate sensitivity to ensemble size M, batch size, and tie-breaking; consider stochastic or tempered soft-assignments (k-winners, temperature-controlled softmax) as ablations.
- Include non-oracle metrics (e.g., single-model accuracy, calibration/ECE) to show sMCL does not degrade base performance excessively.
- Expand baselines to include negative correlation learning or other deep ensemble diversity regularizers where feasible.
- For captioning, compare to beam search across each ensemble member and to ensembling beams to disentangle training vs. inference diversity.

Recommendation
Borderline accept to accept. Although the algorithmic novelty over [8] is incremental, the paper convincingly demonstrates that this small change makes MCL practical for deep learning, with strong empirical benefits, interpretability, and speed. The work is likely to be useful to the community. Additional diversity analysis and a stronger MCL minibatch baseline would further solidify the case.
