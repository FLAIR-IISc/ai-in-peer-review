Summary:
The paper proposes a simple and practical approach to multiple choice learning (MCL) with deep networks. The key idea is a “winner-takes-the-gradient” (WTG) rule during backpropagation: for each training example, only the network in the ensemble that currently predicts it best receives the gradient update. This couples assignment and learning within standard SGD, avoiding combinatorial optimization and enabling end-to-end training. The method is evaluated across three tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—and shows competitive performance.

Strengths:
- Relevance: The problem of MCL for deep learning is important to the NeurIPS community, with broad applicability to ensemble learning and multi-output prediction.
- Simplicity: The proposed WTG mechanism is conceptually simple, easy to implement, and integrates seamlessly into existing deep learning pipelines.
- Breadth of evaluation: Demonstrations on three distinct domains (classification, segmentation, and captioning) suggest the approach is broadly applicable.

Weaknesses and Concerns:
- Diversity is not explicitly modeled: The paper’s title and narrative heavily emphasize “diversity,” but the method does not include a direct diversity-promoting term or constraint. Any diversity arises implicitly via hard assignment/specialization. This creates a disconnect between the stated motivation and the actual objective, which may mislead readers about the guarantees or mechanisms for diversity.
- Incremental novelty: Relative to prior MCL work (e.g., the relaxation by Guzman-Rivera et al.), the contribution can be viewed as an effective, engineering-focused adaptation to deep networks via hard assignment during backprop. While valuable, the conceptual step may appear incremental without deeper analysis or theoretical insight into convergence or specialization behavior.
- Analysis depth: The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of when and why WTG yields beneficial specialization versus mode collapse (e.g., distributions of assignments across learners, per-learner accuracy, and disagreement measures).

Suggestions for Improvement:
- Title and framing: Tone down claims around “diversity,” or provide explicit evidence and/or mechanisms. For example:
  - Report quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise disagreement, KL divergence of output distributions, coverage/entropy of assignments).
  - Include ablations comparing hard vs. soft assignment (e.g., temperature-controlled softmax gating) to show the necessity of the hard WTG rule if applicable.
- Baselines and comparisons:
  - Include or clarify comparisons to strong ensemble baselines such as independently trained networks with different inits, bagging, snapshot ensembles, mixture-of-experts with a learned or fixed gating network, and loss top-k variants.
  - Detail training budgets to ensure fair comparison (same total compute/epochs across methods).
- Stability and convergence:
  - Provide per-network training curves and assignment histograms over epochs to demonstrate that all learners improve and that specialization is not dominated by a single model.
  - Discuss potential pitfalls (e.g., early dominance by one learner) and any mitigation strategies (e.g., warm-up phases, balanced sampling, periodic reassignment).
- Theoretical/empirical insight:
  - Briefly relate WTG to hard EM or mixture-of-experts with hard gating, and discuss any implications for convergence or bias/variance trade-offs.
  - If possible, include a small synthetic experiment illustrating specialization and its relation to data modes.

Minor Comments and Edits:
- Abstract: The first sentence should be rewritten for clarity and precision. Consider stating the problem and your contribution succinctly (e.g., “We present a simple approach to multiple choice learning with deep networks that integrates assignment and training via a winner-takes-the-gradient update.”).
- Tone down the repeated use of “diversity” unless it is explicitly defined and measured.
- Line 108: In the phrase “we fixed the form of …,” the first “f” should be “g.”
- Line 115: There is an extra period in the middle of a sentence; please remove it.
- Provide a concise description of the loss and assignment rule in the main text (not only in supplementary material), and ensure notation is consistent and unambiguous.

Questions for the Authors:
- Baseline MCL with deep learning: How did you ensure that each network in the baseline converged to a reasonable solution? If training of individual learners was curtailed early, the ensemble’s performance could be significantly affected. Please clarify:
  - The stopping criteria and training budget per learner for the baseline.
  - Whether early stopping or shared training schedules were used.
  - Any measures taken to avoid under-training some learners (e.g., equalizing epochs, monitoring validation performance per model).

Overall Assessment:
This is a well-motivated and practically useful contribution that adapts MCL to deep networks via a simple WTG backpropagation rule. The method appears easy to implement and effective across multiple tasks. The main limitations are (1) the heavy emphasis on “diversity” without an explicit mechanism or rigorous measurement, and (2) the incremental nature of the contribution without deeper analysis. Addressing the framing around diversity, strengthening baselines, and adding analysis of assignment/specialization and stability would substantially improve the paper. With these clarifications and additions, the work would be a solid contribution to the literature on ensemble learning and structured prediction with deep models.
