This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a stochastic gradient descent approach to training ensembles of deep networks for Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The core idea is to frame producing multiple outputs as a learning problem where the goal is to minimize loss with respect to an oracle that selects the best prediction from the ensemble. The authors demonstrate sMCL’s applicability across diverse tasks – image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning – and report improved oracle performance compared to baseline methods.

The motivation for MCL is well-established: many real-world perception systems benefit from providing multiple plausible hypotheses rather than a single prediction, particularly in scenarios involving downstream verification or human interaction. However, the novelty of this work is incremental. It largely adapts the existing MCL framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] to deep neural networks, addressing the impracticality of their alternating block coordinate descent approach for large models. While the adaptation to stochastic gradient descent is a valuable contribution, framing it as a fundamentally new approach to MCL might be overstated. 

The paper's clarity could be improved. The technical details of sMCL (Section 3) are presented concisely, but a more thorough explanation of *how* this approach differs from other multi-task learning strategies utilizing gradient descent would be beneficial. Many existing techniques already address gradient conflicts when training models for multiple objectives; the authors need to clearly articulate the specific advantages of their 'winner-take-gradient' approach in this context. The notation is adequate but could be streamlined.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive in terms of the tasks explored, but the reported performance gains are relatively modest. While improvements are reported across all tasks, the absence of stronger baselines limits the impact of these results. Specifically, for semantic segmentation, a comparison to state-of-the-art methods on the Pascal VOC 2012 dataset would provide a more convincing demonstration of sMCL’s effectiveness. The authors should also analyze the computational cost of sMCL compared to other ensemble training methods, especially given its claimed efficiency. A more detailed ablation study, examining the impact of ensemble size and other hyperparameters, would also be valuable. The qualitative results are helpful in illustrating the diversity of the generated outputs, but further quantitative analysis of this diversity (e.g., using metrics like entropy or dissimilarity scores) would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical problem in deploying perception systems in real-world applications.
*   Provides a practical adaptation of the MCL framework to deep neural networks, overcoming limitations of previous approaches.
*   Demonstrates the broad applicability of sMCL across diverse tasks and network architectures.
*   Shows that the approach produces diverse and potentially interpretable solutions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty is incremental; the work builds directly on existing MCL formulations.
*   The performance improvements are modest and the comparison to strong baselines is lacking.
*   The technical explanation could be more thorough, particularly regarding the advantages of sMCL over alternative multi-task learning approaches.
*   The writing style could be clearer and more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable contribution, but it is not a groundbreaking advancement. The proposed method offers a practical solution to a relevant problem, but the limited performance gains and lack of compelling comparison to strong baselines raise concerns. I recommend **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding baseline comparisons and clarifying the technical novelty of their approach. The authors should also consider adding an ablation study and a discussion of computational cost.
