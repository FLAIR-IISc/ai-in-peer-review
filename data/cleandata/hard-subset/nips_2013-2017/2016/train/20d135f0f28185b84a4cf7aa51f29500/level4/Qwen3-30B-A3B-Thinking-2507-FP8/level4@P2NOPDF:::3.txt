

This paper addresses multiple choice learning (MCL) for deep neural networks, proposing a simple yet effective approach that integrates learner training with the assignment problem via a "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer during backpropagation. Current MCL methods fail to generalize to deep networks, and this work demonstrates that the proposed layer enables seamless ensemble training without requiring complex modifications. Experiments validate the method across three distinct tasks: image classification, image segmentation, and image captioning, showing consistent performance gains.

**Strengths:**  
The problem of extending MCL to deep learning is highly relevant to the NIPS community, given the growing adoption of ensemble methods in complex vision and language tasks. The proposed solution exhibits notable conceptual simplicity—requiring only a minor modification to standard backpropagation. Furthermore, the evaluation spans three diverse application domains, providing robust evidence of the method’s versatility and effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**  
The paper’s central claim hinges on "diversity," emphasized in both the title and discussion, yet the model lacks any explicit mechanism to enforce diversity among learners. This disconnect is a significant concern: the authors motivate diversity as a key contribution but ultimately rely on implicit diversity from standard training, which is neither novel nor rigorously justified. This misalignment undermines a core premise of the work. Additionally, the contribution appears incremental relative to Guzman et al.’s relaxation approach for MCL, as the proposed layer merely adapts their framework rather than introducing a fundamentally new principle.

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- The abstract’s first sentence is grammatically awkward and should be rephrased for clarity.  
- The emphasis on "diversity" in the title and text should be moderated to align with the model’s actual design.  
- Line 108: Correct "f" to "g" in "we fixed the form of g..."  
- Line 115: Remove the extraneous period mid-sentence.  

**Key Question:**  
For the baseline MCL approach using deep networks, how did the authors ensure each individual learner achieved reasonable convergence *before* early termination? Early cutoffs could substantially bias the ensemble’s performance if learners are not sufficiently trained, and this critical detail is not addressed.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
While the method is technically sound and empirically validated across multiple tasks, the lack of explicit diversity mechanisms and the incremental nature of the contribution limit its impact. The work would benefit from a more honest framing of its relationship to prior MCL relaxation techniques and a clearer justification for the absence of diversity enforcement. The experimental results are compelling, but the theoretical and conceptual contributions are weaker than the title and narrative suggest. Given the importance of the problem domain, this paper has merit but requires significant revision to address the diversity misalignment and clarify its novelty.
