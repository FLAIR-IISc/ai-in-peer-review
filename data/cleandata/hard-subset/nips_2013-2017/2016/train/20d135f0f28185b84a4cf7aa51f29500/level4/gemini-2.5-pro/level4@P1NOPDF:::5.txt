The authors propose a method to generate multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks. This is achieved by integrating deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm and directly minimizing the oracle loss of the ensemble. To accomplish this, they introduce a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method. The proposed optimization is notable as it can be easily implemented within the standard back-propagation framework, allowing the entire model to be trained end-to-end. Experiments on three tasks, evaluated using the oracle metric, demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, which is particularly useful for applications involving user interaction.

While the approach is promising, I have several concerns that should be addressed:

1.  **Importance of Initialization:** A key concern is the role of model initialization. The paper does not explore how performance is affected if the models are initialized with random values instead of from a pre-trained network. An ablation study on this aspect is needed to understand the method's robustness and dependence on pre-training.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental settings for the baseline comparisons lack sufficient detail. For a fair evaluation, the authors must clarify whether the baseline methods were also initialized using the same pre-trained network as the proposed sMCL method. This information is crucial for interpreting the reported performance gains.

3.  **Evaluation Metrics and Diversity:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on oracle accuracy. While informative, this metric does not capture the diversity of the ensemble members, which is a primary motivation for such methods. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including an analysis and quantitative evaluation of the diversity of the generated outputs.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The authors have overlooked a highly relevant prior work that explores a very similar idea. They are strongly encouraged to discuss and compare their method to the following paper:
    *   Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra. "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks." CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
    Addressing the relationship between the proposed method and this existing work is crucial for properly positioning the paper's contribution within the field.
