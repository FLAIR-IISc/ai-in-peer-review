This paper proposes a straightforward solution to the multiple choice learning problem when applied to deep neural networks. Existing approaches to MCL do not readily extend to deep learning architectures. This paper demonstrates that a simple “winner-takes-the-gradient” layer during backpropagation effectively combines learner training with the assignment problem within an ensemble framework. Experimental results demonstrate strong performance across a variety of tasks – image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem of MCL for deep learning is significant and relevant to the NIPS community.
*   The proposed method’s simplicity is appealing.
*   The experiments demonstrate results on three distinct tasks, showcasing the method's breadth.

**Weaknesses:**

*   My primary concern lies with the paper’s extensive motivation around “diversity” (reflected even in the title), while the model itself doesn’t explicitly enforce it. I was initially eager to see how the authors integrated a diversity term into their model, and was disappointed to find that no such term exists. The emphasis on diversity feels misleading given the implementation.
*   The proposed solution represents an incremental improvement over the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al. While valuable, the contribution feels somewhat limited in scope.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   The first sentence of the abstract could be rephrased for improved clarity.
*   The discussion of “diversity” should be toned down to better reflect the model’s capabilities.
*   Line 108: The first instance of “f” should be “g” in the phrase “we fixed the form of…”.
*   Line 115: Remove the extraneous period mid-sentence. 

**Question for the Authors:**

For the baseline MCL experiments with deep learning, how did you ensure each network had converged to a reasonable solution? Prematurely terminating training of the learners could significantly impact the ensemble’s overall performance.
