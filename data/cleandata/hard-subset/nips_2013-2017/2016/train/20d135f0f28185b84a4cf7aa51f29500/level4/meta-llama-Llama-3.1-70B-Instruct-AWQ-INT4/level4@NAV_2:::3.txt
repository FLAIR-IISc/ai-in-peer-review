This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach for training diverse deep ensembles. The authors pose the task of producing multiple outputs as a learning problem over an ensemble of deep networks, aiming to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle. This work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, which seeks to learn a mapping that produces M solutions to minimize oracle loss.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of MCL for deep learning is relevant to the NIPS community. The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, making it a valuable contribution. The experimental results demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles across various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to generalize to different deep architectures and loss functions, making it a versatile approach. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, showcasing the emergence of interpretable expertise among ensemble members.

However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's emphasis on "diversity." While the authors extensively motivate the need for diversity, the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. This might be seen as a limitation, as the paper's title and introduction suggest a stronger focus on diversity.

Additionally, the proposed solution can be seen as an incremental step, building upon the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al. It would be beneficial to discuss the relationship between sMCL and other ensemble learning methods, highlighting the unique contributions of this approach.

Minor suggestions for improvement include rephrasing the first sentence of the abstract, toning down the emphasis on diversity, and correcting minor errors in the text (e.g., line 108, where "f" should be "g").

One question that arises is how the authors ensured that each of the networks in the baseline MCL with deep learning converged to reasonable results. Cutting the learners early on might significantly affect the ensemble performance, and it would be helpful to clarify this point.

In terms of the NIPS criteria, the paper scores well on quality, as the proposed method is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is also clear and well-organized, making it easy to follow. The originality of the approach is a significant strength, as it provides a novel solution to the MCL problem for deep learning. Finally, the significance of the paper is high, as it has the potential to impact various applications in computer vision and machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and simple solution to the MCL problem for deep learning.
* The experimental results demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's emphasis on diversity might be seen as misleading, as the model does not explicitly enforce diversity.
* The proposed solution can be seen as an incremental step, building upon existing work.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is a valuable contribution to the field. With some minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact at NIPS.
