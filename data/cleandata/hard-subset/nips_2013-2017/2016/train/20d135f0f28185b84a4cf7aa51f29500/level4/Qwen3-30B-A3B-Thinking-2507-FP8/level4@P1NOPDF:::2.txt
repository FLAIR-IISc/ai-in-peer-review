

The paper introduces a novel method for training ensembles of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) or other gradient-based models, leveraging an oracle that identifies the optimal ensemble member for each training instance. During training, backpropagation is applied exclusively to the network producing the selected output, thereby encouraging specialization across the ensemble. This approach effectively covers a broad range of target outputs, as demonstrated across multiple domains with strong empirical results. The core idea is well-motivated and clearly presented, with the method demonstrating effective specialization in classification tasks. The paper's structure is logical, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering diverse datasets and tasks. Overall, this represents a solid contribution to ensemble learning that merits publication.

However, the paper misses a significant opportunity to address how the trained ensemble should be utilized in practice. While the training procedure is thoroughly described and evaluated, the paper does not elaborate on the practical deployment of the resulting ensemble. Specifically, it merely states that the ensemble provides a "broad pool of possible results" and notes consistency with top-k evaluation metrics (e.g., for ImageNet), but offers no concrete guidance on ensemble combination strategies, inference protocols, or how the specialization translates to real-world performance gains. This omission is critical, as the primary value of an ensemble lies in its operational use, not just its training. Without this, the proposed method remains largely theoretical, limiting its immediate applicability. Addressing this would have elevated the work from strong to outstanding, as it is a necessary step for practical adoption.

A minor but notable issue concerns the presentation of Algorithm 2. The pseudocode is provided as a raster image embedded within the figure, rather than as editable text or a vector-based graphic. This makes the algorithm difficult to reference precisely and hinders reproducibility. The authors should present the pseudocode directly in the text or ensure the figure is rendered in a vector format.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing a principled method for training specialized DNN ensembles via oracle-guided backpropagation. The technical approach is sound, the results are compelling, and the writing is clear. The sole major shortcoming is the lack of discussion on ensemble usage post-training, which is a significant gap for a practical methodology. Despite this, the core contribution is substantial and well-executed, making it suitable for publication. The missing element is relatively domain-specific and could be addressed in future work, but its inclusion would have strengthened the paper considerably. The overall quality remains high, and the work deserves acceptance.
