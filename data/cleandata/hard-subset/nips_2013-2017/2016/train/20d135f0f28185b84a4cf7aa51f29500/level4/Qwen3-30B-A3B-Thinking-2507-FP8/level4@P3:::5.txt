

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that minimize oracle loss. The authors extend the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8, 9] to deep networks by introducing a stochastic gradient descent-based optimization method that enables concurrent training of ensemble members. Unlike previous MCL approaches requiring costly retraining [8, 9], sMCL uses a "winner-take-gradient" strategy where during backpropagation, only the gradient of the lowest-error predictor for each example is propagated. The method is architecture-agnostic, parameter-free, and achieves significant improvements over baselines across multiple tasks including image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO).

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it successfully generalizes MCL to deep learning, addressing a gap in the literature where previous methods [8, 9, 5] were not practical for modern deep architectures due to sequential or retraining requirements. Second, the authors demonstrate that sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members without explicit design—e.g., in classification, different ensemble members become specialists for distinct classes (Figure 4c), while in segmentation, members develop expertise in specific object classes or segment shapes (Figure 5b). Third, the method achieves consistent performance gains over baselines (6-10% absolute improvement in oracle metrics) while being 5× faster than MCL. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, covering diverse architectures and tasks, and the oracle metric provides a clear evaluation framework for multiple-output systems.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical details to reproduce results. The connection to prior work is appropriately contextualized, though the related work section could better distinguish the novelty of sMCL from recent ensemble methods. The authors acknowledge the relationship to Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL [8, 9] and Dey et al.'s boosting-like approach [5], but miss a key related work: Lee et al. (2015) [1] propose "Why M Heads Are Better than One" which also trains diverse deep ensembles using a similar "winner-take-all" gradient strategy. The authors should explicitly compare with this work, as it directly addresses the same problem space. Without this comparison, the paper's novelty claim is weakened, as the core idea of using gradient-based specialization appears in Lee et al.

The paper has several strengths in quality and significance. The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical justification (Equation 2) and strong empirical validation across three distinct domains. The significance is high for practical applications where multiple hypotheses are valuable (e.g., autonomous systems, assistive technologies), as the method produces interpretable representations of ambiguity (Figure 1). The method's simplicity and broad applicability (agnostic to architecture/loss) further enhance its significance.

However, the paper has two critical weaknesses. First, the authors do not sufficiently address the role of initialization. The experiments use pre-trained networks for initialization (e.g., "sMCL models are initialized from a standard ensemble trained for 50 epochs"), but it's unclear how sensitive the method is to initialization. The draft review rightly questions: What if models are initialized with random values instead of pre-trained networks? If the method requires pre-training, this limits its applicability to settings without pre-trained models. The authors should test random initialization and report results to demonstrate the method's robustness.

Second, the paper lacks analysis of ensemble diversity beyond oracle metrics. While oracle accuracy measures the quality of the best output, it doesn't capture how diverse the ensemble outputs are. For instance, in image captioning (Figure 7), the standard ensemble produces identical sentences across members, while sMCL generates diverse captions. However, the paper doesn't quantify this diversity (e.g., using metrics like n-gram overlap or entropy) or analyze whether diversity correlates with oracle performance. This is a significant omission, as the paper's core claim is about generating diverse solutions.

The comparison with baselines is also unclear. The paper states that "MCL performance drops substantially on segmentation and captioning tasks" but doesn't specify whether MCL and sMCL used identical initialization (e.g., same pre-trained models). If sMCL benefits from better initialization, this would confound the performance comparison. The authors should clarify initialization protocols for all methods.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and practically useful method for training diverse deep ensembles. The sMCL algorithm is simple, effective, and generalizable. The main weaknesses—lack of initialization analysis, insufficient diversity metrics, and omission of Lee et al. (2015)—are significant enough to warrant major revisions. If addressed, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

Recommendation: Major Revision. The paper has strong technical content and significant practical value, but requires clarification on initialization sensitivity, additional diversity analysis, and comparison with Lee et al. (2015). With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
