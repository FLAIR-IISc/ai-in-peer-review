

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for training ensemble models, extending the framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The authors build upon their work, which defines the ensemble loss as the minimum loss across individual classifiers, thereby promoting diversity among ensemble members. Their key contribution is adapting this ensemble approach to SGD, enabling efficient training for deep neural networks—a significant practical advancement over prior batch-based methods. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and demonstrates promising empirical results across multiple datasets.  

The primary concern centers on the novelty of the proposed method relative to [8]. While the core ensemble loss formulation is inherited, the authors convincingly argue that the shift to SGD—coupled with minor modifications to handle mini-batch updates—substantially broadens applicability to large-scale deep learning tasks. This practical utility, rather than theoretical novelty, constitutes the paper’s strongest contribution. The authors effectively address potential objections by demonstrating scalability and performance gains in experiments, making the incremental nature of the change justified. Given this, I find the contribution meaningful and support acceptance.  

To further strengthen the work, two specific improvements are recommended. First, the paper should directly compare the proposed SGD-based ensemble method with MCL (Minimum Classification Loss) under batch-based training. The original MCL formulation in [8] relies on full-batch optimization, so evaluating its performance with mini-batches would clarify whether the authors’ SGD adaptation provides a clear advantage over a straightforward extension of MCL. This comparison would also shed light on the algorithm’s computational efficiency and generalizability.  

Second, the paper lacks a rigorous analysis of the "diversity" effect—central to the ensemble design. While the authors note that their method promotes diversity, they do not quantify it or correlate it with empirical gains. For instance, metrics such as disagreement error, entropy of predictions, or pairwise similarity between ensemble members should be reported alongside performance. Additionally, ablation studies isolating the impact of diversity (e.g., by comparing with a non-diverse baseline) would better validate the claimed benefits. Without such analysis, the connection between the proposed loss formulation and improved ensemble performance remains somewhat speculative.  

The experimental section is generally solid but could be enhanced by including convergence speed comparisons between SGD and full-batch variants, as well as a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., batch size, learning rate). These additions would provide deeper insight into the algorithm’s practical trade-offs.  

Overall, this is a strong submission that addresses a relevant challenge in ensemble learning with a practical, scalable solution. The minor modifications to [8] are well-justified by their real-world applicability to deep networks, and the paper’s clarity and empirical rigor make it a valuable contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions—particularly the MCL batch comparison and diversity analysis—the work would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
