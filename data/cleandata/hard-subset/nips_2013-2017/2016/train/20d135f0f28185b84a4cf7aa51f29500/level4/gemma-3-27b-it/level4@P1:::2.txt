The paper proposes a novel method for training ensembles of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) – or any gradient-based approach – by leveraging an oracle that identifies the optimal ensemble member for each training instance and applying backpropagation only to that network’s weights. This approach encourages specialization among network members, resulting in ensembles that comprehensively cover a wide range of possible outputs. The method is evaluated across diverse domains and demonstrates strong performance. Overall, this is a strong paper presenting an interesting idea for training DNN ensembles, where an oracle guides backpropagation updates to the network with the best output (according to the ground truth label). The results suggest that this approach effectively enforces specialization, particularly in a classification setting. The presentation is clear and the experiments are conducted on several datasets. This work represents a significant contribution and is deserving of publication. 

I would have considered this paper outstanding had it further addressed the exploitation of the trained DNN ensembles. Currently, the focus is on training with the oracle, but the practical use of the resulting ensemble isn’t fully developed beyond stating it can provide a diverse pool of possible results and aligning with top-k metrics (e.g., ImageNet). Developing this aspect would enhance the practicality of the proposed approach. Nevertheless, the current contribution is substantial; the missing component is relatively problem-specific.

A minor issue: Figure 2 presents pseudo-code as raster images embedded in the paper. It would be preferable to include the pseudo-code directly within the manuscript, or at least embed the figure as a vector image for better readability and scalability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of using an oracle to guide gradient descent during ensemble training is novel and addresses a practical problem in scenarios where multiple plausible solutions are desirable.
*   **Strong Performance:** The method demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing baselines across various tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning).
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental results.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work provides a solid theoretical basis for Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) and connects it to existing work on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL).
*   **Emergent Specialization:** The experiments show that sMCL effectively induces specialization among ensemble members, leading to more diverse and informative predictions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Ensemble Use:** The paper focuses primarily on the training process and lacks a detailed discussion of how the resulting ensembles are best utilized in practice. Further investigation into inference strategies and downstream applications would strengthen the work.
*   **Figure Quality:** The inclusion of rasterized pseudo-code in Figure 2 detracts slightly from the paper’s overall quality.
