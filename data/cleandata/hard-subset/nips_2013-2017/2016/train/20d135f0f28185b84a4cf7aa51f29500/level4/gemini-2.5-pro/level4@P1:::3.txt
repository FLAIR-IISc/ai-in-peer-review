This paper addresses the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in the context of deep networks. The goal of MCL is to train an ensemble of models to produce a set of diverse, high-quality predictions for a given input, such that at least one of the predictions is close to the ground truth. While prior MCL methods exist, their iterative training procedures—which often involve training models to convergence multiple times—are computationally infeasible for modern deep architectures.

The authors propose a simple and effective solution called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The core idea is to integrate the data assignment and model update steps of MCL directly into stochastic gradient descent. This is achieved through a "winner-takes-the-gradient" strategy: for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that produced the lowest loss. This allows all models in the ensemble to be trained concurrently in a single end-to-end process. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across a wide range of applications, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing improved oracle performance over several baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevance and Significance:** The problem of generating multiple plausible hypotheses is of significant practical importance, especially for ambiguous or structured prediction tasks where a single "best" answer may not exist or may be difficult to find. Adapting the MCL framework for efficient use with deep learning is a valuable and timely contribution to the NIPS community.
*   **Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed sMCL method is laudably simple. The "winner-takes-the-gradient" mechanism is intuitive, easy to implement within existing deep learning frameworks, parameter-free, and seamlessly integrates into the standard SGD training pipeline.
*   **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The paper's empirical evaluation is a key strength. By successfully applying sMCL to three distinct and challenging tasks (classification, segmentation, and captioning) using different architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN), the authors convincingly demonstrate the method's general applicability and robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Disconnect Between Framing and Mechanism:** My primary concern lies with the paper's framing. The title and introduction place a strong emphasis on generating "diverse" solutions. This led me to expect a model that explicitly enforces diversity, for instance, through a repulsion term in the loss function or a similar mechanism. However, the method only minimizes the oracle loss, with diversity emerging as a by-product of the models specializing on different parts of the data distribution. While the resulting specialization is interesting, the lack of an explicit diversity-enforcing component feels at odds with the paper's central narrative. Clarifying that diversity is an *emergent property* of the optimization rather than an explicit objective would make the paper's claims more precise.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the adaptation of MCL to a stochastic, end-to-end training regime is a crucial step for practical application with deep networks, the core idea can be seen as a stochastic online variant of the block coordinate descent algorithm proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The conceptual step from their formulation to the proposed "winner-takes-gradient" rule is relatively direct. As such, the main contribution is more an engineering and practical one—enabling MCL for deep learning—than a fundamental reformulation of the learning objective itself.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  For the baseline MCL comparison, could you please provide more detail on the training protocol for the deep network ensembles? Given that each meta-iteration requires training networks to convergence, how was "convergence" defined at each step? Terminating the training of individual models prematurely could significantly impair the ensemble's performance, so it is important to understand how this strong baseline was implemented.

2.  The writing in the abstract could be tightened. The first sentence, for example, is slightly convoluted and could be rewritten for greater clarity and impact.

3.  As mentioned above, I recommend toning down the claims about "diversity" in the title and framing to better reflect that it is an emergent outcome of minimizing the oracle loss, rather than an explicit objective of the algorithm.
