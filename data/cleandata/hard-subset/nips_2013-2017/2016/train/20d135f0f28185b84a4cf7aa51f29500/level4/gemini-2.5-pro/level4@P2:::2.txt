**Summary**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and intuitive method for training an ensemble of deep networks. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss" using a "winner-take-gradient" strategy. During stochastic gradient descent, for each training instance, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only to the single member that achieved the lowest loss. This approach encourages the individual networks within the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution, thereby producing a diverse set of high-quality predictions.

The work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al., but adapts it for modern deep learning. Unlike prior MCL methods that rely on costly alternating optimization (requiring full retraining of models), sMCL integrates the selection step directly into the SGD training loop, allowing all ensemble members to be trained concurrently and efficiently. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), using appropriate deep architectures for each.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed sMCL method is simple, elegant, and highly effective. It presents a practical and efficient solution to the problem of training diverse ensembles by directly optimizing the oracle loss. This is a significant advance over previous methods (e.g., MCL, boosting-style approaches) which are computationally infeasible for deep networks that can take weeks to train. The idea of letting specializations emerge automatically as a consequence of the training objective is a powerful one.

2.  **Technical Quality and Thorough Evaluation:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments across a variety of domains and network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). The method consistently and significantly outperforms relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles and prior work by Dey et al. The analysis, particularly the visualization of emergent class-specialization in the classification task, provides compelling evidence that the method works as intended.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to understand. The motivation for producing multiple diverse outputs is clearly explained with strong illustrative examples (e.g., the horse-cow ambiguity in segmentation). The algorithm is presented clearly, and its simplicity is a major advantage.

4.  **Broad Applicability:** A key strength of sMCL is its generality. It is agnostic to the specific network architecture and the task-dependent loss function, and it is parameter-free. This makes it a widely applicable tool that can be easily integrated into many existing deep learning pipelines.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practical Exploitation at Inference Time:** The primary weakness of the paper is the limited discussion on how to use the resulting ensemble in a practical scenario where an oracle is not available. The training paradigm is designed to produce a strong set of candidate solutions for an oracle to choose from, which aligns well with evaluation metrics like top-k accuracy or human-in-the-loop systems. However, the paper does not explore how one might select the best prediction or combine the diverse outputs at test time for a standard prediction task. Addressing this "downstream" problem of re-ranking or selecting from the diverse hypothesis set would make the contribution more complete and immediately useful for a broader range of applications. While this selection mechanism might be problem-specific, some general discussion or preliminary experiments would have strengthened the paper considerably.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and practical contribution to the field of ensemble learning for deep networks. The idea is simple yet powerful, the experimental validation is thorough, and the results are impressive. While the lack of a proposed mechanism for using the ensemble at inference time without an oracle is a notable limitation, the contribution in training diverse ensembles is significant on its own. The work is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to inspire follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
