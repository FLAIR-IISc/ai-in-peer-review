### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem in the context of deep learning. The goal of MCL is to train an ensemble of models to produce a set of diverse yet plausible hypotheses for a given input, such that the loss of the best hypothesis (the "oracle loss") is minimized. The authors argue that prior methods for MCL, such as the block coordinate descent approach of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], are computationally infeasible for modern deep networks, as they require training each ensemble member to convergence multiple times.

The core contribution is a novel training algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). This method adapts the MCL objective for training with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is simple yet effective: for each example in a mini-batch, the forward pass is computed for all ensemble members, but the backward pass and subsequent gradient update are only performed for the single "winning" model that produced the lowest loss for that example. This "winner-takes-the-gradient" strategy elegantly interleaves the data assignment and model update steps of traditional MCL into a single, parallel training process. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and broad applicability of sMCL across three distinct tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on Pascal VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO, showing significant improvements in oracle performance over independently trained ensembles and other baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The problem of generating multiple high-quality predictions is highly relevant for many real-world applications where a perception module interacts with a human user or another verification component. Effectively adapting the MCL paradigm to the deep learning setting is a valuable and timely contribution for the NIPS community.

2.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL method is laudable for its simplicity and elegance. It is architecture-agnostic, loss-function-agnostic, and parameter-free, making it a general-purpose tool that can be easily integrated into existing deep learning pipelines. The core idea is intuitive and straightforward to implement.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation (Quality):** The paper provides strong and convincing experimental validation across three different domains and network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). This demonstrates the versatility of the approach. The comparisons to relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles, the original MCL algorithm, and a more recent boosting-style approach [5], are comprehensive and clearly establish the superiority of sMCL in terms of both oracle performance and computational efficiency (e.g., a 5x speedup over MCL). The qualitative results and analysis of emergent specialization are also insightful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Framing versus Technical Contribution (Clarity & Originality):** My main concern is a subtle disconnect between the paper's framing and its technical substance. The title and introduction place a strong emphasis on generating "diverse" ensembles, which might lead a reader to expect an explicit diversity-promoting mechanism within the model or loss function. However, the sMCL algorithm does not directly enforce or optimize for diversity. Instead, diversity appears as an emergent property resulting from the individual models specializing to different subsets of the data distribution, driven purely by the minimization of the oracle loss. While the outcome is positive and interesting, the paper would be clearer if it framed the contribution more precisely as an efficient optimization technique for the oracle loss, from which diversity naturally arises, rather than a method for explicitly promoting diversity.

2.  **Incremental Nature of the Contribution (Originality):** While the practical impact is significant, the core conceptual contribution can be viewed as an incremental step from the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The paper effectively translates their block coordinate descent algorithm into a stochastic, online version suitable for SGD. This is a crucial engineering and optimization insight that makes MCL practical for deep networks, but it does not represent a fundamental reformulation of the MCL problem itself. The innovation lies more in the "how" (an efficient SGD-based procedure) than the "what" (the MCL objective).

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   The abstract's first sentence ("Many practical perception systems exist within larger processes that include interactions with users or additional components capable of evaluating the quality of predicted solutions.") is a bit verbose. It could be rephrased for greater impact.
-   The emphasis on "diversity" throughout the paper could be toned down slightly in favor of focusing on the direct optimization of the oracle loss, which is the actual mechanism at play.
-   Line 108: In the phrase "we fix the form of f to be...", the 'f' should likely be 'g' to remain consistent with the notation established in the preceding sentences (`g : X → Y^M`).
-   Line 115: There is an extraneous period in the middle of a sentence.

**Question for the authors:** The comparison with the original MCL [8] is a critical baseline. For the MCL experiments with deep networks, how did you ensure that each network had converged sufficiently in each meta-iteration of the block coordinate descent procedure? Training deep models to full convergence is time-consuming, and terminating the training of an ensemble member prematurely could significantly impair its performance, which in turn would negatively affect the subsequent data assignment step and the overall performance of the MCL baseline. Further details on this experimental choice would strengthen the comparison.
