Summary
The paper proposes a simple and general method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks (or other gradient-based models) using an “oracle” during training: for each training instance, only the ensemble member with the best output (as measured against the true label) receives the backpropagation update. This hard assignment encourages members to specialize, yielding an ensemble that collectively covers a broad set of outputs or modes. The approach is clearly presented, and empirical results across several domains are strong.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and generality: The training rule is model-agnostic and easy to implement on top of standard backpropagation.
- Specialization via hard assignment: Using the oracle to route gradients to the best-performing member promotes diversity without explicit diversity regularizers.
- Empirical breadth: Results on multiple domains show consistent improvements and suggest the method is robust.
- Good presentation quality: The paper is well written overall and the core idea is communicated effectively.

Main weakness / missing piece
- Inference-time exploitation under-specified: The method relies on an oracle during training to select the “best” model per example, but the paper provides limited guidance on how to use the resulting ensemble at test time when no oracle is available. Beyond noting that the ensemble provides a broad pool of candidates and aligns with top-k metrics (e.g., ImageNet), the deployment strategy is not developed. This omission limits practical usability for applications that require a single prediction, low latency, or calibrated probabilities.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Specify and evaluate test-time strategies:
  - Selection: Train a lightweight gating network or use features from the ensemble to predict which member to trust per instance; compare to uniform selection.
  - Combination: Evaluate averaging, logit/score fusion, or stacking; discuss calibration effects.
  - Top-k scenarios: For tasks where top-k is meaningful, report both top-1 and top-k to show trade-offs.
  - Distillation: Explore distilling the ensemble into a single model to address deployment constraints.
- Analyze specialization and load balancing:
  - Report the distribution of assignments across members over training and data subsets to verify genuine specialization and avoid collapse (e.g., one model dominating).
  - Include mechanisms (if any) to prevent early monopolization, such as tie-breaking rules, temperature or exploration during early training.
  - Provide examples illustrating complementary error profiles among members.
- Clarify definition of “best output”:
  - Precisely define the oracle’s criterion (e.g., minimal per-example loss). Specify tie-breaking and how this extends to regression or structured outputs.
- Compare to related paradigms:
  - Mixture-of-Experts with hard gating (hard EM), Boosting, and standard ensembling (independent training) are closely related. A direct empirical and conceptual comparison would situate the contribution and clarify when hard oracle-based assignment offers advantages.
  - Compare with snapshot ensembles or cyclic learning rate ensembles under a matched compute budget.
- Ablations and scalability:
  - Vary ensemble size to show scaling and diminishing returns.
  - Report training/inference cost, memory footprint, and wall-clock time. Ensure comparisons control for total compute so gains are not solely due to increased capacity or training time.
  - Test robustness to class imbalance and noisy labels; the oracle may amplify label noise by rewarding overfitting members.
- Practical guidance:
  - Provide heuristics for choosing ensemble size, learning rates, and initialization diversity.
  - Discuss failure modes and tasks where specialization is less beneficial.

Minor presentation issues
- Pseudo-code formatting: Figure 2 is embedded as a raster image. Please typeset the pseudo-code directly in the manuscript or embed a vector graphic to ensure readability and reproducibility.
- Reproducibility: Include details on hyperparameters, training schedules, and random seeds; releasing code would further strengthen the work.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution. The central idea—routing gradients to the per-example winner to induce specialization—is elegant and broadly applicable, and the reported results are compelling. The primary limitation is the lack of a fully specified test-time exploitation strategy, which is crucial for practical deployment beyond top-k evaluation settings. Addressing inference (selection/combination/distillation), situating the method relative to hard-gated mixture-of-experts and standard ensembles under matched compute, and providing analysis of specialization dynamics would elevate the paper from strong to outstanding. Even in its current form, the contribution is clear and meaningful, and I recommend acceptance.
