

This paper extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8] to deep networks via Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), addressing a critical bottleneck in prior work. While Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] proposed an iterative alternating optimization for MCL that requires costly retraining of ensemble members (infeasible for deep networks), the authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based approach that trains ensemble members concurrently. This formulation is the first practical method for training diverse deep ensembles, eliminating the need for repeated model retraining while achieving superior oracle performance across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks. The work directly builds upon and significantly advances NIPS 2016 MCL research [8] and recent ensemble work (e.g., Hinton et al. [12] at NIPS 2015), but uniquely solves the scalability problem for modern deep architectures.

**Quality**: The technical contribution is sound and well-validated. The paper provides rigorous experiments across three diverse tasks (CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, MSCOCO captioning) demonstrating sMCL outperforms classical ensembles, MCL [8], and Dey et al. [5] by 6–10% on oracle metrics, with a 5× speedup over MCL. The training algorithm (Algorithm 2) is carefully derived from the oracle loss objective, and ablation studies (e.g., varying *k* in the assignment step) robustly support the claim that specialization emerges naturally. The experimental setup is thorough, with appropriate baselines and clear metrics (e.g., oracle accuracy, IoU).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation (Section 3), algorithmic details (Algorithm 2), and experimental analysis (Sections 4.1–4.3) are logically structured and accessible. The qualitative examples (Figures 4c, 5b, 6, 7) effectively illustrate ensemble specialization and interpretability, making the technical contributions tangible for readers.

**Originality**: This work makes a significant and novel contribution by enabling MCL for deep networks via sMCL. While [8] introduced the MCL paradigm, their approach is intractable for deep learning due to retraining overhead. sMCL’s stochastic optimization strategy is a clean, parameter-free solution that is agnostic to architecture and loss function—addressing a key gap in prior NIPS work on ensembles [12, 19, 21, 26]. The emergence of interpretable expertise (e.g., class-specific specialists in Figure 4c) is a compelling new insight not present in earlier methods.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate clear practical impact. Systems requiring multiple hypotheses (e.g., autonomous vehicles, assistive tools) benefit directly from the lower oracle error and interpretable ambiguity handling. The method’s simplicity, broad applicability, and speedup over MCL make it highly relevant for real-world deployment, advancing the state of the art in ensemble learning for structured prediction tasks.

**Strengths**: (1) Solves a critical scalability barrier for MCL in deep learning; (2) Achieves consistent gains across diverse tasks; (3) Reveals emergent specialization as a natural outcome of training; (4) Simple implementation with no additional hyperparameters.

**Weaknesses**: (1) The paper does not quantitatively measure *diversity* beyond oracle performance (e.g., via pairwise disagreement metrics or mutual information between ensemble outputs); this is a notable gap given the paper’s emphasis on diversity. (2) A direct comparison of MCL [8] using mini-batch updates (instead of full-dataset retraining) would strengthen the argument for sMCL’s efficiency, as the draft reviewer suggests.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:  
*Pro*: Novel, impactful solution to a significant practical problem; technically rigorous; well-motivated; outperforms strong baselines with 5× speedup.  
*Con*: Limited diversity analysis; MCL batch comparison would add clarity.  

**Decision**: Accept. The paper’s contribution is substantive, well-executed, and directly addresses a gap in NIPS 2016-era MCL work [8] with immediate applicability to deep learning systems. The weaknesses are minor and addressable in future work; the strengths far outweigh them.
