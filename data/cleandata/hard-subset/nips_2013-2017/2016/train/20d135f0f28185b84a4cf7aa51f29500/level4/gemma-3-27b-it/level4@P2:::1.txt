This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for training diverse ensembles of deep networks. The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which aims to minimize the loss of the best-performing classifier within an ensemble. Their key contribution lies in adapting this approach for deep learning via SGD, enabling efficient training and practical application. 

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details of sMCL. It effectively connects the proposed method to previous work in ensemble learning and structured prediction, particularly highlighting the limitations of prior approaches like MCL in the context of modern deep architectures. The experiments demonstrate sMCL's effectiveness across a range of tasks – image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning – utilizing different network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNNs). The results consistently show sMCL outperforming baseline ensembles and other related methods. 

**Strengths:**

* **Practicality:** sMCL addresses a significant limitation of previous MCL methods [8] by enabling efficient training of deep ensembles using SGD, making it feasible for large-scale applications.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as agnostic to both network architecture and loss function, increasing its broad applicability.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines on diverse tasks, supporting the efficacy of the proposed approach.
* **Interpretability:** The analysis of ensemble member specialization provides insights into how sMCL promotes diversity and captures ambiguity in the data. The visualizations of segmentation and captioning results are compelling.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-structured and explains complex concepts in an accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea builds directly upon Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], and the algorithmic modification (applying SGD) feels somewhat incremental. While the authors convincingly argue this change drastically improves practicality, a more detailed discussion of the theoretical differences would strengthen the contribution.
* **Ablation/Comparison with Batched MCL:** A direct comparison between sMCL and the original MCL algorithm when using mini-batches (rather than the full dataset) would provide valuable insight into the impact of the SGD adaptation and solidify the claim that the change is essential for deep learning.
* **Diversity Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates the emergent specialization of ensemble members, a more quantitative analysis of the diversity of generated outputs (e.g., using metrics like n-gram diversity or semantic similarity) would further strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practical limitation of existing methods for training diverse ensembles.
* The empirical results are strong and consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL.
* The method is generally applicable and easily implementable.
* The analysis of ensemble member specialization provides valuable insights.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The core algorithmic contribution is somewhat incremental to prior work.
* The lack of a direct comparison with batched MCL limits a full understanding of the impact of the SGD adaptation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the incremental nature of the algorithmic contribution, the significant practical improvements and strong empirical results make this paper a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. The authors convincingly demonstrate that sMCL enables the training of diverse deep ensembles in a scalable and effective manner. I recommend acceptance.
