This paper proposes a simple approach to the multiple-choice learning (MCL) problem with deep networks. Prior MCL formulations do not directly extend to deep architectures. The authors introduce a winner-takes-the-gradient mechanism during backpropagation that couples learner training with the assignment problem in an ensemble setting. Experiments on image classification, image segmentation, and captioning indicate strong performance across a wide range of tasks.

Strengths:
- The problem of bringing MCL to deep learning is important and relevant to the NeurIPS community.
- The proposed method is appealingly simple.
- The paper evaluates on three distinct tasks, demonstrating breadth.

Weaknesses:
- My main concern is the heavy emphasis on “diversity” (including in the title) without any explicit mechanism to encourage diversity in the ensemble. I expected a concrete diversity term or constraint and was disappointed to find none; the method relies on implicit differentiation through the winner-takes-the-gradient scheme, which does not guarantee diverse outputs.
- The contribution feels incremental relative to the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is technically new beyond that relaxation and why it matters empirically or theoretically.

Minor suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract should be rewritten for clarity and impact.
- Tone down the claims around “diversity,” or add an explicit method or analysis demonstrating that the approach induces diversity.
- Line 108: the first “f” should be “g” in the phrase “we fixed the form of …”.
- Line 115: there is an extra period in the middle of a sentence.

Question:
- For the deep-learning baseline of MCL, how did you ensure that each network converged to a reasonable solution? Early termination of individual learners could significantly degrade ensemble performance. Please clarify the training protocol (e.g., number of epochs, early stopping criteria, learning-rate schedules) and any measures taken to ensure each component model had sufficient capacity and training time to reach a good local optimum before evaluating the ensemble.
