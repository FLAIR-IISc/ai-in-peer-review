This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for training ensemble models, extending the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The core idea, building upon [8], focuses on minimizing the loss of the *best* output from the ensemble – effectively encouraging diversity among the constituent classifiers. The authors’ key contribution lies in adapting this approach for efficient training with SGD, enabling its application to deep neural networks, which was a limitation of the original method. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings. The motivation for developing an SGD-based approach is convincingly presented, highlighting the impracticality of using the original method with large datasets and deep learning models. The experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed algorithm, showcasing competitive or superior performance compared to existing ensemble methods on the tested datasets.

My primary concern revolves around the incremental nature of the novelty relative to Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. While the application of SGD to this specific ensemble loss function is a significant practical improvement, the fundamental concept of maximizing the minimum output remains largely unchanged. However, the authors successfully argue that this seemingly small change unlocks the applicability of the method to significantly larger and more complex models, particularly deep neural networks. This justification, combined with the empirical validation presented, ultimately leads me to favor acceptance.

Several avenues for future work could further strengthen this contribution. A particularly interesting comparison would be to evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithm against the MCL algorithm (from [8]) when utilizing mini-batch SGD, rather than processing the entire dataset at once. This would provide a more direct assessment of the benefits of the authors' SGD adaptation. 

Furthermore, while the paper demonstrates the improved performance of the ensemble, a more in-depth analysis of the “diversity” aspect would be beneficial. Specifically, quantifying the diversity of the learned classifiers – perhaps through measures of disagreement or feature space coverage – could provide further insight into *why* the algorithm performs well. Exploring the correlation between diversity metrics and the final ensemble performance would be particularly valuable. It would also be helpful to investigate how sensitive the algorithm is to the choice of hyperparameters relating to diversity (if any). 

Despite these suggestions for improvement, this is a solid contribution that addresses a practical limitation of existing ensemble methods. The clear presentation and compelling experimental results support the acceptance of this paper.
