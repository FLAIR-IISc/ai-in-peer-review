- Proposal: Train ensembles so only a single member is active per training example; simple method with surprisingly strong empirical results; reviewer recommends weak accept.

- Test-time procedure unclear: clarify how the active member is chosen at inference without access to labels, whether gating is learned or fixed, and if a proxy (e.g., max predicted probability) is used report sensitivity.

- Baseline definitions missing: specify how “independent” vs. “regular” ensembles are trained (initializations, data splits, schedules), how outputs are combined at test time (probabilities/logits/votes), and how capacity/training budget compare to the proposed method.

- Specialization claims need stronger evidence: provide quantitative analyses (e.g., confusion overlap, assignment entropy, mutual exclusivity of high-confidence regions) or modify the objective to explicitly encourage class-conditional specialization/diversity.

- Related work gap: discuss diversity-regularization literature (e.g., Chen et al.), clarify implicit vs. explicit diversity control, and if possible compare or ablate against a diversity-regularized baseline.

- Strengths: Conceptually clean, easy to implement, empirically strong, and addresses redundancy/inefficiency of standard ensembling.

- Weaknesses: Ambiguity in test-time selection, unclear baselines, insufficient specialization evidence, and missing discussion/comparison to diversity-regularized methods.

- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying test-time methodology, baseline details, specialization analyses, and expanding related-work discussion.
