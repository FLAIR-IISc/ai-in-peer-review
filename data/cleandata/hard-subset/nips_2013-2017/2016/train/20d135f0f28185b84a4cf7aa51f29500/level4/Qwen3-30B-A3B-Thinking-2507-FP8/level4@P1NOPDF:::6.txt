

This paper introduces a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep learners, distinguished by its approach where only a single ensemble member is activated per training example—contrasting with prior deep or classical ensemble methods. During training, the member with the lowest loss relative to the true label is selected and updated via gradient descent. The core idea is elegantly simple yet yields strong empirical results, effectively addressing key limitations in existing techniques. I recommend weak acceptance, pending clarification of several critical points.

1. **Testing Protocol Ambiguity**: The paper fails to clearly describe the ensemble's operation during inference. Line 159 (p.5) suggests ground truth is used to select the "best" member at test time, which is unconventional and potentially problematic. The authors must explicitly clarify whether this approach is intended for training-only use, or if a valid test-time strategy exists without access to labels.

2. **Undefined Baseline Terminology**: The paper repeatedly references "independent ensembles" (labeled "Indp." in figures and line 181, p.5) versus "regular ensembles." However, no explanation is provided for what constitutes an "independent ensemble" baseline, nor how it is constructed. This omission undermines the interpretation of comparative results.

3. **Overstated Specialization Claim**: The authors assert that ensemble members develop "specialization" toward distinct output classes. Yet the training mechanism reassigns the active member *per example* based on loss, not class-specific assignment. This per-example reassignment does not logically imply class-level specialization, and the argument lacks sufficient justification. The authors should either provide evidence supporting specialization (e.g., via class-wise loss analysis) or revise this claim. Crucially, they do not explore whether incorporating specialization directly into the loss function could enhance the method.

4. **Omission of Key Related Work**: The paper neglects to discuss diversity regularization techniques for neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which explicitly aim to foster ensemble diversity through structured loss terms. While Chen et al. focus on regularization (not per-example reassignment), their approach is rigorously grounded and directly relevant to the paper’s goals. Ignoring this work creates a significant gap in the literature context, especially given the method’s focus on ensemble diversity. The authors should address how their approach relates to or improves upon such diversity-aware frameworks.
