- Summary: Introduces sMCL, a winner-take-gradient SGD scheme that minimizes oracle loss for deep ensembles, showing consistent oracle gains and emergent specialization across classification, segmentation, and captioning.  
- Relation to prior work: Stochastic per-batch extension of MCL that is complementary to diverse M-best and ensemble diversity methods and scales better than Dey et al.'s sequential boosting-style approach.  
- Technical soundness: Objective follows MCL upper bound, gradient routing for hard assignments is correct, straightforward to implement, and backed by convincing empirical results.  
- Completeness: Provides ablations and qualitative specialization analysis but lacks more extensive diversity metrics and sensitivity studies.  
- Baselines: Evaluates against independent ensembles, original MCL, Dey et al., and beam search; reports wall-clock speedups over MCL.  
- Clarity: Well-written with clear motivation and interpretation; could improve pseudocode details (e.g., tie-breaking) and compute-budget reporting.  
- Originality: Incremental relative to prior MCL theory but practically important by making MCL usable at deep-learning scale.  
- Significance: Practical and likely adoptable for tasks needing multiple plausible outputs and an oracle selector due to simplicity and architectural agnosticism.  
- Strengths: Parameter-free, SGD-compatible algorithm; strong oracle improvements across tasks; concurrent training with speedups; interpretable emergent specialization; clear exposition.  
- Weaknesses: Limited theoretical analysis and explicit diversity metrics; insufficient exploration of effects on non-oracle metrics, mode collapse, and sensitivity to M/tie-breaking/batch size; potential unfairness in MCL baseline setup and incomplete compute reporting.  
- Suggestions: Compare to a minibatch MCL variant, report explicit diversity measures and non-oracle metrics (e.g., accuracy, ECE), analyze sensitivity and soft-assignments, expand baselines (e.g., negative correlation learning), and disentangle captioning inference vs. training effects.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept to accept — incremental novelty but convincing, useful empirical contribution; more diversity analysis and a stronger MCL minibatch baseline would strengthen the paper.
