This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training ensemble models, building upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The authors' key contribution is the introduction of a stochastic gradient descent approach, enabling the application of this algorithm to deep neural networks. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concept of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and its extension to Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL).

The main idea of the paper is to train an ensemble of deep networks to minimize the oracle loss, which is the loss of the best output of a single classifier in the ensemble. This approach yields a diverse set of classifiers, making it particularly useful for tasks with multiple reasonable interpretations. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The strengths of the paper include its clear explanation of the concept, thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithm, and demonstration of its broad applicability. The authors also provide an in-depth analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, showcasing the emergence of interpretable expertise among ensemble members.

One potential weakness of the paper is the incremental nature of the algorithmic contribution. However, the authors convincingly argue that the introduction of stochastic gradient descent significantly enhances the usability of the algorithm, making it more suitable for deep neural networks.

To further strengthen the paper, I would suggest exploring the following:

1. Comparison with MCL using batches: It would be interesting to investigate how the MCL algorithm performs when using batches instead of the entire dataset. This comparison could provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the stochastic gradient descent approach.
2. Analysis of diversity: While the paper touches on the "diversity" effect, a more in-depth analysis of this aspect would be beneficial. The authors could explore how the proposed algorithm affects the diversity of the ensemble members and how this, in turn, impacts the overall performance.

In conclusion, I believe that the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and I am in favor of acceptance. The authors' work has the potential to impact various applications, and their approach to training diverse deep ensembles is both novel and effective.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training ensemble models.
* The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.
* The paper provides an in-depth analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The algorithmic contribution may be considered incremental, building upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8].
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the "diversity" effect and its impact on the overall performance.

Overall, I believe that the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
