This paper proposes a simple solution to the multiple choice learning problem when applied to deep networks. Existing approaches to multiple choice learning (MCL) do not readily extend to deep learning architectures. The authors demonstrate that a “winner-takes-the-gradient” layer during backpropagation effectively combines learner training with the assignment problem inherent in an ensemble setting. Experimental results demonstrate strong performance across a diverse range of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning. 

Strengths:
- The problem of MCL for deep learning is sufficiently important to the NIPS community.
- The proposed method is notable for its simplicity.
- The experimental evaluation covers three distinct tasks.

Weaknesses:
- My primary concern lies with the emphasis on “diversity” – even reflected in the title – while the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. I anticipated a mechanism for incorporating a diversity term, and was disappointed to find its absence. The observed behavior appears to arise as an emergent property rather than a designed constraint.
- The proposed solution represents an incremental improvement over the relaxation presented by Guzman et al. 

Minor Suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract would benefit from revision for clarity.
- The emphasis on “diversity” should be reduced throughout the paper.
- On line 108, the first instance of “f” should be “g” in the phrase “we fixed the form of…”.
- Line 115 contains an extraneous period mid-sentence.

Question:
For the baseline MCL implementation using deep learning, how did the authors ensure each network converged to a reasonable solution? Prematurely terminating training of the learners could significantly impact the overall ensemble performance.
