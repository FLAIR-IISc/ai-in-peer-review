- Paper proposes sMCL: an SGD-based "winner-take-gradient" method to train deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss by backpropagating only to the lowest-loss member per example.  
- sMCL is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, trains members concurrently, and scales to modern deep networks unlike prior MCL retraining schemes.  
- Empirical gains shown on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MS-COCO captioning, outperforming independent ensembles, a boosting-like baseline, and MCL (with speedups).  
- Qualitative results indicate emergent, interpretable specialization/diversity among ensemble members.  
- Strength: algorithm is simple, easy to implement, and requires no extra hyperparameters.  
- Strength: cross-domain evidence demonstrates consistent oracle metric improvements across different tasks and architectures.  
- Strength: substantial wall-clock speedup (~5x) compared to alternating retraining MCL.  
- Weakness: test-time protocol is unclear—must state that evaluation uses oracle metrics over M hypotheses and explain deployment scenarios (e.g., re-ranking, human-in-the-loop).  
- Weakness: ambiguous baseline terminology—define "Indp."/regular ensembles and provide matched training details (data, epochs, LR schedules) for fairness.  
- Weakness: specialization/diversity claims need quantitative support (e.g., mutual information with class, per-member class histograms, training-assignment fractions, pairwise disagreement, n-gram/shape stats).  
- Suggestion: include ablations (e.g., top-k winners) and consider balanced-assignment or entropy regularizers to address potential collapse/imbalance.  
- Weakness: related work on diversity-inducing methods is under-expanded—cite negative correlation learning, orthogonality/cosine penalties, DPPs, MoE/gating models, and Monte Carlo dropout.  
- Weakness: convergence/stability concerns—winner-take-gradient resembles hard-EM and may underutilize members; report results across seeds, tie-breaking policy, and any annealing/tempering.  
- Weakness: sequence-model comparisons incomplete—compare or combine sMCL with beam search and provide compute-matched baselines plus uniqueness/diversity metrics.  
- Reproducibility: report hardware, training time, memory footprint, overhead of computing M losses per batch, and clarify shared-CNN tree-like captioning setup.  
- Quality: theoretical derivation and empirical results are sound, but stability/diversity analyses and additional baselines would strengthen the work.  
- Clarity: paper is well written but should define "oracle"/"oracle metrics" early, state test-time protocol, unify baseline terminology, and add concise pseudo-code (including tie-breaking).  
- Originality: main novelty is adapting MCL to stochastic, parallel deep-training; related to hard-EM and MoE but a useful new instantiation for deep ensembles.  
- Significance: method is simple, broadly applicable, effective, and likely impactful for applications needing multiple hypotheses.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical algorithm, strong multi-domain results, qualitative insights, and clear speed/performance benefits.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited quantitative diversity/stability analysis, under-discussed baselines/related work, and unclear test-time/baseline definitions.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept.  
- Requested clarifications: explicitly state test-time behavior and oracle computation; define Indp.; quantify specialization/diversity and stability; expand related work; compare/combine with beam search for captioning; provide training/hardware/tie-breaking details and consider releasing code.
