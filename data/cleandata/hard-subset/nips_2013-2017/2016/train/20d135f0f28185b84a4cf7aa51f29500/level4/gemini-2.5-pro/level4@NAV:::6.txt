This paper introduces a novel training methodology for deep learning ensembles. Its primary novelty lies in activating and updating only a single ensemble member per training instance, a departure from conventional deep and classical ensemble approaches. The training procedure identifies the member that yields the minimum loss for a given ground truth label and applies the gradient update exclusively to that member. In essence, this is a straightforward concept that achieves impressive empirical performance and addresses certain limitations of prior techniques. Consequently, I recommend a weak accept for this submission.

1.  The inference procedure is insufficiently described. I am concerned that the authors might be using the ground truth to select the best-performing member during testing, as hinted at in line 159 on page 5, which would be an invalid evaluation protocol.

2.  The manuscript frequently refers to 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' (abbreviated as 'Indp.' in figures and mentioned on page 5, line 181), but a clear definition or explanation for this baseline method could not be found.

3.  A recurring claim in the paper is that ensemble members `specialize` on specific output classes. This argument seems inadequately supported, given that the member selection and update mechanism operates on a per-instance, not per-class, basis. A potential way to strengthen this claim would be to explicitly encourage class-based specialization within the loss function.

4.  Following on the theme of specialization, the paper omits discussion of highly relevant prior work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.). These techniques offer a more formal approach to encouraging diversity and, while their application in modern deep learning is still nascent, their inclusion is essential for contextualizing the proposed method.
