

The paper introduces a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep learners, where only one ensemble member is updated per training example based on the lowest loss with respect to the true label. This approach diverges from conventional ensembles (both classical and deep) by dynamically selecting the most active member per example rather than updating all members. The method is conceptually simple yet achieves strong empirical results, addressing key limitations of prior work. The core contribution is well-motivated and demonstrates clear practical value, warranting weak acceptance. However, several critical clarifications and contextualizations are required before the paper can be accepted.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Testing Phase Ambiguity:** The paper fails to clarify how the ensemble is utilized during inference. The description on page 5 (line 159) suggests the ground truth label is used to select the "best" member for each test example. This is problematic as ground truth is unavailable at test time. The authors must explicitly state the actual testing procedure (e.g., using the ensemble's combined prediction, selecting the member with highest confidence, or a different strategy) and justify its validity.

2.  **Undefined Baseline ("Independent Ensembles"):** The paper repeatedly references a baseline called "independent ensembles" (denoted "Indp." in figures and line 181, page 5), yet provides no definition or description of this method. How is this baseline constructed? How does it differ from standard independent training of ensemble members? Without this explanation, the comparative results (e.g., Figure 3) are difficult to interpret and evaluate. The baseline must be rigorously defined in the methodology or related work section.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for "Specialization":** The authors emphasize that ensemble members develop "specialization" towards specific output classes, a claim central to their argument. However, the training mechanism (reassigning the active member per *example* based solely on immediate loss) does not logically lead to *class-level* specialization. Per-example reassignment encourages *instance-level* adaptation, not inherent class bias. The paper lacks evidence (e.g., visualization of class-wise member usage, loss patterns per class) to support the specialization narrative. Crucially, the authors do not explore whether incorporating a *class-specific* specialization term into the loss function could *explicitly* encourage the desired behavior, which would strengthen their core argument. The current justification is speculative and unsupported by the described mechanism.

4.  **Omission of Key Related Work:** The paper overlooks significant prior work on *diversity regularization* in neural networks, particularly the influential work by Chen et al. (e.g., "Diversity Regularization for Neural Network Ensembles," ICML 2016). While the authors note that "specialization" is related to diversity, their approach differs fundamentally from explicit diversity regularization techniques. The paper should explicitly contrast their dynamic selection mechanism with these established methods, discussing both conceptual similarities and key differences (e.g., their method is implicit and example-driven, while diversity regularization is explicit and often layer-based). The claim that diversity regularization is "more rigorous but yet to be studied in the deep setting" is inaccurate; diversity regularization *has* been actively studied and applied in deep learning contexts.

**Overall Assessment:**

The core idea of dynamically selecting the active ensemble member per example is novel and leads to compelling empirical results that outperform several baselines. The simplicity and effectiveness of the approach are its strongest merits. However, the critical ambiguities in the testing procedure, the undefined baseline, the unsupported claim of class specialization, and the omission of relevant diversity regularization literature significantly undermine the paper's contribution and clarity. Addressing these points is essential for the paper to make a solid contribution to the field.

The authors should revise the manuscript to:
*   Explicitly define the test-time procedure.
*   Provide a clear definition and description of the "independent ensembles" baseline.
*   Substantially revise the discussion of "specialization" with empirical evidence or a modified loss function to justify the claim.
*   Incorporate and discuss the key related work on diversity regularization (e.g., Chen et al.) and position their method within this literature.

With these revisions, the paper's strengths would be fully realized, and the contribution would be significantly clearer. As is, the unresolved issues prevent a stronger recommendation. **Weak Accept.**
