This paper introduces a straightforward method for multiple-choice learning (MCL) with deep networks. Prior MCL approaches do not directly extend to modern deep architectures. The authors show that inserting a “winner-take-the-gradient” mechanism during backpropagation effectively merges example-to-model assignment with joint training of an ensemble. Experiments indicate strong performance across diverse settings: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

Strengths:
- The MCL problem in the context of deep learning is timely and relevant for the NIPS community.
- The proposed approach is appealingly simple.
- The paper demonstrates results on three distinct tasks.

Weaknesses:
- My primary concern is that the paper heavily motivates “diversity” (including in the title) but does not explicitly enforce it in the objective; I expected an explicit diversity-promoting term and was disappointed to find none.
- The contribution feels incremental relative to the relaxation introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al.

Minor suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract should be revised for clarity.
- The emphasis on “diversity” should be moderated.
- Line 108: the first “f” should be “g” in “we fix the form of …”
- Line 115: there is an extra “.” mid-sentence.

One question:
For the deep-learning MCL baseline, how did you ensure that each network was trained to reasonable convergence? Stopping training early could substantially degrade ensemble performance.
