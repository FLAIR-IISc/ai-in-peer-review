

This paper proposes a stochastic gradient descent method incorporating Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for multi-task learning in computer vision. While the authors claim advantages across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks, the work suffers from significant shortcomings in novelty, technical depth, and experimental validation.  

First, the claimed novelty is overstated. The motivation for MCL is reasonable, but the core contribution—applying a neural network adaptation of [8] with minor adjustments—lacks sufficient innovation. The method does not introduce a fundamentally new approach to multi-task learning; rather, it repurposes existing principles within a neural network framework. This incremental contribution does not justify the paper’s central claim of a novel framework, especially given the absence of theoretical justification for why MCL outperforms standard multi-task training strategies.  

Second, the technical exposition is inadequate. Section 3, which details the proposed framework, occupies only one page, leaving critical gaps in methodology. The paper fails to clarify how the MCL framework differs from established multi-task training paradigms (e.g., joint training with shared layers, task-specific heads, or loss weighting). Without a rigorous comparison to these common baselines—particularly in terms of computational overhead, convergence behavior, or architectural flexibility—the proposed method’s utility remains unclear. A concise but precise technical comparison would be essential to establish the framework’s distinct value.  

Third, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming. While the authors test on diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning), the reported performance gains are marginal and lack statistical significance. Crucially, the baselines are weak: the paper does not compare against state-of-the-art multi-task approaches (e.g., [9], [10]) or task-specific SOTA models. For instance, on segmentation tasks, the absence of comparison to recent architectures like DeepLab or PSPNet undermines the claimed benefits. To validate the framework’s efficiency, experiments on PASCAL VOC 2012—using strong baselines like DeepLabv3+ or HRNet—would be necessary. The current results (e.g., <1% mIoU improvement over standard multi-task training) do not convincingly demonstrate superiority.  

Additionally, the writing is inconsistent and imprecise. Terms like "incremental in novelty" are grammatically flawed and misleading. The paper conflates "multiple choice" (a training strategy) with "multiple tasks" (the application domain), creating confusion. Figures and tables lack clarity (e.g., Figure 2’s error bars are absent), and the ablation study omits key experiments (e.g., MCL’s sensitivity to hyperparameters).  

In summary, this work presents a minor adaptation of prior multi-task learning concepts without sufficient theoretical or empirical justification. The technical depth is insufficient, the experiments lack rigorous baselines, and the claimed advantages are not substantiated. To strengthen the submission, the authors must: (1) clarify the method’s distinction from existing multi-task training, (2) expand Section 3 with a detailed comparison to standard approaches, and (3) conduct experiments against robust baselines on standard benchmarks like PASCAL VOC 2012. Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution remains marginal and fails to meet NIPS’s standards for novelty and impact.
