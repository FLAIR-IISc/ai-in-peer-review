This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm to train ensemble models, building upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The authors introduce a new approach, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which enables the training of diverse deep ensembles by minimizing the oracle loss directly. The algorithm is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed method. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the advantages of their approach. The experimental results demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles, outperforming classical ensembles and other strong baselines.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed algorithm and its advantages. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the experimental results, highlighting the emergence of interpretable specializations among ensemble members.

However, I do have some concerns regarding the novelty of the algorithm. While the authors convincingly argue that their approach is more practical and efficient than existing methods, it is unclear whether the algorithm is significantly novel compared to [8]. To address this concern, I would like to see a more detailed comparison between sMCL and the MCL algorithm when batches are used (rather than the entire dataset).

Additionally, I would like to see more analysis regarding the "diversity" effect of the proposed algorithm. While the authors provide some qualitative results, a more quantitative evaluation of the diversity of the ensemble members would be beneficial.

Arguments in favor of acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed method.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the algorithm may be incremental compared to [8].
* More analysis is needed to fully understand the "diversity" effect of the proposed algorithm.

Overall, I believe that the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field, and I am in favor of acceptance. However, I would like to see the authors address the concerns mentioned above to strengthen their paper.
