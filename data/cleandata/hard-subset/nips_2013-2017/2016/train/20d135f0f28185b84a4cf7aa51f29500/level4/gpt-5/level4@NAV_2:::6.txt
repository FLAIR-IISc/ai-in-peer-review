This paper introduces an ensemble training scheme for deep models in which, for each training instance, only the member with the lowest loss receives the gradient update (a “winner-take-gradient” variant of SGD). Unlike conventional deep or classical ensembles where all members are trained independently, or earlier MCL-style procedures that require costly alternating retraining, this approach updates a single best-performing learner per example within standard SGD. The idea is straightforward yet yields strong empirical gains and mitigates limitations of prior methods; I therefore lean toward a weak accept.

1) Test-time procedure is insufficiently specified. It appears the evaluation uses an oracle that selects the best member with respect to ground truth (as suggested around page 5, line 159). Please clarify the exact inference-time protocol: how are predictions produced without access to labels, and how do the reported “oracle” metrics relate to practical deployment?

2) The baseline termed “independent (Indp.)” or “regular” ensembles (e.g., Fig. labels and page 5, line 181) is not clearly defined. Please provide a precise description of this comparator, including training setup, hyperparameters, and how diversity (if any) is induced (e.g., random seeds only).

3) The paper repeatedly attributes “specialization” of ensemble members to different output classes, yet the reassignment during training is per-instance, not explicitly class-conditional. The evidence for class-level specialization is therefore not fully substantiated. Can specialization be directly encouraged or encoded in the objective (e.g., via an explicit term) to make this claim more rigorous?

4) Closely related diversity-regularization work in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.) is not discussed. This line of research offers a more principled route to promoting diversity, even if less explored in deep architectures; it would strengthen the paper to position sMCL relative to such methods and clarify similarities/differences.
