

The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by leveraging an oracle that identifies the best-performing ensemble member for each input instance during training. The method applies backpropagation exclusively to the network producing the lowest error prediction for each example, encouraging specialization among ensemble members. The authors demonstrate sMCL's effectiveness across multiple domains including image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements in oracle performance over baselines while training all ensemble members concurrently.

The work builds on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9], addressing a critical limitation of prior approaches: the need for iterative retraining of ensemble members, which is computationally prohibitive for deep networks. Unlike existing methods that require sequential training or multiple retraining iterations [5,8,9], sMCL operates within a standard stochastic gradient descent framework, making it applicable to modern deep architectures without significant computational overhead. This represents a substantial practical advancement over previous approaches to diverse ensemble training.

The paper excels in several key areas. Quality is strong: the method is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation in stochastic block gradient descent. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering multiple problem domains and architectures. The results consistently show sMCL outperforms baselines (6-10% improvements over classical ensembles) while being 5x faster than MCL. The authors provide thorough analysis of ensemble specialization, demonstrating how the method automatically induces interpretable expertise among members.

Clarity is excellent: the paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts, including the emergence of specialization in ensemble members. The technical contribution is presented in a logical flow that makes it accessible to readers familiar with deep learning.

Originality is significant: while the concept of multiple-choice learning has been explored [8,9], the authors' method is the first to enable efficient training of diverse deep ensembles without costly retraining. The technique is agnostic to architecture and loss function, broadening its applicability.

Significance is compelling: the approach addresses an important practical problem in perception systems where providing multiple hypotheses is preferable to a single prediction. The method has clear potential for applications in autonomous systems, assistive technologies, and other domains where ambiguity is common. The results demonstrate meaningful improvements in oracle performance across multiple tasks, advancing the state of the art in ensemble learning.

The paper's primary weakness is limited discussion of how to practically utilize the resulting diverse ensembles. While the authors mention that ensembles provide "multiple highly likely hypotheses" and reference top-k metrics used in ImageNet, they do not elaborate on how these outputs would be integrated into real-world systems or how the oracle would be implemented in practice. The paper focuses on training the ensembles but does not address inference-time use cases, which is a significant gap for a method targeting practical applications.

Another minor issue is the presentation of Algorithm 2 (Figure 2), which appears as a raster image rather than vectorized text. This makes it difficult to copy the pseudo-code for reference or reproduction.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a novel, practical approach to training diverse deep ensembles with strong empirical validation across multiple domains. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance: The limited discussion of practical deployment of the resulting ensembles represents a notable gap in the paper's contribution, particularly for a method targeting real-world applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to ensemble learning for deep networks. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. While the lack of discussion on practical deployment is a limitation, it does not undermine the core contribution, which is a novel and effective training algorithm. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the presentation of the algorithm and to include a brief discussion of practical deployment considerations. The paper would benefit from a short section explaining how the diverse outputs could be practically used in downstream systems, which would strengthen its real-world relevance without requiring substantial additional work.
