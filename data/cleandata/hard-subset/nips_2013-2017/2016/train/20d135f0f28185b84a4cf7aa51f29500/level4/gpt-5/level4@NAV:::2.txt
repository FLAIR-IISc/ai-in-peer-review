The paper introduces a new method for training an ensemble of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs)—or any gradient-based model—by using an oracle that, for each training instance, identifies which ensemble member is optimal; backpropagation is then applied only to the selected network’s output. The key advantage is that the networks are encouraged to specialize, yielding an ensemble that collectively covers most outputs of interest. Results are reported across multiple domains, with consistently strong performance.

I find this to be a solid paper. It clearly presents an appealing idea: training an ensemble with an oracle that directs the backprop updates to the network producing the best output (with respect to the true label). It appears to enforce specialization effectively in classification settings. The overall presentation is strong, and the experiments span several domains. Overall, this is a strong contribution that merits publication.

I would have considered the paper outstanding if it had also addressed how to exploit the trained ensemble in practice. At present, training depends on the oracle, but the use of the resulting ensemble at inference time is not fully developed, beyond noting that it can provide a broad pool of candidate outputs and that the approach aligns with top-k metrics used in some tasks (e.g., ImageNet). This is a significant missing component for practical deployment. Nevertheless, the core contribution stands, and the missing piece is likely problem-specific.
