

The paper introduces sMCL, a method for generating multiple diverse outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize an oracle loss. This approach is particularly relevant for user-facing applications requiring varied, high-quality predictions. The authors integrate deep networks into the MCL (Multiple-Choice Learning) paradigm and propose a stochastic block gradient descent optimization technique that minimizes the oracle loss. A key strength is the method's compatibility with standard backpropagation, enabling end-to-end training without architectural modifications. Experiments on three tasks demonstrate improved oracle accuracy over baselines, supporting the method's efficacy.

However, several critical aspects require clarification to strengthen the paper's contribution and validity. First, the role of initialization is not adequately addressed. The paper uses pre-trained networks for initialization but does not investigate how performance depends on this choice. Specifically:  
- How sensitive is sMCL to initialization? Do random initializations (without pre-training) yield comparable results, or does pre-training significantly impact convergence and final performance?  
- Crucially, the paper does not specify whether baseline methods (e.g., standard ensembles or single-network approaches) used the *same* pre-trained network for initialization as sMCL. Without this, comparisons may be biased, as pre-training could disproportionately benefit sMCL. The authors should explicitly state initialization protocols for all methods and analyze initialization sensitivity via ablation studies.

Second, while oracle accuracy is used as the primary metric, the paper lacks analysis of ensemble diversity—a core motivation for multi-output methods. Diversity among ensemble members is essential for robustness and avoiding redundant predictions. The authors should provide quantitative measures (e.g., pairwise disagreement, feature coverage, or entropy-based diversity scores) to demonstrate that sMCL genuinely produces varied outputs, not merely improved accuracy. Without this, the claim that the method "generates multiple outputs" remains unsubstantiated.

Third, the paper overlooks a closely related work that directly addresses training diverse deep ensembles: Lee et al. ("Why M Heads are Better than One," CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015). This work proposes a similar objective (diversifying ensemble heads via loss minimization) and shares methodological parallels with sMCL. The authors should explicitly compare against this baseline to contextualize their contribution. Failure to do so risks understating the novelty of their approach or overlooking key technical overlaps.

In summary, sMCL presents a technically sound framework for ensemble learning with strong empirical results. To maximize impact, the authors should:  
1. Clarify initialization protocols and sensitivity across all methods,  
2. Include diversity metrics to validate the multi-output claim,  
3. Compare against Lee et al. (2015) to establish novelty and positioning.  

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's rigor and contribution to the literature. The current draft lacks sufficient detail on these aspects, making it difficult to assess the full significance of the proposed method.
