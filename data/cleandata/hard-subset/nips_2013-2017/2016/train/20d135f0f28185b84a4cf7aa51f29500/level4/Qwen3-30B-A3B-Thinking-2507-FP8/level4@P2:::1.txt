

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss directly. The authors build upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8], which optimizes for the lowest error prediction among ensemble members, but address a critical limitation: MCL's alternating block coordinate descent requires costly retraining of models multiple times, making it impractical for modern deep networks that take weeks to train. The authors propose sMCL, a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm that trains ensemble members concurrently by backpropagating gradients only to the lowest-error predictor for each example during the backward pass. This simple yet effective modification makes the approach applicable to any model trained with SGD, without architectural or loss function constraints.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across three diverse domains: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). sMCL consistently outperforms classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s boosting-like approach [5], achieving 6-10% relative improvements in oracle performance while being 5x faster than MCL. The authors also provide compelling evidence of how sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members - for example, in classification tasks, different ensemble members become specialists for distinct classes, while in segmentation, members develop expertise in specific object categories or segmentation styles.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem statement is well-motivated by practical scenarios where multiple hypotheses are preferable to single predictions (e.g., ambiguous object recognition in autonomous vehicles). The technical formulation is presented accessibly, with clear algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) and thorough experimental validation. The writing is precise, with appropriate figures illustrating both quantitative results and qualitative interpretations of the diverse outputs produced by sMCL.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies and analysis of how specialization emerges automatically under sMCL. The experimental design is robust, comparing against relevant baselines across multiple tasks and architectures. The method's simplicity and broad applicability (architecture/loss-agnostic, parameter-free) are significant strengths.

On originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by adapting MCL to deep learning through a simple stochastic optimization approach. While the core idea of minimizing oracle loss is not new, the authors convincingly demonstrate that their modification (replacing batch-based retraining with SGD) makes the approach practical for modern deep networks. The paper's novelty lies in this practical adaptation rather than theoretical innovation, and the authors thoroughly address how their work differs from prior MCL approaches.

The significance is evident in the practical impact: many real-world perception systems would benefit from diverse output sets rather than single predictions. The paper demonstrates this through compelling examples (e.g., avoiding "mode-seeking" behaviors in captioning that produce generic sentences like "birds on branches"). The approach has clear potential for adoption in applications where multiple hypotheses are valuable, such as human-in-the-loop systems or systems with verification components.

Strengths:
- Practical solution to an important problem (diverse predictions for real-world systems)
- Simple, generalizable approach applicable to various architectures and tasks
- Strong empirical results across multiple domains
- Clear demonstration of automatic specialization
- Well-written and well-organized paper

Weaknesses:
- The novelty over Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] could be more strongly emphasized. While the authors make a convincing case for the practical significance of their modification, a more detailed discussion of how sMCL differs conceptually from MCL would strengthen the contribution.
- The paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of the diversity effect beyond oracle performance. For example, what metrics quantify the diversity of outputs, and how does sMCL compare to other diversity-inducing techniques?
- A comparison of MCL using batched updates (rather than full dataset retraining) would provide a more direct assessment of the advantage of sMCL's concurrent training approach.

I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have successfully adapted the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm to deep networks through a simple but impactful modification that enables practical application. The empirical results are compelling, and the approach is broadly applicable across vision and language tasks. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the practical significance of making MCL feasible for deep learning is substantial.

The authors should address the diversity analysis and strengthen the comparison with MCL's batch-based implementation in the final version. These minor improvements would further solidify the paper's contribution. Overall, this is a high-quality paper with clear significance for both theory and practice. I recommend acceptance.
