This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for training diverse ensembles of deep networks. The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], where the objective is to minimize the loss of the best prediction from an ensemble of classifiers. This approach encourages diversity in the ensemble. The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating that an SGD-based training procedure makes this approach significantly more practical for deep neural networks, avoiding the costly retraining required by the original MCL algorithm. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details of the proposed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) method. 

My primary concern initially revolved around the incremental nature of the algorithmic novelty compared to [8]. However, the authors convincingly demonstrate that this seemingly small change – adapting the algorithm for SGD – unlocks its applicability to modern deep learning architectures, which would otherwise be computationally prohibitive to train using the original method. The extensive experimental results across diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) and network architectures provide strong evidence for the efficacy of sMCL.

Several aspects of the paper are particularly noteworthy. The method is presented as being remarkably general – applicable to any model trained with SGD and agnostic to both the network architecture and the task-specific loss function. This generality, combined with the parameter-free nature of the algorithm, makes it exceptionally accessible. Furthermore, the authors provide insightful analysis of the emergent expertise within the trained ensembles, demonstrating how different members specialize on different subsets of the data or different aspects of the task.

To further strengthen the paper, a comparison to the original MCL algorithm when trained with mini-batches (rather than the entire dataset) would be beneficial. This would provide a more direct assessment of the advantage gained by the proposed SGD approach. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates improved performance, further analysis quantifying the “diversity” of the generated solutions – perhaps through measures beyond oracle performance – would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Addresses a practical limitation:** Successfully adapts the MCL framework to deep learning by enabling efficient training with SGD.
*   **Strong experimental validation:** Demonstrates significant performance improvements across a variety of tasks and network architectures.
*   **Generality and simplicity:** The algorithm is broadly applicable and easy to implement.
*   **Insightful analysis:** Provides compelling evidence of emergent specialization and expertise within the ensembles.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The algorithmic novelty, in isolation, may be considered incremental. However, the demonstrated practical impact outweighs this concern.



**Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper.** It represents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning, offering a practical and effective method for training diverse deep networks. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and supported by compelling experimental results.
