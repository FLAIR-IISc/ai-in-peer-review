This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and efficient method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to optimize the ensemble directly for the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member on any given data instance. During training with stochastic gradient descent, the gradient for each training example is backpropagated only to the single ensemble member that produced the lowest loss for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy encourages individual models to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution. The authors frame this as a practical, stochastic alternative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods, which required iterative retraining of models and were thus ill-suited for computationally expensive deep learning architectures. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), using appropriate CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed sMCL method is elegant in its simplicity. It provides a clear, parameter-free mechanism to directly optimize for a diverse set of outputs by leveraging the oracle loss. The approach is general and can be applied to any architecture trained with stochastic gradient descent and any standard loss function.

2.  **Practical Significance:** The paper effectively addresses a major limitation of prior work in this area. By enabling concurrent training of all ensemble members, sMCL makes the goal of minimizing oracle loss feasible for modern deep networks, which can take days or weeks to train. The reported 5x speedup over the iterative MCL baseline is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across a variety of domains. The results consistently demonstrate that sMCL outperforms standard independently trained ensembles and other relevant baselines. The qualitative results and analysis are particularly compelling, showing how different forms of interpretable specialization (e.g., by class, object shape, or sentence structure) emerge automatically as a result of the training procedure.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is well-contextualized, and the connection to real-world applications where multiple hypotheses are valuable is persuasive.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of the paper is the limited discussion on the practical deployment of the trained sMCL ensemble. The training process relies on an oracle (the ground truth label) to select the "winning" model for the gradient update. However, at inference time, this oracle is unavailable. The paper suggests that the diverse set of outputs is valuable for downstream processes or human-in-the-loop systems, which is a valid and important use case.

However, the work does not address how one might automatically select the best prediction or combine the outputs from the ensemble when such an external oracle is absent. This leaves a gap between the training methodology and its application in many fully automated systems. While developing a universal aggregation or selection method is likely beyond the scope of a single paper and may be task-dependent, a discussion of potential strategies (e.g., training a separate gating network, using model uncertainty, or other post-hoc ranking methods) would have significantly strengthened the paper's practical contribution and made the proposed framework feel more complete.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The pseudo-code in Algorithm 2 appears to be a low-resolution raster image. Using a vector format or typesetting the algorithm directly using a package like `algorithm2e` would improve readability and the overall quality of the manuscript.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   The paper presents a novel, simple, and elegant method for training diverse deep ensembles.
    *   It solves a key practical limitation of previous approaches, making oracle loss minimization feasible for modern deep learning.
    *   The method is thoroughly evaluated on multiple challenging tasks and architectures, with strong quantitative and qualitative results.
    *   The work is well-written and the core ideas are clearly communicated.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The paper lacks a discussion of how to use the trained ensemble at inference time without an oracle, which limits its immediate applicability in fully automated settings.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed training method is clever and practical, and the experimental results are convincing. While the lack of an inference-time exploitation strategy is a notable omission, the contribution in training diverse ensembles is significant enough to warrant publication. The ideas presented are likely to be useful to and built upon by other researchers.
