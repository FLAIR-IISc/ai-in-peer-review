This paper introduces a training scheme for ensembles of deep neural networks—and, more generally, gradient-based models—where an oracle identifies, for each training instance, the ensemble member whose output is best according to the true label. Backpropagation is then applied only to that selected network. This oracle-guided update encourages members to specialize, yielding an ensemble that collectively covers a wide range of outputs.

Strengths:
- The core idea is simple, well-motivated, and appears effective at enforcing specialization in classification settings.
- The presentation is clear, and the empirical results across multiple domains are strong, often competitive or superior to baselines.
- The method is practical to implement and broadly applicable to gradient-based learners, not just DNNs.

Weaknesses / missing pieces:
- The main gap is how to use the trained ensemble at inference time without access to the oracle. While the paper notes that the ensemble can provide a broad pool of candidate outputs and that the approach aligns with top-k evaluation metrics (e.g., ImageNet), it does not develop concrete deployment strategies. For practical use, the paper should discuss and/or evaluate mechanisms such as:
  - How to select a single model at test time (e.g., learned gating, confidence-based routing, meta-classifiers).
  - How to combine outputs (e.g., ensembling, voting, calibration) when top-1 accuracy is required.
  - The trade-offs between top-1 and top-k performance and how specialization affects each.
  - Robustness and calibration of specialized members when faced with distribution shift.
Addressing these questions would significantly strengthen the practical relevance and completeness of the contribution. That said, I acknowledge that the best exploitation strategy may be problem-specific.

Minor presentation issue:
- Figure 2 presents pseudo-code as a rasterized image. Please render the pseudo-code directly in the text or include it as a vector graphic to improve readability and accessibility.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong paper presenting a clear, compelling idea with solid empirical validation. I recommend acceptance. I would consider it outstanding if the authors also provided and evaluated principled inference-time strategies for exploiting the trained ensemble.
