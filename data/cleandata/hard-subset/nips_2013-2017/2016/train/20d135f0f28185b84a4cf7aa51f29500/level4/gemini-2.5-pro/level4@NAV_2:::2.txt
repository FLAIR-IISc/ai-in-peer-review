This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly optimize the oracle loss, where for each training instance, the gradient is backpropagated only through the single ensemble member that produces the lowest error. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy encourages individual networks within the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or modes of the data distribution. This work generalizes the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. to be suitable for deep networks trained with stochastic gradient descent, overcoming the prohibitive computational cost of previous iterative methods that required retraining models to convergence. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their parameter-free approach on a variety of tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing significant improvements in oracle performance over standard ensembles and other baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful idea for training diverse ensembles. By formulating a stochastic, parallel training algorithm for minimizing the oracle loss, it provides the first practical method for applying the MCL paradigm to modern, large-scale deep learning models. The resulting emergent specialization is a compelling and valuable outcome.
*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound, and the claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across three distinct and challenging domains. The quantitative results consistently demonstrate that sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other relevant baselines. The qualitative analysis, particularly the visualizations of class-wise specialization (Fig. 4c) and diverse outputs (Fig. 6, 7), provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed sMCL algorithm is explained concisely, and the connection to prior work is well-established. The experimental setup and results are presented in a clear and easy-to-understand manner.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The primary limitation of this work is the lack of discussion on how the trained ensemble can be exploited in a practical setting where a ground-truth oracle is not available at inference time. The paper is framed around optimizing for oracle metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy), which is a valid and interesting research direction. However, for the method to be fully usable in many real-world applications, one would need a strategy for selecting or combining the multiple hypotheses without access to the true label. While the paper mentions downstream processes or human operators, addressing the challenge of automatic re-ranking or selection would have made this a more complete and impactful contribution. This feels like an important missing piece for broader practical adoption.
*   A minor presentation issue: The pseudo-code in Figure 2 appears to be a low-resolution raster image. Using a proper algorithm environment (e.g., with `algorithmic` or `algorithm2e` in LaTeX) or a vector graphic would improve the professional appearance and readability of the manuscript.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, effective, and broadly applicable method for training diverse deep ensembles. The core idea is elegant, and the experimental validation is thorough. While the practical application at inference time could be further developed, the contribution in its current form is significant and well-suited for publication.
