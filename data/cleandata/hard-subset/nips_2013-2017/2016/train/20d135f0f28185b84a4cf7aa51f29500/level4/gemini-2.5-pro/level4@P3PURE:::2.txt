This paper introduces a novel method for training ensembles of deep neural networks, or indeed any gradient-based model. The core idea is to employ an oracle during training which, for each data instance, identifies the optimal ensemble member based on the ground truth label. The backpropagation update is then applied exclusively to this selected network. This training strategy encourages individual networks within the ensemble to specialize, resulting in a diverse set of models that collectively cover a wide range of potential outputs.

The paper is well-written and presents a compelling and interesting idea. The proposed oracle-guided training scheme appears to be effective at enforcing specialization, particularly in classification contexts. The experimental validation is thorough, with strong performance demonstrated across a variety of domains. The overall presentation is clear and effective.

The primary weakness of the paper, and what prevents it from being an outstanding contribution, is the lack of discussion on how to effectively exploit the trained ensemble at inference time. While the training procedure relies on an oracle, the paper does not develop a corresponding strategy for using the ensemble when the ground truth is unavailable. The authors mention that the ensemble provides a broad pool of possible results and is consistent with top-k evaluation metrics (e.g., for ImageNet), but this is not a complete solution. Addressing the practical deployment of the ensemble is a crucial missing piece for the method's real-world applicability. While this is a significant omission, the core contribution in the training methodology is still valuable, and it is acknowledged that the optimal inference strategy may be problem-specific.

On a minor note concerning presentation, the pseudo-code in Figure 2 is embedded as a low-resolution raster image. For clarity and professionalism, this should be typeset directly within the paper or, at a minimum, included as a vector graphic.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and well-executed core idea. Despite the open question regarding inference, the contribution to training specialized ensembles is significant and warrants publication.
