Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple and general SGD-based method for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. For each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates (“winner-take-gradient”), encouraging members to specialize and jointly cover multi-modal outputs. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free (beyond ensemble size), and trains all members concurrently.

The work builds on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) of Guzman-Rivera et al., who optimize an oracle-loss upper bound via alternating assignments and per-model retraining—a strategy that is impractical for modern deep nets. It is also related to diverse M-best inference in structured models (e.g., Batra et al.; Kirillov et al.) and to boosting-like sequential ensemble methods for diversity (Dey et al.). sMCL provides the first practical SGD formulation that scales to contemporary deep architectures.

Experiments span image classification (CIFAR-10 with CNNs), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCNs), and image captioning (VGG16+LSTM on MSCOCO). Across tasks, sMCL improves oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, Dey et al., and (within feasible retraining budgets) classical MCL, while being substantially faster than MCL (reported ~5x for 5 meta-iterations). Qualitative analyses show emergent specialization (e.g., class-wise experts for CIFAR-10 and structured differences in segmentation and captioning).

Quality
- Soundness: The optimization follows directly from the oracle-loss upper-bound with hard assignments; the gradient behavior is simple and correct. The empirical results across three domains support the claims.
- Empiricism: Comparisons include independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al., and beam search for captioning. Oracle metrics are appropriate for multi-output settings. Training curves and specialization analyses are informative.
- Limitations: Evaluation focuses on oracle metrics; practical deployment typically requires a selection mechanism (learned gating, re-ranking, or user-in-the-loop). The paper stops short of assessing selection strategies beyond the oracle, which leaves a gap for real-world usage. Theoretical analysis of convergence or stability under hard assignments is limited.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized, with intuitive motivation and consistent use of the oracle-loss framework. The “winner-take-gradient” description is helpful.
- Minor presentation issue: the pseudo-code in Fig. 2 appears as a rasterized image; embedding typeset pseudo-code (vector text) would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality
- The idea of optimizing oracle loss for multiple outputs is known, but the stochastic, end-to-end SGD formulation for deep ensembles is novel and practically important. The emergence of interpretable specialization without explicit architectural gating is a nice contribution.

Significance
- The method addresses a common need for multi-hypothesis outputs in perception systems and provides a practical recipe applicable to many architectures/tasks. Gains on oracle metrics, speed-ups over MCL, and generality suggest broad impact. The absence of an evaluation with realistic selection mechanisms slightly tempers practical significance but does not diminish the core contribution.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and parameter-free training procedure that integrates seamlessly with SGD.
- Scales to deep architectures and trains ensemble members concurrently.
- Strong empirical improvements on diverse tasks and architectures.
- Clear evidence of emergent specialization that is interpretable and task-relevant.
- Careful positioning with respect to MCL, diverse M-best, and sequential diversity methods.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical deployment: The paper evaluates only oracle metrics. Please add experiments with simple selection strategies at test time (e.g., highest softmax confidence, sequence log-likelihood, calibration-based re-ranking, or a small learned gating network). Reporting performance under such heuristics would provide a more complete picture.
- Comparison to inference-time diversity: For captioning, beam search over a single model sometimes achieves higher oracle scores for M=5. It would be valuable to combine sMCL with beam search and report gains/costs, as well as wall-clock/runtime comparisons.
- Stability and tie-breaking: The approach uses hard assignments with arbitrary tie-breaking. Consider reporting sensitivity to tie-breaking and adding a soft-assignment variant (e.g., top-k or temperature-controlled softmax over losses) to mitigate oscillations; some of this is hinted at in the k-winners analysis—expanding that analysis would strengthen the paper.
- Resource accounting: Please include detailed training time, memory usage, and compute budgets for all methods (including number of meta-iterations for MCL) to substantiate speed claims and aid reproducibility.
- Diversity metrics: Beyond oracle scores, include explicit diversity measures (e.g., pairwise disagreement, IoU variance, n-gram diversity) to quantify coverage gains.
- Minor presentation: Replace rasterized pseudo-code with selectable text/vector; ensure figures are legible at print scale.

Questions for the authors
1. How do simple test-time selectors (e.g., maximum confidence, learned gating) perform relative to the oracle on your tasks?
2. How sensitive is sMCL to initialization and ensemble size M? Are there diminishing returns, and does specialization persist as M grows?
3. Do you observe assignment oscillations early in training? Would a short warm-up with independent training help?
4. Can sMCL be combined with snapshot/temporal ensembles or MC dropout to further increase diversity?
5. For captioning, what is the trade-off between sMCL and beam search in terms of oracle performance, diversity, and compute?

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical and novel SGD formulation of MCL that scales to modern deep nets.
- Consistent improvements on multiple tasks/architectures with interpretable specialization.
- Simple to implement and broadly applicable; likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of evaluation with realistic selection mechanisms at test time; reliance on oracle metrics may limit practical conclusions.
- Limited analysis of stability/tie-handling and resource usage details.
- In captioning, beam search can match/exceed oracle performance, suggesting the need for a stronger case on when sMCL is preferable.

Overall assessment
A strong paper. sMCL is a simple, general, and effective approach to training diverse deep ensembles under oracle loss, with solid empirical results and clear motivation. Addressing the test-time selection gap and adding a bit more analysis would make it outstanding.
