- Introduces sMCL, a method that trains ensembles to directly minimize oracle loss by backpropagating each example's gradient only to the lowest-loss (winning) ensemble member.  
- The "winner-take-gradient" rule encourages specialization of individual models on different data subsets or aspects.  
- sMCL is a stochastic, practical alternative to prior MCL methods that required iterative retraining and were computationally expensive for deep networks.  
- The method is general, parameter-free, and applicable to any architecture trained with SGD and standard loss functions.  
- Demonstrated empirically on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO) using CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures.  
- Shows strong empirical results: outperforms independent ensembles and baselines, with compelling quantitative and qualitative evidence of specialization.  
- Reports a significant practical speedup (~5x) over iterative MCL, enabling feasible training of modern deep ensembles.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and connects to real-world applications requiring multiple hypotheses.  
- Primary weakness: lack of discussion or methods for selecting or aggregating ensemble outputs at inference time when the oracle (ground truth) is unavailable.  
- This inference-time gap limits applicability in fully automated systems; proposing gating networks, uncertainty measures, or post-hoc rankers would strengthen the work.  
- Acknowledges that universal aggregation may be out of scope, but even a discussion of potential strategies is missing.  
- Minor issue: Algorithm 2 is provided as a low-resolution raster image and should be typeset/vectorized for readability.  
- For acceptance: novel, simple, practical, thoroughly evaluated, and clearly presented contribution to ensemble learning.  
- Against acceptance: insufficient treatment of how to exploit the trained ensemble at test time without an external oracle.
