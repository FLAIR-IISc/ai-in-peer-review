### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and practical algorithm for training ensembles of deep neural networks. The goal is to generate a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input, which is particularly useful for tasks with inherent ambiguity. The method is framed within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, where the objective is to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best prediction among the ensemble's outputs.

The core technical contribution is a "winner-take-gradient" training strategy based on stochastic block gradient descent. For each example in a mini-batch, the forward pass is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that produced the lowest loss. This simple mechanism allows all models to be trained concurrently, which is a significant improvement over prior MCL methods that required costly, iterative retraining of individual models. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks: image classification (CNNs), semantic segmentation (FCNs), and image captioning (CNN+RNNs). The results show that sMCL consistently outperforms baselines like independently trained ensembles and is significantly more efficient than previous MCL approaches. A key finding is that this training strategy leads to the automatic emergence of "specialization" among ensemble members.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple, diverse hypotheses from a model. The proposed sMCL algorithm provides a computationally efficient solution that makes the MCL framework viable for modern, large-scale deep learning architectures that are expensive to train. The method's simplicity, lack of hyperparameters, and agnosticism to model architecture and loss function make it a highly practical and broadly applicable contribution.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. By testing on three different and challenging domains (classification, segmentation, and captioning), the authors convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of their method. In all experiments, sMCL achieves superior oracle performance compared to several relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles and the significantly slower original MCL algorithm.

3.  **Elegant and Simple Idea:** The core idea of updating only the "winning" model for each example is elegant and simple to implement. The fact that this straightforward strategy yields impressive results and encourages diversity is a notable strength. The qualitative results, especially the visualizations of specialization in classification and the diverse captions, are compelling and provide valuable insight into the behavior of the trained ensembles.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity on Evaluation Protocol:** While the use of oracle metrics is standard for this type of evaluation, the manuscript could benefit from a clearer explanation of the testing protocol. Specifically, it should be made explicit that the oracle (which requires ground truth labels) is used purely for *evaluation* to measure the quality of the generated set of predictions, and not as part of the *inference* process in a real-world application. This would prevent any potential misunderstanding from readers unfamiliar with this line of work.

2.  **Inconsistent Terminology for Baselines:** The paper refers to the primary baseline as "Indp. ensembles" in figures, but uses "classical ensembles" and "regular ensembles" interchangeably in the text (e.g., line 181). While the "Baselines" section defines this method as training models independently with different random initializations, adopting a single, consistent term throughout the paper (e.g., "Independently Trained Ensembles") would improve clarity and readability.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for the Specialization Mechanism:** The paper compellingly argues that specialization among ensemble members emerges as a consequence of the sMCL training procedure. However, the underlying mechanism for this phenomenon could be discussed in more detail. Since the winning predictor is chosen on a per-example basis, it is not immediately obvious why a network would become a specialist for an entire *class* or semantic concept, rather than simply for a scattered collection of individual examples. A more detailed discussion on why this instance-level "winner-take-all" dynamic leads to specialization at the class level would strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, do examples from the same class share low-level features that cause one network to consistently "win" them early in training, reinforcing its specialization?

4.  **Positioning with Respect to Diversity Regularization:** The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including techniques that encourage diversity in ensembles through explicit regularization terms in the loss function. For example, work on negative correlation learning in neural networks (cited as [19, 26] in the paper) has explored similar goals of reducing error correlation between ensemble members, albeit with different mechanisms. Positioning sMCL more directly with respect to these alternative approaches to inducing diversity would provide a more complete picture of the field and better highlight the unique aspects of the proposed method.

**Overall Recommendation**

This is a well-written paper that presents a simple, effective, and broadly applicable algorithm for a relevant problem. The experimental results are strong, and the finding of emergent specialization is interesting. Despite some minor weaknesses in clarity and the depth of the analysis regarding the specialization mechanism, the contribution is solid. I recommend acceptance.
