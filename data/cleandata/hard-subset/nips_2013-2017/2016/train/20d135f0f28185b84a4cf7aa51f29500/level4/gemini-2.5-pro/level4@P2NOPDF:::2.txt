This paper introduces a novel and interesting method for training ensembles of deep neural networks, or any other model trained with gradient-based methods. The core idea is to employ an "oracle" during training that, for each instance, identifies the single best-performing ensemble member relative to the ground truth. The backpropagation update is then exclusively applied to this selected network. The authors posit that this selective update strategy encourages specialization among the individual networks, leading to a diverse ensemble where different members become experts on different subsets of the data. The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated through experiments on a variety of domains, where it achieves strong overall performance.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Clarity:** The central idea of using an oracle to guide specialized training within an ensemble is compelling and well-articulated. The paper presents this concept clearly and effectively.
*   **Effective Specialization:** The proposed method appears to successfully encourage specialization among the ensemble members, a desirable property for creating a diverse and powerful committee of models. The experimental results support this claim.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors have validated their approach across several different domains, demonstrating the general applicability and strong performance of the method.
*   **High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the limited discussion on how to effectively utilize the trained ensemble at inference time. The training procedure relies on an oracle (i.e., the ground truth label) to select the best model for each instance. However, at test time, this oracle is unavailable. The paper does not sufficiently address the critical question of how to aggregate the predictions from the specialized models to make a final decision.

While the authors mention that the approach provides a broad pool of possible results and is coherent with top-k evaluation metrics (e.g., for ImageNet), this aspect feels underdeveloped. For the method to be fully practical, a more thorough exploration of inference-time strategies is needed. For instance, how should one combine the outputs? A simple average may not be optimal given the specialized nature of the models. Could a gating network or a meta-learner be trained to predict which specialist to trust for a given unseen instance?

Addressing this would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution by providing a complete and practically applicable framework. While the optimal inference strategy might be somewhat problem-specific, a discussion of general principles or a baseline approach would be invaluable.

**Minor Comments**

The pseudo-code in Figure 2 is presented as a low-resolution raster image, which detracts from the paper's otherwise high-quality presentation. It would be preferable to typeset the pseudo-code directly using a package like `algorithm` or `algorithmicx`, or at a minimum, embed it as a high-quality vector graphic.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and effective method for training specialized deep neural network ensembles. The core contribution—the oracle-guided training procedure—is valuable and well-supported by empirical evidence. While the lack of a detailed strategy for inference-time ensemble exploitation is a notable omission that keeps the work from being outstanding, the contribution in its current form is substantial and worthy of publication. The proposed training method itself is a noteworthy advance. I recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors are strongly encouraged to address the issue of inference-time usage in their final version, as this would greatly enhance the practical impact of their work.
