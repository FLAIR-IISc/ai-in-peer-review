This paper addresses the problem of generating multiple diverse hypotheses from deep learning models, a scenario common in applications involving human interaction or downstream verification. The authors frame this as a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem and propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach to minimize oracle loss when training an ensemble of deep networks. The key contribution lies in formulating a practical optimization strategy—avoiding the costly retraining steps of prior MCL methods—that enables concurrent training of ensemble members and is applicable to various architectures and loss functions. The effectiveness of sMCL is demonstrated across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, achieving lower oracle error compared to existing methods and exhibiting interpretable emergent expertise within the ensemble.

The paper builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm [8, 9] and extends it to deep networks, a significant step forward given the challenges of training large ensembles. Previous work in ensemble learning often focuses on decorrelating models through resampling or data augmentation [27, 21], while sMCL intrinsically encourages diversity by minimizing oracle loss. The method elegantly avoids the iterative retraining required by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] and the sequential training of Dey et al. [5], making it well-suited for modern deep learning workflows. The authors appropriately highlight the advantages of their approach, particularly its simplicity, parameter-free nature, and broad applicability. 

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The proposed sMCL algorithm is technically sound and offers a practical solution to a relevant problem. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines across diverse tasks and architectures. The inclusion of qualitative analysis, illustrating the emergent specialization of ensemble members (e.g., class-wise clustering in image classification, diverse segmentations), adds significant value and supports the authors’ claims about interpretability. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, although some sections contain dense technical detail.

However, several points warrant further consideration. While the authors acknowledge the importance of initialization in deep learning, the impact of different initialization strategies (e.g., random vs. pre-trained weights) on the performance and diversity of the ensembles is not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed comparison, outlining the initialization procedures used for sMCL and the baselines, would strengthen the analysis.  Furthermore, while oracle accuracy is a natural metric for this task, a more explicit quantification of ensemble diversity (e.g., using metrics such as determinantal point processes or maximum mean discrepancy) would provide a more complete picture of the method's performance.  

The authors should also discuss the related work of Lee et al. [https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.06314], which proposes a method for training a diverse ensemble of deep networks.  A comparison to this work, explaining the key differences and potential advantages of sMCL, would be valuable.  Finally, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the computational cost associated with training larger ensembles and potential strategies for mitigating this cost.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
*   Strong experimental results across a variety of tasks and architectures.
*   Clear and well-motivated problem statement with practical relevance.
*   Demonstrated interpretability of the learned ensembles.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of previous MCL methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited analysis of the impact of initialization strategies.
*   Lack of explicit quantification of ensemble diversity.
*   Missing comparison to closely related work (Lee et al., 2015). 
*   Insufficient discussion of computational cost for large ensembles.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses outlined above, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a practical and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles, and the experimental results are convincing. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding initialization, diversity metrics, and related work.
