### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of generating a diverse set of high-quality predictions from a machine learning model, which is particularly useful for tasks where ambiguity is inherent and a downstream user or process can select the best option. The authors frame this as a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem, where the goal is to train an ensemble of models to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member on any given example.

The work builds directly on the MCL framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which uses an alternating block coordinate descent algorithm. The key limitation of that prior work is its requirement to train models to convergence in each iteration, making it infeasible for modern deep neural networks that require extensive training time. The primary contribution of this paper is a novel training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which adapts the MCL objective for training with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). In sMCL, for each example in a mini-batch, the gradient is computed for all ensemble members, but backpropagated only through the member that achieves the lowest loss (a "winner-take-gradient" approach). This allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently in a single pass.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks: image classification (CNNs on CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (FCNs on PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (CNN+RNN on MSCOCO). In all cases, sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines in oracle performance, while also being significantly more efficient than the original MCL algorithm. The paper also provides insightful qualitative analysis showing how the method encourages interpretable specialization among ensemble members.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Practicality:** The paper's main strength lies in its practical contribution. While the original MCL concept is powerful, it was not applicable to the deep learning models that dominate modern perception tasks. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a simple, elegant, and effective solution that makes the MCL paradigm practical for deep ensembles. This is a significant step that could enable wider adoption of oracle-based training.

2.  **Generality and Simplicity:** The sMCL method is appealingly simple to implement. It is agnostic to the specific network architecture and the choice of loss function, and it introduces no new hyperparameters. This generality is demonstrated through its successful application to classification, segmentation, and sequence generation tasks.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments across three challenging and diverse domains. The results consistently show that sMCL outperforms strong baselines, including independently trained ensembles, the original MCL, and a boosting-inspired method by Dey et al. [5]. The performance gains are substantial, and the 5x speedup over MCL is a compelling practical advantage.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed method is described precisely, and the experiments are well-designed. The qualitative results, particularly the visualizations of specialized segmentation masks and diverse image captions, are effective at illustrating the benefits of the approach.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection)**

1.  **Incremental Algorithmic Novelty:** My main concern is that the core algorithmic idea is a direct, albeit clever, stochastic adaptation of the optimization procedure in Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The conceptual leap from the original block coordinate descent to a stochastic "winner-takes-all" update is not enormous. The novelty lies more in the practical application and demonstration of its effectiveness for deep networks rather than in a fundamentally new optimization principle.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Analysis of Diversity:** The paper excellently demonstrates the *effects* of diversity through improved oracle performance and qualitative examples of specialization. However, the claims could be further strengthened by including a quantitative analysis of the diversity itself. For instance, for segmentation, the authors could report the average pairwise IoU between predictions from different ensemble members. For captioning, metrics like the proportion of unique n-grams (as reported in Table 1) are good, and perhaps a measure of semantic dissimilarity between generated sentences could be added.

*   **A More Direct Baseline:** The comparison with the original MCL [8] is valuable but could be made more direct. The original MCL is a full-batch method, while sMCL is a mini-batch method. An interesting ablation would be to implement a mini-batch version of the original MCL, where assignments are updated per-batch but models are still updated based on their assigned examples within that batch. This would help isolate whether the performance gain comes from the concurrent training schedule of sMCL versus the alternating nature of MCL.

**Conclusion**

This is a well-executed and clearly presented paper that makes a valuable practical contribution. It successfully bridges the gap between the Multiple Choice Learning framework and modern deep learning by introducing a simple and effective SGD-based training algorithm. While the algorithmic novelty, when viewed in isolation, is incremental compared to prior work [8], the authors have convincingly demonstrated that this "small change" has a significant impact, making an entire class of methods feasible for deep networks. The strong and comprehensive empirical results across multiple domains underscore the importance of this contribution. The strengths in significance, practicality, and empirical rigor outweigh the concern about incremental novelty. Therefore, I am in favor of accepting this paper.
