Summary
This paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep networks by proposing Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” strategy integrated into standard stochastic gradient descent. For each training example in a mini-batch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives the gradient update, thereby interleaving example assignment and model training without expensive alternating retraining. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all ensemble members concurrently. The authors evaluate sMCL on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCNs), and image captioning (MSCOCO with CNN+LSTM), reporting consistent gains in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, MCL, and a boosting-style baseline, and a notable training speedup over MCL. Qualitatively, sMCL ensembles exhibit emergent specialization across tasks.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.) by replacing the alternating assignment/retraining scheme with a stochastic block gradient approach suitable for deep nets. It complements work on generating diverse outputs from single models (e.g., diverse M-best for MRFs) and ensemble diversity methods (resampling, negative correlation, generalist/specialist designs), offering a practical, end-to-end solution for modern architectures and structured outputs.

Strengths
- Importance: The MCL setting is relevant to many NIPS areas where downstream oracles can select among multiple hypotheses.
- Simplicity and practicality: The winner-take-gradient rule is easy to implement, parameter-free, and compatible with standard deep learning toolchains.
- Breadth of evaluation: Results across three tasks and architectures, with both quantitative oracle metrics and qualitative analyses showing interpretable specialization.
- Efficiency: Concurrent training avoids the prohibitive cost of iterative retraining in prior MCL, yielding substantial speedups.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles and strong baselines, with faster convergence on the oracle loss.

Weaknesses
- “Diversity” is not explicitly enforced: While optimizing oracle loss induces emergent specialization, the method lacks an explicit diversity regularizer. The paper’s framing and title emphasize diversity more than the objective guarantees.
- Incremental novelty: Relative to the MCL relaxation, the main contribution is an SGD-compatible training mechanism. Conceptually valuable but arguably incremental.
- Clarity/notation issues: Minor typographical errors and phrasing (see suggestions below) detract from presentation.

Quality
The method is sound and well-motivated. The winner-take-gradient update follows directly from the subgradient of the MCL upper-bound objective. Experiments are thorough for breadth; additional ablations (e.g., tie-handling, effect of k-best variants, stability across seeds) would further strengthen the empirical case but are not essential.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with intuitive figures and qualitative examples. Minor issues in the abstract and notation should be fixed.

Originality
Moderate. The key idea—stochastic interleaving of assignment and training—is a natural but meaningful extension that makes MCL practical for deep networks.

Significance
High practical significance: sMCL is easy to adopt, scales to modern architectures, and improves oracle performance across tasks where multiple plausible outputs exist.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm that broadens the applicability of MCL to deep learning.
- Strong empirical results across heterogeneous tasks with consistent oracle improvements.
- Clear speed advantage over prior MCL training procedures.
- Emergent specialization provides interpretable behavior without architectural changes.

Cons for acceptance
- No explicit diversity mechanism despite heavy emphasis on “diversity.”
- Conceptual contribution may be viewed as an incremental refinement of existing MCL formulations.
- Minor presentation/notation issues.

Minor suggestions
- Rewrite the first sentence of the abstract for clarity and impact.
- Temper claims around “diversity” or clearly state that diversity emerges implicitly via oracle-loss optimization rather than being enforced.
- Notation: in the sentence “we fix the form of …” (around line 108), the first symbol should be g, not f.
- There is an extra period mid-sentence around line 115; please remove.
- Briefly describe tie-breaking for equal losses and comment on its effect in practice.

One question
For the deep-learning MCL baseline, how did you ensure that each network was trained “to convergence” before reassignment? Early stopping (or insufficient convergence) in the alternating MCL schedule can significantly hurt ensemble performance and may bias the comparison with sMCL. Please clarify the stopping criteria, computational budget, and whether additional meta-iterations were tested.
