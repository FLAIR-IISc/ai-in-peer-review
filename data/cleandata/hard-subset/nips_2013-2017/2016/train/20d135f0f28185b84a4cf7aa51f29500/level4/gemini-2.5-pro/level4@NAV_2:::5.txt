This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," where the objective is to minimize the error of the best prediction within the ensemble for each data point. This extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework, previously applied to other models, to the context of deep networks. The key technical contribution is a novel stochastic block gradient descent optimization strategy. During each training step, for a given example, the gradient is backpropagated only through the single network in the ensemble that currently yields the lowest loss. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently in an end-to-end fashion, which is a significant practical advantage over prior methods like the original MCL that require costly iterative retraining.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the important problem of generating multiple, diverse hypotheses, which is crucial for many real-world applications involving human interaction or downstream verification. The proposed sMCL method is an elegant and practical adaptation of the MCL idea to deep learning. The formulation as a stochastic optimization algorithm that trains all models in parallel is a novel and valuable contribution.
*   **Quality:** The method is well-motivated and technically sound. A major strength is its simplicity and generality: it is parameter-free, agnostic to the specific network architecture or loss function, and easy to implement. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct and challenging domains: image classification (CNNs), semantic segmentation (FCNs), and image captioning (CNN+RNNs). The results consistently show that sMCL outperforms several relevant baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed algorithm is described precisely, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The qualitative analyses and visualizations of the emergent specializations among ensemble members are particularly insightful and provide compelling evidence for the method's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Clarity:** The importance of initialization is not fully addressed. For the semantic segmentation experiments, the models are initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. It is unclear if this pre-training is critical for good performance or if the method works well from random initialization. To assess the method's robustness, results from random initialization should be included. Furthermore, it is not explicitly stated whether the baseline methods were given the same pre-trained initialization, which is crucial for a fair comparison.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation relies heavily on oracle metrics (e.g., oracle accuracy). While this metric aligns directly with the training objective, it does not explicitly measure the diversity of the solutions produced. The paper's central claim is about training *diverse* deep ensembles, so including a quantitative analysis of this diversity (e.g., using measures of disagreement or variance between the predictions of ensemble members) would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   **Related Work:** The paper overlooks a highly relevant piece of prior work: Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks," CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015). This work proposes a very similar idea of training an ensemble of networks to produce diverse outputs. A discussion of the conceptual similarities and differences, and ideally an empirical comparison, is needed to properly contextualize the contribution of sMCL within the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a simple, novel, and general algorithm for a significant problem: training diverse deep ensembles.
2.  The proposed method is computationally efficient compared to prior work (e.g., MCL), making it practical for modern deep learning architectures.
3.  The effectiveness of the approach is convincingly demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on three different tasks and architectures.
4.  The analysis provides interesting insights into how task-specific specializations can emerge automatically from the training process.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The experimental details regarding initialization are unclear, potentially affecting the fairness of the comparisons with baselines.
2.  The evaluation lacks quantitative metrics for diversity, which is a core concept of the paper.
3.  A key, highly related paper (Lee et al., 2015) is not cited or compared against, which is a notable omission in the related work.
