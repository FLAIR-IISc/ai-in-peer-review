## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission – [Paper Title]

This paper addresses the challenging problem of multiple choice (MCL) learning in the context of deep neural networks. Existing MCL approaches often don't translate well to deep learning models. The authors propose a surprisingly simple yet effective solution: a “winner-takes-the-gradient” layer integrated into backpropagation, which allows for simultaneous training of the constituent networks and solution of the assignment problem inherent in an ensemble setting. The method is evaluated on a diverse set of tasks – image classification, image segmentation, and image captioning – demonstrating promising performance across the board.

**Strengths:**

The problem addressed is undoubtedly relevant and of high interest to the NIPS community. Scaling MCL to deep learning architectures is a significant hurdle, and finding tractable solutions is crucial for advancing research in ensemble methods. I particularly appreciate the elegance and simplicity of the proposed approach. It avoids complex architectural modifications and instead leverages a clever modification to the standard backpropagation algorithm. The empirical evaluation is also a strong point, showcasing the method’s versatility through experiments on three distinct tasks. This broad applicability suggests the technique is not overly specific to a particular domain.

**Weaknesses:**

My primary concern lies with the paper’s emphasis on “diversity.” The title and much of the motivation focus on achieving a diverse ensemble, yet the model itself doesn't explicitly enforce diversity in any way. I anticipated a clear mechanism for promoting dissimilarity between the learners, and was disappointed to find this was not implemented. The observed improvements appear to arise from the winner-takes-the-gradient mechanism itself, rather than a dedicated diversity term. While the results are good, framing the contribution around diversity feels misleading. This should be carefully re-evaluated in the revision.

Furthermore, while the method represents a valuable contribution, it feels like an incremental improvement over the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al. The authors should more explicitly contextualize their work within the existing literature and more clearly articulate the novel aspects of their approach beyond the specific application to deep networks. A more detailed comparison to the Guzman et al. work, outlining the specific advantages of this method, would be beneficial.

**Minor Suggestions:**

* The opening sentence of the abstract could be rewritten for clarity and impact. It currently lacks a strong hook.
* The emphasis on “diversity” throughout the paper should be toned down to accurately reflect the model's actual implementation.
* On line 108, the first instance of “f” should be “g” in the phrase "we fixed the form of…".
* Line 115 contains an extraneous period in the middle of a sentence.

**Question for the Authors:**

For the baseline MCL implementation with deep learning networks, how did you ensure convergence of each individual network to a reasonable solution? Prematurely halting training of the learners could significantly impact the overall ensemble performance. Please provide details on the convergence criteria employed or explain how the learning schedule was designed to mitigate this risk. Understanding this aspect is critical for evaluating the fairness of the comparison.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns regarding the overstated emphasis on diversity, this paper presents a solid and well-executed contribution. The proposed method is simple, effective, and broadly applicable. Addressing the issues raised above, particularly regarding the diversity claim and the contextualization within existing work, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing these points in a revision.
