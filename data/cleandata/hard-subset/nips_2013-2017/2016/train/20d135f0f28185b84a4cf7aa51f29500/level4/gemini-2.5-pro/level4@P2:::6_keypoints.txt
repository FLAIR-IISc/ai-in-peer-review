- Summary: Proposes sMCL, a practical "winner-take-gradient" algorithm for training diverse neural network ensembles by backpropagating only through the lowest-loss member per example to minimize oracle loss.  
- Technical contribution: Introduces a stochastic block gradient descent scheme allowing concurrent training of ensemble members, avoiding costly iterative retraining of prior MCL methods.  
- Empirical results: Demonstrates across classification, segmentation, and captioning that sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and is far more efficient than original MCL, with emergent model specialization.  
- Strength — Significance & Practicality: Provides a simple, hyperparameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic method that makes MCL viable for large-scale deep models.  
- Strength — Strong Empirical Validation: Thorough experiments on three domains convincingly show superior oracle performance versus relevant baselines.  
- Strength — Elegance & Interpretability: The winner-take-gradient idea is simple to implement and yields compelling qualitative evidence of specialization and diversity.  
- Weakness — Evaluation clarity: Needs explicit clarification that oracle metrics use ground truth only for evaluation, not for inference in real applications.  
- Weakness — Inconsistent baseline terminology: Uses multiple names for the independent-ensemble baseline; should adopt a single consistent term.  
- Weakness — Specialization mechanism underexplained: Lacks detailed justification for why instance-level winner-take-all updates produce class- or concept-level specialization.  
- Weakness — Related work positioning: Should discuss diversity-encouraging regularization approaches (e.g., negative correlation learning) to better situate sMCL.  
- Overall recommendation: Solid, well-written contribution with strong results and interesting emergent behavior; recommend acceptance.
