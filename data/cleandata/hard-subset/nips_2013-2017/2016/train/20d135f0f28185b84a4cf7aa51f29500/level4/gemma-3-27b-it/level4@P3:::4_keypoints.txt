- Introduces sMCL, a stochastic SGD adaptation of Multiple Choice Learning to train diverse deep ensembles minimizing oracle loss.
- Motivation is solid: producing multiple hypotheses is useful for downstream processes and human evaluation.
- Novelty is incremental: sMCL adapts prior MCL work to deep networks, focusing on an efficient implementation rather than a new conceptual approach.
- Main advantage is computational efficiency by avoiding costly retraining required by original MCL, but not a conceptual breakthrough.
- Section 3 (technical contribution) is brief and would benefit from a more detailed explanation of gradient computation and properties.
- Insufficient discussion of how sMCL compares to standard multi-task learning, regularization, or data-resampling ensemble strategies.
- Notation is generally clear but could use more consistent subscripts for clarity.
- Experimental evaluation is a strength, demonstrating applicability across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks.
- Reported performance gains are often modest and sometimes marginal over baseline ensembles.
- Lack of comparison to stronger, more recent baselines (e.g., modern FCN architectures on PASCAL VOC 2012) weakens empirical claims.
- Omission of original MCL experiments is justified by implementation difficulty, but limited-iteration MCL results would be informative.
- More ablation studies (ensemble size, loss variants) are needed to assess sensitivity and optimal configuration.
- Arguments for acceptance: practical problem, computationally efficient method, multi-task applicability, and interpretable member specialization.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, small gains over strong baselines, shallow technical exposition, and incomplete experimental rigor.
- Overall recommendation: weak accept contingent on addressing technical depth and strengthening baseline comparisons.
