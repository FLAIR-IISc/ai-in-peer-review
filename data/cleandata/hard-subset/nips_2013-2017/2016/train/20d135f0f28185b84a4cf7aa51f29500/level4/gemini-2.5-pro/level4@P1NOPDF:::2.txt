This paper proposes a novel method for training ensembles of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), or other gradient-based models. The core idea is to use an oracle during the training process that, for each data instance, identifies the single best-performing member of the ensemble. Backpropagation is then applied exclusively to this selected network. This approach has the advantage of encouraging each network to specialize, resulting in an ensemble whose members collectively provide strong coverage over the range of possible outputs. The method is evaluated across a variety of domains and demonstrates impressive overall performance.

This is a very good paper that presents an interesting and effective idea. The use of an oracle to guide backpropagation to the most suitable network at the instance level appears to enforce specialization quite well, particularly in classification contexts. The overall presentation is excellent, and the experiments are comprehensive, spanning several domains. This is a strong work that deserves to be published.

My main reservation, which prevents me from rating the paper as outstanding, is the lack of discussion on how to effectively utilize the trained ensemble at inference time. The training procedure relies on an oracle, but the paper does not develop a corresponding strategy for using the resulting collection of specialized models. The authors state that the ensemble provides a broad pool of possible results and that the approach is consistent with top-k evaluation metrics (e.g., for ImageNet), but this point is not explored in detail. This is a significant omission that limits the immediate practical applicability of the proposed method. A discussion of how to combine or select from the specialized models at test time is a crucial missing piece for a complete, end-to-end approach.

Nevertheless, the contribution to training methodology is significant in its own right. The challenge of deploying the resulting ensemble could be considered a separate, potentially problem-specific, area for future work.

Finally, a minor point on presentation: the pseudo-code in Figure 2 appears to be a rasterized image embedded in the paper. For clarity and professionalism, it would be much better to typeset the pseudo-code directly or, at a minimum, embed it as a vector graphic.
