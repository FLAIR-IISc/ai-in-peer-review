The authors of this paper propose a stochastic gradient descent-based approach to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training diverse deep ensembles, which they call Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). This work builds upon the MCL paradigm introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], adapting it to the context of deep networks. The main contribution of this paper is the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach that minimizes the oracle loss in ensembles of deep networks.

The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, highlighting the limitations of traditional supervised learning in scenarios where a single prediction is not sufficient. They demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL on a wide range of tasks and deep architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The technical part of the paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The authors provide a concise explanation of their proposed framework, highlighting the key differences between sMCL and alternative training methods. They also provide a detailed analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple tasks and comparing the proposed method with several strong baselines. The results show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure. The authors also provide qualitative results, demonstrating the diversity and interpretability of the solutions produced by sMCL ensembles.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors could provide more insight into the theoretical foundations of their proposed framework, particularly in relation to the optimization process. Additionally, while the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, the authors could provide more detailed analysis of the results, particularly in terms of the performance gains achieved by sMCL.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, providing a novel and effective approach to training diverse deep ensembles. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of their proposed framework, and the results show significant performance gains over strong baselines.
