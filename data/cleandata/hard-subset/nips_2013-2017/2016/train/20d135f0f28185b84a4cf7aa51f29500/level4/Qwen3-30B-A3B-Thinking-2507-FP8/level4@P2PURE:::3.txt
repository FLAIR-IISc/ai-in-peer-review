

This paper addresses the multiple-choice learning (MCL) problem in the context of deep neural networks, where existing MCL approaches do not directly extend to deep learning. The authors propose a simple solution involving a winner-takes-all gradient mechanism integrated into backpropagation, enabling the simultaneous training of learners and resolution of the assignment problem within an ensemble framework. Experimental results demonstrate strong performance across diverse tasks: image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.  

Strengths:  
- The MCL problem for deep learning is sufficiently significant for the NIPS community.  
- The proposed method exhibits notable simplicity.  
- Experiments validate performance across three distinct tasks.  

Weaknesses:  
- The primary concern is the extensive emphasis on "diversity" (including in the title), despite the model not explicitly enforcing diversity. I was initially excited to see how the authors incorporated diversity into their approach but was disappointed to find no explicit diversity mechanism.  
- The proposed solution represents only a minor incremental step relative to the relaxation framework introduced by Guzman et al.  

Minor suggestions:  
- Rewrite the first sentence of the abstract for clarity.  
- Tone down the emphasis on "diversity" throughout.  
- Correct the typo on line 108: change the first "f" to "g" in "we fixed the form of...".  
- Remove the extraneous period in line 115, where a period appears mid-sentence.  

One question:  
For the baseline MCL method using deep learning, how did the authors ensure each network in the ensemble had converged to a reasonable solution? Early termination of individual learners could significantly impact ensemble performance.
