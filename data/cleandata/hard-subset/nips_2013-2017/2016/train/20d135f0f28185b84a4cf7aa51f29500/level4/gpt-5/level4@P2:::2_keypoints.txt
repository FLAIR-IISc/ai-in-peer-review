- Proposal: Introduces sMCL, an SGD "winner-take-gradient" training method for deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss by backpropagating only through the lowest-loss member per example.  
- Method properties: Architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, trains ensemble members concurrently.  
- Experiments: Evaluated on CIFAR-10 (classification), Pascal VOC (segmentation), and MSCOCO (captioning) with consistent oracle-metric gains.  
- Baselines/results: Outperforms independent ensembles, prior MCL with much less training time, and a submodular/boosting-style approach.  
- Qualitative findings: Emergent, interpretable specialization of experts (e.g., class/structure-specific specialists).  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on MCL and diverse-output literature but contributes a practical concurrent SGD formulation for deep nets without gating or distillation.  
- Technical quality: Simple, sound derivation from an upper-bound objective; broad empirical evaluation and meaningful speedup over iterative MCL.  
- Limitations in evaluation: Focuses on oracle metrics; lacks analysis of inference-time selection, stability (ties, expert imbalance), and compute/memory trade-offs at test time.  
- Clarity: Well-written with effective visualizations; pseudocode should be provided as text/vector for reproducibility.  
- Originality: Novel and practically important application of winner-take-gradient SGD to deep networks, with new observations about emergent expert behavior.  
- Significance: Practical approach for multi-hypothesis prediction in ambiguous tasks; likely of broad interest and to inspire follow-up work.  
- Strengths: Elegant SGD integration, generality, concurrent training, strong empirical gains across modalities, interpretable specialization, and practical speedup.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: Needs inference-time selection strategy or simulation, discussion of inference cost/trade-offs, stability diagnostics (assignment entropy, per-expert share, tie-breaking), and comparisons to learned-gating MoE and top-k variants.  
- Presentation/reproducibility: Add implementation details (initialization, tie-breaking, hyperparameters) and replace rasterized pseudocode.  
- Acceptance arguments: Practical, general, consistently improves oracle metrics and scales MCL to deep nets with insightful analyses.  
- Rejection arguments: Missing inference-selection methods, incomplete comparison to alternative expert-selection frameworks, and missing analyses of stability and compute trade-offs.  
- Overall: Strong, well-executed paper that makes MCL feasible for deep networks and is recommended for acceptance despite practical gaps that can be addressed later.
