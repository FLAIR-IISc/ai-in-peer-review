Summary:
The paper proposes a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training procedure for ensemble models whose loss is defined as the minimum loss over ensemble members for each example, following the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. By moving from a full-dataset or combinatorial assignment approach to an SGD-based formulation, the authors make the method practical for training deep neural networks while preserving the diversity-inducing property of the min-over-members objective. The paper is clearly written and the idea is intuitive.

Strengths:
- Practical contribution: Recasting the MCL objective for SGD makes the approach scalable and applicable to modern deep architectures.
- Clarity and simplicity: The algorithmic description is easy to follow and appears straightforward to implement.
- Potential impact: Enabling MCL-style training for deep networks could broaden the use of diverse, specialized ensembles.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Incremental novelty: The core objective and diversity mechanism are inherited from [8]; the main contribution is an optimization approach (SGD) rather than a new modeling idea. While important in practice, the conceptual advance over [8] feels modest.
- Limited analysis of diversity: The paper would benefit from a deeper empirical and quantitative analysis of how and why diversity emerges, how it correlates with performance, and under what conditions it persists.
- Missing baseline: It would be informative to compare against the original MCL when trained with mini-batches (or a straightforward stochastic approximation), to isolate the gain from the specific SGD variant proposed versus simply using batching with [8].

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Objective and subgradients: Since the min-over-members loss is non-smooth, please clarify how subgradients are chosen, especially under ties. Are the stochastic updates unbiased (or consistent) estimates of a subgradient of the population objective?
- Assignment granularity: Is example-to-member assignment performed per mini-batch or with any memory across batches/epochs? How sensitive is performance to batch size and to the number of ensemble members?
- Stability and optimization: Provide results on convergence behavior, variance of assignments early vs. late in training, and any techniques (e.g., temperature/soft-min, entropy regularization, or delayed specialization) that improve stability.
- Diversity metrics: Report quantitative measures such as pairwise disagreement, error correlation, KL divergence between predictive distributions, or negative correlation metrics. Relate these to accuracy to show whether diversity is beneficial rather than incidental.
- Ablations: 
  - Random assignment vs. min-loss assignment.
  - Training members independently without the min-loss coupling.
  - Soft-min (log-sum-exp) vs. hard min.
  - Different batch sizes and learning-rate schedules.
- Comparisons: In addition to [8], consider comparisons to other diversity-inducing or specialization methods (e.g., mixture-of-experts with gating, negative correlation learning, bagging/boosting variants, snapshot ensembles) to contextualize the gains.
- Computational cost: Report wall-clock time, memory footprint, and total FLOPs relative to training K independent models and to [8]. If the method reduces compute by avoiding global assignments, quantify the savings.
- Test-time strategy: Clarify how predictions are produced at inference. If the training loss is the min over members, what is the test-time selection rule (e.g., argmax likelihood across members, learned gating, or averaging)? Discuss any mismatch between training and inference and its impact on performance.

Overall evaluation:
Despite the incremental nature of the algorithmic novelty relative to [8], the authors make a persuasive case that their SGD formulation substantially improves practicality and scalability, enabling application to deep neural networks and larger datasets. The paper is well written and the idea is clean. With stronger empirical analysis of diversity, a comparison to a mini-batch version of MCL, and additional ablations, the contribution would be even clearer. On balance, I am in favor of acceptance due to the practical utility and potential impact on ensemble training for deep models.
