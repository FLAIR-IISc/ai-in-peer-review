This paper proposes a simple solution to the multiple choice learning (MCL) problem for deep networks, addressing a significant gap in current solutions that do not directly extend to deep networks. The authors demonstrate that by incorporating a winner-takes-the-gradient layer in backpropagation, they can effectively combine the training of individual learners with the assignment problem in an ensemble setting. Experimental results showcase the method's efficacy across a wide range of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the problem of MCL for deep learning is highly relevant to the NIPS community, making it a timely and important contribution. Secondly, the proposed method is commendably simple, which is a significant advantage. Lastly, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three diverse tasks.

However, there are also some weaknesses that need to be addressed. A major concern is that the paper extensively motivates the importance of "diversity" (even incorporating it into the title), yet the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. This discrepancy between the motivation and the actual method may be misleading. Furthermore, the proposed solution can be seen as an incremental step, building upon the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions for improvement include rephrasing the first sentence of the abstract to better capture the essence of the paper. Additionally, the emphasis on "diversity" should be toned down to avoid confusion. There are also some minor errors, such as the incorrect use of "f" instead of "g" in line 108 and an extra period in the middle of a sentence in line 115.

One question that arises from the paper is how the authors ensured that each of the networks in the baseline MCL with deep learning converged to reasonable results. It is possible that cutting the learners early on may have significantly impacted the ensemble performance, and clarification on this point would be beneficial.
