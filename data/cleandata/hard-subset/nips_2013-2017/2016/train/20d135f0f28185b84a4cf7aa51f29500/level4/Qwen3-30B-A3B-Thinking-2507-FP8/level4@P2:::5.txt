

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss—the error of the best prediction in a set of M outputs. The authors formalize Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep networks, addressing a key limitation of prior work (Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS 2015) which required costly iterative retraining. sMCL uses a stochastic gradient descent approach where, during backpropagation, gradients are only applied to the ensemble member producing the lowest loss for each example. This enables concurrent training of all ensemble members, making it efficient and applicable to modern deep architectures without architectural or loss-function modifications. The method is validated across three tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), consistently outperforming baselines (including classical ensembles and MCL) by 6-10% in oracle metrics while being 5x faster than MCL. Crucially, sMCL automatically induces interpretable specialization among ensemble members—e.g., classifiers focusing on distinct classes, segmenters capturing different object shapes—which aligns with the paper’s core thesis that multiple outputs better handle ambiguity in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality & Significance (High):* The technical contribution is sound and well-justified. The sMCL optimization strategy is rigorously derived from the oracle loss objective (Eq. 1–2) and validated through comprehensive experiments. The method directly addresses a practical gap in deep learning: most models produce a single prediction despite real-world systems often benefiting from multiple hypotheses (e.g., autonomous vehicles needing to handle ambiguous object classifications). The results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines, with consistent gains across diverse architectures and tasks. The paper clearly articulates the significance: sMCL enables "interpretable representations of task ambiguity" (e.g., segmenting a horse-cow hybrid as either class) where single-prediction models fail, advancing the state-of-the-art in ensemble learning for structured outputs.  
*Originality (High):* While MCL was introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2015), sMCL is the first to adapt it to deep networks via stochastic optimization. It differs fundamentally from prior ensemble diversity methods (e.g., data resampling [27], boosting [5], or explicit diversity loss [2]) by leveraging oracle loss minimization to *automatically* induce specialization without additional constraints. The paper correctly contextualizes this against related work (Section 2), emphasizing that sMCL is the first practical approach for deep ensembles.  
*Clarity (High):* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation (Section 3) is precise, the algorithm (Algorithm 2) is clearly described, and results (Section 4) are presented with sufficient detail and visualizations (e.g., Figure 4c–e showing class specialization). The "Interpretable Expertise" analysis effectively links training dynamics to real-world utility.

**Weaknesses & Opportunities for Improvement:**  
*Clarity of Baseline Comparison (Moderate):* The paper lacks explicit details on baseline initialization protocols. For instance, sMCL is initialized from a pre-trained ensemble, but it is unclear whether classical ensembles (Indp.) and MCL use identical initializations (e.g., same random seeds or pre-trained weights). This is critical for fair comparison, as random initialization could disproportionately affect methods sensitive to starting points. The authors should clarify this in the experimental section.  
*Diversity Analysis (Moderate):* While oracle accuracy is the primary metric, the paper does not quantify *diversity* between ensemble members (e.g., via pairwise KL divergence or mutual information). The qualitative examples (Figures 5b, 7) illustrate diversity but lack statistical support. A brief analysis of diversity metrics would strengthen the claim that sMCL *automatically* produces diverse outputs.  
*Comparison with Lee et al. (CoRR 2015) (Moderate):* The draft review rightly flags a closely related work: Lee et al. ("Why M Heads are Better than One," CoRR 2015). This paper also trains diverse deep ensembles but uses a *different objective* (negative correlation loss) to encourage diversity, not oracle loss minimization. sMCL’s focus on directly optimizing the downstream oracle metric is distinct and more aligned with the MCL paradigm, but the paper should explicitly discuss this distinction and why sMCL outperforms Lee et al.’s approach (e.g., in oracle accuracy). The current comparison to Lee et al. is insufficient; a brief ablation showing sMCL’s oracle performance versus Lee et al. would be valuable.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong, high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to ensemble learning and deep learning for ambiguous prediction tasks. The technical innovation (sMCL) is novel, well-motivated, and validated through rigorous experiments across multiple domains. The clarity of writing and analysis is exemplary. The weaknesses noted are minor and addressable with minor revisions (e.g., clarifying initialization, adding diversity metrics, and properly contextualizing Lee et al.). The work is highly relevant to NIPS, advancing the field by providing a practical, efficient method to train diverse deep ensembles that directly optimize for downstream utility.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper’s significance (solving a real-world problem with a scalable method), originality (first SGD-based MCL for deep networks), and quality (sound theory, robust experiments) meet NIPS’ highest standards. The suggested clarifications would further strengthen an already excellent contribution.
