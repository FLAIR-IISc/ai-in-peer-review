The paper proposes a method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks (and, more generally, any gradient-based model) using an oracle that, for each training instance, identifies which ensemble member is optimal. Gradients are then backpropagated only through the selected network. This encourages the members to specialize, yielding an ensemble that collectively covers most (if not all) outputs of interest.

Results are reported across multiple domains and are consistently strong. The idea is clearly presented: the oracle-guided selection of the network with the best output (relative to the true label) effectively enforces specialization in classification settings. The paper is well written, and the experimental evaluation is carried out on several datasets. Overall, this is a solid piece of work that deserves to be published.

I would have considered the paper outstanding if it had also addressed how to exploit the trained ensemble at inference time. Training relies on the oracle, but the paper does not develop how to use the resulting ensemble in practice, beyond noting that it yields a broad pool of candidate results and aligns with top-k metrics (e.g., ImageNet). This missing component is important for making the approach fully usable in real-world settings. That said, the gap is likely problem-specific, and the core contribution remains valuable even in its current form.
