This paper proposes a novel approach to the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem, where the learners are deep networks. The authors introduce Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a stochastic gradient descent-based method that minimizes the oracle loss directly. The proposed approach is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of MCL for deep learning is of significant importance to the NIPS community. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the MCL problem and its relevance to various applications. The proposed sMCL method is also well-explained, and the experimental results demonstrate its efficacy across a wide range of tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

One of the strengths of the paper is its simplicity. The authors propose a straightforward solution to the MCL problem, which is easy to understand and implement. The experimental results are also convincing, showing that sMCL outperforms existing baselines and produces sets of outputs with high oracle performance.

However, there are some concerns with the paper. Firstly, the authors extensively motivate "diversity" in the introduction, but the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. This might be seen as a weakness, as the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how diversity is achieved. Secondly, the proposed solution is an incremental step considering the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions include rewriting the first sentence of the abstract, toning down the emphasis on diversity, and correcting minor errors in the text (e.g., line 108, the first "f" should be "g" in "we fixed the form of ..").

One question that arises is how the authors ensured that each of the networks in the baseline MCL with deep learning converged to reasonable results. Cutting the learners early on might significantly affect the ensemble performance, and it would be helpful to provide more details on this aspect.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed sMCL method is a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. However, addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of the proposed approach.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to the MCL problem for deep learning.
* The proposed sMCL method is simple to implement and agnostic to both architecture and loss function.
* The experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL across a wide range of tasks.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The model does not explicitly enforce diversity, which might be seen as a weakness.
* The proposed solution is an incremental step considering the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.
* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how diversity is achieved.
* The authors do not provide sufficient details on how they ensured that each of the networks in the baseline MCL with deep learning converged to reasonable results.
