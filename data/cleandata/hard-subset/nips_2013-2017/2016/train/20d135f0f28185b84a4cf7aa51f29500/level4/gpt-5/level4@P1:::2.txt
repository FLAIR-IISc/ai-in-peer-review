Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-friendly approach to train ensembles of deep networks to minimize an oracle loss. For each training instance, the method forwards the batch through all M ensemble members, computes the loss per member, and backpropagates the gradient only through the member with the lowest loss (“winner-take-gradient”). This encourages ensemble members to specialize and collectively cover diverse, high-probability modes of the output space. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all members concurrently.

The work builds on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, which formalizes training to minimize an oracle loss over multiple predictions. Unlike prior MCL formulations that alternate between assignment and retraining to convergence (which is impractical for modern deep networks), sMCL interleaves assignment and learning at the minibatch level via standard backpropagation. The paper demonstrates the method on three domains—image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCNs), and image captioning (MSCOCO with CNN+RNN)—and reports consistently strong oracle metrics. The qualitative analyses show interpretable specialization: label-space partitioning in classification and complementary segmentations/captions that reflect underlying ambiguity. This aligns with the top-k evaluation used in settings like ImageNet classification.

Quality
The technical idea is clearly described and straightforward to implement. The optimization is well motivated by inspecting the gradient of the MCL upper-bound objective: only the minimum-loss predictor should receive a nonzero gradient per example. The experiments are run across diverse tasks and architectures, and they compare against reasonable baselines: independent ensembles, MCL with a fixed number of meta-iterations, and a boosting-like sequential method. Reported results indicate that sMCL achieves higher oracle performance than independent ensembles and the boosting-style method, and is competitive with or better than MCL while being substantially faster (about 5× in the reported setup). The paper provides qualitative evidence of specialization that is consistent with the training objective.

One important limitation is that the paper focuses on training under an oracle but does not develop how to exploit the resulting ensembles at inference time beyond noting coherence with top-k metrics. In many applications, the downstream “oracle” is nontrivial; practical guidance or evaluation on selection/reranking mechanisms would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized, with intuitive motivation and informative qualitative examples. One minor presentation issue: the pseudo-code in Fig. 2 is embedded as a raster image. It would be preferable to typeset the pseudo-code directly in the paper or at least include it as a vector graphic for readability.

Originality
The core novelty is a practical stochastic training procedure for MCL with deep networks, avoiding costly alternating retraining. While conceptually simple, this is a meaningful innovation that makes MCL applicable to modern architectures and losses, and it empirically induces specialization without architectural changes or diversity-promoting heuristics.

Significance
Given its simplicity, generality, and empirical gains on multiple tasks, this method is likely to be adopted by practitioners seeking to generate diverse, high-recall predictions. The main missing piece for broader impact is a more thorough treatment of how to use such ensembles in deployed systems (e.g., how the “oracle” is instantiated or approximated in practice), which is admittedly problem-specific but central to end-to-end utility.

Pros
- Simple, parameter-free, and broadly applicable training scheme that integrates seamlessly with SGD.
- Demonstrated across classification, segmentation, and captioning with strong oracle performance.
- Clear speed advantage over traditional MCL due to concurrent training and batchwise assignments.
- Emergent, interpretable specialization among ensemble members without bespoke architectures.

Cons
- The paper does not address how to exploit the trained ensembles at inference time beyond top-k evaluations; practical downstream selection/reranking remains unspecified.
- Minor presentation issue: pseudo-code provided as raster images reduces readability.

Recommendation
A strong paper that presents a practical and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles under an oracle loss. The idea is well executed and broadly useful. Addressing the deployment/usage aspect of the resulting ensembles would elevate the work from very good to outstanding, but even in its current form the contribution merits publication.
