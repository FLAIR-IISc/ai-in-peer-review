This paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks that minimize the oracle loss. The oracle loss is a useful metric when interacting with users, as it allows the model to provide multiple highly likely hypotheses rather than a single prediction. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), on three tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The main contribution of this paper is the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach to minimize the oracle loss in ensembles of deep networks. This approach is generalizable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent and is thus applicable to a wide range of modern deep networks. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

The strengths of this paper include its clear and concise writing style, its thorough evaluation of the proposed method, and its ability to provide interpretable results. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, including classical ensembles, Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), and the method of Dey et al.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the initialization of the models. They mention that the models are initialized with a pre-trained network, but it is unclear how important this is to the success of the method. It would be useful to see an evaluation of the method with random initialization to determine its robustness.

Secondly, the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the diversity of the ensemble members. While they do provide some analysis of the specialization of the ensemble members, it would be useful to see a more detailed evaluation of the diversity of the outputs produced by the ensemble.

Finally, the authors do not compare their method with a similar approach proposed by Stefan Lee et al. in their paper "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks". It would be useful to see a comparison with this method to determine the relative strengths and weaknesses of the two approaches.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria, I would rate this paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10. The paper is well-written and provides a clear and concise description of the proposed method. The evaluation is thorough, but there are a few areas where it could be improved.

* Originality: 8/10. The paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles, but it builds on existing work in the area.
* Significance: 8/10. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on three tasks, but it is unclear how widely applicable the method is.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication at NIPS. However, I would suggest that the authors address the areas mentioned above to improve the paper.
