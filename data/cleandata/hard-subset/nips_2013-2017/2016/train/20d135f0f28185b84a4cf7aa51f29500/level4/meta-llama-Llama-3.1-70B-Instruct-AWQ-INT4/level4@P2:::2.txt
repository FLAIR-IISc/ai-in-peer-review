The paper proposes a novel method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks (DNNs) using a stochastic gradient descent-based approach, which they term Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The key idea is to utilize an oracle that selects the best output from the ensemble for each training instance and applies backpropagation only to the corresponding network. This approach enables the networks to specialize in different themes, resulting in ensembles that cover a wide range of possible outputs.

The paper is well-written, and the idea of training an ensemble of DNNs with an oracle is interesting and well-presented. The experiments are conducted on various domains, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, and the results demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, showcasing the emergence of interpretable specializations among ensemble members.

The paper's strengths include its clear presentation, well-designed experiments, and significant performance improvements over baseline methods. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work, highlighting the advantages of their approach over existing methods.

However, the paper falls short in addressing the exploitation of ensembles trained with the proposed approach. While the authors mention that the resulting ensemble can provide a broad pool of possible results and that their approach is coherent with the top-k metric proposed for some problems (e.g., ImageNet), they do not develop this idea further. This omission is an important missing part for making the proposed approach fully usable in practice.

Additionally, the paper includes pseudo-code figures that are raster images embedded in the text. It would be more readable and professional to include the pseudo-code directly within the paper or embed the figures as vectorial images.

Overall, the paper presents a strong contribution to the field of deep learning, and with some revisions to address the exploitation of trained ensembles, it has the potential to be outstanding. As it stands, the paper is well-suited for publication, but the authors should consider expanding on the practical applications of their approach in future work.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting idea for training ensembles of DNNs.
* The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate significant performance improvements over baseline methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the idea and its advantages.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not fully address the exploitation of ensembles trained with the proposed approach.
* The pseudo-code figures are raster images embedded in the text, which could be improved for readability.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the exploitation of trained ensembles and improve the presentation of pseudo-code figures.
