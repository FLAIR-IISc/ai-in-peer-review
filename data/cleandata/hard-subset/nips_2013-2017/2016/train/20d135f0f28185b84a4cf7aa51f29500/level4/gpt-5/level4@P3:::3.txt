Summary
This paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in the context of deep networks, where the goal is to train an ensemble that outputs M hypotheses per input and is evaluated by oracle loss (the minimum loss over the M predictions). Prior MCL formulations (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS 2012; AISTATS 2014) require alternating assignment and full retraining, which is impractical for modern deep architectures. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme: for each training example, only the lowest-loss model in the ensemble receives gradient updates. This yields a stochastic block gradient descent on an upper bound of oracle loss, removes the need for costly retraining/sequential schedules, and is architecture- and loss-agnostic.

Empirically, sMCL improves oracle metrics across three domains and architectures: CNNs on CIFAR-10 (classification), FCNs on PASCAL VOC (semantic segmentation), and CNN+LSTM on MSCOCO (captioning). The authors also analyze the specialization that emerges in the ensemble, with predictors focusing on different classes (classification) or structures/classes (segmentation) and producing more varied n-grams (captioning). Compared to related work on diversity and multiple outputs—diverse M-best inference in graphical models (e.g., Batra et al., ECCV 2012; Kirillov et al., NIPS/ICCV), submodular/diversity-based training (Dey et al., ICCV 2015), and ensemble diversification techniques—the novelty here is a practical, concurrent SGD training scheme for deep ensembles that directly targets oracle loss.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The gradient argument is straightforward and correct for the surrogate objective with hard assignments: the winning model receives the standard gradient; others receive zero. While a convergence analysis is not provided, the method is consistent with hard-EM/K-means-style alternating optimization at minibatch scale and is backed by solid empirical results.
- Experimental rigor: The method is evaluated on three tasks with consistent improvements in oracle metrics and qualitative analyses of specialization. Comparisons include independent ensembles, the original MCL (with a limited number of meta-iterations), and a boosting-like method (Dey et al.). For captioning, beam search from a single model is also included. Some details could be strengthened (see questions/suggestions), but the breadth and consistency are compelling.
- Fairness of baselines: The MCL baseline is reported for five meta-iterations; fully converged MCL runs may be infeasible, but the paper should clarify convergence criteria per meta-iteration and potential early-stopping effects. In captioning, the shared CNN due to memory constraints should be discussed as it may affect cross-method comparability.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is simple and described clearly. More explicit training details and tie-breaking rules would help reimplementation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with effective figures illustrating specialization. Minor issues:
- Abstract, first sentence: consider rewriting for clarity and concision.
- Line 108: “we fix the form of g…” (typo: g, not f).
- Line 115: remove the extra period.
- Please clarify tie-breaking among equal-loss predictors and whether any balancing/anti-starvation mechanism is used.
- In captioning, clearly separate the “frozen-CNN” vs “fine-tuned CNN” setups when reporting metrics.

Originality
The key contribution is not a new objective but a practical, general SGD-based optimization strategy for oracle loss in deep ensembles, avoiding the retraining/sequential drawbacks of prior MCL/diversity methods. This is an incremental but important advance that makes MCL viable for deep learning.

Significance
Given the prevalence of downstream oracles and top-k metrics in vision and language, a simple, general method that improves oracle performance and induces meaningful specialization is valuable. The demonstrated 5x speedup vs. MCL and consistent gains (typically 6–10% in oracle metrics) suggest that practitioners will adopt this method.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a winner-take-gradient strategy that integrates naturally with SGD.
- Applicable across architectures and loss functions; parameter-free and easy to implement.
- Broad empirical validation on three representative tasks with qualitative analyses of emergent expertise.
- Substantial runtime advantage over alternating MCL.

Weaknesses
- Despite extensive motivation around “diversity,” the method does not explicitly enforce diversity; specialization emerges as a byproduct of minimizing oracle loss. Claims and framing around diversity should be tempered or clarified as oracle-driven specialization.
- The contribution is incremental relative to prior MCL formulations (e.g., replacing full retraining with stochastic assignments/backprop). A deeper theoretical treatment (e.g., convergence properties, bounds) would strengthen the paper.
- Baseline details for MCL convergence and the implications of shared CNNs in captioning need clarification.
- Limited discussion of potential starvation/imbalance across ensemble members and sensitivity to batch size and ensemble size.

Questions for the authors
1) MCL baseline: How did you verify convergence within each meta-iteration? Could early stopping or incomplete convergence bias against MCL? Please provide per-meta-iteration training curves and stopping criteria.
2) Assignment imbalance: Did you observe starvation of some learners (e.g., receiving few gradients)? Any steps to mitigate (e.g., temperature smoothing, k-winners, load balancing)? Please report assignment distributions over training.
3) Tie-breaking: How are ties handled in practice, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
4) Sensitivity: How do results vary with ensemble size M, batch size, and dataset size? Please add ablations.
5) Captioning: Given the shared CNN for memory reasons, could this limit diversity across ensemble members relative to independently finetuned CNNs? How comparable are baselines under this constraint?
6) Non-oracle metrics: Does sMCL degrade single-prediction performance or calibration relative to independent ensembles?

Suggestions for improvement
- Temper the emphasis on “diversity” in title/abstract; consider framing as “oracle loss minimization and emergent specialization.” Alternatively, explicitly compare to diversity-enforcing baselines or include a lightweight diversity regularizer ablation.
- Expand related work to include recent “diverse decoding” approaches for structured outputs and language (e.g., diverse M-best, submodular selection).
- Provide more granular training details (learning rates, schedules, data splits) and runtime comparisons, including absolute training times.
- Report the k-winners variant more fully (accuracy vs. k and specialization metrics), as it directly connects to the diversity/coverage trade-off.
- Release code or pseudo-code sufficient for reproduction.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practical method that makes MCL viable for deep networks.
- Strong empirical results across multiple tasks with interpretable specialization.
- Meaningful speedup over prior MCL training strategies.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental advance over existing MCL formulations without strong theoretical guarantees.
- Diversity is not explicitly enforced despite heavy motivation.
- Some baseline and experimental details require clarification to fully assess fairness.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a practical and effective training strategy with broad applicability and convincing empirical gains, despite the incremental nature of the contribution and the need to moderate claims about “diversity.”
