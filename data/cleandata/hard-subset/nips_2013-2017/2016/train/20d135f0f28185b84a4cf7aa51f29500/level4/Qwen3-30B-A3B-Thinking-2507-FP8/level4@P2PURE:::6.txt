

The paper introduces an ensemble training algorithm for deep learners that operates uniquely by activating only a single ensemble member per training example, contrasting with prior deep and classical ensemble methods. During training, the member exhibiting the lowest loss with respect to the true label is selected and updated via gradient descent. This simple yet effective approach yields surprisingly strong experimental results and addresses limitations of existing methods, warranting weak acceptance.  

Continued from summary:  
1. The testing procedure remains ambiguous. The paper appears to use the true labels during inference to select the best-performing member (as suggested in line 159, page 5), but this is not explicitly clarified.  
2. The manuscript references "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" (denoted as "Indp." in figures and line 181, page 5), yet fails to define or justify this baseline comparison.  
3. The authors repeatedly emphasize "specialization" of ensemble members toward distinct output classes. However, the per-example reassignment mechanism does not inherently support this claim. The justification for specialization is insufficient; could specialization be explicitly incorporated into the loss function?  
4. The work overlooks closely related prior research on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which provides a more rigorous theoretical foundation for ensemble diversity. This technique, while not yet extensively studied in deep learning contexts, merits discussion.
