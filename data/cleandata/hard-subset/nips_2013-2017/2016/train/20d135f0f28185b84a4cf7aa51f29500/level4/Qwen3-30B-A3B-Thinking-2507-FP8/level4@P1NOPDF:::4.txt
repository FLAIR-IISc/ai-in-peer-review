

This paper introduces a stochastic gradient descent method incorporating Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), claiming advantages across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks. However, the contribution and experimental validation require significant strengthening.  

1. **Novelty and Motivation**: The authors assert that sMCL is incrementally novel, but the core motivation for MCL is neither new nor sufficiently distinguished from prior work. The proposed method essentially adapts the framework from [8] with minor neural network adjustments, lacking substantive innovation. The incremental nature of the contribution is not compelling, and the paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from established approaches.  

2. **Technical Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is poorly structured and lacks technical depth. Section 3, which details the proposed framework, occupies only one page, leaving critical gaps. Crucially, the authors do not explicitly contrast their method with standard multi-task training paradigms—widely adopted in the community for simultaneous task optimization. Without this comparison, it remains unclear what specific advantages sMCL offers over existing practices, undermining the paper’s central claim.  

3. **Experimental Rigor**: While experiments span multiple computer vision tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning), they suffer from two major flaws. First, the baselines are insufficiently strong; comparisons against state-of-the-art multi-task methods are absent, making it difficult to assess the method’s true efficacy. Second, the reported performance gains are marginal (e.g., <1% improvement on most benchmarks), failing to demonstrate meaningful progress. To validate the framework’s utility, the authors must conduct experiments on PASCAL VOC 2012 with robust baselines—such as multi-task networks with shared bottlenecks or task-specific heads—comparing against recent SOTA approaches. Without this, the empirical claims lack convincing support.  

In summary, the paper’s technical contribution is overstated, its presentation lacks clarity, and its experimental validation is inadequate. The method appears as a minor adaptation of prior work without sufficient novelty or compelling evidence of superiority. Addressing these issues would be necessary for publication.
