This paper proposes a novel method for training diverse deep ensembles, dubbed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which leverages an oracle to guide the training process. The approach is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. By interleaving the assignment step with batch updates in stochastic gradient descent, sMCL enables the training of ensemble members concurrently, thereby overcoming the shortcomings of existing methods.

The paper is well-written, and the experiments are conducted on a wide range of tasks and deep architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The results demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL, outperforming classical ensembles and other strong baselines. The analysis shows that sMCL automatically generates specializations among ensemble members along different task-specific dimensions, leading to interpretable emergent expertise.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the problem of training diverse deep ensembles in a principled manner. The proposed approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying principles. The experimental results are convincing, and the comparisons with existing methods are thorough.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. Firstly, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the exploitation of ensemble of DNNs trained with the proposed approach. While the authors mention that the approach is coherent with the top-k metric proposed for some problems, they do not provide a clear explanation of how the resulting ensemble can be used in practice. This is an important aspect that needs to be addressed to make the proposed approach fully usable.

Additionally, the pseudo-code in Figure 2 is presented as a raster image, which makes it difficult to read. It would be better to present the pseudo-code directly within the paper or embed it as a vectorial image.

In terms of the quality criteria, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10 (the paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough, but there are some areas that could be improved)
* Clarity: 9/10 (the paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying principles)
* Originality: 8/10 (the proposed approach is novel, but it builds upon existing work in the field)
* Significance: 9/10 (the paper addresses an important problem in the field, and the results have the potential to impact the development of deep learning models)

Overall, I think that this is a strong paper that deserves to be published. However, I would like to see the authors address the issue of exploiting the ensemble of DNNs trained with the proposed approach and improve the presentation of the pseudo-code.
