This paper proposes a novel training algorithm for deep network ensembles, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The goal is to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input, which is particularly useful in systems where a downstream component or human user (an "oracle") can select the best solution from multiple hypotheses. The core idea is simple yet effective: during training with stochastic gradient descent, for each example in a batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members. However, the gradient is backpropagated only to the single "winner" network—the one that achieved the lowest loss for that specific example.

This approach is framed as a stochastic, parallelizable version of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8], which used a block coordinate descent method requiring full retraining of models, making it infeasible for modern deep architectures. By integrating the selection step directly into the SGD backward pass, sMCL trains all ensemble members concurrently in a single training run. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness across a range of tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), using appropriate CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures. The results show that sMCL consistently outperforms baselines, including independently trained ensembles, in terms of oracle performance metrics. A key claim is that this training strategy leads to the automatic emergence of "specialization," where different ensemble members become experts on different subsets of the data or output space.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating diverse predictions. While the concept of MCL is not new, adapting it into a simple and efficient SGD-based algorithm (sMCL) that is practical for deep learning is a significant and novel contribution. This "winner-take-gradient" approach provides an elegant solution to a previously computationally expensive problem.

2.  **Quality and Soundness:** The method is technically sound and the motivation is clear. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors apply sMCL to three distinct and challenging problems, demonstrating its versatility across different tasks, loss functions, and network architectures. The quantitative results consistently show that sMCL improves oracle performance over several relevant baselines.

3.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** A major strength of sMCL is its simplicity. It is parameter-free, easy to implement (conceptually a single selection step before the backward pass), and significantly more efficient than prior MCL methods. This makes it a highly practical tool that could be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the core idea is explained clearly. The qualitative results, particularly for segmentation and image captioning, provide compelling illustrations of the diverse and meaningful outputs generated by the sMCL-trained ensembles compared to standard ones.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on Evaluation Protocol:** The use of "oracle evaluation" could be a source of confusion for some readers. The testing procedure involves calculating the best possible score from the ensemble's M predictions against the single ground truth label. While this is a standard metric for evaluating hypothesis sets, the paper could be more explicit in clarifying that this is an analysis tool to measure the quality of the generated set, not the performance of a standalone system that lacks a downstream oracle to perform the selection. A brief clarification on how such a system would be deployed in practice (e.g., presenting options to a user) would strengthen the motivation.

2.  **Analysis of Specialization:** The paper repeatedly highlights the "emergent specialization" of ensemble members as a key benefit. While the empirical evidence for this is compelling (e.g., Figure 4c showing class-based specialization), the underlying dynamics are not deeply analyzed. The training process assigns examples to winners on a per-instance basis, and it is not immediately obvious why this should lead to stable, high-level specializations (e.g., by class). A more detailed discussion or analysis of how the per-example competition fosters this emergent behavior would make the claims more robust.

3.  **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by discussing its relationship to other methods for encouraging diversity in ensembles, particularly those that use explicit diversity-promoting regularization terms. For example, some approaches penalize the correlation between the outputs or gradients of different ensemble members. Situating sMCL in the context of these alternative diversity-enforcement techniques would provide a more complete picture of the landscape. For instance, work by Chen et al. ("Diversity-Promoting Ensemble for Deep Neural Networks," CVPR 2019 workshop) explores related ideas, though it is contemporary or later, earlier work on diversity regularization exists in the broader machine learning literature.

4.  **Baseline Clarification:** The baseline referred to as "Indp. ensembles" or "classical ensembles" is defined as models trained with independent losses and different random initializations. While this is a standard and appropriate baseline, its definition is slightly buried within the experimental section. Stating this definition more prominently at the beginning of Section 4 would improve clarity.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper introduces a simple, novel, and highly effective method for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   The contribution is practical, parameter-free, and applicable to a wide range of tasks and architectures.
*   The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates significant performance gains on oracle metrics across three different domains.
*   The work successfully makes the previously impractical Multiple Choice Learning paradigm feasible for state-of-the-art deep networks.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   The analysis of the "specialization" phenomenon, a central claim of the paper, could be more rigorous.
*   The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly concerning explicit diversity regularization techniques.
*   Minor clarifications regarding the evaluation protocol and baseline definitions would improve the paper's overall clarity.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a valuable and practical contribution to the field of ensemble learning for deep networks. The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant in its simplicity and demonstrably effective. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
