This paper proposes a straightforward solution to the multiple-choice learning problem (MCL) for deep networks, addressing a significant limitation of current MCL solutions, which do not directly extend to deep networks. The authors demonstrate that incorporating a simple "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer in backpropagation enables the combination of learner training with the assignment problem in an ensemble setting. Experimental results show that this method achieves strong performance across a diverse range of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the problem of MCL for deep learning is highly relevant to the NIPS community, making it an important contribution. Secondly, the proposed method is commendably simple. Lastly, the experiments provide results on three different tasks, demonstrating the method's versatility.

However, there are also some weaknesses. My primary concern is that, despite extensively motivating the concept of "diversity" (even featuring it in the title), the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. I was initially excited to see how the authors incorporated a diversity term into their model but was disappointed to find that no such term exists. Furthermore, the proposed solution can be seen as an incremental step, building upon the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions for improvement include rewriting the first sentence of the abstract for clarity. Additionally, the emphasis on "diversity" should be toned down to avoid misleading readers. There are also a few minor errors: the first "f" in line 108 should be replaced with "g" in "we fixed the form of," and an extra period in the middle of a sentence in line 115 should be removed.

One question remains: when implementing the baseline MCL with deep learning, how did the authors ensure that each network converged to reasonable results? Prematurely cutting off the learners could significantly impact the ensemble's performance.
