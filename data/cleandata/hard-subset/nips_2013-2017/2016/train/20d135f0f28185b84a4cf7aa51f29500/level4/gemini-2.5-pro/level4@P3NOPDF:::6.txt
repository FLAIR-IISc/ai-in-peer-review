This paper introduces a novel and efficient method for training ensembles of deep neural networks. The core idea is simple yet effective: for each training example, only one member of the ensemble—the one with the lowest loss—is updated via backpropagation. This "winner-takes-all" approach to gradient updates is a departure from classical deep ensemble methods where all members are trained either independently or jointly. The experiments demonstrate surprisingly strong performance, suggesting that this method effectively encourages diversity and specialization among ensemble members. Given the novelty of the idea, its simplicity, and the compelling empirical results, I recommend weak acceptance for this paper, contingent on the authors addressing several key points outlined below.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed training algorithm is straightforward to understand and implement. The concept of updating only the "best" learner for a given example is an intuitive way to encourage differentiation among ensemble members.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results presented are impressive, showing that the proposed method outperforms several baseline and existing ensemble techniques across the datasets tested.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Compared to training multiple deep learners independently from scratch, this method could be more efficient as it involves a single training loop where only one forward/backward pass through a single member is computed per example.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarification of the Inference/Testing Procedure:** A critical aspect of the methodology is not clearly explained: how is a prediction made for a new, unseen example at test time? The training procedure relies on the ground truth label to identify the "best" member (i.e., the one with the lowest loss). This is not possible during inference. The text (e.g., page 5, line 159) seems to imply a similar selection of the best member, but it is unclear how this would be done without the true label. If the testing protocol also uses the ground truth to select the oracle/best member, the reported results would not be valid for real-world application and the comparisons to other methods would be unfair. Please provide a detailed explanation of the aggregation strategy used at test time (e.g., simple averaging, majority vote, or another selection mechanism).

2.  **Undefined Baseline:** The paper repeatedly compares its performance against "independent ensembles" or "regular ensembles," denoted as "Indp." in the figures and text (e.g., page 5, line 181). However, a clear definition or explanation of how this baseline ensemble was trained is missing. Are these models trained completely separately on the same data? Is there any form of data bagging involved? A precise description of this baseline is essential for the reader to properly evaluate the contributions of the proposed method.

3.  **Justification of the "Specialization" Claim:** The authors frequently argue that the proposed method encourages ensemble members to "specialize" on different output classes. While the results suggest that diversity is indeed achieved, the link between the training mechanism and class-level specialization is not sufficiently justified. The reassignment of an example to an ensemble member is done on a per-example basis, not a per-class basis. It is plausible that different examples from the *same class* could be assigned to different members across different training epochs. This dynamic assignment seems to contradict the notion of a stable, class-based specialization. Could the authors provide further analysis to substantiate this claim, for example, by showing the distribution of class assignments to members after training has converged? Furthermore, have the authors considered explicitly incorporating a class-specialization objective into the loss function to more rigorously enforce this behavior?

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by discussing the highly relevant literature on diversity regularization in neural network ensembles. For instance, techniques that add an explicit diversity-promoting term to the loss function (e.g., based on correlation or information-theoretic measures, such as the work by Chen et al.) aim to solve a similar problem of creating a diverse and effective ensemble. While these methods may be more complex, they represent a more formal approach to achieving the "specialization" goal. A discussion of how the proposed method relates to, and differs from, these explicit diversity regularization techniques would provide valuable context for the paper's contribution.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and simple idea for training deep ensembles. However, the current manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity on crucial methodological points and could benefit from a more thorough justification of its claims and a broader discussion of related work. My recommendation for acceptance is conditional on the authors' ability to convincingly address these concerns in their rebuttal.
