

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep ensembles that directly optimizes for the oracle loss over multiple predictions. The authors address a practical need in perception systems where providing multiple plausible hypotheses rather than a single prediction is beneficial for downstream processes. The work builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8], which formalizes the problem as minimizing the oracle loss (the minimum loss among M predictions), but extends it to deep networks by introducing a stochastic gradient descent approach that avoids the computationally expensive retraining required by prior methods.

The paper makes a clear contribution by formulating sMCL as a "winner-take-gradient" strategy where, during training, gradients are only backpropagated to the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss for each example. This approach is agnostic to network architecture and loss function, parameter-free, and enables concurrent training of ensemble members. The authors demonstrate sMCL's effectiveness across three diverse domains: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements over classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s sequential boosting approach.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality:** The paper significantly extends prior work on Multiple Choice Learning [8, 9] to deep networks, addressing a critical gap in the literature. While Guzman-Rivera et al. proposed an alternating block coordinate descent method requiring multiple retraining cycles, sMCL enables efficient concurrent training suitable for modern deep architectures. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from related work on ensemble diversity [2, 19, 21, 26, 27] and multiple solution generation [3, 5, 15, 16, 23, 24], particularly noting that prior methods are not applicable to general deep architectures.
- **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound and well-justified. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical formulation (Section 3) and thorough experimental validation across multiple tasks and architectures. The results consistently demonstrate sMCL's superiority over strong baselines (6-10% improvement in oracle metrics) while being 5x faster than MCL. The paper carefully addresses potential limitations (e.g., MCL's retraining inefficiency) and provides ablation studies showing the emergence of interpretable specializations in ensemble members.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 1 showing the practical benefits of multiple predictions, Figure 4c demonstrating class-wise specialization). The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1 and 2) are precise and easy to follow. The paper successfully bridges theoretical concepts with practical implementation details.
- **Significance:** The work addresses a meaningful gap in perception systems where single-prediction models fail to capture ambiguity. The paper demonstrates practical utility across diverse domains, showing how sMCL enables interpretable emergence of specialist ensemble members that collectively cover the space of high-probability predictions. The method's simplicity (requiring only one new layer in existing architectures) and broad applicability make it immediately useful for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Diversity Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improved oracle performance, the analysis of diversity could be strengthened. The authors show that sMCL produces specialized ensemble members, but provide limited quantitative analysis of diversity metrics beyond oracle performance (e.g., measures of inter-member disagreement or coverage of solution space). A comparison of diversity metrics (e.g., normalized mutual information, Hamming distance between predictions) between sMCL and baselines would strengthen the claims about diversity.
- **MCL with Batching:** The draft review's suggestion to compare MCL with batch processing is valid. The authors acknowledge that MCL requires retraining after convergence, but don't provide an explicit comparison of MCL with a batched variant (which would be more comparable to sMCL). This would help clarify whether the speed advantage of sMCL is due to the stochastic approach or the elimination of retraining cycles.
- **Limitations of Specialization:** The paper notes that specialization is less evident in segmentation than classification, but doesn't explore why. A deeper analysis of when and how specialization emerges across different task types would provide additional insights.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:*
- Novel and practical solution to a real-world problem in perception systems
- Clear theoretical contribution extending prior MCL work to deep networks
- Strong experimental validation across multiple domains with significant performance gains
- Simple implementation that's broadly applicable to existing deep learning frameworks
- Demonstrates emergent interpretability through specialized ensemble members

*Con:*
- Limited quantitative analysis of diversity beyond oracle performance
- Could provide more direct comparison of MCL with batch processing
- Doesn't fully explore why specialization varies across task types

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by enabling practical training of diverse deep ensembles. The sMCL algorithm is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. While the diversity analysis could be strengthened, the paper's strengths in originality, quality, clarity, and significance substantially outweigh these minor limitations. The work is broadly applicable to many perception tasks and provides a valuable tool for practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
