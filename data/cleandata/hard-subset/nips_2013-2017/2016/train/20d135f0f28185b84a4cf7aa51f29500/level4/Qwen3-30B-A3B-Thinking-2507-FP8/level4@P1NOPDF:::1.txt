

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for training ensemble models, extending Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The core idea builds on their approach where the ensemble loss is defined as the minimum loss across individual classifiers in the ensemble—this design inherently promotes diversity among ensemble members. The authors' key contribution is adapting this framework for SGD training, enabling efficient application to deep neural networks. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the technical content accessible.  

My primary concern pertains to the novelty of the proposed algorithm. While the approach leverages [8]’s foundation, the modification—replacing batch training with SGD—appears incremental at first glance. However, the authors persuasively argue that this shift significantly enhances practicality for large-scale deep learning tasks, effectively bridging a critical gap. Given this justification, I find the contribution meaningful rather than merely incremental.  

To strengthen the work, I recommend two specific additions. First, the authors should evaluate the MCL algorithm (from [8]) under mini-batch SGD settings—comparing its performance against the proposed method when using batches instead of the full dataset. This would clarify whether the SGD adaptation itself drives the observed benefits or if the gains stem from batch processing alone. Second, the paper lacks a quantitative analysis of the "diversity" effect. While diversity is cited as a key advantage, the authors should provide empirical evidence (e.g., pairwise disagreement metrics, entropy measures) to demonstrate how the algorithm actively fosters diverse classifiers compared to standard ensembles.  

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a technically sound approach. The clarity of the presentation and the compelling argument for SGD’s practical utility outweigh my initial novelty concerns. With the suggested clarifications on MCL’s batch behavior and diversity quantification, the work would be significantly more robust. I recommend acceptance.
