This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep neural network ensembles. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," which is the minimum loss achieved by any member of the ensemble for a given example. This is implemented as a "winner-take-gradient" strategy within a standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) framework: for each training example, only the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss is updated via backpropagation. This approach contrasts with standard ensembles where members are trained independently, and with previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods that require iterative, full retraining of models, making them impractical for deep learning. The authors demonstrate that sMCL is simple to implement, computationally efficient, and broadly applicable across different architectures and tasks. They present strong empirical results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing that sMCL ensembles achieve superior oracle performance and produce more diverse outputs compared to several baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The sMCL algorithm is elegant in its simplicity, requiring minimal changes to a standard ensemble training pipeline. Its parameter-free nature and agnosticism to architecture and loss function make it a highly practical and general-purpose tool.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** By training all ensemble members concurrently in a single pass, sMCL offers a substantial speedup over iterative methods like the original MCL, making it feasible for training contemporary deep networks.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method is shown to consistently and significantly outperform strong baselines across a variety of complex tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning), demonstrating its broad applicability and effectiveness.
*   **Interpretable Specialization:** The analysis showing that ensemble members learn to specialize in different subspaces of the problem (e.g., specific object classes or caption styles) is a compelling result that provides insight into the learning dynamics.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Clarity on Evaluation and Practical Application:** The evaluation relies on an "oracle" that selects the best prediction at test time. While this is a standard metric for this line of work, the practical utility is less clear. How would a user or downstream system choose from the M predictions without access to the ground truth? A brief discussion on potential re-ranking or selection mechanisms would strengthen the paper's connection to real-world applications.

2.  **Definition of Baselines:** The baseline referred to as "Indp. ensembles" or "classical ensembles" could be defined more explicitly for clarity and reproducibility. I assume these are standard ensembles where each member is trained independently on the full dataset from a different random initialization, but it would be beneficial to state this plainly in the text.

3.  **Justification of Specialization Mechanism:** The authors claim that sMCL encourages specialization, and the empirical results are suggestive (e.g., Fig 4c). However, the link between the per-example "winner-take-gradient" update and the emergence of stable, class-level specialization could be further elaborated. Since the assignment of an example to a "winner" can change throughout training, it is not immediately obvious why this process should converge to such semantically meaningful specializations.

4.  **Missing Related Work on Diversity Regularization:** The paper could be strengthened by discussing its relationship to other methods that explicitly encourage diversity in ensembles, for instance, through diversity-promoting regularization terms in the loss function. Contrasting sMCL's implicit approach with techniques that add explicit penalties to decorrelate the predictions of ensemble members would better contextualize the work.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this paper presents a simple, effective, and practical algorithm for an important problem. The impressive experimental results and the analysis of emergent model specialization make it a valuable contribution. While there are minor points that could be clarified, the strengths of the work are significant. I recommend acceptance.
