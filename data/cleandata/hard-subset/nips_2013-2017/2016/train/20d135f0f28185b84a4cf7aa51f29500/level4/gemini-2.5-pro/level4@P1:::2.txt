This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and effective method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks, or indeed any model trained with gradient-based optimization. The core idea is to directly optimize the ensemble for the oracle loss. During stochastic gradient descent, for each training instance, the loss is backpropagated only through the single ensemble member that produces the best prediction (i.e., the lowest loss) for that instance. This "winner-take-gradient" approach encourages the individual networks within the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution. The goal is to produce a diverse set of high-quality hypotheses that effectively cover the space of plausible outputs, which is particularly useful for ambiguous tasks. The authors demonstrate the method's versatility and strong performance on a variety of domains and architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and well-motivated. It provides an elegant solution to the problem of training for output diversity by reformulating the Multiple Choice Learning objective for modern deep learning frameworks. The ability to train all ensemble members concurrently is a significant practical advantage over previous sequential or iterative methods.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Experiments:** A major strength of this paper is its thorough empirical evaluation. The authors successfully apply sMCL to a wide range of tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) and architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs), convincingly demonstrating the method's generality and broad applicability.

3.  **Excellent Results:** The results show that sMCL consistently and significantly outperforms relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles and more complex boosting-like approaches. The analysis, particularly in the classification experiments, provides clear evidence that the method successfully induces specialization among the ensemble members, directly supporting the paper's central claims.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is described precisely, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The analysis of the emergent specialization is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Guidance on Inference-Time Application:** The primary limitation of the work is the lack of a detailed discussion on how the resulting ensemble should be deployed in a practical setting where a ground-truth oracle is not available. The paper demonstrates that the ensemble can generate a strong set of candidate solutions, which aligns well with oracle evaluation metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy). However, it does not address the subsequent problem of selecting a single best prediction or combining the outputs for a downstream task without an oracle. While the authors suggest the outputs can be passed to a human operator or a verification component, the paper would be significantly strengthened by exploring or proposing automated methods for this "exploitation" phase. This is a crucial missing piece for the method's practical utility in fully automated systems. Nevertheless, I acknowledge that this downstream selection problem can be highly application-specific, and the core contribution of training a diverse ensemble remains significant.

2.  **Minor Presentation Issue:** The pseudo-code in Figure 2 appears to be a low-resolution raster image. Using typeset algorithms (e.g., with the `algorithmic` package) or at least a vector graphic would improve the manuscript's professional appearance and readability.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that introduces a valuable and practical technique for training diverse deep ensembles. The method is simple, general, and empirically shown to be effective across multiple challenging tasks. Despite the weakness regarding the practical deployment of the trained models at inference time, the contribution in the training methodology itself is significant and novel. This work is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance.
