This paper introduces a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep neural networks. Its key innovation lies in activating only a single member of the ensemble for each training example – a departure from traditional ensemble methods where all members contribute to each prediction. During training, the member network achieving the lowest loss for a given example is selected and updated via gradient descent. Despite its simplicity, the method demonstrates surprisingly strong empirical performance and addresses limitations of existing ensemble techniques, leading me to recommend weak acceptance.

However, several aspects of the paper require clarification. First, the testing procedure is not fully detailed. It appears the authors utilize ground truth labels to identify the best-performing member for each example, as suggested in line 159 of page 5, but a more explicit description is needed.

Second, the paper frequently contrasts performance against “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (denoted as ‘Indp.’ in figures and line 181 on page 5). The definition of these baselines is absent from the text, hindering a complete understanding of the experimental results. Providing a clear explanation of how these ensembles are constructed is crucial.

Third, the authors emphasize the “specialization” of ensemble members toward different output classes. This claim, however, seems weakly supported given the per-example reassignment strategy employed during training. While observing specialization is interesting, the training process doesn’t inherently *promote* it. It would strengthen the argument if the authors could explore incorporating specialization directly into the loss function – perhaps via a regularization term encouraging distinct representations across members. 

Finally, the paper lacks discussion of related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, such as the approach by Chen et al. This technique provides a more principled method for encouraging diversity among network parameters, and a comparison – even noting its current lack of extensive study in deep learning – would contextualize the authors’ contribution and demonstrate awareness of the broader research landscape. Addressing these points would significantly improve the clarity and impact of the paper.
