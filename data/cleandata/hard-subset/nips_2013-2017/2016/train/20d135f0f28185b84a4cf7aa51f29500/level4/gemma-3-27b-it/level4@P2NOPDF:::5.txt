This paper introduces a novel method for generating diverse outputs from an ensemble of deep networks. The authors address the challenge of interactive systems requiring multiple plausible responses by formulating an objective that directly minimizes the oracle loss – the loss incurred by selecting the best output from the ensemble in hindsight. This approach effectively integrates deep learning with the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, and the authors propose a stochastic block gradient descent optimization technique to tackle the resulting non-convex problem. A key strength of the method is its compatibility with standard backpropagation, enabling end-to-end training. The experimental results on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

However, several aspects of the paper warrant further discussion. The sensitivity of the method to initialization is a concern. The experiments utilize pre-trained networks for initialization, but the impact of random initialization is not explored. Understanding whether the observed performance relies heavily on these pre-trained weights is crucial. Specifically, it would be helpful to know if the baseline methods also leverage the same pre-trained network as sMCL for their initializations. Clarification on this point is needed to accurately assess the method's advantages.

Furthermore, the evaluation currently focuses solely on oracle accuracy. While a high oracle accuracy is a positive indicator, it doesn't fully capture the benefits of an ensemble. A crucial aspect of a successful ensemble is the diversity of its members. The authors should include an analysis of ensemble diversity, potentially using metrics such as average pairwise distance in feature space, or examining the variance of predictions across the ensemble. Without this analysis, it's difficult to ascertain whether the improvements stem from genuinely diverse predictions or simply from redundant, highly-confident outputs. 

Finally, the authors should consider related work more comprehensively. In particular, the paper "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks" by Lee et al. (CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015) presents a closely related approach to learning diverse ensembles, and a direct comparison would be valuable. Addressing how this work differs from, and potentially builds upon, Lee et al.’s findings would strengthen the paper’s contribution. 

In summary, the paper presents a promising approach to learning ensembles for interactive systems. Addressing the concerns regarding initialization, ensemble diversity, and related work will significantly improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
