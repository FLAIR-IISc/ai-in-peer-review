The paper presents an ensemble training algorithm for deep learning models with a novel approach: unlike traditional ensembles, only one member is active for each training example. During training, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is identified and updated using the gradient. This is a simple yet surprisingly effective idea, leading to strong experimental results and addressing limitations of existing ensemble methods. I recommend weak acceptance.

1. The testing phase lacks clarity. I suspect the authors utilize the ground truth label to select the best-performing member, as suggested in line 159 of page 5. A more detailed explanation of the testing procedure would be beneficial.

2. The paper refers to ‘independent ensembles’ and ‘regular ensembles’ (denoted as ‘Indp.’ in figures and line 181 on page 5) without providing a clear definition or justification for these baselines. Further explanation is needed to understand the purpose of these comparisons.

3. The authors emphasize the `specialization` of ensemble members toward different output classes. However, given that member reassignment occurs on a per-example basis, the extent to which true specialization emerges is questionable, and this argument is not sufficiently justified. Could incorporating a specialization term directly into the loss function encourage and measure this behavior?

4. Related to the above point, the authors do not discuss relevant work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.). While potentially more complex, this technique offers a more rigorous approach to promoting diversity and warrants consideration, particularly given its limited exploration within deep learning settings.
