

The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by updating only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each training example. This work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8,9] but addresses its key limitation for deep networks—requiring costly retraining of individual models through a stochastic gradient descent-based optimization that trains all ensemble members concurrently. Unlike previous ensemble methods that focus on diversity through data resampling or joint loss functions, sMCL discovers specialization automatically by minimizing oracle loss, making it applicable to both classification and structured prediction tasks. The paper demonstrates broad applicability across image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO) tasks, showing consistent improvements over baselines including classical independent ensembles, MCL [8], and Dey et al. [5].

Strengths: The method is technically sound, parameter-free, and agnostic to architecture and loss function, making it highly practical for diverse deep learning applications. The experimental results are compelling, with sMCL achieving significant improvements (6-10%) in oracle performance while being 5x faster than MCL. The analysis of ensemble specialization—where different members emerge as experts for different classes or visual features—provides valuable interpretability. The paper also properly addresses the significance of producing diverse hypotheses in real-world applications where ambiguity is common, such as in autonomous vehicles or assistive technologies.

Weaknesses: The testing phase description is insufficiently detailed; it's unclear how the oracle mechanism operates during inference (e.g., whether ground truth is used to select the best member as suggested in line 159 of the paper). The baseline "independent ensembles" (denoted as "Indp." in figures and line 181) is not clearly defined or justified, making it difficult to assess the relative performance gains. The argument for ensemble member specialization is not sufficiently supported—the reassignment occurs on a per-example basis rather than per-class, making it unclear how specialization emerges as a class-specific phenomenon. The paper also omits discussion of related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which offers a more formal approach to diversity that has been studied in deep learning contexts.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical motivation and strong empirical validation across multiple domains. The method avoids the retraining bottleneck of prior work, demonstrating good practical utility.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though the testing procedure and baseline definition require clarification to ensure reproducibility.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by adapting Multiple Choice Learning to deep networks through a stochastic optimization approach, differing significantly from prior work that required retraining or sequential model training.

Significance: The work addresses a meaningful problem in perception systems where providing multiple hypotheses is more useful than a single prediction, with potential impact across computer vision and natural language processing applications. The demonstrated improvements over baselines and the interpretability of the resulting ensembles advance the state of the art in ensemble learning for deep networks.

Overall, the paper presents a strong technical contribution with practical value, though some methodological details need clarification. I recommend weak acceptance.
