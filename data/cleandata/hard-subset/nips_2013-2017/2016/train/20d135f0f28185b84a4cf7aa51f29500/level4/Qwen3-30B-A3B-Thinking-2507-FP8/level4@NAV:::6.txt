

The paper introduces an ensemble training algorithm for deep learners, distinguished by its approach where only a single ensemble member is active per training example, contrasting with prior deep and classical ensemble methods. During training, the member exhibiting the lowest loss relative to the true label is selected and updated via gradient descent. This straightforward concept yields notably strong experimental results and addresses key limitations of existing techniques, warranting a weak acceptance recommendation.  

(Continued from summary)  
1. The testing procedure remains unclear; the authors appear to rely on ground truth labels to select the optimal member (as referenced in line 159, p. 5), but this is not explicitly justified.  
2. The manuscript references "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" (denoted as 'Indp.' in figures and line 181, p. 5) without defining this baseline or explaining its rationale.  
3. The authors emphasize "specialization" of ensemble members toward distinct output classes, yet the training reassignment occurs on a per-example basis. This claim is insufficiently supported; could specialization be formally incorporated into the loss function?  
4. The work overlooks related diversity regularization techniques in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which offer a more theoretically grounded approach to ensemble diversity and remain underexplored in deep learning contexts.
