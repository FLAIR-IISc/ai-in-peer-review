- Introduces sMCL, an SGD-based "winner-take-gradient" method for training ensembles to minimize oracle loss by updating only the best-performing model per example.  
- sMCL is practical, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and avoids costly alternating retraining used by prior MCL methods.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning with consistent oracle improvements over independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.  
- Reports qualitative emergent specialization across ensemble members (class specialization, structured diversity in segmentation and captioning).  
- Builds directly on Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL; frames a stochastic block gradient descent suitable for deep networks.  
- Related to M-Best/diverse structured-prediction methods and concurrent-training approaches, and should be compared to Lee et al. (2015) “Why M Heads are Better than One.”  
- Strength: simple, clear, and easy to implement within standard backprop for a wide range of models and losses.  
- Strength: concurrent training yields substantial speedups versus repeated full retraining in prior MCL.  
- Strength: broad empirical evaluation and parameter-free design with minimal engineering overhead.  
- Weakness: unclear sensitivity to initialization—need results for random vs. pre-trained initializations, especially for captioning and segmentation.  
- Weakness: potential baseline unfairness—must clarify whether baselines used identical initializations, schedules, learning rates, and epochs.  
- Weakness: evaluation focuses on oracle metrics without explicit quantitative diversity measurements (disagreement, pairwise distance, n-gram diversity, coverage).  
- Quality: methodologically sound and well-motivated, but experimental setup details are insufficient to assess fairness.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written but needs precise descriptions of initialization, training schedules, tie-breaking, batch sizing, regularization, and pretraining usage.  
- Originality: a practical stochastic adaptation of MCL to deep nets; novelty should be clarified relative to Lee et al. (2015).  
- Significance: high—simple, general, and efficient approach likely to be widely useful for multi-hypothesis prediction.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical algorithm, concurrent training speedups, consistent oracle gains, and interpretable specialization.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing initialization/baseline details, limited diversity quantification, and unclear relation to very similar prior work.  
- Requested revisions: report random vs. pre-trained initialization results and enforce identical baselines; provide explicit diversity metrics and analyses; clarify differences from Lee et al. (2015).
