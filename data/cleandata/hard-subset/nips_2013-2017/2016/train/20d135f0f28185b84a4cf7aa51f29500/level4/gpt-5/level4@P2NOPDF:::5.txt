This paper presents a method for producing multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize an oracle loss, which is especially useful for interactive settings. The approach integrates deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework and introduces a stochastic block gradient descent procedure to minimize the MCL oracle loss. The optimization is easy to implement within standard backpropagation, allowing end-to-end training. Oracle-based evaluations on three tasks suggest the method is effective.

While the idea is compelling and the results are promising, several clarifications and additional analyses would strengthen the paper:

- Sensitivity to initialization: How critical is pretraining for the success of the method? What happens if all ensemble members are initialized randomly instead of starting from a pretrained network? Please report performance, convergence behavior, and variance across multiple random seeds to assess stability and robustness. If pretraining is essential, discuss why and how it affects the partitioning of data among ensemble members under the oracle loss.

- Fairness and details of comparisons: The paper does not provide enough detail about the initialization and training settings used for baselines. Do the baseline methods use the same pretrained network as sMCL for initialization? Are learning rate schedules, data augmentation, training duration, and model capacity matched across methods? A clear description of these settings is necessary to ensure a fair comparison and to isolate the contribution of the proposed optimization.

- Evaluation beyond oracle accuracy: Reporting only oracle accuracy does not reveal how diverse the ensemble members are or how they cover different modes of the data. Please include diversity analyses, such as pairwise disagreement, error correlation, or other diversity metrics, and possibly coverage statistics across modes or classes. Qualitative examples illustrating complementary predictions among ensemble members would also be valuable. If the method explicitly encourages specialization, the paper should quantify the degree of specialization and its relationship to oracle gains.

- Related work and comparisons: The approach is closely related to prior work on training diverse deep ensembles, particularly:
  Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks,” CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
  The authors should discuss similarities and differences (e.g., independent networks vs. multi-head architectures, loss formulations, and training protocols) and, if possible, include empirical comparisons. Even if architectures differ, a controlled comparison (e.g., shared trunk vs. independent models) would help position the contribution and clarify where the proposed method excels.

Overall, integrating deep networks with MCL and optimizing the oracle loss via stochastic block gradient descent is a neat and practical idea, and the end-to-end implementation is a clear strength. Addressing the questions about initialization sensitivity, ensuring fair and well-documented baselines, and providing diversity analyses (along with a comparison to the closely related Lee et al. work) would significantly improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
