Summary
The paper addresses multiple-hypothesis prediction for perception systems embedded in larger pipelines, where downstream “oracles” (human or automated) can select the best among several predictions. The authors cast this as minimizing oracle loss over an ensemble of M predictors and propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL): a simple, SGD-friendly “winner-take-gradient” scheme that backpropagates the loss for each example only through the ensemble member with the lowest loss on that example (ties broken arbitrarily). This is a stochastic block-coordinate descent approximation to the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) objective of Guzman-Rivera et al., but avoids repeated retraining and is agnostic to architecture and loss. The method is demonstrated on CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM), showing improved oracle metrics over independent ensembles, MCL (with far less training time), and Dey et al.’s submodular boosting-like method. Qualitative analyses suggest emergent specialist behavior among ensemble members.

Relation to prior work
- Multiple diverse solutions in structured prediction has a rich history (e.g., M-best diverse solutions, submodular approaches). Prior MCL work optimized an upper bound on oracle loss with alternating assignment and retraining, which is impractical for deep nets.
- Ensemble learning and diversity-inducing methods (bagging, negative correlation, mixture-of-experts) are relevant, but often do not directly target oracle loss or structured outputs.
- The paper’s main novelty is a practical, SGD-compatible training scheme for deep ensembles minimizing oracle loss, and its application across architectures and tasks.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy to implement; integrates seamlessly with backprop and common losses without extra hyperparameters.
- Strong empirical performance across three distinct tasks/architectures, with consistent gains in oracle metrics and substantial training-time savings compared to prior MCL.
- Clear intuition and connection to hard-EM/K-means style assignments; insightful qualitative analysis showing emergent specialization.
- Useful ablation via the k-winner variant illustrating the trade-off between specialization and oracle performance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Initialization and fairness of comparisons: For segmentation and captioning, models appear to be initialized or fine-tuned from pre-trained networks, and in captioning the CNN is shared due to memory constraints. Please clarify:
  - Whether all baselines (independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.) used identical initializations and training budgets (epochs, learning rates, data order, pretraining).
  - Whether sMCL benefits from warm-starts more than baselines; include results from pure random initialization for all methods, or a comparison showing robustness of sMCL to initialization.
- Diversity metrics beyond oracle performance: Oracle accuracy conflates quality and diversity. Please add quantitative diversity analyses:
  - Classification: pairwise disagreement, ambiguity coverage, class-level entropy of assignments.
  - Segmentation: intersection-over-union overlaps across ensemble members, boundary diversity, segment count statistics.
  - Captioning: in addition to unique n-grams, report self-BLEU or distinct-n, and semantic diversity metrics (e.g., SPICE variants), to quantify diversity vs redundancy.
- Load balancing and specialization dynamics: Do some members dominate (starvation of others)? Report assignment histograms over training epochs, per-member utilization, and the effect of M and batch size on balance. Any regularization or annealing (e.g., temperature, soft-assignments) needed to stabilize training?
- Theoretical aspects: The objective involves non-differentiable min and hard assignments. A brief discussion of convergence behavior, stability, and relation to mixture-of-experts with hard gating would strengthen the paper. Is the gradient estimator biased? Under what conditions does sMCL approximate the block coordinate optimum?
- Additional baselines/ablations:
  - Mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network (hard or soft) trained end-to-end to minimize oracle loss.
  - Shared-trunk multi-head architectures vs fully independent models (compute- and memory-matched).
  - Impact of beam search breadth (for all methods) and top-k evaluation beyond oracle to understand trade-offs.
- Reporting: Include top-1/top-k (non-oracle) metrics to show any degradation or trade-off relative to single-prediction performance, since some applications require a good primary prediction in addition to oracle improvements.
- Related work overlap: Please clarify the relationship to “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks” (Lee et al., arXiv:1511.06314). If this is an earlier version or closely related preprint, explicitly state what is new here (algorithmic details, theoretical framing, experiments).

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-motivated, with comprehensive experiments and sensible baselines. However, clearer details on initialization, training budgets, and fairness across baselines are needed. Additional quantitative diversity metrics and analysis of specialization dynamics would improve rigor.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Algorithm 2 is easy to follow. Suggestions: describe tie-breaking; specify all training schedules and shared components for each baseline; move additional implementation details (e.g., k-winner variant, assignment tracking) to an appendix.

Originality
Moderate to good. The sMCL training scheme is a practical and elegant adaptation of MCL to modern deep nets via SGD. While conceptually related to hard-EM, winner-take-all, and mixture-of-experts, targeting oracle loss across diverse deep architectures is novel and practically valuable.

Significance
High practical significance: a simple, general recipe to produce diverse, high-coverage ensembles useful in many downstream systems. Demonstrated gains across tasks/architectures suggest the approach will be adopted and extended.

Pros
- Simple, general, parameter-free, and easy to implement.
- Strong and consistent improvements in oracle metrics across tasks.
- Significant training speedup over prior MCL; trains all members concurrently.
- Emergent, interpretable specialization; informative qualitative results.
- Ablation (k-winner) illuminates the mechanism.

Cons
- Lack of comprehensive diversity metrics and load-balancing analysis.
- Unclear fairness and impact of initialization and shared components across baselines.
- Limited theoretical discussion of convergence/stability.
- Missing or unclear comparison to closely related “M Heads” preprint and to mixture-of-experts with learned gating.

Recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on clarifying experimental details (initialization and fairness), adding quantitative diversity analyses, and strengthening the discussion of related work and training dynamics. The method’s simplicity, generality, and empirical gains make it a valuable contribution.
