Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based training procedure for deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss. At each training step, the example’s gradient is backpropagated only through the ensemble member with the lowest loss (“winner-take-gradient”), encouraging members to specialize on different regions of the data distribution. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all members concurrently. Experiments span image classification (CIFAR-10 with CNNs), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCNs), and image captioning (MSCOCO with CNN+LSTM), showing consistent gains in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, over MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al.] with substantially less training time, and over a submodular/boosting-style approach [Dey et al.]. Qualitative analyses suggest emergent, interpretable expertise (e.g., class- or structure-specific specialists) among ensemble members.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS; AISTATS], which alternates between assigning data to experts and retraining, and on generating diverse outputs in structured models (e.g., M-best/diverse solutions in MRFs [Batra et al.; Kirillov et al.]). Unlike these, sMCL provides a practical, concurrent SGD algorithm suited to modern deep nets. It also connects to ensemble diversity methods (bagging, negative correlation) and generalist/specialist or mixture-of-experts architectures (e.g., Ahmed et al.; Hinton et al.), but differs in learning specialization purely from the oracle-loss objective without additional gating or distillation machinery.

Quality
The core idea is technically sound and simple: replace the hard assignment-retrain cycle of MCL with stochastic hard assignment per minibatch and backprop only through the current “winner.” The formulation follows directly from the gradient of the upper-bound objective. The empirical evaluation is broad, uses standard datasets and metrics (oracle Top-k, IoU, CIDEr-D), and compares to reasonable baselines. The speedup over MCL (by avoiding iterative retraining) is meaningful. Potential limitations: the evaluation centers on oracle metrics rather than end-to-end downstream use; there is limited analysis of stability (e.g., tie-breaking, imbalance among experts) and of compute/memory trade-offs at inference.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and the main algorithm is easy to follow. The qualitative visualizations effectively communicate diversity and specialization. A minor presentation issue: the pseudocode in Figure 2 appears as a raster image; embedding it as text or vector graphics would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality
While the objective and learning setting are rooted in MCL, the winner-take-gradient SGD formulation for deep networks is, to my knowledge, novel and practically important. The method’s generality across architectures and losses, and the observation of emergent expert behavior, are new and interesting contributions relative to prior work on diverse output generation and ensemble training.

Significance
Producing multiple high-quality hypotheses is valuable for tasks with inherent ambiguity and for systems with downstream selection or human-in-the-loop components. sMCL offers a practical way to train such ensembles at scale, with demonstrated gains across three modalities. This should be of broad interest to the NIPS community and could spur follow-up work on inference-time selection, calibration, and diversity-aware training.

Strengths
- Simple, elegant algorithm that integrates seamlessly with SGD.
- Architecture- and loss-agnostic; parameter-free; trains members concurrently.
- Strong empirical results across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Clear qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization.
- Significant practical speedup versus prior MCL-style training.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Deployment/inference: The paper focuses on oracle metrics; practical use requires an explicit selection mechanism at test time (e.g., learned gating, calibrated confidence, human verification, or downstream re-ranking). Including even a lightweight selector or human-in-the-loop simulation would strengthen the case.
- Cost at inference: Running M models increases compute/memory. A discussion of cost-accuracy trade-offs, possible early-exit or cascade strategies, or distillation of the ensemble into a single model would be valuable.
- Stability and fairness: Analyze how often experts become underutilized, how ties are handled, and whether class/attribute coverage is balanced. Simple diagnostics (assignment entropy, per-expert data share, effect of different tie-breaking rules) would help.
- Baselines: Consider comparing to mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network, and to top-k training variants (e.g., penalizing the lowest-k losses) as an intermediate between independent training and strict oracle loss.
- Presentation: Replace rasterized pseudocode with text/vector; include more implementation details for reproducibility (initialization, tie-breaking policy, per-task hyperparameters consolidated).

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, general, and easily adoptable method with clear algorithmic contribution.
- Demonstrated, consistent improvements on diverse tasks and models.
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization and diversity.
- Addresses a timely need for multi-hypothesis prediction in ambiguous settings.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited treatment of inference-time selection and end-to-end utility beyond oracle metrics.
- Incomplete comparison to alternative expert-selection frameworks (e.g., learned gating MoE).
- Some analyses (stability, compute/memory trade-offs) are missing.

Overall evaluation
A strong and well-executed paper. The winner-take-gradient formulation makes MCL feasible for deep networks and yields tangible gains. While the lack of an explicit inference-time selection strategy is a practical gap, it is problem-dependent and can be addressed in follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
