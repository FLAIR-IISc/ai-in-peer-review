This paper introduces a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep neural networks. A key feature of the proposed method is that only a single member of the ensemble is updated for any given training example; specifically, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the ground truth label is selected for a gradient update. The core idea is simple and elegant, and the experimental results are surprisingly strong, appearing to address certain deficiencies of existing ensemble techniques. However, there are several points that require clarification and improvement.

1.  **Clarity of the Testing Phase:** The procedure for inference at test time is not clearly described. The description on page 5, line 159, seems to imply that the best-performing member is selected using the ground truth label. If this is the case, it would be an invalid evaluation protocol, as ground truth is unavailable at test time. The authors must clarify the exact mechanism used for making predictions on new, unseen data.

2.  **Undefined Baseline:** The paper frequently refers to a baseline method called 'independent ensembles' or 'regular ensembles' (abbreviated as 'Indp.' in figures and mentioned on page 5, line 181). However, I was unable to find a definition or explanation of this baseline in the text. A clear description is necessary for the reader to properly interpret the experimental comparisons.

3.  **Justification of "Specialization":** The authors frequently argue that the proposed method encourages ensemble members to "specialize" on different output classes. This claim seems at odds with the training procedure, where the assignment of an example to an ensemble member is performed on a per-example basis, not a per-class basis. Therefore, the justification for class-level specialization is not sufficiently convincing. As a suggestion, have the authors considered explicitly encouraging class-based specialization by incorporating it directly into the loss function?

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The discussion of related work is missing a relevant line of research on diversity regularization in neural network ensembles (e.g., Chen et al.). These techniques, while arguably more formal in their approach to encouraging diversity, have not been extensively studied in the context of modern deep learning, making a comparison or discussion highly relevant to this work.

In summary, the paper presents a simple and effective idea with strong empirical results. Despite the concerns raised above regarding clarity, the justification of claims, and missing related work, the contribution appears valuable. I therefore lean towards a weak acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing these points in a satisfactory manner.
