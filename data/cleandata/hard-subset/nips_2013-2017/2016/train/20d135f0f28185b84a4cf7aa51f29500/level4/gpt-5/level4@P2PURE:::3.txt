This paper introduces a simple approach to multiple-choice learning (MCL) when the learners are deep networks. Prior MCL methods do not directly extend to deep architectures. The authors add a winner-takes-the-gradient layer during backpropagation, which couples learner training with the assignment problem in an ensemble. Experiments show good performance across a broad range of tasks: image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

Strengths:
- The MCL problem for deep learning is important to the NIPS community.
- The proposed method is appealingly simple.
- The experimental evaluation spans three distinct tasks.

Weaknesses:
- My primary concern is the heavy emphasis on “diversity” (including in the title) without any explicit mechanism to enforce it. I expected a concrete diversity term or constraint in the model; its absence is disappointing.
- The contribution feels incremental relative to the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions:
- Rewrite the first sentence of the abstract.
- Tone down claims and framing around “diversity.”
- Line 108: in “we fixed the form of …,” the first “f” should be “g.”
- Line 115: there is an extra period in the middle of a sentence.

Question:
- For the deep-learning baseline of MCL, how did the authors ensure that each network converged to a reasonable solution? Stopping learners early could substantially degrade ensemble performance.
