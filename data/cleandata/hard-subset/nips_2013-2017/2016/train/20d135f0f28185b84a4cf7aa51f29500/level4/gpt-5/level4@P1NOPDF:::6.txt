The paper proposes an ensemble training algorithm for deep models in which only a single ensemble member is active for each training example—unlike prior work on both deep and classical ensembles. During training, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is identified and updated via its gradient. The idea is simple yet yields surprisingly strong empirical results and appears to address several shortcomings of existing approaches. I recommend a weak accept.

Main comments:
1) Test-time procedure unclear. The testing phase is not explained clearly. From the description (e.g., line 159 on page 5), I worry the selection of the “best” member might implicitly use information tied to the ground-truth label, which is not available at test time. Please clarify precisely how the active member is chosen at inference without access to the label, and whether the gating/selection mechanism is learned or fixed. If a proxy (e.g., maximum predicted probability) is used, specify it and report sensitivity.

2) Baseline definitions (“independent” vs. “regular”) are missing. Figures and text refer to “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (denoted “Indp.” in the figures and at line 181 on page 5), but I could not find a clear definition. Please detail:
- How these baselines are trained (shared vs. separate initializations, data splits, optimization schedules).
- Whether they average probabilities, logits, or votes at test time.
- How capacity and total training budget compare to your method.

3) Specialization claims need stronger justification. The paper repeatedly claims that ensemble members “specialize” to different output classes. However, the training assignment is performed per example, not explicitly per class or cluster, so the evidence for class-level specialization is not fully convincing. Please either:
- Provide quantitative analyses demonstrating class- or region-specific specialization (e.g., confusion overlap, mutual exclusivity of high-confidence regions, or assignment entropy by class), or
- Incorporate specialization more explicitly into the objective, for example by adding a term that encourages diversity or class-conditional gating among members. As written, the mechanism could simply be a hard-EM style winner-take-all on individual examples without true class-level partitioning.

4) Missing related work on diversity regularization. The paper does not discuss closely related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.). This line of work provides a more principled way to promote member diversity, though it has been less explored in deep settings. Please position your approach relative to these methods, clarify conceptual similarities/differences (e.g., implicit vs. explicit diversity control), and, if feasible, include a comparison or ablation with a simple diversity-regularized baseline.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Conceptually clean method; easy to implement; empirically strong; addresses limitations of standard ensembling (e.g., redundancy and inefficiency).
- Weaknesses: Ambiguity around the test-time selection mechanism; unclear baseline definitions; insufficient evidence for the claimed specialization; missing discussion of diversity-regularized alternatives.

With clearer test-time methodology, precise baseline descriptions, stronger analyses of specialization, and an expanded related-work discussion (including diversity regularization), the paper would be significantly strengthened. I recommend weak acceptance.
