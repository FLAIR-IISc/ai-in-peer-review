The paper presents an ensemble training algorithm for deep learners that stands out from previous deep and classical ensemble methods in that only one learner of the ensemble is active for each training example. During training, the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is identified and updated with the gradient. Overall, this simple yet effective idea leads to surprisingly good experimental results and addresses some of the deficiencies of existing methods, which is why I recommend it for weak acceptance.

One aspect that requires clarification is the testing phase. Upon closer examination, it appears that the authors use the ground truth to find the best member, as explained in line 159 on page 5. However, a more detailed explanation of the testing phase would be beneficial for readers. 

Another point of confusion arises from the authors' references to 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' in the figures and text (denoted as 'Indp.' in figures and line 181 on page 5). Unfortunately, I was unable to find a clear explanation for this baseline, and including one would strengthen the paper.

The authors repeatedly emphasize the 'specialization' of ensemble members towards different output classes. However, the reassignment in training occurs on a per-example basis, which makes this argument less convincing. To further justify this claim, it would be interesting to incorporate specialization into the loss function. 

Furthermore, the authors do not discuss the related work of diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which is a more rigorous technique that has yet to be explored in the deep learning setting. Including a discussion of this related work would enhance the paper's contribution and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed algorithm.
