Summary
This paper addresses Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in the context of deep networks. The goal is to train an ensemble of M predictors to minimize oracle loss, i.e., the loss of the best prediction among M for each input. Building on prior MCL work (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al.), the authors propose a simple and practical SGD-based procedure—Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL)—that interleaves example-to-model assignment with training: for each mini-batch, each example’s loss is backpropagated only through the ensemble member that incurs the smallest loss (“winner-takes-the-gradient”). The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all members concurrently.

Experiments cover three settings:
- Image classification on CIFAR-10 using a small CNN (“CIFAR10-Quick”).
- Semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC using Fully Convolutional Networks.
- Image captioning on MSCOCO using a VGG16 encoder and LSTM decoder (neuraltalk2), with and without CNN fine-tuning.

Across tasks, sMCL improves oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, the submodular/boosting approach of Dey et al., and a reimplementation of MCL that alternates assignment and retraining. The authors report roughly 5× faster training than MCL (with five meta-iterations) and qualitative evidence of emergent specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class biases in classification, different segmentation modes, and distinct n-grams in captioning). The paper situates sMCL relative to ensemble diversity methods (e.g., bagging, negative correlation), expert architectures, and diverse M-best techniques in probabilistic structured prediction, emphasizing that sMCL avoids retraining or sequential procedures and applies broadly to deep architectures.

Strengths
- Importance and scope: MCL for deep networks is timely and relevant to the NIPS community, especially for systems that benefit from multiple plausible hypotheses and oracle-based downstream selection.
- Simplicity and practicality: The “winner-take-gradient” mechanism is easy to implement, parameter-free, and compatible with standard SGD pipelines and losses.
- Breadth of evaluation: Results on three distinct tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) and multiple architectures demonstrate generality.
- Empirical gains and speed: Consistent improvements in oracle metrics over strong baselines, and a clear runtime advantage over iterative MCL. The qualitative analyses of emergent “expertise” are compelling.

Weaknesses
- Emphasis on “diversity” without an explicit mechanism: Although the paper motivates diversity heavily (including in the title), the method does not explicitly enforce diversity; instead, diversity emerges as a byproduct of minimizing oracle loss. I was expecting a principled diversity regularizer or constraint; the absence of one makes the contribution feel more incremental in light of prior MCL formulations.
- Incremental advance over prior MCL: Conceptually, sMCL is a straightforward SGD reinterpretation of the alternating MCL relaxation of Guzman-Rivera et al. The main novelty is computational—making MCL practical for deep networks—rather than a new objective or theory.

Quality
- The technical idea is sound and clearly derived from the MCL objective’s gradients. The implementation details are straightforward.
- Results are convincing across tasks, with reasonable baselines. However, for the MCL baseline, the degree of convergence for each meta-iteration may strongly affect performance; more details would strengthen the fairness of the comparison (see question below).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The training protocols and evaluation metrics are clearly stated.
- Minor issues listed below (notation and copy-editing).

Originality
- Moderate. The winner-take-gradient view is an incremental but useful operationalization of the MCL relaxation for deep networks. The contribution lies in making MCL feasible and effective in practice for modern architectures.

Significance
- Practical significance is high: sMCL provides a simple recipe for training diverse deep ensembles optimized for oracle metrics. This is likely to be adopted by practitioners who need multiple hypotheses.
- Conceptual significance is more modest given the reliance on existing MCL formulations and the lack of an explicit diversity mechanism.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and easy-to-adopt method with clear empirical benefits.
- Demonstrated across multiple tasks/architectures with consistent improvements and speedups.
- Insightful qualitative analyses showing emergent specialization.

Cons for acceptance
- Overemphasis on “diversity” without an explicit diversity component in the training objective.
- Incremental conceptual advance relative to Guzman-Rivera et al.; primary novelty is computational/practical.

Minor comments and suggestions
- Abstract: The first sentence is awkward; consider rewriting for clarity.
- Tone down claims around “diversity” in the title and narrative, or add an explicit diversity-inducing mechanism/analysis to substantiate the emphasis.
- Notation: In Section 3 (“we fix the form of ...”), the first “f” should be “g.”
- Typo: Extra period appears mid-sentence near line 115.
- Consider clarifying tie-breaking when multiple predictors have identical loss on an example, and whether stochastic tie-breaking affects stability.

Question for the authors
- MCL baseline convergence: For the alternating MCL baseline with deep networks, how did you ensure that each network was trained sufficiently close to convergence at each meta-iteration before reassigning examples? Early stopping or insufficient convergence could substantially disadvantage MCL and amplify the reported speed/performance gap. Please provide training schedules, stopping criteria, and any measures taken to verify convergence across meta-iterations.
