This paper addresses the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in the context of deep neural networks. As the authors correctly point out, many existing solutions for MCL are not readily applicable to deep learning settings. The proposed solution involves a simple yet effective modification to the training process: a "winner-takes-the-gradient" mechanism in the backpropagation step. This allows for the simultaneous, end-to-end training of an ensemble of deep networks (the "learners") and the assignment of data points to the most appropriate learner. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated across a diverse range of applications, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, where it achieves strong performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles the important and timely problem of adapting ensemble methods like Multiple Choice Learning to modern deep learning architectures. This is a topic of significant interest to the NIPS community.

2.  **Simplicity of the Method:** The proposed approach is notable for its simplicity and elegance. It provides a straightforward mechanism for joint optimization that integrates cleanly into the standard backpropagation algorithm without requiring complex architectural changes or significant computational overhead.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors have validated their method on three distinct and challenging tasks. Demonstrating success in classification, segmentation, and captioning provides compelling evidence for the general applicability and robustness of the proposed technique.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Mismatch between Motivation and Method:** My primary concern is the significant disconnect between the paper's central motivation—diversity—and the technical implementation. The title and introduction heavily emphasize the importance of encouraging diversity among the learners in the ensemble. This framing leads the reader to expect an explicit mechanism, such as a diversity-enforcing regularizer or loss term, within the model. However, the proposed method does not contain any such component. Diversity appears to be an emergent property of the competitive "winner-takes-the-gradient" process rather than an explicitly optimized objective. This feels somewhat misleading. The paper's core contribution is a novel joint training strategy, and it would be more accurately framed as such, rather than as a method for promoting diversity.

2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The novelty of the proposed solution appears somewhat limited when considered in the context of prior work, specifically the relaxation for MCL proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. The contribution could be more accurately positioned as an effective adaptation and application of this concept to the end-to-end training of deep networks, rather than a fundamentally new approach to the assignment problem in MCL.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   The first sentence of the abstract is somewhat awkward and could be rewritten for greater clarity and impact.
*   In line with the major weakness identified above, the emphasis on "diversity" should be toned down throughout the manuscript, including the title, to better align with the paper's actual contribution.
*   Line 108: In the phrase "we fixed the form of f...", the first 'f' should likely be 'g' to be consistent with the surrounding notation.
*   Line 115: There is an extraneous period in the middle of the sentence.

**Question for the Authors:**

Could you please elaborate on the experimental setup for the deep learning baseline for MCL? The standard two-stage approach (first training individual learners to convergence, then training the gating network) is highly sensitive to the quality of the pre-trained learners. If the individual networks in the baseline were not trained for a sufficient duration, their suboptimal performance could inadvertently inflate the perceived benefits of your proposed joint-training method. What measures were taken to ensure that each learner in the baseline ensemble had converged to a reasonable solution before the gating network was trained?
