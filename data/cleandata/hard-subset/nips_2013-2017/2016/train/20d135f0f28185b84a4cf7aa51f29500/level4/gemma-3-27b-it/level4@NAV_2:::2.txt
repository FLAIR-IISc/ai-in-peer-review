The paper proposes a novel method for training an ensemble of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) – or any other gradient-based approach – by leveraging an oracle to identify the optimal ensemble member for each training instance and applying backpropagation only to that network’s weights. This approach encourages specialization among the network members, leading to ensembles that effectively cover a wide range of possible outputs. Results are presented across diverse domains, demonstrating strong performance gains. Overall, this is a strong paper presenting an interesting idea for training DNN ensembles with an oracle, guiding backpropagation updates to the network producing the best output (according to the ground truth label). The method appears to effectively enforce specialization, particularly in a classification setting. The presentation is clear and well-organized, and the experiments are conducted on a variety of tasks. This is a valuable contribution that merits publication. 

The work would be even more compelling if it further explored the practical implications of using ensembles trained with this approach. Currently, the focus is on training with the oracle; however, the utilization of the resulting ensemble is not developed beyond noting its ability to provide a diverse pool of potential solutions and its consistency with top-k metrics (e.g., ImageNet). Addressing this aspect would enhance the practical usability of the proposed method. Nevertheless, the core contribution is significant in its current form, with the remaining considerations being relatively problem-specific. 

A minor point: Figure 2 presents pseudocode as raster images embedded in the paper. Consider including the pseudocode directly within the text, or embedding the figure as a vector image for improved clarity and scalability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-supported by experimental results across multiple domains (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning).
*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning – training diverse ensembles – with a novel and effective approach.
*   The stochastic gradient descent-based optimization is efficient and applicable to a wide range of architectures and loss functions.
*   Demonstrates clear evidence of emergent expertise and specialization among ensemble members.
*   The method outperforms existing baselines, including established approaches like MCL.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the practical implications and downstream applications of the trained ensembles beyond simply generating multiple outputs.
*   Figure 2 contains rasterized pseudocode, which could be improved with a vector graphic or direct inclusion in the text.
