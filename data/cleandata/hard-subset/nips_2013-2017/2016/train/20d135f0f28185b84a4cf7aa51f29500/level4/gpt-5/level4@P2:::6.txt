Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based procedure for training deep ensembles to optimize oracle loss. In each batch, for every example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss (“winner”) backpropagates its gradient, yielding a winner-take-gradient update. This approach can be seen as a stochastic, fine-grained alternative to the alternating retraining strategy of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) by Guzman-Rivera et al., avoiding repeated full retraining and enabling concurrent training of all members. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and intended to promote diversity through emergent specialization. Experiments span CIFAR10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning with CNN+LSTM, comparing against independent ensembles, MCL, and a sequential boosting-like approach (Dey et al.). The authors report consistent gains in oracle metrics and substantial speedups over MCL.

Strengths:
- Quality: The core idea is technically sound and well-motivated by the gradient structure of the upper-bound oracle objective. The algorithm is easy to implement within standard SGD frameworks and appears stable across tasks. Empirical results are broad (classification, segmentation, captioning), show clear oracle improvements over strong baselines, and indicate meaningful speedups versus MCL.
- Clarity: The problem setting and the winner-take-gradient update are presented clearly, with a clean connection to the MCL objective. Visualizations of member “specialization” are informative and support the claim that diversity emerges under oracle training.
- Originality: While related to MCL, hard-EM/K-means-style partitioning, and mixture-of-experts ideas, the stochastic assignment and concurrent training for deep ensembles optimizing oracle loss is a useful and practical contribution that removes a key bottleneck (retraining).
- Significance: The method is likely to be adopted because it is simple, general, and delivers better oracle performance, which is the right objective when downstream selection/reranking exists. The qualitative analyses (e.g., alternative segmentations, varied captions) are compelling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Test-time protocol and oracle evaluation: The manuscript uses oracle metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy, max IoU, oracle CIDEr) for evaluation. Please clarify explicitly the test-time procedure: are M predictions per input generated and scored against ground truth to compute the oracle metric (no ground truth used for selection), or is any form of re-ranking/selection simulated? A brief algorithmic description mirroring the training pseudocode would help.
- Baseline definition: The paper refers to “independent”/“regular” ensembles (Indp.) but does not clearly define training budgets and protocols. Please specify: identical architectures and hyperparameters? Same number of total optimization steps and wall-clock budget as sMCL? Any variance across random seeds? This is important for fairness.
- Specialization claims: The paper attributes class- or structure-level specialization to sMCL, yet assignment is per example, not explicitly encouraged at the class/structure level. The observation is intriguing but would benefit from stronger evidence (e.g., confusion-matrix analyses, per-class calibration, or stability across seeds). Could the loss incorporate explicit specialization or diversity (e.g., k-winners, entropy/diversity regularizers, mutual exclusivity, or determinantal/diversity-promoting terms) to make the effect more principled?
- Related work on diversity regularization: The discussion of ensemble diversity omits a large literature on diversity-promoting objectives for neural networks, including negative correlation learning (Liu & Yao, 1999) and more recent diversity regularizers (e.g., diversity-encouraging penalties over outputs/representations). Situating sMCL relative to such regularization-based approaches would strengthen the paper.
- Fairness in captioning comparisons: Beam search over a single model can raise oracle scores while reducing diversity. Since sMCL ensembles also permit beam search, it would be fair to report sMCL+beam results to disentangle training vs inference-time gains. Also clarify the CNN sharing/freeze/fine-tune choices and memory constraints, and whether sharing the encoder affects ensemble diversity.
- Optimization details and stability: Please discuss tie-breaking among winners, sensitivity to batch size, convergence behavior, and robustness across random initializations. Some ablation on the k-winner variant (already hinted at) and its effect on the specialization/accuracy trade-off would be useful.
- Downstream impact: Since oracle metrics proxy systems with downstream selection, one example of a simple downstream selector (e.g., a learned reranker) would help demonstrate practical end-to-end gains, not just oracle improvements.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Solid, with convincing empirical evidence and a clear algorithmic foundation. Some missing details (test-time protocol, baseline fairness, optimization stability) should be addressed.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but definitions of baselines and test-time evaluation should be made explicit; minor organizational tweaks (e.g., a brief test-time pseudocode) would help.
- Originality: Moderately high. The stochastic, concurrent variant of MCL for deep ensembles is a practical and timely contribution distinct from prior alternating/retraining schemes.
- Significance: High practical significance given breadth of applicability, simplicity, and consistent gains in oracle metrics. Strong potential for adoption.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and parameter-free training procedure that directly targets oracle loss.
- Clear and consistent improvements over strong baselines across diverse tasks.
- Substantial training speedups over prior MCL-style methods.
- Emergent, interpretable diversity among ensemble members.

Cons for acceptance:
- Test-time/measurement protocol and baseline definitions need clarification for full reproducibility and fairness.
- Specialization claims are mostly observational; stronger quantitative support or explicit diversity mechanisms would help.
- Related work on diversity-promoting regularization in neural networks is under-cited.
- No demonstration of downstream selection performance beyond oracle metrics.

Questions for the authors:
1) Precisely how are test-time oracle metrics computed? Is any ground-truth information used for selection beyond scoring for evaluation?
2) How are “independent ensembles” trained (data shuffling, seeds, training budget, early stopping)? Are total FLOPs/wall-clock comparable to sMCL?
3) How are ties between members handled during training? How sensitive are results to batch size and learning rate?
4) Can you report sMCL+beam for captioning to match the single-model beam baseline?
5) Have you evaluated stability across seeds and the variance of specialization patterns?
6) Could you compare against a diversity-regularized ensemble baseline (e.g., negative correlation learning) or report a simple diversity penalty variant?

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The method is practical, broadly applicable, and empirically strong, with clear advantages over prior MCL-style approaches. Addressing the noted clarity and related-work gaps would make the paper even stronger.
