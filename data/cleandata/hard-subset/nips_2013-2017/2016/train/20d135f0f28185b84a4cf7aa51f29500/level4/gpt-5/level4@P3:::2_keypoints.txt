- Proposal: sMCL — a simple SGD-based "winner-take-gradient" method to train deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss, updating only the lowest-loss member per example.
- Novelty: First practical, stochastic end-to-end SGD formulation of Multiple Choice Learning that scales to modern deep architectures.
- Generality: Architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free beyond ensemble size, and trains all members concurrently.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.), diverse M-best inference, and sequential diversity/boosting methods, addressing MCL’s impractical retraining loop.
- Experiments: Evaluated on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning with consistent oracle metric improvements.
- Baselines: Compared against independent ensembles, classical MCL (within retraining budget), Dey et al., and beam search for captioning.
- Empirical findings: sMCL improves oracle metrics, yields emergent specialization (e.g., class-wise experts), and is substantially faster than classical MCL.
- Soundness: Optimization follows oracle-loss upper-bound with hard assignments; gradient behavior is simple and correct.
- Empiricism caveat: Evaluation centers on oracle metrics, leaving test-time selection strategies under-explored.
- Theoretical gap: Limited analysis of convergence, stability, and effects of hard assignments.
- Clarity: Well-written with clear motivation and helpful "winner-take-gradient" description.
- Presentation issue: Pseudo-code is rasterized in Fig.2; vector/text pseudo-code would aid readability and reproducibility.
- Significance: Practical recipe for multi-hypothesis outputs with broad applicability and potential impact despite selection-gap caveat.
- Strength: Simple, general, parameter-free method integrating with SGD and scaling to deep nets.
- Strength: Strong empirical improvements across tasks and clear interpretable specialization.
- Weakness: Lacks evaluation of realistic test-time selectors (e.g., confidence, re-ranking, learned gating).
- Weakness: Need comparisons combining sMCL with inference-time diversity (e.g., beam search) and wall-clock/runtime trade-offs.
- Weakness: Sensitivity to tie-breaking and potential oscillations not thoroughly analyzed; soft-assignment variants suggested.
- Weakness: Insufficient resource accounting — training time, memory, compute budgets for all methods.
- Weakness: Missing explicit diversity metrics beyond oracle scores (e.g., pairwise disagreement, IoU variance, n-gram diversity).
- Minor fixes: Replace rasterized pseudo-code and ensure legible figures at print scale.
- Questions for authors: Performance of simple test-time selectors vs. oracle?
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to initialization and ensemble size M; diminishing returns and persistence of specialization?
- Questions for authors: Do assignment oscillations occur early; would independent warm-up help?
- Questions for authors: Can sMCL combine with snapshot/temporal ensembles or MC dropout for more diversity?
- Questions for authors: For captioning, what's the trade-off between sMCL and beam search in oracle performance, diversity, and compute?
- Arguments for acceptance: Practical, novel SGD MCL formulation with consistent multi-task improvements and interpretable specialization.
- Arguments against acceptance: Reliance on oracle metrics without realistic selection evaluation, limited stability/resource analysis, and beam search competitiveness in captioning.
- Overall assessment: Strong paper with a simple, effective method; addressing test-time selection and additional analyses would make it outstanding.
