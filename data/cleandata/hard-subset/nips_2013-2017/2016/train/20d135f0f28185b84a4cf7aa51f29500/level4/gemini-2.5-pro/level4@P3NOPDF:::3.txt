This paper addresses the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in the context of deep neural networks. As standard MCL methods are not directly applicable to deep learning, the authors propose a simple and effective solution. Their method introduces a "winner-takes-the-gradient" mechanism during backpropagation, which allows for the joint, end-to-end training of the ensemble of learners and the assignment of data points to them. The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated through a series of experiments on diverse tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Relevance:** The problem of adapting Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep neural networks is timely and highly relevant to the NIPS community. Finding effective ways to train ensembles of deep models is a significant challenge, and this paper makes a clear contribution to this area.

2.  **Method Simplicity:** The proposed method is notable for its simplicity and elegance. The "winner-takes-the-gradient" approach is an intuitive way to integrate the learner selection and training processes into a single, unified backpropagation pass, avoiding the complexities of traditional alternating optimization schemes.

3.  **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across three distinct and challenging tasks. Showing positive results on classification, segmentation, and captioning strengthens the paper's claims of generality and broad applicability.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Disconnect Between Motivation and Method:** My primary concern is the significant disconnect between the paper's motivation and the proposed method. The title and introduction place a strong emphasis on fostering "diversity" among the ensemble learners, which initially suggests a novel mechanism for encouraging varied specializations. However, the proposed method does not include any explicit term, loss function, or constraint to enforce this diversity. The gradients are simply routed to the winning learner for a given instance. While this encourages specialization, it does not guarantee a diverse set of specializations across the ensemble. This discrepancy makes the framing of the paper around "diversity" feel somewhat misleading.

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The contribution, while effective, could be viewed as an incremental improvement upon existing work. Specifically, the method appears to be a direct application of the relaxation technique for latent variable models proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. to the context of deep learning ensembles. The authors should more clearly articulate the novel technical contributions of their work beyond this direct application to differentiate it more strongly.

### Question for the Authors

1.  Regarding the baseline comparison for MCL with deep networks: Could you please elaborate on the training protocol for the individual learners in the baseline? Specifically, how was convergence determined for each network before the assignment step? The performance of traditional MCL is sensitive to the quality of the individual learners, and premature termination of their training could significantly hinder the overall ensemble performance, potentially leading to an unfair comparison with your end-to-end method.

### Minor Suggestions

*   The opening sentence of the abstract could be revised for better clarity and impact.
*   Given the lack of an explicit diversity mechanism in the model, I strongly recommend that the authors tone down the emphasis on "diversity" throughout the manuscript, particularly in the title and introduction, to better align the paper's narrative with its technical contribution.
*   **Line 108:** In the phrase "we fixed the form of f...", the first 'f' should likely be 'g'.
*   **Line 115:** There appears to be an extraneous period in the middle of the sentence.
