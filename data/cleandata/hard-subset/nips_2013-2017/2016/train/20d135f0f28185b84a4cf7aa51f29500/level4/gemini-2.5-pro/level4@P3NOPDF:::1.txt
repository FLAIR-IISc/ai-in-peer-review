### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) approach for training ensemble models. The method is an extension of the Minimum Classification Loss (MCL) framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], in which the ensemble's loss is defined by the loss of the single best-performing classifier on a given example. This objective encourages the individual classifiers within the ensemble to become diverse specialists. The primary contribution of this work is the adaptation of this MCL objective for training with SGD. This modification is significant because it enables the method to scale effectively to large datasets and complex models, such as deep neural networks, which was a major limitation of the original full-batch approach in [8].

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and clearly articulates its contributions relative to prior work. The core idea is explained effectively.

2.  **Practical Significance:** While the algorithmic change from the full-batch method in [8] to an SGD-based one is conceptually straightforward, its practical implications are substantial. By enabling SGD-based training, the authors make the powerful idea of minimum classification loss ensembles applicable to modern deep learning architectures and large-scale problems, a significant and valuable step forward.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

My primary initial concern was that the contribution might be too incremental over the work of [8]. However, the paper effectively argues that this adaptation to SGD is non-trivial and unlocks significant practical utility, which largely mitigates this concern. Nonetheless, the experimental analysis could be strengthened to provide deeper insights and more conclusive evidence.

1.  **Comparison to a Mini-batch Baseline:** The original MCL algorithm in [8] was designed for full-batch training. A crucial comparison that would strengthen the paper's claims would be against a naive mini-batch implementation of the original MCL algorithm (i.e., applying the original update rule on mini-batches instead of the full dataset). This experiment would help isolate the benefits of the proposed stochastic formulation from the more general benefits of simply using mini-batches for optimization. It is unclear if the current comparison to [8] already does this, but this point should be clarified and, if it is a new experiment, its results would be very informative.

2.  **Deeper Analysis of Diversity:** The paper posits that the MCL objective promotes classifier diversity, which is a key motivation for the method. While the empirical results are promising, the paper would benefit from a more direct and quantitative analysis of this diversity. For instance, the authors could include measures such as the disagreement rate or the Q-statistic among the ensemble members. Visualizing how different members of the ensemble specialize on different subsets of the data (e.g., by identifying examples where only one classifier is correct) would also provide valuable insight into the method's behavior.

**Conclusion**

Overall, the paper presents a valuable and practical extension to an interesting ensemble training framework. The core idea is simple but powerful, enabling a promising technique to be applied to the current landscape of deep neural networks. Despite my initial reservations about the level of novelty, the authors have successfully demonstrated the significance of their contribution by making the method scalable and broadly applicable. Therefore, I am in favor of acceptance. I encourage the authors to consider the suggested additions to the experimental analysis, as they would further strengthen the paper.
