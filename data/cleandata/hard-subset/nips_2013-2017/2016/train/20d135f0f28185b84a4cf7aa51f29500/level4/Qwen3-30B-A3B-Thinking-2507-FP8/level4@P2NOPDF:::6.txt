

This paper introduces a conceptually simple yet effective ensemble training algorithm for deep learners. The key innovation lies in activating only a single ensemble member per training example—specifically, the member with the lowest loss relative to the true label—which is then updated via gradient descent. This approach contrasts with conventional ensembles that typically update all members simultaneously. The method demonstrates strong empirical performance across benchmarks, addressing limitations in existing techniques such as inefficient resource utilization and lack of member specialization. I recommend weak acceptance due to its novelty and effectiveness, though several clarifications are needed.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Testing Phase Ambiguity:** The paper does not clearly describe the inference procedure. The methodology appears to rely on ground-truth labels during testing to select the best-performing member (as suggested in Line 159, Page 5), which is unrealistic for practical deployment. The authors must explicitly clarify how the ensemble operates *without* access to true labels at test time (e.g., by using the member with the highest confidence prediction or an alternative selection strategy).

2.  **Undefined Baseline ("Independent Ensembles"):** The paper references "independent ensembles" (denoted "Indp." in figures and Line 181, Page 5) as a baseline but provides no definition or description of this method. Without understanding how this baseline is constructed (e.g., are members trained independently without interaction? How are they combined?), it is impossible to evaluate its relevance or the claimed superiority of the proposed method. The authors should either define this baseline or remove it from the comparison.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for "Specialization":** The authors repeatedly emphasize that ensemble members "specialize" toward different output classes. However, the training mechanism reassigns *per-example* activation based solely on immediate loss, not on class-specific learning goals. This per-example reassignment does not inherently lead to class specialization; it may instead cause members to compete for the same examples. The argument lacks sufficient theoretical or empirical support. The authors should either: (a) provide evidence of class-specific specialization (e.g., via activation maps or class-wise loss distributions), or (b) explicitly incorporate specialization into the loss function (e.g., by penalizing members for overlapping class assignments) and demonstrate its impact.

4.  **Omission of Key Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss diversity regularization techniques in neural networks, which directly address the goal of creating specialized ensemble members. For instance, Chen et al. (2015) propose a rigorous framework for enforcing diversity via loss terms, though its application to deep learning remains underexplored. Ignoring this work weakens the paper's positioning and misses an opportunity to contextualize the novelty of the proposed approach. The authors should engage with this literature, clarifying how their method differs from or complements existing diversity-focused strategies.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*   **Strengths:** The core idea is elegant, computationally efficient, and empirically validated. The method effectively leverages per-example selection to improve performance over standard ensembles, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are constrained.
*   **Weaknesses:** Critical gaps exist in the description of the testing procedure, the definition of a key baseline, the justification for the specialization claim, and the discussion of related work on diversity. These issues prevent a full assessment of the method's theoretical grounding and practical advantages.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach with strong empirical results. However, the ambiguities and omissions outlined above require substantial clarification before the work can be considered for publication. I recommend *weak acceptance* contingent on the authors addressing all four points above in the camera-ready version. Specifically, they must resolve the testing ambiguity, define the "independent ensembles" baseline, provide evidence or a formalization for specialization, and incorporate relevant diversity regularization work into the discussion. Without these revisions, the paper's claims and contributions remain partially unsupported.
