This paper presents a simple method for applying multiple choice learning (MCL) to deep networks, a setting where existing MCL solutions are not directly applicable. The authors propose a "winner-takes-the-gradient" mechanism, implemented as a layer during backpropagation, which allows for the end-to-end training of an ensemble by jointly learning the individual models and the assignment of data points to them. The efficacy of the proposed method is demonstrated on a diverse set of tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.

**Strengths:**
- The problem of adapting Multiple Choice Learning for deep networks is timely and relevant to the NIPS community.
- The proposed method is appealing in its simplicity.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three distinct and challenging tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The primary weakness is a significant disconnect between the paper's motivation and its technical contribution. The paper, including its title, heavily emphasizes the concept of "diversity," yet the proposed model does not include any explicit mechanism to enforce or encourage it. The diversity appears to be an emergent property rather than a direct objective of the optimization.
- The contribution appears to be an incremental improvement over prior work, particularly the relaxation method proposed by Guzman et al.

**Minor Suggestions:**
- The first sentence of the abstract is unclear and should be rewritten.
- The authors should tone down the claims regarding "diversity" throughout the manuscript to better align with the method's actual mechanism.
- Correction: On line 108, in the phrase "we fixed the form of f...", the first 'f' should likely be 'g'.
- Correction: There is an extraneous period in the middle of a sentence on line 115.

**Question:**
- Regarding the baseline implementation of MCL with deep networks: what criteria were used to ensure that each individual network had converged to a satisfactory state before being used in the ensemble? The performance of the ensemble could be sensitive to the premature termination of training for the individual learners, so details on the convergence criteria are important for reproducibility and fair comparison.
