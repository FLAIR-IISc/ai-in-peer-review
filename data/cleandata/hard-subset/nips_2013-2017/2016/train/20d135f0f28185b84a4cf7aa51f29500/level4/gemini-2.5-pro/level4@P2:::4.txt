This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly optimize for the "oracle loss," where for any given input, the loss is calculated only with respect to the single best prediction from the entire ensemble. The proposed training algorithm implements this by backpropagating the gradient for each training example only to the ensemble member that achieved the lowest loss on that example—a "winner-take-gradient" strategy. This work extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which used a block coordinate descent approach requiring full retraining of models. In contrast, sMCL is an efficient, SGD-based method that trains all ensemble members concurrently, making it suitable for modern deep learning architectures. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing that it outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines in terms of oracle performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple plausible hypotheses for perception tasks, which often have inherent ambiguity. The motivation is well-articulated, and optimizing for oracle loss is a sensible objective in scenarios where a downstream process or human user can select the best output.

2.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL method is elegant, simple to implement, and general. It is agnostic to the specific network architecture and loss function, and it introduces no new hyperparameters. This makes it a potentially valuable and widely applicable tool for practitioners.

3.  **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors validate their approach across three distinct and challenging tasks involving different model architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). This breadth of experimentation effectively demonstrates the method's versatility. The qualitative results and analysis of emergent model specialization are particularly insightful, showing how different ensemble members learn to focus on different data subsets or modes of the output distribution (e.g., specific classes or caption styles).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality:** The primary contribution is the adaptation of the existing MCL framework [8] to a stochastic, gradient-based optimization suitable for deep networks. While this is a valuable engineering contribution that makes the paradigm practical for a new class of models, the core conceptual novelty beyond [8] is limited. The paper could be strengthened by more clearly positioning itself as an efficient optimization strategy for MCL in the context of deep learning, rather than a fundamentally new learning paradigm.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Contribution:** The technical description in Section 3 is quite brief, spanning less than a page. While the algorithm is simple, a more detailed discussion would improve clarity. For example, the paper could benefit from a discussion comparing sMCL to other related optimization schemes, such as alternating training or hard-EM updates, and clarifying why the proposed approach is more suitable.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** While the breadth of experiments is a strength, the evaluation could be more rigorous in a few areas:
    *   The performance improvements over the independently trained ensembles, while consistent, are sometimes modest. A more in-depth analysis of when and why sMCL provides a significant advantage would be beneficial.
    *   The choice of baselines and datasets could be stronger. For the semantic segmentation task, the experiments are conducted on a subset of the PASCAL VOC 2011 validation set. To make a more compelling case for the method's effectiveness, the authors should consider evaluating on the standard PASCAL VOC 2012 benchmark and comparing against a wider range of strong, contemporary baselines for promoting diversity or improving ensemble performance.
    *   For the image captioning task, the comparison with beam search is interesting. However, beam search is an inference-time procedure, whereas sMCL is a training-time procedure. A more direct comparison would involve applying beam search to each model within the sMCL ensemble to see if the benefits are complementary.

In summary, the paper presents a simple and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles. The main strengths are the clear motivation, the generality of the approach, and the broad experimental validation. The primary weaknesses relate to the incremental nature of the conceptual contribution and the need for more rigorous experimental comparisons on standard benchmarks against stronger baselines.
