- Proposes sMCL: a winner-take-gradient SGD procedure that trains deep ensembles by assigning each minibatch example’s gradient only to the lowest-loss model to minimize oracle loss.  
- Makes MCL practical for deep networks by interleaving assignment and optimization, enabling concurrent training without repeated full retraining.  
- Method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, simple to implement.  
- Experiments on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning show consistent oracle metric improvements over independent ensembles and prior boosting-style baselines.  
- sMCL matches MCL on CIFAR-10 while being ~5x faster (for five meta-iterations) and outperforms baselines on larger tasks where MCL retraining is infeasible.  
- Qualitative analyses demonstrate emergent specialization among ensemble members (class, shape/frequency, n-gram usage).  
- Builds directly on MCL [8,9] by providing a stochastic/block-gradient realization; contrasts with diversity-inducing resampling/decorrelation methods and diverse m-best inference.  
- Strength: practical, minimal SGD modification with broad applicability and strong empirical gains.  
- Strength: clear writing and thorough qualitative analysis.  
- Weakness: incremental algorithmic novelty relative to original MCL; main contribution is practicality rather than a new objective.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to stronger/batched/online MCL variants due to compute constraints.  
- Weakness: diversity analysis is mostly qualitative; lacks quantitative diversity metrics (pairwise disagreement, error correlation, distinct-n, etc.).  
- Weakness: missing sensitivity/ablation studies (batch size, tie-breaking, ensemble size M).  
- Suggestion: compare to batched/online MCL to isolate schedule vs. stochastic formulation effects.  
- Suggestion: report quantitative diversity metrics and connect them to oracle gains; include sensitivity analyses and k-winner exploration.  
- Suggestion: clarify fairness of captioning comparisons (beam search per member and compute-normalized comparisons).  
- Evaluation: technically sound, clear, conceptually incremental but practically significant; high practical impact.  
- Recommendation: accept—small elegant change that makes MCL feasible for deep learning with convincing empirical benefits.
