The authors propose a method to generate multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks that minimize the oracle loss, which is useful when interacting with users. The oracle experiments on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. This paper integrates deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm and proposes a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to minimize the oracle loss of MCL. The proposed optimization method can be easily implemented within the back-propagation learning framework, and the whole model can be trained in an end-to-end fashion.

However, I have several concerns regarding the proposed method. Firstly, the importance of initialization is not thoroughly discussed. Specifically, the authors should investigate how the performance of the model changes when the deep networks are initialized with random values instead of a pre-trained network. Furthermore, the experimental setting for comparison with baseline methods is not clearly explained. It is unclear whether the same pre-trained network was used for initialization in both the proposed method (sMCL) and the baseline methods.

In addition, the authors use oracle accuracy as the primary metric for evaluation. While this is a useful metric, it would be beneficial to see more analysis on the diversity of the ensemble members, as this is a crucial aspect of the proposed method. For instance, the authors could report metrics such as pairwise distance or ensemble diversity to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance.

Finally, the authors may want to consider comparing their method with a similar approach proposed by Stefan Lee et al. in their work "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks" (CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015). This comparison could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method and help to further establish its contribution to the field.
