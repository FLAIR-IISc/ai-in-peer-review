### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep network ensembles. The goal is to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input, which is particularly useful in applications where a downstream oracle (e.g., a human user) can select the best option. The method builds directly on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which minimizes the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble for each training example.

The key contribution of this work is the adaptation of the MCL objective to the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) training paradigm common to deep learning. Instead of the costly alternating optimization of MCL, which requires retraining models to convergence, sMCL trains all ensemble members concurrently. In each SGD step, the loss for a given example is backpropagated only to the single ensemble member that produced the lowest error on that example. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this simple "winner-take-gradient" approach on three distinct tasks: image classification (CNNs), semantic segmentation (FCNs), and image captioning (CNN+RNNs). Across all tasks, sMCL is shown to outperform standard independently trained ensembles and other related methods in oracle performance, while also producing qualitatively diverse and interpretable outputs.

**Strengths**

1.  **Practical Significance and Efficiency:** The primary strength of this paper is its practical contribution. It takes the compelling but computationally prohibitive MCL framework and makes it feasible for modern deep learning architectures. The original MCL requires multiple rounds of training models to convergence, which is intractable for networks that take weeks to train. By reformulating the optimization for SGD and enabling concurrent training, sMCL provides a simple and efficient method to achieve a similar goal. The reported 5x speedup over a 5-iteration MCL baseline is a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Generality and Simplicity:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is impressively general. It is agnostic to the network architecture, the specific loss function, and is parameter-free. The authors convincingly demonstrate this generality by applying the exact same algorithm to vastly different domains (classification, segmentation, captioning) and observing meaningful, task-specific specializations emerge automatically. This makes the method a broadly applicable tool for the community.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper is supported by a thorough set of experiments across three challenging tasks. The results consistently show that sMCL outperforms relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles, the sequential method of Dey et al. [5], and the original MCL. The qualitative results are particularly compelling, clearly illustrating the diversity of predictions and the emergence of interpretable "experts" within the ensemble.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed method is explained concisely, and the connection to prior work is well-established.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The main weakness is the limited conceptual novelty of the core algorithm. The objective function is identical to that proposed in [8]. The contribution is the insight that this objective can be optimized in a stochastic, mini-batch setting by selectively backpropagating gradients. While this adaptation is highly effective and of great practical importance, the underlying algorithmic idea is an adaptation rather than a completely new formulation.

2.  **Analysis of Diversity:** The paper claims that sMCL produces diverse outputs, and the qualitative examples support this. However, the quantitative analysis of diversity could be stronger. The primary evaluation is based on oracle metrics (e.g., oracle accuracy, oracle IoU), which measure the quality of the *best* prediction in the set, not the diversity of the set itself. While some analysis of n-gram uniqueness is provided for captioning, adding more direct diversity metrics would strengthen the claims. For instance, the authors could report the average pairwise dissimilarity between the M predictions for a given input (e.g., JS-divergence for classification, 1-IoU for segmentation).

3.  **Missing Comparison:** The original MCL algorithm [8] uses batch optimization. A natural question is how a mini-batch version of MCL would perform, where assignments are updated after each epoch rather than after full convergence. Comparing sMCL to such a baseline would help disentangle the benefits of concurrent training from the effects of more frequent data re-assignment.

**Recommendation**

I am in favor of accepting this paper. While the core algorithmic idea is an incremental step beyond Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], the practical impact of this step is significant. The authors have successfully bridged the gap between the MCL framework and the practicalities of training large-scale deep networks. The proposed sMCL method is simple, general, and demonstrably effective across multiple domains. The resulting ability to train ensembles that produce diverse, high-quality hypotheses is a valuable contribution to the field. The paper is well-executed and clearly presented.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   I encourage the authors to acknowledge the incrementality of the algorithmic contribution more directly while emphasizing that the main contribution is making the overall approach practical and scalable for deep learning.
*   To strengthen the claims about diversity, consider adding quantitative diversity metrics to the experimental evaluation, as suggested in the "Weaknesses" section.
*   If computationally feasible, a comparison to a mini-batch version of the original MCL algorithm would be a valuable addition to better situate the contribution of sMCL.
