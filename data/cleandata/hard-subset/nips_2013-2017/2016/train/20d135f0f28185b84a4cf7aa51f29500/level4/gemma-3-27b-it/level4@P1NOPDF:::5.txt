The authors propose a method for generating multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks, minimizing the oracle loss—a particularly useful approach when interacting with users. Experiments on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of their method. This paper integrates deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm and introduces a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to minimize the MCL oracle loss. A key benefit of this optimization method is its ease of implementation within a standard back-propagation learning framework, allowing for end-to-end training of the entire model.

My primary concern relates to the sensitivity of the method to initialization. How important is pre-training? What performance would be observed if the models were initialized with random values, rather than a pre-trained network? The paper lacks detail regarding the experimental setup for comparison with existing methods. Specifically, do the baseline methods also utilize the same pre-trained network as sMCL for initialization? 

The authors evaluate their method using oracle accuracy as a primary metric. However, I believe it’s crucial to also consider the diversity of the ensemble members. I would expect to see further analysis quantifying and evaluating this diversity.

Finally, I would like to bring to the authors’ attention a related work with a similar core idea that they should consider comparing against: Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks,” CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
