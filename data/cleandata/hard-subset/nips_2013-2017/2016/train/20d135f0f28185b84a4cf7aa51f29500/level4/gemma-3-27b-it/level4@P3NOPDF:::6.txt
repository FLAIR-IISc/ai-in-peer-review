## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title – *Please Replace with Actual Title*]

This paper introduces a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep learning models. The key innovation lies in its selective activation strategy: unlike traditional ensembles, only a single member is activated and trained on each individual training example, specifically the member achieving the lowest loss for that example. This approach is surprisingly effective, yielding strong experimental results and addressing limitations present in existing ensemble methods. Overall, the idea is simple yet impactful, and I recommend weak acceptance.

However, several aspects of the paper require clarification and further discussion.

**1. Testing Procedure:** The description of the testing phase is ambiguous. The paper hints at utilizing ground truth labels to select the best-performing member (as noted in line 159, page 5), but a clear and explicit explanation of the inference process is missing. It is crucial to detail how predictions are generated during testing – is ground truth available at test time? If not, how is the optimal member selected for each test instance?

**2. Baseline Definition – “Independent Ensembles”:** The authors repeatedly contrast their method against “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (denoted as ‘Indp.’ in figures and line 181, page 5).  The paper lacks a clear definition of these baselines.  What constitutes an “independent ensemble” in this context? What differentiates it from a “regular ensemble”? Providing a precise definition is necessary for a fair comparison and understanding of the contributions.

**3. Justification of Specialization:** A central claim of the paper is that the proposed method induces specialization among ensemble members, with different members becoming proficient at classifying different output classes. However, the training process involves reassignment of members on a per-example basis. While this *can* lead to specialization, the current justification feels insufficient.  The link between per-example reassignment and sustained specialization across the entire dataset is not convincingly established. The authors might consider exploring whether the observed specialization is statistically significant or simply a byproduct of the training dynamics. Furthermore, could specialization be *explicitly* encouraged through modifications to the loss function? For instance, incorporating a term that penalizes overlap in the predictions of different members could promote greater diversity and specialization.

**4. Related Work – Diversity Regularization:** The paper omits discussion of a highly relevant area of research: diversity regularization techniques in neural networks. Works such as Chen et al. [ *Please add citation here* ] employ regularization strategies to explicitly encourage diversity among network parameters or outputs. While these techniques haven’t been extensively studied within a deep ensemble framework, they represent a more rigorous approach to achieving diversity than the proposed per-example selection. Addressing this related work would strengthen the paper’s contribution and contextualize its novelty. Discussing the potential benefits and drawbacks of their approach compared to diversity regularization would be particularly valuable.



**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The selective activation training scheme is a unique approach to ensemble learning.
* **Simplicity:** The algorithm is conceptually simple and easy to implement.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, outperforming existing approaches on the tested datasets.
* **Addresses Deficiencies:** The paper clearly articulates how the proposed method addresses limitations of traditional ensembles. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Ambiguous Testing Procedure:** The testing phase is not clearly explained.
* **Undefined Baselines:** The definitions of “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” are missing.
* **Weak Justification for Specialization:** The argument for specialization is not fully supported by the evidence presented.
* **Missing Related Work:** A relevant body of work on diversity regularization is not discussed.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to deep ensemble learning. The simplicity and effectiveness of the method are commendable. However, addressing the concerns outlined above – particularly regarding the testing procedure, baseline definitions, justification of specialization, and related work – would significantly strengthen the paper and improve its clarity.  Given the current state, I recommend weak acceptance, contingent on the authors providing clarifications and revisions.
