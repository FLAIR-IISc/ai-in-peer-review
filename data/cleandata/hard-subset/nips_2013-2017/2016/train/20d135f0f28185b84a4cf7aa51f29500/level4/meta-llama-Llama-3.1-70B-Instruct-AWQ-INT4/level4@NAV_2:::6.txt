The paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach for training diverse deep ensembles, referred to as Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The method is designed to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle, which is beneficial in practical perception systems that require multiple highly likely hypotheses rather than a single prediction. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and its relation to previous work.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. The authors provide sufficient background information and references to related work, making it easy to follow the narrative. The technical content is sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on various tasks and deep architectures.

The strengths of the paper include the simplicity and generality of the proposed approach, which is agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

One of the weaknesses of the paper is the lack of clarity in explaining the testing phase. The authors mention using the ground truth to find the best member, but this is not explicitly stated in the testing phase description. Additionally, the authors refer to 'independent ensembles' and 'regular ensembles' as baselines, but the explanation for these terms is not provided.

The authors' argument about the specialization of ensemble members towards different output classes is not entirely convincing, as the reassignment in training is on a per-example basis. It would be beneficial to incorporate specialization in the loss function to strengthen this argument. Furthermore, the authors do not discuss the related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, which is a more rigorous technique that has yet to be studied in the deep setting.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on various tasks and deep architectures, which is a notable achievement. However, the idea of using a 'winner-take-gradient' training strategy is not entirely new, and the authors could have provided more context on how their approach differs from previous work.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the performance of deep ensembles in various applications, particularly in situations where multiple highly likely hypotheses are required. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL, which makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

Overall, I recommend the paper for weak acceptance. While it has some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper, including the simplicity and generality of the proposed approach, the thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors, and the demonstration of the effectiveness of sMCL on various tasks and deep architectures, make it a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:
