The paper introduces a method for producing multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize oracle loss, a setting particularly relevant when predictions are consumed by downstream systems or human users. The authors extend the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework to deep architectures and propose a stochastic block gradient descent strategy to directly optimize oracle loss across ensemble members. The approach is straightforward to implement within standard backpropagation, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and supports end-to-end training. Empirical results on three tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—demonstrate strong oracle performance, indicating practical effectiveness.

I have several concerns and questions:
- How sensitive is the method to initialization? What happens when models are initialized from scratch rather than from pretrained weights?
- The comparison setup needs more detail. Were all baselines initialized identically (e.g., using the same pretrained model) to ensure fairness with sMCL?
- Evaluation focuses on oracle accuracy. What about measuring diversity among ensemble members? More analysis of diversity would be helpful.
- There is closely related prior work that should be compared against: Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks,” CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
