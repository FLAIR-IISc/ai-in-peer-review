This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The objective is to minimize the "oracle loss," which is the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble for any given input. The proposed sMCL approach adapts the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8] for models trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). During each training step, an input is processed by all ensemble members, but the loss is backpropagated only to the member that produced the lowest error—a "winner-take-gradient" strategy. This allows for the concurrent training of all models, thereby avoiding the costly iterative retraining required by the original MCL algorithm. The authors demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of their method across three distinct tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO. The results show that sMCL encourages interpretable specialization among the ensemble members.

### Strengths

*   **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple, diverse hypotheses for perception tasks where ambiguity is inherent. Optimizing directly for oracle loss is a compelling approach, as it can lead to more useful systems that interact with downstream processes or human users.
*   **Method Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple to implement, intuitive, and broadly applicable. It is agnostic to the specific network architecture and loss function and does not introduce new hyperparameters. Its primary advantage is efficiency; by training all ensemble members in parallel, it offers a practical solution for modern deep learning architectures compared to the sequential or iterative nature of previous methods [5, 8].
*   **Breadth of Experiments:** The authors validate their approach on a wide range of tasks and model architectures (CNNs for classification, FCNs for segmentation, and CNN+RNNs for captioning). This effectively demonstrates the generality of the framework.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative results and the analysis of emergent specialization (e.g., class-specialization in classification, n-gram specialization in captioning) are insightful and provide a clear understanding of how and why the method works.

### Weaknesses

*   **Originality:** A primary concern is that the novelty of the work appears incremental. The core concept of minimizing oracle loss through partitioned training is inherited directly from the MCL framework [8]. The main contribution is the adaptation of this idea to an SGD setting by using a hard assignment for gradient updates within each mini-batch. While this is a useful engineering contribution that makes MCL practical for deep networks, the conceptual leap from the original work is modest.
*   **Clarity of Technical Contribution:** The technical description in Section 3 is quite brief. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how sMCL relates to other optimization techniques. For example, the authors should clarify the distinction between their "winner-take-gradient" approach and other alternating or partitioned training schemes that are sometimes employed in multi-task or competitive learning settings.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** Although the experiments cover multiple domains, they could be more convincing. The baselines used for comparison are somewhat limited. To better establish the significance of the results, particularly for a competitive conference like NIPS, the authors should compare against stronger, state-of-the-art methods for generating diverse outputs where available. For the semantic segmentation task, for example, evaluating on the standard PASCAL VOC 2012 benchmark instead of VOC 2011 and comparing against more contemporary baselines would provide a stronger case for the method's effectiveness.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a simple and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles. The approach is practical, general, and supported by experiments across several domains. However, the contribution is arguably more of an engineering improvement over existing work [8] than a fundamental new concept. The limited novelty, combined with experimental evaluations that could be more rigorous, tempers the overall impact of the paper. Clarifying the method's positioning with respect to related optimization techniques and strengthening the empirical results on standard benchmarks would significantly improve the submission.
