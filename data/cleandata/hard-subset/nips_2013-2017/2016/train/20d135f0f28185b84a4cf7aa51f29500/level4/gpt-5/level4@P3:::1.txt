Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the problem of producing multiple plausible outputs by training an ensemble to minimize oracle loss—the loss of the best member prediction per example. Building on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS’12; AISTATS’13], which optimizes a hard assignment of training examples to the current best predictor via alternating block coordinate descent, the authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). sMCL implements the same objective in a stochastic setting by a “winner-take-gradient” rule: for each training example in a mini-batch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss backpropagates gradients. This makes the MCL paradigm practical for deep networks, avoiding repeated retraining and enabling concurrent training of all members with standard SGD.

The authors evaluate sMCL across three representative domains and architectures: CIFAR-10 classification with a small CNN, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning with VGG16+LSTM. sMCL consistently improves oracle metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy, IoU, CIDEr-D) over independent ensembles, the boosting-style approach of Dey et al., and MCL trained for a limited number of meta-iterations, while being roughly 5x faster than MCL due to avoiding repeated convergence cycles. Qualitatively, the method induces interpretable specialization: per-class experts in classification, shape/frequency biases in segmentation, and diverse n-gram “styles” in captioning. The work is related to ensemble diversity methods (e.g., bagging, negative correlation, decorrelation), M-Best/diverse inference for structured models, and recent expert architectures, but is distinguished by a simple, parameter-free SGD-compatible formulation that directly minimizes oracle loss for deep nets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The connection from the MCL upper-bound with indicator variables to a stochastic approximation is clear. The per-example winner-take-gradient update is a straightforward and correct subgradient step on the block objective (with ties handled arbitrarily).
- Empirical support: Experiments span three tasks and show consistent gains in oracle metrics, with typical improvements of 6–10% over classical ensembles and a substantial speedup over MCL. The analyses of specialization (e.g., class-wise assignments, top-k winner variants) strengthen the claims.
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: The independent ensemble and MCL baselines are appropriate. The Dey et al. sequential boosting baseline is included and saturates as expected in larger ensembles. One concern is that MCL is run for five meta-iterations; while this is a reasonable computational compromise, a stronger “online/mini-batch MCL” variant (alternating assignments within an epoch without full retraining) would be a useful comparison to more directly isolate the benefit of stochastic updates versus full retraining. For captioning, the beam-search baseline from a single model achieves higher oracle in one setting; it would be informative to combine beam search with sMCL to show complementarity under equal inference budgets.
- Reproducibility: Key training details are provided for each domain. It would help to document tie-breaking, batch sizes’ effect on specialization, and whether any mechanisms are needed to prevent member starvation early in training.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem formulation, relation to MCL, and the sMCL algorithm are concise and easy to follow. Figures illustrating specialization and diversity are compelling. A few clarifications would further improve clarity:
- Explicit pseudocode for tie handling and for the k-winner generalization.
- Discussion of computational/memory cost when sharing versus not sharing early layers (as in captioning).
- A brief note on stability: do assignments oscillate heavily early on, and does performance depend on initialization?

Originality
Conceptually, the objective and learning setting come from MCL. The main novelty is the stochastic, concurrent SGD formulation that makes MCL practical for deep networks without retraining. While incremental in theory, this is a meaningful and timely contribution given the high training cost of modern architectures and the growing importance of multiple-hypothesis outputs. The empirical finding that specialization emerges reliably across tasks is also of interest.

Significance
Minimizing oracle loss is directly relevant to real systems that will rerank or verify multiple hypotheses. sMCL’s simplicity, generality (architecture- and loss-agnostic), and speed make it likely to be adopted. The demonstrated improvements on classification, segmentation, and captioning, coupled with interpretable emergent expertise, suggest the method could become a standard tool for training diverse deep ensembles.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a mini-batch/online MCL baseline (assign with current models each batch or epoch, but train all members jointly) to separate the effect of stochasticity from the cost of retraining.
- Report additional diversity metrics alongside oracle scores: pairwise disagreement on labels/IoU, entropy of per-class assignments, and coverage of unique n-grams; the captioning section includes n-gram counts—similar measures in classification/segmentation would be informative.
- Explore soft assignments (e.g., softmin/temperature or top-k with weights) as a continuous relaxation; the k-winner ablation is a good start—adding a temperature sweep could reveal accuracy–diversity trade-offs.
- Analyze sensitivity to initialization, batch size, and ensemble size M, including potential member starvation and mitigation (e.g., occasional exploration updates).
- For captioning, compare sMCL+beam search under equalized inference budgets and report diversity-aware metrics; also consider oracle BLEU/METEOR for completeness.

Pros
- Simple, general, and practical SGD-based algorithm for oracle-loss minimization in deep ensembles.
- Consistent gains in oracle metrics across diverse tasks and architectures; 5x faster than MCL.
- Clear analyses and qualitative demonstrations of emergent specialization and diversity.
- Parameter-free and easy to integrate into existing training pipelines.

Cons
- Theoretical novelty is incremental relative to MCL; primarily a practical stochastic reformulation.
- MCL baseline trained for limited meta-iterations; no online/mini-batch MCL comparison.
- Limited analysis of stability, initialization sensitivity, and explicit diversity metrics beyond oracle.
- Captioning comparison shows a single-model beam search can match/surpass oracle in one setting; combined baselines would strengthen the case.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clear, impactful, and well-validated method that makes Multiple Choice Learning practical for deep networks and demonstrates tangible benefits across multiple domains. With minor additions to baselines and diversity analysis, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
