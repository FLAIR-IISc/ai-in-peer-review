The authors propose a method to generate multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks that minimize the oracle loss, which is useful when interacting with users. The oracle experiments on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. This paper integrates deep networks into the Multi-Class Learning (MCL) paradigm and proposes a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to minimize the oracle loss of MCL. The proposed optimization method can be easily implemented within the back-propagation learning framework, and the whole model can be trained in an end-to-end fashion.

However, I have several concerns regarding the proposed method. Firstly, the importance of initialization is not thoroughly discussed. The authors use a pre-trained network for initialization, but it is unclear how the method would perform if the models were initialized with random values instead. A comparison of the proposed method's performance under different initialization settings would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the authors do not provide sufficient details about the settings used for comparison with baseline methods. Specifically, it is unclear whether the same pre-trained network was used for initialization in both the proposed method (sMCL) and the baseline methods. Clarifying this point would help to ensure a fair comparison.

In terms of evaluation metrics, the authors primarily focus on oracle accuracy. While this metric is useful, it does not provide insight into the diversity of the ensemble members. I expect to see more analysis on the diversity of the generated outputs, as this is an essential aspect of the proposed method.

Additionally, the authors may want to consider comparing their work with a similar idea presented in "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks" by Stefan Lee et al. (CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015). This comparison would help to contextualize the proposed method within the broader research landscape.

Overall, the proposed method shows promise, but addressing these concerns would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the approach.
