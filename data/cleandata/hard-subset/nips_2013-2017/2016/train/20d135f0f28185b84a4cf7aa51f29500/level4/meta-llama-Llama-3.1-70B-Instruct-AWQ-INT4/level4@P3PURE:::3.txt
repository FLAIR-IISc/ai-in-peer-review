This paper proposes a simple solution to the multiple choice learning problem where the learners are deep networks. Current solutions to MCL do not directly extend to deep networks, making this a valuable contribution. The authors demonstrate that by introducing a winner-takes-the-gradient layer in back propagation, they can effectively combine the training of the learners with the assignment problem in an ensemble setting.

The experimental results show that this method performs well across a wide range of problems, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning. The strengths of this paper include its relevance to the NIPS community, as the problem of MCL for deep learning is of significant importance. The proposed method is also commendable for its simplicity. Furthermore, the experiments provide results on three different tasks, showcasing the method's versatility.

However, there are some weaknesses that detract from the overall impact of the paper. A major concern is that the paper extensively motivates the concept of "diversity" (even incorporating it into the title), but fails to explicitly enforce diversity in the model. This omission is disappointing, as one would expect to see a clear mechanism for promoting diversity in the proposed solution. Additionally, the solution can be seen as an incremental step, building upon the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

In terms of minor suggestions, the first sentence of the abstract could be rephrased for improved clarity. Furthermore, the emphasis on diversity should be toned down, given its absence in the actual model. There are also a few typographical errors that need to be corrected, including changing the first "f" to "g" in line 108 ("we fixed the form of ..") and removing the extra period in the middle of a sentence in line 115.

One question remains regarding the baseline MCL with deep learning: how did the authors ensure that each of the networks converged to reasonable results? Prematurely cutting off the learners could significantly impact the ensemble performance, and it is unclear whether the authors took any measures to mitigate this issue.
