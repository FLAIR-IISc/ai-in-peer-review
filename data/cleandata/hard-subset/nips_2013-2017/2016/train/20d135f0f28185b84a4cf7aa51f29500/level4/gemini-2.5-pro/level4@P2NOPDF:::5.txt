This paper presents a method for training an ensemble of deep networks by directly minimizing the oracle loss. The goal is to generate a diverse set of high-quality outputs, a scenario particularly relevant for user-interactive applications. The core contribution is the integration of deep networks into the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework, along with a novel stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to train the resulting model (sMCL). A key advantage of this approach is its straightforward implementation within standard back-propagation frameworks, allowing for end-to-end training. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method through oracle experiments on three different tasks.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses the important problem of generating multiple, diverse outputs from a single model, which has practical applications in areas requiring interaction with a human user.
*   The integration of the MCL paradigm with deep networks is a sound and interesting contribution.
*   The proposed stochastic block gradient descent algorithm is well-motivated and practical, as it integrates seamlessly into existing end-to-end deep learning pipelines.
*   The experimental results successfully demonstrate the method's ability to improve oracle accuracy compared to baselines.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Importance of Initialization:** The role of initialization seems critical but is not fully explored. The experiments rely on pre-trained networks, which may provide a very strong starting point. It would strengthen the paper to include an ablation study on the importance of this pre-training. For instance, how does the method perform when the ensemble members are initialized randomly? This analysis would help disentangle the performance gains attributable to the sMCL objective from those stemming from the strong initialization.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Comparison:** The experimental setup for the baseline methods lacks detail, particularly regarding the initialization protocol. For a fair comparison, it is essential to confirm whether the baseline models were also initialized with the same pre-trained network used for sMCL. The authors should clarify this point to ensure that the reported improvements are not an artifact of different initialization strategies.

3.  **Evaluation of Diversity:** The evaluation focuses solely on oracle accuracy. While this metric is appropriate for the stated goal, it does not provide insight into the diversity of the generated outputs. Since a primary motivation for ensemble methods is output diversity, the paper would be significantly improved by including a quantitative analysis of this aspect. Metrics that measure the disagreement or distance between the predictions of different models in the ensemble would offer a more complete picture of the method's behavior and better support the claims of generating varied outputs.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper appears to be missing a discussion of highly relevant work by Lee et al. ("Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks," CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015), which explores a very similar idea. The authors should discuss the relationship between their method and Lee et al.'s, highlighting the key similarities and differences in methodology and goals. A direct empirical comparison would be ideal, but at a minimum, a thorough discussion is necessary to properly position this work within the existing literature.
