Summary
This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based method for training deep ensembles to minimize an oracle loss, i.e., the loss of the best prediction among M outputs per input. The key idea is a winner-take-gradient update: for each training example in a minibatch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives the gradient. This provides a practical alternative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches that require repeated retraining or sequential boosting-like procedures, which are infeasible for modern deep networks. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all members concurrently.

The paper positions sMCL relative to ensemble learning and multi-hypothesis generation. It relates to Guzman-Rivera et al.’s MCL (alternating block coordinate descent) and to structured-output methods for generating diverse M-best solutions, while arguing that sMCL scales to deep models. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MS-COCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM) show consistent oracle metric gains over independent ensembles, a boosting-like approach (Dey et al.), and MCL (with significant speedups). Qualitative analyses suggest interpretable specialization of ensemble members.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm: winner-take-gradient is easy to implement and plug into existing deep learning pipelines; no extra hyperparameters.
- Strong cross-domain evidence: improvements on classification (top-k), segmentation (oracle IoU), and captioning (oracle CIDEr) across different architectures, including joint CNN+RNN settings.
- Efficiency: substantial wall-clock speedup vs. alternating retraining methods (reported ~5x).
- Emergent specialization/diversity: empirical evidence that members specialize along task-relevant dimensions, with interpretable outputs and improved coverage of ambiguous cases.

Weaknesses and questions
- Test-time protocol clarity: The paper should more clearly state that evaluation uses oracle metrics computed with ground truth (i.e., not that ground truth is used to select a single model at deployment). Please explicitly describe what the system outputs at test time (M hypotheses) and how oracle metrics are computed, and discuss operational scenarios (e.g., downstream re-ranking or human-in-the-loop).
- Baseline definition and terminology: The term “independent” or “regular” ensembles (“Indp.” in figures) should be defined clearly and consistently in the main text when first introduced. As I understand it, these are independently initialized models trained under standard single-model loss with no interaction; please confirm and state training details (data, epochs, LR schedules) to ensure fairness.
- Specialization claims: While the per-example reassignment naturally induces specialization, the paper should quantify specialization/diversity beyond qualitative plots. Suggestions:
  - Report mutual information between the oracle-selected member and ground-truth class, and per-member class histograms across multiple random seeds.
  - Track the fraction of training data assigned to each member over epochs (to detect collapse/imbalance).
  - Provide ablations (e.g., varying k in the “top-k winners” variant) with quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise disagreement, n-gram diversity, segment shape statistics).
  - Consider a simple balanced-assignment or entropy regularizer (even if only as a discussion) to formalize specialization.
- Related work coverage: Please expand the discussion of diversity-inducing methods in neural ensembles. In addition to negative correlation learning (Liu & Yao), consider diversity regularization via orthogonality/cosine penalties on logits or features, determinantal point process (DPP)-based diversity, and mixture-of-experts/gating models (e.g., hard EM-like assignments). Also discuss Monte Carlo dropout and test-time stochasticity as alternative ways to produce multiple hypotheses.
- Convergence and stability: Winner-take-gradient resembles hard-EM and could risk underutilized members. Provide evidence across random initializations (mean/variance of oracle metrics), tie-breaking policy details, and any annealing/tempering tried to stabilize assignments.
- Comparisons for sequence models: For captioning, beam search is a strong competing way to produce multiple hypotheses. You compare to a single-model beam; it would be helpful to combine sMCL with beam search (e.g., per-member small beams) to show additivity or to report compute-matched comparisons. Also report uniqueness/diversity metrics alongside oracle score for both settings.
- Reproducibility details: Please include hardware, training time, and memory footprint; specify overhead of computing losses from all M members per batch; and clarify the shared-CNN “tree-like” setup for captioning.

Quality
- Soundness: The gradient derivation from the MCL upper bound is correct and leads to a straightforward SGD-compatible algorithm. The empirical results are solid and cover multiple domains. More analysis of stability and diversity metrics would strengthen the claims.
- Completeness: Experiments are broad but could use additional baselines (MC dropout; mixture-of-experts with learned gating; diversity-regularized ensembles) or at least a discussion of why they are out of scope in this work.
- Evaluation: Oracle metrics are appropriate for multi-hypothesis settings, but adding a simple downstream selector (e.g., a learned re-ranker using image-text matching for captions) would better ground the practical value.

Clarity
- Overall well written. Please:
  - Define “oracle” and “oracle metrics” early and state the test-time protocol explicitly.
  - Unify baseline terminology and clearly describe training procedures and hyperparameters for all methods.
  - Add concise pseudo-code for sMCL (forward pass computing losses for all M; backward pass to the argmin per example; tie-breaking policy).

Originality
- The main novelty is adapting MCL to a stochastic, parallel, deep-learning-friendly procedure. While conceptually related to hard-EM and mixture-of-experts, the specific instantiation for deep ensembles is useful and, to my knowledge, new at the time.

Significance
- The method is simple, broadly applicable, and effective across tasks, and it lowers the barrier to training diverse deep ensembles optimized for oracle selection. This is likely to influence practice in settings where presenting multiple hypotheses is valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, simple, and general algorithm with clear speed and performance gains.
- Strong, multi-domain empirical results and qualitative insights.
- Addresses a real need: generating diverse, high-quality hypotheses for downstream selection.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative analysis of diversity/specialization and stability across runs.
- Some related work and alternative baselines (e.g., MC dropout, gating MoE, diversity regularizers) are under-discussed.
- Test-time protocol and baseline definitions should be clarified.

Recommendation
- Weak accept.

Requested clarifications and suggestions
1) Explicitly describe test-time behavior and oracle metric computation; add a brief discussion of deployment (with or without a selector).  
2) Define “Indp.”/“regular” ensembles precisely and ensure fair, matched training details.  
3) Quantify specialization/diversity and show stability across seeds; report per-member data shares.  
4) Expand related work to include diversity regularization, mixture-of-experts with gating, and MC dropout; discuss differences and trade-offs.  
5) For captioning, include compute-matched comparisons with beam search and, if possible, combine sMCL with beam search.  
6) Provide training time, hardware, and tie-breaking details; consider releasing code.
