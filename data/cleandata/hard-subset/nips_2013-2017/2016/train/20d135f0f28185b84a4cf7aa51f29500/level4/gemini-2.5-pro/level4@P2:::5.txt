### **Summary**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training procedure for deep network ensembles designed to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input. The motivation is to better handle ambiguity in perception tasks where multiple plausible interpretations exist, making the system more useful for downstream processes involving a human user or another validation component (an "oracle").

The core idea is to adapt the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework, which aims to minimize the "oracle loss" (the loss of the best prediction in the set), to the context of deep learning. The main technical contribution is a simple and efficient stochastic gradient descent (SGD) based optimization strategy. During training, for each example in a mini-batch, the forward pass is computed for all ensemble members. However, during the backward pass, the gradient is backpropagated *only* to the single ensemble member that produced the lowest loss for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" approach encourages different network "experts" to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). In all cases, sMCL ensembles achieve superior oracle performance compared to baselines such as independently trained ensembles and previous, computationally expensive MCL methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple, diverse hypotheses from a single system. Standard models trained to minimize expected loss often produce "safe" or averaged-out predictions that are not useful in ambiguous scenarios. Optimizing for oracle loss is a well-principled way to address this.

2.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is an elegant and clever adaptation of the block coordinate descent approach of MCL to the SGD setting. The "winner-take-gradient" mechanism is simple to implement, parameter-free, and can be integrated into existing deep learning frameworks with minimal effort. This represents a significant practical improvement over prior methods that required costly sequential training or retraining of models to convergence.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Strong Results:** The method's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated across three very different tasks and architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). The consistent improvement in oracle metrics over several baselines, including independently trained ensembles and the original MCL, is a strong result. The qualitative examples, particularly for segmentation and captioning, compellingly illustrate that the method produces diverse and meaningful outputs.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Clarity on Experimental Setup and Initialization:** The paper could be clearer about the experimental details, particularly concerning model initialization.
    *   For the semantic segmentation experiment (Section 4.2), the models are initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. This raises the question of how crucial this initialization is. Would sMCL be as effective if trained from random initialization?
    *   For fairness, it is important to clarify whether the baseline methods (e.g., standard ensembles, MCL) were also given the same pre-trained initialization. A fair comparison requires all methods to start from the same footing, whether it be random weights or a common pre-trained network.

2.  **Lack of Quantitative Diversity Analysis:** While the stated goal is to produce a *diverse* set of outputs, the evaluation relies exclusively on oracle metrics (e.g., oracle accuracy, oracle IoU). While improved oracle performance implies some level of useful diversity, a more direct, quantitative analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, the authors could report metrics like the average pairwise distance between the predictions of different ensemble members (e.g., Jaccard distance for segmentation masks or BLEU/CIDEr scores for captions). The qualitative examples are insightful, but a quantitative measure would make the diversity argument more rigorous.

3.  **Missing Comparison to a Key Related Work:** The paper is missing a discussion of a highly relevant concurrent work:
    *   Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks," CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).

    This work shares a very similar goal of training a diverse ensemble of deep networks. While their proposed method (which involves a repulsion term in the loss function) is different, the motivation and objective are closely aligned. A discussion of this paper, highlighting the similarities and differences in approach and performance, is essential for positioning the current work within the literature.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a well-written paper that presents a simple, effective, and broadly applicable method for a significant problem. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a solid contribution that makes the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm practical for modern deep networks. The experimental results are strong and compelling. The primary weaknesses are a lack of clarity in the experimental details regarding initialization, an absence of quantitative diversity metrics, and the omission of a key related work. These points should be addressed to further strengthen the paper, but they do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.
