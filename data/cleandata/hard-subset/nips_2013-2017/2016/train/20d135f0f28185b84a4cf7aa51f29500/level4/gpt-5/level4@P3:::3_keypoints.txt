- Paper proposes sMCL, a simple SGD-compatible "winner-take-gradient" scheme for Multiple Choice Learning that updates only the lowest-loss ensemble member per example.  
- sMCL removes costly alternating assignment/retraining of prior MCL and is architecture- and loss-agnostic.  
- The gradient argument for the surrogate objective is straightforward and correct; no convergence proof is provided but the method is consistent with hard-EM/K-means style optimization.  
- Empirical evaluation shows consistent oracle metric improvements on classification (CIFAR-10), segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and captioning (MSCOCO).  
- sMCL induces specialization in ensemble members (different classes/structures and more diverse n-grams).  
- Comparisons include independent ensembles, original MCL (limited meta-iterations), and a boosting-like method; beam search baseline included for captioning.  
- Experimental details are broadly convincing but some specifics (training curves, stopping criteria, tie-breaking) are missing.  
- Fairness concern: MCL baseline ran for five meta-iterations; full convergence/early-stopping effects need clarification.  
- Fairness concern: shared CNN in captioning due to memory constraints may affect comparability across methods.  
- Reproducibility is aided by algorithm simplicity, but more explicit training details and tie-breaking rules are needed.  
- Clarity is generally good; minor typos and presentation issues (abstract phrasing, line typos) should be fixed.  
- Tie-breaking among equal-loss predictors and any anti-starvation mechanisms should be clarified.  
- Originality: contribution is a practical SGD optimization strategy for oracle loss rather than a new objective—incremental but useful.  
- Significance: method is practical, yields ~6–10% oracle gains and ~5x speedup versus MCL, likely to be adopted by practitioners.  
- Strength: parameter-free, easy to implement, broadly applicable across architectures and losses.  
- Strength: extensive empirical validation and qualitative analyses of emergent expertise.  
- Weakness: does not explicitly enforce diversity; specialization is an emergent byproduct and diversity claims should be tempered.  
- Weakness: lacks deeper theoretical treatment (convergence/bounds) to strengthen the contribution.  
- Weakness: possible starvation/imbalance of learners and sensitivity to batch/ensemble size not fully explored.  
- Question: How was convergence verified within each MCL meta-iteration, and could early stopping bias results?  
- Question: Were some learners starved of gradients, and were any mitigations (temperature, k-winners, load balancing) used?  
- Question: How are ties handled in practice and how sensitive are results to tie-breaking?  
- Question: Sensitivity to ensemble size M, batch size, and dataset size—please provide ablations.  
- Question: In captioning, does the shared CNN limit diversity compared to independently fine-tuned CNNs, and are baselines comparable?  
- Question: Does sMCL degrade single-prediction performance or calibration relative to independent ensembles?  
- Suggestion: Temper "diversity" framing or add explicit diversity regularizer/comparison to diversity-enforcing baselines.  
- Suggestion: Expand related work to include diverse M-best and submodular selection approaches.  
- Suggestion: Provide more granular training details, runtime comparisons, and report k-winners variant results.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — practical and effective with broad applicability, but incremental and needing clarification on diversity claims and baseline details.
