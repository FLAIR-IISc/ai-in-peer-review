

The paper introduces sMCL, a method for generating diverse multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize the oracle loss—a compelling approach for interactive user applications. The core contribution lies in integrating deep networks into the Multi-Classifier Learning (MCL) framework and proposing a stochastic block gradient descent optimization technique that minimizes the oracle loss. This method is efficiently implementable within standard backpropagation and enables end-to-end training of the entire model, a significant practical advantage. The experiments across three tasks demonstrate consistent improvements in oracle accuracy over baselines, supporting the method’s efficacy.  

However, three critical concerns require clarification before the paper can be accepted. First, the paper relies on pre-trained networks for initialization but fails to address the impact of random initialization. The results could be highly sensitive to initialization quality, yet the authors do not evaluate performance with randomly initialized models. A comparative analysis (e.g., pre-trained vs. random initialization) is essential to establish whether the method’s success hinges on strong priors or generalizes robustly. Second, the comparison setup lacks transparency. The paper states that sMCL uses pre-trained networks, but it does not specify whether baseline methods (e.g., standard ensemble approaches or prior work) employ the *same* initialization. Without this, the reported gains may reflect initialization differences rather than methodological superiority. The authors should explicitly state baseline initialization protocols and justify their choices.  

Third, the evaluation focuses solely on oracle accuracy, omitting analysis of ensemble diversity—a central premise of the work. For multi-output systems, diversity among ensemble members is as crucial as accuracy; without it, the method risks producing redundant outputs. The authors should include diversity metrics (e.g., average pairwise disagreement, mutual information, or entropy-based measures) across the ensemble members and correlate these with performance gains. This would strengthen the argument for the oracle loss’s role in promoting diversity.  

Additionally, the paper overlooks a closely related 2015 work by Lee et al. ("Why M Heads Are Better than One"), which also explores training diverse deep ensembles via multi-head architectures and optimization techniques targeting diversity. This work directly addresses the same problem space and should be compared against. The authors must discuss how sMCL differs from Lee et al.’s approach (e.g., in optimization, loss formulation, or diversity guarantees) and evaluate against their baselines. Omitting this comparison undermines the novelty claim, as the core idea of leveraging multiple outputs via ensemble optimization is not entirely new.  

The paper’s strengths lie in its practical end-to-end training framework and solid empirical validation of oracle accuracy gains. However, the unresolved concerns about initialization, comparison fairness, and diversity analysis weaken the contribution’s significance. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.  

**Recommendation**: Minor Revision. The authors should:  
1. Conduct experiments with random initialization and report sensitivity analysis.  
2. Clarify baseline initialization protocols and ensure fair comparisons.  
3. Add diversity metrics to the evaluation and discuss their relationship with oracle accuracy.  
4. Compare against Lee et al. (2015) in both methodology and results, explicitly highlighting differences.  

The paper has potential but requires these clarifications to fully justify its claims and position within the literature. With these revisions, it would make a valuable contribution to ensemble learning for interactive applications.
