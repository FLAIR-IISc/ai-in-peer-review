This paper proposes a method, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly minimize the "oracle loss," which is the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble for a given data point. This setting is particularly relevant for applications where a human user or a downstream module can select the correct output from a set of plausible hypotheses. The proposed sMCL algorithm implements this by employing a simple "winner-take-gradient" strategy within a standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) framework: for each example in a mini-batch, the gradient is backpropagated only through the single network in the ensemble that currently yields the lowest loss.

This work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9]. However, the original MCL formulation relied on a block coordinate descent approach that required iteratively training models to convergence, making it infeasible for modern deep networks that are computationally expensive to train. The primary contribution of this paper is the formulation of a stochastic, end-to-end trainable version of MCL that trains all ensemble members concurrently, making it a practical and efficient solution for deep learning.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple, diverse outputs for tasks with inherent ambiguity. Optimizing for oracle performance rather than a single best guess is a valuable framing for many real-world systems. The proposed sMCL method is elegant, simple to implement, parameter-free, and computationally efficient, representing a significant practical improvement over the previous MCL algorithm.

2.  **Generality and Broad Applicability:** A key strength of sMCL is its agnosticism to the network architecture and the specific loss function. The authors convincingly demonstrate this by applying the exact same algorithm to three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification with CNNs, semantic segmentation with FCNs, and image captioning with CNN+RNN models. This breadth of experimentation strongly supports the general applicability of the proposed method.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and demonstrate that sMCL consistently and significantly outperforms several baselines, including independently trained ensembles, the original MCL method (while being 5x faster), and the boosting-style approach of Dey et al. [5], across all three tasks. The qualitative results are also compelling, providing insightful examples of how the ensemble members learn interpretable, task-specific specializations (e.g., specializing on certain object classes or generating syntactically diverse captions).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Missing Comparison to Highly Relevant Work:** The most significant weakness is the lack of discussion and comparison with the contemporaneous work of Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks" (arXiv:1511.06314, 2015). That paper proposes a very similar idea of training a diverse ensemble by encouraging specialization, also using a form of winner-take-all update during training. A thorough comparison, both conceptual and empirical, with this highly related method is essential to properly contextualize the paper's contribution and originality.

2.  **Clarity on Experimental Setup and Initialization:** The paper could be improved by providing more details on the experimental setup, particularly regarding model initialization. For the semantic segmentation experiments, the authors state that the sMCL model is initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. This raises several questions:
    *   Is this pre-training step necessary for sMCL to perform well, or can it be trained effectively from a random initialization? An ablation study on this point would be valuable.
    *   For a fair comparison, were the baseline methods (MCL, Dey et al.) also initialized using the same pre-trained network? If not, the performance gains of sMCL might be partially attributed to a more favorable initialization strategy. Clarifying these details is crucial for assessing the quality and reproducibility of the results.

3.  **Quantitative Analysis of Diversity:** While the qualitative examples of specialization are illustrative, the evaluation of diversity could be strengthened with quantitative metrics. The paper focuses on oracle performance, which demonstrates that the ensemble *collectively* performs well. However, it would be beneficial to also include metrics that directly measure the diversity of the predictions, such as the average pairwise disagreement between ensemble members or the variation in predicted outputs. This would provide a more complete picture of how sMCL achieves its strong oracle performance and how it compares to baselines in generating truly distinct solutions.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a simple, effective, and broadly applicable method for a significant problem. The strengths—in particular, the method's simplicity, generality, and strong empirical performance across multiple domains—are substantial. The main weakness is the omission of a key related work by Lee et al. (2015), which should be addressed. Minor weaknesses include a lack of clarity on the initialization protocol for baselines and the absence of quantitative diversity metrics.

Despite these points, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field of ensemble learning for deep networks. I recommend acceptance, but strongly urge the authors to address the points above, especially the comparison to Lee et al., in the final version of the paper.
