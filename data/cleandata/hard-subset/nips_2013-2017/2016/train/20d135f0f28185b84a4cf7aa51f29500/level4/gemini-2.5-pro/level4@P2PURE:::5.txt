This paper proposes a method for generating multiple outputs by integrating deep networks into the Minimum Conditional Loss (MCL) paradigm. The authors develop a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to jointly train an ensemble of deep networks that directly minimizes the oracle loss, an objective well-suited for applications involving user interaction. A significant strength of this approach is that the optimization method integrates seamlessly with standard back-propagation, allowing the entire model to be trained end-to-end. Experiments on three tasks demonstrate the method's effectiveness.

However, the paper could be strengthened in several areas. The importance of network initialization is not adequately addressed. How would performance be affected if the models were initialized randomly instead of with a pre-trained network? Furthermore, the experimental setup for the baseline comparisons lacks sufficient detail. For instance, it is unclear if the baseline methods were initialized using the same pre-trained network as the proposed sMCL method, which is crucial information for assessing the fairness of the comparison.

The evaluation relies on oracle accuracy, but an analysis of the diversity among the ensemble members is notably absent. Including such an analysis would provide a more complete picture of the method's benefits and would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

Finally, the authors should acknowledge and compare their approach with the following highly relevant work on training diverse ensembles:
Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks." CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015).
