

This paper addresses the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep ensembles, where the goal is to produce multiple diverse hypotheses rather than a single prediction. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based method that trains ensemble members concurrently by backpropagating gradients only to the lowest-loss predictor per example. This avoids the costly retraining required by prior MCL approaches [8, 9] and is agnostic to architecture and loss function. The method is evaluated across image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), demonstrating consistent improvements in oracle performance over baselines including independent ensembles, MCL [8], and Dey et al. [5]. The paper shows that sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members without explicit diversity constraints.

**Strengths:**  
- The problem of MCL for deep networks is significant for real-world perception systems requiring multiple hypotheses.  
- The proposed sMCL method is technically sound, simple to implement, and broadly applicable across architectures/tasks.  
- Experiments comprehensively validate the approach on three diverse domains with clear oracle performance gains (6-10% over baselines).  
- The analysis of emergent specialization (e.g., class-specific experts in classification) provides valuable insights into ensemble behavior.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The title and motivation overemphasize "diversity" as a primary contribution, yet the method achieves diversity implicitly via oracle loss minimization rather than through explicit diversity mechanisms (e.g., diversity regularization or constraints). This creates a disconnect between the paper's framing and technical contribution.  
- The approach builds incrementally on Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] (who first formalized MCL) by avoiding their retraining requirement, but does not introduce novel theoretical insights or fundamentally new algorithmic principles.  
- Minor clarity issues: The abstract’s first sentence is vague; the line 108 typo ("f" instead of "g") misstates the ensemble formulation; an extra period appears in line 115.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Strong empirical validation across multiple tasks, practical implementation simplicity, and clear demonstration of oracle loss minimization.  
*Con:* Misalignment between "diversity" claims and method (no explicit diversity mechanism), and incremental nature of the contribution relative to prior MCL work.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a useful engineering contribution for practitioners but offers limited theoretical novelty. While the empirical results are compelling, the overstatement of "diversity" as a core innovation weakens the significance claim. The method is a valuable practical tool, but the contribution falls short of NIPS's threshold for transformative significance. I recommend **weak acceptance** (borderline) given the broad applicability and strong experiments, but the title and discussion should be revised to accurately reflect the implicit diversity mechanism. The baseline MCL implementation details (e.g., convergence checks) should also be clarified.
