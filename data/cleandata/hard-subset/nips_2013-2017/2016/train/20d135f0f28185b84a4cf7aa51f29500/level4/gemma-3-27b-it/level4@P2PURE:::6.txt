This paper introduces an ensemble training algorithm for deep learning models with a novel approach: unlike traditional ensembles, only a single member is active for each training example. During training, the member network with the lowest loss for a given example is identified and updated using gradient descent. Despite its simplicity, the method achieves surprisingly strong empirical results and addresses limitations of existing ensemble techniques, leading me to recommend weak acceptance. (Continuing from the summary) 1. The testing procedure lacks clarity. I infer that ground truth labels are used to select the best-performing member, as suggested in line 159 of page 5. 2. The paper references ‘independent ensembles’ and ‘regular ensembles’ (denoted as ‘Indp.’ in figures and line 181 on page 5) without providing a clear definition or explanation of this baseline. 3. The authors emphasize the `specialization` of ensemble members to different output classes; however, the per-example reassignment strategy during training raises concerns about the validity of this claim. Could specialization be more directly encouraged through a modified loss function? 4. Related to the above point, the authors do not discuss relevant work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), a technique offering a more rigorous, though less explored, approach to achieving diversity in deep learning ensembles.
