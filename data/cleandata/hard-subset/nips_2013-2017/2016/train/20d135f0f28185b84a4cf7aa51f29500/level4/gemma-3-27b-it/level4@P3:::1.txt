This paper addresses the problem of training diverse deep ensembles for scenarios where multiple plausible hypotheses are desired, rather than a single prediction. The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8], framing the task as minimizing the loss with respect to an oracle that selects the best output from an ensemble. Their key contribution is the Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) algorithm, a stochastic gradient descent approach designed to make this training paradigm practical for deep networks – a significant limitation of the original MCL approach which required costly retraining. 

The paper is well-motivated, connecting the problem to real-world applications in computer vision and natural language processing where ambiguity is inherent. It clearly articulates the benefits of diverse solutions, such as capturing implicit class confusion and addressing mode-seeking behaviors in models. The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature on ensemble learning [2, 12, 19, 26, 27] and methods for generating multiple solutions [3, 5, 8, 9, 15, 16, 23, 24, 29]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed sMCL algorithm appears technically sound and provides a practical solution to a previously challenging problem. The derivation in Section 3 is clear and logically presented.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation, problem formulation, and proposed solution are all presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide extensive experimental results across diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) and network architectures. The consistent improvements over baselines demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL.
* **Interpretability:** The analysis of ensemble member specialization – showcasing how different networks learn to focus on specific classes or aspects of the data – is a significant strength. This adds valuable insight beyond purely quantitative results.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm’s parameter-free nature and applicability to any model trained with SGD make it highly practical and broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While the sMCL algorithm is a significant improvement in practicality compared to [8], the core idea of minimizing oracle loss remains largely unchanged. The novelty lies primarily in the adaptation to stochastic gradient descent.  A more thorough comparison to a batched implementation of the original MCL algorithm (as suggested by the authors in the draft) would help to more definitively establish the benefits of sMCL.
* **Diversity Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improved oracle performance, a more in-depth analysis of the *type* of diversity achieved would be beneficial. Quantifying diversity metrics (e.g., measuring the dissimilarity between generated outputs) beyond the specialization analysis presented in the experiments could strengthen the claims. 
* **Limited Comparison to Beam Search:** The comparison to beam search in the image captioning experiments is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive evaluation, potentially varying the beam width more extensively, could better contextualize the benefits of the ensemble approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* sMCL offers a practical and effective solution to a relevant problem in machine learning – training diverse ensembles for scenarios requiring multiple hypotheses.
* The paper demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines across a range of tasks and architectures.
* The interpretability analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the learned ensembles.
* The algorithm is easy to implement and widely applicable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The core idea builds heavily on prior work [8] and the novelty is largely incremental.
* The analysis of diversity could be more thorough and quantitative.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. While the core idea builds on previous work, the sMCL algorithm represents a significant step forward in terms of practicality and enables the application of this approach to modern deep learning models. The thorough experimental evaluation and insightful analysis of ensemble behavior further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with a minor recommendation to consider including a comparison to a batched version of the original MCL algorithm and potentially a more detailed analysis of diversity metrics.
