Summary
This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based method for training ensembles of deep networks to minimize oracle loss—that is, the loss of the best prediction among M outputs. The key idea is a “winner-take-gradient” update: for each training example, only the model with the lowest loss receives gradient updates. This provides a practical, architecture- and loss-agnostic way to optimize the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) objective in deep networks without costly alternating retraining. The authors evaluate sMCL on three tasks—CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation, and MSCOCO image captioning—showing consistent oracle improvements over independent ensembles, MCL (with large speedups), and the boosting-like method of Dey et al. The paper also reports qualitative evidence of emergent specialization across ensemble members (e.g., class specialization in classification, structured diversity in segmentation and captioning).

Relation to prior work
This work builds directly on MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.), which minimizes oracle loss by alternating assignments and full retraining. sMCL replaces that procedure with a stochastic block gradient descent formulation suited to deep networks. It is also related to work on producing multiple diverse outputs in structured prediction (e.g., M-Best/diverse methods), which typically target probabilistic graphical models rather than general deep architectures. Compared with sequential/boosting-style approaches (Dey et al.), sMCL trains all members concurrently. The paper should also explicitly compare or clarify its relationship to: Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, Dhruv Batra, “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks,” CoRR abs/1511.06314 (2015), which appears very similar in spirit.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and practical algorithm: easy to implement within standard backprop and applicable to a wide range of deep architectures and loss functions.
- Concurrent training of ensemble members avoids the prohibitive cost of repeated retraining in prior MCL approaches, yielding substantial speedups.
- Broad empirical evaluation across three tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) with consistent oracle improvements over strong baselines; qualitative analyses suggest interpretable specialization emerges naturally.
- Parameter-free design and minimal engineering overhead increase the method’s appeal and potential impact.

Weaknesses and questions
- Initialization sensitivity: How important is initialization? What happens if models are initialized randomly rather than from a pre-trained network? This is especially relevant for captioning (where VGG16 pretraining is used) and segmentation (where sMCL is fine-tuned from a standard ensemble). Please report sensitivity to random initialization and any convergence/quality differences.
- Baseline fairness and setup clarity: Do the baselines (independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.) use the same pre-trained initializations as sMCL? The paper should clearly state initialization, training schedules, learning rates, and number of epochs/meta-iterations for all baselines to ensure a fair comparison.
- Evaluation focus on oracle metrics: Oracle accuracy/IoU/CIDEr are appropriate for multiple-output settings, but the paper should also analyze diversity explicitly. For example, quantify diversity via disagreement measures, output variance, pairwise distance in feature or label space, n-gram diversity (beyond counts), or coverage metrics, and relate these to oracle gains.
- Relation to prior closely related work: Please compare or clarify how this submission differs from “Why M Heads are Better than One” (Lee et al., 2015). If that is an earlier version of this work, state what is new here (algorithms, theory, experiments, analysis).

Quality
Methodologically, the “winner-take-gradient” formulation is sound and a natural stochastic relaxation of the MCL objective. The approach is well-motivated and aligns with how oracle loss gradients decompose across ensemble members. Empirical results are convincing, covering different architectures and loss functions. However, more detail on experimental setup (especially initializations and training protocols across methods) is needed to assess fairness. Additional diversity quantification would strengthen the claims about specialization and coverage.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The high-level idea and algorithm are clear, and the qualitative analyses are helpful. Clarity would benefit from:
- A precise description of initialization and training schedules for all methods (including how ties are handled, batch sizing, and any regularization).
- A consolidated discussion of when pretraining is used, and how results change without it.
- Explicit diversity metrics and their correlation with oracle improvements.

Originality
Conceptually, sMCL is a straightforward but useful stochastic adaptation of existing MCL ideas to deep networks. The contribution is primarily practical and algorithmic rather than theoretical, but is nonetheless meaningful given the training cost of deep models. The relationship to the Lee et al. 2015 paper should be clarified to establish novelty.

Significance
High. Producing multiple plausible outputs is important in many real-world systems and structured prediction tasks. A simple, general, and efficient method that consistently improves oracle performance across diverse architectures is likely to see adoption and spur further work on multi-hypothesis deep learning and emergent specialization.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general algorithm with strong practical value.
- Concurrent training avoids the prohibitive costs of prior MCL approaches.
- Consistent oracle gains across tasks and architectures.
- Interesting qualitative evidence of emergent expertise.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient detail on initialization and baseline fairness; sensitivity to pretraining not explored.
- Limited quantitative analysis of diversity beyond oracle metrics.
- Unclear relationship to very similar prior work (Lee et al., 2015).

Requested clarifications/revisions
- Report results with random initialization vs. pretraining, and ensure identical initializations for baselines.
- Provide explicit diversity metrics and analyze how diversity relates to oracle gains.
- Clarify the relationship and differences with the 2015 “Why M Heads are Better than One” work.
