The authors propose a method for generating multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks that minimize the oracle loss, which is particularly useful in interactive settings. Experiments on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. This paper integrates deep networks into the Monte Carlo Learning (MCL) paradigm and proposes a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to minimize the oracle loss of MCL. The proposed optimization method can be easily implemented within the back-propagation learning framework, and the entire model can be trained end-to-end.

However, I have concerns regarding the importance of initialization in this approach. How crucial is it to initialize the models with a pre-trained network, and what if they were instead initialized with random values? The authors do not provide sufficient explanation on this point. Furthermore, the settings for comparison with baseline methods are not clearly explained. Do the authors use the same pre-trained network for initialization in both their method and the baseline methods? This information is essential to ensure a fair comparison.

The authors use oracle accuracy as the primary metric for evaluation. While this is a relevant metric, I expect to see more analysis on the diversity of the ensemble members. Evaluating the diversity of the generated outputs is essential to understand the effectiveness of the proposed method in producing a varied set of solutions.

Additionally, I would like to bring to the authors' attention a work with a very similar idea, which they may want to compare with: Stefan Lee, Senthil Purushwalkam, Michael Cogswell, David J. Crandall, and Dhruv Batra, "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks" (CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015). Comparing their method with this existing work would provide a more comprehensive understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.
