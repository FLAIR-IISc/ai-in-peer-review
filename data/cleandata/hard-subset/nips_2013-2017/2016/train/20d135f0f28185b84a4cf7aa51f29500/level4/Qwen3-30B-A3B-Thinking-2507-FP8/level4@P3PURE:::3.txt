

This paper addresses the multiple choice learning (MCL) problem for deep networks, where existing MCL solutions do not directly extend to deep learning architectures. The authors propose a simple modification to backpropagation—adding a winner-takes-the-gradient layer—that integrates the training of individual learners with the assignment problem within an ensemble framework. Experimental results demonstrate strong performance across diverse tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning.  

**Strengths:**  
- The MCL problem for deep learning is sufficiently important to warrant attention within the NIPS community.  
- The proposed method is notably simple and elegant.  
- The experimental validation spans three distinct task domains, strengthening the claim of generality.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The most significant concern is the paper’s extensive emphasis on "diversity" (evident in the title and throughout the text), despite the absence of any explicit mechanism to enforce diversity in the model. The authors’ central claim regarding diversity is undermined by the lack of a diversity-promoting component, rendering the term largely descriptive rather than prescriptive.  
- The solution represents only a minor incremental step beyond the relaxation framework proposed by Guzman et al. (2015), offering limited theoretical or methodological novelty.  

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- The first sentence of the abstract requires revision for clarity and precision.  
- The term "diversity" should be de-emphasized throughout the paper to align with the absence of diversity enforcement.  
- Line 108: Correct "f" to "g" in "we fixed the form of...".  
- Line 115: Remove the extraneous period in the middle of the sentence.  

**Question:**  
For the baseline MCL approach using deep networks, how did the authors ensure that each individual network in the ensemble had converged to a reasonable solution prior to ensemble formation? Early termination of training for individual learners could significantly compromise the overall ensemble performance.
