Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the problem of generating multiple plausible outputs by training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize oracle loss: for each input, only the lowest-loss prediction among M outputs is penalized. The key contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” training scheme that interleaves assignment and optimization within standard SGD. For each mini-batch example, only the current best-performing model receives gradient updates, allowing ensemble members to specialize. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all members jointly.

The work builds on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) by Guzman-Rivera et al., which optimizes an upper bound on oracle loss via alternating assignments and full retraining—impractical for modern deep nets. It is also related to diversity-oriented inference for structured prediction (e.g., DivMBest, diverse M-best in MRFs) and ensemble diversity methods (e.g., data resampling, negative correlation). Unlike those, sMCL integrates diversity into training for general deep architectures and structured outputs. The paper compares to classical independent ensembles, MCL with meta-iterations, and the boosting-style approach of Dey et al.

Strengths:
- Simple and general: sMCL can be dropped into standard backprop pipelines without extra hyperparameters.
- Concurrent, efficient training: avoids expensive alternating retraining in MCL; reported ~5x speedup.
- Broad applicability: demonstrated on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCN), and image captioning (COCO with CNN+LSTM), consistently improving oracle metrics over baselines.
- Emergent specialization: clear qualitative and some quantitative evidence that different members focus on different regions of label/structure space, which is desirable for oracle-based downstream systems.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Initialization and fairness of comparisons: The paper occasionally initializes from pretrained models (e.g., segmentation, captioning). How sensitive is sMCL to initialization? What happens with purely random initializations? Were all baselines—including independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.—initialized identically (same pretrained backbones and random seeds where appropriate)? Please clarify to ensure a fair comparison.
- Diversity measurement: Oracle accuracy is necessary but not sufficient. Please quantify diversity explicitly: e.g., pairwise prediction disagreement, KL/JSD between class distributions, segment IoU disagreement, n-gram diversity beyond counts (self-BLEU), and coverage of rare classes/modes. An analysis of diversity vs oracle performance as M varies would strengthen the claims.
- Baseline scope: In addition to independent ensembles, consider multi-head architectures that share a backbone but branch into M heads trained with sMCL vs standard loss (which your own arXiv preprint “Why M Heads are Better than One,” arXiv:1511.06314, seems to address). Please reconcile this submission with that preprint: what is new here beyond renaming/packaging? If this paper is the archival version, clarify differences, added experiments, or theoretical insights.
- Details of the training procedure: How are ties among equal-loss predictors broken, and does tie-breaking influence stability/diversity? Is there any mechanism (e.g., entropy or load balancing) to prevent collapse where one model dominates early assignments? The k-winner variant is interesting—please report full ablations (k=1..M) across tasks and discuss trade-offs.
- Practicality beyond oracle evaluation: In realistic systems, the downstream oracle may be imperfect. How does sMCL affect calibration and the re-ranking/selection stage? Reporting non-oracle metrics (e.g., single-prediction accuracy/IoU/caption scores for the best member or an average of members) would help assess broader utility.
- Compute accounting: While you report a 5x speedup over MCL (with five meta-iterations), please provide absolute wall-clock times and GPU-hours; also report per-epoch overhead of routing gradients and computing M losses per example. For captioning, sharing a CNN backbone due to memory limits changes the objective; please clarify which results use shared vs independent backbones and discuss the impact on diversity.
- Scaling with M: Provide plots of oracle performance, diversity metrics, and training time as functions of M. Are gains diminishing? Does specialization persist or fragment?

Quality:
Technically sound and well-motivated. The optimization strategy is straightforward and compatible with SGD. However, several experimental details (initialization, tie-breaking, fairness of baselines, compute) and broader analyses (diversity quantification, scaling) should be clarified to fully support the claims.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well-organized. I recommend adding: precise pseudocode with tie-breaking; exact training schedules and data splits; initialization policy across methods; memory/computation details for the captioning setups; and explicit definitions of all oracle metrics used.

Originality:
The core idea—optimizing oracle loss with concurrent SGD updates—is a useful and practical extension of MCL to deep nets. However, its novelty must be positioned relative to the authors’ own earlier preprint (“Why M Heads are Better than One”). Please clearly delineate new contributions.

Significance:
High potential impact. A simple, general recipe for training diverse deep ensembles is valuable across many tasks, and the empirical improvements on three domains suggest broad utility. Stronger diversity analyses and clearer baselines would further enhance significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, broadly applicable method with consistent empirical gains on multiple tasks.
- Practical training procedure that makes oracle-loss optimization feasible for deep nets.
- Emergent, interpretable specialization.

Cons for acceptance:
- Reliance on oracle metrics without robust diversity quantification.
- Incomplete experimental details and fairness checks (initialization, compute, tie-breaking).
- Overlap with prior arXiv work not clearly resolved.

Suggestions:
- Add diversity metrics and ablations (k-winner, scaling with M).
- Clarify initialization and ensure equal footing for baselines.
- Provide wall-clock compute and memory details.
- Compare explicitly with multi-head/shared-backbone baselines and reconcile with the 2015 arXiv preprint.
