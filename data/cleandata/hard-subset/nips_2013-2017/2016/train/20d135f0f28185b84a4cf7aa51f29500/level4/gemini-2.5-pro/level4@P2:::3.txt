### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where the objective is to train an ensemble of models to produce a set of plausible outputs that collectively minimize an oracle loss (i.e., the loss of the best single prediction in the set). The authors correctly point out that existing MCL algorithms, which often rely on iterative retraining of ensemble members, are computationally prohibitive for modern deep neural networks.

The primary contribution is a simple and effective algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The method adapts the MCL objective for training with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). For each example in a mini-batch, the forward pass is computed for all ensemble members, but the loss gradient is backpropagated only through the single member that achieved the lowest error. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows all models in the ensemble to be trained concurrently in a single training run. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing that it consistently outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines in terms of oracle performance.

### **Review**

#### **Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a timely and important problem. As deep learning models are applied to more complex tasks with inherent ambiguity, generating a diverse set of high-quality hypotheses is more valuable than producing a single, potentially averaged-out prediction. Making the MCL framework practical for computationally expensive deep models is a significant contribution.
*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant, simple to implement, and requires no additional hyperparameters. Its agnosticism to both network architecture and loss function makes it a general and highly practical tool that could be easily adopted by other researchers and practitioners.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. Demonstrating consistent performance gains on three distinct and challenging tasks (classification, segmentation, and captioning) using different modern architectures strongly supports the authors' claims about the method's effectiveness and versatility.

#### **Weaknesses**

*   **Mismatch Between Framing and Method:** A major concern is the paper's framing around "diversity." The title, abstract, and introduction heavily emphasize the goal of training "diverse" ensembles. However, the sMCL method does not explicitly include a diversity term in its objective function; it solely minimizes the oracle loss. While diversity is shown to be an emergent property of the training process, the contribution is more accurately described as an efficient algorithm for oracle loss optimization, not a new method for encouraging diversity directly. This framing could be misleading to readers.
*   **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** The proposed method is best understood as a stochastic, online adaptation of the block coordinate descent optimization strategy previously proposed for MCL by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. While successfully adapting this idea to the mini-batch SGD setting for deep networks is a valuable engineering achievement, the conceptual leap beyond prior work is modest. The paper would be strengthened by positioning the work more clearly as a novel and scalable optimization strategy for an existing learning objective.

Overall, the paper presents a simple, practical, and effective method for a significant problem. While the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, the practical utility and strong empirical results make this a valuable contribution.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the implementation of the MCL baseline from Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]? Since this method requires training models "to convergence" before re-assigning data points, its performance is highly dependent on the convergence criteria. What specific criteria were used to determine convergence for the deep network models in each of the five meta-iterations? An insufficient number of training iterations could significantly hamper the baseline's ability to specialize and thus affect the performance comparison.

### **Minor Suggestions & Typos**

*   To better align the paper's claims with its technical contribution, consider toning down the emphasis on "diversity" in the title and abstract and instead highlighting the optimization of the oracle loss.
*   The first sentence of the abstract is slightly complex and could be rewritten for improved clarity.
*   Line 108: "we fix the form of this mapping g...": The draft I reviewed stated there was a typo where an "f" should be a "g". Please double-check this sentence for correctness.
*   Line 115: There appears to be an extra period in the middle of a sentence.
