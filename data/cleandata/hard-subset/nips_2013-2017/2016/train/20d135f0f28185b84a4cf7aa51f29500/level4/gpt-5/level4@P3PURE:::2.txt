This paper proposes a method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks (or, more generally, any gradient-based model) using an oracle that, for each training instance, identifies the ensemble member that is optimal for that instance. Backpropagation is applied only to the selected network. This strategy encourages the individual networks to specialize, leading to an ensemble that collectively covers most or all outputs of interest. Results are reported across a variety of domains and show strong overall performance.

The idea is clear and compelling: use an oracle during training to route the gradient update to the network that produces the best output according to the ground-truth label. The method appears to enforce specialization effectively in classification settings. The paper is well written and the experimental evaluation spans several domains. Overall, this is strong work that deserves to be published.

That said, the paper would be outstanding if it also addressed how to exploit the trained ensemble in practice. Training relies on the oracle, but the paper does not develop how the resulting ensemble should be used at inference time, beyond noting that it offers a broad pool of possible outputs and aligns with top-k metrics used in some benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet). This is an important omission for practical deployment. I acknowledge that the best solution may be problem-specific, but some concrete guidance or experiments on inference-time usage would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Minor presentation issue: Figure 2’s pseudo-code appears as a raster image. Please include the pseudo-code as text in the paper, or at least use a vector graphic to improve readability.
