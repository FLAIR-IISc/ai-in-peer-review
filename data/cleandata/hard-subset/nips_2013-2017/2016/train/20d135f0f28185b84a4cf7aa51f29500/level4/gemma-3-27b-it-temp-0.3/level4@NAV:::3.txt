This paper addresses the challenging multiple choice learning (MCL) problem in the context of deep neural networks, a setting where existing MCL solutions do not readily apply. The authors propose a straightforward yet effective approach – a “winner-takes-the-gradient” layer integrated into the backpropagation process – to jointly train an ensemble of deep networks and solve the associated assignment problem. The method demonstrates strong performance across diverse tasks including image classification, segmentation, and captioning. 

The paper’s strengths lie in the significance of the MCL problem for deep learning, which is highly relevant to the NIPS community, and the elegance of the proposed solution. Furthermore, the experimental validation across three distinct tasks is commendable.

However, a key concern is the emphasis on “diversity” throughout the paper – reflected even in the title – without a corresponding explicit mechanism to enforce it. The motivation led me to anticipate a specific diversity term within the model, which was ultimately absent. Additionally, while effective, the proposed method represents an incremental advancement building upon the relaxation introduced by Guzman et al.

Minor points for improvement include rewriting the opening sentence of the abstract for clarity, reducing the prominence of “diversity” in the presentation, and correcting typographical errors on line 108 (“f” should be “g”) and line 115 (extraneous period).

Finally, I have a question regarding the baseline MCL implementation with deep learning: how was convergence ensured for each network within the ensemble before terminating training? Premature termination could potentially impact the overall ensemble performance.
