- Introduces sMCL: a simple training method for deep ensembles that produces multiple plausible hypotheses instead of single predictions.  
- Solves a major limitation of prior MCL by eliminating costly iterative retraining for deep networks.  
- Method: backpropagate gradients only to the lowest-error predictor for each example during training.  
- Applicable to any deep architecture trained with SGD, making it broadly useful across CV and NLP tasks.  
- Builds on Guzman-Rivera et al.’s MCL framework but integrates assignment and training into a single stochastic process.  
- Relates to ensemble and diverse-solution literature, addressing their scalability limits for deep networks.  
- Strong technical soundness with both theoretical analysis and empirical support.  
- Thorough experiments on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.  
- Quantitative gains: consistent 6–10% improvements over baselines in oracle performance.  
- Qualitative analysis shows ensemble members often develop specialized expertise.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and includes a clear Algorithm 2 implementation guide.  
- Minor grammatical issues noted but not harmful to understanding.  
- Novel and practical extension of MCL to deep learning; parameter-free and efficient.  
- Demonstrated practical significance for ambiguous prediction scenarios and human-in-the-loop systems.  
- Major strengths: simplicity, applicability, consistent performance gains, qualitative insights, and faster training (≈5x).  
- Main weakness: paper overemphasizes "diversity" though diversity is a byproduct, not explicitly enforced.  
- Needs clearer discussion of specialization vs. diversity and how specialization is ensured.  
- Clarification required on MCL baseline convergence and how ensemble size (M) affects performance.  
- Overall positive recommendation: valuable, well-supported contribution suitable for NIPS with minor revisions.
