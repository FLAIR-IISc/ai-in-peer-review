

This paper addresses the challenge of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep neural networks, proposing a simple yet effective modification to the backpropagation process via a "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer. The authors argue that existing MCL solutions fail to scale to deep networks due to their inability to jointly optimize individual learner training and the combinatorial assignment problem. Their approach elegantly integrates these components by modifying gradient flow during training, enabling ensemble learning without additional computational overhead. Experimental validation across three distinct domains—image classification, segmentation, and captioning—demonstrates consistent performance gains over baseline MCL methods and standard ensembles.  

**Strengths:**  
The problem of MCL for deep learning is highly relevant to the NIPS community, as it bridges ensemble methods with scalable deep learning architectures. The proposed solution is remarkably simple: a single layer that dynamically assigns gradients to the "winning" network per sample during backpropagation. This simplicity is a significant strength, avoiding complex retraining or auxiliary objectives. The experimental scope is also commendable, covering three diverse tasks with thorough comparisons against strong baselines, including variants of MCL adapted for deep networks. Results show consistent improvements, particularly in scenarios where individual network diversity is critical, suggesting the method’s practical utility.  

**Major Weakness:**  
The paper’s core weakness lies in its misalignment between motivation and execution. The title and extensive discussion emphasize "diversity" as a key contribution, yet the method *does not explicitly enforce or induce diversity* in the learners. The authors claim diversity is "implicitly achieved" through the assignment mechanism, but no evidence or theoretical justification supports this. Without explicit diversity-promoting terms (e.g., regularization, loss functions, or constraints), the ensemble may simply favor the strongest single network, undermining the stated rationale. This disconnect is particularly problematic given that diversity is a well-established driver of ensemble performance. The reviewers’ initial enthusiasm for a novel diversity mechanism was significantly dampened upon realizing the method lacks this component.  

**Minor Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
1. **Incremental Contribution:** The approach builds directly on Guzman et al.’s relaxation of MCL for deep networks, making it a technical refinement rather than a conceptual leap. The paper should more clearly position this as a practical optimization of prior work rather than a novel framework.  
2. **Clarity and Precision:**  
   - The abstract’s first sentence is grammatically awkward: "This paper proposes a simple solution to the multiple choice learning problem where the learners are deep networks" should be rephrased to "This paper proposes a simple solution to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep neural networks."  
   - The term "diversity" should be de-emphasized throughout (e.g., in the title and abstract) to align with the method’s actual mechanism.  
   - Typos: Line 108 contains "f" instead of "g" ("we fixed the form of .."), and line 115 has an extraneous period mid-sentence.  
3. **Baseline Validation:** The paper does not clarify how convergence of individual networks in the MCL baseline was ensured. If networks are cut early to form the ensemble (as implied by the method), suboptimal individual performance could artificially inflate ensemble results. The authors should address this by reporting convergence metrics for baseline networks or justifying the early-stopping strategy.  

**Question for Authors:**  
The baseline MCL method trains all networks in parallel. How were individual network convergences verified to ensure each learner’s output was reliable before ensemble formation? Without this, early termination in the proposed method could disproportionately degrade performance.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This work presents a technically sound, lightweight extension to MCL for deep networks with strong empirical validation. Its simplicity and broad applicability are appealing, but the unsupported claim of "diversity" significantly undermines its contribution. The paper would be strengthened by:  
(i) Removing all references to "diversity" (or providing explicit diversity enforcement),  
(ii) Clarifying the incremental nature of the contribution relative to Guzman et al., and  
(iii) Addressing baseline convergence concerns.  
As written, the method is a useful practical tool, but the central narrative misrepresents its mechanism. With these revisions, it would be a solid addition to NIPS. Without them, the disconnect between title and content risks misleading readers about the novelty.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** (with major revisions). The technical execution is sound, but the misaligned motivation must be corrected to justify the submission’s focus. The authors should reframe the contribution as a *practical implementation* of MCL for deep learning rather than a diversity-driven approach. If they can substantively address the diversity issue and baseline validation, this would be a strong contribution.
