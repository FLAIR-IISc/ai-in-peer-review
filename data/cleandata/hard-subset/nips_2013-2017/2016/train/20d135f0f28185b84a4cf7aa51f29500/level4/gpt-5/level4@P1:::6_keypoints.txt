- Proposal: Introduces sMCL, an SGD-friendly "winner-take-gradient" method to train deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss by backpropagating only through the lowest-loss member per example.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), diverse N-best/MRF methods, and ensemble diversity techniques, claiming novelty in a parameter-free, concurrent SGD formulation.

- Strength: Simple, architecture- and loss-agnostic rule that integrates easily into existing SGD pipelines.

- Strength: Consistent and significant oracle-performance gains across CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning, and faster than retraining-based MCL.

- Strength: Empirical analyses show interpretable specialization emerging among ensemble members.

- Strength: Parameter-free and concurrent training is practical for deep networks where repeated retraining is costly.

- Weakness: Test-time procedure unclear—paper appears to use ground-truth to select best member for evaluation; deployment without an oracle is not discussed.

- Weakness: Lack of non-oracle metrics and practical selection/re-ranking strategies to contextualize real-world utility.

- Weakness: Unclear baseline specification for "independent" ensembles (training details, augmentation, shared/frozen components for captioning) affecting fairness.

- Weakness: Specialization claims are not fully substantiated by the per-example gradient rule; qualitative histograms are suggestive but need stronger quantitative evidence.

- Weakness: Missing discussion and comparison with diversity-regularization methods in neural nets (e.g., negative correlation learning, diversity regularization).

- Suggestion: Provide MCL convergence curves or justify limited meta-iterations to ensure fair comparison with MCL.

- Suggestion: Report sensitivity/stability analyses (initialization, tie-breaking, batch size, ensemble size), and detail any warm-up or soft/k-winner variants.

- Suggestion: Discuss captioning setup implications (shared/frozen CNN) on diversity and whether joint fine-tuning would change results.

- Suggestion: Report absolute training times and per-epoch overhead for loss computation/routing to quantify computational cost.

- Evaluation: Idea is sound, novel in formulation, empirically supported on oracle metrics, but needs clearer methodology, baseline definitions, and stronger specialization/diversity analysis.

- Pros: Practical, parameter-free, easy to implement, strong oracle gains, concurrent training with substantial speedups, and insightful qualitative diversity analyses.

- Cons: Ambiguity in test-time/oracle evaluation and deployment, incomplete baseline descriptions, overclaimed specialization without diversity-regularization comparisons, and limited stability/sensitivity analysis.

- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on clarifying test/evaluation protocol, expanding related work on diversity regularization, and strengthening specialization and stability analyses.
