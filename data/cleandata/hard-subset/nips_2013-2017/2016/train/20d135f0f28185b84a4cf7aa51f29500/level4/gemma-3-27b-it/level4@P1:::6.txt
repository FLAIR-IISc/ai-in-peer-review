The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep learning models. The core idea is to train an ensemble where, for each training example, only the member with the lowest loss is updated with the gradient – effectively a ‘winner-take-gradient’ approach. This contrasts with traditional ensemble methods where all members are typically updated simultaneously. The authors demonstrate strong experimental results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, and highlight that sMCL addresses limitations of existing methods. Overall, this is a simple yet effective idea that leads to significant improvements, and I recommend weak acceptance.

The paper is well-motivated, framing the problem within the context of real-world perception systems that require multiple plausible hypotheses. The authors effectively connect their work to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and clearly articulate the challenges of applying prior MCL methods to deep networks, specifically the computational cost of retraining. sMCL offers a practical solution by enabling concurrent training of ensemble members using standard stochastic gradient descent. The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate the broad applicability of the proposed method, with consistent improvements over baselines across different tasks and architectures. The analysis of ensemble member specialization is insightful and offers qualitative understanding of the learned representations.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. First, the testing phase is not fully elaborated. It is implied (line 159, page 5) that the best-performing member is selected based on the ground truth, but a more detailed explanation of the inference procedure would be beneficial. Second, the authors frequently refer to “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (denoted as “Indp.” in figures and line 181 on page 5), but the precise definition of these baselines is unclear. Providing a clear description of how these ensembles are trained would strengthen the comparison. Third, while the authors emphasize the “specialization” of ensemble members, the training process reassigns examples to members on a per-example basis. The connection between this per-example assignment and true specialization is not fully justified and could be strengthened. Exploring ways to explicitly encourage specialization within the loss function might be a valuable direction for future work. Finally, the authors do not discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al., 2018). While they acknowledge related work on ensemble learning and generating multiple solutions, diversity regularization offers a more rigorous approach to encouraging diversity, and a discussion of its limitations in the deep learning setting would be relevant. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   Addresses a practical problem in perception systems requiring multiple hypotheses.
*   Strong experimental results across a variety of tasks and architectures.
*   Clear and well-motivated presentation.
*   Addresses limitations of existing methods (e.g., retraining costs of previous MCL approaches).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Testing phase is not fully explained.
*   Definition of “independent” and “regular” ensembles is unclear.
*   Justification for “specialization” claim is not fully convincing.
*   Missing discussion of related work on diversity regularization.



**Reference:**
Chen, B., et al. "Diversity Regularization for Generative Models." *ICLR 2018*.
