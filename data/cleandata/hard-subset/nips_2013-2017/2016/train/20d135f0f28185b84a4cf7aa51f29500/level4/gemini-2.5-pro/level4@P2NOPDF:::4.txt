This paper proposes a stochastic gradient descent method integrated with Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) and demonstrates its application across several computer vision tasks, including classification, segmentation, and image captioning. While the paper addresses an interesting problem, there are several major concerns regarding its novelty, clarity, and experimental validation.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution. The motivation behind Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) is well-established, and its core principles cannot be credited as a novel contribution of this work. The proposed sMCL framework appears to be a direct adaptation of the method presented in [8] for neural network-based architectures, with only minor modifications. The paper needs to better articulate what its fundamental contributions are beyond this adaptation.

2.  **Insufficient Technical Detail and Clarity:** The paper's presentation is not clear, and the technical exposition is too brief. The core methodology (Section 3) is allocated only a single page, which is insufficient for a comprehensive understanding of the framework and its implementation details. Crucially, the paper fails to discuss the relationship between the proposed method and other common multi-task or alternative training strategies. The authors should clarify the key differences and advantages of their framework compared to standard alternating optimization schemes, which are widely adopted for training models on multiple objectives.

3.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** While the authors apply their method to a variety of tasks, the experimental setup is not convincing enough to support their claims. The evaluation suffers from two main issues:
    *   **Lack of Strong Baselines:** The experiments do not include comparisons with strong, state-of-the-art baseline methods for the selected tasks. This makes it difficult to assess the true effectiveness and competitiveness of the proposed framework.
    *   **Marginal Performance Gains:** The reported improvements over the existing baselines are consistently small, which raises questions about the practical significance of the proposed method.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider a more rigorous empirical evaluation. A compelling demonstration of the framework's effectiveness would involve experiments on a challenging and standardized benchmark, such as PASCAL VOC 2012 for segmentation. Comparing against more recent and stronger baselines on such a benchmark would provide a much clearer assessment of the method's value.
