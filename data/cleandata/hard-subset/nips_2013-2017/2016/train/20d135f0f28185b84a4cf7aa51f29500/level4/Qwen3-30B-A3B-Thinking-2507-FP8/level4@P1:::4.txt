

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple outputs rather than a single prediction. The authors frame this as an extension of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8, 9] to deep networks, where the goal is to minimize the oracle loss - the loss of the best prediction in a set of M predictions. The core innovation is a "winner-takes-gradient" approach where gradients are only backpropagated to the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss for each example, enabling concurrent training of all ensemble members without the need for costly retraining.

The paper makes a clear connection to previous work on Multiple Choice Learning [8, 9] and extends it to deep networks. The authors correctly identify that existing MCL approaches are impractical for deep networks due to their requirement for multiple retraining cycles. Their proposed sMCL method avoids this limitation while maintaining the oracle loss minimization objective. The paper demonstrates the method on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing consistent improvements over baselines.

Strengths:
- The problem addressed is well-motivated by practical applications where multiple hypotheses are more useful than single predictions (e.g., ambiguous visual scenes, structured prediction with multiple reasonable interpretations).
- The sMCL method is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and parameter-free, making it broadly applicable.
- The paper provides compelling evidence of emergent specialization among ensemble members, which is both interpretable and useful for capturing task ambiguity.
- The method achieves significant improvements (6-10%) over baselines in oracle performance across multiple tasks.

Weaknesses:
- The novelty is incremental rather than transformative. While the authors correctly identify the limitations of prior MCL approaches for deep networks, the core method is a natural extension of [8, 9] rather than a fundamentally new contribution. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how sMCL differs from standard multi-task learning or other ensemble training strategies.
- Section 3 (the technical core) is insufficiently detailed (only one page). A more comprehensive explanation of the optimization procedure, comparison to alternative training approaches, and analysis of computational complexity would strengthen the paper.
- The experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines. The paper compares against classical independent ensembles, MCL [8], and Dey et al. [5], but omits comparisons with more recent and advanced ensemble diversity methods (e.g., [19, 21, 26]) or standard multi-task learning approaches. The modest performance gains (6-10%) are difficult to evaluate without stronger baselines.
- The experiments are limited to specific datasets (CIFAR10, Pascal VOC 2011, MSCOCO). To establish broader applicability, the authors should include results on additional benchmarks like Pascal VOC 2012, which is a standard segmentation benchmark.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments, but the theoretical analysis is limited. The method works well in practice, though the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why it outperforms alternatives.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written but suffers from insufficient technical detail in Section 3. The authors could improve clarity by elaborating on the optimization procedure and explicitly contrasting sMCL with alternative training strategies.

Originality: The core idea is an incremental extension of [8, 9] to deep networks. While the application to deep learning is valuable, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing ensemble training methods.

Significance: The problem is meaningful for practical perception systems, and the method has clear utility. However, the performance gains are modest, and the paper doesn't provide strong evidence that this approach will significantly advance the field beyond existing ensemble methods.

Pro:
- Clear problem statement with practical motivation
- Simple, practical implementation
- Demonstrated effectiveness across diverse tasks
- Evidence of interpretable emergent specialization

Con:
- Incremental novelty compared to prior work
- Insufficient technical detail in the core method
- Limited comparison with strong baselines
- Modest performance improvements

Overall, the paper presents a useful extension of Multiple Choice Learning to deep networks with a practical implementation that achieves consistent improvements over baselines. However, the novelty is limited, the technical explanation is too brief, and the experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these weaknesses, particularly by providing more detailed technical justification, comparing against more relevant baselines, and demonstrating broader applicability.
