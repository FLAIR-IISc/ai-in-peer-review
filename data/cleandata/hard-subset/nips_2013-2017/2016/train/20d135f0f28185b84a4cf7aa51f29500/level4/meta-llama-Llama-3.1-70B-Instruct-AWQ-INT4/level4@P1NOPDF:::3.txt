This paper proposes a simple solution to the multiple choice learning (MCL) problem when the learners are deep networks. Current solutions to MCL do not directly extend to deep networks, making this work a valuable contribution. The authors demonstrate that a simple modification to the backpropagation process, incorporating a "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer, enables the combination of learner training with the assignment problem in an ensemble setting.

Experimental results show that this method performs well across a wide range of problems, including image classification, image segmentation, and captioning. 

Strengths:
- The problem of MCL for deep learning is of significant importance to the NIPS community, making this work relevant and timely.
- The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, which is a considerable advantage.
- The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on three different tasks, providing a thorough evaluation.

Weaknesses:
- My biggest concern with this paper is that it extensively motivates the importance of "diversity" (even including the word in the title), but the model does not explicitly enforce diversity. I was initially excited to see how the authors incorporated the diversity term into their model, but was disappointed to find that it was not included.
- The proposed solution can be seen as an incremental step, building on the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract could be rephrased for clarity.
- The emphasis on "diversity" should be toned down, as it is not a key component of the proposed method.
- Line 108, the first "f" should be corrected to "g" in "we fixed the form of .."
- An extra period in the middle of a sentence in line 115 should be removed.

One question remains: For the baseline MCL with deep learning, how did the authors ensure that each of the networks converged to reasonable results? Cutting the learners early on might significantly affect the ensemble performance.
