This paper proposes a simple approach to multiple-choice learning (MCL) with deep networks. Existing MCL methods do not directly extend to deep architectures. The authors introduce a winner-takes-the-gradient mechanism during backpropagation, which routes gradients only to the best-performing learner for each example. This couples learner training with the assignment step within an ensemble. Experiments demonstrate strong performance across diverse tasks, including image classification, segmentation, and captioning.

Strengths:
- The problem of MCL for deep learning is important and relevant to the NIPS community.
- The method is conceptually simple and easy to implement.
- The empirical evaluation spans three distinct tasks, suggesting broad applicability.

Weaknesses:
- The paper heavily emphasizes “diversity” (even in the title), yet the method does not explicitly enforce or quantify diversity. The framing creates an expectation of a diversity-promoting objective or constraint that is not present.
- Relative to prior work (e.g., the relaxation by Guzman et al.), the contribution feels incremental.

Minor suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract should be rewritten for clarity.
- Either tone down claims about diversity or include an explicit mechanism and/or measurements to substantiate it.
- Line 108: in “we fixed the form of ...,” the first “f” should be “g.”
- Line 115: there is an extra period in the middle of a sentence; please remove it.

Question:
- For the deep-learning MCL baseline, how did the authors ensure that each network converged to a reasonable solution? If some learners are stopped early or insufficiently trained, ensemble performance could be unfairly degraded. Please clarify the training protocol and convergence criteria for the baseline.
