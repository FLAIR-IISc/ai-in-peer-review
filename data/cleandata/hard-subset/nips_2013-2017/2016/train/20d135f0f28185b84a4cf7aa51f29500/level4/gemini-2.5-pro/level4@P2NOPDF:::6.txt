This paper proposes a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep neural networks. The key idea is that for each training example, only a single member of the ensemble—the one exhibiting the lowest loss with respect to the ground truth label—is selected and updated via gradient descent. This "winner-takes-all" approach contrasts with traditional ensemble methods where all members are typically trained concurrently. The proposed method is conceptually simple yet demonstrates surprisingly strong empirical performance and appears to address certain limitations of existing techniques. For these reasons, I lean towards acceptance, but have several points that the authors should address to strengthen the paper.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarity of the Inference Procedure:** The description of the testing phase is unclear. During training, the best-performing ensemble member is identified using the ground truth label (as described on page 5, line 159). However, it is not explained how the ensemble's final prediction is generated at test time when the ground truth is unavailable. Is the final output an average of all members' predictions, a majority vote, or is a single "best" member selected through some other mechanism? The authors should explicitly detail this procedure.

2.  **Undefined Baseline:** The paper frequently compares its method against a baseline referred to as an "independent ensemble" or "regular ensemble" (denoted as "Indp." in figures and mentioned on page 5, line 181). The manuscript does not seem to provide a definition or reference for this baseline. To ensure the experimental results are understandable and reproducible, the authors should provide a precise description of how this baseline is constructed and trained.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for "Specialization":** A central claim of the paper is that the proposed training method encourages ensemble members to "specialize" on different subsets of the data, potentially corresponding to different output classes. This argument is not sufficiently justified. The mechanism for selecting a member to update operates on a per-example basis, and it is not immediately obvious why this would lead to stable, class-level specialization rather than a more dynamic, instance-level division of labor. The authors should provide a more thorough analysis or empirical evidence (e.g., by measuring which members are most frequently updated for examples of a given class) to substantiate this claim. As a suggestion, explicitly encouraging class-based specialization could be achieved by incorporating a diversity or specialization term directly into the loss function.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including literature on diversity regularization in neural network ensembles. The goal of encouraging specialization is closely related to the well-studied concept of promoting diversity among ensemble members. For instance, techniques that explicitly add a diversity-promoting term to the loss function (e.g., Chen et al.) are highly relevant. While these methods may not have been extensively applied to modern deep learning architectures, discussing them would provide important context and a more complete view of the research landscape.
