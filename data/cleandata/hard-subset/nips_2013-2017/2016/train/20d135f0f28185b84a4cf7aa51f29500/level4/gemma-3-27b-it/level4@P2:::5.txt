This paper addresses the problem of generating multiple plausible hypotheses from deep learning models, motivated by applications where a human or downstream process evaluates the predictions. The authors frame this as a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem and propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach for training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize oracle loss. sMCL is presented as a parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and efficiently trainable method. The results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning demonstrate that sMCL outperforms existing methods and produces diverse solutions that can capture task ambiguity. 

This work makes a valuable contribution by adapting the MCL paradigm to deep networks, a space where previous methods required computationally expensive retraining steps. The proposed sMCL algorithm elegantly integrates into standard backpropagation frameworks, enabling end-to-end training of the ensemble. The experiments showcase the effectiveness of the approach across different architectures and tasks, and the qualitative results highlight the interpretability of the diverse outputs. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

However, several points warrant further consideration. The impact of initialization on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated. The paper would be strengthened by exploring the performance of sMCL with randomly initialized models, in addition to pre-trained networks. Clarification is also needed regarding the experimental setup and baseline comparisons. Specifically, do the baseline methods utilize the same pre-trained networks for initialization as sMCL? Ensuring a fair comparison is crucial. 

Furthermore, while the paper highlights the diversity of the generated solutions, a quantitative assessment of this diversity would be beneficial. Metrics beyond oracle accuracy, such as measures of inter-model dissimilarity, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The authors should also discuss how the ensemble size affects performance and diversity. Finally, the work of Lee et al. (2015) – “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks” – presents a closely related approach and warrants a direct comparison and discussion of the differences.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of MCL to deep ensembles, addressing a practical problem with a clear motivation.
*   The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant, efficient, and integrates seamlessly with existing deep learning workflows.
*   Strong empirical results across multiple tasks and architectures.
*   Clear writing and detailed explanations facilitate understanding and reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited analysis of the impact of initialization on performance.
*   Lack of quantitative evaluation of ensemble diversity.
*   Missing comparison to closely related work (Lee et al., 2015).
*   Some ambiguity regarding experimental setup and baseline comparisons.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. While some aspects could be further strengthened, the novelty, effectiveness, and clarity of the work make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised regarding initialization, diversity quantification, and comparison to related work.
