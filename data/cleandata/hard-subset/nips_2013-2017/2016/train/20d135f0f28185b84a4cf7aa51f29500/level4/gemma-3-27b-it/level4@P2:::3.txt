This paper addresses the multiple choice learning (MCL) problem in the context of deep learning, a setting where providing a set of plausible hypotheses is beneficial for downstream tasks. Existing MCL solutions struggle to scale to deep networks. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a straightforward modification to standard stochastic gradient descent – a “winner-takes-the-gradient” approach – that simultaneously optimizes ensemble member training and the assignment of examples to the best-performing member. The method is evaluated across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, demonstrating strong performance gains.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The problem of efficiently training diverse ensembles for multi-hypothesis scenarios is relevant and important to the NIPS community, particularly given the growing integration of perception systems within larger, interactive applications.
*   **Simplicity:** The proposed sMCL method is conceptually simple and easy to implement, requiring only a minor modification to existing training pipelines. Its parameter-free nature is also appealing.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of sMCL across a variety of tasks and deep learning architectures (CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNNs), suggesting its generalizability.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show sMCL outperforming both classical ensembles and a baseline implementation of the existing MCL approach by Guzman-Rivera et al.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Diversity Enforcement:** While the paper extensively motivates the importance of *diversity* in the generated solutions, the method itself does not explicitly enforce diversity. The observed diversity emerges as a consequence of minimizing oracle loss, rather than being directly optimized for. This distinction is important and could be clarified. The emphasis on diversity in the title and introduction feels somewhat misleading given the lack of a dedicated diversity objective.
*   **Incremental Advance:** The proposed method represents an incremental improvement over the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. by addressing the scalability issues of their alternating block coordinate descent approach. While significant, the core idea builds directly upon prior work.
*   **Baseline Convergence:** The description of the baseline MCL implementation lacks detail regarding how convergence was ensured for each network. Prematurely stopping training of individual models within the ensemble could significantly impact performance.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Abstract Revision:** The first sentence of the abstract could be more concise and impactful.
*   **Tone Down “Diversity”:** Reduce the emphasis on “diversity” throughout the paper to more accurately reflect the method's reliance on oracle loss minimization as the driver of ensemble variation.
*   **Typographical Errors:** Correct the typographical errors identified (line 108: “f” should be “g”; line 115: remove extra period).



**Question for the Authors:**

For the baseline MCL implementation, what criteria were used to determine convergence for each network, and how was the impact of potential premature convergence mitigated?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem in deep learning.
*   The proposed method is simple, effective, and broadly applicable.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing approaches.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of sMCL.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of explicit diversity enforcement might limit the method's effectiveness in scenarios where highly diverse solutions are critical.
*   The contribution is relatively incremental, building directly upon previous work.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the baseline MCL implementation raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning for deep networks. While the lack of explicit diversity control and the incremental nature of the advance are weaknesses, the simplicity, effectiveness, and broad applicability of sMCL make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the emphasis on diversity and the details of the baseline implementation.
