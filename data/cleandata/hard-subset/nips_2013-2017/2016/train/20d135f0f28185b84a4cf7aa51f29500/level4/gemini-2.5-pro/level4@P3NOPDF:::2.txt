This paper introduces a novel method for training ensembles of gradient-based models, such as Deep Neural Networks. The core idea is to leverage an oracle during training that, for each data instance, identifies the optimal ensemble member (i.e., the network whose output is closest to the ground truth). The backpropagation update is then exclusively applied to this single, selected network. The main advantage of this approach is that it encourages the individual networks within the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data, resulting in a diverse set of experts that collectively cover a wider range of possible outputs. The authors validate their method with strong empirical results across a variety of domains.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Intuition:** The paper presents an interesting and intuitive idea for training ensembles. The use of an oracle to guide updates and enforce specialization is a creative approach that appears to be highly effective, particularly in classification contexts.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is explained clearly. The motivation behind encouraging specialization is well-argued.
*   **Thorough Experiments:** The empirical validation is a significant strength. The experiments are conducted on several different domains, and the consistently strong performance effectively demonstrates the benefits of the proposed training scheme.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

My primary concern, and what prevents this from being an outstanding paper, is the lack of discussion on how to effectively exploit the trained ensemble at inference time. The entire training procedure is predicated on an oracle that uses the ground truth label to select the best network. At test time, however, this oracle is unavailable. The paper does not propose a concrete mechanism for combining the outputs of the specialized networks to make a final prediction.

The authors briefly mention that their approach provides a broad pool of possible results and is coherent with top-k evaluation metrics (as used in ImageNet), but this point is not developed further. This is a significant omission, as it leaves a critical gap between the training methodology and its practical application. Without a clear and principled strategy for inference, the utility of the resulting ensemble is unclear. While the optimal inference strategy may be problem-specific, addressing this challenge—even by discussing potential avenues, exploring simple baselines (e.g., averaging, voting), or framing it as a direction for future work—would have substantially strengthened the paper's contribution.

On a minor presentational note, the pseudo-code in Figure 2 is embedded as a raster image. For improved clarity, readability, and professionalism, this should be typeset directly within the paper (e.g., using an algorithmic package) or at least be included as a vector graphic.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and well-motivated technique for training specialized network ensembles. The core contribution regarding the training methodology is clear, novel, and supported by compelling empirical evidence. While the unaddressed question of how to use the ensemble at inference time is a notable weakness, the novelty of the training method itself constitutes a solid contribution that is worthy of publication. The ideas presented are likely to inspire further research into both the training and exploitation of specialized ensemble models. I recommend accepting this paper.
