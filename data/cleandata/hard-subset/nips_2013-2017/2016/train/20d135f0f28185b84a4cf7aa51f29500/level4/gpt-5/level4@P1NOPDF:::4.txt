This paper formulates a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) method for Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), termed sMCL, and claims advantages across classification, segmentation, and captioning.

1. Novelty: The contribution appears incremental. While the motivation behind MCL is clear, it is not specific to this work and thus should not be counted as a primary contribution here. The proposed sMCL framework reads largely as a neural network instantiation of [8] with some minor adjustments, rather than a fundamentally new approach.

2. Clarity and technical depth: The paper is not well written, and the technical content is thin. The core technical section (Section 3) is only one page, which makes it difficult to assess the method rigorously. Key algorithmic details, training dynamics, and theoretical or empirical justification are underdeveloped. In particular, please clarify how the proposed framework differs from standard alternating training schemes that are widely adopted when multiple tasks are trained jointly. What concrete advantages—algorithmic, computational, or empirical—does sMCL provide over these established training strategies?

3. Experiments and baselines: Although the paper reports results on several computer vision tasks (classification, image segmentation, and captioning), the experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines, and the reported gains are small. To convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness and generality of the framework, the paper should compare against stronger, state-of-the-art baselines. In particular, experiments on PASCAL VOC 2012 would provide a more rigorous and widely recognized benchmark for segmentation and allow for more meaningful comparisons.

Overall, the idea is potentially useful, but the current submission is limited by incremental novelty, insufficient technical exposition, and an underpowered empirical evaluation. Strengthening the technical section, clearly differentiating sMCL from widely used joint/alternating training paradigms, and evaluating on stronger baselines (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012) would significantly improve the paper.
