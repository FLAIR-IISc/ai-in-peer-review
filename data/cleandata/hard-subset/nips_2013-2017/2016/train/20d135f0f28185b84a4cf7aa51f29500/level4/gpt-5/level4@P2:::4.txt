Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based “winner-take-gradient” procedure for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. Instead of producing a single prediction per input, the ensemble generates M hypotheses; a downstream oracle is assumed to select the best one. The method integrates the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9] into standard deep learning training loops by interleaving per-example assignment and gradient updates within minibatches, avoiding the costly alternating retraining used in prior MCL work. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all ensemble members concurrently.

Experiments span three settings: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM). The paper reports consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, over the sequential/submodular approach of Dey et al. [5], and over a limited retraining version of MCL. Qualitative analyses suggest interpretable specialization of ensemble members (e.g., class or shape biases in vision; n-gram diversity in captions).

The work sits at the intersection of ensemble learning and multi-hypothesis prediction. It builds directly on MCL [8, 9] and is related to diverse M-best methods in structured prediction [3, 15, 16, 23, 24]. Compared to prior ensemble diversity techniques (e.g., resampling [27], artificial data [21], negative correlation [19], joint losses [26], expert architectures [2, 12]), the novelty here is focusing on oracle loss in deep models with an efficient SGD-based assignment mechanism applicable beyond standard classification.

Strengths:
- Practicality: A simple, parameter-free training procedure that integrates cleanly with SGD and supports arbitrary architectures and loss functions.
- Efficiency: Trains all members in parallel and empirically reduces wall-clock time versus prior MCL-style alternating retraining.
- Breadth: Demonstrated across classification, segmentation, and captioning, with both quantitative oracle gains and qualitative diversity/specialization.
- Interpretability: Clear evidence of emergent specialization among ensemble members without explicit gating.
- Relevance: Oracle-style multi-hypothesis outputs are useful in downstream pipelines (e.g., verification, human-in-the-loop).

Weaknesses:
- Incremental novelty: Conceptually, sMCL is a straightforward hard-EM/K-means style update applied within SGD to the MCL objective; theoretical contributions (e.g., convergence, stability) are minimal and Section 3 is short.
- Clarity and exposition: The technical section is brief; more detail on implementation (tie-breaking, batch-level assignment, preventing collapse, soft vs. hard assignment) would improve reproducibility and understanding.
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited. For segmentation, evaluation on VOC 2011 rather than VOC 2012 reduces comparability to stronger baselines; more competitive baselines (e.g., expert-gated mixtures, negative correlation ensembles [19], stronger FCN variants) would strengthen claims. For captioning, while beam search is compared, analysis of non-oracle metrics and calibration is limited.
- Scope of evaluation: Oracle metrics alone do not quantify trade-offs in single-prediction performance or downstream selection; ablations on M, batch size, tie strategies, and “top-k winners” variants are light.

Quality:
The method is technically sound as an SGD-compatible approximation to the MCL objective, and the winner-only gradient rule follows directly from the indicator relaxation. However, the paper could better discuss optimization behavior (e.g., oscillations, fairness across members, assignment imbalance) and provide more rigorous analysis or diagnostics (e.g., assignment entropy over training, collapse checks, convergence plots).

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable, but Section 3 is underdeveloped relative to the central contribution. Adding algorithmic pseudocode with all edge cases, clearer discussion of differences from standard joint/alternate training, and more detailed training settings (e.g., batch sizes per member, assignment tie policies, gradient scaling) would improve clarity.

Originality:
The core idea—optimizing oracle loss for deep ensembles via hard per-example assignment within SGD—is a natural and useful extension of MCL to modern deep learning. That said, it is conceptually close to existing alternating/minimax formulations (MCL, hard-EM, mixture-of-experts without learned gating), so the novelty lies primarily in practicality and scope rather than in a new theoretical framework.

Significance:
When an oracle or re-ranking component is available, multi-hypothesis outputs matter; sMCL offers a general, easy-to-use approach that delivers consistent oracle gains and interpretable diversity. The empirical gains reported are moderate but consistent; demonstrating improvements against stronger baselines and on standard benchmarks (e.g., VOC 2012) would increase impact.

Questions and suggestions:
- Please clarify how ties are handled and whether you explored soft assignments (e.g., top-k winners) during training beyond the ablation in Fig. 4d–e.
- Do non-winning members receive any regularization or auxiliary gradients to avoid starvation/collapse? Are data rebalancing or assignment smoothing strategies helpful?
- Report standard deviations across runs and assignment imbalance statistics over training.
- Strengthen baselines, particularly on VOC 2012, and include expert-gated or negative-correlation ensembles [19]. For segmentation, compare to stronger FCN variants or recent architectures.
- Include non-oracle metrics and calibration analyses to assess trade-offs with single-prediction performance.

Pros:
- Simple, general, and efficient approach to training diverse deep ensembles for oracle metrics.
- Consistent improvements across three tasks with qualitative evidence of meaningful specialization.
- Practical contribution likely to be adopted by practitioners needing multi-hypothesis outputs.

Cons:
- Incremental conceptual novelty relative to MCL/hard-EM; limited theoretical analysis.
- Exposition of the core algorithm is brief; implementation details are sparse.
- Baseline coverage and benchmark choices limit the strength of empirical claims; gains are modest in places.

Overall:
A practical and broadly applicable extension of Multiple Choice Learning to deep networks via an SGD-compatible training scheme. While the novelty is incremental and the exposition and baselines could be stronger (especially on VOC 2012 and with more competitive ensemble methods), the method is simple, effective, and relevant. Strengthening clarity and comparisons would notably improve the paper.
