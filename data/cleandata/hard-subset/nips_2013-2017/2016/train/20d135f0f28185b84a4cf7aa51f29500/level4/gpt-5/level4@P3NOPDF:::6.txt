Summary
The paper introduces a simple ensemble-training scheme for deep networks in which, for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss relative to the true label is updated. This “winner-take-gradient” strategy differs from standard deep or classical ensembles where all members are trained on all examples (often with different seeds or bootstraps). Despite its simplicity, the method yields strong empirical results and appears to address some deficiencies of standard ensembles. I lean toward a weak accept, contingent on clarifying the evaluation protocol and baseline definitions, and better supporting the claims about specialization.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: per-example selection of a single learner for gradient updates is an elegant idea that is easy to implement.
- Empirical performance: results are surprisingly strong given the minimal modification to standard training, suggesting practical value.
- Potential to induce specialization: the procedure plausibly encourages different members to focus on different regions of the input/output space, which can benefit ensembles.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Test-time procedure is unclear. The paper’s description leaves ambiguity about how predictions are combined at inference. At one point (line 159, p. 5), the text suggests selecting the “best” member using the ground truth, which is not available at test time. Please specify unambiguously:
- Is the final prediction an average/majority vote across members, a max/argmax over members’ confidences, or selected by a learned gating function? 
- If a gating mechanism is used, how is it trained and does it match the training-time selection rule? 
- Is there any risk of label leakage in the reported results?

2) Baselines “independent ensembles” vs “regular ensembles” are not defined. Figures and text refer to “Indp.” and “regular” ensembles (e.g., line 181, p. 5), but the setup is unclear. Please detail:
- How each baseline is trained (same data for all members? bootstrapping? data augmentation? different initializations?).
- How predictions are combined at test time for each baseline.
- Whether parameter budgets and training compute are matched across methods (e.g., number of members, total parameters, epochs).
- Any hyperparameter tuning differences.

3) Specialization claims are insufficiently substantiated. The paper argues that members specialize to different output classes, but the selection is performed per-example without an explicit class-based constraint. Please provide quantitative evidence:
- For each member, the distribution of assigned training examples across classes (class entropy per member, or class concentration indices).
- Per-member confusion matrices or per-class accuracy improvements demonstrating complementary expertise.
- Diversity metrics across members’ predictions (pairwise disagreement, Q-statistic, correlation of logits, or KL divergence between predictive distributions).
- Agreement/disagreement analysis as a function of input difficulty.

4) Encouraging specialization in the objective. If specialization is a central thesis, consider incorporating it explicitly into the loss. Options include:
- Diversity regularization: penalize high correlation between members’ logits or gradients (negative correlation learning; ambiguity-based terms).
- Orthogonality or cosine-similarity penalties on final-layer weight vectors across members to reduce redundancy.
- Class-aware priors: encourage individual members to focus on distinct label subsets (e.g., through a soft assignment/gating prior or group-sparse/exclusive lasso penalties on class weights).
- Load balancing: add an entropy or coverage term over member assignments to prevent collapse to a single dominant expert while maintaining specialization.
- Soft selection: annealed or tempered softmax over per-member losses to interpolate between hard and soft assignment and potentially stabilize training.

5) Related work is incomplete. The paper should discuss:
- Mixture-of-experts and hard/soft gating (e.g., classic MoE frameworks), which are conceptually close to selecting a single expert per example.
- Diversity-promoting regularization in ensembles and neural networks (e.g., negative correlation learning; orthogonality/diversity penalties; determinantal point-process-based selection; ensemble ambiguity frameworks).
- How the proposed approach compares theoretically and empirically to these methods, especially regarding specialization, diversity, and robustness.

Additional suggestions and clarifications
- Provide ablations on ensemble size K, the effect of hard vs soft selection, and sensitivity to label noise and class imbalance (hard selection may overfit noisy labels).
- Discuss training stability. Does one member dominate early, starving others of updates? If so, do you use safeguards (e.g., exploration, assignment smoothing, or per-member learning-rate schedules)?
- Report compute cost. While only one member updates per example, forward passes for all members may be needed to pick the winner. Please report training/inference wall-clock and FLOPs, and compare to standard ensembles with matched budgets.
- Clarify whether members share any parameters or features. If they are fully independent, explain how initialization diversity was controlled. If partial sharing exists, describe the architecture and its implications for diversity.
- Include pseudocode for the training and inference procedures to avoid ambiguity and facilitate reproducibility.

Clarity and reproducibility
- The paper is generally readable, but the evaluation protocol and baseline definitions must be made explicit.
- Please include all hyperparameters, data preprocessing, and code availability plans. Clearly specify the randomization (seeds, shuffles), data splits, and early-stopping criteria.

Overall assessment
This is a neat, minimal-change training strategy for deep ensembles that empirically performs well and is easy to adopt. The idea is intuitive and potentially impactful. However, the current draft underspecifies the test-time procedure, baseline definitions, and evidence for specialization. Addressing these points, expanding the related work to include mixture-of-experts and diversity regularization, and adding targeted analyses/ablations would substantially strengthen the paper. I recommend weak accept, conditional on clarifying the evaluation methodology and providing stronger quantitative support for the specialization and diversity claims.
