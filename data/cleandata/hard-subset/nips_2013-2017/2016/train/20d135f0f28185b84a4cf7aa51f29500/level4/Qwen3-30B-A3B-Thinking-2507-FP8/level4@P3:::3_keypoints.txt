- Introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), training ensembles to produce multiple diverse predictions via a "winner-takes-gradient" update.  
- Targets practical need for multiple plausible hypotheses for downstream human/verification processes.  
- Core algorithm: only the ensemble member with lowest loss for an example receives gradient updates, directly minimizing oracle loss.  
- Builds on Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL but removes costly retraining and sequential model fitting, making it suitable for deep nets.  
- sMCL is parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and enables concurrent training of ensemble members within standard SGD pipelines.  
- Provides theoretical justification for the gradient rule and clear mathematical description (Eq. 2).  
- Thorough experiments on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO showing consistent 6–10% gains in oracle metrics and ~5× speedup vs MCL.  
- Empirical analysis shows interpretable, task-specific ensemble specialization emerges without explicit diversity constraints.  
- Paper is well-written and organized with sufficient experimental and implementation details; minor typos and a slightly ambiguous abstract sentence noted.  
- Original contribution: novel application of the winner-takes-gradient idea to deep ensembles, overcoming prior MCL limitations.  
- Significant practical impact: simple to implement (add sMCL layer), broadly applicable, and likely to be adopted in ambiguous-output tasks.  
- Strengths: solves a practical problem simply, strong empirical results across domains, avoids retraining, and yields natural specialization.  
- Weaknesses: emphasizing "diversity" may mislead since diversity is emergent, not enforced; limited comparison to some recent diverse-output work.  
- Additional weaknesses: motivation for preferring oracle loss could be bolstered with concrete practical examples; MCL baseline convergence details are unclear.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant, practical contribution with sound theory and comprehensive experiments.
