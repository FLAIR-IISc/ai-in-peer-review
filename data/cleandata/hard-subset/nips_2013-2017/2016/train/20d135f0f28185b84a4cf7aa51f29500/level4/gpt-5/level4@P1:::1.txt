Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based procedure for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. Building on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8], where an ensemble is trained so that the loss is computed only on the best-performing predictor for each example, the authors replace the alternating retrain-and-reassign strategy with a simple “winner-take-gradient” update: for each example in a mini-batch, only the lowest-loss model receives a gradient. This makes MCL practical for modern deep networks by enabling concurrent training without repeated full retraining. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and easy to implement.

Experiments span three settings: CIFAR-10 classification with CNNs, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning with CNN+RNNs. The authors compare against standard independently trained ensembles, MCL [8] (with a limited number of meta-iterations due to cost), and the boosting-style approach of Dey et al. [5]. sMCL improves oracle metrics across tasks, matches MCL on CIFAR-10 while being about 5x faster (for five meta-iterations), and substantially outperforms the baselines on segmentation and captioning. Qualitative analyses show emergent specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class specialization in classification, shape/frequency specialization in segmentation, and diverse n-gram usage in captioning).

Relation to prior work
The paper is a direct evolution of MCL [8, 9], addressing its computational impracticality for deep nets by interleaving assignment and optimization within SGD. Unlike diversity-inducing ensemble methods based on resampling or decorrelation [21, 27], sMCL explicitly optimizes oracle loss and applies naturally to structured outputs, complementing “diverse m-best” inference methods [3, 15, 16, 23, 24] that target probabilistic structured models rather than general deep architectures. It also contrasts with the sequential training of Dey et al. [5], which can saturate as ensemble size grows.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: a minimal modification to standard SGD training that is widely applicable, parameter-free, and easy to integrate.
- Broad applicability: demonstrated on classification, segmentation, and captioning with different architectures, including joint CNN+RNN training.
- Strong empirical results: consistent gains in oracle accuracy/IoU/CIDEr-D over independent ensembles and [5], with similar accuracy to MCL on CIFAR-10 at a fraction of the cost and better performance on larger tasks where MCL retraining is infeasible.
- Emergent specialization: clear, interpretable diversity among ensemble members that aligns with task ambiguity.
- Clear writing and thorough qualitative analysis.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Incremental novelty relative to MCL [8]: algorithmically, sMCL is a straightforward stochastic/block gradient realization of the MCL objective. The contribution is primarily in making MCL practical for deep networks rather than introducing a fundamentally new objective.
- Limited comparison to stronger MCL variants: due to cost, the MCL baseline is reported with five meta-iterations. It remains unclear how much further MCL would improve with more iterations or with batched/online assignment updates.
- Diversity analysis could be deeper: while qualitative examples and some specialization analyses are provided, more quantitative measures of diversity (e.g., pairwise disagreement/error correlation, diversity–oracle trade-offs, and sensitivity to M) would strengthen the claims.
- Sensitivity/ablations: the method may depend on batch size, tie-breaking policy, and the number of ensemble members; a brief sensitivity study would be useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against a batched or online variant of MCL: emulate MCL’s assignment step within mini-batches (assign to current best, then take several optimization steps) to examine whether the performance gap is due to the training schedule vs. the stochastic formulation. This addresses whether “MCL with batches” closes the gap to sMCL.
- Quantify diversity: report pairwise prediction disagreement, error correlation, and calibration across members; for captioning, include distinct-n or self-BLEU; for segmentation, analyze per-class IoU disagreement. Connect these metrics to oracle gains.
- Sensitivity analysis: vary batch size, tie-breaking rules, and ensemble size M; report training dynamics and compute vs. performance.
- Explore k-winner variants: the paper briefly mentions penalizing the lowest k predictors; showing accuracy/diversity curves vs. k across tasks would illuminate the specialization–oracle trade-off.
- Fairness of captioning comparisons: clarify whether beam search is also applied per member in sMCL ensembles and discuss compute-normalized comparisons between beam search and multi-model outputs.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and well-supported by experiments and qualitative analyses. Some baselines (full MCL) are constrained by compute; proposed additional comparisons would further solidify the case.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Implementation details are sufficient to reproduce core results.
- Originality: Conceptually incremental to [8], but the stochastic formulation is novel in this context and important for making MCL feasible for deep learning.
- Significance: High practical impact. The method is simple, broadly applicable, and delivers consistent, interpretable gains in oracle metrics across tasks.

Recommendation
While the algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to [8], the paper convincingly demonstrates that this small but elegant change makes MCL practical for deep networks and yields strong empirical benefits. I am in favor of acceptance.
