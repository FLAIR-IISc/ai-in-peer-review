This paper proposes an ensemble-training algorithm for deep networks in which only a single ensemble member is active for each training example. Concretely, for each example the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label is identified and updated via its gradient, while the others receive no update. This hard assignment differentiates the method from prior deep and classical ensembles. The approach is simple yet yields surprisingly strong empirical results and appears to remedy some deficiencies of existing methods. I recommend weak acceptance.

Strengths
- Clear, simple training rule (select-and-update the lowest-loss member) with intuitive appeal.
- Strong experimental results relative to standard ensemble baselines.
- Potential to address issues in conventional ensembles by encouraging members to focus on different parts of the data.

Concerns and questions
1) Test-time procedure unclear. The paper does not clearly explain how predictions are produced at test time. From the description around line 159 on page 5, I suspect the selection may be based on the ground-truth label (i.e., choosing the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label), which would not be permissible at test time. Please clarify the exact inference procedure, ensuring no use of ground-truth labels, and report results accordingly if there was any leakage.

2) Baseline definitions (“independent” vs. “regular” ensembles). Figures and text refer to “independent ensembles” and “regular ensembles” (abbreviated “Indp.” in figures and mentioned around line 181 on page 5), but I could not find a clear description of these baselines. Please define:
- How “independent” and “regular” ensembles differ in architecture, training data splits (if any), optimization, and aggregation.
- Whether members share initialization, data order, augmentations, or hyperparameters.
- The exact prediction aggregation rule used for each baseline.

3) Specialization claims. The paper repeatedly asserts that ensemble members “specialize” to different output classes. However, the reassignment during training is performed per example, not per class, and there is no explicit mechanism in the objective that encourages class-wise (or region-wise) specialization. The current evidence and reasoning do not fully justify this claim. Could you either (a) provide stronger empirical evidence of class-level specialization (e.g., confusion matrices per member, per-class routing statistics, or analysis of member responsibility distributions), or (b) incorporate specialization more directly into the loss? For example, adding a term that biases members toward consistent responsibility for particular classes or regions of the input space, or otherwise regularizes the assignment to promote stable specialization.

4) Missing related work on diversity regularization. The manuscript does not discuss closely related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.). This line of work offers a more formal approach to promoting diversity among ensemble members, though it has been less explored in deep settings. Please position your method relative to these approaches: how does your per-example selection compare conceptually and empirically to explicit diversity regularization? Are they complementary (e.g., could diversity penalties be added to further encourage specialization), or does your approach subsume their benefits?

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarification of the test-time procedure and clearer baseline definitions. The idea is appealing and empirically promising, but the claims about specialization and the connection to prior diversity-encouraging methods should be better supported and contextualized.
