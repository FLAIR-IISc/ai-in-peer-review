This paper proposes a simple and effective solution to the multiple choice learning (MCL) problem in the context of deep learning. Existing approaches to MCL do not readily extend to deep networks due to the computational cost of retraining. This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method that leverages a “winner-takes-gradient” approach during backpropagation to concurrently train an ensemble while addressing the assignment problem. The authors demonstrate strong performance across diverse tasks – image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning – suggesting broad applicability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Importance:** The problem of efficiently training diverse ensembles for scenarios requiring multiple plausible hypotheses is highly relevant to the NIPS community, particularly given the increasing deployment of AI systems in real-world applications with downstream oracle mechanisms (e.g., human-in-the-loop systems).
*   **Simplicity:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is remarkably simple to implement, requiring only a minor modification to standard stochastic gradient descent training procedures. This ease of adoption is a significant advantage.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents compelling experimental results across a diverse set of tasks and network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNNs), demonstrating the effectiveness and generalizability of sMCL. The inclusion of these diverse experiments strengthens the claims made by the authors.
* **Parameter-free:** The approach does not introduce any new hyperparameters, simplifying the application of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Diversity Mechanism:** While the paper extensively motivates the importance of diversity, the proposed method does not explicitly enforce it. The diversity emerges as a consequence of minimizing oracle loss, rather than being directly encouraged. This distinction is important and might be perceived as a missed opportunity. The title’s emphasis on "diverse" ensembles sets an expectation of a diversity-inducing mechanism that isn’t fully met.
*   **Incremental Advance:** The work builds on prior work by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9] by proposing a stochastic approximation. While valuable, the advancement is relatively incremental compared to the original formulation of MCL. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how sMCL addresses the limitations of previous approaches beyond simply avoiding retraining.
* **Lack of theoretical analysis:** The paper lacks a formal theoretical analysis of why the proposed method leads to diversity, or a more in-depth exploration of the properties of the oracle loss landscape.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, some minor improvements could enhance clarity:

*   The first sentence of the abstract is somewhat awkward and could be rephrased for greater impact.
*   The emphasis on "diversity" could be toned down to better reflect the method’s reliance on oracle loss minimization as the primary driver of ensemble behavior.
*   Minor typographical errors (e.g., line 108, “f” should be “g”, extra period on line 115) should be addressed.

**Originality:**

The originality of this work lies in the application of a stochastic gradient descent-based approach to training ensembles specifically for the MCL paradigm, circumventing the computational limitations of previous iterative methods. While the core idea of minimizing oracle loss isn’t novel, the practical implementation and demonstration of its effectiveness in deep learning settings represent a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**

The results presented in this paper are significant. The ability to train diverse deep ensembles efficiently has practical implications for a variety of applications, including those requiring multiple plausible hypotheses or uncertainty quantification. The demonstrated performance gains across multiple tasks suggest that sMCL has the potential to be adopted by researchers and practitioners in the field. The method addresses a challenging problem in a pragmatic and effective manner.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in deep learning.
*   The proposed method is simple, efficient, and easy to implement.
*   The experimental results demonstrate strong performance across diverse tasks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The method does not explicitly enforce diversity, despite the strong emphasis on it in the paper.
*   The advancement is somewhat incremental, building on existing work in MCL.
*   A theoretical analysis of the method's properties is lacking.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field and warrants acceptance to NIPS 2016. While the lack of explicit diversity enforcement and incremental nature of the advance are weaknesses, the simplicity, effectiveness, and broad applicability of the proposed method outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and to moderate the emphasis on diversity.

**Question to the Authors:**

For the baseline MCL implementation (Guzman et al.), how did you ensure that each ensemble member converged to a reasonable solution before proceeding to the next iteration? Early stopping might significantly impact ensemble performance, and a clear description of the convergence criteria would be helpful.
