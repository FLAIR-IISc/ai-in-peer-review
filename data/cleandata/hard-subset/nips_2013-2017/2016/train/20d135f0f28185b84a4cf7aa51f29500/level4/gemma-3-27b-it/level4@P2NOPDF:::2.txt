This paper introduces a novel method for training ensembles of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), and more generally, any gradient-based model. The core idea is to leverage an oracle – which identifies the best-performing network within the ensemble for each individual training instance – and apply backpropagation only to that selected network. This approach encourages specialization among the networks, leading to an ensemble that effectively covers a diverse range of possible outputs. The authors demonstrate strong performance across several domains.

I found this to be a well-written and compelling paper presenting an interesting approach to DNN ensemble training. The use of an oracle to guide backpropagation to the network producing the most accurate output for a given instance demonstrably promotes specialization, particularly in a classification setting. The paper is clearly presented and supported by experiments conducted on multiple datasets. Overall, this is a strong contribution that warrants publication.

However, the paper’s potential impact could be significantly increased by further investigation into the practical utilization of the trained ensemble. Currently, the work focuses on the *training* process with oracle guidance, but lacks exploration of how to effectively *use* the resulting ensemble in inference. While the authors mention the potential for a broad range of outputs and its alignment with top-k metrics (such as those used in ImageNet), a more detailed analysis of ensemble combination strategies or inference-time behavior is missing. Addressing this would make the proposed approach more readily applicable in real-world scenarios. This missing aspect, while important, appears to be relatively problem-specific and doesn't detract fundamentally from the value of the current work.

Finally, a minor point regarding presentation: the pseudo-code in Figure 2 is presented as raster images embedded within the paper. This limits readability and scalability. I recommend including the pseudo-code directly within the paper text or, at a minimum, embedding it as a vector graphic for improved clarity and resolution.
