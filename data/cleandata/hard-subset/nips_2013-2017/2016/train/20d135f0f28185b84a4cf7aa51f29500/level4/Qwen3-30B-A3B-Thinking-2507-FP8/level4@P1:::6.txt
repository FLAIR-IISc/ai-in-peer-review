

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple high-quality predictions rather than a single output. The authors frame this as a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem where the goal is to minimize the oracle loss—defined as the minimum loss among M predictions—rather than the standard per-prediction loss. The key insight is that during training, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for a given example receives gradient updates, enabling efficient specialization without the need for costly retraining or sequential model training as in previous MCL approaches.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it generalizes the MCL paradigm to deep networks, which previous work (Guzman-Rivera et al., 2016) had not addressed due to computational constraints. Second, it proposes a simple yet effective training algorithm (sMCL) that operates within standard stochastic gradient descent frameworks, making it applicable to any deep learning architecture without requiring architectural modifications. Third, it demonstrates broad applicability across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks, showing consistent improvements over baselines including classical ensembles and the previous MCL approach.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The technical approach is elegant in its simplicity: by restricting gradient updates to the "winning" ensemble member for each example, the method naturally encourages specialization while avoiding the computational overhead of existing MCL methods. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, showing significant improvements (6-10% relative gains) in oracle metrics across multiple tasks and architectures. The analysis of specialization—where ensemble members develop expertise in different output classes or semantic regions—provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The paper also clearly articulates the practical benefits of diverse predictions in real-world systems, particularly for handling ambiguity in perception tasks.

However, the review identifies several weaknesses that need addressing before acceptance. First, the testing phase description is incomplete. The paper states that during inference, "the oracle selects the best prediction" (line 159), but doesn't specify how this is implemented. Does the system require ground truth labels during evaluation, or is there an oracle mechanism that can operate without them? Clarification is needed on how this would work in a real deployment where ground truth is unavailable.

Second, the baseline "independent ensembles" (denoted "Indp." in figures) is inadequately explained. The paper refers to this baseline multiple times but doesn't define it clearly in the methodology section. Is this simply an ensemble where each model is trained independently with different random initializations? If so, this should be explicitly stated, as it's a common baseline that requires clear definition.

Third, the claim of "specialization" requires stronger justification. The paper shows that ensemble members become specialized to different classes or visual concepts, but the training procedure updates only the lowest-loss member per example without any explicit regularization for diversity. This makes the specialization a consequence of the training dynamics rather than a designed property. The authors should address whether this specialization emerges naturally or could be reinforced through additional mechanisms. Furthermore, the paper doesn't discuss how to incorporate explicit diversity regularization techniques that have been studied in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al., 2016), which could provide theoretical grounding for the observed specialization.

The paper's significance is high as it addresses a practical need in perception systems where multiple hypotheses are valuable for downstream evaluation. The method is simple, efficient (5x faster than MCL), and broadly applicable across architectures. The authors make a strong case for why this approach is superior to existing methods, particularly for structured prediction tasks where ambiguity is common.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with thorough experiments across multiple domains. The methodology is well-described and reproducible, and the results are well-supported by data. The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some sections (particularly the baseline explanation and testing phase) could benefit from additional detail.

The originality is moderate to high. While the MCL framework was previously introduced (Guzman-Rivera et al., 2016), the authors' adaptation to deep networks using a stochastic gradient approach is novel. The paper properly distinguishes its contribution from previous work, particularly in noting that sMCL avoids the retraining overhead of prior methods.

Given these considerations, the paper makes a valuable contribution to ensemble learning for deep networks, but requires clarification on the testing procedure, baseline definition, and the theoretical basis for specialization. The weaknesses are not fundamental flaws but need addressing for the paper to meet NIPS standards. I recommend weak acceptance with the understanding that the authors will address these points in the camera-ready version.

Pro: Simple, effective method that generalizes MCL to deep networks; significant experimental improvements across multiple tasks; clear demonstration of specialization; broad applicability; efficient training.

Con: Incomplete testing phase description; insufficient baseline definition; insufficient theoretical justification for specialization; omission of related diversity regularization work.
