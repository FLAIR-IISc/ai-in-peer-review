This paper introduces a novel method for training ensembles of Deep Neural Networks (or other gradient-based models). The core idea is to employ an oracle during training that, for each data instance, identifies the best-performing member of the ensemble with respect to the ground truth label. The backpropagation update is then applied exclusively to this selected network.

A key advantage of this approach is that it encourages the individual networks to specialize, resulting in an ensemble that provides broad coverage of the output space. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method with strong empirical results across a variety of domains. The paper is well-written, the core idea is compelling, and the experimental validation is thorough. Overall, this is a strong contribution that merits publication.

My main reservation, which prevents me from rating this paper as outstanding, concerns the practical application of the trained ensemble at inference time. While the oracle-guided training procedure is well-defined, the paper does not adequately address how to effectively utilize the resulting specialized ensemble without access to the oracle. The discussion is limited to noting that the ensemble provides a diverse pool of outputs and that the method is well-suited for top-k evaluation metrics (e.g., in ImageNet). This omission is a significant limitation for the method's practical deployment. Although the contribution to training methodology is valuable on its own and the optimal inference strategy may be application-dependent, a more thorough treatment of this aspect would have substantially strengthened the work.
