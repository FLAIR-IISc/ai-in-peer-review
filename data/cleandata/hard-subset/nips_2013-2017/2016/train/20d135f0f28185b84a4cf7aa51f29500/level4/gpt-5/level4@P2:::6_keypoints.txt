- Introduces sMCL: a simple SGD winner-take-gradient method for training deep ensembles to optimize oracle loss without retraining.  
- Concurrent, stochastic alternative to MCL that is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and promotes emergent specialization.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR10, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning; shows consistent oracle gains and large speedups vs MCL.  

Strengths:
- Core idea is technically sound, easy to implement, and stable across tasks.  
- Empirical results are broad and show clear oracle improvements over strong baselines.  
- Presentation is clear with informative visualizations of member specialization.  
- Originality: practical stochastic concurrent training distinct from prior MCL/retraining schemes.  
- Significance: likely to be adopted due to simplicity, generality, and practical gains; qualitative analyses are compelling.  

Weaknesses / clarifications needed:
- Test-time protocol unclear: need explicit description of how M predictions are generated and scored to compute oracle metrics.  
- Baseline definition ambiguous: independent ensemble training budgets, hyperparameters, seeds, and wall-clock/FLOPs parity with sMCL need specification.  
- Specialization claims are observational; require stronger quantitative evidence (confusion matrices, per-class analyses, seed stability).  
- Missing discussion of prior diversity-regularization literature (e.g., negative correlation learning and recent diversity penalties).  
- Captioning comparisons may be unfair: report sMCL+beam, clarify CNN sharing/freeze/fine-tune and memory impacts on diversity.  
- Optimization details lacking: tie-breaking, sensitivity to batch size/learning rate, convergence behavior, and k-winner ablation need reporting.  
- Downstream impact not demonstrated: add a simple reranker or selector to show end-to-end gains beyond oracle metrics.  

Evaluation vs NIPS criteria:
- Quality: solid with convincing evidence but missing key experimental and protocol details.  
- Clarity: generally clear but needs explicit baseline/test-time definitions and minor pseudocode additions.  
- Originality: moderately high—practical and timely variant of MCL for deep ensembles.  
- Significance: high practical significance given simplicity and consistent oracle gains.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general, parameter-free method targeting oracle loss.  
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines across tasks.  
- Significant training speedups over MCL.  
- Emergent, interpretable ensemble diversity.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Unclear test-time evaluation and baseline definitions hinder reproducibility/fairness.  
- Specialization evidence is limited and mostly qualitative.  
- Under-citation of diversity-promoting methods in related work.  
- No demonstration of real downstream selection performance beyond oracle metrics.  

Questions for authors:
- How exactly are oracle metrics computed at test time; is any ground truth used for selection?  
- How are independent ensembles trained (data shuffling, seeds, budgets); are FLOPs/wall-clock comparable?  
- How are ties handled during training and how sensitive are results to batch size and learning rate?  
- Can you report sMCL+beam for captioning and clarify encoder sharing/fine-tuning choices?  
- Have you measured stability across seeds and variance in specialization patterns?  
- Can you compare to diversity-regularized baselines (e.g., negative correlation learning) or add a diversity penalty variant?  

Recommendation: Weak Accept — practical, broadly applicable, and empirically strong, pending clarifications and stronger quantitative analyses.
