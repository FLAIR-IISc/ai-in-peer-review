## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please Replace*]

This paper presents a novel approach to generating multiple outputs by jointly training an ensemble of deep networks. The core idea is to minimize the oracle loss, a particularly relevant objective when considering interactive applications where user feedback is incorporated. The authors effectively integrate deep networks into the Mixture of Conditional Likelihoods (MCL) paradigm and introduce a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method that allows for end-to-end training within a standard backpropagation framework. The experimental results on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The strength of this work lies in its elegant combination of MCL with deep learning, providing a principled way to learn diverse and accurate ensembles. The ability to train the entire model end-to-end is a significant advantage, simplifying the training pipeline compared to methods requiring separate training stages. The presented results clearly indicate an improvement over existing approaches on the evaluated tasks.

However, several points require further clarification and address. A primary concern revolves around the sensitivity of the method to initialization. The paper currently relies on initializing the ensemble members with pre-trained networks. It is unclear how performance would degrade with random initialization, and exploring this sensitivity is crucial for understanding the practical applicability of the approach. Would the benefits observed here still hold without pre-training, or is it a necessary component for successful learning?

Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for comprehensive evaluation. The paper does not adequately describe the comparative setting. Specifically, do baseline methods also utilize the same pre-trained network for initialization as sMCL? Providing this information is critical to ensure a fair and interpretable comparison. Without it, it’s difficult to isolate the contribution of the proposed optimization method and the joint training scheme.

The evaluation focuses solely on oracle accuracy. While important, this metric provides an incomplete picture of ensemble quality. A key benefit of ensembles is their ability to reduce variance and improve robustness. Therefore, analyzing the *diversity* of the ensemble members is essential. Metrics such as average pairwise disagreement or the variance of predictions across the ensemble should be included to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method beyond simply achieving high accuracy. Without this analysis, it's unclear whether the performance gains are due to genuine ensemble diversity or simply due to the optimization converging to a highly confident (but potentially brittle) solution.

Finally, the authors should consider related work more thoroughly. In particular, the paper “Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks” (Lee et al., 2015) presents a conceptually similar approach focused on promoting diversity during training. A comparison to this work, detailing the differences in methodology and results, would strengthen the paper and better position the contribution within the broader research landscape. Addressing how this work differs from, and potentially improves upon, Lee et al. is essential.

In summary, this paper presents a promising approach to learning diverse ensembles for interactive applications. The end-to-end training framework and experimental results are encouraging. However, addressing the concerns regarding initialization sensitivity, experimental details, diversity analysis, and related work is crucial to elevate the paper to a higher level of confidence and impact. I recommend further investigation and clarification in these areas before acceptance.
