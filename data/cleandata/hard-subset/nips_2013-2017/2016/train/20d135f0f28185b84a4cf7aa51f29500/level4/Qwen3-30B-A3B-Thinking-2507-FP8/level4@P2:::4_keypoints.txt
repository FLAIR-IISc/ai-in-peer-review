- Introduces sMCL, a stochastic method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly optimizes oracle loss by backpropagating only to the lowest-error predictor per example.
- Frames the problem as minimizing oracle-selected loss over an ensemble to generate multiple high-quality predictions.
- Generalizes Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) to deep networks, removing the costly retraining requirement of prior MCL work.
- Algorithm is simple, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and enables concurrent training of ensemble members.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over classical ensembles and prior MCL on classification, segmentation, and image captioning.
- Strength: clear problem formulation addressing practical need for multiple hypotheses in real-world systems.
- Strength: avoids computational burden of prior methods with a straightforward stochastic optimization strategy.
- Strength: comprehensive empirical validation across multiple domains.
- Strength: provides analysis showing natural emergence of specialization among ensemble members.
- Weakness: Section 3 (technical contribution) is underdeveloped and too brief for a central contribution.
- Weakness: Fails to sufficiently distinguish sMCL from standard ensemble training methods that may implicitly encourage diversity.
- Weakness: Writing is dense with insufficient explanation and weak connection between oracle loss motivation and practical applications.
- Weakness: Does not adequately compare to other multi-solution methods ([5, 9, 23, 24]) or recent deep-architecture approaches.
- Weakness: Reported performance gains (6–10%) over strong baselines are modest, limiting perceived significance.
- Experimental suggestions: compare with more recent strong baselines (e.g., modern segmentation methods for Pascal VOC 2012).
- Experimental suggestions: include ablation studies to show oracle loss directly causes specialization.
- Experimental suggestions: justify the choice of ensemble size M=4 across tasks.
- Experimental suggestions: provide deeper analysis of when and why sMCL outperforms baselines.
- Needs a more nuanced discussion of trade-offs between oracle performance improvements and computational efficiency (e.g., is 5× speedup worth marginal gains).
- Overall: meaningful but incremental contribution—adaptation of MCL to deep learning is valuable, but technical presentation and comparative experiments need strengthening.
- Recommendation: suitable for acceptance with minor revisions focused on technical detail, clearer comparisons, and stronger experimental analysis.
