The paper introduces a method for training ensembles of deep neural networks (or any SGD-based models) by using an oracle during training that, for each training example, selects the ensemble member with the lowest loss; gradients are then backpropagated only through that selected model. This induces automatic specialization among members, yielding ensembles that collectively cover a broad set of plausible outputs. The approach is demonstrated across multiple domains and delivers strong empirical performance.

Overall, this is a solid paper. The central idea—oracle-guided training that directs gradient updates to the best-performing member relative to the ground truth—is clearly presented and compelling. It appears to promote specialization effectively, especially in classification. The writing is clear, and the experiments span several application areas. In sum, a strong submission that merits acceptance.

I would consider this work outstanding if it also addressed how to exploit the trained ensemble at test time. As it stands, training relies on an oracle, but practical deployment and selection without an oracle are not developed beyond noting that the method can provide a diverse set of hypotheses and aligns with top-k evaluation (e.g., on ImageNet). This is a key missing piece for making the approach fully practical. That said, the core contribution is substantial, and the missing part is likely problem-specific.
