

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm for training ensemble models, extending the framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], where the ensemble loss is defined as the loss of the single best-performing classifier within the ensemble. The key contribution lies in adapting this approach for deep neural networks via SGD, enabling efficient training on large-scale datasets—a significant practical advancement over the original work, which relied on full-batch optimization. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and effectively demonstrates the algorithm's efficacy on standard benchmarks, showing competitive performance with state-of-the-art ensemble methods.

The primary strength of the work is its practical relevance. By leveraging SGD, the authors successfully bridge a critical gap: the original [8] framework was computationally prohibitive for deep learning due to its reliance on full-batch optimization. The proposed method not only scales efficiently but also maintains the diversity-promoting property of the ensemble, which is crucial for robust generalization. The experimental results convincingly show that the approach achieves comparable or superior accuracy to baselines while reducing training time, making it a valuable tool for practitioners.

However, the novelty claim requires careful scrutiny. While the adaptation of [8] to SGD is non-trivial and enables deep learning applications, the core ensemble loss formulation remains unchanged from [8]. The authors persuasively argue that this modification—though seemingly small—substantially broadens the method's applicability, particularly for modern deep architectures. Given the lack of prior SGD-based ensemble training for this specific loss formulation, I consider the contribution to be meaningful and not merely incremental. The paper’s emphasis on scalability and empirical validation strengthens this case, and I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions.

Two key areas warrant deeper exploration to bolster the paper’s impact. First, the authors should compare their method against MCL (Multi-Class Loss) [8] under *mini-batch training*—a direct test of whether SGD alone drives the performance gains or if the ensemble mechanism is the primary factor. The current experiments use full-batch MCL, making it unclear whether the observed improvements stem from the SGD adaptation or the ensemble design. A fairer comparison would evaluate MCL with batch processing to isolate the effect of the proposed SGD adaptation.

Second, the paper lacks quantitative analysis of the claimed "diversity" effect. While the ensemble loss inherently encourages diversity, the work does not measure how classifier diversity correlates with performance gains. The authors should include diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise disagreement, entropy-based measures) and analyze their relationship with test accuracy across different model configurations. For instance, does higher diversity consistently lead to better generalization, or are there diminishing returns? Such analysis would validate the core premise of the approach and provide actionable insights for practitioners.

In summary, this paper presents a timely and well-executed extension of ensemble learning for deep networks. The SGD adaptation is a pragmatic and impactful step forward, and the empirical results are robust. Addressing the suggested comparisons and diversity analysis would significantly enhance the paper’s rigor and contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the novelty argument and expand the experimental validation. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and offers a practical solution for scalable ensemble training in deep learning.
