- Introduces sMCL: a "winner-take-gradient" multiple-choice learning approach that updates only the lowest-loss ensemble member per example.  
- Frames ensemble learning as multiple-choice optimization to train diverse deep ensembles without costly retraining.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness across image classification, segmentation, and captioning, with competitive or superior empirical results.  
- Shows emergent specialization of ensemble members, suggesting interpretability and robustness benefits.  
- Novel contribution: directly optimizes oracle loss with a computationally efficient method.  
- Practical: simple to implement, parameter-free, and applicable to many architectures and loss functions.  
- Strong empirical performance across diverse tasks and baselines.  
- Writing is generally clear and explains core ideas effectively.  
- Testing-phase description is unclear; inference procedure (how ensemble predictions are produced) needs explicit explanation.  
- Baseline definitions (“independent” and “regular” ensembles) are not clearly specified and require concise clarification.  
- Claim of specialization is insufficiently justified; needs stronger evidence or mechanisms to encourage/measure specialization.  
- Related work omission: lacks discussion of diversity-regularization techniques in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al. and similar work).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses practical ensemble training cost, offers a simple effective solution, exhibits promising specialization, and is broadly applicable.  
- Arguments for rejection: incomplete related-work comparison, weak justification of specialization, and unclear testing/inference details.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept if authors clarify inference and baselines, cite and discuss diversity-regularization work, and strengthen specialization justification.
