Summary:
The paper proposes a simple approach to multiple-choice learning (MCL) with deep networks. Existing MCL formulations do not directly extend to deep architectures; the authors introduce a winner-takes-the-gradient mechanism during backpropagation to jointly handle learner training and assignment within an ensemble. Empirical results indicate strong performance across diverse tasks, including image classification, image segmentation, and image captioning.

Strengths:
- The problem of MCL for deep learning is important and relevant to the NIPS community.
- The method is appealing in its simplicity.
- Experiments cover three distinct tasks.

Weaknesses:
- My primary concern is the strong emphasis on “diversity” (including in the title) without any explicit mechanism to enforce diversity. I expected a concrete diversity term or constraint in the model and was disappointed to find none.
- The contribution appears incremental relative to the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al.

Minor suggestions:
- The first sentence of the abstract should be rewritten for clarity.
- The emphasis on “diversity” should be toned down given that it is not explicitly enforced.
- Line 108: the first “f” should be “g” in “we fixed the form of …”
- Line 115: there is an extra period in the middle of a sentence.

Question:
- For the baseline MCL with deep learning, how did the authors ensure that each network converged to a reasonable solution? Prematurely cutting the learners could significantly affect the ensemble’s performance.

Overall:
The paper addresses a timely and relevant problem with a commendably simple technique and demonstrates results across multiple tasks. However, the heavy reliance on “diversity” in the framing is not matched by an explicit diversity mechanism, and the contribution feels incremental relative to prior relaxation-based approaches.
