This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality outputs. The core idea is to directly minimize the oracle loss, where the objective is to ensure that at least one of the M predictions made by the ensemble is correct. This is particularly relevant for applications with a human-in-the-loop or a downstream verification component that can select the best hypothesis from a given set.

The proposed sMCL method extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm to deep networks by formulating the optimization as a form of stochastic block gradient descent. In practice, this translates to a simple and efficient "winner-take-gradient" strategy: for each training example, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that produced the lowest error. This approach allows all models in the ensemble to be trained concurrently and in an end-to-end fashion. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct tasks: image classification (CNNs on CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (FCNs on Pascal VOC), and image captioning (CNN+RNN on MSCOCO). The results show that sMCL consistently outperforms standard ensembles and other relevant baselines in terms of oracle performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by adapting the MCL framework for modern deep learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is an elegant and practical solution to the challenge of training diverse ensembles, overcoming the prohibitive computational cost of previous iterative methods like the original MCL, which required full retraining of models in each iteration.

2.  **Generality and Simplicity:** The sMCL method is impressively general. It is agnostic to the network architecture and the specific loss function, making it widely applicable. Its implementation is straightforward, requiring only a minor modification to the standard backpropagation process, which is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering a diverse set of tasks and architectures. The consistent and significant improvements in oracle performance over baselines—including independently trained ensembles, the original MCL, and a boosting-like approach—strongly support the effectiveness of the proposed method. The 5x speedup over MCL is a particularly compelling result.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The qualitative analysis provides interesting insights into the emergent behavior of the ensembles. The paper clearly demonstrates how sMCL encourages specialization among network members in a task-dependent manner, such as specializing on specific object classes in classification or on different plausible interpretations in segmentation and captioning.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarity on Initialization:** The role of initialization in the model's performance is not fully explored. For the semantic segmentation experiments, the sMCL ensemble was initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. It is unclear how critical this initialization strategy is. Would the method perform as well if trained from a random initialization? Clarifying this point would help in understanding the robustness and general applicability of sMCL.

2.  **Details for Baseline Comparisons:** The paper could provide more details to ensure the fairness of the experimental comparisons. For instance, in the segmentation task, was the same pre-trained network used to initialize the baseline methods (e.g., standard ensemble, MCL)? Using identical initialization strategies across all compared methods is crucial for drawing definitive conclusions about their relative performance.

3.  **Quantitative Diversity Analysis:** While the primary goal is to optimize oracle performance, the underlying mechanism is to generate diversity. The paper relies on qualitative examples to demonstrate this diversity. The review would be strengthened by a more quantitative analysis. Including metrics that explicitly measure the dissimilarity between the predictions of ensemble members (e.g., average pairwise distance in the output space for segmentation, or n-gram dissimilarity for captioning) would provide more objective evidence of the diversity encouraged by sMCL.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The authors should discuss and differentiate their work from the highly relevant contemporaneous paper by Lee et al. (2015), which also focuses on training diverse ensembles of deep networks. While the technical approach is different (Lee et al. introduce an explicit diversity-encouraging term in the loss function), the high-level goal is very similar. A discussion of the conceptual similarities and differences would better position the paper's contribution in the literature.
    *   Lee, S., Purushwalkam, S., Cogswell, M., Crandall, D. J., & Batra, D. (2015). Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks. *arXiv preprint arXiv:1511.06314*.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a simple, effective, and broadly applicable method for training diverse deep ensembles. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed through additional clarification and discussion in the final version.
