- Problem importance: Efficiently training diverse ensembles for multiple plausible hypotheses is highly relevant for real-world AI and NIPS community.
- Simplicity: sMCL is a minor SGD modification that is easy to implement and adopt.
- Empirical validation: Strong, diverse experiments (classification, segmentation, captioning) show effectiveness across architectures.
- Parameter-free: Method introduces no new hyperparameters, simplifying application.

- Limited diversity mechanism: The method does not explicitly enforce diversity; diversity arises indirectly via oracle loss minimization.
- Incremental advance: sMCL is a stochastic approximation of prior MCL work and is a relatively incremental contribution.
- Lack of theoretical analysis: No formal explanation for why sMCL promotes diversity or analysis of the oracle loss landscape.

- Clarity — overall: Paper is generally well-written and organized.
- Clarity — abstract/title: First abstract sentence awkward; emphasis on “diversity” should be toned down to match method’s behavior.
- Clarity — typos: Minor typographical errors noted (e.g., line 108, line 115).

- Originality: Novel application of SGD-based stochastic training to MCL for deep networks; practical implementation is valuable even if core idea builds on prior work.
- Significance: Results are significant with practical implications for multi-hypothesis and uncertainty-aware systems.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important problem, simple and efficient method, strong empirical results, clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: Does not explicitly enforce diversity, is incremental over prior MCL, and lacks theoretical grounding.

- Overall assessment: Solid contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarity and moderation of diversity claims.
- Question to authors: For the baseline MCL (Guzman et al.) implementation, what convergence criteria or early-stopping protocol ensured each ensemble member reached a reasonable solution before the next iteration?
