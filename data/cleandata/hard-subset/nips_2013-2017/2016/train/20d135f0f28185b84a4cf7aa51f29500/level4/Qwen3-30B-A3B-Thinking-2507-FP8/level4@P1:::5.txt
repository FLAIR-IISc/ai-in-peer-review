

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training deep neural network ensembles to produce multiple diverse predictions that minimize oracle loss. The authors frame the problem as training an ensemble of predictors to collectively provide a set of outputs from which an oracle can select the best solution, rather than producing a single prediction. The key insight is that by applying a "winner-take-gradient" strategy during training—where only the lowest-loss ensemble member receives gradient updates per example—the method can jointly optimize all ensemble members in a single pass, avoiding the costly retraining required by prior work. The authors demonstrate sMCL's effectiveness across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing significant improvements over classical ensembles and the MCL baseline while being 5x faster.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm to deep networks with a practical training algorithm. Prior work by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9] formalized MCL but required iterative retraining of ensemble members, which is prohibitively expensive for deep networks. sMCL elegantly sidesteps this limitation by integrating directly into standard SGD training, making it applicable to modern deep architectures without architectural modifications. The authors also provide compelling evidence that sMCL induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members, with each network developing expertise in different aspects of the problem domain.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with strong technical content that aligns with NIPS's focus on advancing machine learning theory and practice. The experimental design is thorough, covering three distinct domains (classification, segmentation, captioning) with appropriate baselines. The oracle evaluation metric is well-justified and commonly used in this line of work, and the results consistently show sMCL outperforming baselines with significant gains (6-10% relative improvement) while maintaining computational efficiency.

However, I have several concerns that need clarification before I can fully endorse this work. First, the paper does not adequately address the impact of initialization on the resulting ensemble diversity. The authors state they initialize sMCL models from standard ensembles, but they do not clarify whether baseline methods used the same initialization strategy. When comparing to classical ensembles (which typically use different random seeds), it's unclear whether the performance gains are due to sMCL's training algorithm or simply better initialization. I recommend including ablation studies on initialization strategies to isolate the contribution of the proposed method.

Second, while the paper demonstrates superior oracle performance, it lacks sufficient analysis of the diversity of ensemble outputs. The authors claim that sMCL produces "interpretable representations of task ambiguity," but they provide minimal quantitative measures of diversity (e.g., pairwise dissimilarity between outputs, coverage of the solution space). Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess whether the improved oracle performance stems from genuine diversity or merely better selection of a single model's output.

Third, the authors overlook a recent related work that presents a very similar idea: Lee et al.'s "Why M Heads are Better than One" (CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015). This paper also proposes training multiple heads on a shared backbone network to produce diverse predictions, using a similar "winner-take-all" strategy during training. The authors should explicitly compare their approach to Lee et al., discussing both similarities and differences, and clarify how sMCL advances beyond this prior work.

Regarding the four review criteria:
- Quality: The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The method is technically correct, and the experimental results robustly support the claims. However, the missing initialization analysis and lack of diversity metrics weaken the evaluation.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The method is described with sufficient mathematical precision and the experiments are well-designed.
- Originality: The integration of MCL with deep networks via a practical SGD-based training algorithm is novel. While the core idea of multiple outputs isn't new, the specific optimization approach is a meaningful contribution.
- Significance: The method is broadly applicable to many perception tasks where multiple hypotheses are useful (e.g., autonomous systems, assistive technologies). The demonstrated improvements in oracle performance suggest practical value.

Pro arguments for acceptance: 
1. sMCL provides a practical solution to training diverse deep ensembles, avoiding the computational infeasibility of prior MCL approaches.
2. The method is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and parameter-free.
3. The results across three diverse tasks demonstrate broad applicability and consistent improvements.

Con arguments against acceptance:
1. The paper does not sufficiently address the impact of initialization on ensemble performance.
2. The lack of diversity metrics makes it difficult to assess whether the improved oracle performance stems from genuine diversity.
3. The omission of Lee et al.'s work (2015) represents a significant gap in related work.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution with clear practical value. With the authors addressing the initialization strategy, adding diversity metrics, and comparing to Lee et al.'s work, this paper would make a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper is well-written and technically sound but lacks sufficient analysis to fully validate the claimed benefits. I recommend rejection with the understanding that the authors could substantially strengthen the paper with minor revisions.
