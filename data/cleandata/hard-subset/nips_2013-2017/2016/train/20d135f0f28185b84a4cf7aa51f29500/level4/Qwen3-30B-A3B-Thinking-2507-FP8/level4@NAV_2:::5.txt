

The authors introduce a novel approach for generating diverse outputs by training deep network ensembles to minimize oracle loss, which is particularly valuable in scenarios requiring interaction with user or downstream evaluation mechanisms. The paper effectively extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework to deep networks, proposing a stochastic block gradient descent method (sMCL) that enables efficient, end-to-end training of diverse ensembles. The proposed method is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and parameter-free, as it directly integrates with standard backpropagation. The experimental evaluation across three distinct tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) convincingly demonstrates the superiority of sMCL over baseline methods, achieving significant improvements in oracle metrics while providing a 5x speedup over the MCL approach.

However, I have several concerns that should be addressed. First, the paper does not thoroughly discuss the impact of initialization strategies. It is unclear whether the method's performance is sensitive to the initial weights of the ensemble members, particularly when compared to using randomly initialized networks versus pre-trained models. The authors should clarify whether the baselines used the same initialization protocol as sMCL, as this could significantly affect the comparative results.

Second, while the paper evaluates performance using oracle accuracy, it lacks a detailed analysis of the diversity among ensemble members. The authors should provide additional metrics or qualitative analysis to demonstrate how the sMCL method specifically enhances diversity beyond what is achieved by standard ensemble methods. This is particularly important given the paper's focus on producing diverse, interpretable solutions.

Third, the authors should compare their work with the recent paper by Lee et al. ("Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks," CoRR abs/1511.06314, 2015), which proposes a similar approach for training diverse deep ensembles. This comparison is crucial for establishing the novelty and significance of the proposed method relative to existing work.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to training diverse deep ensembles with strong empirical results. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the contribution and clarity of the work.
