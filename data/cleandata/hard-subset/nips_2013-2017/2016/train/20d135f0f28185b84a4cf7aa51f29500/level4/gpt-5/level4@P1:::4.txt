Summary
The paper proposes a stochastic gradient descent variant of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. The method is applied to several computer vision tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—and the authors claim advantages across these settings. Conceptually, this extends prior MCL work [8] to modern neural networks by interleaving assignment and training within SGD.

Relation to prior work
The motivation for producing multiple plausible outputs is clear and well-established in MCL and related “M-best/diverse” literature. However, the core contribution here appears to be a neural-network instantiation of [8] with relatively minor adjustments, rather than a fundamentally new learning paradigm.

Strengths
- The paper targets an important and practical problem: training ensembles that produce diverse outputs optimized for oracle selection.
- The approach is simple and is evaluated across multiple tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning), which helps assess generality.

Weaknesses
1) Incremental novelty: The main idea is essentially a neural-network version of MCL [8]. While the motivation is solid, it is not unique to this work and the technical delta over [8] seems small.
2) Clarity and technical depth: The paper is not well written, and the key technical section (Section 3) is only about one page. It is unclear how the proposed framework differs from standard alternating training strategies widely used when training multiple tasks or components together. The paper needs a precise comparison, both conceptually and algorithmically, to established alternating/block-coordinate training procedures.
3) Experimental evaluation: Although the method is tested on several tasks, the baselines are not strong enough and the reported gains are small. To convincingly demonstrate efficiency and competitiveness, stronger baselines are needed. In particular, for semantic segmentation, evaluation on PASCAL VOC 2012 with comparisons to stronger methods would make the case more compelling.

Quality
The idea is plausible, but the paper provides limited technical detail and insufficient analysis contrasting the proposed method with alternative/alternating training. Without stronger baselines and clearer ablations, it is hard to assess the robustness and practical gains.

Clarity
The presentation needs significant improvement. The core method is under-explained, and the differences from existing alternating or block-coordinate approaches are not clearly articulated. Expanding Section 3 with formal derivations, algorithmic details, and training dynamics would help.

Originality
The contribution appears incremental: essentially adapting MCL [8] to deep networks via a “winner-take-gradient” rule within SGD. The paper does not convincingly establish novelty beyond this adaptation.

Significance
Given the small improvements and lack of strong baselines, the impact is uncertain. Demonstrating clear, consistent benefits over competitive baselines on standard benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012 for segmentation) is necessary to establish significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Elaborate the technical section: provide detailed derivations, clarify how the method differs from standard alternating training, and explain any theoretical or empirical advantages.
- Strengthen baselines across tasks, including stronger ensemble and structured prediction baselines.
- For segmentation, include experiments on PASCAL VOC 2012 and comparisons against stronger methods.
- Provide ablations (e.g., assignment strategies, tie-breaking, effect of ensemble size) and more detailed training analysis.

Pros
- Addresses a practical problem and evaluates across multiple tasks.
- Simple and potentially broadly applicable approach.

Cons
- Incremental over [8]; novelty is limited.
- Poor clarity and minimal technical exposition.
- Weak baselines and small performance gains.
- Missing evaluation on stronger benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012) for a convincing case.
