

The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple plausible outputs rather than a single prediction. This addresses a practical need in perception systems where downstream processes benefit from multiple hypotheses (e.g., autonomous vehicles or assistive technologies for visually impaired users). The authors formalize the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm for deep networks, introducing an SGD-based training algorithm that updates only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each example during training. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy enables automatic specialization of ensemble members without requiring costly retraining or sequential model training, distinguishing sMCL from prior work like Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL and Dey et al.'s boosting-based approach.

**Quality**: The technical contribution is well-executed, with thorough experimental validation across three diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) using multiple architectures. The method achieves consistent improvements over baselines (6-10% relative gains in oracle metrics) while being 5x faster than MCL. However, the theoretical justification for how per-example reassignment leads to meaningful specialization remains underdeveloped. The authors claim specialization emerges naturally, but don't provide evidence that this specialization correlates with meaningful task structure rather than random assignment. The connection to diversity regularization techniques in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al., "Diversity Regularization in Deep Learning," ICML 2015) is notably absent, despite being directly relevant to the problem of inducing ensemble diversity.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, with strong experimental results. The sMCL algorithm (Algorithm 2) is straightforward to implement and well-explained. However, the baseline "independent ensembles" (denoted "Indp." in figures) is inadequately defined - the paper describes it as "classical ensembles trained with different random initializations" but fails to clarify how these differ from standard bagging or other ensemble methods. The testing phase is also unclear: the authors mention using ground truth to identify the "best member" (line 159, page 5) but don't explain how this is implemented or why it's necessary for evaluation.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by applying Multiple Choice Learning to deep networks with a practical SGD-based approach, avoiding the retraining bottleneck of prior methods. This is a meaningful extension from Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL work, which required multiple meta-iterations of retraining. While the "winner-take-gradient" idea is simple, its application to deep networks is novel and well-motivated. However, the paper underutilizes the NIPS proceedings to establish novelty; it should more explicitly contrast with related work on ensemble diversity (e.g., "Ensemble Learning via Negative Correlation" by Liu and Yao, Neural Networks 2000).

**Significance**: The problem addressed has clear practical relevance for real-world perception systems where ambiguity is common. The method's simplicity, architecture agnosticism, and parameter-free nature make it broadly applicable. The results demonstrate that sMCL produces interpretable specializations (e.g., segmentation specialists for specific classes) that match human-like handling of ambiguity. The paper convincingly shows how sMCL outperforms existing methods across multiple domains, suggesting real potential for adoption in applications requiring multiple hypotheses.

**Strengths**:
- Novel application of Multiple Choice Learning to deep networks with a practical SGD-based algorithm
- Strong empirical results across three diverse tasks and architectures
- Simple implementation that's compatible with existing deep learning frameworks
- Clear demonstration of automatic specialization through interpretable examples
- Practical relevance for real-world perception systems with downstream oracles

**Weaknesses**:
- Insufficient theoretical justification for how per-example reassignment leads to meaningful specialization
- Inadequate definition and justification of the "independent ensembles" baseline
- Missing discussion of related work on diversity regularization in neural networks
- Ambiguous explanation of the testing phase and oracle evaluation
- Limited analysis of the trade-offs between ensemble size and performance gains

The paper makes a valuable contribution to ensemble learning for deep networks, addressing a practical need with a simple yet effective algorithm. While the technical execution is strong, the theoretical foundation and related work discussion require strengthening. The method's simplicity and effectiveness make it a promising direction for future work, particularly in applications where multiple hypotheses are beneficial. Given the solid experimental results, clear problem motivation, and practical implementation, I recommend weak acceptance. With minor revisions addressing the theoretical justification and related work, this could be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
