Summary:
The paper formulates a stochastic gradient descent–based Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) method and claims advantages across multiple tasks, including classification, segmentation, and captioning.

Main concerns:
1) Limited novelty: The sMCL method appears incremental in novelty. While the motivation behind MCL is clear, it does not constitute a contribution specific to this work. The proposed approach reads largely as a neural network (NN) version of [8] with some adjustments.
2) Clarity and technical depth: The paper is not well written. The core technical exposition (Section 3) spans only one page and lacks sufficient detail. It remains unclear how the proposed framework differs from alternative training, which is widely adopted when multiple tasks are trained together.
3) Experimental evaluation: Although the authors present experiments on several computer vision tasks (classification, image segmentation, and captioning), the evaluation lacks strong baselines, and the performance gains are small. To convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework, the authors should include experiments on PASCAL VOC 2012 and compare against stronger baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and rigorously differentiate the proposed method from existing approaches, particularly alternative training strategies commonly used in multi-task settings, and from [8], beyond being an NN instantiation.
- Substantially expand the technical section with precise algorithmic details, assumptions, and theoretical or empirical justifications for design choices.
- Strengthen the experimental section by adding comparisons to strong, state-of-the-art baselines and evaluating on standard benchmarks such as PASCAL VOC 2012 to better substantiate the claimed advantages.

Overall assessment:
This work’s contribution appears incremental relative to prior MCL formulations (notably [8]), the technical presentation is too brief to assess novelty and rigor, and the empirical gains are modest without strong baselines. Substantial clarification, deeper technical content, and stronger experimental comparisons are needed to support the paper’s claims.
