

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly optimizes for oracle loss. The authors pose the problem of generating multiple high-quality predictions as a learning task over an ensemble of networks, where the goal is to minimize the loss achieved by an oracle selecting the best prediction from the ensemble. The authors formalize this as a stochastic gradient descent approach that backpropagates gradients only to the lowest-error predictor for each example, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members without costly retraining. The approach is demonstrated across classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over classical ensembles and the prior MCL method.

The paper makes a significant contribution by generalizing the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8] to deep networks, addressing a key limitation of previous work that required costly retraining. While the core idea of minimizing oracle loss is not entirely novel, the authors successfully adapt it to deep learning through their stochastic optimization approach, which is agnostic to architecture and loss function. This is a meaningful contribution to the field of ensemble learning, particularly for applications where multiple plausible predictions are valuable.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. Clear problem formulation that addresses a practical need in real-world AI systems where multiple hypotheses are preferable to single predictions
2. A simple yet effective algorithm that avoids the computational burden of prior methods
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple domains showing consistent improvements
4. Insightful analysis of how specialization emerges naturally in ensemble members

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses:

Regarding Quality: The technical contribution in Section 3 is underdeveloped, occupying only one page despite being central to the paper. The authors fail to sufficiently distinguish their stochastic approach from standard ensemble training methods that might implicitly encourage diversity. The paper should better explain why standard approaches (like training multiple models with different initializations) don't achieve the same effect as their oracle-loss minimization. The experimental design is generally sound but could be strengthened with more thorough ablation studies.

Regarding Clarity: The writing is somewhat dense and technical, with insufficient explanation of key concepts. The motivation for the oracle loss formulation could be better connected to the practical applications described in the introduction. The paper would benefit from more detailed explanations of the stochastic optimization strategy and clearer comparisons with alternative training approaches.

Regarding Originality: While the core idea of MCL is not new [8], the authors successfully adapt it to deep learning in a way that overcomes the major limitation of prior work (retraining). However, the paper doesn't sufficiently contrast their approach with other methods that generate multiple solutions (e.g., [5, 9, 23, 24]), particularly those that might be more directly applicable to deep architectures. The claim of novelty is somewhat overstated given the existing MCL work.

Regarding Significance: The paper addresses a meaningful practical problem where multiple predictions are valuable (e.g., handling ambiguous inputs), but the significance is somewhat limited by the relatively modest performance gains (6-10%) over strong baselines. The approach is promising for applications requiring diverse predictions, but the paper doesn't sufficiently argue why this is a more important problem than the standard single-prediction paradigm.

The experiments are well-designed but would be strengthened by:
1. Comparing with more recent strong baselines for each task (e.g., modern segmentation methods for Pascal VOC 2012)
2. Including ablation studies showing how the oracle loss minimization directly leads to specialization
3. Better justification for the choice of M=4 ensemble size across different tasks
4. More detailed analysis of when and why the method excels compared to baselines

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between oracle performance and computational efficiency. While the 5x speedup over MCL is notable, the paper doesn't clearly establish whether this efficiency gain is worth the marginal performance improvements observed.

Overall, the paper presents a practical approach to training diverse deep ensembles that addresses a genuine limitation in prior work. While the novelty is somewhat incremental (adapting MCL to deep learning), the simplicity and effectiveness of the proposed method make it a valuable contribution to the ensemble learning literature. The technical contribution is sound but underdeveloped, and the experiments are generally convincing but could be strengthened with more thorough comparisons to recent baselines.

Pros: Practical problem formulation, simple yet effective algorithm, comprehensive empirical validation across multiple domains, insightful analysis of specialization emergence.

Cons: Underdeveloped technical contribution, limited comparison to recent strong baselines, relatively modest performance gains, insufficient distinction from standard ensemble training approaches.

The paper would benefit from a more focused contribution statement and stronger justification for why the proposed method is necessary rather than just another ensemble approach. With minor revisions to strengthen the technical presentation and experimental comparisons, this could be a valuable contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper makes a meaningful but incremental contribution to ensemble learning that would be suitable for acceptance with minor revisions.
