This paper proposes a novel ensemble training algorithm for deep neural networks. A key distinction of this method is that, for each training example, only a single member of the ensemble is updated. This contrasts with traditional ensemble methods, where multiple or all members are typically trained on each example. Specifically, the algorithm identifies the ensemble member with the lowest loss for a given training example and updates only that member's parameters via backpropagation. Overall, this is a simple yet elegant idea that yields surprisingly strong experimental results and addresses some deficiencies of existing approaches. For these reasons, my recommendation is for weak acceptance, pending clarification on the following points.

1.  **Clarity of the Testing Phase:** The procedure for inference (the testing phase) is not clearly explained. The training procedure selects the best-performing model for an example using the ground-truth label. How is this selection performed at test time when the ground-truth label is unavailable? Line 159 on page 5 seems to suggest a similar mechanism, which would be a significant issue. The authors must clarify how ensemble predictions are aggregated during inference.

2.  **Undefined Baseline:** The paper repeatedly refers to a baseline called 'independent ensembles' or 'regular ensembles' (e.g., denoted as 'Indp.' in the figures and mentioned on page 5, line 181). However, I was unable to find a clear definition or explanation of how this baseline is constructed. Is this a standard ensemble of independently trained models? This should be explicitly defined for the experimental comparison to be meaningful.

3.  **Justification for Specialization:** The authors claim that the proposed method encourages ensemble members to 'specialize' on different output classes. However, the mechanism for selecting which member to update operates on a per-example basis, not a per-class basis. Therefore, the connection between the training procedure and class specialization is not well-justified. It is unclear why a model that is best for one example of a class would necessarily be best for other examples of the same class. To make this claim stronger, the authors could provide an analysis of the distribution of updates per class for each member, or perhaps discuss incorporating an explicit specialization term into the loss function.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** Related to the point on specialization, the paper fails to discuss the closely related literature on promoting diversity in ensembles through regularization. Techniques like diversity regularization (e.g., Chen et al.) explicitly encourage members to be different. While these methods may not have been extensively studied in the context of modern deep learning, they represent a more direct and rigorous approach to achieving the kind of specialization the authors claim their method produces. A discussion of this work and a comparison of the conceptual differences would strengthen the paper.
