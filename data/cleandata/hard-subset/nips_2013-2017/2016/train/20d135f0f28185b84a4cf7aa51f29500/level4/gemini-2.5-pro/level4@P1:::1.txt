This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep network ensembles. The goal is to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input, which is particularly useful in applications where a human or another system can select the best option from a list of hypotheses (the "oracle" setting). The work builds directly on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which optimizes for the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble. The key limitation of the original MCL is its use of a block coordinate descent algorithm that requires retraining models to convergence, making it infeasible for deep learning. This paper's primary contribution is to adapt this idea to the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) setting. In sMCL, for each example in a mini-batch, the gradient is backpropagated only through the single ensemble member that produced the lowest loss. This simple "winner-take-gradient" strategy allows for efficient, parallel training of all ensemble members. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing that it outperforms standard ensembles and other baselines while being significantly faster than the original MCL.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Practical Significance:** The main strength of this work is that it takes a conceptually appealing but practically infeasible algorithm (MCL) and makes it applicable to the deep learning paradigm. The modification from a full-batch, iterative retraining scheme to a per-batch stochastic update is a crucial step that enables the training of diverse deep ensembles. The reported 5x speedup over a limited version of MCL underscores this practical advantage.

2.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple to understand and implement. It is parameter-free and agnostic to the network architecture and the specific loss function, making it a broadly applicable tool. The paper convincingly demonstrates this generality by applying the exact same algorithm to CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNN models for classification, segmentation, and captioning, respectively.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and cover a diverse set of challenging tasks. In all cases, sMCL significantly outperforms independently trained ensembles and other relevant baselines in terms of oracle performance. The analysis showing the emergence of interpretable specialization among ensemble members (e.g., specializing in certain object classes or caption structures) is a compelling result that validates the method's core objective.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the proposed method is described concisely, and the connection to prior work is established clearly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Algorithmic Novelty:** My main concern is that the algorithmic contribution could be viewed as an incremental adaptation of the block coordinate descent method from [8] to a stochastic setting. However, I am convinced by the authors that this adaptation is non-trivial and of high practical importance. It transforms an infeasible training paradigm into an efficient and effective one for modern deep networks. The practical significance of this step arguably outweighs the perceived lack of deep theoretical novelty.

2.  **Analysis of Diversity:** While the paper provides compelling qualitative examples and analysis of emergent specialization, a more quantitative analysis of the "diversity" effect would strengthen the claims. For instance, in the image captioning experiment, the authors report the number of unique n-grams, which is a good step. It would be beneficial to include similar quantitative diversity metrics for the classification and segmentation tasks (e.g., measuring the average distance between predictions in the output or a feature space). This would provide a more objective measure of how sMCL encourages diversity compared to the baselines.

3.  **Baseline Comparison:** A potentially interesting comparison would be with a more direct stochastic version of the original MCL algorithm. For instance, one could update the data-to-model assignments after each epoch (or even each mini-batch) and then train each model for one pass on its assigned subset. This would help to further disentangle the benefits of the "winner-take-gradient" approach from the general benefits of using a stochastic, non-convergent update schedule.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a well-executed and interesting paper. While the core idea is built upon prior work, the proposed stochastic adaptation is a crucial and enabling contribution that makes the concept of oracle loss minimization practical for deep learning. The method is simple, general, and supported by strong empirical results across multiple domains. For these reasons, I am in favor of acceptance.
