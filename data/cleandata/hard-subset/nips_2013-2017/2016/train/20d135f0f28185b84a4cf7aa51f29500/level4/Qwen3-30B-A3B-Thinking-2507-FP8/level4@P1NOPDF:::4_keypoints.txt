- sMCL is presented as incrementally novel, but the core MCL motivation is not new and the method largely adapts [8] with only minor neural-network tweaks, lacking substantive innovation.  
- The paper does not clearly distinguish or argue how sMCL differs from prior work, making the claimed contribution unconvincing.  
- Technical presentation is poor and underdeveloped (Section 3 is too brief), leaving important methodological details and depth missing.  
- The authors fail to explicitly compare sMCL to standard multi-task training paradigms, so claimed advantages over established practices are unclear.  
- Experiments span multiple tasks but use weak baselines and omit comparisons to state-of-the-art multi-task methods.  
- Reported performance gains are marginal (often <1%), insufficient to demonstrate meaningful progress.  
- The reviewer recommends stronger empirical validation (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012, multi-task networks with shared bottlenecks or task-specific heads, and recent SOTA comparisons).  
- Overall assessment: contribution overstated, presentation unclear, and experimental validation inadequate — substantial revisions required for publication.
