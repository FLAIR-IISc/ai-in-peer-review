This paper proposes a method for generating multiple outputs by jointly learning an ensemble of deep networks that minimize the oracle loss, which is useful when interacting with users. The authors generalize the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm to deep networks and present a novel training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), that avoids costly retraining. The proposed method is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. The oracle experiments on three tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The paper integrates deep networks into the MCL paradigm and proposes a stochastic block gradient descent optimization method to minimize the oracle loss of MCL. The proposed optimization method can be easily implemented within the back-propagation learning framework, and the whole model can be trained in an end-to-end fashion.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL. The method outperforms existing baselines and produces sets of outputs with high oracle performance.

However, I have some concerns and suggestions for improvement. First, the authors do not explain the importance of initialization. What if models are initialized with random values instead of a pre-trained network? It would be beneficial to include an analysis of the impact of initialization on the performance of sMCL.

Second, the authors do not provide enough details about the setting for comparison. Do the authors use the same pre-trained network to baseline methods as sMCL for initialization? A clear explanation of the experimental setup would be helpful.

Third, the authors use the oracle accuracy as a metric for evaluation. However, I expect to see more analysis on the diversity of ensemble members. The authors claim that sMCL induces interpretable expertise among ensemble members, but more quantitative analysis would strengthen this claim.

Finally, I suggest that the authors compare their method with a similar work, "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks" by Stefan Lee et al. (2015). This comparison would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of sMCL.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles. With some additional analysis and clarification, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The method is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles.

Weaknesses:

* The authors do not explain the importance of initialization.
* The authors do not provide enough details about the setting for comparison.
* The authors do not provide enough analysis on the diversity of ensemble members.
* The authors do not compare their method with similar works.

Acceptance:

Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns and suggestions mentioned above to strengthen the paper.
