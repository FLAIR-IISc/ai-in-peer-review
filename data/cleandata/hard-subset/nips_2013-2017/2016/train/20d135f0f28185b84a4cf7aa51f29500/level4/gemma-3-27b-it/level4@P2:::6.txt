This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles. The core idea is to simulate a “winner-take-all” scenario during training, where only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for a given example receives a gradient update. This contrasts with traditional ensemble methods where each member is typically trained independently or with more complex coordination strategies. The authors demonstrate that this simple approach achieves strong performance across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, outperforming existing baselines and exhibiting a compelling ability to induce specialization among ensemble members.

The work builds on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8, 9] but addresses the limitations of previous methods, particularly the computational cost associated with retraining models. sMCL offers a parameter-free, efficient, and generally applicable solution for training diverse ensembles with stochastic gradient descent. The authors convincingly argue that the induced diversity is beneficial for tasks with inherent ambiguity or multi-modal solutions, as illustrated by examples in semantic segmentation and image captioning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The sMCL algorithm presents a conceptually simple yet effective approach to training diverse ensembles, differing significantly from existing methods.
*   **Efficiency:** The algorithm avoids the costly retraining steps required by previous MCL approaches [8, 9], making it practical for deep learning models.
*   **Generality:** sMCL is agnostic to both network architecture and loss function, increasing its applicability across a broad range of tasks.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements over strong baselines on diverse tasks and datasets.
*   **Interpretability:** The observed specialization of ensemble members provides insights into the learned representations and highlights the algorithm's ability to capture task ambiguity.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Testing Phase Clarification:** The paper lacks a clear description of the testing phase. It is inferred that the best-performing member is selected based on the ground truth, but this should be explicitly stated (e.g., line 159, page 5).
*   **Baseline Definition:** The term “independent ensembles” (and “regular ensembles”) is used without a clear definition. A more detailed explanation of how these baselines are constructed is necessary (e.g., line 181, page 5). Are they trained with the same hyper-parameters and random initializations?
*   **Specialization Justification:** While the authors observe specialization in ensemble members, the connection between per-example reassignment during training and the emergence of specialization is not fully justified. Exploring ways to explicitly encourage specialization in the loss function could strengthen this argument.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper does not discuss relevant work on diversity regularization techniques in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al., [add citation if known]). These techniques, while perhaps more rigorous, could provide valuable context and comparison.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and efficient algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing baselines across multiple tasks.
*   The concept of specialization is intriguing and offers insights into the learned representations.
*   The algorithm is readily implementable and applicable to a wide range of problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The explanation of the testing phase is lacking.
*   The definition of the "independent ensemble" baseline is unclear.
*   The justification for specialization based on per-example reassignment is somewhat weak.
*   The omission of related work on diversity regularization represents a minor oversight.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. While some minor clarifications and additions would strengthen the work, the novelty, efficiency, and strong empirical results justify acceptance. The sMCL algorithm offers a practical and effective approach to training diverse ensembles that is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners. I recommend **weak acceptance**.
