

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple yet effective method for training diverse deep ensembles by implementing a "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer during backpropagation. The approach directly minimizes oracle loss by backpropagating gradients only to the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each example, addressing a key limitation in existing Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods that require costly retraining of ensemble members (Guzman-Rivera et al.).

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it provides a practical solution for deep learning practitioners by enabling concurrent training of ensemble members without expensive retraining cycles. Second, it demonstrates robust empirical performance across three diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning), with significant improvements over baseline methods including classical ensembles and the MCL approach. Third, the method is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and parameter-free, making it highly accessible to practitioners.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. Despite heavily emphasizing "diversity" as a key contribution, the method does not enforce any explicit diversity term in the loss function, which contradicts the paper's central motivation. This is particularly problematic given the existing work by Guzman-Rivera et al. that formalized the MCL paradigm, making sMCL appear incremental rather than transformative. The paper also fails to address how baseline MCL networks were ensured to have fully converged, which could impact the validity of the comparison.

The paper is well-written overall, though minor issues require attention: the abstract's first sentence should be reworded, the title and text should tone down the emphasis on diversity, and there are a few typos (line 108: "f" should be "g"; line 115: remove extra period). The experimental design is strong with thorough evaluation across multiple tasks, but the theoretical contribution appears limited.

In summary, while the paper presents a practical and effective method with strong empirical results, the contribution appears incremental relative to prior work, and the lack of explicit diversity enforcement undermines the paper's core motivation. The method would be significantly strengthened with clearer theoretical differentiation from Guzman-Rivera's work and a more principled approach to diversity.
