

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss—the loss incurred by selecting the best prediction from the ensemble. The work extends Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2015, [8]) by introducing a stochastic gradient descent-based approach that avoids the computationally prohibitive retraining steps required by prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods. sMCL trains all ensemble members concurrently by backpropagating gradients only to the "winning" predictor (lowest loss) for each example in a batch, making it applicable to modern deep networks without architectural or loss-function modifications. The authors demonstrate sMCL's effectiveness across image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements over classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. (ICCV 2015), with up to 10% higher oracle accuracy and 5× speedup over MCL.

**Strengths**  
The paper’s primary strength lies in its **practical significance**. By enabling efficient training of diverse deep ensembles, sMCL solves a critical bottleneck in applying MCL to real-world deep learning systems. The method is truly parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and requires minimal implementation changes (just adding a single "sMCL layer"), making it highly adoptable. The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, covering three distinct domains with thorough analysis of ensemble specialization (e.g., class-wise clustering in classification, shape/segment biases in segmentation). The oracle loss framework is compellingly motivated by real-world scenarios where multiple hypotheses (e.g., ambiguous object recognition, multi-modal captions) outperform single predictions. The paper is **clearly written** with effective visualizations (e.g., Fig. 5b, 7) illustrating emergent expertise. The evaluation using oracle metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy, oracle IoU) is appropriate and aligns with prior work.

**Weaknesses**  
The **originality** claim requires careful assessment. While the core optimization strategy (minimizing oracle loss via gradient routing) builds directly on [8], the authors convincingly argue that the stochastic implementation is a *practical necessity* for deep networks—prior MCL was infeasible for architectures requiring weeks of training. However, the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The paper could better distinguish its contribution from [8] by explicitly addressing: (1) why the stochastic approach is *fundamentally* more scalable (not just "faster"), and (2) whether the *same* optimization strategy would apply to non-deep models. A more rigorous comparison with a batch-based MCL variant (e.g., MCL using mini-batches instead of full retraining) would strengthen the case for sMCL’s efficiency gains. Additionally, while the paper shows qualitative diversity (e.g., Fig. 7), it lacks quantitative metrics for "diversity" (e.g., pairwise dissimilarity between ensemble outputs), which is central to the claim of producing "interpretable representations of task ambiguity."

**Quality and Significance**  
The **quality** is high: experiments are well-executed, ablation studies (e.g., varying *k* in Eq. 1, Fig. 4d-e) provide insight into the algorithm’s behavior, and results are statistically significant (e.g., 6–10% improvements over baselines). The theoretical grounding in stochastic optimization is sound, and the authors transparently acknowledge limitations (e.g., MCL’s infeasibility for large-scale tasks). **Significance** is strong for practitioners: the method directly addresses a gap in deploying diverse outputs for real-world systems (e.g., autonomous vehicles, accessibility tools), where single-prediction models fail due to ambiguity. The broad applicability across architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs) and tasks further elevates its impact. While not a theoretical breakthrough, sMCL is a *pragmatic advancement* that enables a valuable paradigm (MCL) to be used with modern deep learning pipelines.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a **well-executed, practically significant contribution** to ensemble learning. The incremental novelty is justified by the critical scalability problem it resolves—without sMCL, MCL remains a theoretical curiosity for deep networks. The authors’ focus on real-world applicability (e.g., "diverse solutions for ambiguous evidence") aligns perfectly with NIPS’ emphasis on impactful AI research. While the originality is modest, the practical utility and thorough evaluation outweigh this concern. The paper is **clearly written** and **technically sound**, with no major flaws in methodology or analysis. I recommend **acceptance**. To strengthen future work, the authors should quantify diversity (e.g., via entropy or overlap metrics) and clarify the theoretical distinction between sMCL and batch-based MCL. The proposed method is a valuable tool for the deep learning community, and its simplicity ensures immediate adoption.
