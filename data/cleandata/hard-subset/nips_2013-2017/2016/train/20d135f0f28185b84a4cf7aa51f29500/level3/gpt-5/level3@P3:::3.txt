Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep networks by proposing a simple stochastic optimization scheme that integrates assignment and training within standard backprop. The key idea is a “winner-takes-the-gradient” layer: for each example in a minibatch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates (ties broken arbitrarily). This realizes a stochastic block gradient descent on the standard MCL upper-bound objective (Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS’12; AISTATS’14), avoiding the costly alternating retrain-and-reassign procedure that is impractical for modern deep nets. The method is parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and trains all members in parallel.

Experiments span three domains and architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (Caffe CIFAR-Quick CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (VGG16+LSTM, with both frozen and finetuned CNN variants). The evaluation uses oracle versions of standard metrics (top-k accuracy, IoU, CIDEr-D), consistent with MCL literature and M-best/diversity work (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.). Baselines include independent ensembles, alternating MCL, and the boosting-style submodular approach of Dey et al. sMCL improves oracle metrics across tasks and ensemble sizes and trains ≈5× faster than alternating MCL; qualitative analyses show emergent specialization by class, shape/frequency biases in segmentation, and diverse n-gram patterns in captions.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The stochastic gradient derivation directly follows from the MCL surrogate with binary assignment variables; the resulting gradient is exactly the single-model gradient for the winning predictor and zero otherwise. The algorithm is clear and easy to implement in any SGD framework.
- Empirical support: Results are persuasive across three diverse tasks and architectures, with typical oracle gains of 6–10% over strong baselines and large speed-ups over alternating MCL. Analyses of specialization (class-wise assignment, k-winner ablations) support the narrative that specialization emerges from the oracle objective.
- Fairness/clarifications: Please clarify whether the MCL baseline was trained to true convergence at each meta-iteration; if not, early cutting of learners could suppress ensemble capacity and bias the comparison. Also report sensitivity to batch size and tie-breaking strategies, and whether any load-balancing intervention was needed to avoid degenerate specialization (e.g., one winner dominating).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear algorithmic description and experimental protocols. The notion of oracle metrics is well explained and consistent with prior work. Minor issues and suggestions:
- Abstract first sentence: Consider simplifying to “Many perception systems are embedded in larger processes that can evaluate candidate predictions, making it useful to return multiple plausible hypotheses rather than a single output.”
- Tone down “diversity” framing in title/text. The method optimizes oracle loss; diversity is emergent, not explicitly enforced. For accuracy, a title such as “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Deep Ensembles” would be sufficient.
- Line 108: first “f” should be “g.”
- Line 115: remove the extra period in the middle of the sentence.
- Consider adding explicit diversity measurements (pairwise disagreement, coverage, KL between predictive distributions), in addition to oracle metrics and n-gram counts.

Originality
The contribution is primarily a practical, stochastic optimization of the established MCL objective for deep nets. Conceptually, the winner-take-gradient update resembles online hard-EM/K-Means or hard mixture-of-experts assignment, and the objective traces to Guzman-Rivera et al. The novelty lies in showing that a simple, parameter-free, in-backprop assignment suffices to make MCL practical and effective across modern deep architectures. This is incremental in theory but impactful in practice.

Significance
High practical significance. Many applications benefit from multiple hypotheses, and a drop-in, training-time method that improves oracle performance with minimal engineering is valuable. The breadth of tasks makes a compelling case for adoption. Beyond oracle improvements, the method appears to induce interpretable specialization without explicit diversity regularizers, which is appealing for analysis and downstream system design.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, timely problem: making MCL feasible for deep learning at scale.
- Method is simple, parameter-free, and easy to implement as a single layer in backprop.
- Strong, consistent experimental performance across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Thoughtful analyses of specialization and ablations (k-winner relaxation).

Weaknesses
- The paper heavily motivates “diversity” but does not explicitly optimize for it; the objective is purely oracle loss. The framing should reflect this or introduce an explicit diversity term/regularizer.
- Conceptual increment over Guzman-Rivera et al.’s relaxation; the main novelty is the stochastic optimization scheme (albeit important for practicality).
- Some baseline questions remain (see below), and additional diversity metrics would strengthen claims.

Questions for the authors
- MCL baseline training: How did you ensure convergence at each meta-iteration for deep networks? Could truncating training or using too few meta-iterations significantly undercut MCL’s potential and thereby exaggerate sMCL’s advantage?
- Stability and collapse: Did you observe any collapse where a single learner dominates assignments? Any mechanisms (e.g., temperature/softmin, entropy regularization, load balancing) tried or needed?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive is sMCL to batch size, learning rate, and tie-breaking? Are results robust across seeds?
- Captioning: You compare to beam search on a single model; did you also try beam search per ensemble member and then take the oracle across beams? How does this affect the oracle vs. diversity trade-off?
- Top-k relaxation: The k-winner variant is interesting; could you report a fuller sweep of k and its impact on both oracle and diversity metrics?

Minor comments and typos
- Reword the abstract’s first sentence as suggested above.
- Tone down “diverse” in title/text to better match the objective.
- Line 108: replace first “f” with “g.”
- Line 115: remove the extra period.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, simple, broadly applicable method that makes MCL usable for deep nets.
- Strong empirical gains and comprehensive evaluation across three modalities.
- Clear presentation and useful qualitative analyses.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical novelty is limited; largely an online/SGD instantiation of prior MCL.
- Diversity is emphasized but not explicitly enforced or quantified beyond oracle and n-grams.
- Possible under-training of the alternating MCL baseline may affect fairness.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The work is a solid, impactful contribution that will likely be adopted by practitioners, despite its incremental theoretical novelty. Addressing the clarity issues and baseline questions would further strengthen the paper.
