- Proposes sMCL, a practical method for training deep ensembles using a "winner-takes-the-gradient" strategy that assigns each example to the lowest-loss member during SGD.  
- Enables concurrent training of ensemble members without costly retraining or alternating optimization used in prior MCL methods.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning with multiple architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN).  
- Method is simple and easy to implement, requiring only a small change to standard SGD loops.  
- sMCL consistently outperforms baselines including classical ensembles and prior MCL while being significantly faster.  
- Analysis shows ensemble members specialize, producing interpretable expertise in tasks like image classification.  
- Well-motivated and positioned relative to ensemble learning and multiple-solution literature, addressing a relevant problem for real-world perception systems.  
- Main weakness: diversity is implicit (emerges from oracle loss) rather than enforced by an explicit diversity regularizer, limiting control over diversity.  
- Contribution is somewhat incremental—essentially a stochastic relaxation of Guzman-Rivera et al.'s alternating block coordinate descent—so novelty magnitude could be questioned.  
- Evaluation focuses on oracle performance; lacks direct quantification of diversity (e.g., pairwise output distances) to strengthen claims about diverse solutions.  
- Minor issues: awkward abstract sentence, tone down emphasis on "diversity" in title/text, typos on lines 108 and 115.  
- Methodological concern: paper does not clearly state how baseline MCL deep networks were ensured to converge; alternating optimization may prematurely cut off learners.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, is simple and efficient, shows broad strong empirical results, and is clearly written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: contribution may be incremental and absence of explicit diversity control could limit impact.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify baseline convergence and include more direct diversity measures.
