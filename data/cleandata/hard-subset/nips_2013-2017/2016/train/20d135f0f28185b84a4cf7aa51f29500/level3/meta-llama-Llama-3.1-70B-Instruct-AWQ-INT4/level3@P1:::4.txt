This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss. The authors' main contribution is a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm that adapts the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm to deep networks. While the idea of sMCL is intriguing, the paper's impact is somewhat diminished by its incremental novelty and lack of clarity in differentiating the proposed framework from existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they aim to solve. They argue that in many real-world applications, providing multiple highly likely hypotheses is more beneficial than a single prediction. The authors also demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL on a range of tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

However, the technical section of the paper is brief, and the authors could have provided more details about the algorithm and its implementation. The distinction between sMCL and widely used alternative/multi-task training methods is not clearly explained, which may lead to confusion among readers. Additionally, the experiments, while covering various tasks, use weak baselines and show only small improvements.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the paper's clarity is compromised by its poor writing style, which makes it difficult to follow at times. The authors could have improved the paper's readability by providing more detailed explanations, using clearer language, and including more visual aids.

The originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing work on MCL. However, the authors' adaptation of MCL to deep networks is a notable contribution. The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the performance of deep ensembles in various applications. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability of sMCL, and their results show promise for improving oracle performance in multiple tasks.

To further validate the effectiveness of sMCL, I recommend that the authors evaluate their approach on more challenging datasets, such as PASCAL VOC 2012, and compare it against stronger baselines. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's strengths and weaknesses.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss.
* The authors demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL on a range of tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.
* The paper has the potential to improve the performance of deep ensembles in various applications.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's novelty is incremental, and it builds upon existing work on MCL.
* The technical section is brief, and the authors could have provided more details about the algorithm and its implementation.
* The experiments use weak baselines and show only small improvements.
* The paper's clarity is compromised by its poor writing style.
