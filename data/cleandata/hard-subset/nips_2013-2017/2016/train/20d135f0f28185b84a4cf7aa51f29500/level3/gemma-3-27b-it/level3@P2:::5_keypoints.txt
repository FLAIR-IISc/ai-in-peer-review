- Introduces sMCL: an SGD-based method to train diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss, enabling concurrent training of members.  
- Extends Multiple Choice Learning to modern deep networks, removing costly iterative retraining and improving scalability.  
- Empirical results on classification, segmentation, and captioning show improved oracle performance and interpretable specialization among ensemble members.  
- Technical soundness: clear derivation of stochastic gradient update and compatibility with existing SGD frameworks.  
- Clarity: well-written, well-organized, consistent notation, and easy-to-follow algorithm description.  
- Originality: novel application of MCL principles to deep ensembles via a practical sMCL optimization strategy.  
- Significance: addresses a relevant problem with potential real-world impact; specialization aids debugging and understanding model behavior.  
- Experimental rigor concern: paper omits whether baselines used the same pre-trained initialization, which can materially affect results.  
- Diversity evaluation concern: relies mainly on oracle accuracy and lacks quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise distances, prediction entropy).  
- Limited related-work comparison: should compare to more recent diversity-promoting methods (e.g., "Why M Heads are Better than One" by Lee et al., 2015).  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity concern: despite "parameter-free" claim, no analysis of sensitivity to SGD hyperparameters (learning rate, momentum, weight decay).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid, promising results across tasks, and easy to integrate into existing frameworks.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental details, incomplete diversity analysis, and limited comparisons to recent methods.  
- Overall assessment: strong, impactful paper; recommends weak accept contingent on clarifying baseline initialization and adding quantitative diversity analyses.
