## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a straightforward approach to training diverse deep ensembles by adapting the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework for stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is a “winner-takes-the-gradient” layer in backpropagation, effectively assigning each training example to the ensemble member that achieves the lowest loss. The authors demonstrate strong empirical performance across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, highlighting the method's broad applicability.

The paper addresses a relevant and important problem – effectively training diverse ensembles within the deep learning paradigm. Existing methods often suffer from scalability issues due to retraining requirements (Guzman et al.) or sequential training limitations. sMCL’s simplicity and ease of implementation are definite strengths. The comprehensive evaluation across three diverse tasks is also commendable, demonstrating the method’s robustness. The qualitative analysis suggesting interpretable specializations within the ensemble is a promising observation. 

However, despite emphasizing “diversity” throughout the paper, the method doesn’t explicitly enforce a diversity term in the loss function. While the oracle loss implicitly encourages diversity, a more direct diversity regularization could potentially yield even better results. Furthermore, the contribution feels somewhat incremental relative to the work of Guzman et al. (2016). While sMCL elegantly addresses the scalability issue, it’s essentially a relaxation of their approach, and the novelty arguably lies in the successful adaptation rather than a fundamentally new concept. 

Several minor points require attention. The first sentence of the abstract should be reworded for clarity. The authors should tone down the emphasis on “diversity” in the title and throughout the text, as it's not explicitly enforced. A typo exists on line 108 (“f” should be “g”), and a superfluous period appears on line 115. 

Finally, a crucial question arises: How were the baseline MCL deep networks ensured to have converged? Early cutting of learners in the alternating block coordinate descent method could significantly harm ensemble performance, potentially skewing the comparison with sMCL. Providing details on the convergence criteria for the baseline is essential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Addresses an important problem, provides a simple and scalable solution, and demonstrates strong experimental results across diverse tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Incremental contribution compared to existing MCL work, lacks explicit diversity enforcement, and requires clarification regarding baseline convergence.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but addressing the weaknesses and the open question would significantly strengthen the work.
