This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a winner-take-gradient variant of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss (min over M predictions). The key idea is to compute per-example losses across ensemble members and backpropagate gradients only through the lowest-loss predictor, enabling concurrent SGD-based training. The authors evaluate on classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), reporting modest oracle improvements and qualitative evidence of emergent specialization.

Relation to prior work: The formulation closely follows Guzman-Rivera et al.’s MCL [8] and related diverse M-best inference (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.) and sequential/boosting-style methods (Dey et al.). The main difference is an SGD instantiation that avoids repeated retraining, akin to hard-EM/mixture-of-experts with hard assignment but without a learned gating network.

Quality: The method is simple and appears implementable, but the technical development is thin (Sec. 3 is one page). Important details are underspecified: handling of ties, stability under noisy assignments, regularization to prevent collapse, sensitivity to batch size/ensemble size, and convergence behavior. Experimental gains over baselines are small and sometimes within expected variance; baselines are weak (e.g., older FCN on a VOC11 subset, shallow CIFAR-10 model) and do not include stronger alternatives (mixture-of-experts with gating, modern ensembles, top-k losses).

Clarity: The paper is unevenly written; the core algorithm lacks a precise and complete description and ablations. The distinction from standard alternative training paradigms (multi-task/multi-head models, snapshot/bagging ensembles, negative correlation learning) is not clearly articulated.

Originality: Incremental. sMCL reads as a neural-network adaptation of MCL with minor adjustments. The winner-take-gradient scheme is a straightforward block-stochastic analog of the prior alternating procedure.

Significance: Potentially useful as a practical recipe, but current evidence is limited. The improvements are small, and the oracle metric alone does not establish broader utility.

Strengths:
- Simple, parameter-free, broadly applicable recipe.
- Concurrent training; plausible speedup versus alternating MCL.
- Qualitative examples of emergent specialization.

Weaknesses:
- Incremental novelty; limited technical depth.
- Unclear relation to mixture-of-experts and multi-task alternatives.
- Weak baselines; small gains; limited datasets.

Suggestions:
- Evaluate on Pascal VOC 2012 with stronger segmentation baselines (e.g., DeepLab/ResNet), and on stronger classifiers; include robust ablations.
- Compare against learned-gating mixture-of-experts, top-k loss, stronger ensemble baselines, and beam/top-k from a single model.
- Provide rigorous algorithmic details, convergence discussion, and diversity metrics beyond oracle.

Overall: Interesting practicality but not yet compelling; improvements and positioning need strengthening.
