This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an algorithm for training ensembles of deep neural networks to produce diverse outputs. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," where for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives a gradient update. This approach builds directly on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which used an alternating, full-batch optimization scheme. The primary contribution of this work is adapting this concept to a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) setting, thereby making it practical for training modern, large-scale deep networks concurrently.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Quality:** The paper addresses the important goal of training diverse deep ensembles in an efficient and principled manner. The proposed sMCL method is simple, elegant, and requires no additional parameters. The empirical validation is a key strength; the authors demonstrate significant performance gains over strong baselines across three distinct and complex tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. This showcases the method's wide applicability and effectiveness. Making the MCL paradigm feasible for deep learning is a substantial practical advance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the algorithm is presented precisely, and the experimental setup and results are well-documented.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Originality:** The primary concern is that the algorithmic novelty over Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] is incremental. The conceptual framework of minimizing oracle loss via winner-take-all updates is not new. The main contribution is its formulation within a standard SGD loop, which is a straightforward, albeit highly effective, adaptation.
*   **Analysis:** The comparison to the original MCL could be more direct. Since MCL is presented as a batch method, it is unclear if its poorer performance on complex tasks is due to its alternating nature or its inability to handle the data stochastically. A stronger baseline would be an implementation of MCL that also operates on mini-batches to better isolate the benefits of sMCL’s concurrent training.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
I would suggest the authors consider a more thorough analysis of the "diversity" fostered by sMCL. While the qualitative results and class-specialization plots are insightful, adding quantitative metrics of diversity (e.g., inter-model prediction disagreement) and correlating them with oracle performance would strengthen the paper's claims.

Overall, while the algorithmic leap is modest, its practical impact is significant. The paper convincingly demonstrates that this simple modification enables a powerful idea to be unlocked for the deep learning community. I therefore lean in favor of acceptance.
