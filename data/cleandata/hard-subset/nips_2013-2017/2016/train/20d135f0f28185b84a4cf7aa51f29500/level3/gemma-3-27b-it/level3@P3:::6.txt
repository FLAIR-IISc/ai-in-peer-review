This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel ensemble training algorithm where only one learner is updated per training example – the member with the lowest loss with respect to the true label. The core idea is simple and elegantly addresses the challenge of training diverse deep ensembles for Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The authors demonstrate strong empirical results across various tasks and architectures, showing improvements over existing methods. While the paper presents a promising approach, several concerns regarding the testing phase, unclear terminology, and justification for claimed member specialization warrant further clarification.

The work builds upon the MCL paradigm introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9], but crucially overcomes the limitations of those methods concerning computational cost for deep networks. Prior work often involved retraining models multiple times or sequential training schemes, which are impractical for large deep architectures. sMCL, in contrast, allows for concurrent training of ensemble members via a stochastic gradient descent approach, making it more scalable and efficient. The paper appropriately positions itself within the broader landscape of ensemble learning, referencing techniques like resampling [27], diversity regularization [19, 26], and recent explorations of ensemble-of-expert architectures [2, 12]. It also acknowledges related work on generating multiple solutions from single models [3, 15, 16, 23, 24], highlighting the novelty of their approach in addressing the challenges of deep learning specifically.

**Strengths:**

*   **Simplicity and Elegance:** The core idea of sMCL is remarkably simple – a “winner-take-gradient” scheme – yet effective.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements over baselines across diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning).
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm is designed to be scalable to deep architectures, a key advantage over previous MCL approaches.
*   **Parameter-Free:**  The method is parameter-free, reducing the burden of hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Emergent Specialization:** The observation of emergent expertise and specialization among ensemble members is intriguing and adds to the interpretability of the learned ensembles.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Unclear Testing Phase:** The paper’s description of the testing phase is vague. Specifically, the reviewer’s suspicion regarding the use of ground truth to select the best member (line 159, p.5) is a serious concern. If the testing phase relies on knowing the ground truth to pick the best prediction, it invalidates the claim of evaluating a multiple-prediction system as a whole and significantly diminishes the value of the results. The authors *must* clarify how the oracle loss is evaluated at test time.
*   **Undefined Terminology:** The terms "independent ensembles" (Indp.) and "regular ensembles" are used in the text and figures (line 181, p.5) without any explanation. This makes it difficult to interpret the results and understand the comparison with the proposed method. A clear definition of these terms is essential.
*   **Questionable Specialization Justification:** The claim of member "specialization" to different output classes is not convincingly justified. The training reassignments occur on a per-example basis, meaning a member can be the “winner” for different classes across the training dataset. This doesn’t necessarily equate to long-term specialization.  The authors need to provide more compelling evidence to support this claim.
*   **Missed Related Work:** The paper neglects to discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, particularly work by Chen et al. and others.  This literature offers more rigorous approaches to promoting diversity and could provide valuable context and comparison for sMCL.  The authors should acknowledge and discuss these approaches, addressing how sMCL differs and potentially complements them.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the authors compare against several baselines, an ablation study exploring the sensitivity of sMCL to different hyperparameters or network architectures would strengthen the findings.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning – training diverse ensembles for multiple-prediction scenarios.
*   The proposed algorithm is simple, efficient, and demonstrates strong empirical performance.
*   The concept of emergent specialization is intriguing and offers potential for interpretability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The unclear testing phase raises concerns about the validity of the experimental results.
*   The lack of clarity regarding terminology hinders understanding and interpretation.
*   The justification for claimed member specialization is weak.
*   The omission of relevant related work weakens the paper’s contextualization.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The simplicity and efficiency of sMCL, coupled with its strong empirical results, are commendable. However, the concerns regarding the testing phase, undefined terminology, and justification for specialization are significant.  

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept.  I recommend weak acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly providing a clear explanation of the testing procedure and clarifying the terminology used in the paper. Addressing the other weaknesses would further strengthen the manuscript and increase its impact.
