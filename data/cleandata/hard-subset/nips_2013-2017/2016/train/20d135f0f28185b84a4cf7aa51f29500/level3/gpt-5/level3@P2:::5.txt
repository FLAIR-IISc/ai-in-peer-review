Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the problem of producing multiple plausible outputs for ambiguous perceptual tasks by minimizing the oracle loss of an ensemble—i.e., the loss of the best hypothesis among M predictions. Building on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, the authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” SGD procedure that interleaves assignment and learning: for each training example, only the network with the lowest loss receives a gradient update for that example. This avoids the costly alternating retraining loop of prior MCL work.

The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, and the authors demonstrate its applicability on three domains with deep architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM). Across tasks, sMCL improves oracle metrics over independent ensembles, over Guzman-Rivera et al.’s MCL (with much lower training time), and over a boosting-like sequential diversity method. The paper also analyzes the emergent specialization of ensemble members, showing clear class-wise specialization in classification and more nuanced, task-dependent diversity in segmentation and captioning.

The idea is closely related to prior MCL work [Guzman-Rivera et al.], diverse M-best inference for structured models, and ensemble diversity techniques. Conceptually, it also connects to hard-EM/K-Means style partitioning, hard mixture-of-experts without an explicit gating network (Jacobs et al., 1991), and multi-head/shared-trunk architectures that promote diversity (e.g., “Why M Heads are Better than One,” Lee et al., 2015). The contribution here is to instantiate and validate a practical, efficient SGD-based optimization for oracle loss with deep networks trained end-to-end.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm that integrates cleanly with backprop for arbitrary architectures and losses.
- Strong empirical results on three substantially different tasks, with consistent gains in oracle metrics; clear speed advantage over prior MCL training.
- Insightful qualitative and quantitative analyses of emergent specialization; the k-winner ablation is particularly informative.
- Parameter-free and easy to reproduce conceptually; the method is likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Initialization and fairness of baselines: It is unclear whether all baselines (independent, MCL, boosting-like) used identical initialization/pretraining and training budgets. For segmentation and captioning, sMCL models appear initialized from an independently trained ensemble; please clarify whether baseline ensembles were initialized identically and fine-tuned for the same number of epochs/learning rates.
- Oracle-only evaluation: While appropriate for the stated goal, the paper would benefit from additional diversity/coverage analyses beyond a few plots (e.g., pairwise disagreement for classification/segmentation, mutual information, intra-ensemble IoU diversity, risk–coverage curves). For captioning, n-gram uniqueness is reported but could be complemented by self-CIDEr or novelty metrics.
- Missing/limited related work: A comparison and discussion of multi-head/shared-trunk diversity (e.g., Lee et al., 2015) would strengthen positioning. Also consider discussing classic mixture-of-experts with hard gating (Jacobs et al., 1991) and how sMCL differs (no explicit gate, task-agnostic loss).
- Potential gradient starvation: Hard “winner-take-gradient” can cause dead or under-trained experts. The paper partly addresses this via emergent specialization, but experiments showing stability across seeds, effect of M, tie-breaking, and the k-winner/soft-assignment variant would be valuable (e.g., a temperature-controlled softmin over losses).
- Beam search comparison: For captioning, the comparison between multi-head ensembles and single-model beam search is informative but not apples-to-apples; please discuss computational parity and extend beam widths or combine beam search with sMCL heads to clarify where the benefits come from.

Quality
- Technically sound and well-motivated. The gradient derivation for the hard assignment objective is clear. The optimization is consistent with block coordinate descent but implemented stochastically.
- Empirical evaluation is solid across domains, though additional controls (variance across runs, matched initialization/training budgets, and diversity metrics) would improve rigor.
- Claims about speedups are plausible; reporting wall-clock times and compute used would make the comparison more concrete.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and easy to follow. The intuition, algorithm, and experiments are clear.
- Please add more implementation details: tie-breaking policy, handling equal losses, mini-batch sampling, and any regularization used to avoid collapse/dead experts.
- Include standard deviations or error bars when possible.

Originality
- Extends MCL to a practical, parallel, SGD-friendly training procedure for deep networks. While the hard-assignment idea echoes classic clustering/hard-MoE, the adaptation to oracle loss with deep ensembles and the thorough cross-domain evaluation are new and useful.

Significance
- Practical impact is high: a drop-in training strategy that improves oracle performance and yields interpretable ensemble specialization.
- The method is likely to be influential for tasks where multiple hypotheses are valuable and for pipelines with downstream oracle/selection components.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Initialization: Did all baselines use the same initialization (random vs. pretrained), data order, and training schedule? For segmentation/captioning, were all methods fine-tuned from the same checkpoint with identical hyperparameters?
- Stability: How sensitive is sMCL to random seeds and M? Please report variance and show specialization patterns across runs.
- Soft assignments: Have you tried softmin over losses with temperature τ (or the k-winner variant with k>1) to mitigate dead experts and improve convergence? Any trade-offs in oracle metrics/diversity?
- Diversity metrics: Please add quantitative disagreement/diversity metrics for classification/segmentation and richer diversity measures for captioning (e.g., self-CIDEr, distinct-n).
- Related work: Compare with “Why M Heads are Better than One” and discuss connections/differences to hard mixture-of-experts without learned gating.

Pros
- Simple, general, efficient algorithm that consistently improves oracle metrics.
- Broad applicability across tasks/architectures; interpretable emergent expertise.
- Useful ablations (k-winner) and clear practical guidance.

Cons
- Oracle-only focus; limited diversity quantification and downstream impact analysis.
- Some ambiguity around baseline fairness/initialization and variance across runs.
- Missing discussion/experiments on closely related multi-head and hard-MoE literature.

Overall recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The method is simple, broadly applicable, and empirically effective, and the paper is clear and well-motivated. Addressing the concerns about initialization fairness, diversity quantification, and related work would further strengthen the contribution.
