- Proposes sMCL: an SGD-based “winner-take-gradient” per-example update that trains deep ensembles to optimize oracle (min-of-losses) performance.  
- Replaces MCL’s global alternating assignment/retraining with concurrent, parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic mini-batch updates.  
- Empirically shows consistent oracle improvements on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO captioning versus independent ensembles and prior work (Dey et al.).  
- Demonstrates strong speed advantage over original MCL by avoiding full retraining loops.  
- Documents emergent specialization and diversity patterns among ensemble members.  
- Method is technically sound; the gradient argument is simple and correct under the hard assignment objective.  
- Results span multiple tasks and architectures, showing clear oracle gains and runtime speedups.  
- Novelty is incremental relative to Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], essentially replacing block-coordinate retraining with stochastic hard assignment.  
- High practical significance as a simple, drop-in procedure likely to be adopted by practitioners.  
- Pros: simple, general, effective; scales MCL to deep nets; multi-domain empirical validation; interpretable specializations; parameter-free and easy to implement.  
- Cons: conceptual novelty is incremental; missing comparison to a batchwise/online MCL variant; limited analysis of diversity mechanisms and training dynamics.  
- Key missing comparison: an MCL variant that updates assignments within mini-batches rather than retraining to convergence.  
- Requested deeper analyses: convergence/stability under tie-breaking, sensitivity to batch size, ensemble size M, and curriculum.  
- Requested ablations: hard versus soft assignments (e.g., temperature smoothing or top-k updates) and statistical significance across runs.  
- Clarity is good—well organized with intuitive figures—but implementation details need: tie-breaking policy, wall-clock/time-per-epoch, and ensured fairness in epoch counts for baselines.  
- Recommendation: weak accept / accept; practical contribution is valuable but would be strengthened by the requested comparisons and analyses.
