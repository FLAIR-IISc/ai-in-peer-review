Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible procedure for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. For each training example, the algorithm routes the gradient only to the ensemble member that currently incurs the lowest loss (“winner-take-gradient”), effectively performing a hard assignment akin to K-means/Hard-EM but interleaved with standard minibatch SGD. This encourages members to specialize on different regions of the input/label space and jointly cover multiple plausible outputs. The paper positions sMCL as a practical, concurrent alternative to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS’12; AISTATS’13], which relies on alternating training and reassignment and is infeasible with modern deep nets. It also relates to work on diverse M-best for structured models [e.g., Kirillov et al.; Batra et al.], ensemble diversity methods, and to expert-based architectures (mixture-of-experts), though here specialization emerges implicitly from the oracle objective without a learned gating network.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 (classification), Pascal VOC with FCNs (segmentation), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM). sMCL consistently outperforms independently trained ensembles, Dey et al.’s submodular/boosting-like approach, and matches or exceeds MCL while being substantially faster. The paper provides qualitative analyses showing emergent specialization (e.g., class-wise clustering in CIFAR-10; different structural biases for segmentation; diverse sentence structures in captioning).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, parameter-free algorithm that drops into any SGD-trained model and any loss; very easy to implement.
- Clear connection to MCL with a practical stochastic optimization that removes the costly retrain/alternate loop.
- Strong, thorough experiments across three domains and architectures, including qualitative analyses of specialization and a useful ablation (top-k winners) showing specialization–performance trade-offs.
- Good presentation of oracle metrics and diversity analyses; comparisons to independent ensembles and relevant baselines.

Weaknesses
- Major omission: the paper does not address how to use the trained ensemble at test time without an oracle. All results are in oracle (top-k) terms; there is no selector/gating, re-ranking, or decision procedure. This limits practical usability and makes comparisons to real deployment scenarios incomplete.
- Exploitation strategies are problem-specific and not explored here (e.g., learned selector, confidence-based routing, diversity-aware re-ranking, or distillation), so it is unclear how the reported oracle gains translate to end-task performance.
- Potential load imbalance/instability concerns of winner-take-all routing (early dominance of one expert) are not deeply analyzed; no balancing regularizer or annealing is explored beyond the k-winners ablation.
- Related work could more directly compare to mixture-of-experts with learned gating (classic MoE), which is the canonical way to obtain specialization and test-time routing.
- Presentation issue: Fig. 2 pseudocode appears as a rasterized image rather than selectable text/vector; this hampers readability and reproducibility.

Quality
- Technically sound: the gradient routing follows directly from the MCL upper-bound formulation; the non-differentiable argmin is handled in the forward assignment step, consistent with hard-EM style training. Claims (speedup vs. MCL, diversity/specialization, oracle gains) are well supported empirically.
- Missing: test-time exploitation experiments. Even simple heuristics (e.g., choose the member with the highest calibrated confidence; a small gating network trained on a validation set; per-task re-rankers) would make the contribution much stronger and demonstrate impact beyond oracle metrics.
- Some additional diagnostics would help: assignment entropy over epochs (to check collapse), per-class/member coverage, wall-clock training cost vs. independent ensembles.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the method is easy to understand and reproduce. Figures illustrating specialization are effective.
- Please replace the rasterized pseudocode with selectable text or vector graphics; include tie-breaking details and default hyperparameters. Minor suggestion: make explicit the initialization/training schedule to avoid early collapse.

Originality
- The core idea—optimizing oracle loss via hard assignment—builds directly on MCL, but the stochastic, concurrent SGD formulation for deep nets is novel, practical, and impactful. The work is distinct from “diverse M-best” structured inference and from prior ensemble-diversity methods. Positioning with respect to mixture-of-experts/gating could be strengthened.

Significance
- The method is broadly applicable and produces sizable oracle gains on important tasks. Many practitioners want multiple high-quality hypotheses; this will likely be used or extended.
- However, significance for real deployments hinges on test-time selection. Without an oracle or a proposed selector, it is difficult to assess end-to-end gains. Including even simple exploitation studies would substantially increase impact.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide at least one generic, lightweight exploitation strategy:
  - Train a small gating network on held-out data to predict the winning expert per instance; report top-1 performance and calibration.
  - Confidence-based selection (e.g., max softmax/sequence log-prob, entropy) with or without temperature scaling; compare to independent ensembles.
  - Distillation of the per-instance winning outputs into a single student model (knowledge distillation) to remove oracle dependence.
- Analyze load balancing: distribution of assignments per member over epochs; consider a mild balancing regularizer or an annealed k-winners schedule.
- Clarify tie-breaking and initialization; did you observe early collapse? Any warm-up with independent training?
- Provide wall-clock time/memory comparisons vs. independent ensembles and MCL for each task.
- For captioning, complement unique n-grams with diversity metrics (e.g., Self-BLEU/mBLEU) and show sMCL+beam search results.

Pros
- Simple, general, and parameter-free training algorithm.
- Strong and broad empirical improvements on oracle metrics.
- Emergent specialization and insightful analyses.
- Practical speedup over alternating MCL.

Cons
- No test-time exploitation mechanism; practicality limited without an oracle.
- Limited discussion of load balancing/assignment dynamics.
- Missing comparison to learned-gating MoE baselines.
- Minor presentation issue (rasterized pseudocode).
