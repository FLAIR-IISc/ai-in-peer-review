This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-compatible procedure for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss: for each training instance, the “winner-take-gradient” member (the one with lowest loss) receives the backprop signal, encouraging specialization across the ensemble. The work builds directly on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) by Guzman-Rivera et al., replacing costly alternating retraining with a simple per-batch assignment, and relates to diversity-promoting inference in structured prediction (e.g., M-Best Diverse) and ensemble learning. Unlike mixture-of-experts, sMCL has no gating network; diversity arises from oracle-driven assignments.

Quality: The method is technically sound and easy to implement in standard SGD frameworks. Empirical results are strong and broad (classification, segmentation, captioning), with consistent oracle gains over independent ensembles, boosting-style approaches, and (when feasible) MCL, plus compelling analyses of emergent specialization. However, the paper does not address how to exploit the trained ensemble at inference time when no oracle exists. Without a selection mechanism or downstream integration strategy, practical deployment remains unclear and problem-specific. Additional evaluation of non-oracle metrics (e.g., average accuracy or mIoU when forced to pick a single output) would clarify trade-offs.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and the experimental section is thorough. Minor presentation issue: the pseudocode in Fig. 2 is embedded as a raster image; please replace with selectable text or a vector graphic for readability and reproducibility.

Originality: The core idea—using per-instance oracle selection to route gradients—is a crisp and novel adaptation of MCL to deep networks, avoiding alternating retraining. The distinction from prior ensembles, diversity sampling, and structured M-best inference is clear and appropriately cited.

Significance: Given its simplicity, speed, and strong empirical gains, sMCL is likely to be adopted as a practical baseline for training diverse deep ensembles and studying specialization. Its impact would be higher with a concrete, general-purpose inference strategy.

Pros:
- Simple, general, parameter-free algorithm with strong results across domains/architectures.
- Clear evidence of meaningful specialization and diversity.
- Significant speed advantage over classical MCL.

Cons:
- Major omission: no strategy to use the ensemble at test time without an oracle.
- Practical usability is limited; exploitation is delegated to downstream systems but not explored.
- Minor presentation issue (rasterized pseudocode).

Suggestions: Discuss and evaluate generic exploitation options (learned selector/gating, confidence calibration, reranking, mixture-of-experts post hoc), report single-prediction metrics, and normalize compute in comparisons (e.g., beam size vs ensemble size).
