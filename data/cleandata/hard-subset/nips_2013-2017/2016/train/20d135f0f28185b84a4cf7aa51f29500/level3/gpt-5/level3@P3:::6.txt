Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-era reformulation of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. In each mini-batch, the example’s loss is computed under each of M learners; the gradient is backpropagated only through the member with minimum loss (“winner-take-gradient,” a hard EM–like update). This interleaves assignment and optimization and avoids the costly alternating retrain-to-convergence schedules in prior MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al.]. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and parameter-free. Empirically, sMCL shows strong oracle improvements on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM). The paper argues that emergent “specialization” across ensemble members explains the gains.

The approach is closely related to: (i) classic MCL [NIPS’12; AISTATS’13], which introduced the oracle-loss objective and hard assignments; (ii) hard mixture-of-experts (MoE) and k-means / hard-EM, where assignment is recomputed and parameters updated for the assigned expert; (iii) ensemble diversity methods (e.g., negative correlation learning [Liu & Yao, 1999]) and more recent diversity regularization for neural networks (e.g., work by Chen et al. encouraging orthogonality/decorrelation), which are not discussed but are conceptually relevant; and (iv) multi-output inference methods (M-Best / diverse M-Best). Positioning sMCL more explicitly against MoE (no gating net here; assignment uses ground-truth losses) and diversity-regularized ensembles would strengthen the paper.

Strengths
- Simple, general training recipe that integrates seamlessly with SGD and modern deep nets.
- Strong and consistent empirical oracle gains across three modalities, with sensible analyses of k-winner variants and qualitative diversity.
- Practical contribution: concurrent training is far faster than alternating MCL, and likely the only feasible route for deep nets.
- The idea is clear and the gradient expression is correct given the hard-assignment upper bound.

Weaknesses and key concerns
- Test-time usage and evaluation: The paper reports oracle metrics (the “oracle picks the best prediction”), which implicitly use the ground truth at test time. This is fine as a diagnostic, but the intended downstream selector is not modeled or evaluated. Please clarify the testing protocol at line 159, p.5, and add non-oracle results using an automatic selector (e.g., max confidence; a small gating network trained on training-time assignments; ranker/reranker) to quantify practical utility.
- Baseline clarity: “Independent ensembles”/“regular ensembles” (Indp.) appear in text/figures but are insufficiently defined (line 181, p.5). Please specify training schedule, data ordering, augmentation, compute budget, and parameter counts to ensure a fair comparison. Report absolute training time/GPU hours for all methods.
- Specialization claims: The per-example reassignment scheme can induce transient “ownership” but does not guarantee stable or interpretable specialization. Provide stronger evidence: (i) temporal stability of assignments over epochs, (ii) mutual information between member ID and class (or other attributes), (iii) per-member usage rates and balance, and (iv) effect on calibration and error correlation.
- Missing related work on diversity regularization: Discuss and compare to diversity-encouraging ensemble training in neural nets (e.g., Chen et al. on orthogonality/diversity penalties; negative correlation learning; cosine/logit decorrelation; DPP-style penalties). These methods are close in spirit and more explicit, though under-studied in deep settings.
- Theory: While the gradient derivation is straightforward from the hard-assignment upper bound, there is no analysis of convergence/stability under stochastic reassignments (ties, noise). Even a brief discussion of conditions under which assignments stabilize, or connections to hard-EM/MoE convergence behavior, would help.
- Ablations: Provide sensitivity to M, batch size, tie-breaking, and k-winner (beyond qualitative). Report variance across seeds and error bars.

Quality
Technically sound in its core idea and implementation. The empirical section is substantial and uses standard architectures and datasets. However, the exclusive reliance on oracle metrics limits practical significance; additional non-oracle selection results are needed. Baselines need clearer definition; compute-matched comparisons (equalized total updates/epochs) would increase confidence.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow. Specific places need clarification:
- Testing protocol and the role of oracle metrics (line 159, p.5).
- Definition and training details of “Indp.”/“regular” ensembles (line 181, p.5).
- Tie-breaking and its impact on stability; whether assignments are per-example per-iteration or smoothed.
- Whether class imbalance or example difficulty causes member under-utilization; any balancing mechanism.

Originality
Moderate. The contribution is a neat and practically important SGD-era instantiation of MCL/hard-EM for deep nets. The “winner-take-gradient” update is intuitive and related to MoE with hard gating; the novelty is in its simplicity, applicability, and empirical breadth.

Significance
High practical significance for training diverse deep ensembles with multiple outputs. If accompanied by non-oracle selection results, the impact would broaden to real systems. The method is likely to be adopted due to its simplicity.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide non-oracle evaluation by adding: (a) max-confidence selector, (b) a learned gating network trained on sMCL assignments, and (c) simple aggregation (e.g., averaging logits) for classification/segmentation; report top-1 IoU/accuracy/CIDEr.
- Explicit specialization: Could you incorporate specialization into the loss? Examples: (i) diversity regularizers on logits/features (cosine/orthogonality/NC loss), (ii) balanced assignment constraints over classes (soft priors on pi,m), (iii) DPP/coverage loss over outputs. How do these affect oracle and non-oracle metrics?
- Quantify assignment stability and member expertise (temporal plots, MI, per-class purity).
- Compare to hard MoE: add a small gating net trained jointly without access to ground truth at test time; contrast oracle and non-oracle performance.
- Report absolute training time and ensure compute-matched baselines; include variance over multiple runs.

Pros
- Simple, parameter-free, broadly applicable method.
- Strong oracle improvements across diverse tasks.
- Significant training-time savings vs alternating MCL.
- Convincing qualitative diversity.

Cons
- Test-time story unclear; only oracle evaluation.
- Baselines insufficiently specified.
- Specialization claims need stronger quantification.
- Missing discussion of diversity-regularized ensembles.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
A solid, simple idea with strong empirical oracle results and clear practical relevance. Addressing the test-time selection/evaluation, baseline clarity, and specialization analysis would strengthen the paper significantly. Reviewer confidence: medium.
