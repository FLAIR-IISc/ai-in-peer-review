### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep neural network ensembles. The goal is to generate a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input, which is particularly useful in contexts where a downstream oracle (e.g., a human user) can select the best one. The core idea is simple yet effective: for each example in a training batch, only the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss (the "winner") receives a gradient update. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy is framed as a stochastic block gradient descent approach to directly minimize the oracle loss. The authors demonstrate the method's versatility and effectiveness across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). They show that sMCL significantly outperforms ensembles trained independently and other related methods, while being computationally much more efficient than prior work like Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) by Guzman-Rivera et al. A key finding highlighted is that this training procedure leads to emergent "specialization" among ensemble members, where different networks become experts on different subsets of the data (e.g., specific object classes).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a straightforward and clever adaptation of the MCL objective to the stochastic gradient descent setting, making it practical for deep learning. The claims of superior performance are well-supported by extensive experiments on three different tasks and architectures. The comparison against independently trained ensembles, the original MCL, and the method of Dey et al. is thorough and demonstrates significant improvements in oracle performance. The analysis of emergent specialization is interesting, though the justification for this phenomenon could be stronger (see weaknesses).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for generating multiple diverse outputs is clearly articulated in the introduction. The algorithm itself is explained simply and is easy to grasp. However, there are a few points that could be clarified:
1.  **Evaluation Procedure:** The use of "oracle" metrics is central to the evaluation, but the implications for a real-world system are not fully discussed. The paper states (line 159, p.5) that oracle evaluation separates the performance from a downstream re-ranking system. While this is a valid way to measure the quality of the generated set, it relies on having the ground truth at test time. The paper would be stronger if it briefly discussed how one might use the sMCL-trained ensemble in a practical setting where the oracle is not available (e.g., using model confidence, or feeding all hypotheses to a human). This is a crucial point for assessing the practical significance of the results.
2.  **Baselines:** In Figure 4b, the term "regular ensembles" is used, while the text primarily refers to "Indp. ensembles" or "Classical ensembles". While it seems they refer to the same thing (models trained independently from different initializations), this should be made explicit for consistency. The definition of "classical ensembles" is provided, but this minor inconsistency could cause confusion.

**Originality:** The core idea of minimizing oracle loss is not new and builds directly on the MCL framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. However, the key contribution—adapting this to a concurrent, stochastic gradient-based training procedure suitable for deep networks—is novel and significant. This adaptation makes the MCL paradigm practical for the large-scale models prevalent today. The paper does, however, miss an opportunity to connect its work to a related line of research on explicit diversity regularization in ensembles (e.g., negative correlation learning). These methods add explicit penalty terms to the loss function to encourage decorrelation between ensemble members. A discussion of the relationship between sMCL's implicit diversity promotion (via oracle loss) and these more explicit techniques would better contextualize the work.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem: generating multiple plausible hypotheses for ambiguous inputs. The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple, parameter-free, broadly applicable, and empirically effective. Given its ease of implementation and strong performance, it is likely that researchers and practitioners could adopt this method for tasks where output diversity is valued. The 5x speedup over the previous state-of-the-art (MCL) is a substantial practical contribution that enables this line of work for deep architectures.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Simple and Effective Idea:** The "winner-take-gradient" approach is an elegant and simple method for optimizing the oracle loss directly.
2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Results:** The method is shown to be effective across three very different, modern deep learning tasks, consistently outperforming relevant baselines.
3.  **Practicality:** Unlike prior work (MCL), sMCL is computationally efficient and trains all ensemble members concurrently, making it a viable tool for deep learning practitioners.
4.  **Generality:** The method is agnostic to the network architecture and loss function, highlighting its broad applicability.

### Arguments for Rejection/Revision:
1.  **Unclear Practical Deployment:** The evaluation relies entirely on an oracle at test time. The paper lacks discussion on how the diverse outputs would be utilized in a real-world scenario without access to ground truth.
2.  **Weak Justification for Specialization:** The claim that networks "specialize" on object classes is intriguing but not fully explained. Since assignments are made on a per-example basis, it is not immediately obvious why specialization should occur at the class level rather than at a finer-grained, instance level. The empirical evidence is present, but the link to the training mechanism could be better justified or discussed more critically.
3.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss the literature on explicit diversity regularization in neural network ensembles, which is a closely related area.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:
-   Could you elaborate on how an sMCL-trained ensemble would be deployed in practice? Without a ground-truth oracle, how would a final prediction be selected, or how would the set of hypotheses be presented to a user?
-   Regarding the emergence of class-level specialization (Fig 4c), this is a fascinating result. Do you have a hypothesis for why the per-example winner-take-all strategy leads to such a coarse, class-level division of labor, as opposed to a more complex, inter-class partitioning?
-   As a follow-up, have you considered explicitly encouraging specialization? For example, one could augment the loss with a term that encourages each network to become an expert on a pre-assigned or dynamically discovered subset of classes. How might such an approach compare to sMCL?
-   Please consider adding a discussion of how sMCL relates to methods that explicitly enforce diversity through regularization terms in the loss function (e.g., negative correlation learning). This would help position your contribution more clearly within the broader ensemble literature.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The paper presents a simple, practical, and effective method for a relevant problem. The empirical results are strong. The main weaknesses concern clarity of the evaluation's practical implications and the positioning with respect to some related work, which can be addressed in a revision.
