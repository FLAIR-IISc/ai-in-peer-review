- Proposes sMCL: a simple, practical method that trains ensembles by backpropagating gradients only through the per-example "winner" model to optimize an oracle loss.
- Clearly motivated need for diverse multiple predictions useful for human-in-the-loop or downstream verification systems.
- Method is simple, efficient, and enables concurrent training of ensemble members, avoiding costly sequential retraining.
- General approach applicable across architectures and losses (classification, segmentation, captioning) demonstrating flexibility.
- Limited conceptual novelty — primarily an SGD-based adaptation of prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) rather than a fundamentally new idea.
- Section 3 is too brief; lacks rigorous derivation showing how the stochastic winner-take-gradient update optimizes the joint oracle objective.
- Paper fails to situate sMCL relative to related training schemes (multi-task alternatives, hard-attention, mixture-of-experts), leaving unclear distinctions and novelty.
- Experimental baselines are weak (mainly independently trained ensembles), limiting the strength of comparisons.
- Empirical gains are modest (small oracle accuracy gains on CIFAR-10, ~7% relative IoU improvement for segmentation), not clearly impactful.
- Uses outdated or non-standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC 2011 val, small CIFAR "Quick" network), reducing confidence in real-world effectiveness.
- Suggest expanding Section 3 with a clearer technical link between objective and stochastic algorithm.
- Suggest adding discussion contrasting sMCL with alternative training, hard-attention, and mixture-of-experts methods to clarify contributions.
- Recommend stronger empirical validation: evaluate segmentation on PASCAL VOC 2012 test and compare to contemporary, diversity-promoting ensemble methods.
- Overall: practical and well-motivated method with engineering value, but limited novelty, weak positioning, and only modest experimental improvements make it a borderline contribution for a top venue.
