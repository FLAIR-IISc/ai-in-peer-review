This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple and effective algorithm for training ensembles of deep networks to produce diverse sets of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to optimize the ensemble for "oracle loss," where for any given training example, the loss is defined by the performance of the single best model in the ensemble. The proposed sMCL algorithm operationalizes this by backpropagating the gradient for each example in a mini-batch only to the "winner" model—the one with the lowest loss on that example. This approach is a direct adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] to the stochastic gradient descent setting, which is the key contribution that enables its application to large-scale deep neural networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—showing significant improvements in oracle performance and qualitatively more diverse outputs compared to standard ensembles and other baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses the important problem of generating multiple plausible hypotheses, which is highly relevant for systems that interact with human operators or other downstream components. The primary contribution is taking the elegant but computationally prohibitive MCL framework and making it practical for modern deep learning. The resulting sMCL algorithm is simple, general (agnostic to loss function and architecture), parameter-free, and efficient, as it trains all ensemble members concurrently. This simplicity and effectiveness make it a highly useful tool that researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt.

2.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors test their method on three challenging and diverse tasks, using standard architectures for each (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). The consistent and significant gains in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles and other baselines strongly support the paper's claims. The qualitative results, particularly the visualizations of emergent specialization (e.g., Figures 4c, 6, and 7), provide compelling intuitive evidence for how the method works.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is described concisely, and the relationship to prior work is stated transparently. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incremental Originality:** The main concern with this paper is that the core algorithmic idea is not entirely new. The concept of minimizing oracle loss via an alternating assignment-and-retraining procedure was introduced in the original MCL paper [8]. The contribution here is the adaptation of this idea to an online, stochastic setting ("winner-takes-gradient"). While this adaptation is what makes the method scalable and impactful, the conceptual leap from the original MCL is arguably small. The novelty lies in the implementation and application rather than a fundamental new learning principle.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

Despite the concern about incremental novelty, I believe the practical significance of making this learning paradigm viable for deep networks outweighs this weakness. The paper presents a complete piece of work with strong results. I lean strongly towards acceptance. However, I believe the paper could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Comparison with a Batched Version of MCL:** The paper compares sMCL to the original MCL algorithm [8], which operates on the entire dataset in an alternating fashion and is understandably slow. A more direct and informative baseline would be a mini-batch version of the MCL algorithm. That is, an algorithm that alternates between (a) assigning each example in a mini-batch to its current best-performing model, and (b) updating each model only on the examples assigned to it within that batch. This would help clarify whether the key advantage of sMCL comes from the tight interleaving of the assignment and update steps, or simply from moving to a batch-based setting.

2.  **More Quantitative Analysis of Diversity:** The paper effectively argues that sMCL promotes diversity and provides compelling qualitative examples. This could be substantiated with more quantitative analysis. For instance:
    *   In the classification experiments, what is the average number of distinct classes predicted by the ensemble for a given image? How does this compare to a standard ensemble?
    *   For segmentation, one could measure the diversity by computing the average pairwise IoU between the predictions from different ensemble members for the same image. Lower pairwise IoU would indicate greater diversity.
    *   For captioning, the authors note that sMCL produces more unique n-grams, which is a good start. This could be extended by reporting metrics like the average pairwise BLEU or CIDEr scores between the M generated captions for each image. This would provide a more direct measure of semantic diversity.

In summary, this is a well-executed and clearly communicated paper presenting a simple but highly effective method for training diverse deep ensembles. While the core idea builds directly on previous work, its adaptation to the stochastic setting is a significant and valuable contribution that makes it practical for a wide range of important problems. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the utility of the proposed sMCL algorithm. I recommend acceptance.
