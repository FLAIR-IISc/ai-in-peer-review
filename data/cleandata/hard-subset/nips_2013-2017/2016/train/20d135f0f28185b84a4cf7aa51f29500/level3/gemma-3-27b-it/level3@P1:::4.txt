This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss. The core idea builds on previous work on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8, 9] but adapts it to the context of deep networks using a stochastic gradient descent approach. The authors argue that generating multiple hypotheses is beneficial in applications where a downstream process (an "oracle") evaluates the quality of solutions, and sMCL aims to train ensembles that produce sets of diverse and accurate predictions. The method is presented as parameter-free, applicable to various architectures and loss functions, and computationally efficient. Experiments are conducted on image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), demonstrating improvements over standard ensemble methods.

The paper’s strength lies in framing the problem of generating diverse outputs as a learning problem focused on minimizing oracle loss. This is a sensible and practical approach for applications where the final decision isn't solely based on the model's single prediction. The authors correctly identify the limitations of single-prediction models in ambiguous scenarios and propose a solution that attempts to mimic human reasoning by providing multiple plausible hypotheses. The application of the MCL paradigm to deep networks is a logical extension of prior work and potentially useful, especially given the challenges of retraining approaches like [8]. Furthermore, the claim of parameter-freedom and architectural agnosticism is appealing from a practical standpoint.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the originality of the contribution is limited. The work largely adapts existing concepts from MCL [8] to deep learning, and the core technical innovation—using stochastic gradient descent—feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. While addressing the scalability issues of earlier MCL approaches is valuable, it doesn’t constitute a paradigm shift.  The connection to related work feels superficial; the authors briefly mention alternatives but fail to deeply engage with or differentiate their work.

Secondly, the technical presentation, particularly Section 3, is surprisingly concise. Given the importance of the stochastic gradient descent formulation, a more detailed mathematical derivation and discussion would have strengthened the paper.  The jump from the objective function to the proposed algorithm feels abrupt, lacking sufficient explanation. The paper fails to clearly articulate how sMCL differs from established multi-task learning or ensemble training techniques using regularization to promote diversity. This ambiguity leaves the reader questioning the fundamental advantage of the proposed approach.

Thirdly, the experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing. The baselines chosen—standard ensembles—are relatively weak. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art ensemble methods (e.g., those utilizing dropout, adversarial training, or specific diversity-inducing techniques) is crucial to demonstrating the true effectiveness of sMCL. While improvements are reported across the tested tasks, the gains appear modest and may not justify the added complexity of the method. The authors acknowledge the need for stronger baselines in the conclusion. 

Finally, the writing quality is subpar. The paper contains instances of convoluted phrasing and lacks clarity in several sections. A more polished and concise presentation would significantly improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a practically relevant problem: generating diverse outputs for applications involving human-in-the-loop or verification steps.
* Provides a scalable adaptation of the MCL framework to deep networks.
* Demonstrates improvements over simple ensemble baselines across multiple tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited originality: primarily an application of existing concepts.
* Weak experimental evaluation with insufficient comparison to strong baselines.
* Concisely written technical section lacking sufficient detail.
* Unclear differentiation from related techniques like multi-task learning.
* Subpar writing quality and lack of clarity in presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a reasonable extension of existing work but falls short of being a significant contribution to the field.  While the idea of applying MCL to deep ensembles is promising, the limited novelty, weak evaluation, and technical shortcomings raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** with the suggestion that the authors strengthen the experimental validation (comparing against stronger baselines on a more challenging dataset such as PASCAL VOC 2012) and provide a more detailed and rigorous technical analysis to justify the benefits of their approach over existing methods. Addressing the clarity issues in the writing would also significantly improve the paper's impact.
