- Introduces sMCL: a winner-take-gradient training algorithm for ensembles that minimizes oracle loss by updating only the lowest-loss member per example.  
- Demonstrates sMCL across CIFAR-10, PASCAL VOC, and MSCOCO, outperforming independently trained ensembles and prior MCL variants.  
- sMCL is computationally efficient (≈5x speedup over prior MCL) and trains all ensemble members concurrently.  
- Training leads to emergent specialization, with ensemble members becoming experts on different data subsets (e.g., object classes).  
- Method is technically sound, simple, parameter-free, and broadly applicable to architectures and loss functions.  
- Strong empirical support with thorough comparisons to independent ensembles, original MCL, and Dey et al.  
- Paper is generally well-written and the algorithm is clearly explained, but some evaluation points need clarification.  
- Major concern: evaluation relies on an oracle at test time and lacks discussion of practical deployment without ground-truth.  
- Clarify inconsistent terminology for baselines ("regular ensembles" vs. "Indp. ensembles"/"Classical ensembles").  
- Weak justification for why per-example winner-take-all produces class-level specialization rather than instance-level partitioning.  
- Missing discussion of related work on explicit diversity regularization (e.g., negative correlation learning) and how sMCL compares.  
- Suggest authors discuss practical use cases (confidence-based selection, human-in-the-loop) and potential extensions encouraging specialization.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept — strong empirical and practical contribution but needs clearer evaluation implications and related-work positioning.
