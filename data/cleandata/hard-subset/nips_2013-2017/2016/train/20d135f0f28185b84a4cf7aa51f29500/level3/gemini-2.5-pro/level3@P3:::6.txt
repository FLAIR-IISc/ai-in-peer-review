This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple and efficient algorithm for training diverse deep learning ensembles. The core idea is to apply a "winner-take-gradient" strategy during training: for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that achieved the lowest loss. This approach directly optimizes the "oracle loss" in a stochastic, online fashion, making it suitable for deep networks. The authors position this work as a practical and scalable alternative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods that required costly retraining of models. The effectiveness of sMCL is demonstrated across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing significant improvements in oracle performance over baseline ensembles.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant in its simplicity. It is easy to implement, parameter-free, and agnostic to the specific network architecture or loss function. By training all ensemble members concurrently and avoiding the iterative retraining of previous MCL approaches, it provides a practical method for training diverse ensembles in the deep learning era.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents compelling and consistent results across a variety of domains. sMCL significantly outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines on oracle metrics for classification, segmentation, and captioning. The performance gains (e.g., ~7% relative improvement in segmentation IoU) demonstrate that the method is highly effective at its stated goal of minimizing oracle loss.

3.  **Interesting Emergent Specialization:** The qualitative results, particularly for classification and captioning, are intriguing. The observation that ensemble members appear to specialize on different semantic classes (Fig. 4c) or produce varied linguistic structures (Fig. 7) without any explicit supervision is a valuable finding. This suggests that optimizing for oracle loss is a powerful, implicit mechanism for encouraging functional diversity.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of Evaluation:** The testing and evaluation protocol is a major point of confusion. The "Oracle Evaluation" section (line 159) states that oracle metrics "report the highest score over all outputs for a given input." This implies that the ground truth label is used at test time to select the best-performing member for each example. While using an oracle for *evaluation* is a standard way to measure the potential of a diverse hypothesis set, the paper does not clearly distinguish this from a practical deployment scenario. This lack of clarity could lead readers to misinterpret the results as the performance of a deployed system, which is misleading. The manuscript would be greatly strengthened by explicitly stating that this metric evaluates the quality of the generated set, and discussing how such a set might be used in a real application (e.g., by a human-in-the-loop or a downstream re-ranking model).

2.  **Undefined Baselines:** The paper frequently refers to "independent ensembles," "regular ensembles," or "Indp. ensembles" (line 181) as a primary baseline. However, the training procedure for this baseline is never explicitly defined. One assumes this means training M models from different random initializations on the full dataset, but this should be clearly stated for clarity and reproducibility.

3.  **Justification of Specialization Claim:** The paper claims that sMCL induces member "specialization," particularly on specific output classes in the CIFAR-10 experiment. While the evidence in Figure 4c is striking, the mechanism is not fully justified. Since assignments are made on a per-example basis, it is not immediately obvious why this should lead to stable, class-level specialization. An alternative hypothesis is that members specialize on particular difficult examples, and the observed class-level effects are a secondary consequence. A more in-depth analysis—for example, tracking the stability of assignments to examples from a specific class over the course of training—would make this claim more convincing.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks a significant body of literature on promoting diversity in ensembles via explicit regularization. Techniques like Negative Correlation Learning (e.g., Liu & Yao, Neural Networks 1999) directly add a penalty term to the loss function to encourage decorrelation among ensemble members. While much of this work predates modern deep learning, the principles are highly relevant and more rigorous in their approach to diversity. Discussing sMCL in the context of these explicit diversity regularization methods would better situate its contribution and highlight the difference between implicit (via oracle loss) and explicit (via penalty terms) approaches.

**Questions and Suggestions for Authors:**

*   Please clarify the evaluation section to explicitly state that oracle metrics are used to measure the quality of the hypothesis set and do not assume a real-time oracle for prediction.
*   Please provide a precise definition of the "independent ensemble" baseline.
*   The connection between per-example winner selection and emergent class-level specialization is fascinating but not fully explained. Could you provide further analysis to support this link? For instance, how much do the "winning" member assignments for a class fluctuate during training?
*   As a follow-up to the specialization point: have you considered whether specialization could be encouraged more directly? For instance, could an explicit diversity term be added to the loss function to complement the implicit pressure from sMCL? This could provide an interesting comparison point and connect your work to the broader literature on ensemble diversity.
*   Please consider adding a discussion of related work on explicit diversity regularization to better contextualize your approach.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **Weak Acceptance**. The paper proposes a simple, novel, and empirically effective method for training diverse deep ensembles, which is a valuable contribution. The strong results across multiple challenging tasks demonstrate the significance of the approach. However, the paper is held back by a lack of clarity in its evaluation protocol, unsubstantiated claims about the specialization mechanism, and a failure to engage with relevant prior work on diversity regularization. If the authors can address these weaknesses, particularly the clarity of the evaluation, I believe this could be a strong paper.
