This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple, parameter-free SGD procedure that trains deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss via a winner-takes-the-gradient backprop rule. Relative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) work (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al.), which alternates assignment and full retraining, sMCL interleaves assignment with minibatch updates, enabling concurrent training and substantial speedups. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, and is evaluated on three diverse tasks—CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning—showing consistent oracle improvements over independent ensembles, MCL (at practical iteration budgets), and Dey et al.’s boosting-like approach.

Quality: The approach is technically straightforward and sound; the gradient derivation aligns with a block coordinate view of the MCL objective. Empirical support is strong across tasks, with sensible analyses of emergent specialization. However, despite heavy emphasis on “diversity,” no explicit diversity regularizer is used; diversity appears as a byproduct of optimizing oracle loss. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of stability (tie-breaking, assignment churn) and safeguards against learner starvation. A key experimental question: How were baseline MCL deep networks ensured to have converged—could early termination of learners harm ensemble performance?

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with reproducible details. Minor issues: reword the first abstract sentence for concision (“Many perception systems are embedded in larger pipelines with user or algorithmic oracles that can evaluate predictions.”). Tone down the emphasis on “diversity” in title/text to match the lack of an explicit term. Typo on line 108: first “f” should be “g.” Remove the extra period on line 115.

Originality: Conceptually incremental to MCL’s relaxation but novel and valuable in its practical SGD formulation for deep nets. The winner-takes-gradient layer is an elegant operationalization of the assignment step within backprop.

Significance: High. The method is easy to adopt, widely applicable, and provides consistent oracle gains and training speedups; likely to influence practice for multi-hypothesis deep learning.

Pros:
- Important problem; simple, general method.
- Strong results across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Concurrent training; substantial speedups over MCL.

Cons:
- Diversity is motivated but not explicitly modeled.
- Incremental vs. prior MCL; limited theoretical analysis of convergence/stability.
- Need clearer assurances about baseline convergence (especially MCL).
