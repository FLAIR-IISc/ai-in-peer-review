This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a set of multiple, high-quality predictions for a given input. This setting, known as Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), aims to minimize the "oracle loss," which is the loss of the best-performing hypothesis in the set. This is particularly relevant for real-world perception systems where ambiguity is common and providing a few plausible options to a downstream user or process is more valuable than a single, potentially incorrect guess.

The main contribution is a novel training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which adapts the MCL framework for modern deep learning. Previous work on MCL, such as Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2012), relied on a block coordinate descent algorithm that required retraining models to convergence, a process that is infeasible for deep networks. In contrast, sMCL trains all ensemble members concurrently using stochastic gradient descent. The core mechanism is elegantly simple: for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only to the single member that achieved the lowest loss on that example. This "winner-take-gradient" approach directly optimizes an upper bound on the oracle loss, is agnostic to the specific network architecture or loss function, and requires no additional hyperparameters. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across three diverse and challenging tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing significant improvements over independently trained ensembles and other baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Relevance and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. As deep learning models are deployed in complex systems, the ability to represent and handle ambiguity by producing multiple outputs is of great practical importance. This work provides a practical and effective solution for training deep ensembles to this end, making the MCL paradigm accessible for state-of-the-art models.

2.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL method is exceptionally simple to understand and implement. It can be realized by adding a straightforward selection step in the backward pass of an ensemble architecture. Its generality is a major advantage, as it can be applied to any model trainable with SGD and any standard loss function without modification.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a significant strength of this work. The authors test their method on three distinct tasks—classification, segmentation, and captioning—using established CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures. In all cases, sMCL demonstrates superior oracle performance compared to independently trained ensembles and other relevant baselines. The analysis of how different forms of specialization emerge (e.g., class-based for classification, structural for captioning) provides compelling insight into the behavior of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Framing Around "Diversity":** The paper heavily emphasizes the concept of "diversity" in the title, abstract, and motivation. However, the sMCL objective function does not contain an explicit term that encourages diversity. Diversity appears to be an emergent property of minimizing the oracle loss, rather than a direct optimization goal. This creates a slight mismatch between the framing and the technical contribution. While the resulting ensembles are indeed diverse, a more precise framing might center on "oracle loss minimization for deep ensembles."

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The proposed method can be viewed as a stochastic, online adaptation of the alternating optimization algorithm presented by Guzman-Rivera et al. (2012). Their method alternates between assigning each data point to its best predictor and then updating each predictor based on its assigned data. sMCL performs the same conceptual steps but on a per-batch basis within SGD. While this adaptation is crucial for making the approach viable for deep learning and represents a significant practical contribution, the core conceptual advance over prior work in MCL may be seen as incremental.

**Questions and Minor Points:**

*   The first sentence of the abstract could be rephrased for greater clarity and impact.
*   Given the point above about framing, the authors might consider slightly toning down the emphasis on "diversity" as the primary goal, and instead frame it more as a beneficial outcome of oracle loss minimization.
*   There is a typo on line 108: the function being learned is `g`, so the expression should read `argmin g,pi,m`.
*   There is an extra period in the middle of the sentence on line 115.
*   Could the authors provide more detail on the training protocol for the MCL baseline? The paper mentions it was infeasible to train to full convergence. How was the decision made to stop training for each meta-iteration? It is possible that prematurely stopping the training of individual learners could have unfairly handicapped the performance of this baseline.

In summary, this is a well-written and executed paper that makes a strong contribution. It introduces a simple, effective, and broadly applicable method for training deep ensembles to produce multiple high-quality outputs. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate clear benefits across several challenging domains. While the conceptual novelty relative to prior MCL work is debatable, the practical significance of successfully adapting this paradigm to deep learning is undeniable. The strengths of the paper strongly outweigh its weaknesses.
