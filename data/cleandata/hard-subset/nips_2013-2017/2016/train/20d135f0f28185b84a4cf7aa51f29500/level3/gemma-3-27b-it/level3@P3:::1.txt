This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training diverse ensembles of deep networks. The core idea builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] by framing ensemble learning as a multiple-choice problem where the goal is to minimize oracle loss – the loss incurred by selecting the best prediction from the ensemble. The key contribution lies in adapting this approach to deep networks by leveraging SGD, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members and avoiding the computationally expensive retraining required by the original method. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, method, and experimental results.

The work addresses a relevant problem: generating diverse and informative outputs, particularly in scenarios where a single prediction is insufficient (e.g., autonomous vehicles, human-computer interaction). The proposed sMCL algorithm is a practical and scalable solution for training such ensembles, demonstrated across diverse architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs) and tasks (image classification, segmentation, captioning). The experimental results consistently show improvements over standard ensembles and a comparison to the original MCL method shows both performance parity and significant speedup. The analysis suggesting emergent specialization of ensemble members is also compelling and provides some insight into the mechanism by which sMCL achieves its benefits.

However, the algorithmic novelty relative to Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] feels somewhat incremental. While the application of SGD to this problem is a significant engineering step, the core idea of minimizing oracle loss remains the same. The authors acknowledge this, and convincingly argue that the scalability afforded by SGD makes the approach far more practical. Nonetheless, a more detailed discussion of the theoretical implications of using SGD compared to the alternating block coordinate descent of [8] would be valuable. Is the stochasticity beneficial in any way beyond simply enabling scalability? Does it introduce any biases or limitations?

A key strength of the paper is the strong empirical validation. However, when using batches (as is typical in deep learning), a comparison against other batch-based ensemble diversity methods like MCL (which *can* be adapted for batch updates, although not as efficiently) is missing. Specifically, it would be informative to compare sMCL to a batch-implementation of the algorithm from Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], to better isolate the advantages of the SGD approach. 

Furthermore, while the paper demonstrates performance gains and provides some qualitative evidence of diversity (e.g., specialized classes in image classification, diverse segmentations), a more thorough analysis of the “diversity” effect would be beneficial. Simply observing specialization isn’t sufficient. Metrics beyond oracle performance, such as measuring the dissimilarity between the outputs of different ensemble members (e.g., using KL divergence or other distance metrics on the output distributions), could provide a more quantitative assessment of diversity. Exploring the trade-off between oracle performance and diversity would also be interesting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Practicality:** sMCL addresses a key limitation of prior work ([8]) by enabling scalable training of deep ensembles.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates performance improvements across various tasks and architectures.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Relevance:** The problem of generating diverse outputs is important in many real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** The core algorithmic idea builds heavily on existing work ([8]) and the novelty is largely in the engineering adaptation to deep learning.
*   **Missing Comparison:** A direct comparison with a batch-based implementation of [8] is absent.
*   **Limited Diversity Analysis:** The analysis of diversity is largely qualitative and lacks quantitative metrics.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe the strengths of this paper outweigh its weaknesses. While the algorithmic novelty may be incremental, the practical contribution of enabling scalable training of diverse deep ensembles is significant. The strong empirical results and clear presentation further support acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper, contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer’s concerns by: 1) including a comparison with a batch-based implementation of the MCL algorithm from [8], and 2) adding a more quantitative analysis of the diversity of the ensemble outputs.
