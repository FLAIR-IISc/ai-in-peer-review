## Review of “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles”

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by leveraging an oracle to select the optimal network for each instance during backpropagation. The core idea – encouraging specialization within an ensemble to cover a wider range of outputs – is compelling and well-motivated by the context of perception systems embedded in larger applications requiring multiple plausible hypotheses. The paper positions this work within the broader landscape of ensemble learning and Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), clearly differentiating sMCL from prior work that often suffers from computational costs associated with retraining or sequential training. 

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The approach is conceptually simple, broadly applicable (agnostic to architecture and loss function), and computationally efficient. The experimental results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning are strong, consistently demonstrating sMCL's superiority over baseline ensembles and other related methods like MCL and Dey et al. The analysis revealing emergent specialization in ensemble members – visualized effectively in the classification experiments – adds further insight into the method's behavior. The paper is well-written and the experiments are thorough, providing convincing evidence of sMCL’s effectiveness.

However, a significant weakness severely limits the practical usability of this work. The paper completely omits any discussion of *how to actually use* the trained ensemble at inference time *without* access to the oracle. While the training process is elegantly designed around oracle feedback, real-world deployment scenarios often lack such a direct evaluation mechanism. The authors acknowledge the problem-specificity of exploitation strategies but offer no exploration or suggestions, leaving a critical gap in the overall contribution. This makes the method more of a theoretical advancement than a directly applicable solution.

Furthermore, the presentation contains a minor flaw: Fig. 2’s pseudocode is embedded as a raster image, hindering readability and preventing easy copy-pasting or vector scaling. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Strong empirical results across diverse tasks.
* Novel and well-motivated approach to ensemble learning.
* Clear and thorough presentation of experimental setup and results.
* Significant improvement over existing methods (MCL, baseline ensembles).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Critical omission of inference strategies without the oracle.
* Limited practical usability due to lack of exploitation strategies.
* Minor presentation issue with Fig. 2.



**Overall:** While the paper presents a technically sound and original contribution with strong empirical validation, the lack of discussion regarding practical inference significantly diminishes its impact. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the inference problem in future work. Addressing this crucial gap would elevate this paper from a compelling research study to a truly impactful contribution to the field.
