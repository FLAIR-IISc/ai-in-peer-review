Summary
This paper addresses the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) setting for deep networks, where a system outputs M hypotheses and is evaluated by an oracle that selects the best one. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” training strategy: for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates; others receive zero gradients for that example. This provides an online analogue to the hard assignment in MCL [8, 9], but trains all ensemble members concurrently with standard SGD. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and does not require additional hyperparameters. Experiments across three domains—CIFAR10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM)—show consistent gains in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, sequential/boosting-like ensembles [5], and a reimplementation of classical MCL [8]. Qualitatively, the authors report interpretable specializations (e.g., class specialists for CIFAR10, shape/frequency biases for segmentation, and n-gram/style diversity for captioning).

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on MCL [8, 9], which used alternating assignment and full retraining; sMCL can be viewed as a stochastic block gradient descent variant that interleaves assignment with SGD updates. It is related to mixture-of-experts and hard-EM with latent assignment variables, but notably uses the task loss to induce specialization without an explicit gating network. It complements diversity-inducing ensemble methods [19, 21, 26] and diverse M-best inference in structured models [3, 15, 16, 23, 24], and is practically distinct from sequential submodular/boosting approaches [5] by enabling parallel training of deep ensembles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The gradient derivation is straightforward and correct given the hard 0/1 assignment (Eq. 2). The algorithm is easy to implement and fits modern deep learning training loops. However, the paper lacks formal convergence guarantees or analysis of assignment stability (e.g., under mini-batch noise).
- Empirical support: Results on three tasks and multiple architectures are convincing and consistently favor sMCL in oracle metrics. The analysis of specialization (e.g., class-wise assignments and k-winner ablation) is insightful. Fairness of comparisons is mostly adequate, though details about MCL’s convergence and compute budget are important (see questions).
- Limitations: The paper emphasizes “diversity” but achieves it implicitly through oracle-loss minimization rather than an explicit diversity regularizer; it would help to position the contribution as specialization under oracle loss.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful figures and ablations. The description of Algorithm 2 is concise and sufficient for reproduction, but a few implementation details would improve clarity (tie-breaking, batch-size sensitivity, and initialization effects on specialization). Minor wording/typo suggestions are listed below.

Originality
The key idea—interleaving MCL-like hard assignments with SGD by routing gradients to the current winner—is a natural and practical extension of [8, 9] tailored to deep networks. Conceptually, this is incremental relative to prior MCL but is original in its deep learning instantiation and practical significance (parallel training, no retraining). The positioning relative to mixture-of-experts and specialist/generalist architectures [2, 12] could be sharpened.

Significance
The contribution is practically useful: it provides the first scalable, simple procedure to train deep ensembles for oracle metrics across classification, segmentation, and captioning. Many applications care about top-k or oracle performance; thus, the method is likely to be adopted. The qualitative emergence of interpretable expertise is appealing, though more quantitative diversity/specialization metrics would strengthen the case.

Strengths
- Simple, parameter-free procedure easily dropped into existing training loops.
- Concurrent training avoids the retraining/sequential overheads of [8, 5]; demonstrated 5x speedup over MCL baseline.
- Strong, consistent oracle improvements across diverse tasks/architectures.
- Insightful analyses of specialization and an informative k-winner ablation.
- Broad relevance to NIPS topics on ensembles, structured prediction, and deep learning.

Weaknesses
- Conceptually incremental to MCL [8, 9]; limited theoretical analysis (no convergence guarantees, assignment stability).
- Heavy emphasis on “diversity” without an explicit diversity mechanism; diversity is emergent and task-loss driven.
- Oracle-metric focus is appropriate but does not show impact on downstream selection/reranking (e.g., learned or human-in-the-loop oracles).
- Fairness/compute details for the MCL baseline are under-specified; early stopping may bias results.

Questions for the authors
- MCL baseline: How did you ensure each meta-iteration reached convergence? What stopping criteria were used and what were the wall-clock budgets per method? Could premature stopping of some learners reduce ensemble diversity and hurt the reported MCL results?
- Tie breaking and stability: How are ties between ensemble members resolved in practice? Is performance sensitive to batch size or data order (e.g., winner oscillations early in training)?
- Collapse avoidance: Did you observe “expert collapse” (one member winning most examples)? If so, how did you mitigate it (e.g., warm-start with independent pretraining, learning-rate schedules)?
- Load balance: Can you report entropy of assignment distributions across members during training and at convergence?
- Captioning: In addition to oracle CIDEr, can you report diversity metrics (e.g., distinct-n) and human preference for variety? How does increasing beam width interact with sMCL?
- Beyond k=1: The k-winner ablation is interesting; could a soft top-k weighting (e.g., temperature-controlled softmax over per-example losses) provide smoother training?

Minor comments and typos
- Abstract first sentence: consider rewording for concision.
- Title/text: consider toning down “diverse” or clarifying “diverse via oracle-loss specialization.”
- Line 108: first “f” should be “g.”
- Line 115: remove extra period in the middle of the sentence.
- Clarify “parameter-free”: M must be chosen; perhaps clarify that no new continuous hyperparameters are introduced.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, simple algorithm with broad applicability and strong empirical gains.
- Addresses a timely and relevant problem (top-k/oracle training for deep ensembles).
- Clear analyses showing emergent specializations and ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental relative to prior MCL; limited theoretical depth.
- Emphasis on “diversity” may be overstated without explicit mechanisms.
- Some baseline/computation details need clarification to confirm fairness.

Overall assessment
A solid, practical contribution that will likely be useful to the community. Despite its conceptual simplicity, the paper convincingly demonstrates that sMCL is an effective and scalable way to train deep ensembles for oracle metrics across varied tasks. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the MCL baseline protocol and adjusting claims around “diversity.”
