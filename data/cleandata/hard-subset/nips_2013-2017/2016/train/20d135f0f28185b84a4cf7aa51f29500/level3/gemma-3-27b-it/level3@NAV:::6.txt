## Review of “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles”

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep ensembles designed to produce diverse outputs suitable for scenarios requiring multiple hypotheses (Multiple Choice Learning). The core idea – updating only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for a given training example – is elegantly simple and demonstrated empirically to achieve strong performance on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks. The approach is presented as a parameter-free, architecture-agnostic extension of stochastic gradient descent, which is a significant advantage.

The work builds upon previous research in ensemble learning and Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), specifically referencing Guzman-Rivera et al.’s work. However, it distinguishes itself by providing a computationally tractable solution for deep networks, addressing a key limitation of earlier approaches that required costly retraining or sequential training. The authors effectively demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms classical ensembles and other baselines, achieving competitive oracle performance. 

Despite the strong empirical results, several aspects require clarification. The testing phase is vaguely described; it’s strongly suspected (and stated in the rebuttal) that the authors utilize ground truth during testing to select the best member (line 159, p.5), which significantly impacts the reported results and needs a more transparent explanation. Furthermore, the terms “independent ensembles” (Indp.) and “regular ensembles” are repeatedly used in figures and text without definition, hindering complete understanding. 

The claim of member “specialization” to different output classes, particularly in the classification task, feels overstated given the per-example training reassignments. While label-space clustering is observed, this emerges as a consequence of optimization rather than being explicitly enforced. A natural question arises: could specialization be more effectively incorporated by including a diversity-promoting term directly in the loss function? This could potentially enhance and stabilize the observed specialization. Finally, the paper overlooks relevant related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, such as Chen et al.’s work, which offers more rigorous theoretical frameworks for achieving diversity, especially within deep learning settings. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Simple yet effective algorithm with strong empirical results.
*   Addresses a practical problem (generating multiple hypotheses) with a novel solution.
*   Computationally efficient and readily applicable to existing deep learning frameworks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Testing procedure lacks transparency and may overestimate performance.
*   Undefined terminology ("independent ensembles," "regular ensembles").
*   Claim of member specialization feels weak and lacks thorough justification.
*   Missing discussion of relevant work in diversity regularization.



**Overall:** While not without its flaws, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. Given the strong empirical results and the simplicity of the proposed approach, I recommend **weak acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the testing procedure, clarifying the undefined terms, and discussing related work on diversity regularization.
