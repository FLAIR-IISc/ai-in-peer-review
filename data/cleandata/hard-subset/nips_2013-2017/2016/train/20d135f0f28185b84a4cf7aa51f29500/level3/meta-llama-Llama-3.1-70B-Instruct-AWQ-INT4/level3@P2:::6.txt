The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle in the context of multiple choice learning. The authors introduce a new algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. The method is evaluated on various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, and demonstrates lower oracle error compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution. The related work section is comprehensive, and the authors adequately reference previous contributions in the field. However, the reviewer notes that the related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.) is not discussed, which is a closely related and more rigorous literature that is under-studied in deep settings.

The proposed algorithm, sMCL, is straightforward to implement and appears to be effective in minimizing the oracle loss. However, the testing phase is unclear, and the reviewer suspects that the authors use ground truth to select the best member. The terms "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" (Indp.) appear in the text and figures but are not explained, which may cause confusion for readers.

The claim of member "specialization" to different output classes is not convincingly justified, as training reassignments occur on a per-example basis. The reviewer wonders whether specialization could be explicitly incorporated into the loss function to improve the results. Nevertheless, the empirical results presented in the paper are strong, and the algorithm appears to be effective in various tasks.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. The paper is well-written, and the organization is clear.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. However, some minor clarifications are needed, such as explaining the terms "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" (Indp.).

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel algorithm that is different from previous contributions in the field. The authors provide a unique perspective on the problem of multiple choice learning and propose a solution that is simple, effective, and widely applicable.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in machine learning, and the proposed algorithm has the potential to be widely used in various applications. The results presented in the paper are strong, and the algorithm appears to be effective in various tasks.
