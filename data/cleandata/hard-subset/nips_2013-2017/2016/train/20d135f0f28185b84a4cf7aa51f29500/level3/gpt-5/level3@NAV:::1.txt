This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-based training scheme for deep ensembles that optimize oracle (min-of-losses) performance. Building on Multiple Choice Learning of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which alternates between assigning examples to the lowest-loss model and retraining to convergence, the authors replace global alternating minimization with a “winner-take-gradient” mini-batch update: for each example, only the lowest-loss predictor receives the gradient. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and concurrently trains all ensemble members. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning show consistent oracle improvements over independent ensembles, Dey et al. [5], and a strong speed advantage over MCL. The paper also documents emergent specialization/diversity patterns.

Quality: The method is technically sound; the gradient argument is simple and correct under the hard assignment objective. Empirical results span multiple tasks and architectures with clear oracle gains and speedups. That said, deeper analysis would strengthen the claims: (i) convergence/stability under tie-breaking, (ii) sensitivity to batch size, ensemble size M, and curriculum, (iii) ablations of hard vs soft assignments (e.g., temperature-smoothed or top-k updates), and (iv) statistical significance across runs. A particularly important missing comparison is to an MCL variant that updates assignments within mini-batches rather than retraining to full convergence.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized, with clean exposition, motivating examples, and intuitive figures. Implementation details are mostly sufficient; please specify tie-breaking policy, report wall-clock/time-per-epoch, and ensure fairness in the number of epochs for all baselines.

Originality: Algorithmic novelty over [8] is incremental—essentially replacing block-coordinate retraining with stochastic hard assignment—but it is a meaningful change that enables practical scaling to modern deep nets.

Significance: High practical impact. A simple drop-in procedure that reliably improves oracle metrics and induces useful diversity is likely to be adopted by practitioners across tasks.

Pros:
- Simple, general, and effective; scales MCL to deep networks
- Strong, multi-domain empirical results; interpretable specializations
- Parameter-free and easy to implement

Cons:
- Incremental conceptual novelty vs. [8]
- Missing comparison to batchwise/online MCL
- Limited analysis of diversity mechanisms and training dynamics

Recommendation: Weak accept/accept. The contribution is primarily practical but valuable; addressing the requested comparisons and diversity analyses would solidify the paper.
