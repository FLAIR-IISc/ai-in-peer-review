This paper tackles the problem of training deep ensembles to produce multiple hypotheses optimized for oracle (top-k) performance. Building on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS], the authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” variant compatible with SGD: for each example in a minibatch, only the ensemble member incurring the lowest loss backpropagates a gradient; others receive zero signal for that example. This interleaves assignment and learning at every step, avoids repeated retraining of models, and is applicable across architectures and loss functions. The method is evaluated on three tasks—CIFAR10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM)—where sMCL consistently improves oracle metrics over independent ensembles, submodular/boosting-style diversity [Dey et al., ICCV], and a block-coordinate MCL baseline (while being about 5x faster than the latter). The qualitative analyses show interpretable specializations emerging across ensemble members (e.g., class specialists in CIFAR10, segmentation shape/frequency biases, and n-gram/style differences in captioning).

Relation to prior work: The paper positions itself against (i) classical ensembling for error decorrelation, (ii) methods for generating multiple diverse outputs in structured models (M-best diverse, DPPs, etc.), and (iii) MCL-style oracle-loss training. The main novelty over Guzman-Rivera et al. is making MCL practical for deep networks via an SGD-compatible update that performs assignments on-the-fly rather than via expensive alternating retraining. Compared to submodular/boosting approaches that add models sequentially, sMCL trains all members concurrently and is architecture-agnostic.

Quality: The method is technically simple and sound, derived directly from the block-coordinate MCL objective; the gradient expression makes the “winner-take-gradient” rule explicit. Experiments are broad and show consistent oracle improvements (reported as top-k accuracy/IoU/CIDEr-D). The analysis of emergent specialization is insightful. Some aspects merit further scrutiny: (a) fairness of the MCL baseline given limited meta-iterations; (b) sensitivity to initialization, batch size, and tie-breaking; (c) trade-offs on standard (non-oracle) metrics; and (d) robustness against “dead” experts or starvation early in training.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful figures and ablations (e.g., varying k winners). A few notation and editorial issues remain (see minor comments). The method description would benefit from a short paragraph on practicalities: tie-breaking policy, gradient scaling when only one model per example gets signal, and heuristics (if any) to prevent expert starvation.

Originality: Conceptually, sMCL is a direct stochastic instantiation of the MCL relaxation with indicator variables. The primary novelty is in the training procedure—bringing MCL to deep learning in a parameter-free, general, and efficient manner. While incremental relative to the MCL objective, the contribution is nontrivial in practice and broadly useful.

Significance: The ability to efficiently train multi-hypothesis deep ensembles is important across perception tasks with inherent ambiguity. The demonstrated gains, speedups over MCL, and emergent interpretable specializations make this contribution likely to be adopted and built upon. That said, the paper focuses on oracle metrics; demonstrating downstream selection/reranking or human-in-the-loop utility would further strengthen impact.

Strengths
- Simple, parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic algorithm that makes MCL practical for deep networks.
- Strong empirical results across three diverse tasks and model families, with consistent oracle gains.
- Concurrent training with substantial speedup over MCL; clear qualitative evidence of specialization.
- Clean connection to the MCL objective and straightforward implementation.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is primarily procedural; the objective mirrors prior MCL formulations.
- Diversity is emergent rather than enforced; paper heavily motivates “diversity” without an explicit term.
- Baseline MCL may be disadvantaged by limited meta-iterations; convergence fairness is unclear.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity (initialization, batch size, tie-breaking) and of non-oracle performance trade-offs.
- Captioning comparison blurs training vs inference (beam search) and could be contextualized more carefully.

Questions for the authors
- How did you determine convergence for the MCL baseline? Could early stopping of meta-iterations understate its potential, especially on segmentation/captioning?
- Did you observe “dead” experts? Any stabilization (e.g., warm starts, entropy regularization, periodic reassignment) to prevent starvation?
- How sensitive is sMCL to batch size and learning rate when only one learner gets a gradient per example? Did you adjust learning rates or use gradient scaling?
- How are ties broken in practice, and did different policies affect outcomes?
- Can you report standard (non-oracle) metrics to quantify trade-offs (e.g., top-1 vs top-k; caption quality vs diversity, e.g., Self-BLEU/distinct-n)?

Suggestions
- Include sensitivity analyses (initialization seeds, batch size, tie-breaking), and report non-oracle metrics.
- For captioning, complement unique n-grams with standard diversity metrics and human preference for set quality.
- Discuss lightweight mechanisms to mitigate expert starvation (e.g., occasional “exploration” updates).

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, widely applicable method that advances MCL for deep learning with clear empirical benefits.
- Strong, diverse experimental evidence and compelling qualitative insights.
- Simplicity and generality likely to catalyze follow-up work and adoption.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental conceptual advance relative to prior MCL; lack of explicit diversity mechanisms despite emphasis.
- Potentially unfair MCL baseline; limited analysis of stability/sensitivity and non-oracle trade-offs.

Minor comments and editorial fixes
- Abstract, first sentence: consider rewording for clarity and concision (e.g., “Many perception systems operate within larger processes that can evaluate candidate predictions.”).
- Title/text: consider toning down “diverse” unless an explicit diversity mechanism is introduced; “multiple hypotheses” or “oracle loss” may be more precise.
- Notation: line 108—first “f” should be “g.”
- Typo: line 115—remove the extra period “..”.
- Clarify in Algorithm 2 that ties are broken deterministically/randomly, and note any effect on results.
- Consider adding a brief note on computational overhead per batch (extra forward passes, negligible backward for losers).

Overall: a solid, practically impactful paper with broad applicability; I lean positive, contingent on clarifying the MCL baseline comparison and adding limited sensitivity/robustness analyses.
