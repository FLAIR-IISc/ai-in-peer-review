This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The goal is to minimize the "oracle loss," which is the loss of the best prediction within the ensemble for a given input. This setting is motivated by practical applications where a downstream system or a human user can select the correct output from a set of plausible hypotheses. The core technical contribution is a simple and efficient stochastic block gradient descent optimization strategy: for each training example in a mini-batch, only the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss (the "winner") receives a gradient update for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows for concurrent, end-to-end training of all models.

The proposed method builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9]. However, previous MCL methods relied on an alternating optimization scheme that required retraining models to convergence, making them infeasible for modern deep networks that can take weeks to train. By integrating the assignment step directly into the stochastic gradient descent loop, sMCL provides the first practical algorithm for applying the MCL objective to deep learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification (CNNs), semantic segmentation (FCNs), and image captioning (CNN+RNNs), showing significant improvements in oracle performance over baselines.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of sMCL as a stochastic optimization of the MCL objective is clear and well-motivated. The claim that this approach is general, parameter-free, and efficient seems well-supported. The experiments are conducted on standard datasets and with contemporary deep learning architectures, providing convincing evidence that sMCL outperforms several baselines, including independently trained ensembles, the original MCL algorithm, and a more recent boosting-style method [5].

However, the experimental setup raises a significant concern regarding the fairness of comparisons. In the semantic segmentation experiment (Section 4.2), the sMCL models are initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. It is not specified whether the baseline methods (MCL and Dey et al.) were provided with the same pre-trained initialization. If the baselines were trained from random initialization while sMCL received a strong "warm start," the comparison would be unfair and could overstate the benefits of sMCL. The authors should clarify this point and, ideally, report results for all methods under identical initialization schemes. Furthermore, the paper does not investigate the sensitivity of sMCL to initialization, which would be an important aspect for understanding the method's robustness.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for generating multiple diverse outputs is clearly explained with compelling examples in Figure 1. The proposed algorithm is presented with laudable simplicity in Algorithm 2, making it easy to understand and implement. The experiments are described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented clearly through figures and tables. The qualitative examples in Figures 6 and 7 are particularly effective at illustrating the kind of diverse and meaningful outputs the sMCL-trained ensembles can produce.

**Originality**
The paper's primary contribution is the novel optimization scheme that makes the existing MCL paradigm practical for deep learning. While the idea of training ensembles to minimize oracle loss is not new [8], adapting it into an efficient, concurrent SGD-based procedure is a significant and original contribution. The resulting "winner-take-gradient" rule is an elegant and non-obvious solution to this problem.

However, the paper misses a citation and comparison to a highly relevant piece of work from the same time period:
- Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One: Training a Diverse Ensemble of Deep Networks," arXiv:1511.06314, Nov 2015.
This paper also proposes a method for training a diverse ensemble of deep networks (or "heads") in an end-to-end fashion by adding a diversity-encouraging loss term. A discussion of how sMCL relates to this approach would be necessary to fully contextualize its originality and contribution.

**Significance**
The work is highly significant. It addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple plausible hypotheses for complex perception tasks where ambiguity is inherent. By providing a simple, general, and computationally efficient method, the paper makes training diverse deep ensembles much more accessible. Researchers and practitioners working on tasks like autonomous driving, medical imaging, or human-computer interaction, where providing a set of options to a downstream module is valuable, are likely to find this method useful. The finding that different forms of interpretable specialization emerge automatically across different tasks is also an interesting and important result that could inspire future work on model specialization and interpretability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Practical Algorithm:** Proposes sMCL, a simple, elegant, and computationally efficient algorithm that makes the powerful MCL framework practical for deep learning.
2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates significant performance gains over multiple strong baselines on three diverse and challenging tasks using different network architectures.
3.  **High Significance and Impact:** Addresses an important problem with a general solution that is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.
4.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Potential Unfairness in Experiments:** The lack of clarity on the initialization scheme used for baseline methods in the segmentation task is a major concern that could undermine the validity of the reported performance gains.
2.  **Limited Evaluation of Diversity:** While the goal is to produce diverse outputs, the paper relies solely on oracle performance metrics. The inclusion of a direct quantitative measure of diversity would have made the claims stronger.
3.  **Missing Related Work:** The failure to discuss or compare with the highly relevant work of Lee et al. (2015) is a notable omission.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the weaknesses, the strengths of the paper—particularly the novelty, simplicity, and significance of the proposed method—are compelling. The concerns about experimental fairness and missing related work are significant but appear correctable. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address these points in the final version of the paper. Clarifying the experimental details and adding a discussion of related work would substantially improve the quality of this already strong contribution.
