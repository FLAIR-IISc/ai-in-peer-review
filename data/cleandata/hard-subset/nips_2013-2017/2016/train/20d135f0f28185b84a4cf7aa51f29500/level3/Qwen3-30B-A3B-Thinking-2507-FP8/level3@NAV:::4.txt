

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an approach to train diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss through a "winner-take-gradient" strategy. The authors frame their work as extending the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8] to deep networks, claiming their method avoids the costly retraining required by prior MCL approaches. The core idea is to backpropagate gradients only to the ensemble member producing the lowest loss for each example, enabling concurrent training of all ensemble members.

While the problem statement is well-motivated and the method is technically sound, the contribution appears incremental rather than substantive. The approach is essentially a neural-network adaptation of the MCL framework [8], with only minor modifications to make it compatible with SGD-based training. The technical section (Section 3) is alarmingly brief (just one page) and fails to sufficiently differentiate sMCL from standard multi-task learning or other ensemble methods. The distinction between sMCL and existing techniques for producing diverse predictions is particularly unclear.

The experimental evaluation is weak, using only modest improvements over baselines (typically 6-10%) with inadequate comparisons. The baselines include only simple independent ensembles and the original MCL approach, omitting stronger alternatives like recent diversity-focused methods. The paper lacks validation on standard benchmarks like PASCAL VOC 2012, which would be necessary to properly assess effectiveness in computer vision. While the qualitative analysis of "interpretable expertise" is interesting, it doesn't compensate for the lack of rigorous quantitative validation.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, simplicity of implementation, and broad applicability across multiple tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning). However, these strengths are overshadowed by the incremental nature of the contribution, poor technical exposition, and insufficient experimental validation. The authors fail to demonstrate that sMCL provides a meaningful advancement over existing approaches for training diverse ensembles. 

Pro: Clear problem statement, simple implementation, broad applicability
Con: Incremental contribution, weak baselines, poor technical exposition, insufficient validation

Overall, while the paper addresses a relevant problem, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors should substantially strengthen their evaluation with stronger baselines and more rigorous validation before reconsidering publication.
