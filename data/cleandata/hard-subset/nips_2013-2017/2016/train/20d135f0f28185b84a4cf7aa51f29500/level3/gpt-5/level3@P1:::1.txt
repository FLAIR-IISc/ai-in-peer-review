This paper addresses the multiple-prediction setting where an oracle can select the best of M outputs per input. Building on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8], the authors propose a stochastic gradient descent variant (sMCL) for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss directly. The key idea is a “winner-take-gradient” rule: for each training example, only the predictor with the lowest loss receives gradient signal; others receive zero. This yields specialization across ensemble members and can be applied to any differentiable architecture and loss. The paper demonstrates improved oracle performance and faster training versus prior MCL on three domains: CNNs for CIFAR-10 classification, FCNs for PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation, and CNN+RNN models for MSCOCO captioning. The authors also provide qualitative analyses showing interpretable specializations (e.g., class specialization in CIFAR-10, shape/frequency or class biases in segmentation, and n-gram diversity in captioning).

Relation to prior work: The work connects to diverse M-best inference and diverse decoding in structured prediction [3, 15, 16, 23, 24], to the MCL paradigm of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9], and to submodular/boosting-style sequential ensemble construction [5]. It also relates to ensemble methods that encourage diversity (e.g., data resampling, negative correlation, or joint losses [19, 26, 27]) and to expert architectures informed by a generalist [2, 12]. The novelty here is to bring MCL into the deep learning era by integrating the assignment and learning steps into a single, parallel, SGD-friendly procedure, which makes it practical at deep net scales.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and parameter-free algorithm that drops neatly into standard SGD workflows and supports arbitrary losses and architectures.
- Strong empirical results across heterogeneous tasks with consistent oracle improvements over independent ensembles, [5], and (where feasible) [8]; reported 5x wall-clock speedup over MCL.
- Clear evidence of emergent specialization; the k-winner ablation nicely connects diversity to oracle gains.
- Writing and exposition are clear; algorithm is easy to reproduce.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to [8]: replacing offline alternating minimization with an online/SGD interleaving of assignment and update. While impactful, the conceptual gap is modest.
- Limited quantitative analysis of diversity beyond oracle metrics; more systematic measures would strengthen the claims.
- Comparisons to MCL are limited by retraining cost; a batch-wise block-coordinate variant of MCL could provide a fairer head-to-head.
- Oracle-centric evaluation is appropriate for the setting but does not show downstream end-to-end gains; some discussion of selection/reranking integration would help.

Quality
- The method is technically sound and well-motivated from the gradient of the MCL objective (Eq. 2). The winner-take-gradient rule is a natural stochastic block coordinate descent instantiation.
- Empirics are solid: diverse tasks, architectures, and metrics. The captioning experiments include shared-backbone vs independent LSTMs and compare to beam search. Training details are sufficient for reproduction.
- Some experimental design aspects could be tightened: (i) compare ensembles with matched parameter counts/compute against a single multi-head model; (ii) provide more complete MCL baselines via mini-batch alternation; (iii) report variability over multiple runs and statistical significance.

Clarity
- The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. Algorithm 2 is concise and communicates the training loop clearly. Figures illustrating specialization are informative.
- Minor clarifications requested: tie-breaking policy and its effect on stability; sensitivity to batch size; behavior when members share early layers (captioning) vs are fully independent.

Originality
- The problem setting is not new (MCL) and diverse output generation is well studied, but the proposed SGD formulation is a practical advance that enables deep, end-to-end training without costly retraining or sequential procedures. The simplicity and breadth of applicability make the contribution worthwhile despite incremental conceptual novelty.

Significance
- Practical impact is high: many modern systems benefit from multiple high-quality hypotheses. sMCL provides a drop-in recipe to get diverse, oracle-optimized outputs across vision and language tasks.
- The observed specializations suggest further research (e.g., routing, confidence, or active learning). The method should be useful to practitioners and could become a standard baseline for multi-hypothesis training.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a mini-batch/block-coordinate MCL baseline: alternate a small number of assignment/update steps within each epoch to reduce the retraining burden and better isolate the benefit of stochastic assignments.
- Analyze diversity quantitatively in addition to oracle metrics: pairwise disagreement (classification), mean pairwise IoU or boundary F-scores (segmentation), self-BLEU/distinct-n conditioned on oracle score (captioning), and divergence between members’ predictive distributions.
- Ablations: effect of M, batch size, curriculum (starting with k-winners >1, annealing to 1), and shared vs independent backbones. Report training dynamics (how quickly assignments stabilize; degree of churn).
- Fair compute comparisons: match total parameters and test-time budget against single-model beam search or a single model generating M outputs, and discuss scenarios where sMCL’s multiple forward passes are preferable.
- Discuss integration with downstream selectors (e.g., learned rerankers), and whether sMCL changes calibration or uncertainty estimates.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable solution to a relevant problem with clear wins across multiple challenging tasks.
- Very simple to implement and likely to be adopted.
- Convincing qualitative and quantitative evidence of useful diversity and specialization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty over [8] is modest and theoretical analysis is limited.
- Diversity claims would benefit from stronger, task-specific quantitative measures and broader baselines (e.g., a batch-alternating MCL).

Overall, I favor acceptance: the contribution is pragmatic but meaningful, the paper is clear, and the empirical evidence suggests sMCL will be useful to the community.
