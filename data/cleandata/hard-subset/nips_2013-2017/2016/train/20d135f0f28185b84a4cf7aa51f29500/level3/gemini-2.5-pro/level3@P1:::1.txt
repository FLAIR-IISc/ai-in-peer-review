This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," where the loss for a given input is defined by the error of the *best* prediction among all ensemble members. The proposed sMCL algorithm implements this by backpropagating the gradient for each training example only to the single ensemble member that produced the lowest loss on that example. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy encourages different models in the ensemble to specialize on different parts of the data distribution, thereby increasing the diversity of the output set.

The work is a direct extension of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. While the original MCL used a block coordinate descent approach that required iteratively training each model to convergence on an assigned subset of the data, sMCL adapts this idea to the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) setting. This adaptation allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently in a single training run, making the approach practical for large-scale deep neural networks, which was a major limitation of the original MCL. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks: image classification with CNNs, semantic segmentation with FCNs, and image captioning with CNN+RNN models. In all cases, sMCL outperforms baselines, including independently trained ensembles.

**Quality**

The paper is of high quality. The technical claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple domains and network architectures. The authors demonstrate that sMCL not only achieves lower oracle error but also does so more efficiently than previous methods like MCL. The qualitative results and the analysis of emergent model specialization (e.g., class-based specialists for classification, segmentation-style specialists, and n-gram specialists for captioning) provide convincing evidence that the method works as intended. The work appears complete and the claims are presented honestly.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for generating multiple diverse hypotheses is clearly articulated with compelling examples in the introduction. The proposed algorithm is simple, and its connection to the MCL objective function is explained clearly in Section 3. The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner, making the paper easy to follow and understand. The figures and tables are informative and support the main claims effectively.

**Originality**

The primary concern regarding this paper is its originality. The core concept of minimizing the oracle loss via an alternating optimization scheme is not new and was introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The objective function in Equation 1 is taken directly from this prior work. The main contribution of this paper is the adaptation of this objective to a stochastic, mini-batch setting suitable for training deep networks with SGD. While this adaptation is simple—essentially applying a switch during the backward pass to route gradients—its practical impact is significant. The novelty is therefore more practical than theoretical. The paper does an adequate job of citing and differentiating itself from related work.

**Significance**

Despite the incremental nature of the algorithmic novelty, the significance of this work is high. The key contribution is making the powerful idea of Multiple Choice Learning practical for the deep learning era. Training modern deep networks can take weeks, rendering the iterative retraining approach of the original MCL infeasible. By developing a concurrent, SGD-based training algorithm, the authors have unlocked this training paradigm for a vast range of modern applications. The method is general, parameter-free, and simple to implement, which increases the likelihood that it will be adopted by other researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated ability to generate diverse and high-quality outputs for ambiguous tasks like semantic segmentation and image captioning is an important step forward.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Practical Impact:** The paper introduces a simple and effective method that makes a previously impractical learning paradigm (MCL) feasible for training modern deep neural networks. The claimed 5x speedup over MCL is substantial.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is validated across three diverse and challenging tasks, consistently outperforming strong baselines. The qualitative results are compelling and clearly demonstrate the benefits of the approach.
3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is very well-written, and the proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant and easy to implement. This lowers the barrier to adoption.
4.  **Interesting Analysis:** The analysis of how different forms of specialization emerge automatically depending on the task is insightful and adds value to the paper.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The core algorithmic idea is a straightforward adaptation of the existing MCL framework to SGD. One could argue that the contribution is more of an engineering insight than a fundamental research advance.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper could be strengthened with a more direct comparison to the original MCL. The current implementation of MCL involves training models to convergence on the full dataset partition. A fairer and more insightful baseline would be a mini-batch version of MCL, where assignments are updated after each epoch (or a set number of batches) rather than after full convergence. This would help isolate the benefits of the concurrent "winner-take-gradient" updates from the benefits of more frequent re-assignments.

Additionally, while the paper claims to improve "diversity" and shows compelling qualitative examples, the analysis could benefit from more quantitative measures of diversity. For instance, in segmentation, one could measure the average IoU between different predictions for the same image. For captioning, metrics beyond the number of unique n-grams, such as the semantic distance between generated sentences, could provide a more rigorous validation of the diversity claim.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, this is a strong paper. While the algorithmic novelty over prior work is incremental, the practical contribution is significant. By enabling oracle loss minimization for deep ensembles, the authors provide a valuable and widely applicable tool for the community. The thorough experiments and clear presentation make a convincing case for the method's effectiveness. I believe the paper's strengths, particularly its practical significance and strong empirical results, outweigh the concerns about its originality. I recommend acceptance.
