- Introduces sMCL: a stochastic "winner-takes-gradient" method that minimizes oracle loss for ensemble outputs and avoids costly retraining required by prior MCL.  
- Frames the problem as stochastic optimization for deep networks and demonstrates sMCL on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO.  
- Method is technically sound with clear theoretical justification.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive and compares to multiple baselines (independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.).  
- sMCL yields consistent gains: ~6–10% improvement in oracle performance and ~5× faster training than MCL.  
- Analysis shows ensemble members naturally specialize, providing insight into how the method works.  
- The paper should better address convergence of the baseline MCL method and effects of early stopping.  
- Well-written and clearly organized; experimental setup and results are well presented, with minor typos and an abstract wording suggestion.  
- The "winner-takes-gradient" idea is novel for deep learning, but the distinction from Guzman-Rivera’s relaxation could be clearer.  
- Contribution is mainly practical rather than theoretical; it doesn’t explicitly enforce diversity despite emphasizing it.  
- Method is simple, architecture-agnostic, parameter-free, and broadly applicable—valuable to practitioners.  
- The contribution is incremental relative to Guzman-Rivera’s MCL and should be framed as a practical implementation for deep networks.  
- Recommendation: accept — practical, effective, broadly useful method with strong empirical support and fixable minor issues.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical solution to an important problem, simple to implement, strong multi-domain results, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty and misleading emphasis on diversity since diversity is a byproduct, not enforced.
