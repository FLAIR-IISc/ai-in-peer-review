## Review of “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles”

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks by leveraging an oracle to select the optimal member during backpropagation. The core idea – encouraging specialization within the ensemble to cover a diverse range of outputs – is compelling and well-motivated by the prevalence of perception systems embedded within larger, oracle-guided processes. The authors effectively position their work within the context of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and highlight the limitations of existing methods, particularly their computational cost when applied to deep networks. 

The paper is strong in its empirical validation. Results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline ensembles and existing MCL approaches. The authors present a thorough experimental setup, including ablation studies and qualitative analysis that supports the claim of emergent specialization among ensemble members. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized, and the inclusion of illustrative examples (e.g., Fig. 6) is helpful. The method itself is elegantly simple, being parameter-free and easily integrated into existing SGD-based training pipelines.

However, a significant weakness lies in the lack of discussion regarding *inference*. While the training procedure is well-defined, the paper completely omits how to effectively exploit the trained ensemble *without* access to the oracle during deployment. The authors acknowledge this limitation, stating that exploitation strategies are problem-specific, but failing to explore even basic approaches (e.g., averaging, voting, or learned combination weights) severely limits the practical usability of sMCL. This omission is a major drawback, as the value of a trained ensemble is contingent on its performance at inference time. Furthermore, the pseudocode in Fig. 2 is presented as a raster image, hindering readability and preventing easy copy-pasting for implementation. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-supported by strong empirical results.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in the context of real-world AI systems.
*   Novel and efficient training procedure for diverse ensembles.
*   Clear and well-written presentation (aside from Fig. 2).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Critical omission of inference strategies, severely limiting practical applicability.
*   Fig. 2 should be a vector graphic or actual text for readability.



**Overall:** While sMCL presents a valuable contribution to ensemble learning with a clever training approach, the lack of discussion around inference significantly diminishes its impact. I recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement that the authors address the inference issue in a future version of the paper. A minor revision to improve Fig. 2 is also necessary.
