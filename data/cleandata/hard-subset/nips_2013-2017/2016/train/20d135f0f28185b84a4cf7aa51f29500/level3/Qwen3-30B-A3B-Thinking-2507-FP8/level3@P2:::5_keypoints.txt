- Proposes sMCL: stochastic training that backprops only to the lowest-loss ensemble member per example to minimize oracle loss; simple and architecture-/loss-agnostic.  
- Presents an elegant stochastic block gradient descent formulation (Algorithm 2) with solid theoretical motivation.  
- Empirical evaluation on CIFAR10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO shows consistent oracle accuracy gains (≈6–10%) over classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.  
- Avoids costly retraining required by prior MCL methods and is easy to implement in existing frameworks.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly structured; problem formulation and method are explained with sufficient mathematical detail.  
- Lacks analysis of initialization effects — unclear whether specialization and performance depend on initialization or matched baselines.  
- Evaluation focuses only on oracle accuracy and omits quantitative diversity metrics despite diversity being the core motivation.  
- Provides limited concrete evidence that ensembles produce genuinely diverse outputs or that diversity improves downstream tasks.  
- Qualitative specialization examples are shown but more concrete/specific examples would improve clarity.  
- Insufficient comparison and explicit differentiation from related work on multiple outputs, particularly Lee et al. ("Why M Heads are Better than One").  
- Original contribution: practical stochastic optimization for MCL applicable to deep networks, but needs clearer distinction from prior multi-head approaches.  
- Strengths: novel practical approach, simplicity, broad applicability, and consistent empirical improvements.  
- Weaknesses: missing diversity analysis, incomplete prior-work comparisons, and unclear dependence on initialization.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to add diversity metrics/analysis, clarify relation to prior work, and investigate initialization effects.
