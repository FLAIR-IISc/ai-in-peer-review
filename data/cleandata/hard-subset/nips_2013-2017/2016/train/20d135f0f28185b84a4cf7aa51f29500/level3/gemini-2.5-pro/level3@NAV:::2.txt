This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training procedure for deep ensembles. The core idea is to encourage diversity and specialization among ensemble members by using an oracle to identify the optimal member for each training instance. During the backward pass, the gradient is backpropagated only to this "winning" network. This approach extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. to the context of deep learning, cleverly adapting it to stochastic gradient descent to avoid the prohibitively expensive retraining required by the original MCL algorithm.

The strengths of this work are significant. The proposed sMCL method is simple, elegant, and parameter-free, making it broadly applicable. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness with a comprehensive and well-executed set of experiments across diverse and challenging domains: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The results are consistently strong, showing clear improvements in oracle performance over standard ensembles and other baselines. The qualitative analysis is particularly compelling, providing interpretable evidence that sMCL successfully induces meaningful specialization among the ensemble members, a key goal of the work. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

However, the paper suffers from a major omission that significantly impacts its practical significance. The entire training paradigm is predicated on the existence of an oracle, yet the paper fails to address how the resulting specialized ensemble can be used at inference time when no such oracle is available. Without a strategy for selecting from or combining the diverse outputs, the practical utility of the trained model is unclear. The authors motivate the work by suggesting its use in systems with a downstream human operator or verification component, but they do not explore or propose any concrete exploitation strategies. This makes the contribution feel incomplete, presenting a novel training method without a clear path to deployment. A minor presentation issue is that the pseudocode in Figure 2 appears to be a low-resolution raster image, which detracts from the paper's professional quality and clarity.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, original, and technically sound training technique with strong empirical validation. However, the lack of a proposed inference or exploitation mechanism is a critical weakness that limits the work's overall contribution and significance. While the training methodology is a valuable idea, its practical impact remains an open question.
