This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple winner-take-gradient training scheme for deep ensembles where, for each training example, only the member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates. The objective is to minimize oracle loss (min-m over ensemble members of the task loss), thereby producing multiple plausible outputs from which a downstream “oracle” can select. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and easily implemented within SGD. Empirically, sMCL improves oracle metrics across three tasks—CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation, and MSCOCO image captioning—compared to independently trained ensembles, MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al.], and the sequential boosting-style approach of Dey et al. The paper also documents emergent specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class-level clusters in classification; more nuanced differences in segmentation and captioning).

Relation to prior work: The formulation follows MCL [NIPS’12; AISTATS’14] but replaces alternating retraining with a stochastic block gradient descent strategy, akin to hard-EM or competitive learning. The paper positions itself relative to classical ensemble diversity mechanisms (bagging, negative correlation learning), multi-output/structured “diverse M-best” methods [e.g., Batra et al.], and recent ensemble-of-experts designs. A closer link to historical mixture-of-experts (Jacobs et al., 1991) and competitive learning would strengthen context—sMCL can be seen as a mixture without an explicit gating network. Additionally, diversity-regularized neural ensembles (e.g., Chen et al.; determinantal point processes for diversity; orthogonality/anti-correlation penalties) are not discussed and offer relevant baselines and theoretical framing.

Strengths
- Practical and general algorithm: trivial to implement within existing SGD loops; trains all members concurrently; no extra hyperparameters.
- Broad empirical applicability: consistent oracle gains across distinct architectures and tasks; informative qualitative analyses in segmentation and captioning.
- Efficiency: a single training pass versus the multi-round retraining of prior MCL-style methods; reported ~5x speedup relative to MCL with 5 meta-iterations.
- Insightful analyses: assignment distributions, k-best backprop variant, and qualitative diversity visualizations illuminate the behavior of the method.

Weaknesses and clarifications
- Testing/inference protocol is unclear (line 159, p.5). The paper evaluates oracle metrics that assume access to ground truth at test time. For practical deployment, how is the single output chosen? Please specify whether any learned gating, simple heuristics (e.g., entropy, confidence), or downstream re-ranking is used, and report non-oracle performance with such selectors.
- Baseline definitions are underspecified: “independent/regular ensembles” (Indp.) are referenced in the text/figures but not described (line 181, p.5). Please define training schedules, initialization, data augmentation, and compute budgets to ensure fairness.
- Specialization claims are suggestive but not conclusive. Because assignments are per-example and non-winning members receive zero gradient, apparent class-level specialization might reflect dataset frequency and local minima. Quantify specialization with: (a) per-member data load (assignment entropy), (b) per-class coverage, (c) per-member confusion matrices, and (d) diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise KL/IoU diversity). Report stability across random seeds.
- Missing related work on diversity regularization in deep ensembles (e.g., Chen et al.; DPP-based diversity; negative correlation learning). These provide alternative mechanisms to induce specialization/diversity and merit empirical comparison or discussion.
- Some results show trade-offs: in captioning, beam search from a single model can have higher oracle than sMCL (M=5) while being less diverse. A deeper discussion of this trade-off and the complementary role of inference-time search versus training-time diversity would help.
- Potential degeneracy: winner-take-gradient may lead to collapsed experts or poor load balancing. Did the authors observe starvation? Simple diagnostics and mitigation (e.g., occasional exploration, top-k backprop, softmin with temperature, or mild load-balancing regularization) would be useful.

Quality
- Technically sound derivation from the MCL objective; the stochastic optimization is a natural and correct surrogate for the min operator. Empirical methodology is generally solid, though some baseline details and fairness controls are missing.
- Additional ablations would strengthen the case: effect of ensemble size beyond 6; sensitivity to batch size; tie-breaking strategies; soft- vs hard-assignments; shared-backbone vs independent parameters; wall-clock and throughput measurements for all methods.

Clarity
- Well written overall, with clear exposition of the algorithm and qualitative analyses. Please clarify the test-time protocol, define the Indp. baseline, and explicitly state all training hyperparameters used for each baseline. It would help to move the oracle-metric definition earlier and reinforce that ground-truth is used only for evaluation.

Originality
- The core idea is straightforward—SGD-based hard assignment—and closely related to prior MCL/competitive learning, but the contribution lies in making MCL practical for deep nets with a simple, general procedure and demonstrating it across tasks. This is a modest but meaningful increment.

Significance
- The approach is likely to be adopted because of its simplicity and observed gains on oracle metrics in settings where multiple hypotheses are valuable (segmentation ambiguity, captioning). Its impact would be higher with practical selection mechanisms and/or integration with gating.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify test-time selection and report non-oracle metrics using simple selectors and a learned gating network; compare to beam search and reranking.
- Add diversity-regularization baselines (negative correlation, orthogonality, DPP) and discuss theoretical connections.
- Quantify specialization and load balancing; report assignment dynamics over training.
- Explore explicit specialization in the loss: softmin/temperature over members; top-k backprop; auxiliary diversity terms; mild balancing regularizer.
- Provide wall-clock training times and compute budgets for all methods; ensure fair baseline tuning.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is simple but useful, empirically effective, and broadly applicable. Addressing the clarity issues and adding comparisons to diversity-regularized baselines and practical selection results would strengthen the paper. Pros: simplicity, speed, consistent oracle gains, insightful analyses. Cons: reliance on oracle evaluation, incomplete baselines/related work, limited evidence for specialization beyond per-example assignments.
