- Proposal: sMCL — an SGD-era reformulation of Multiple Choice Learning using “winner-take-gradient” hard assignments to train deep ensembles, architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and faster than alternating MCL.

- Related work: Closely related to classic MCL, hard MoE / k-means / hard-EM, ensemble diversity methods, and M-Best; should be positioned explicitly against MoE (no gating) and diversity-regularized ensembles.

- Strength: Simple, general training recipe that integrates with SGD and modern nets.

- Strength: Strong and consistent oracle improvements across CIFAR-10, PASCAL segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning with sensible analyses.

- Strength: Concurrent training is far faster and more practical than alternating MCL for deep nets.

- Strength: Idea is clear and gradient derivation is correct under the hard-assignment upper bound.

- Major concern — test-time evaluation: Paper reports only oracle metrics (uses ground truth at test time); needs non-oracle selectors (max-confidence, learned gating, reranker) to show practical utility.

- Major concern — baselines unclear: “Independent/regular ensembles” lack details (training schedule, data ordering, augmentation, compute, params); report absolute training time/GPU hours and compute-matched comparisons.

- Major concern — specialization claims weak: Provide evidence for stable/interpretable specialization (temporal stability, MI between member and class, per-member usage/balance, calibration and error correlation).

- Major concern — missing related work: Discuss diversity-encouraging methods (orthogonality/diversity penalties, negative correlation, DPP-style) and contrast to sMCL.

- Major concern — theory gap: No analysis of convergence/stability under stochastic reassignments, ties, or noise; add discussion/conditions or connect to hard-EM/MoE convergence behavior.

- Major concern — ablations missing: Report sensitivity to M, batch size, tie-breaking, k-winner, plus variance/error bars across seeds.

- Quality: Technically sound and empirically substantial, but exclusive reliance on oracle metrics limits practical significance; clearer, compute-matched baselines would increase confidence.

- Clarity: Generally well written but needs clarifications on testing protocol, definition/training of “Indp.” ensembles, tie-breaking/smoothing of assignments, and class-imbalance handling.

- Originality: Moderate — an intuitive, practical SGD instantiation of MCL/hard-EM; novelty lies in simplicity and breadth of empirical evaluation.

- Significance: High practical significance for training diverse ensembles; impact would broaden with non-oracle selection results.

- Suggestions: Add non-oracle evaluations (max-confidence, learned gating, averaging logits), explore diversity/balancing losses, quantify assignment stability and member expertise, compare to hard MoE with learned gating, and report training time and variance.

- Pros: Simple parameter-free method, strong oracle gains across tasks, significant training-time savings, convincing qualitative diversity.

- Cons: Test-time selection unclear, baselines under-specified, specialization not well quantified, and missing discussion of diversity-regularized ensemble methods.

- Recommendation: Weak Accept — solid idea with strong oracle results; address test-time selection, baseline clarity, and specialization analysis to strengthen the paper (reviewer confidence: medium).
