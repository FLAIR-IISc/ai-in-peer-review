Paper introduces sMCL: adapts Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep nets using SGD to minimize oracle loss.  
Motivation for multiple hypotheses is clear and relevant for oracle-interactive systems.  
Novelty is incremental—main contribution is adapting prior MCL to deep learning, not a new learning paradigm.  
Technical exposition (Section 3) is terse and unclear; derivation and optimization nuances are underexplained.  
Key design choices (e.g., applying gradients to the lowest-error predictor) lack sufficient justification.  
Fails to convincingly differentiate sMCL from standard multi-task/ensemble methods and diversity regularization techniques.  
Experiments cover classification, segmentation, and captioning and report improvements over baselines.  
Baselines are relatively weak; missing comparisons to modern diversity-promoting methods (negative correlation, adversarial approaches).  
Observed improvements are modest and not consistently substantial.  
No robust ablation study or sensitivity analysis for ensemble size and hyperparameters.  
Segmentation evaluation uses a subset; should evaluate on full standard benchmark (PASCAL VOC 2012).  
Captioning results rely on CIDEr-D; need broader metrics and qualitative analysis of diversity and coherence.  
Strengths: clear motivation, broad task applicability, emergent interpretable expertise, simple/parameter-free implementation.  
Weaknesses: incremental novelty, poor clarity, insufficient differentiation from related work, weak baselines, limited ablations, modest gains.  
Arguments for acceptance: practical problem, simple to implement, some performance gains, insightful emergent expertise analysis.  
Arguments against acceptance: incremental technical contribution, clarity issues, unconvincing experiments, incomplete related-work comparison.  
Overall recommendation: weak reject; revise by expanding technical details, strengthening experiments/baselines, and clarifying unique contributions.
