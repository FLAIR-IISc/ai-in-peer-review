This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm to train ensemble models, specifically designed for the task of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The authors build upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] and extend it to deep neural networks. The main contribution of this paper is the use of SGD, which enables the method to scale to deep neural networks.

The paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm and its applications to various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which outperforms existing baselines and produces sets of outputs with high oracle performance.

One of the major concerns with this paper is that the algorithmic novelty may be only incremental compared to [8]. However, I believe that the small change makes the algorithm more useful and practical, especially for deep neural networks. The use of SGD enables the method to be more efficient and scalable, which is a significant advantage over the original MCL algorithm.

I would like to see more analysis of the algorithm's "diversity" effect, as mentioned in the paper. The authors provide some qualitative results, but a more quantitative evaluation of the diversity of the produced solutions would be beneficial. Additionally, it would be interesting to compare the proposed method with other ensemble learning techniques, such as bagging and boosting.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its applications. The experimental results are thorough and well-presented. However, I would like to see more discussion on the limitations of the proposed method and potential future work.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel algorithm that extends the MCL paradigm to deep neural networks. The use of SGD is a significant contribution, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in various tasks. However, the idea of MCL itself is not new, and the authors build upon existing work.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the performance of deep neural networks in various tasks. The proposed method can be used to produce multiple diverse solutions, which can be useful in applications where a single solution is not sufficient. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability of the proposed method, and the experimental results show significant improvements over existing baselines.

Overall, I believe that the paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly explained. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm and its applications. While the algorithmic novelty may be only incremental, I believe that the small change makes the algorithm more useful and practical, especially for deep neural networks.
