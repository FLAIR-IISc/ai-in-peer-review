This paper addresses the problem of generating a diverse set of high-quality predictions from an ensemble of models, which is particularly useful in systems where a downstream user or component can select the best hypothesis from a provided set. The authors frame this as a Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem, where the goal is to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing model in the ensemble for any given input. The main contribution is a novel training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which adapts the MCL framework for modern deep networks. sMCL employs a simple and effective "winner-take-gradient" strategy: during stochastic gradient descent, for each example in a batch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives a gradient update. This avoids the costly retraining required by previous MCL methods, allowing for efficient, end-to-end training of all ensemble members in parallel. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks—image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO)—using appropriate deep architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). In all cases, sMCL is shown to achieve superior oracle performance compared to independently trained ensembles and other related baselines.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of sMCL as a stochastic block gradient descent approach to minimize the oracle loss objective is clever and well-motivated. The claim that this approach is practical for deep networks is convincingly supported by strong empirical results across three challenging and diverse tasks. The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms baselines, including a 5x speedup over the original MCL formulation.

However, there are a few points that weaken the experimental claims. A critical detail in the experimental setup is the model initialization. For the semantic segmentation task, the authors state that sMCL models were initialized from a standard ensemble trained for 50 epochs. It is unclear if the baseline methods (e.g., Independent ensembles, Dey et al.) also benefited from this same pre-trained initialization. If the baselines were trained from random initializations while sMCL started from a strong pre-trained state, the comparison is not entirely fair, and the reported performance gains may be partially attributed to the superior initialization rather than the sMCL algorithm itself. This point needs to be clarified for all experiments to ensure the claims are fully supported.

Furthermore, while the paper’s motivation is to produce a *diverse* set of solutions, the evaluation is based exclusively on oracle performance metrics (oracle accuracy, oracle IoU, etc.). While oracle performance is the direct objective, it does not explicitly measure the diversity of the generated outputs. It would strengthen the paper's claims to include a quantitative analysis of diversity (e.g., average pairwise distance between predicted segmentations or Jaccard distance between generated captions) to show that sMCL is not just finding a better minimum for the oracle loss but is doing so by producing genuinely distinct hypotheses.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for generating multiple hypotheses is clearly articulated with compelling examples. The proposed sMCL algorithm is explained with precision, and its connection to the original MCL framework is well-established. The inclusion of Algorithm 2 and the clear description of the "winner-take-gradient" backward pass make the method straightforward to understand and implement. The figures and tables effectively summarize the results and provide insightful qualitative examples.

**Originality:**
The core idea of minimizing oracle loss via MCL is not new, as the authors appropriately cite Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9]. However, the primary contribution—a practical, stochastic algorithm that makes this paradigm feasible for training large, deep neural networks—is novel and significant. The simple yet effective modification to the backpropagation step is an original and valuable contribution. The paper is clear about how its work differs from previous contributions, particularly in avoiding the sequential or iterative retraining schemes that are impractical for deep learning. One piece of potentially relevant work that could be discussed is Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One" (ICML 2015), which also explores joint training of multiple classifiers (or "heads") on a shared representation, albeit for a different goal (improving the accuracy of a single distilled model). A comparison or discussion could further contextualize the paper's contribution.

**Significance:**
The problem this paper addresses is of high practical importance. Many real-world AI systems, from autonomous navigation to creative content generation, benefit from providing a set of plausible options rather than a single, potentially flawed prediction. The proposed sMCL method is a significant advance because it provides a general, simple, and efficient tool to train models for this purpose. Its agnosticism to the specific architecture and loss function, combined with its parameter-free nature, makes it highly likely to be adopted by other researchers and practitioners. The demonstrable improvements in oracle performance suggest that sMCL could advance the state-of-the-art in applications where interaction with a human or another system component is possible.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Practical Algorithm:** Proposes sMCL, a novel, simple, and computationally efficient algorithm to train deep ensembles to generate diverse, high-quality outputs.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated with significant performance gains over strong baselines across three different and challenging tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning).
3.  **High Significance and Broad Applicability:** The problem of generating multiple hypotheses is important for many real-world applications. The proposed method is general-purpose, parameter-free, and easy to implement, making it highly likely to have a broad impact.
4.  **Excellent Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making the motivation, method, and results easy to understand.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Unclear Experimental Details:** Lack of clarity regarding the initialization strategy for baseline models raises concerns about the fairness of the experimental comparisons.
2.  **Incomplete Evaluation:** The paper claims to produce diverse outputs but lacks quantitative metrics to measure this diversity, relying solely on oracle performance and qualitative examples.
3.  **Minor Omission in Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by discussing conceptually related methods for joint ensemble training, such as Lee et al. (2015).
