## Review of “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles”

This paper presents Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training ensembles of deep networks to produce diverse outputs, framed within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm. The authors build upon the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] by proposing a method that avoids the costly retraining required by the original approach, enabling scaling to deep neural networks. The core idea is a “winner-take-gradient” approach during SGD, where only the network producing the lowest-error output for a given example receives a gradient update. 

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. It effectively connects the work to existing literature in ensemble learning and multiple-output prediction, particularly highlighting the limitations of previous MCL implementations for deep learning. The experimental results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline ensemble methods, along with insightful analysis revealing emergent specialization within the trained ensembles. The interpretable expertise demonstrated – for example, the network specialization in class prediction – is a particularly strong aspect of the work.

A significant strength of this paper is its practical contribution. While the algorithmic novelty might be incremental compared to Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], as noted by the authors and my assessment, the ability to apply the MCL principle to deep networks using a simple SGD-based approach is a substantial advancement. This makes the method significantly more accessible and scalable for real-world applications. I am convinced that this seemingly small change unlocks a much broader utility for the technique.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with other diversity-inducing techniques when using mini-batches, such as the MCL algorithm itself, even if only briefly. It would be helpful to see a clearer quantification of the ‘diversity’ achieved, beyond the qualitative examples provided. While the specialization is shown, metrics to quantify this diversity (e.g., measuring the dissimilarity of outputs across ensemble members) would strengthen the analysis. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical limitation of existing MCL methods.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results across diverse tasks and architectures.
*   Clear writing and well-motivated methodology.
*   Provides insightful analysis of emergent expertise in the ensembles.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Algorithmic novelty is incremental.
*   Limited quantitative analysis of diversity.



Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution, while not groundbreaking in terms of novel theoretical concepts, is significant in its practical impact and opens up new avenues for research in diverse ensemble learning. Addressing the minor suggestions regarding diversity quantification would further strengthen the work.
