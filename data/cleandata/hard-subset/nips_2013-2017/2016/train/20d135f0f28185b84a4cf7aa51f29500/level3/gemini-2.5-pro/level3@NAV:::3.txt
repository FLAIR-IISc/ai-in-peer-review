This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training procedure for deep network ensembles designed to minimize oracle loss. The core contribution is a simple and efficient method that jointly trains all ensemble members by backpropagating the gradient for each training example only to the single "winning" learner—the one that achieves the lowest loss on that example. This approach frames the hard assignment step of prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) work (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al., 2012) as a "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer, enabling end-to-end training with standard stochastic gradient descent and avoiding the prohibitive cost of retraining models to convergence.

The paper has several significant strengths. Firstly, it addresses an important and timely problem: generating multiple plausible hypotheses from deep networks, which is highly relevant for tasks with inherent ambiguity. The quality of the work is high, supported by extensive experiments across three diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, and captioning) that convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness over baselines. A key strength is the method's simplicity and ease of implementation, which significantly increases its practical value and likelihood of adoption by the community.

However, there are some weaknesses to consider. The primary concern is a mismatch between the paper's heavy motivation for "diversity" and the objective function itself, which does not contain an explicit diversity term. While diversity is shown to be an emergent property, the framing could be more precise. The originality could also be viewed as somewhat incremental, as the method is effectively a stochastic online relaxation of the block coordinate descent algorithm proposed by Guzman et al. A key question remains regarding the experimental setup: How was convergence ensured for the baseline deep MCL networks? Cutting off learner training early in each meta-iteration could significantly and unfairly handicap the performance of this important baseline.

Minor suggestions for improvement include rewording the first sentence of the abstract for better impact and toning down the emphasis on "diversity" in the title and text to better align with the method's mechanics. There are also minor typos on lines 108 (the first "f" should be "g") and 115 (an extra period).

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting a simple, practical, and effective solution to a relevant problem. The weaknesses are addressable, and the work represents a solid contribution to the field.
