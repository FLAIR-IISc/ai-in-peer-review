

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training ensembles of deep networks that produce multiple diverse outputs rather than a single prediction. The work builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8], which minimizes oracle loss by assigning examples to their lowest-error predictors in an alternating block coordinate descent procedure. The key innovation is replacing this computationally expensive retraining process with a stochastic gradient descent approach that allows concurrent training of ensemble members. This enables the method to scale to deep neural networks, which was previously infeasible with MCL due to training time constraints.

The paper makes a compelling case for the utility of multiple-output systems in practical applications where downstream oracles can select the best solution from a set of hypotheses. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness across three distinct domains: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). Their results consistently show that sMCL outperforms classical independent ensembles and Dey et al.'s [5] sequential boosting approach, with significant improvements in oracle metrics (6-10% over baselines). Crucially, sMCL achieves these gains while being 5x faster than MCL [8], making it practical for modern deep learning applications.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem statement is well-motivated with concrete examples of where multiple outputs are beneficial (e.g., handling class ambiguity in semantic segmentation, capturing ambiguity in image captioning). The technical exposition of sMCL is straightforward, with Algorithm 2 clearly describing the simple modification to standard SGD that enables the method. The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple architectures and tasks, and the qualitative analyses of ensemble specialization (Figures 4c, 5b, and 7) effectively demonstrate the emergence of interpretable expertise among ensemble members.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with appropriate baselines and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly acknowledging the computational advantages of sMCL over MCL. The significance of this work is substantial: it provides the first practical method for training diverse deep ensembles that can be applied to a wide range of architectures and tasks without modification. The method's simplicity, parameter-freeness, and agnosticism to architecture and loss function make it highly appealing for practitioners.

The main concern regarding originality is valid: the core algorithmic idea is incremental over [8], as the authors themselves acknowledge. However, this small change (replacing alternating retraining with SGD) has profound practical implications, enabling the method to scale to deep networks where MCL was previously infeasible. The paper convincingly argues that this practical utility outweighs the incremental nature of the contribution. The reviewer is convinced that this practical improvement makes a significant difference in real-world applications, making the paper valuable to the NIPS community.

I recommend the following specific improvements: (1) Include a comparison with MCL when using batches rather than the entire dataset (the current comparison uses MCL with 5 meta-iterations, but the paper's speedup claim assumes MCL would require more iterations), and (2) Provide more detailed analysis of the algorithm's "diversity" effect through metrics beyond oracle accuracy, such as inter-ensemble correlation or coverage of the solution space.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning and deep learning applications. While the novelty is incremental, the practical impact of enabling diverse deep ensembles through a simple SGD modification is substantial. The method's broad applicability, simplicity, and effectiveness across multiple domains make it a strong contribution to NIPS.

Pro-acceptance arguments: 
- Provides the first practical method for training diverse deep ensembles
- Simple to implement and parameter-free
- Demonstrated effectiveness across multiple domains and architectures
- Clear, well-motivated, and well-organized
- Practical utility for real-world systems requiring multiple hypotheses

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Incremental novelty compared to MCL [8]
- Limited analysis of diversity beyond oracle performance

Given the practical significance, clear writing, and strong experimental validation, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The minor concern about incremental novelty is outweighed by the substantial practical benefits of the method. I recommend acceptance.
