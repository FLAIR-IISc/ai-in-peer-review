## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel stochastic gradient descent algorithm for training ensembles of deep networks to produce diverse outputs, framed within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm. The core idea builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] by adapting their approach to be compatible with the scale of modern deep learning through the use of SGD, avoiding the computationally expensive retraining steps of the original method. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improved oracle performance and qualitatively demonstrating the emergence of interpretable specialization within the ensemble members.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The motivation for MCL is well-articulated, and the connection to real-world applications where multiple hypotheses are valuable is compelling. The technical explanation of sMCL is concise and easy to follow, and the experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to relevant baselines. The results consistently demonstrate the benefits of sMCL, and the analysis of ensemble diversity is insightful. 

However, the algorithmic novelty, while practically significant, feels somewhat incremental. The authors acknowledge this connection to [8], and the primary contribution lies in the adaptation to SGD for scalability. While this is a substantial engineering achievement and unlocks the method for deep networks, it doesn't represent a fundamentally new theoretical approach. A comparison to the MCL algorithm when using batches (rather than the entire dataset) would be valuable to further quantify the benefit of the SGD approach. Furthermore, while the authors provide qualitative examples of diversity, a more quantitative analysis of the “diversity” effect – perhaps using metrics like determinant of the covariance matrix of ensemble outputs – would strengthen the paper. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The method demonstrably improves performance on a variety of tasks, addresses a practical limitation of previous MCL approaches, and is well-motivated and clearly explained. The scalability enabled by SGD is a significant contribution.
**Arguments against acceptance:** The core algorithmic innovation is relatively incremental compared to [8].

**Overall:** Despite the incremental nature of the algorithmic novelty, I believe the practical benefits and clear presentation of this work justify acceptance. The authors have successfully adapted a promising approach to a wider range of applications, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with the minor suggestions outlined above.
