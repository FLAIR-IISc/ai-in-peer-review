Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” variant of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss directly. Instead of alternating between example assignment and full retraining as in classical MCL, sMCL performs per-batch assignments: for each training example, only the lowest-loss predictor in the ensemble receives gradient. This stochastic block gradient descent is parameter-free, end-to-end, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and easy to implement within standard backprop.

The method is positioned within two relevant threads: (i) multiple-hypothesis generation (M-Best/Diverse-M Best in MRFs; MCL; submodular/boosting-style methods) and (ii) ensemble learning/diversity. In contrast to prior MCL-style methods that require repeated retraining or sequential learning, sMCL makes the paradigm practical for deep nets. Compared with techniques that explicitly regularize diversity or resample data, sMCL induces task-dependent specialization emergently by directly optimizing oracle loss. The work is also closely related to contemporaneous “multi-head” approaches (e.g., “Why M Heads are Better than One,” Lee et al., 2015), which share a trunk with multiple heads to encourage diverse outputs; sMCL differs in objective (oracle loss vs. explicit diversity regularization) and in training dynamics (hard assignment gating vs. joint loss). A direct comparison and discussion would strengthen positioning.

Empirically, oracle experiments on three tasks—CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM)—show consistent improvements over independently trained ensembles, MCL with meta-iterations, and a sequential submodular/boosting-style method. Qualitative analyses indicate emergent specialization (e.g., class clusters in classification; shape/frequency biases in segmentation; n-gram/style diversity in captioning).

Strengths:
- Clear, practical contribution: a simple training rule that makes MCL feasible for deep networks. Integrates seamlessly into backprop, is parameter-free, and supports end-to-end training.
- Broad applicability: demonstrated across three different architectures/tasks, suggesting generality.
- Strong oracle performance: consistent, often substantial gains over relevant baselines; faster training than MCL (5x in reported settings).
- Insightful qualitative analyses: evidence of interpretable specialization and coverage of ambiguity.
- Conceptual clarity: directly optimizing the quantity of interest (oracle loss) for multi-hypothesis systems.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Initialization and fairness of comparisons: For segmentation, sMCL is initialized from a standard ensemble and then fine-tuned. Did the baselines (independent, MCL, sequential) receive the same initialization and training budget? How sensitive is sMCL to initialization (random vs. pre-trained)? Please provide ablations on initialization for all tasks.
- Evaluation focuses almost exclusively on oracle metrics. While appropriate for multi-hypothesis settings, the paper would benefit from quantitative diversity analyses (e.g., pairwise prediction disagreement, error correlation, KL/JS divergence of predictive distributions, mutual information, inter-member IoU overlap, diversity–accuracy trade-offs) and from reporting single-prediction performance (e.g., best-member top-1/top-5, ensemble-averaged top-1) to understand any cost of specialization.
- Comparisons with multi-head methods: Please compare to “Why M Heads are Better than One” (Lee et al., 2015) and discuss differences (shared trunk vs. separate models; diversity regularizers vs. oracle loss; compute/memory trade-offs; oracle vs. single-prediction performance).
- Optimization details and stability: The min-assignment is non-differentiable at ties; how are ties broken and how sensitive are results to tie-breaking? Any evidence of assignment churn or mode collapse early in training? How do batch size, learning rate, or k-winner variants affect stability and final performance?
- Compute/memory budget: sMCL is 5x faster than MCL, but how does its wall-clock and memory cost compare to independent ensembles with matched epochs, especially when sharing trunks (as in captioning)? Please clarify whether total training steps, data passes, and hyperparameters are matched across baselines.
- Captioning baseline: Single-model beam search (w=5) achieves strong oracle. What is the oracle if one unions beams from sMCL members (e.g., beam=2 per member), and how many unique n-grams does that produce? This seems the fairest comparison for inference-time multi-hypothesis generation.

Quality:
- Technically sound and well-justified. The gradient gating derivation is straightforward, and the approach is consistent with block coordinate descent approximations of the MCL objective. More analysis on convergence/stability would be welcome, but the empirical evidence across tasks is compelling.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well-organized. Algorithmic description is easy to follow. Please add explicit training details ensuring fair baselines (initialization, epochs, early stopping) and a brief discussion on tie-handling and sensitivity.

Originality:
- Solid and timely. While rooted in known MCL formulations, the stochastic “winner-take-gradient” adaptation for deep networks is novel and practically important. Positioning vs. multi-head/diversity-regularized ensembles should be expanded.

Significance:
- High practical significance for systems that need multiple plausible hypotheses (vision and language). The method is simple to adopt and likely to be used. Results suggest meaningful improvements and interpretable specialization.

Requested additional experiments/analyses:
- Initialization ablations: random vs. pre-trained (and shared-trunk pretraining where applicable) for sMCL and baselines.
- Diversity metrics and their relationship to oracle performance; include pairwise disagreement/error correlation, distributional diversity, and coverage analysis.
- Comparison to multi-head methods (Lee et al., 2015) under matched compute; optionally a shared-trunk sMCL variant.
- k-winner variants (already partly shown): report quantitative trade-offs across k and M.
- In captioning, union-of-beams for sMCL vs. single-model beam search with matched total number of hypotheses.

Pros:
- Simple, general, parameter-free method with strong empirical gains and clear intuition.
- Makes MCL feasible for modern deep networks; faster than prior MCL training.
- Emergent, interpretable specialization; broad applicability across tasks.

Cons:
- Limited quantitative analysis of diversity; little reporting on single-prediction performance.
- Unclear impact of initialization and fairness of baseline initializations/training budgets.
- Missing comparisons to closely related multi-head/diversity-regularized approaches.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (leaning Accept)
The methodological contribution is clear and practical, the experiments are broad and positive, and the idea is likely to have impact. Addressing the concerns on initialization, diversity quantification, and comparisons to multi-head methods would solidify the paper.

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high.
