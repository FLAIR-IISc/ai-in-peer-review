

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training diverse deep ensembles by updating only the network with the lowest loss per example during training. The authors frame their approach as a solution to the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem, where the goal is to produce multiple hypotheses that minimize oracle error (the loss of the best hypothesis in the set). The core idea is simple: during training, for each example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss (relative to the ground truth) receives gradient updates, while others are frozen. The authors claim this approach is parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and achieves superior oracle performance across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks compared to baselines.

The paper makes a strong empirical case for sMCL's effectiveness. On CIFAR-10, sMCL ensembles achieve 6-10% higher oracle accuracy than independent ensembles and outperform MCL (a previous MCL method) by 5x speedup. In semantic segmentation on Pascal VOC, sMCL shows ~7% relative improvement over baselines, and in image captioning, it significantly outperforms alternatives in oracle CIDEr-D scores. The authors demonstrate that sMCL naturally induces specialization among ensemble members—e.g., in classification, members become specialists for specific classes (Figure 4c), and in segmentation, they develop expertise for specific object categories (Figure 5b). The method is technically sound, with a clear optimization procedure (Algorithm 2) that generalizes to any SGD-trained model.

The paper's main strength lies in its simplicity and broad applicability. sMCL requires no architectural changes beyond standard ensemble training and is straightforward to implement. The authors convincingly show that it outperforms existing methods (including MCL and Dey et al.'s boosting approach) across diverse tasks. The demonstration of emergent specialization is particularly compelling, as it suggests the method automatically discovers useful task-specific expertise without explicit design.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that affect its quality and clarity. First, the testing procedure is unclear: the authors state that during inference, "the oracle selects the best output," but it's not specified how this oracle operates in practice. The key phrase "line 159, p.5" suggests the authors might be using ground truth to select the best member during evaluation, which would be problematic for real-world applications where ground truth is unavailable. This needs explicit clarification.

Second, the paper fails to properly define key terms. The "independent ensembles" (referred to as "Indp.") baseline is used extensively in figures but not clearly explained in the text (line 181). Without understanding what this baseline entails, readers cannot properly assess the comparison.

Third, the claim of "specialization" is questionable. The authors attribute specialization to the sMCL procedure, but the mechanism—per-example reassignment of training responsibility—does not inherently create specialized models. It's possible that the observed specialization is simply a consequence of the optimization process rather than a meaningful emergence of expertise. The paper would be stronger if it provided additional analysis to distinguish between true specialization and mere assignment bias.

Fourth, the related work section omits significant literature on diversity regularization in neural networks. For example, Chen et al. (2016) and others have developed principled methods for inducing diversity in ensembles using regularization terms in the loss function. While the authors reference "ensemble learning" broadly, they don't engage with this more rigorous literature, which is directly relevant to their approach. This omission weakens the paper's originality claim.

The significance of the work is limited by these issues. While the method is practical for tasks where multiple predictions are valuable (e.g., ambiguous perception scenarios), the unclear testing procedure and questionable specialization claim reduce confidence in the results. The approach is not a fundamental advance in ensemble learning but rather a practical adaptation of MCL to deep networks.

Pro arguments for acceptance: 
- Simple, effective method with strong empirical results across multiple domains
- Clear demonstration of emergent specialization in ensemble members
- Practical and immediately applicable to diverse deep learning tasks

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Unclear testing procedure potentially undermines practical significance
- Poorly defined baselines (independent ensembles) limit reproducibility
- Questionable justification for specialization claim
- Missing engagement with key related work on diversity in neural networks

Overall, the paper presents a useful practical technique with promising results, but the weaknesses in methodology explanation and related work discussion prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors should clarify the testing procedure, properly define all baselines, provide stronger evidence for the specialization claim, and incorporate relevant diversity literature. With these revisions, the paper would be a solid contribution, but as-is, it warrants a weak acceptance.
