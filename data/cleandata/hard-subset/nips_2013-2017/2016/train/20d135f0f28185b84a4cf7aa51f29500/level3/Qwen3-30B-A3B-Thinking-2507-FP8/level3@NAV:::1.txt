

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss through a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm. The work builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8], which minimizes the loss of the best single classifier in an ensemble but requires costly retraining. sMCL innovates by using a "winner-take-gradient" strategy where, for each example, only the ensemble member producing the lowest loss receives gradient updates during backpropagation. This enables concurrent training of ensemble members, making it scalable to deep networks without the iterative retraining required by MCL.

The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates the method's effectiveness across three diverse tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). sMCL consistently outperforms classical ensembles, MCL (with a 5x speedup), and Dey et al.'s sequential approach, while automatically inducing interpretable specializations among ensemble members. The authors provide compelling qualitative evidence of how sMCL produces diverse, interpretable outputs that capture task ambiguity.

Regarding quality, the experimental design is strong with thorough comparisons across multiple architectures and tasks. However, the theoretical analysis is limited, and the paper would benefit from a more rigorous exploration of the diversity mechanism. The novelty is incremental compared to [8], as the core idea of using the best single-classifier output for ensemble training is not new, but the practical SGD adaptation is valuable for deep learning.

The clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections and clear explanations of both the method and results. The significance is high for practical applications where diverse outputs are valuable, such as in systems with downstream oracle mechanisms. While the algorithmic novelty is modest, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by enabling the MCL framework for modern deep learning pipelines.

I recommend acceptance, as the practical utility of sMCL outweighs the incremental nature of the contribution. To strengthen the paper, I suggest: (1) explicitly comparing MCL with batch-based training (rather than full-dataset retraining) to better contextualize the speedup claim; (2) providing deeper analysis of how the algorithm induces diversity, including metrics beyond oracle accuracy to quantify ensemble diversity. These additions would further solidify the paper's contribution.
