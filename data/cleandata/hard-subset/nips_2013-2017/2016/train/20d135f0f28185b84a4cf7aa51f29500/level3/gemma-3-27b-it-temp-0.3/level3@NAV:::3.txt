## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by adapting the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework to stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is a “winner-takes-the-gradient” approach during backpropagation, effectively assigning each training example to the ensemble member that minimizes loss, thereby encouraging specialization. The authors demonstrate strong performance across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showcasing the method’s broad applicability.

The paper addresses a relevant and important problem – generating diverse and informative outputs from deep learning models, particularly in scenarios where multiple plausible solutions are desired. The proposed method is commendably simple and easy to implement, a significant advantage for adoption by the community. The comprehensive evaluation across three diverse tasks further strengthens the paper, demonstrating the robustness of the approach. 

However, while the paper heavily emphasizes the importance of “diversity,” it lacks an explicit diversity-promoting term in the objective function. The diversity emerges as a *consequence* of minimizing oracle loss, rather than being directly enforced. This feels somewhat unsatisfying, as a dedicated diversity component might lead to even more robust and interpretable ensembles. Furthermore, the contribution, while valuable, appears incremental compared to the relaxation of the MCL problem proposed by Guzman et al. The authors acknowledge this prior work, but the improvements offered by sMCL (concurrent training) are primarily practical rather than fundamentally novel in terms of the optimization objective. 

Several minor points require attention: the first sentence of the abstract could be reworded for clarity; the emphasis on “diversity” in the title and text should be toned down to reflect the method's reliance on oracle loss minimization; and there’s a typo on line 108 (“f” should be “g”) and a superfluous period on line 115. 

Finally, a crucial question remains: How were the baseline MCL deep networks ensured to have converged? Early cutting of learners during the alternating block coordinate descent could potentially harm ensemble performance. A more detailed discussion of convergence criteria for the baselines would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses an important problem, simple and easy to implement, strong empirical results across diverse tasks.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Incremental contribution, lacks explicit diversity term, convergence of baselines unclear.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a practical and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles, and the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. Addressing the convergence question and refining the discussion around diversity would further strengthen the contribution.
