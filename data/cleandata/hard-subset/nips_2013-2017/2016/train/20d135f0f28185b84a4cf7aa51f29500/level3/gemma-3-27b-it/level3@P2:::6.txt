This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by framing the problem as minimizing oracle loss. The core idea is elegantly simple: during stochastic gradient descent, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for a given example receives a gradient update, effectively implementing a “winner-take-gradient” strategy. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing sMCL outperforms existing methods, including a direct implementation of the original Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) algorithm. The method is parameter-free, agnostic to architecture and loss function, and easy to implement.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the practical need for generating multiple hypotheses in perception systems embedded within larger applications. The introduction effectively articulates this motivation and connects it to the broader field of Multiple Choice Learning. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of ensemble learning and methods for generating diverse solutions, providing a relevant overview of related work including Guzman-Rivera et al.’s original MCL formulation, and recent approaches like Dey et al.’s boosting-like method. The technical contribution – adapting MCL for efficient training of deep networks via stochastic gradient descent – is significant, as the original MCL approach is computationally prohibitive for large models. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering diverse tasks and architectures, and the inclusion of qualitative examples, particularly for semantic segmentation and captioning, adds to the paper’s impact. 

However, there are some weaknesses. The description of the evaluation phase is somewhat unclear. Specifically, line 159 on page 5 states, “we present results as oracle versions of the task-dependent performance metrics.” The reviewer rightly questions whether this relies on ground truth to select the best member during testing, which would not represent a realistic scenario. Clarification is needed on how the oracle performance is measured during evaluation and whether it genuinely reflects the benefits of having multiple hypotheses available *before* knowing the ground truth.

The terminology used could be improved. Terms like “Indp. ensembles” (line 181, p.5) are introduced without a clear definition, assuming reader familiarity that may not be present. A concise explanation of what constitutes an “independent ensemble” would enhance clarity. 

The claim of ensemble member “specialization” is not entirely convincing. While the results suggest some degree of specialization, particularly in the image classification experiment, the training process relies on per-example assignment. This raises the question of whether the specialization is a genuine emergent property or simply a consequence of different members being “chosen” for different examples. The reviewer poses a pertinent question: could specialization be explicitly incorporated into the loss function to encourage more deliberate differentiation between ensemble members? 

Finally, the related work section could be strengthened by acknowledging the literature on diversity regularization in neural networks. Techniques for encouraging diversity, even if not directly tied to the MCL framework, are highly relevant and provide a broader context for understanding the challenges and potential solutions in this area. Specifically, works focusing on decorrelation of network outputs or encouraging exploration of different modes of the data distribution (e.g., Chen et al., 2018 – though many others exist) would be valuable additions. These methods offer more rigorous approaches to diversity than simply relying on the winner-take-all mechanism.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method is technically sound and offers a practical solution to a challenging problem.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing baselines.
*   The method is simple to implement and widely applicable to different architectures and loss functions.
*   The paper addresses a relevant problem with practical implications for real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The evaluation methodology lacks clarity, particularly regarding the testing phase and use of ground truth.
*   The claim of ensemble member specialization needs further justification.
*   The related work section is incomplete and does not adequately acknowledge relevant literature on diversity regularization.
*   Minor clarity issues with terminology.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The simplicity and effectiveness of sMCL, coupled with the strong empirical results, make it a noteworthy advancement. The reviewer’s recommendation of a “weak accept” is justified. Addressing the issues raised regarding evaluation clarity and related work would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance contingent on minor revisions addressing these points.
