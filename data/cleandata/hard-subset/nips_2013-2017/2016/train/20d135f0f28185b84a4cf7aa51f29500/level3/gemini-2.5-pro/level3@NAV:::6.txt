This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple and effective training algorithm for deep ensembles. The core idea is that for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss with respect to the ground truth label receives a gradient update. This "winner-take-gradient" approach is a stochastic, online adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, making it practical for training modern deep networks. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, showing improved oracle performance over baselines on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

The primary strengths of this work are its simplicity and efficacy. The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant, parameter-free, and can be easily integrated into existing training pipelines for any architecture trained with SGD. The reported performance gains over standard ensembles and prior methods are significant, and the computational efficiency compared to iterative MCL approaches is a major practical advantage.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper my enthusiasm.

**Clarity and Quality:** My most significant concern regards the evaluation protocol. The description of oracle evaluation (line 159, p.5) is ambiguous and suggests that the ground truth label might be used to select the best-performing ensemble member at test time. If this is the case, the reported oracle metrics are not representative of a real-world scenario where an oracle would lack access to the ground truth. This point requires immediate and explicit clarification. Furthermore, the baseline referred to as "independent ensembles" or "Indp." (line 181, p.5) is not adequately defined, making it difficult to fully contextualize the improvements.

**Significance and Originality:** The claim that sMCL induces member "specialization" is not convincingly supported. Since the winning member is reassigned on a per-example basis during stochastic training, it is unclear how stable, class-level expertise can emerge. A suggestion for the authors would be to explore whether specialization could be more explicitly incorporated into the loss function. Additionally, the paper omits a crucial body of related work on diversity regularization in neural network ensembles (e.g., Chen et al., 2019). This literature offers more rigorous methods for encouraging diversity and its absence is a notable oversight.

In summary, while the paper presents a simple and promising idea with strong results, critical issues with clarity in the evaluation and unsubstantiated claims prevent a stronger recommendation.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. My recommendation is contingent on the authors providing a clear explanation of the testing-phase oracle and addressing the other concerns raised.
