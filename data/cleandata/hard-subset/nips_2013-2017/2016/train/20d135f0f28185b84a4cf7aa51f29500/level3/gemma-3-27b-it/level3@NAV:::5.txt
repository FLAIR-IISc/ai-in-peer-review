## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks to generate diverse outputs for scenarios where an oracle evaluates the predictions. The core idea is to jointly learn the ensemble by minimizing oracle loss using a stochastic block gradient descent optimization, which is elegantly integrated into standard backpropagation. This allows for end-to-end training and avoids the computationally expensive retraining steps of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over baseline ensembles and existing MCL approaches.

The paper builds upon the MCL paradigm [Guzman-Rivera et al., 2008, 2009] but significantly advances it by making it practical for deep learning models. It’s a relevant contribution given the increasing use of AI systems within larger processes requiring interaction and evaluation, where diverse outputs are valuable. The technique is broadly applicable—agnostic to network architecture and loss function—and the authors emphasize its simplicity and parameter-free nature. The experimental results across diverse tasks support the claims of improved oracle performance, and the qualitative examples hint at the emergence of interpretable expertise within the ensemble members.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The impact of initialization is not adequately addressed. While the authors mention pre-trained models in passing, the experimental setup doesn't clearly state whether baselines utilized the *same* pre-trained initialization as sMCL. This is a critical factor as initialization can significantly affect ensemble diversity and performance. Furthermore, the evaluation relies solely on oracle accuracy, lacking a quantitative analysis of ensemble diversity itself. Metrics like the average distance between outputs, or the entropy of the ensemble's predictions, would provide valuable insights. Finally, a comparison to Lee et al. (2015) "Why M Heads are Better than One" would be beneficial, as that work also explores ensemble diversity, albeit through a different mechanism. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective approach to training diverse ensembles.
*   Clear theoretical grounding and practical implementation.
*   Strong empirical results on diverse tasks.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of analysis on the impact of initialization.
*   Missing quantitative evaluation of ensemble diversity.
*   Absence of comparison to closely related work (Lee et al., 2015).



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a well-defined problem, a compelling solution, and promising results. Addressing the concerns regarding initialization, diversity metrics, and comparison to related work would significantly strengthen the paper. I would lean towards **acceptance with minor revisions**.
