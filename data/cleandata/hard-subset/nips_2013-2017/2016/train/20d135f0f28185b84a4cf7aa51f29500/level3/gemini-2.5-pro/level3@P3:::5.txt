This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training procedure for deep network ensembles designed to generate a diverse set of high-quality outputs. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," where the ensemble is only penalized for the error of its best-performing member on any given example. This approach extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework, previously proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. (2012), to the context of modern deep learning. The key technical contribution is a stochastic block gradient descent algorithm that implements a "winner-take-gradient" strategy. During training, for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that produced the lowest loss. This allows for concurrent, end-to-end training of all models, overcoming the prohibitive computational cost of the original MCL's iterative, full-retraining approach. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—showing significant improvements in oracle performance over baseline methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem: generating multiple plausible hypotheses for perception tasks where ambiguity is inherent. While the concept of MCL is not new, this work's main contribution is making it practical for deep learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a novel and elegant solution that bridges the gap between the oracle loss objective and the realities of training large-scale deep networks with SGD. This is a significant step forward from previous methods that required sequential or full iterative retraining.

2.  **Generality and Simplicity:** The sMCL method is conceptually simple, parameter-free, and agnostic to the network architecture and the specific loss function. The authors convincingly demonstrate this generality by applying the exact same algorithm to a standard CNN for classification, an FCN for segmentation, and a CNN+RNN for captioning. The ease of implementation—essentially routing gradients to the "winning" model per example—makes it highly accessible and likely to be adopted by other researchers and practitioners.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and cover a diverse set of challenging tasks. The consistent and significant outperformance of sMCL over independently trained ensembles and other baselines in terms of oracle accuracy provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The qualitative results, particularly in segmentation and captioning, are compelling and clearly illustrate that sMCL encourages the ensemble members to learn different, meaningful specializations (e.g., focusing on different object classes or generating captions with different sentence structures), which directly addresses the paper's motivation.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Importance of Initialization:** The paper's clarity could be improved regarding the initialization of the models. For the semantic segmentation task, the authors state they initialize from a "standard ensemble trained for 50 epochs." This raises a critical question: how crucial is this pre-trained initialization to the success of sMCL? Would the method achieve similar performance and specialization if started from random initializations? An ablation study exploring the effect of random vs. pre-trained initialization would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the robustness of the training procedure.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Comparisons:** Related to the point above, it is unclear if the baseline methods received the same initialization as the sMCL models. Specifically, for the segmentation experiment, was the "classical ensemble" baseline also fine-tuned from the same pre-trained starting point? If sMCL was fine-tuned while the baselines were trained from scratch or a different initialization, the comparison is not entirely fair. Clarifying that all methods started from an identical footing is essential for validating the reported performance gains.

3.  **Evaluation Limited to Oracle Performance:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on oracle metrics (e.g., oracle accuracy, oracle IoU). While minimizing oracle loss is the direct objective, the paper is motivated by generating *diverse* solutions. The current evaluation does not quantitatively measure this diversity. The qualitative examples are helpful, but the analysis would be much stronger if it included quantitative diversity metrics. For instance, what is the average pairwise distance (e.g., IoU for segmentation, BLEU/CIDEr for captions) between the outputs of the ensemble members? This would provide concrete evidence that sMCL produces more distinct outputs than baseline ensembles, rather than just minor variations of the same hypothesis.

4.  **Missing Comparison to Relevant Work:** The paper would benefit from a discussion and comparison with "Why M Heads are Better than One" (Lee et al., ICLR 2016 workshop, arXiv:1511.06314). This work also proposes a method for jointly training an ensemble of deep networks to encourage diversity. While their objective function is different (involving an adversarial-style penalty), the high-level goal is very similar. Situating sMCL with respect to this highly relevant prior work would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

**Summary of Review:**

This paper introduces a simple yet powerful method, sMCL, for training diverse deep ensembles by directly optimizing the oracle loss. The approach is well-motivated, the proposed algorithm is novel for this context, and the experimental results across three challenging tasks are strong. The key weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity in the experimental setup (particularly regarding initialization) and an evaluation that, while positive, could be strengthened by including quantitative diversity metrics and a comparison to more closely related work. Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that is likely to be impactful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
-   Proposes a novel, practical, and effective method to apply the MCL paradigm to deep learning.
-   The method is simple, general, and easy to implement.
-   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on a diverse set of important vision and language tasks.
-   The qualitative analysis showing emergent specialization is insightful.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
-   Unclear experimental details regarding model initialization may call the fairness of the baseline comparisons into question.
-   The evaluation lacks quantitative measures of diversity, which is a core motivation of the work.
-   Fails to discuss or compare against highly relevant concurrent/prior work (Lee et al., 2015).
