- Proposes sMCL, a simple, parameter-free training algorithm for deep ensembles that updates only the lowest-loss member per example to encourage diverse outputs and minimize oracle loss.  
- Demonstrates sMCL across classification, segmentation, and captioning, showing consistent oracle-metric improvements over baselines.  
- Claims generality to architectures and loss functions and contrasts with prior MCL by enabling feasible deep-network training.

- Strength: Novel, simple "winner-takes-gradient" algorithm that avoids expensive MCL retraining and is easy to implement.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains (6–10% in oracle metrics) with appropriate baselines and metrics.  
- Strength: Broad applicability shown across CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures and multiple tasks.  
- Strength: Provides evidence of emergent, interpretable specialization among ensemble members.

- Weakness: Ambiguous testing procedure—unclear how the best output is chosen at inference; reviewer suspects improper use of ground truth.  
- Weakness: Terms like "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles (Indp.)" are undefined, causing confusion.  
- Weakness: Claim of meaningful specialization is not well-justified; specialization may be an artifact of training assignments.  
- Weakness: Fails to discuss related work on diversity regularization in deep networks (e.g., Chen et al. NIPS 2015).

- Evaluation (Quality): Technically sound with strong results but weakened by testing ambiguity and missing discussions of limitations.  
- Evaluation (Clarity): Generally clear algorithm description, but unclear inference procedure and undefined terminology reduce clarity.  
- Evaluation (Originality): Novel adaptation of MCL to deep networks, but omission of diversity-regularization literature lessens perceived novelty.  
- Evaluation (Significance): Addresses an important practical problem with a practical solution, but real-world applicability is unclear without inference details and related-work context.

- Recommendation: Weak accept (3); promising contribution but needs clarification on testing, terminology, and engagement with diversity-regularization literature.
