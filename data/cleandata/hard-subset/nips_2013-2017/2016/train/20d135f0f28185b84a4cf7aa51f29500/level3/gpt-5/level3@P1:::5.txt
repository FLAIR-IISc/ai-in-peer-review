This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple, parameter-free SGD-based procedure to jointly train an ensemble of deep networks to minimize oracle loss. Rather than averaging or voting, the ensemble is optimized so that at test time at least one member produces a high-quality prediction for each input, which is appropriate for downstream pipelines with verification or human-in-the-loop selection. The key idea is a “winner-take-gradient” update: for each training example, only the member with the lowest loss receives gradient signal. This yields emergent specialization across members without explicit architectural gating or diversity regularizers. The authors position sMCL as a practical deep instantiation of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) that avoids the expensive alternating retraining used in prior work.

Relation to prior work: The work builds on MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al., AISTATS/NIPS), diverse M-best inference in structured models, and ensemble learning/diversity methods (negative correlation learning, data resampling/decoration). It differs from probabilistic structured-output diversity methods that are not directly applicable to general deep nets, and from sequential/boosting-style approaches (Dey et al.) that train members one at a time. Compared to expert architectures/gating (e.g., “network of experts,” distillation-informed specialization), sMCL discovers specialization implicitly through the oracle objective. A closely related deep-ensemble line not discussed is “Why M Heads are Better than One” (Lee et al., 2015), which trains multiple classifier heads to encourage diversity; a comparison and discussion would help situate sMCL among deep diversity-promoting strategies.

Empirical evaluation spans three tasks and architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM). In all cases, sMCL improves oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, sequential diversity methods, and (where feasible) alternating MCL, while being substantially faster than the latter. Qualitatively, ensembles trained with sMCL exhibit interpretable specialization: near-disjoint class coverage in CIFAR-10, class/shape/frequency biases in segmentation, and n-gram/style diversity in captioning.

Quality:
- Strengths: The optimization is technically sound and well-justified by the gradient structure of the upper-bounded oracle objective. The implementation is straightforward (a routing layer in backprop), architecture/loss-agnostic, and trains all members concurrently. Experiments are broad, include strong baselines (independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.), and provide both quantitative and qualitative analyses. The observed emergent expertise is compelling and aligns with the objective.
- Weaknesses: The evaluation leans heavily on oracle metrics; additional diversity measures (e.g., pairwise disagreement/KL of predictive distributions, diversity in segmentation masks beyond IoU, mBLEU/Self-BLEU for captions) would more fully characterize tradeoffs. Top-1 (or average) performance of individual members is not reported; it would be useful to document any degradation vs independent training. The sensitivity to initialization and training dynamics is not systematically studied: do early “winners” monopolize examples, leading to imbalanced learning or dead members? Are results stable across random seeds? For segmentation and captioning, it is unclear whether all baselines (including MCL and independent ensembles) used identical pretraining/initialization and fine-tuning budgets as sMCL. Finally, while the paper explores a top-k variant, a more thorough analysis of soft vs hard assignment (e.g., annealing k or using a softmin) could address potential gradient starvation.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides intuitive motivation and pseudo-code. Figures effectively illustrate emergent specialization. Some experimental details need clarification: training budgets for each baseline to ensure fairness; tie-breaking policy; batch size effects on assignments; handling of class imbalance; and whether member capacities are identical.

Originality:
- The core novelty is adapting MCL to deep networks with a stochastic block gradient procedure that enables one-pass, parallel training. While conceptually related to hard-EM and mixture-of-experts with hard gating, the instantiation to generic deep nets and demonstration across tasks is original and timely for NIPS 2016. A discussion contrasting sMCL with multi-head diversity methods (Lee et al., 2015) and expert gating would strengthen the positioning.

Significance:
- The problem is important for systems that benefit from multiple plausible hypotheses. The method is practical, generic, and shows consistent gains on three representative tasks. The simplicity and plug-in nature of sMCL suggest it could see real adoption. The main caveat is the focus on oracle metrics; demonstrating downstream gains with a simple selector (e.g., learned reranker or weak oracle) would further cement impact.

Pros:
- Simple, parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic training procedure.
- Concurrent training; large speedup vs alternating MCL with comparable or better oracle performance.
- Consistent improvements across classification, segmentation, and captioning, with insightful qualitative analyses of specialization.
- Clear exposition and broadly relevant problem formulation.

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited quantitative diversity analysis and no downstream selection experiments.
- Unclear fairness of pretraining/initialization and training budget across baselines; lack of sensitivity analysis to initialization and tie-breaking.
- Potential risk of gradient starvation/imbalance across members not fully addressed.
- Missing comparison to closely related deep ensemble diversity methods (e.g., “Why M Heads are Better than One”).

Suggestions for the authors:
- Report additional diversity metrics and average/top-1 performance of members to quantify tradeoffs.
- Clarify baseline setups: identical initializations (random vs pretrained), training schedules, and compute budgets; include multiple seeds.
- Analyze assignment dynamics: member load balancing over time, effect of batch size, and stability across seeds; consider soft/annealed assignment (e.g., top-k with schedule) to mitigate early winner lock-in.
- Include a simple downstream selector (e.g., a learned reranker) to show that oracle gains translate to practical improvements.
- Add comparisons/discussion with multi-head diversity methods and mixture-of-experts/gating approaches; evaluate sMCL with modern backbones (e.g., ResNet) if feasible.

Overall, this is a strong, practical contribution with solid empirical evidence and broad applicability. Addressing the experimental clarity and diversity/selection analyses would further strengthen the paper.
