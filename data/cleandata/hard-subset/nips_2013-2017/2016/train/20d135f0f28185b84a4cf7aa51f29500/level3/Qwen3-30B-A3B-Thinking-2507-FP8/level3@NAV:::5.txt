

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training deep ensembles that jointly minimize oracle loss to produce multiple diverse outputs. The authors formalize the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm within deep learning by introducing a stochastic block gradient descent method that backpropagates gradients only to the ensemble member producing the lowest loss for each example. This avoids the costly retraining required by previous MCL approaches, enabling efficient end-to-end training of deep ensembles.

The paper makes several strong contributions: it successfully applies MCL to diverse deep architectures (CNNs for classification, FCNs for segmentation, CNN+RNN for captioning), demonstrates significant performance improvements over baselines (6-10% oracle accuracy gains), and reveals how sMCL automatically induces interpretable specialization among ensemble members. The method is technically sound, parameter-free, and agnostic to architecture and loss function.

However, several concerns limit the paper's impact. First, the experimental comparison lacks crucial details: it's unclear whether baselines used the same initialization as sMCL, potentially biasing results. Second, the evaluation focuses exclusively on oracle accuracy while neglecting analysis of ensemble diversity - a core aspect of the proposed method. Third, the authors fail to compare with Lee et al. (2015) "Why M Heads are Better than One," which directly addresses multiple outputs in neural networks and would provide valuable context.

The paper excels in technical quality and significance, offering a practical solution for systems requiring multiple hypotheses. It demonstrates clear applicability across vision and language tasks, advancing the state of the art in ensemble learning for structured prediction. The clarity of the method description is excellent, making implementation straightforward.

The main weaknesses center on incomplete evaluation (missing diversity analysis, unclear baseline comparisons) and insufficient related work coverage. While the technical approach is novel and well-executed, the lack of proper comparison with Lee et al. (2015) and insufficient analysis of initialization effects weaken the claims about the method's superiority.

In summary, this is a strong technical contribution with practical value, but the evaluation needs strengthening to fully support the claims. With additional analysis of diversity and proper comparison with relevant prior work, this paper would be highly impactful. As presented, it's a solid contribution that advances ensemble learning for multiple-output prediction tasks.
