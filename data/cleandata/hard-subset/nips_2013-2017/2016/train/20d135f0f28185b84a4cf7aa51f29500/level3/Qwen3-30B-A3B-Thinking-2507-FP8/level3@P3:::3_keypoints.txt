- Proposes sMCL, a simple "winner-takes-the-gradient" concurrent training method for diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss.  
- sMCL avoids expensive retraining/sequential construction required by prior MCL methods, making it practical for deep networks.  
- Demonstrates broad applicability and consistent improvements on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning.  
- Shows 6–10% relative gains over baselines (independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.) with qualitative evidence of emergent specialist behaviors.  
- Method is simple to implement (one extra layer) and parameter-free, increasing practical adoption potential.  
- Claims of "diversity" are central to the paper but no explicit diversity regularizer is used; diversity arises implicitly and this limitation is not clearly framed.  
- Contribution is somewhat incremental relative to Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL; sMCL is a practical relaxation but builds directly on prior work.  
- Title and text overemphasize "diversity," potentially misleading about the theoretical basis of the approach.  
- Experimental design and results are technically sound and convincing, though the paper should acknowledge the theoretical gap on explicit diversity enforcement.  
- Paper is well-written and organized; minor clarity/typo issues should be fixed (abstract wording, Line 108 "f"→"g", extra period at Line 115).  
- Novelty lies in applying stochastic SGD with winner-takes-the-gradient to deep networks, distinct from prior ensemble/diversity methods.  
- Significance is high for real-world systems requiring multiple hypotheses; emergent specialist ensembles are particularly valuable.  
- Raises an open question: whether baseline MCL deep networks were fully converged and if retraining/early termination could harm their performance versus sMCL.  
- Recommendation: Accept — practical, effective, broadly applicable contribution with minor revisions and clearer framing about diversity.
