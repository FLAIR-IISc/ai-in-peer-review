- Proposes sMCL: a stochastic block gradient descent ("winner-take-gradient") algorithm that optimizes oracle loss to make MCL practical for deep networks.  
- sMCL routes gradients only to the ensemble member with lowest loss per example, enabling concurrent end-to-end training.  
- Demonstrated on classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning with significant oracle performance gains over baselines.  
- Strength: Addresses an important problem (multiple plausible hypotheses) and makes prior MCL ideas practical for modern deep learning.  
- Strength: Method is simple, parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and easy to implement.  
- Strength: Strong, diverse empirical results and qualitative evidence of ensemble members specializing in meaningful ways.  
- Weakness: Initialization details are unclear—segmentation used a pre-trained ensemble—raising questions about dependence on pre-training and need for ablation.  
- Weakness: Unclear whether baselines used identical initialization, which may affect fairness of comparisons.  
- Weakness: Evaluation focuses only on oracle metrics and lacks quantitative diversity measures (e.g., pairwise IoU, BLEU/CIDEr distances).  
- Weakness: Missing discussion/comparison to closely related work (Lee et al., "Why M Heads are Better than One", 2015).  
- Summary: sMCL is a simple, novel, and effective contribution, but experimental clarity and quantitative diversity/comparative analyses should be strengthened.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/practical extension of MCL, general and easy to adopt, strong empirical and qualitative results.  
- Arguments for rejection: possible unfair baseline initialization, lack of quantitative diversity evaluation, and omission of closely related prior work.
