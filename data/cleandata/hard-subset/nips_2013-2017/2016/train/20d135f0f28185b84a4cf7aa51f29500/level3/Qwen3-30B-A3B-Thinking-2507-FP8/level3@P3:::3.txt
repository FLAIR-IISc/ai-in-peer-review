

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" (NIPS 2016)

This paper addresses an important problem in deep learning: training diverse ensembles of deep networks that produce multiple plausible predictions rather than a single output. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple method that trains ensemble members concurrently by implementing a "winner-takes-the-gradient" strategy during backpropagation. This approach directly minimizes the oracle loss (the loss of the best prediction among the ensemble) without requiring retraining or sequential model construction, which was necessary in previous work by Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2013). The authors demonstrate the broad applicability of sMCL across three distinct tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing significant improvements over baselines including classical independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s method.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles, which is particularly relevant for real-world applications where multiple hypotheses are needed for downstream evaluation (e.g., autonomous vehicles, accessibility systems). The authors effectively contrast their approach with prior ensemble work that focuses on diversity through resampling or joint loss functions, and with methods for generating multiple solutions from single models that aren't directly applicable to deep architectures.

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical applications where multiple predictions are beneficial rather than a single output.
- The method is remarkably simple to implement (adding just one "winner-takes-the-gradient" layer to existing ensemble architectures) and is parameter-free.
- The evaluation across three diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) convincingly demonstrates broad applicability.
- The paper provides compelling evidence that sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members without explicit diversity constraints.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper heavily emphasizes "diversity" in motivation and discussion but fails to enforce an explicit diversity term. The diversity emerges as a byproduct of the training procedure, which is not clearly articulated as a limitation. This creates a disconnect between the paper's framing and its technical approach.
- The contribution appears somewhat incremental relative to Guzman-Rivera et al.'s work. While their approach required expensive retraining, sMCL is a natural relaxation that makes the method practical for deep networks. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently acknowledge how much this builds on prior work.
- The title and text overemphasize "diversity," which isn't explicitly enforced, potentially misleading readers about the method's theoretical basis.

**Minor issues:**
- The first sentence of the abstract should be reworded for clarity and impact.
- The emphasis on "diversity" in the title and throughout the text should be toned down to better reflect the actual technical contribution.
- Line 108 contains a typo: "f" should be "g."
- Line 115 contains an extra period in the middle of a sentence.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments across multiple tasks. The results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over baselines (6-10% relative gains) while providing qualitative evidence of emergent specializations. However, the lack of explicit diversity enforcement is a notable theoretical gap that should be acknowledged.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the method and its advantages. The experimental setup and results are thoroughly presented. The minor issues noted above should be fixed in the final version.

**Originality:** The core idea of using stochastic gradient descent with winner-takes-the-gradient is novel for deep networks, though it builds directly on Guzman-Rivera et al.'s MCL framework. The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work by providing a practical implementation for deep learning.

**Significance:** The problem of producing multiple predictions is increasingly important for real-world AI systems. sMCL offers a simple, effective solution that could be widely adopted across computer vision and NLP applications. The automatic emergence of specialist ensembles is particularly valuable for understanding model behavior.

**Additional question:** The paper doesn't address how baseline MCL deep networks were ensured to have converged. Could early termination of learners (due to the retraining requirement) harm ensemble performance compared to sMCL's concurrent training?

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper presents a simple yet effective solution to an important practical problem. The method is easy to implement, broadly applicable, and significantly outperforms baselines across multiple tasks. While the contribution appears somewhat incremental, the practical value for deep learning practitioners is substantial. The minor issues noted can be easily addressed in the final version. The work represents a meaningful step toward practical ensemble methods for deep learning that produce diverse, interpretable predictions.
