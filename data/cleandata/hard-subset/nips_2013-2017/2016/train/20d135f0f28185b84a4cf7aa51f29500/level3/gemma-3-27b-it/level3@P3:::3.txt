This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple method for training diverse deep ensembles by adapting a multiple choice learning (MCL) framework to stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is a “winner-takes-the-gradient” approach during backpropagation, effectively assigning each training example to the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss. The authors demonstrate strong performance across image classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks, highlighting the method’s simplicity, broad applicability, and ability to generate interpretable diverse solutions.

This work builds upon the MCL paradigm introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9], addressing the impracticality of their alternating block coordinate descent approach for training modern deep networks. The authors contrast their method with related work in ensemble learning and diverse solution generation, emphasizing sMCL’s ability to discover specialization automatically and its parameter-free nature. It also relates to recent work on submodular optimization for diversity [5], again highlighting the efficiency advantage of sMCL.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** The paper tackles a relevant and challenging problem – generating diverse and informative outputs from deep learning models, particularly in scenarios where multiple plausible solutions exist. This is crucial for real-world applications requiring robustness to ambiguity and providing interpretable predictions.
*   **Simplicity and ease of implementation:** sMCL is remarkably simple, requiring only a minor modification to standard stochastic gradient descent training. This makes it easily accessible and readily applicable to various architectures and loss functions.
*   **Comprehensive evaluation:** The evaluation covers three diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) with different network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNNs), demonstrating the method’s generality.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The results consistently show that sMCL outperforms baseline ensembles and, where comparable, surpasses the performance of existing MCL approaches (while being significantly faster).
*   **Emergent specialization:** The analysis showing that ensemble members automatically specialize on different aspects of the data distribution (e.g., classes in classification, segment types in segmentation) is a compelling demonstration of the method’s effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited explicit diversity enforcement:** Despite motivating the method around the concept of diversity, sMCL doesn’t explicitly enforce a diversity term in the loss function. While the “winner-takes-all” gradient approach encourages specialization, it doesn't guarantee a diverse solution set. The observed diversity emerges as a consequence of minimizing the oracle loss, but a more direct diversity objective might lead to even better results.
*   **Incremental contribution:** While sMCL provides a practical solution for deep networks, the core contribution feels somewhat incremental relative to the relaxation proposed by Guzman et al. [8, 9]. The primary advance is adapting the existing MCL framework to a stochastic gradient descent setting, rather than introducing a fundamentally new learning principle.
*   **Convergence of baseline MCL networks:** The paper states that baseline MCL networks were trained to convergence, but it's unclear how convergence was determined, particularly given the iterative nature of the alternating block coordinate descent algorithm. Early stopping could potentially harm the ensemble performance. A more detailed discussion of the convergence criteria would be beneficial.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The first sentence of the abstract could be reworded for clarity. It's a bit verbose and doesn't immediately convey the core idea of the paper.
*   The emphasis on “diversity” in the title and throughout the text could be toned down slightly. While diversity is a desirable outcome, the method’s primary objective is to minimize oracle loss, and framing it solely as a diversity-focused approach might be misleading.
*   Typo on line 108: the first “f” should be “g.”
*   Remove the extra “.” in the middle of the sentence on line 115.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in deep learning.
*   The proposed method is simple, effective, and readily implementable.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates strong performance.
*   The analysis provides insights into the emergent behavior of the learned ensembles.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is somewhat incremental, building on existing MCL work.
*   The lack of explicit diversity enforcement might limit the potential performance gains.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a practical and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles. While the contribution isn't groundbreaking, it addresses a significant challenge and provides a valuable tool for practitioners. The strengths of the paper—its simplicity, broad applicability, and strong experimental results—outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above (reword abstract, tone down diversity emphasis, fix typos, and clarify baseline convergence). I am particularly interested in seeing a response to my question regarding the criteria used to determine convergence for the baseline MCL networks.
