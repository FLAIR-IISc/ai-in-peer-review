The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel approach for training deep ensembles to produce multiple diverse solutions for a given input. The authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which minimizes the oracle loss directly. The algorithm is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The main idea of sMCL is to update only the learner with the lowest loss for each training example, which is a straightforward optimization strategy for learners trained by stochastic gradient descent-based solvers. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The paper presents several key contributions, including the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach well-suited to minimizing the oracle loss in ensembles of deep networks, and the demonstration of the automatic emergence of different interpretable notions of specializations among ensemble members.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed algorithm is simple to implement and has strong empirical results.
* The authors demonstrate the broad applicability of sMCL on various problem domains and network architectures.
* The paper presents a novel approach for training deep ensembles to produce multiple diverse solutions.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

* The testing phase is unclear, and it is suspected that the authors use ground truth to select the best member.
* The terms "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" (Indp.) are not explained.
* The claim of member "specialization" to different output classes is not convincingly justified since training reassignments occur on a per-example basis.
* The paper does not discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, which is closely related and more rigorous but under-studied in deep settings.

To address these weaknesses, the authors could provide more details on the testing phase, explain the terms "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles", and provide more convincing evidence for member specialization. Additionally, the authors could discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach for training deep ensembles to produce multiple diverse solutions. While the idea of updating only the learner with the lowest loss for each training example is simple, the authors demonstrate its effectiveness on various problem domains and network architectures.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the performance of deep ensembles on various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The proposed approach could also be useful in situations where multiple diverse solutions are desired, such as in image captioning where multiple sentences can be generated to describe an image.
