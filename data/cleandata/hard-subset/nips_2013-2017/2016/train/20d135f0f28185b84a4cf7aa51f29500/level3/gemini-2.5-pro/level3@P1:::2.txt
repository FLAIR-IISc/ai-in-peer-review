### Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep network ensembles designed to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The work is framed within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, where the goal is to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the single best prediction from an ensemble for a given input. The key idea behind sMCL is a simple yet effective "winner-take-gradient" strategy: for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that produced the lowest loss. This approach is a stochastic approximation of the block coordinate descent method used in prior MCL work (Guzman-Rivera et al., 2012), but is far more efficient and directly applicable to deep learning, as it allows for concurrent training of all ensemble members. The authors demonstrate that this training objective naturally encourages individual networks in the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution. The method's effectiveness is validated extensively across three distinct domains: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), consistently outperforming baseline methods like independently trained ensembles.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is technically sound and the proposed sMCL algorithm is a clever and practical solution to the problem of training diverse deep ensembles. The claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments across multiple challenging tasks and different network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). The qualitative and quantitative results convincingly demonstrate that sMCL achieves lower oracle error and produces more diverse outputs than baselines. The analysis of the "interpretable expertise" that emerges from training is particularly insightful, showing how networks specialize on different classes (classification), segmentation styles, or n-grams (captioning).

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, the proposed method is described concisely, and the experimental setup and results are presented in a detailed and easy-to-follow manner.

One minor presentation issue is that the pseudocode in Figure 2 appears to be a low-resolution raster image, which makes it difficult to read. This should be replaced with typeset text or a vector graphic for the final version.

### Originality and Significance

The core idea of minimizing an oracle loss via an ensemble is not new and builds directly on the MCL framework. However, the contribution of this paper is a novel, simple, and highly effective *stochastic algorithm* to achieve this for deep networks. Previous methods required costly retraining or sequential training, making them infeasible for modern deep learning models. The proposed sMCL method is original in its formulation as a single backward pass where gradients are selectively routed, making it efficient and easy to implement. This is a significant practical contribution that makes MCL a viable training strategy for the deep learning era.

The significance of the work lies in its potential to address problems where a single best answer is insufficient or ill-defined due to inherent ambiguity in the data. The ability to generate a diverse set of plausible hypotheses is valuable for many real-world systems that involve a human-in-the-loop or a downstream verification module. The method is general, parameter-free, and agnostic to both the network architecture and the loss function, which broadens its potential impact.

### Weaknesses

The primary and most significant weakness of this paper is the complete omission of how to use the trained ensemble at inference time. The entire evaluation framework relies on an oracle to select the best hypothesis from the M outputs. In any practical application, this oracle does not exist. The paper does not propose or evaluate any strategy for exploiting the diverse set of outputs, such as re-ranking, combining predictions, or even simply presenting them to a user.

This omission makes the work feel incomplete and substantially limits its practical significance. While the paper successfully demonstrates that sMCL can produce an output set that *contains* a better solution than a standard ensemble, it provides no mechanism for a user or a system to *find* that better solution. The impressive gains in oracle accuracy are therefore difficult to translate into concrete improvements in a real-world scenario. The practical utility of the method is left as an open, and non-trivial, problem for future work. Strategies for exploiting the ensemble are likely to be problem-specific and would require their own careful design and evaluation, but the lack of any discussion on this front is a major limitation.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Elegant Algorithm:** sMCL is a simple, clever, and novel algorithm that makes the Multiple Choice Learning objective practical for training large-scale deep ensembles.
2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness across three different, challenging tasks and architectures. The results are thorough and well-presented.
3.  **Interesting Emergent Behavior:** The analysis of how specialization emerges among ensemble members is insightful and provides a deeper understanding of the training dynamics.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Major Omission of Inference Strategy:** The paper fails to address the crucial question of how to use the ensemble's outputs without an oracle. This severely limits the practical applicability of the work and makes the reported "oracle" performance gains difficult to interpret in a real-world context. The paper essentially solves only half of the problem.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a strong technical contribution with a novel and effective training method. The experimental validation is excellent. However, the work is hampered by a major conceptual omission regarding inference. While the contribution to training methodology is valuable, the lack of a corresponding inference strategy makes this a somewhat incomplete piece of research. Despite this significant limitation, the novelty of the training algorithm and the strength of the empirical results likely warrant acceptance, with the strong recommendation that the authors explicitly acknowledge and discuss the inference problem as a critical area for future work in the final version.
