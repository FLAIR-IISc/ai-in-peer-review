## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks to generate multiple, diverse outputs for scenarios involving oracles (e.g., human-in-the-loop systems). The core idea is to directly minimize oracle loss during training by employing a stochastic block gradient descent optimization, effectively assigning gradient updates only to the ensemble member providing the best prediction for each example. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showcasing improved oracle accuracy compared to baseline methods. 

The work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, addressing the impracticality of previous methods (like alternating block coordinate descent) for deep networks by proposing a computationally efficient, end-to-end trainable solution. The simplicity of implementation – requiring only a minor modification to existing training loops – is a significant strength. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating consistent gains across diverse tasks and architectures. The analysis revealing emergent specialization within ensemble members is insightful and supports the claim that sMCL fosters diversity.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The importance of initialization is not adequately addressed. While the authors mention using random initialization, the impact of pre-trained weights (a common practice in deep learning) is unknown and could significantly influence the results. Crucially, it’s unclear whether the baseline methods utilized the *same* initialization strategy as sMCL. A fair comparison necessitates controlling for this factor. Furthermore, the evaluation focuses solely on oracle accuracy, neglecting a more in-depth analysis of ensemble diversity. Metrics quantifying the dissimilarity between generated outputs (e.g., pairwise distance in feature space) would strengthen the claims of improved diversity. 

The paper would benefit from a comparison with Lee et al. (2015) – "Why M Heads are Better than One" – which also explores ensemble diversity, albeit through a different mechanism. This would help position sMCL within the broader landscape of diversity-inducing techniques. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to training diverse ensembles with a practical, end-to-end implementation.
*   Consistent performance improvements across multiple tasks and architectures.
*   Insightful analysis of emergent specialization within ensemble members.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of investigation into the impact of initialization strategies.
*   Unclear comparison with baselines regarding initialization.
*   Insufficient analysis of ensemble diversity beyond oracle accuracy.
*   Missing comparison to relevant work (Lee et al., 2015).



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. While the concerns regarding initialization and diversity analysis should be addressed, the novelty, practical implementation, and promising results warrant consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
