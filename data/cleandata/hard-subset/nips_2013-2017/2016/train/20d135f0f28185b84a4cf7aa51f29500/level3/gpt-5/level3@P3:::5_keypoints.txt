- Proposal: sMCL — a per-batch “winner-take-gradient” variant of Multiple Choice Learning where only the lowest-loss predictor for each example receives the gradient; parameter-free, end-to-end, and easy to integrate into backprop.  
- Positioning: Framed against multiple-hypothesis generation and ensemble/diversity methods; contrasts with classical MCL (retraining) and explicit diversity regularizers; related but different from multi-head approaches (objective and training dynamics differ).  
- Empirics: Consistent oracle improvements on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning versus independent ensembles, MCL meta-iterations, and a sequential submodular method.  
- Qualitative findings: Emergent specialization observed (class clusters in classification, shape/frequency biases in segmentation, n-gram/style diversity in captioning).  
- Strength — practicality: Simple, practical training rule making MCL feasible for deep nets; integrates into standard backprop and is parameter-free.  
- Strength — generality: Demonstrated across diverse architectures/tasks, suggesting broad applicability.  
- Strength — performance: Strong oracle gains and reported faster training than prior MCL (~5× in reported settings).  
- Strength — interpretability: Qualitative analyses show interpretable specialization and improved coverage of ambiguous cases.  
- Strength — conceptual clarity: Directly optimizes oracle loss, aligning objective with multi-hypothesis needs.  
- Weakness — initialization fairness: Unclear if baselines used identical initializations/training budgets; need ablations on random vs. pre-trained initialization and sensitivity across tasks.  
- Weakness — narrow evaluation: Focuses almost exclusively on oracle metrics; requests quantitative diversity metrics and single-prediction performance to assess specialization costs.  
- Weakness — missing multi-head comparison: Lacks direct comparison/discussion with “Why M Heads are Better than One” (Lee et al., 2015) and shared-trunk variants.  
- Weakness — optimization/stability questions: Tie-breaking for min-assignment, potential assignment churn or mode collapse, and sensitivity to batch size, learning rate, and k-winner variants need analysis.  
- Weakness — compute/memory clarity: Need comparisons of wall-clock and memory cost versus independent ensembles with matched epochs/steps and discussion of shared-trunk setups.  
- Weakness — captioning baseline fairness: Suggest evaluating union-of-beams from sMCL members (e.g., beam=2 per member) and reporting unique n-gram counts as a fairer inference-time comparison.  
- Quality: Technically sound derivation consistent with block coordinate descent; empirical evidence across tasks is compelling but more convergence/stability analysis would help.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well organized; requests for explicit training details, tie-handling description, and sensitivity analyses.  
- Originality: Novel and practically important stochastic adaptation of MCL for deep networks, but needs stronger positioning versus multi-head/diversity-regularized work.  
- Significance: High practical significance for systems requiring multiple plausible hypotheses; method is simple to adopt and likely impactful.  
- Requested additional experiments: Initialization ablations, diversity metrics and coverage analysis, multi-head comparisons under matched compute, k-winner trade-offs, and captioning union-of-beams experiments.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept (leaning Accept); addressing initialization, diversity quantification, and multi-head comparisons would strengthen the paper.
