Summary and contributions
This paper tackles the problem of producing multiple plausible outputs by training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss (the loss of the best member’s prediction per example). The core contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” SGD procedure: for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates. This replaces prior alternating retraining schemes for Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) with an efficient, parallelizable per-batch assignment-and-update mechanism. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free (in its basic form), and shown empirically on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation, and MSCOCO image captioning. The paper claims improved oracle performance versus independent ensembles, sequential/boosting-style methods, and alternating MCL, and illustrates emergent specialization and diversity in the ensemble’s outputs.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al.] but makes it practical for deep networks via stochastic block gradient descent, avoiding multiple costly retrainings. It complements lines of work on producing multiple diverse solutions (M-best, DivMBest, diverse decoding for structured models) that typically assume probabilistic graphical models or specific structure, and on ensemble diversity (e.g., resampling, negative correlation learning [Liu & Yao], artificial data). The paper briefly cites generalist-to-expert architectures and sequential diversity methods [Dey et al.], but could deepen the discussion of explicit diversity regularization in neural ensembles (e.g., negative-correlation variants and more recent deep diversity regularizers such as those explored by Chen et al.), and clarify how sMCL differs from or could incorporate such regularizers.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm that fits neatly into SGD-based training and works across tasks and architectures without bespoke modeling.
- Strong empirical gains in oracle metrics across three domains, with useful qualitative diversity in outputs.
- Clear speed advantage over alternating MCL while matching or exceeding oracle performance, making the MCL paradigm applicable to modern deep nets.
- Interesting qualitative analysis indicating emergent specialization (class-level for CIFAR-10; structural/shape diversity for segmentation; linguistic diversity for captioning).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Testing phase is underspecified. Results are reported on oracle metrics, which necessarily use ground truth at evaluation time to select the best hypothesis. Please clarify:
  - At test time in a practical system, what mechanism replaces the oracle? Can you report results with a simple downstream selector (e.g., a learned verifier or reranker) to demonstrate end-to-end utility?
  - Ensure the paper clearly states that oracle selection uses ground truth only for evaluation, not for inference.
- Baselines terminology is unclear. The terms “independent ensembles,” “regular ensembles,” and “Indp.” appear in text/figures but are not consistently defined. Please specify exactly how “Indp.” models are trained (initializations, data augmentation, optimizer settings, early stopping) and whether training compute is matched across methods.
- Specialization claims could be better supported. Since assignments are per-example, it is not yet fully convincing that members robustly specialize to stable subpopulations/classes. Suggested analyses:
  - Longitudinal assignment stability over epochs; entropy of assignment distributions; per-member per-class confusion matrices and F1; cluster coherence of assigned examples in feature space.
  - Ablations with k-winners (you include some results) and with soft assignments (e.g., softmin over losses with temperature) to test whether harder gating is essential for specialization.
- Fairness of MCL baseline. Only five meta-iterations are reported due to cost; this weakens the comparison. Consider either (a) a smaller setting where full convergence of MCL is feasible, (b) time-normalized comparisons (equal wall-clock), or (c) stronger evidence that performance has plateaued for MCL.
- Diversity measurement for captions. Unique n-gram counts are a start but can be gamed. Consider reporting diversity metrics such as distinct-n, mBLEU/self-BLEU, and recall@M of reference n-grams, along with standard oracle CIDEr/SPICE.
- Related work on diversity regularization. Beyond negative correlation learning, there is a body of work that explicitly regularizes diversity in neural ensembles (e.g., correlation penalties on outputs/logits, pairwise KL or cosine repulsion, determinantal/submodular objectives). A discussion (and possibly an ablation combining sMCL with such regularizers) would strengthen the paper.
- Minor details: tie-breaking when multiple members attain equal loss; early training dynamics (risk of starvation of some members); computational/memory cost at larger M; sensitivity to initialization and seeds.

Quality
- Technically sound and well-motivated. The derivation from the MCL objective to a per-batch winner-take-gradient rule is straightforward and correct for SGD-based learners.
- Experimental results are compelling across domains. Some baselines (MCL) are hampered by compute, and analysis of specialization could be deeper, but overall the evidence supports the main claims.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. However, clarify the test-time/oracle evaluation protocol, define baselines precisely, and add missing training/inference details (tie-breaking, seeds, compute budget). Consider moving more of the k-winners ablation and assignment analyses into the main text.

Originality
- Conceptually a simple but nontrivial adaptation: bringing MCL into stochastic gradient training with per-example gating. The idea is natural yet impactful; novelty is moderate but the practical contribution is significant.

Significance
- Useful for many settings where multiple hypotheses are beneficial. Likely to be adopted due to simplicity and speed. The cross-domain results suggest broad applicability. Potential to become a default baseline for multi-hypothesis deep learning.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Can you report end-to-end results with a learned oracle/selector (e.g., a verification network or human-in-the-loop simulation) to demonstrate practical gains beyond oracle metrics?
- Provide stability analyses of specialization as suggested above. Do members truly become experts on persistent slices of the data?
- Explore soft assignment (temperature-controlled softmin over losses) and compare to k-winners; does partial credit help prevent member starvation early in training?
- Could specialization be explicitly encouraged in the loss? Examples: negative correlation penalties between member logits, pairwise KL divergence between members’ predictive distributions, DPP/submodular diversity on outputs, or an auxiliary gating loss that promotes partitioning.
- Clarify and strengthen baselines: fully specify “Indp.”, add compute-matched comparisons, and if possible include a small-scale fully converged MCL.
- For captioning, add diversity metrics (distinct-n, self-BLEU) and compute-matched comparisons to beam search (e.g., equal number of forward passes).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general algorithm; easy to adopt.
- Strong empirical improvements in oracle metrics across diverse tasks.
- Significant speedup over prior MCL while retaining its benefits.
- Appealing qualitative diversity and emergent specialization.

Cons against acceptance
- Oracle-only evaluation leaves practical test-time utility somewhat indirect.
- Some baselines not fully comparable due to compute limitations; “Indp.” setup lacks detail.
- Specialization claim could be supported with deeper analyses.
- Related work on explicit diversity regularization in neural ensembles is under-discussed.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The method is practical and broadly useful, with solid empirical gains. Addressing the clarity issues, strengthening baselines, and adding analyses of specialization and test-time selection would make the paper stronger.

Confidence
Medium.
