This paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm that minimizes the oracle loss directly, allowing the ensemble members to specialize on subsets of the data distribution. The method is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution. The related work section is comprehensive, and the authors adequately reference previous contributions in the field.

However, the technical section is too short and lacks detail, making it difficult to fully understand the proposed method. The authors could benefit from providing more mathematical derivations and explanations of the algorithm.

The experiments cover a range of tasks, including classification, segmentation, and captioning, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL in training diverse deep ensembles. However, the baselines used in the experiments are weak, and the authors could benefit from comparing their method to stronger baselines.

The paper's significance is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors provide unique data and conclusions on existing data, and their method has the potential to advance the state of the art in deep learning.

However, the paper's quality could be improved by providing more detail in the technical section and using stronger baselines in the experiments. Additionally, the authors could benefit from evaluating their method on more challenging datasets, such as PASCAL VOC 2012, to further demonstrate its effectiveness.

Overall, I would recommend this paper for acceptance, but with revisions to address the above-mentioned concerns.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The method is simple to implement and agnostic to both architecture and loss function.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL in training diverse deep ensembles.
* The paper's significance is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The technical section is too short and lacks detail.
* The baselines used in the experiments are weak.
* The authors could benefit from evaluating their method on more challenging datasets.
* The paper's quality could be improved by providing more detail in the technical section and using stronger baselines in the experiments.
