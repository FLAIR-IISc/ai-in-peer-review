This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to minimize the "oracle loss," where for each training instance, only the ensemble member that produces the best prediction (i.e., lowest loss) receives a gradient update. This extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] to modern deep learning architectures by adapting the optimization for stochastic gradient descent, allowing for concurrent training of all ensemble members. The authors demonstrate this approach on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

The primary strength of this work is its well-motivated problem statement. Generating multiple plausible hypotheses is a valuable goal for many real-world systems where a downstream oracle, human or automated, can select the best option. The proposed sMCL method is simple, intuitive, and broadly applicable across different network architectures and loss functions without requiring additional parameters.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution. The originality is incremental, as the method is largely a neural-network adaptation of the prior MCL framework [8] with minor adjustments for SGD. The conceptual novelty is therefore limited. Furthermore, the clarity of the paper is a major concern. The technical description in Section 3 is extremely brief (a single page), lacking sufficient detail for other researchers to confidently reproduce the work. The paper also fails to adequately distinguish sMCL from other widely-used techniques like alternative or multi-task training for ensembles.

The experimental evaluation is not convincing. While the authors apply sMCL to three different tasks, the improvements over baselines are marginal and the baselines themselves appear weak. For the claims of effectiveness to be substantiated, the method must be evaluated against stronger, contemporary baselines on established benchmarks. For instance, for semantic segmentation, a more thorough evaluation on the standard PASCAL VOC 2012 test set would be more compelling. In its current form, the paper presents an interesting idea but lacks the technical depth and strong empirical validation necessary for acceptance.
