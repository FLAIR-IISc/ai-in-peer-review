

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss. The authors pose the problem of producing multiple high-quality outputs as a learning problem over an ensemble of deep networks, where the goal is to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle that selects the best prediction from the ensemble. The key innovation is a simple stochastic gradient descent-based method that backpropagates gradients only to the "winning" predictor for each example (the one with the lowest error), effectively combining the assignment step with model training. This avoids the costly retraining required by previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches, making it practical for modern deep networks.

The paper builds on the MCL framework introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2015), which proposed an alternating block coordinate descent approach requiring retraining models multiple times. While the authors acknowledge this prior work, their key contribution is formulating the problem as a stochastic optimization approach suitable for deep learning, which they demonstrate across image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO) tasks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical justification for the sMCL approach. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three distinct tasks with multiple baselines (including classical independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s method). The results consistently show sMCL outperforms baselines by 6-10% in oracle performance while being 5x faster than MCL. The analysis of specialization emerging in ensemble members provides valuable insights into how the method works. However, the paper could better address how the authors ensured convergence of the baseline MCL method, as the question of whether early stopping of MCL learners would harm performance is not addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the method accessible to the NIPS audience. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented effectively. Minor issues noted include a typo on line 108 ("f" should be "g"), an extra period on line 115, and the abstract's first sentence could be reworded for greater precision.

**Originality:** The core idea of using a "winner-takes-gradient" approach is novel in the context of deep learning, though the paper could better distinguish how this differs from the Guzman-Rivera relaxation. The contribution is primarily practical rather than theoretical, making it a valuable tool for practitioners but not a major theoretical breakthrough. The paper's emphasis on "diversity" is somewhat misleading, as the method does not explicitly enforce diversity—it simply minimizes oracle loss, which happens to produce diverse outputs as a byproduct. This is a notable disconnect between the paper's focus and its actual contribution.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant for practical AI systems that benefit from multiple hypotheses (e.g., autonomous vehicles, accessibility tools). The method is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and parameter-free, making it immediately useful to practitioners. The broad applicability across three diverse tasks demonstrates its potential impact. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the incremental nature of the contribution relative to the MCL framework.

**Strengths:** The paper effectively demonstrates the practical utility of sMCL across multiple domains with strong empirical results. The simplicity of the method (requiring only a small modification to existing training pipelines) is a major strength. The analysis of how specialization emerges in ensemble members provides valuable insights into the method's behavior.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the disconnect between the paper's emphasis on "diversity" and the fact that the method doesn't explicitly enforce it. The paper could more honestly frame its contribution as "a simple, practical implementation of MCL for deep networks" rather than emphasizing diversity as a primary goal. The incremental nature of the contribution relative to Guzman-Rivera's work is also underplayed.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. While the contribution is incremental rather than revolutionary, it solves a practical problem in a simple, effective way with broad applicability. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a practical method for training diverse deep ensembles that was previously infeasible with existing MCL approaches. The empirical results are strong, and the method is straightforward to implement. The minor issues noted (typos, overemphasis on diversity) can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Practical solution to an important problem, simple and effective method with strong empirical results across multiple domains, well-written and clear presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, the emphasis on diversity is misleading since the method doesn't explicitly enforce it.
