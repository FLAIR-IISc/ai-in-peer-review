- Proposes sMCL: an SGD-based "winner-take-gradient" method to train deep ensembles that minimize oracle loss and produce multiple hypotheses.  
- Claims contributions: (1) stochastic algorithm for oracle loss, (2) empirical results on classification/segmentation/captioning, (3) analysis showing automatic specialization.  
- Technical approach is sound and has theoretical justification, but the central technical section (Section 3) is too brief.  
- Experimental validation is limited: baselines are weak (independent ensembles, prior MCL), gains are marginal (6–10% relative), and standard benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012) are missing.  
- Novelty is incremental: adapts existing MCL ideas to deep networks; the "stochastic" change is a minor modification rather than a substantive new theory.  
- Clarity is poor: writing is vague, distinctions from standard multi-task/ensemble methods and other diversity techniques are not clearly explained.  
- Significance is limited: claimed parameter-free/architecture-agnostic properties and interpretable ambiguity representations are not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Specialization analysis (Figure 4c) is interesting but not clearly novel beyond implications of MCL.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, simple to implement, and the winner-take-gradient idea is conceptually appealing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental contribution, insufficient technical detail, weak experimental comparisons, and poor presentation.  
- Recommendation: reject; authors should strengthen theoretical justification, evaluate stronger baselines and standard datasets, and clarify how sMCL differs from related approaches before resubmission.
