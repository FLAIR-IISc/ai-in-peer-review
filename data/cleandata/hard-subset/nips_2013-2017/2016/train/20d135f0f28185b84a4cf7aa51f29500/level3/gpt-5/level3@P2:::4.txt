Summary
This paper targets the multiple-prediction setting where a downstream “oracle” can select from M hypotheses. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a “winner-take-gradient” training scheme for deep ensembles: for each training example, only the model with the lowest loss receives gradient signal. This directly approximates minimizing the oracle loss minm ℓ(yi, fm(xi)) with an online, SGD-compatible surrogate derived from the classical MCL formulation [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS’12/13]. The method is described as architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and amenable to concurrent training. Experiments are shown on CIFAR-10 classification (small CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM), with oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr). The paper reports modest to moderate gains over independently trained ensembles and a speedup over alternating MCL, along with qualitative evidence that different ensemble members specialize (e.g., by classes or segment shapes).

Relation to prior work
The method is closest to MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS’12; AISTATS’14], but replaces costly alternating retraining with online assignments within each minibatch. The approach is also closely related to hard-EM and hard mixture-of-experts/winner-take-all training (e.g., Jacobs et al., 1991), and to diversity-seeking ensembles (negative correlation, bagging/boosting) and “M-best/diverse” methods for structured prediction [Batra et al., ECCV’12; Kirillov et al., NIPS/ICCV’15], though those are often tied to specific probabilistic models. The paper positions itself relative to MCL and sequential boosting-like MCL [Dey et al., ICCV’15], but does not clearly discuss connections/differences to classic hard-gated MoE or to multi-branch/multi-task training, where competing branches also receive selective supervision.

Strengths
- Simple, general idea that is easy to implement in standard deep learning codebases; parameter-free and architecture-agnostic.
- Clear motivation for oracle loss; useful in practice when a downstream selector exists.
- Concurrent training is a practical advantage over alternating MCL; the formulation explains the gradient flow cleanly.
- Demonstrates applicability across three tasks and model families; qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization is compelling.
- The “k-winners” ablation (varying k from 1 to M) is informative about specialization vs. oracle performance.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: sMCL is essentially an SGD-friendly, online version of existing MCL/“hard EM” with per-example hard assignment; the technical contribution is limited and Section 3 is very short.
- Missing connections: the relation to hard mixture-of-experts/winner-take-all networks and to multi-branch or multi-task training is not made explicit, leaving readers unclear about what is substantively new versus repackaging known training heuristics.
- Empirical evaluation uses weak baselines and dated architectures: CIFAR10-Quick rather than stronger CNNs; segmentation on a VOC2011 subset rather than the standard VOC2012 benchmark; captioning with neuraltalk2. Reported improvements over independent ensembles are small/modest and sometimes comparable to standard beam search from a single model (which can even yield higher oracle).
- Comparisons to stronger, contemporary baselines are missing: e.g., more competitive deep ensembles (bagging, snapshot ensembles), diversity regularizers (negative correlation/decorrelation), DPP-based selection, mixture-of-experts with hard gating, multi-hypothesis decoders, and more competitive structured prediction baselines.
- Limited analysis of stability and training dynamics: tie-breaking policy, sensitivity to batch size and learning rate, effect on per-model accuracy vs. oracle accuracy, and convergence properties are not discussed. No statistical significance testing.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and gradients are straightforward, but there is no deeper analysis (e.g., convergence behavior, degeneracies such as collapse to a single expert, or mitigation strategies). Claims about generality are plausible; claims about superiority are only weakly supported given the baselines.
- Empirical rigor: Broad but shallow. The datasets/architectures are not state-of-the-art; the gains are modest and sometimes offset by stronger baseline inference (beam search). Timings and fairness of the “5× faster” claim versus MCL depend on chosen meta-iterations; wall-clock and compute budgets should be reported.
- Reproducibility: High-level details are given, but Section 3 is terse. More thorough pseudo-code, tie-handling, initialization, and training schedules are needed.

Clarity
The paper is readable but the key technical section is short and omits important details (assignment tie-breaking, computational overhead per batch, memory footprint with many ensemble members, handling non-differentiable task losses). The distinction from alternative/multi-task/multi-branch training is not clarified. Writing could be tightened; some figures and tables are informative.

Originality
Limited. The core idea—per-example hard assignment and updating only the “winning” predictor—is long-standing (hard EM, MoE) and has been previously instantiated as MCL; the novelty here is adapting it to synchronous SGD for deep networks. This is useful but not a substantial conceptual advance.

Significance
Practical value as a simple recipe for multi-hypothesis training is plausible, but the current evidence is not strong enough to claim broad impact. Demonstrating gains on stronger benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet top-k, Pascal VOC2012/Cityscapes with modern backbones, MSCOCO captioning with stronger decoders) and against stronger baselines would materially increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines and datasets: VOC2012 for segmentation; stronger classifiers (e.g., ResNet) for classification; modern captioning setups. Include ImageNet top-k.
- Compare against: hard/soft mixture-of-experts with learned gating; negative-correlation ensembles; bagging; snapshot ensembles; DPP-based selection; multi-hypothesis decoders; broader beam widths; diverse M-best methods where applicable.
- Expand Section 3 with detailed algorithmic description, complexity, tie-breaking, stability, and ablations (batch size, k-winners, loss choices).
- Provide quantitative diversity measures beyond oracle (pairwise disagreement, IoU variance, unique n-grams, self-BLEU) and statistical tests.
- Analyze specialization more deeply (confusion matrices, per-expert calibration, effect on single-prediction accuracy).
- Clarify how sMCL differs from multi-task/multi-branch training and classic MoE; add appropriate citations.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and easy-to-use training strategy with some empirical benefits.
- Concurrent training is practical and potentially widely adoptable.
- Qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental contribution relative to MCL/hard MoE; limited novelty.
- Weak baselines and modest improvements; stronger benchmarks missing.
- Technical section is short and lacks analysis and details.

Recommendation
Lean toward reject in current form. With stronger experimental validation on standard benchmarks and clearer positioning relative to MoE/multi-branch training, this could be a solid workshop or future conference contribution.

Reviewer confidence
High.
