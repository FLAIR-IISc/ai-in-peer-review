- Introduces sMCL, a stochastic "winner-take-gradient" SGD method to train ensembles that minimize oracle loss by updating only the lowest-loss member per example.
- Makes MCL practical for deep learning by integrating assignment into SGD, avoiding costly alternating retraining.
- Method is general, parameter-free, efficient, and demonstrated on CNN classification, FCN segmentation, and CNN+RNN captioning.
- Empirical results show significant oracle-performance gains over independently trained ensembles, original MCL, and a boosting-style baseline.
- Experiments use standard datasets and contemporary architectures, providing convincing evidence of effectiveness.
- Major experimental concern: possible unfairness in segmentation experiments due to sMCL being initialized from a pre-trained ensemble while baselines may not have been.
- Paper does not report sensitivity of sMCL to initialization, leaving robustness unclear.
- Presentation is very clear, well-organized, and includes effective qualitative examples and an easy-to-understand algorithm.
- Primary novelty is an elegant, non-obvious SGD-based adaptation of MCL enabling concurrent training of deep ensembles.
- Missing citation/comparison: Lee et al. (2015) on diverse "M heads" for deep ensembles should be discussed and compared.
- Evaluation focuses on oracle metrics only; lacks direct quantitative measures of diversity among ensemble outputs.
- Significance is high: practical solution for producing multiple plausible hypotheses in ambiguous perception tasks with potential wide adoption.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/practical algorithm, strong empirical validation across tasks, high impact, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: potential experimental unfairness, limited diversity evaluation, and omission of relevant related work.
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on clarifying initialization details, evaluating sensitivity to initialization, adding diversity metrics, and discussing Lee et al. (2015).
