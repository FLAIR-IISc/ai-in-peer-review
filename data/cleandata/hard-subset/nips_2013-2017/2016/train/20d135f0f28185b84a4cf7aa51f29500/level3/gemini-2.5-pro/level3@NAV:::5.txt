This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for jointly optimizing an ensemble of deep networks. The core contribution is to directly minimize the oracle loss, encouraging the ensemble to produce a diverse set of high-quality hypotheses for a given input. This work extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, previously proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al., to the modern deep learning setting. The key technical innovation is a stochastic block gradient descent optimization, which updates only the "winning" network (the one with the lowest loss) for each example in a batch. This makes the approach efficient and compatible with standard backpropagation, avoiding the costly iterative retraining of prior MCL methods.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The problem of generating multiple plausible outputs is highly relevant for systems involving human interaction or verification, where a single, potentially incorrect prediction is less useful than a small set of strong candidates. The proposed sMCL method is elegant, simple to implement, and general-purpose, being agnostic to network architecture and loss function. The authors convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness with oracle experiments on three distinct and challenging tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—showing clear improvements over independently trained ensembles and other baselines.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its impact. A primary concern is the clarity of the experimental setup, particularly regarding initialization. In the segmentation experiments, sMCL is fine-tuned from a pre-trained model, but it is unclear if baseline methods were afforded the same initialization. This ambiguity makes it difficult to ascertain the true source of the performance gains. Furthermore, the paper's evaluation relies exclusively on oracle accuracy. While this demonstrates that at least one good solution is present, it fails to quantitatively measure the diversity of the ensemble's outputs—a key motivation for the work. Finally, the authors should consider a comparison with related work such as Lee et al.'s "Why M Heads are Better than One" (2015), which explores a similar goal of generating multiple predictions from a single network.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution. The proposed sMCL method is a practical and effective technique for training diverse deep ensembles. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by clarifying initialization procedures and including diversity metrics, the novelty and significance of the core idea are strong.
