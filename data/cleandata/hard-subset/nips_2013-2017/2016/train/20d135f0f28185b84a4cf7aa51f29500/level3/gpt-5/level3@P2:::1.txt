Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-friendly algorithm for training an ensemble of deep networks to minimize oracle loss, i.e., the loss of the best prediction among M outputs. Concretely, the ensemble members are trained jointly with a winner-take-gradient strategy: for each training example in a minibatch, the loss is backpropagated only through the member that currently has the smallest loss on that example (ties broken arbitrarily). This is a stochastic analogue of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS’12, AISTATS’14), which alternates between reassigning data to the current best model and retraining each model to convergence. By interleaving assignment and learning within SGD, sMCL avoids costly retraining, is parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and scales to modern deep nets.

The work is also related to the literature on generating multiple diverse hypotheses in structured prediction (e.g., M-Best/DivMBest and N-best decoders), and to ensemble methods that encourage diversity (resampling, negative correlation learning). Unlike those, sMCL directly optimizes oracle loss for general differentiable models and structured outputs. Conceptually, it resembles hard-EM or mixture-of-experts with implicit, non-parametric hard gating, but without a learned router; specialization emerges from the oracle objective.

Experiments on three domains—CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM)—show consistent gains in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles and over Dey et al.’s submodular/boosting approach; performance is comparable to or better than MCL while being ~5x faster. Qualitative analyses suggest interpretable specialization (class specialists in CIFAR-10, shape/class-frequency specialists in segmentation, n-gram/style specialists in captioning).

Quality:
- The method is simple and technically sound. The gradient argument is correct under the hard assignment objective; the algorithm is a natural SGD instantiation of MCL’s upper bound.
- The empirical evaluation is broad and uses appropriate oracle metrics (top-k, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr). Baselines are reasonable; training details are provided at a useful level.
- Some aspects of the evaluation could be strengthened:
  - The MCL baseline is limited by the need to retrain to convergence. A more apples-to-apples variant would implement MCL with mini-epoch or batch-level alternations (partial training between reassignment), which could narrow the gap and clarify where the gains come from.
  - Diversity is mostly inferred from specialization plots and qualitative examples. Quantitative diversity metrics would make the claims more rigorous (e.g., pairwise prediction disagreement, average pairwise KL between class distributions, coverage vs. oracle curves, n-gram diversity vs. CIDEr).
  - Stability: report variance across seeds and sensitivity to batch size, learning rate, ensemble size M, and tie-breaking strategy. Is there model starvation? How often do ties occur and how are they resolved?
  - Computational aspects: beyond a 5x wall-clock speedup vs. 5-iteration MCL, a breakdown of time/epoch, memory overhead (especially for shared CNN in captioning), and scaling with M would be useful.
  - For captioning, disentangle effects of beam search: compare beam search over a single model to sMCL with the same total number of decoded hypotheses; explore combining sMCL with beam search.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow. The problem setting, algorithm, and experiments are described concisely. Pseudocode for sMCL is helpful.
- Suggested clarifications:
  - Specify tie-breaking and any balancing heuristics to avoid starvation.
  - Provide more detail on the captioning “tree” structure with shared CNN, and confirm whether gradients from all LSTMs are summed at the shared CNN.
  - For the k-winners ablation, include complete results (accuracy/diversity vs. k) and a short discussion.

Originality:
- The core idea—SGD with winner-take-all gradients for MCL—is incremental relative to Guzman-Rivera et al. Nonetheless, adapting MCL to a practical, concurrent, parameter-free SGD routine for deep nets is non-trivial and, to my knowledge, new. The paper clearly positions itself against prior MCL, DivMBest/N-best work, and ensemble-diversity methods.

Significance:
- Practical significance is high: many systems benefit from multiple plausible hypotheses, and sMCL provides a simple, general recipe that improves oracle performance and induces specialization across diverse tasks.
- The work should be of interest to practitioners and researchers building structured-output deep models and ensembles.

Pros:
- Simple, general, parameter-free algorithm easily integrated into SGD training.
- Broad experimental coverage (classification, segmentation, captioning) with consistent gains in oracle metrics.
- Clear qualitative evidence of emergent specialization; insightful analysis across tasks.
- Significant computational advantage over classic MCL; concurrent training fits modern deep learning workflows.

Cons:
- Algorithmic novelty is modest; conceptually close to hard-EM/MCL with stochastic updates.
- MCL comparison could be fairer with batch/mini-epoch alternation; current gap may mix algorithmic and computational factors.
- Limited quantitative analysis of diversity and stability; potential model starvation and tie-breaking effects not systematically studied.
- Missing some baselines that could be adapted to deep nets (e.g., negative correlation ensembles; Monte Carlo dropout for multiple hypotheses).

Requests and suggestions:
- Add a batch-wise or mini-epoch MCL baseline (reassign examples after a fixed number of SGD steps rather than full convergence) to isolate the benefit of interleaving assignment and learning.
- Provide quantitative diversity measures and coverage–performance trade-offs, and report variance across seeds.
- Analyze assignment dynamics over training (e.g., entropy of assignment per sample/class, frequency of reassignments).
- Discuss and, if possible, mitigate starvation (e.g., random tie-breaking, occasional exploration assignments).
- Consider an additional baseline that samples multiple outputs from a single model (e.g., MC dropout) and reports oracle metrics.
- Report wall-clock time, memory usage, and scaling with ensemble size M.

Overall recommendation:
I lean toward acceptance. While the algorithmic step from MCL to sMCL is incremental, it materially improves practicality and scalability for deep networks, and the empirical results are compelling across multiple domains. Strengthening the MCL comparison and diversity analysis would further solidify the contribution.
