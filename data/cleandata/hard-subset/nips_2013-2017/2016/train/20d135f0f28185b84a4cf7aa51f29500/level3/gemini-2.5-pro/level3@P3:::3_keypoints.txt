- Paper proposes sMCL, a practical SGD-compatible adaptation of Multiple Choice Learning that routes gradients to the single best learner per example ("winner-takes-the-gradient").
- sMCL enables concurrent training of ensemble members without iterative retraining required by prior MCL block coordinate descent.
- Empirical evaluation on classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning shows significant oracle-performance gains over independent ensembles and other baselines.
- Method is simple and easy to implement (add a gradient-routing layer), making it practical for large-scale deep networks.
- Thorough experiments across architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) and qualitative analyses demonstrate learners specialize on different data subsets.
- Strength: addresses an important, relevant problem—producing multiple plausible hypotheses for downstream decision-making.
- Weakness: paper heavily frames the work as promoting “diversity,” but the objective (oracle loss) contains no explicit diversity term; diversity is only emergent.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental—core idea of assigning examples to a single best learner follows prior MCL; main advance is adaptation to SGD.
- Question: unclear how the original MCL baseline [8] was implemented—did meta-iterations fully converge, and what was the training budget per iteration?
- Suggestion: clarify/justify convergence criteria and training budget for the MCL baseline to ensure a fair comparison.
- Suggestion: reword the first sentence of the abstract to be more direct and concise.
- Suggestion: tone down or clarify emphasis on “diversity” in title/text, noting it is an emergent property rather than an explicit optimization target.
- Minor corrections: Line 108 likely has a typo (first f should be g); Line 115 contains an extra period.  
- Overall assessment: well-written, simple, effective, and broadly applicable work whose practical contributions and empirical validation outweigh concerns about incremental novelty and framing.
