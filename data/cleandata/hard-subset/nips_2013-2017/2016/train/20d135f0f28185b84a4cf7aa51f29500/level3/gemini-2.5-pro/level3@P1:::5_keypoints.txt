- Paper tackles generating diverse, high-quality predictions from ensembles by minimizing oracle loss (best model per input).
- Main contribution: Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) with a “winner-take-gradient” rule during SGD.
- sMCL enables efficient end-to-end parallel training of deep ensemble members, avoiding costly retraining of prior MCL methods.
- Demonstrated on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning using CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures.
- Empirical results show sMCL consistently outperforms independently trained ensembles and related baselines in oracle performance.
- Method is technically sound and well-motivated as a stochastic block gradient descent minimizing oracle loss.
- sMCL yields a reported ~5x speedup over original MCL formulation.
- Key experimental concern: unclear initialization details—sMCL segmentation models were initialized from a pretrained ensemble, and it’s unclear if baselines had the same initialization.
- Fairness of comparisons may be compromised if baselines did not share the same pre-training; clarification needed across experiments.
- Evaluation focuses solely on oracle metrics and qualitative examples, lacking quantitative measures of output diversity.
- Paper should include diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise distances, Jaccard) to support claims of producing genuinely distinct hypotheses.
- Writing, organization, and presentation are excellent; algorithm description and figures are clear and implementable.
- Contribution is novel in making MCL practical for large deep networks via a simple backprop modification.
- Suggested related work to discuss: Lee et al., “Why M Heads are Better than One” (ICML 2015) for joint multi-head training context.
- High practical significance: general, parameter-free, architecture-agnostic method likely to be widely adopted in applications needing multiple hypotheses.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/practical algorithm, strong empirical validation across tasks, broad applicability, and excellent clarity.
- Arguments for rejection: unclear experimental initialization details, incomplete evaluation of diversity, and a minor omission in related work discussion.
