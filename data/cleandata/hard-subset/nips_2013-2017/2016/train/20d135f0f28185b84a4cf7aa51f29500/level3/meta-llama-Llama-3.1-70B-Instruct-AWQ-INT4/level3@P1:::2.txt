The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel approach to training deep ensembles by utilizing an oracle to select the optimal member for each instance and backpropagating only on that network. This approach encourages specialization among ensemble members, resulting in ensembles that cover diverse outputs.

The authors provide a thorough and well-presented experimental evaluation of their approach, demonstrating strong empirical results across multiple domains, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The proposed method, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), is shown to outperform existing baselines, including classical ensembles and other strong methods such as Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and the approach of Dey et al.

One of the key strengths of the paper is its ability to provide interpretable expertise among ensemble members. The authors demonstrate that sMCL can induce label-space clustering, where different ensemble members specialize in different classes. This is particularly evident in the image classification experiments, where the authors show that the class-wise distribution of the assignment of test datapoints to the oracle or 'winning' predictor is striking, with most predictors becoming specialists for certain classes.

The paper also explores the emergence of different task-specific specializations among ensemble members. For example, in the semantic segmentation experiments, the authors show that specialization can occur in terms of shape and frequency of predicted segments, in addition to class divisions. Similarly, in the image captioning experiments, the authors demonstrate that sMCL can produce diverse sentences that capture ambiguity both in language and perception.

However, the paper has a major omission, as it does not address how to exploit or use the trained ensemble at inference without the oracle. The authors do not provide any strategies for selecting the optimal member for each instance at test time, which is a critical component of any ensemble method. This limitation makes the practical usability of the approach limited, as exploitation strategies are problem-specific and not explored.

While it has some limitations, the paper is well-written, thorough, and provides strong empirical results. With some additional work on addressing the exploitation of the trained ensemble at inference, this approach has the potential to be widely adopted in practice.
