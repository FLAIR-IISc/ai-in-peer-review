This paper proposes a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The main idea is to use an oracle to select the optimal member of the ensemble for each instance and backpropagate only on that network. This approach encourages specialization among the ensemble members, yielding ensembles that cover diverse outputs. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across multiple domains, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, and provide strong empirical results.

The paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough and well-presented. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed approach and its advantages over existing methods. The use of oracle loss as a metric to evaluate the performance of the ensemble is also a good choice, as it allows for a fair comparison with other methods.

The proposed approach is related to previous work on ensemble learning and multiple choice learning. However, the authors provide a clear distinction between their approach and existing methods, highlighting the advantages of sMCL. The use of stochastic gradient descent to train the ensemble members concurrently is also a key contribution of the paper.

One of the major strengths of the paper is its ability to provide interpretable results. The authors show that the ensemble members specialize in different aspects of the task, such as label-space clustering in image classification and segmentation specialists in semantic segmentation. This is a significant advantage over existing methods, as it allows for a better understanding of how the ensemble is making predictions.

However, there are a few limitations to the paper. One major omission is the lack of discussion on how to exploit or use the trained ensemble at inference without the oracle. The authors do not provide any guidance on how to select the optimal member of the ensemble for a given instance, which is a critical step in deploying the model in a real-world setting. This limitation reduces the practical usability of the approach, as exploitation strategies are problem-specific and not explored.

Another limitation is the presentation issue with Figure 2, which is embedded as a raster image instead of real text or a vector graphic. This makes it difficult to read and understand the pseudocode.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. The paper is also well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks. The use of oracle loss as a metric to evaluate the performance of the ensemble is also a new contribution. The paper is also significant, as it provides a new way of thinking about ensemble learning and multiple choice learning.

The paper is relevant to the NIPS conference, as it addresses a key problem in machine learning, namely, how to train ensembles of deep networks to make accurate predictions. The approach proposed in the paper has the potential to be widely applicable, and the results are likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
