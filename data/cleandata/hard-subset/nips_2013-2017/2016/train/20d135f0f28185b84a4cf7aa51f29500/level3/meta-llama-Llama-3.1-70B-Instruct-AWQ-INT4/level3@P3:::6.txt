The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent-based approach to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle in the context of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The authors introduce a new training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The main idea of the paper is to train an ensemble of deep networks that produce a set of solutions with minimal loss with respect to an oracle that selects only the lowest-error prediction. The authors argue that this approach is particularly useful in situations where there is ambiguity in the data, and a single prediction may not be sufficient.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, including ensemble learning and generating multiple solutions. They also provide a detailed analysis of their proposed method, including its advantages and limitations.

The experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles. The authors show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure. They also provide qualitative results that demonstrate the diversity of the solutions produced by sMCL.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a simple and efficient solution to the problem of training diverse deep ensembles. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be applied to a wide range of tasks and deep architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the lack of a clear explanation of the testing phase. The authors mention that they use ground truth to select the best member, but it is not clear how this is done in practice. Additionally, the terms "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" are not well-defined, and it is not clear how they differ from the proposed sMCL approach.

Another limitation of the paper is the lack of a clear justification for the claim of member specialization. The authors argue that the training reassignments occur on a per-example basis, but it is not clear how this leads to specialization. The reviewer suggests that specialization could be explicitly incorporated into the loss function, but this is not explored in the paper.

Finally, the paper does not discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, which is a closely related area of research. The reviewer suggests that this literature is more rigorous and under-studied in deep settings.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. However, there are some limitations that need to be addressed in future work.
