The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks that minimizes the oracle loss directly. The authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) that trains ensemble members concurrently and encourages specialization among them. This approach is applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent and is agnostic to the form of the task-dependent loss.

The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The results show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide interpretable emergent expertise among ensemble members. The authors show that sMCL induces label-space clustering, where each predictor becomes a specialist for certain classes. This is particularly useful in tasks with multiple reasonable interpretations, where the goal is to produce multiple highly likely hypotheses rather than a single prediction.

However, one major omission in the paper is the lack of discussion on how to exploit or use the trained ensemble at inference without the oracle. The authors do not provide any guidance on how to select the optimal member of the ensemble for a given input, which is a crucial step in deploying the model in practice. This limitation reduces the practical usability of the approach.

Additionally, the presentation of the paper could be improved. For example, Figure 2 is embedded as a raster image instead of real text or a vector graphic, which makes it difficult to read.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

The clarity of the paper is good, and the writing is well-organized. However, some sections, such as the related work section, could be improved for better readability.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions and adequately reference related work.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results have the potential to impact the field of deep learning and ensemble methods, and the approach could be useful for a wide range of applications.

In conclusion, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning and ensemble methods. While it has some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and it is a good candidate for acceptance.
