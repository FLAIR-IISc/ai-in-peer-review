This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel algorithm for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The goal is to minimize the "oracle loss," which is the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble for any given input. This is particularly useful for ambiguous tasks where multiple plausible solutions exist. The core idea of sMCL is a simple "winner-take-gradient" strategy within a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) framework: for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is only backpropagated through the single member that achieved the lowest loss.

This work builds directly upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2012), which used a block coordinate descent algorithm to minimize the same oracle loss objective. However, the original MCL required iteratively training models to convergence, making it infeasible for modern deep networks. The main contribution of this paper is the formulation of a stochastic, concurrent training algorithm that makes the MCL objective practical for deep learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct and challenging tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—showing significant improvements in oracle performance over independently trained ensembles and other baselines. A key finding is that the method encourages the emergence of interpretable, task-specific specialization among the ensemble members without any explicit supervision.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The evaluation across three different domains (classification, segmentation, captioning) with corresponding state-of-the-art deep architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) convincingly demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed method. The quantitative results clearly show that sMCL outperforms baselines in terms of oracle metrics, and the qualitative examples provide compelling evidence of the increased diversity and quality of the generated outputs. The analysis of how specialization emerges in different tasks (e.g., class-based for classification vs. n-gram-based for captioning) is insightful.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated with illustrative examples in the introduction. The proposed algorithm is explained simply and effectively, and its connection to and improvement over prior work (MCL) is made explicit. The experimental setup and results are presented with clarity, and the figures are informative and easy to interpret.

**Originality:** The core idea of minimizing an oracle loss for an ensemble is not new and comes from Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The primary novelty of this work lies in adapting this idea into a practical and scalable SGD-based algorithm for deep networks. While one might argue that moving from block coordinate descent to a stochastic variant is an incremental step, its impact is substantial. This "small" change is precisely what enables the application of the MCL principle to the large-scale models that dominate modern machine learning, which is a significant and valuable contribution. It is the first work to provide a practical method for training diverse deep ensembles in this manner.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. As deep learning models are deployed in complex, real-world systems, the ability to generate a set of diverse and plausible hypotheses instead of a single, potentially overconfident prediction is crucial. This paper provides a simple, general, parameter-free, and effective method to achieve this. Given its ease of implementation (essentially a custom backpropagation rule), the method is likely to be adopted by other researchers and practitioners working on problems with inherent ambiguity. It demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in training diverse ensembles.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Practical Impact:** The paper successfully makes the powerful but previously impractical Multiple Choice Learning framework applicable to deep learning, which is a significant practical achievement.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to be effective across a wide range of tasks and architectures, consistently outperforming strong baselines.
3.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The sMCL algorithm is simple, elegant, and can be applied to any architecture trained with SGD without requiring any changes to the model or loss function.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making the ideas and results easy to understand and appreciate.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The main criticism is that the algorithmic contribution could be viewed as an incremental improvement over Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], as it primarily translates a known objective into a standard SGD setting.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:

Overall, I am convinced that the practical contribution and strong empirical results outweigh the concern about incremental novelty, and I lean strongly towards acceptance. The authors have done an excellent job of demonstrating the utility of their approach. I have a few suggestions that could further strengthen the paper:

1.  **Ablation of the Optimization Strategy:** The comparison to the original MCL [8] is slightly unfair, as MCL involves full retraining cycles. A more direct and insightful baseline would be a mini-batch version of the original hard-EM style algorithm. That is, for each mini-batch, (1) perform a forward pass and assign each example to its "winning" model, and then (2) perform a gradient update for each model *only* on the subset of examples assigned to it. This would help disentangle the benefits of concurrent training from the specific "winner-take-gradient" rule of sMCL.

2.  **Deeper Analysis of Diversity:** The paper excellently demonstrates that specialization and diversity emerge as a result of the training procedure. It would be beneficial to quantify this diversity more directly, beyond the oracle performance metrics. For example, for segmentation, one could measure the average IoU *between* the M predictions for a given input. For captioning, one could report metrics on the dissimilarity of the generated sentences (e.g., BLEU score of one caption against the others). This would provide a more direct measure of the diversity induced by sMCL compared to the baselines.
