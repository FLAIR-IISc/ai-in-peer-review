This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles. The core idea is to frame ensemble learning as a multiple-choice problem where an ‘oracle’ selects the best output from the ensemble for each input. Instead of jointly optimizing all ensemble members, sMCL leverages stochastic gradient descent to backpropagate gradients only through the best-performing member, effectively encouraging specialization. The method is presented as parameter-free, broadly applicable, and computationally efficient compared to previous approaches like MCL [8, 9] which require iterative retraining.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, consistently achieving superior performance compared to independent ensembles and other diversity-inducing techniques.  The paper is well-structured, providing a clear introduction to the problem, a detailed explanation of the proposed algorithm, and comprehensive experimental results.

The work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, directly addressing the limitations of earlier approaches, particularly the high computational cost associated with retraining models multiple times.  While ensemble learning is a well-established field [19, 26, 27], sMCL distinguishes itself by directly optimizing for the oracle loss in a scalable manner.  Existing methods for generating diverse solutions from single models [3, 15, 16, 23, 24] are often not applicable to general deep architectures, whereas sMCL is explicitly designed for this purpose. The authors appropriately reference related work, including recent advancements in ensemble diversity and oracle-based learning [2, 5, 12].  The paper also correctly points out the mode-seeking behavior of many models and how producing multiple predictions can address this issue, aligning with ongoing discussions in the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of MCL as a stochastic gradient descent problem for ensembles is a significant contribution. It overcomes the computational bottleneck of previous approaches like alternating block coordinate descent.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements across diverse tasks and architectures. The authors thoroughly evaluate their method against strong baselines and provide detailed analysis of the results.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the sMCL algorithm and the explanation of how it encourages specialization are clear and logically sound.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as agnostic to both architecture and loss function, enhancing its potential for widespread adoption.
* **Interpretability:** The observed specialization of ensemble members adds a layer of interpretability to the learning process, allowing insights into how the ensemble collectively addresses task ambiguity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Inference Strategy:** A critical omission is a discussion of how to effectively *use* the trained ensemble at inference time without access to the oracle. While the paper excels at training diverse ensembles, it doesn't address the practical challenge of combining the outputs of these specialized networks in a real-world scenario. Exploiting this diversity for improved performance requires problem-specific downstream processing, which is not explored.
* **Limited Practical Usability:** The reliance on a downstream oracle hinders the immediate practical applicability of sMCL.  While the concept is valuable, translating it into a usable system requires additional engineering effort to design appropriate exploitation strategies.
* **Presentation Issue:**  Figure 2, presenting the sMCL algorithm, is included as a raster image. This reduces readability and prevents easy copy-pasting of the algorithm for implementation. Converting this to vector graphics or actual text would significantly improve the paper's usability.
* **Oracle-Dependent Performance:** While showing improvement over baselines *given* an oracle, the paper doesn't rigorously explore the sensitivity of sMCL’s performance to the quality or cost of the oracle itself. A discussion of these trade-offs would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across multiple tasks.
* The method addresses a significant challenge in practical perception systems where multiple hypotheses are often required.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of discussion regarding inference strategies limits the immediate practical usability of the method.
* The reliance on an oracle introduces a potential bottleneck in real-world applications.
* The presentation issue with Figure 2 detracts from the paper's overall quality.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations concerning inference and practical usability, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The authors have identified an important problem and developed a promising solution. I recommend **acceptance**, with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the inference strategy issue in future work and to revise Figure 2 for improved readability. Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
