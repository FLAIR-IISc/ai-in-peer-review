This paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which minimizes oracle loss directly. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on three tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages over existing approaches.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors propose a novel and effective approach to training diverse deep ensembles, which is simple to implement and agnostic to both architecture and loss function.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.
* The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures.
* The paper includes a thorough comparison with existing baselines, including classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s approach.

However, there are also some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The authors do not provide a clear analysis of the importance of initialization in their approach. It is unclear whether the use of pre-trained initialization is necessary or whether random initialization would be sufficient.
* The experimental comparison with baselines is not entirely clear, as it is unclear whether the baselines used the same pre-trained initialization as sMCL.
* The paper only evaluates the performance of the ensembles using oracle accuracy, which may not be sufficient to capture the full range of benefits of the approach.
* The authors do not provide a comparison with related work, such as "Why M Heads are Better than One" (Lee et al., 2015), which may be relevant to their approach.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the authors' approach and its advantages. However, there are some areas for improvement, including providing a more thorough analysis of the importance of initialization and comparing the approach to related work.
