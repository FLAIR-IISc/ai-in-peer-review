This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple winner-take-gradient training scheme for deep ensembles: for each example, only the member with lowest loss w.r.t. the ground truth receives a gradient update. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all members jointly via SGD. Relative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) work (Guzman-Rivera et al.), which alternates assignment and full retraining, sMCL interleaves assignment with mini-batch updates, yielding substantial speedups and strong gains in oracle metrics on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and COCO captioning. The paper also argues that “specialization” emerges across members.

Quality: The core idea is technically sound and well-motivated; the empirical results are strong and broad. However, the testing phase is unclear: line 159 suggests the oracle (i.e., ground truth) is used to pick the best member at test time. If the claim is merely that sMCL produces M hypotheses and downstream components will select, the paper should explicitly state this and also report a non-oracle evaluation with a learned selector (e.g., a light gating/re-ranking module trained on validation data). The “specialization” claim is suggestive but not fully justified: per-example reassignment does not guarantee stable partitions. Please add quantitative evidence (e.g., mutual information between member ID and class, per-sample assignment entropy, stability across random seeds) and ablate the k-winner variant systematically. Clarity issues include the use of “independent/regular ensembles (Indp.)” in figures without an early, precise definition (line 181), and missing details on tie-breaking, inference cost vs M, and whether batch-norm statistics are per-member.

Originality: Conceptually close to hard-EM, k-means-style assignment, and mixture-of-experts with hard gating, but the adaptation to concurrent SGD for deep ensembles is a neat, practical contribution. The paper overlooks closely related diversity-regularization literature for neural ensembles (e.g., negative correlation learning [Liu & Yao, 1999] and work by Chen et al. on diversity regularization), as well as DPP-based diversity; a discussion and comparisons where feasible would strengthen positioning.

Significance: If paired with a deployable selector, sMCL could be impactful for multi-hypothesis perception. The oracle-only evaluation limits external validity; adding downstream results would increase confidence.

Pros:
- Simple, general algorithm; strong empirical gains and 5x speedup over MCL.
- Broad applicability across tasks/architectures.

Cons:
- Unclear test-time protocol; oracle-only evaluation.
- Insufficient justification/metrics for “specialization.”
- Missing related work on diversity regularization; unclear baseline definitions.

Recommendation: Weak accept. Constructive revisions addressing evaluation, clarity, and related work would make this a solid contribution.
