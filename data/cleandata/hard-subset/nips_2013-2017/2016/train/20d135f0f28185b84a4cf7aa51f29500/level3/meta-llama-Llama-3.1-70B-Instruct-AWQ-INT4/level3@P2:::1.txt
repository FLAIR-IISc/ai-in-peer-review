The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent based approach to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle in the context of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The authors aim to produce multiple highly likely hypotheses rather than a single prediction, which is beneficial in practical perception systems that include interactions with users or additional components capable of evaluating the quality of predicted solutions.

The main idea of the paper is to build on the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which introduced the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm, but with a key modification that enables the method to scale to deep neural networks. The authors propose using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to minimize the oracle loss, which is equal to the best single-classifier output. This approach encourages diversity among the ensemble members and allows the method to be applied to a wide range of tasks and deep architectures.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, related work, and technical contributions. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance on various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The results show that the proposed method, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), outperforms existing baselines and produces sets of outputs with high oracle performance.

The main technical contribution of the paper is the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach that is well-suited to minimizing the oracle loss in ensembles of deep networks. The authors demonstrate that sMCL is generalizable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent and is thus applicable to an extensive range of modern deep networks. Unlike the iterative training schedule of MCL, sMCL ensembles need only be trained to convergence once in parallel.

The paper has several strengths, including its clarity, organization, and thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, including MCL and classical ensembles. The results demonstrate that sMCL is a significant improvement over existing methods, particularly in terms of its ability to scale to deep neural networks.

However, one potential concern is that the algorithmic novelty of the paper may be only incremental compared to [8]. The reviewer is convinced that the small change makes the algorithm more useful, but it is worth considering whether the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance.

To further strengthen the paper, it would be useful to compare the performance of sMCL with the MCL algorithm when using batches (rather than the entire dataset). This would provide a more direct comparison between the two methods and help to clarify the benefits of using SGD.

Additionally, the reviewer requests more analysis of the algorithm's "diversity" effect. While the paper provides some qualitative examples of the diverse solutions produced by sMCL, it would be useful to provide more quantitative analysis of the diversity of the ensemble members. This could include metrics such as the pairwise distance between ensemble members or the entropy of the ensemble's output distribution.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on existing work in the field of Multiple Choice Learning, but the use of SGD to minimize the oracle loss is a novel contribution. The paper is well-positioned in the context of recent work on deep learning and ensemble methods.

Overall, the paper is well-written, clearly explains the technical contributions, and provides a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance. While there are some potential concerns regarding the algorithmic novelty and diversity effect, the reviewer is convinced that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and favors acceptance.
