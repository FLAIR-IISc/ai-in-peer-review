

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing the oracle loss (the lowest loss among ensemble members for a given input). The authors formulate the problem as a stochastic gradient descent optimization where gradients are backpropagated only to the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each example. This approach is simple to implement, agnostic to architecture and loss function, and avoids the costly retraining required by previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods [8,9]. The paper demonstrates sMCL on three tasks: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements over baselines including classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s method [5].

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method with clear theoretical motivation. The stochastic block gradient descent formulation (Algorithm 2) is elegant and well-justified. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive across multiple tasks and architectures, demonstrating consistent improvements in oracle accuracy (6-10% over baselines). However, the paper lacks analysis of how initialization affects performance - it's unclear whether the baselines used the same initialization strategy as sMCL. This is particularly important as the authors note that sMCL can induce "interpretable expertise" through specialization, which may be initialization-dependent. Additionally, the evaluation focuses solely on oracle accuracy without sufficient analysis of ensemble diversity, which is the core motivation of the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The problem formulation is precise, and the method is explained with sufficient mathematical detail. The experimental setup is well-described, though the lack of diversity metrics makes it difficult to fully assess the claimed "diverse solutions." The authors could improve clarity by providing more concrete examples of how the ensembles specialize (beyond the qualitative examples in Figure 6) and by explicitly comparing with Lee et al.'s "Why M Heads are Better than One" [1], which also explores multiple heads for improved performance.

**Originality:** The paper builds on the Multiple Choice Learning framework [8,9] but makes a significant contribution by introducing a practical stochastic optimization approach suitable for deep networks. This avoids the retraining bottleneck of prior methods. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing work on multiple outputs, particularly Lee et al. [1] which explores multiple heads for improved performance in classification. The authors should clarify how sMCL differs from and improves upon this prior work.

**Significance:** The problem of generating diverse outputs is highly relevant for practical applications where downstream systems benefit from multiple plausible hypotheses. The paper's method is simple to implement and broadly applicable, making it valuable for practitioners. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of diversity analysis - without demonstrating that the ensembles produce genuinely diverse outputs (beyond just lower oracle loss), it's unclear whether the method truly addresses the stated motivation of capturing ambiguity. The paper would be stronger if it showed how the ensembles' diversity leads to improved downstream performance in real applications.

**Strengths:**
- Novel stochastic optimization approach for MCL that avoids retraining
- Simple to implement within existing deep learning frameworks
- Demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple tasks and architectures
- Provides qualitative evidence of ensemble specialization

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks analysis of initialization effects on performance
- Evaluation focuses only on oracle accuracy without diversity metrics
- Insufficient comparison with relevant prior work (e.g., Lee et al. [1])
- Fails to provide concrete evidence that ensembles produce genuinely diverse outputs

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Solves a practical problem in real-world AI systems
- Method is simple, general, and applicable to various architectures
- Strong empirical results across multiple tasks
- Clear and well-written paper

*Con:*
- Critical missing analysis of ensemble diversity
- Incomplete comparison with relevant prior work
- Unclear whether results depend on initialization
- Limited demonstration of how diversity translates to practical benefits

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid technical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem with a simple, practical solution. While the method shows clear empirical advantages over baselines, the paper would benefit significantly from additional analysis of ensemble diversity and a more thorough comparison with related work. The significance would be stronger if the authors could demonstrate how the diversity of outputs leads to tangible benefits in downstream applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the diversity analysis and clarify the relationship with prior work on multiple outputs. The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field if these aspects are strengthened.
