

# Review for "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by updating only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each training example (the "winner-take-gradient" strategy). The authors frame this as a generalization of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8,9] to deep networks, presenting a simple, parameter-free training algorithm that avoids the costly retraining required by prior MCL methods. The work is positioned as the first practical approach for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss.

**Quality**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with strong empirical validation across multiple domains (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning). The sMCL algorithm is well-motivated and effectively implements a stochastic approximation of the oracle loss minimization problem. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate sMCL's superiority over baselines (classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.), with consistent improvements of 6-10% in oracle metrics. However, the paper's quality is somewhat undermined by two key issues: (1) the unclear testing procedure that appears to use ground truth to select the best ensemble member (line 159, p.5), which would invalidate the claimed oracle performance; and (2) the insufficient justification for the claimed "specialization" of ensemble members, as the per-example reassignment strategy could produce similar results without true specialization. The authors should clarify the testing procedure and provide evidence that specialization emerges beyond simple per-example assignment.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts. However, several critical terms and baselines lack proper explanation: "independent ensembles" (Indp.) and "regular ensembles" appear in the text and figures (line 181, p.5) without definition, which confuses readers unfamiliar with the authors' terminology. The explanation of how sMCL induces specialization (Section 4.1) would benefit from more precise analysis of the specialization mechanism beyond class-wise assignment patterns. The paper could also better clarify the relationship between k in the relaxed oracle loss (Figures 4d-4e) and the final specialization observed.

**Originality**
The paper presents a novel and practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles, which is a significant contribution given the limitations of prior MCL methods. The core idea of winner-take-gradient training is simple yet effective, and the authors are correct that this is the first method to generalize MCL to deep networks without costly retraining. However, the paper misses an opportunity to position this work more precisely within the broader literature on diversity in ensemble learning. The authors should discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.'s "Ensemble Learning via Negative Correlation" [19], which explicitly incorporates diversity into the loss function), which is closely related and more rigorously studied in deep learning contexts despite being underexplored in the deep setting.

**Significance**
The problem addressed—producing diverse, high-quality multiple predictions—is highly relevant to practical AI systems where ambiguity is common. The paper's demonstration that sMCL produces interpretable specialized behavior (e.g., segmentation specialists for cows, tables, and sofas) provides concrete evidence of the method's practical value. The broad applicability across multiple architectures and tasks (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNN) further enhances the significance. The method's simplicity, parameter-free nature, and speed (5x faster than MCL) make it particularly appealing for practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
- Novel, simple, and practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles
- Strong empirical results across multiple tasks with consistent improvements over baselines
- Demonstrates interpretable specialization that aligns with human-like handling of ambiguity
- Practical advantages (parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, 5x faster than MCL)

**Arguments against Acceptance**
- Unclear testing procedure that may invalidate claimed oracle performance
- Insufficient justification for claimed "specialization" beyond per-example reassignment
- Missing discussion of related diversity regularization literature (e.g., negative correlation)
- Key terms (independent/regular ensembles) lack proper definition

**Recommendation**
This paper makes a solid technical contribution to the problem of training diverse deep ensembles with practical implications. The core idea is novel, well-executed, and supported by strong empirical results. The main concerns about testing procedure clarity and insufficient justification of specialization are serious but fixable with minor revisions. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the testing procedure, a more rigorous analysis of the specialization mechanism, and expanded discussion of related diversity work. Given the paper's significance and the straightforward nature of the required revisions, I recommend weak acceptance. The authors should address these concerns in the camera-ready version to strengthen the paper's contribution and clarity.
