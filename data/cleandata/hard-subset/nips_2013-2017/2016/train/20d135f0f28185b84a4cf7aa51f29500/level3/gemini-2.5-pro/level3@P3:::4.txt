This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to optimize the "oracle loss," where the total loss for a given input is the loss of the single best-performing model in the ensemble. To achieve this, sMCL employs a "winner-take-gradient" strategy during SGD: for each training example in a batch, the loss is calculated for all models, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the model that achieved the minimum loss. This work builds directly on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], adapting its alternating optimization scheme into a stochastic, parallel training procedure suitable for deep networks. The authors demonstrate the method's versatility on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing that it outperforms independently trained ensembles and the original, more costly MCL approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for multiple, diverse predictions in practical perception systems. The concept of optimizing for an oracle that can select the best hypothesis from a set is compelling and relevant for many applications involving human-in-the-loop systems or downstream verification modules.
2.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed sMCL method is simple to understand and implement. By turning the alternating block coordinate descent of the original MCL into a stochastic gradient selection rule, it allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently. This is a significant practical improvement over methods requiring sequential training or full retraining of models [8, 5], making the approach feasible for large-scale deep learning.
3.  **Generality:** The approach is agnostic to the specific network architecture and loss function, as demonstrated by its application to CNNs for classification, FCNs for segmentation, and CNN+RNN models for captioning. This flexibility is a notable strength.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The primary weakness is the limited conceptual novelty. The paper is best described as a straightforward adaptation of the existing MCL framework [8] to learners trained with SGD. While the motivation for MCL is well-argued, this is not a new contribution of the present work. The core technical idea—identifying the "winner" for each example and only updating its parameters—is a natural translation of the assignment step in [8] into a stochastic context.
2.  **Clarity and Positioning:** The paper's writing and positioning could be significantly improved.
    *   The technical description in Section 3 is overly brief. At just one page, it does not provide a rigorous justification for how the stochastic update rule (Algorithm 2) relates to the joint objective function (Eq. 1). A more detailed derivation or discussion would strengthen the paper's technical foundation.
    *   The paper fails to discuss the relationship between sMCL and other widely used training schemes. For example, how does this method differ from alternative training strategies where different models or network heads are trained on different examples within a mini-batch? The "winner-take-gradient" idea bears resemblance to techniques used in multi-task learning or mixture-of-experts models, yet this connection is never explored. This omission makes it difficult to situate the contribution within the broader literature.
3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments cover a commendable range of tasks, their execution and the strength of the results are questionable.
    *   **Weak Baselines:** The main baseline is an ensemble of independently trained models ("Indp."), which is a standard but often weak benchmark for methods designed to explicitly encourage diversity.
    *   **Modest Improvements:** The performance gains over the baselines are consistent but small. For instance, the oracle accuracy on CIFAR-10 improves by only a few percentage points, and the relative improvement in IoU for segmentation is approximately 7%. While positive, these gains are not substantial enough to claim a major advance.
    *   **Outdated Benchmarks:** The chosen experimental settings are not state-of-the-art, which makes it hard to assess the method's true impact. The segmentation experiments are performed on the PASCAL VOC 2011 validation set, whereas the standard benchmark for this task has long been the PASCAL VOC 2012 test set. Demonstrating effectiveness on a more challenging and current benchmark is crucial for validation. Similarly, the CIFAR-10 experiment uses a small "Quick" network, which may not be representative of behavior on more complex, large-scale classification problems.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Elaborate on the technical details in Section 3, providing a clearer link between the objective function and the proposed stochastic optimization algorithm.
*   Add a discussion that clearly distinguishes sMCL from related concepts like alternative training, hard-attention mechanisms in multi-task learning, and mixture-of-experts models. This would help clarify the paper's unique contribution.
*   To more convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness, I strongly recommend evaluating the semantic segmentation task on the standard PASCAL VOC 2012 test benchmark. This would allow for a more direct comparison with the state of the art.
*   Consider comparing against stronger, more contemporary ensemble methods that also aim to improve diversity, beyond just independent training and the direct predecessors [5, 8].

In summary, the paper presents a simple and efficient method for training diverse deep ensembles. However, its contribution is largely an engineering adaptation of prior work, and it is let down by a brief technical section, unclear positioning relative to similar ideas, and experimental results on non-standard benchmarks that show only modest improvements. While the idea is sound and practical, the paper in its current form does not feel like a complete and significant contribution for NIPS.
