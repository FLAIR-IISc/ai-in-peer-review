

This paper proposes "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning" (sMCL), a simple yet effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles. The core idea is to update only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each training example, which the authors claim leads to automatic specialization of members across different classes or tasks. The method is parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and shows strong empirical results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, outperforming baselines like independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. in oracle performance.

The paper's main strength lies in its simplicity and strong experimental validation. The sMCL approach is easily implementable and demonstrates significant improvements in oracle metrics across diverse tasks, which is a meaningful contribution to applications requiring multiple plausible predictions. The motivation for producing diverse outputs to handle ambiguity is well-justified with practical examples.

However, several concerns limit the paper's quality and clarity. First, the testing procedure is unclear: the authors appear to use ground truth to select the best member during inference (line 159), which is problematic as this would not be possible in real-world applications where the true label is unavailable. Second, the key baseline "independent ensembles" (Indp.) is used without proper explanation (line 181), confusing readers. Third, the claim of "specialization" is not convincingly supported—the per-example reassignment mechanism doesn't necessarily lead to class-specific specialization, as the authors acknowledge. Finally, the paper omits crucial related work on diversity regularization in neural networks (e.g., Chen et al.), which would have strengthened the theoretical foundation and positioned the work more clearly.

The paper's significance is moderate: while the method has practical utility, the lack of theoretical grounding and incomplete explanation of key concepts weaken the contribution. The authors should address the testing ambiguity, clarify terminology, and incorporate relevant literature to strengthen their claims about specialization. Despite these issues, the empirical results are compelling, suggesting the method has practical value. I recommend weak acceptance with the understanding that these concerns should be addressed in revision.
