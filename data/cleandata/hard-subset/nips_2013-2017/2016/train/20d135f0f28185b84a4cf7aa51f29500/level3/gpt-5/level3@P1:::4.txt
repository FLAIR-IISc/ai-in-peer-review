This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based “winner-take-gradient” scheme to train ensembles of deep networks to minimize oracle loss (min over M predictions per input). The method replaces the alternating training/reassignment of prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) with per-batch hard assignment: for each example, only the current lowest-loss predictor receives gradient. The authors argue this makes MCL practical for deep nets, is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all ensemble members concurrently. Experiments on CIFAR-10 (classification), PASCAL VOC (segmentation), and COCO (captioning) report consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, sequential boosting-style methods, and a limited MCL baseline; qualitative analyses show emergent specialization across ensemble members.

Relation to prior work: The motivation of producing multiple plausible outputs for downstream oracles is well established (Diverse M-Best in graphical models; MCL; submodular/diversity approaches; diverse decoding in structured prediction). The contribution here is essentially an SGD-era adaptation of MCL to deep architectures by interleaving assignment with gradient updates. Compared to mixture-of-experts and ensemble-of-experts designs (e.g., gating networks, hard-EM), sMCL uses ground-truth-induced “gating” during training without a learned gate and outputs all M hypotheses at test time, deferring selection to an oracle. This is a reasonable reframing but relatively incremental.

Strengths
- Clear, practically useful objective: minimizing oracle loss to deliver sets of plausible hypotheses is compelling for ambiguous tasks.
- Simplicity and generality: the “winner-take-gradient” mechanism is easy to drop into existing SGD pipelines, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and parameter-free.
- Efficiency: concurrent training avoids costly retraining/sequential procedures in prior MCL-style methods; reported 5x speedup over the alternating MCL schedule.
- Breadth of applications: classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning with both CNN and CNN+RNN architectures; qualitative diversity and emergent specialization are convincing and interpretable.
- Empirical evidence that per-example specialization emerges naturally and correlates with oracle performance.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the core idea is a straightforward stochastic/block-coordinate instantiation of prior MCL/hard-EM-style assignment with deep nets; the technical section is very short and light on analysis beyond the obvious gradient masking.
- Clarity and exposition: Section 3 is underdeveloped. Important details are missing (tie-breaking, stability, convergence behavior, collapse avoidance/load balancing, effect of batch size and assignment noise).
- Scope and strength of baselines: comparisons omit several competitive alternatives for promoting diversity in deep ensembles and multi-output prediction (e.g., learned gating MoE, diversity-promoting regularizers such as negative correlation/decorrelation, snapshot/fast ensembles, dropout sampling, diverse beam search for captions). For captions, a single-model beam search rivals or exceeds oracle performance for M=5, undercutting the central claim.
- Metrics and datasets: heavy reliance on oracle metrics without analysis of non-oracle quality or downstream selection; segmentation is on VOC 2011 subset. Stronger validation on PASCAL VOC 2012 and larger/deeper backbones would be more convincing.
- Distinction from multi-task/multi-head training is not clarified: the paper should position sMCL relative to multi-branch networks that share a backbone and encourage head diversity.

Quality
- Technically sound at the level presented, but analysis is minimal. No discussion of convergence, variance of assignments, or conditions under which specialization collapses or becomes imbalanced. No ablations on key design choices (e.g., top-k variant, shared vs separate backbones, batch size).
- Experimental protocol is reasonable but would benefit from stronger baselines, larger models, and more rigorous statistical reporting (variance across seeds).

Clarity
- Writing quality is uneven; the key algorithmic section is only about a page and omits details that practitioners need to reproduce and reason about behavior. The paper would benefit from clearer pseudocode, complexity/runtime reporting, and a more careful positioning versus related work (particularly MoE and diverse decoding).

Originality
- Incremental. The main conceptual move—interleaving hard assignment with SGD updates—is a natural extension of earlier MCL/hard-EM approaches to deep nets.

Significance
- Moderate in practice due to simplicity and ease of adoption; however, current empirical gains appear modest and sometimes matched by stronger inference baselines (e.g., beam search). Clearer evidence on harder benchmarks and stronger baselines would raise impact.

Questions for the authors
- How do you prevent degenerate specialization (one model winning most examples)? Do you observe load imbalance? Any balancing regularizer tested?
- How sensitive is sMCL to batch size, tie-breaking, and initialization? Please report variance across runs.
- What is the effect of sharing a backbone vs training fully independent models?
- Can you compare against a learned gating mixture-of-experts trained with hard EM, and against decorrelation/negative-correlation ensemble regularizers?
- For captioning, how does sMCL compare to diverse beam search and to sampling-based decoding?
- Do oracle improvements translate to improved downstream selection (e.g., simple re-rankers) or human-in-the-loop utility?

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate on PASCAL VOC 2012 and with stronger/deeper architectures; report absolute and relative gains with confidence intervals.
- Add stronger baselines (MoE with gating, decorrelation losses, snapshot ensembles, dropout sampling; diverse beam search for captioning).
- Provide ablations: top-k variant, ensemble size scaling, shared vs separate backbones, assignment noise/temperature, tie-breaking strategies.
- Report training time and GPU-days for all methods; include MCL with enough meta-iterations to converge or explain why infeasible.
- Include diversity metrics (n-gram diversity, segment diversity) and non-oracle metrics to assess trade-offs.
- Clarify the distinction from multi-task/multi-head training and alternative multi-hypothesis networks.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, broadly applicable method with low engineering overhead.
- Consistent oracle improvements across tasks; emergent, interpretable specialization.
- Makes MCL-style training feasible for deep nets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental contribution with limited technical depth.
- Weak/partial baselines; improvements are small and sometimes matched by inference methods.
- Underdeveloped analysis and clarity; key methodological details are missing.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is practical and potentially useful, but the paper needs stronger empirical validation, clearer positioning against competitive baselines, and a more thorough technical treatment.
