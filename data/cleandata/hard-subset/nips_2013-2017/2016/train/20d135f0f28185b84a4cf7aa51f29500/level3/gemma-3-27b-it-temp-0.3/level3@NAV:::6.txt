## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and surprisingly simple algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles. The core idea – updating only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for a given training example – is elegantly motivated by the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework and offers a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods like alternating block coordinate descent. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, suggesting broad applicability. 

The work builds upon the MCL paradigm [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS 2014], but crucially addresses the impracticality of retraining models repeatedly in deep learning settings. While previous work focused on diversity through resampling or joint loss functions [Liu & Yao, 2000; Hinton et al., 2015], sMCL achieves diversity as an emergent property of minimizing oracle loss, leading to interpretable specialization of ensemble members. The claim of specialization is compelling, particularly in the classification experiments, where clear class-wise clustering of predictors is observed.

However, the testing phase remains somewhat unclear. The paper states oracle performance is evaluated, but it’s strongly suspected (as noted by the original reviewer) that the best member is selected *using* ground truth labels (p.5, line 159). This would inflate performance and undermine the evaluation of the ensemble’s ability to provide genuinely useful diverse predictions. Clarification on this point is critical. 

Furthermore, the terms “independent ensembles” (Indp.) and “regular ensembles” are used in figures and text without proper definition. The distinction is crucial for interpreting the results, and the authors should explicitly define these terms. While the observed specialization is interesting, the justification based on per-example reassignments feels weak. It’s unclear why this leads to stable specialization rather than constant oscillation. The authors should consider whether explicitly incorporating specialization into the loss function (as suggested by the reviewer) could improve stability and performance.

Finally, the paper overlooks relevant work on diversity regularization in neural networks, such as Chen et al. [2018], which offer more rigorous approaches to encouraging diversity, particularly in deep settings. Discussing this literature would strengthen the paper’s positioning within the broader field.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Simple, effective algorithm with strong empirical results. Addresses a practical limitation of previous MCL approaches. Demonstrates emergent specialization.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Unclear testing procedure. Undefined terminology. Weak justification for specialization. Missing related work.

**Overall:** Despite these weaknesses, the simplicity and effectiveness of sMCL are noteworthy. I recommend **weak acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the testing phase, clarifying terminology, and discussing relevant related work.
