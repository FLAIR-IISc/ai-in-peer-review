This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training method for deep ensembles that leverages an oracle to select the best prediction from each member during training, effectively backpropagating gradients only through the selected network. The core idea – encouraging specialization within the ensemble to cover a diverse output space – is well-motivated by the practical context of perception systems embedded in larger applications requiring multiple hypotheses. The method is presented as a significant improvement over existing approaches, particularly in terms of training efficiency, as it avoids the costly retraining steps of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating improved oracle performance across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

The work builds on the existing Multiple Choice Learning paradigm [8, 9] and addresses a key limitation of those methods—scalability to deep networks. Prior work often relied on alternating optimization or sequential training, making them impractical for modern architectures. sMCL offers a concurrent, SGD-based approach that circumvents these issues. The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of ensemble learning, contrasting it with approaches focused on decorrelation and highlighting the benefit of specialization driven by the oracle loss. Connections to related work on generating multiple solutions [3, 5, 15, 16, 23, 24] are also clearly drawn, and the advantages of sMCL (particularly its efficiency) are well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is theoretically sound and elegantly addresses the challenges of training ensembles with an oracle. The derivation of the stochastic gradient update rule is clear and concise.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over baselines across diverse tasks and network architectures. The consistent performance gains across different domains strengthen the validity of the approach.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the method is explained in a straightforward manner. The inclusion of illustrative examples (e.g., Fig. 1, Fig. 6, Fig. 7) is helpful.
* **Experimental Thoroughness:** The authors present a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing sMCL to several relevant baselines. The ablation studies and analysis of ensemble member specialization (e.g., Fig. 4c, Fig. 5b) provide valuable insights into the behavior of the method.
* **Originality:**  The application of stochastic gradient descent to directly minimize oracle loss within an ensemble framework represents a novel contribution. Addressing the scalability issues of previous MCL methods is a significant advancement.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Inference Strategy Omission:** The most significant weakness is the complete lack of discussion regarding *how* to exploit the trained ensemble at inference time *without* access to the oracle. The entire training process is predicated on the existence of an oracle, but the paper fails to address the crucial question of how the ensemble's diversity translates to improved performance in a real-world scenario where the oracle is unavailable. This severely limits the practical usability of the method.
* **Problem-Specific Exploitation:** While acknowledging that exploitation strategies are problem-specific, the authors do not even offer preliminary explorations or suggestions for how to leverage the ensemble diversity in different contexts. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true value of the specialization achieved during training.
* **Figure 2 Quality:** The pseudocode for Algorithm 2 is presented as a raster image (Fig. 2), which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. This makes the pseudocode difficult to read, copy, and potentially hinders reproducibility. It should be rendered as vector graphics or actual text.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The method addresses a practical problem in the context of perception systems interacting with downstream processes.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, method, and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of discussion regarding inference strategies without the oracle significantly limits the practical impact of the work.
* The omission of even preliminary explorations of exploitation methods casts doubt on the ultimate utility of the learned ensemble diversity.
* The low-quality pseudocode in Fig. 2 detracts from the overall presentation and reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling technical contribution and impressive experimental results. However, the critical omission of any discussion about inference without the oracle is a major drawback. While the authors convincingly demonstrate the benefits of training diverse ensembles *with* an oracle, they fail to address the fundamental question of *how* to leverage this diversity in a real-world setting.  

I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors *must* address the inference issue in a revised version of the paper. At a minimum, they should discuss potential exploitation strategies, even if these are problem-specific, and provide preliminary results demonstrating their effectiveness. Additionally, Fig. 2 should be replaced with a vector graphic or text-based pseudocode.  Without addressing these points, the paper remains largely a theoretical contribution with limited practical relevance.
