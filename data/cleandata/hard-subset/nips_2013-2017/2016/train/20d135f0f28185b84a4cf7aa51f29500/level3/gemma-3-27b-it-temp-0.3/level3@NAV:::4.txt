## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training ensembles of deep networks to produce diverse outputs suitable for scenarios requiring multiple hypotheses. The core idea is to adapt the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8] to deep learning by employing a stochastic gradient descent approach that efficiently minimizes oracle loss. The authors claim sMCL is parameter-free, agnostic to architecture and loss function, and faster than previous MCL methods.

The motivation for producing diverse outputs in perception systems is well-established and clearly articulated. However, the novelty of sMCL feels incremental. The method essentially adapts a known technique (MCL) to the deep learning setting using a standard optimization procedure (SGD). While this adaptation is non-trivial, it doesn’t represent a fundamental breakthrough. The connection to prior work [8] is acknowledged, but the paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight how sMCL meaningfully advances beyond it, beyond simply addressing the computational cost of retraining. 

A significant weakness is the paper’s clarity and technical depth. Section 3, detailing the sMCL algorithm, is surprisingly short (one page) given its central role. The explanation feels rushed, and the mathematical derivation lacks sufficient detail. Furthermore, the distinction between sMCL and standard multi-task learning or other ensemble training techniques isn’t clearly delineated. It’s unclear why framing this as MCL provides a unique benefit.

The experimental evaluation, while covering diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning), suffers from weak baselines. Comparing against simple independent ensembles and a limited implementation of [8] doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL. The reported improvements are relatively small. The authors acknowledge the limitations of comparing to [5] due to saturation issues, but a stronger comparison is needed. 

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection in its current form. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: 1) Evaluate on PASCAL VOC 2012 as suggested, providing a more standard benchmark. 2) Compare against stronger, more recent baselines for diversity in ensembles. 3) Expand Section 3 with a more detailed technical explanation and justification for the MCL framing. 4) Clarify the distinction between sMCL and related techniques like multi-task learning. 5) Improve the overall writing quality and organization.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in perception systems.
*   Demonstrates some improvement over weak baselines on multiple tasks.
*   The method is relatively simple to implement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; adaptation of existing work rather than a novel contribution.
*   Poorly written and lacks technical depth.
*   Weak baselines and small improvements.
*   Unclear distinction from related techniques.
