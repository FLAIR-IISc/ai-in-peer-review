

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple plausible hypotheses rather than a single prediction. The authors frame this as an extension of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8,9], where the goal is to minimize the oracle loss - the loss of the best prediction among a set of M predictions. The authors claim their sMCL method, which trains ensemble members concurrently via a "winner-take-gradient" approach, is simpler, more efficient, and more general than prior MCL methods that require costly retraining.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm for training diverse ensembles directly minimizing oracle loss; (2) empirical demonstration across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks; and (3) analysis showing how specialization emerges automatically in ensemble members.

**Quality Assessment:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated within the MCL framework. The authors provide theoretical justification for their method (Section 3) and demonstrate its efficacy across multiple tasks. However, the experimental validation is limited: baselines are weak (comparing to independent ensembles and MCL [8] which the authors acknowledge is slower), improvements are marginal (6-10% relative gains), and the paper does not evaluate on standard benchmarks like PASCAL VOC 2012. The method appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing MCL ideas to deep networks rather than a fundamentally new approach. The analysis of specialization (Figure 4c) is interesting but doesn't demonstrate substantial novelty beyond what's implied by the MCL framework.

**Clarity Assessment:** The paper is poorly written. The technical section (Section 3) is overly brief (only one page) for such a central contribution, and key distinctions between sMCL and standard multi-task training or ensemble methods are not clearly articulated. The relationship between the proposed method and existing techniques for inducing diversity (Section 2) is confusingly presented, particularly regarding how sMCL differs from other ensemble approaches. The writing is often vague, with insufficient explanation of why the proposed method is better than alternatives.

**Originality Assessment:** The core idea of using oracle loss for training diverse ensembles is not new; it was introduced in [8,9] and the current work represents a straightforward adaptation to deep networks. The authors claim novelty through the "stochastic" aspect of their method, but this appears to be a minor modification of existing MCL approaches rather than a substantive theoretical contribution. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from standard ensemble methods or multi-task learning, which also produce diverse outputs. The novelty appears incremental rather than transformative.

**Significance Assessment:** The significance is limited by the marginal improvements over baselines and weak experimental validation. The authors claim the method is "parameter-free" and "agnostic to architecture and loss function," but this is not convincingly demonstrated. While the applications to image classification, segmentation, and captioning are relevant, the paper fails to show that these results translate to meaningful improvements in real-world systems. The claimed "interpretable representations of task ambiguity" are not sufficiently validated with compelling evidence.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The problem of producing diverse outputs is important for practical perception systems
- The method is simple to implement and appears to work across multiple tasks
- The "winner-take-gradient" training approach is conceptually interesting

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The contribution is incremental rather than novel (repackaging MCL for deep networks)
- Technical section is too brief and lacks sufficient detail
- Experimental validation is weak with poor baseline comparisons
- The paper fails to demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods
- The writing is poor and fails to clearly distinguish the method from alternatives

The paper addresses an interesting problem but fails to make a sufficiently strong contribution. The method appears to be a straightforward adaptation of prior work rather than a novel approach, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of broad applicability and efficacy. The authors should provide stronger baselines, evaluate on standard benchmarks like PASCAL VOC 2012, and clarify how their method differs from standard multi-task training approaches.

I recommend rejection. While the problem is important, the paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant publication at NIPS. The marginal improvements over baselines, weak experimental validation, and lack of clear novelty are significant concerns that outweigh the paper's modest strengths. The authors should substantially strengthen their theoretical justification, experimental validation, and clarity before considering resubmission.
