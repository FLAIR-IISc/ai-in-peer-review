This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent based approach, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), to train diverse deep ensembles that minimize oracle loss for generating multiple outputs. The authors generalize the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm to jointly learn ensembles of deep networks, which is a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free.

The main idea of sMCL is to interleave the assignment step with batch updates in stochastic gradient descent, where the gradient of the loss for each example is backpropagated only to the lowest error predictor on that example. This approach is generalizable to any learner trained by stochastic gradient descent and is thus applicable to an extensive range of modern deep networks. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on three tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages over existing approaches. The experiments are thorough, and the results show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure. The analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles is also informative, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. First, the importance of initialization is not thoroughly explored. The authors mention that the effect of random vs. pre-trained initialization is unknown, and it would be beneficial to investigate this aspect further. Additionally, the experimental comparison with baselines is unclear, and it is not specified whether the baselines used the same pre-trained initialization as sMCL. Furthermore, the evaluation only uses oracle accuracy, and it would be useful to include an analysis of ensemble diversity to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness.

In terms of related work, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with "Why M Heads are Better than One" (Lee et al., 2015), as suggested by the authors. This would provide a more complete understanding of how sMCL relates to existing approaches in the field.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, and the proposed method has the potential to be widely adopted. With some additional exploration of the importance of initialization and a more comprehensive evaluation, the paper could be even stronger.
