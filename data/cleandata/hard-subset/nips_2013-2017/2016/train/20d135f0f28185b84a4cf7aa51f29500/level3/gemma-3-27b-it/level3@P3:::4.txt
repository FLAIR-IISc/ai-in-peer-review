This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training ensembles of deep networks to produce multiple diverse outputs, motivated by scenarios where an oracle selects the best prediction from a set of hypotheses. While the motivation is clear – improving performance in applications requiring multiple plausible solutions – the technical contribution feels incremental and lacks substantial novelty. The core idea appears to be a stochastic gradient descent adaptation of existing Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) work [8], with minor adjustments tailored for deep networks.

The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The writing is often convoluted, and Section 3, detailing the technical contribution, is surprisingly short (only one page) considering the core algorithmic proposal. The explanation of sMCL, while conceptually straightforward, lacks sufficient detail to fully grasp its nuances and potential implementation challenges. Crucially, the paper fails to adequately distinguish sMCL from standard multi-task learning or other ensemble training techniques that implicitly encourage diversity. The authors assert sMCL avoids costly retraining of [8], but don’t clearly highlight *how* it achieves this beyond stating it interleaves assignment with batch updates – a detail that feels more like a consequence of SGD application than a novel contribution.

The experimental evaluation covers image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, which is commendable in demonstrating broad applicability. However, the baselines used are consistently weak. Comparing against “classical ensembles” with independent random initializations isn't particularly challenging, and the implementation of MCL [8] appears to be limited (only 5 meta-iterations), understating its performance. While improvements are reported, they are generally small, raising concerns about the practical significance of sMCL. The performance gains over the independently trained ensembles, while statistically significant, don't appear dramatic enough to justify a new algorithm, particularly given the conceptual simplicity of the approach. 

A significant weakness is the lack of evaluation on more challenging benchmarks. The authors mention PASCAL VOC 2011 for segmentation, but results are reported without comparison to state-of-the-art methods on PASCAL VOC 2012 – a standard benchmark. A thorough evaluation against stronger baselines, including more recent diversity-promoting ensemble methods, is essential to validate the effectiveness of sMCL.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem: generating diverse predictions for scenarios with oracle feedback.
*   Demonstrates applicability across multiple tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning).
*   sMCL is relatively simple to implement and integrates with existing SGD-based training pipelines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited technical novelty: primarily an adaptation of existing MCL work [8] to deep networks.
*   Poor clarity and conciseness: convoluted writing, short technical section.
*   Weak baselines and modest improvements in experiments.
*   Lack of evaluation on standard, challenging benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012).
*   Insufficiently clarifies the distinction between sMCL and related techniques like multi-task learning.



**Quality:** The paper's technical soundness is questionable given the lack of detail and rigorous comparison. Claims are supported by experiments, but the weak baselines diminish the strength of the evidence. It feels incomplete, lacking a deeper analysis of *why* sMCL works and under what conditions it is most effective.

**Clarity:** The paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. Section 3 needs significant expansion and clarification. The organization could be improved to better highlight the core contributions.

**Originality:** The originality is low. The paper builds upon existing MCL work [8] with minimal modification.

**Significance:** The potential significance is limited by the modest improvements and the lack of compelling evidence that sMCL offers a substantial advantage over existing techniques.



**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. The paper presents a reasonable idea, but the technical contribution is incremental, the presentation is unclear, and the experimental validation is insufficient. The authors should significantly expand the technical details, compare against stronger baselines (including evaluation on PASCAL VOC 2012), and more clearly articulate the novel aspects of their approach.
