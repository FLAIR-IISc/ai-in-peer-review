This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” variant of MCL that jointly trains an ensemble of deep networks to minimize oracle loss and thereby produce multiple plausible predictions for downstream selection. The method replaces MCL’s expensive alternating retraining with a stochastic block gradient descent procedure that, per minibatch example, backpropagates only through the member with lowest loss. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and integrates seamlessly into backprop for end-to-end training. Experiments on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO) show consistent gains in oracle accuracy over independently trained ensembles, boosting-like baselines, and a much faster training time than classical MCL. The work is well positioned relative to MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.) and diverse-M-best inference in structured models, and is also related to multi-head architectures such as “Why M Heads are Better than One” (Lee et al., 2015), mixture-of-experts, and diversity-inducing ensemble methods.

Quality: The optimization is technically sound and easy to implement. The empirical results are strong across three tasks, demonstrating specialization and improved oracle metrics. However, the evaluation emphasizes oracle accuracy almost exclusively. There is no quantitative analysis of diversity (e.g., pairwise disagreement/KL, diversity vs. oracle trade-offs), and the sensitivity to initialization is not studied: it is unclear whether random vs. pre-trained initialization affects specialization or convergence, and whether baselines and sMCL used identical initializations (especially for segmentation/captioning where pretraining matters).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful qualitative visualizations. The experimental setup should explicitly state initialization parity across methods, tie-breaking, and any regularization to prevent collapse.

Originality: The idea of directly minimizing oracle loss is not new (MCL), but the stochastic, backprop-compatible formulation for deep networks is a novel and practical contribution.

Significance: Providing a practical recipe to train diverse deep ensembles for multi-hypothesis outputs is impactful; results suggest others can adopt this easily.

Suggestions: (1) Add quantitative diversity measures and analyze diversity–oracle trade-offs; (2) control initialization across baselines and report ablations (random vs. pre-trained, ensemble size, tie-breaking); (3) compare against “M Heads” (Lee et al., 2015) and related multi-branch/experts; (4) report non-oracle metrics with simple rerankers to demonstrate downstream utility.

Pros:
- Simple, general, and effective; substantial empirical gains and speedups over MCL.
- Clear evidence of emergent specialization across tasks.

Cons:
- Lack of diversity analysis and ablations on initialization/fairness.
- Missing comparisons to closely related multi-head baselines.
