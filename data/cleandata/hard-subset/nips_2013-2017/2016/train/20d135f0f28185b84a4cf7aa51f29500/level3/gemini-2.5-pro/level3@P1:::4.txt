This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The motivation is to better handle ambiguity in perception tasks where multiple valid interpretations of an input may exist. The proposed method, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), extends the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. In sMCL, an ensemble of M models is trained concurrently. For each training example in a mini-batch, the forward pass is computed for all M models. The loss is calculated for each model, and the model with the minimum loss (the "winner") is identified. During the backward pass, the gradient is backpropagated only through this winning model, while the other models receive no update for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" approach encourages different models in the ensemble to specialize on different subsets of the data or different modes of the output distribution. The authors demonstrate this approach on three tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC 2011), and image captioning (MSCOCO).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for generating multiple, diverse hypotheses in practical perception systems. The discussion of implicit class confusion, ambiguous evidence, and mode-seeking behavior provides a strong justification for moving beyond single-prediction models. The examples in Figure 1 are particularly illustrative.

2.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple to understand and implement. It is a general-purpose technique that can be applied to any architecture trained with stochastic gradient descent, regardless of the specific loss function. This makes it a potentially valuable and widely applicable tool.

3.  **Breadth of Experiments:** The authors validate their approach across three distinct and challenging tasks involving different network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). This demonstrates the versatility of the proposed method and shows that it can induce different forms of interpretable specialization depending on the task.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The primary weakness of this work is its limited originality. The core idea is a direct, stochastic adaptation of the batch algorithm from Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The original MCL proposes an alternating block coordinate descent that assigns data points to models and then retrains the models on their assigned partitions. sMCL essentially performs the same steps but at the mini-batch level: assignment is done for each example, and "retraining" is replaced by a single gradient step. While this adaptation is crucial for making the approach feasible for deep networks, the conceptual contribution beyond the original MCL paper is incremental. The motivation and problem formulation are largely inherited from prior work.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Technical Depth:** The paper is not clearly written, and the technical description is overly brief. Section 3, which contains the main technical contribution, is less than a page long. It presents the algorithm but provides no deeper analysis of its properties. For instance:
    *   What are the optimization dynamics? Does this "hard" assignment of gradients lead to training instability or slow convergence compared to a "soft" version?
    *   The connection to other related training paradigms is not discussed. The "winner-take-all" update rule is reminiscent of competitive learning or hard-EM algorithms. A discussion of how sMCL relates to these well-established concepts would strengthen the paper and better contextualize the contribution.
    *   The distinction from multi-task learning, where different heads of a network are trained on different objectives, or alternative training schemes is not made clear.

3.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** While the breadth of experiments is a strength, the depth and rigor of the evaluation are questionable.
    *   **Baselines:** The baselines used for comparison are relatively weak. The "Indp." ensemble (training models independently from different random initializations) is a standard but often not very strong baseline. The comparison against Dey et al. [5] is useful, but that method appears to underperform even the independent ensemble for larger M. The most relevant baseline, MCL [8], is shown to be computationally infeasible, which makes the 5x speedup claim less impactful—it's faster than a method that is already acknowledged as impractical for this setting.
    *   **Significance of Improvements:** The reported improvements over the independent ensemble baseline are modest. For CIFAR-10 classification, the oracle accuracy gain is around 2% absolute for M=6. For segmentation, the relative improvement is ~7%, which corresponds to about 4% absolute IoU. It is unclear if these gains are practically significant enough to justify the method's complexity.
    *   **Outdated Datasets/Protocols:** The use of the PASCAL VOC 2011 validation set for segmentation is outdated for a 2016 paper. To make a compelling case, the authors should evaluate on the standard PASCAL VOC 2012 test set and compare against stronger contemporary methods.
    *   **Insufficient Ablation:** In the captioning experiment, it is unclear how sMCL compares to simply ensembling the outputs of several independently trained models at test time. The main comparison is against beam search from a single model, which is a different approach to generating diversity.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Expand Section 3 to provide a more thorough technical discussion of the sMCL algorithm, including its convergence properties and its relationship to other learning paradigms like hard-EM.
*   Strengthen the experimental evaluation by comparing against stronger baselines. This could include more sophisticated ensemble techniques or other diversity-promoting methods from the literature.
*   For the segmentation task, please report results on the PASCAL VOC 2012 test server to allow for a fair comparison with the state of the art.
*   Clarify the comparison in the image captioning experiment. A direct comparison of oracle scores between an sMCL ensemble and a standard "Indp." ensemble would be more informative.

**Conclusion:**

The paper presents a simple and practical method for training diverse deep ensembles to address ambiguity in perception tasks. However, the contribution is an incremental adaptation of prior work. The paper's clarity is hampered by a very brief technical section, and the experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous to convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing techniques. While the problem is important and the qualitative results are interesting, the weaknesses in novelty and experimental validation prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.
