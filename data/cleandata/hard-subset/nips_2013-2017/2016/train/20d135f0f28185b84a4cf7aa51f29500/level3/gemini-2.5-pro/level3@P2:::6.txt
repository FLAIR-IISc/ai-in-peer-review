### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training procedure for deep network ensembles. The goal is to produce a diverse set of high-quality outputs for a given input, which is useful in applications where a downstream oracle (e.g., a human user) can select the best one. The core idea is simple yet effective: for each example in a training batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the single member that achieved the lowest loss (the "winner"). This "winner-take-gradient" approach is presented as a stochastic, online version of the block coordinate descent algorithm from Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8], making it far more efficient and scalable for training large, deep models. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO. The results show that sMCL consistently outperforms baselines like independently trained ensembles and is significantly faster than the original MCL method, while producing diverse and interpretable outputs that reveal emergent specialization among ensemble members.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The empirical evaluation is a significant strength, covering a diverse set of challenging problems and modern architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). The results consistently demonstrate that sMCL achieves lower oracle error than the baselines. The qualitative examples, particularly for segmentation and captioning, effectively illustrate the diversity of the generated outputs.

However, the claims about emergent "specialization" are not fully convincing. The paper argues that ensemble members become experts on specific classes or modes of the output space (e.g., Figure 4c). While the data supports this observation, the underlying mechanism is not deeply explored. Since the "winner" is chosen on a per-example basis in each mini-batch, it is unclear how stable these class-level specializations become or why they emerge so cleanly rather than members specializing on more complex, fine-grained partitions of the input space. A more thorough analysis of the assignment dynamics during training would strengthen this claim.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core method is explained clearly. The algorithm is intuitive and its simplicity is a major advantage.

There are a few areas that could be improved. Firstly, the evaluation protocol needs to be stated more carefully. The paper reports "oracle" metrics, where the best prediction from the set is selected using the ground truth label. While this is the correct way to evaluate performance under the MCL problem setting, it can be confusing. It should be made explicit that this metric evaluates the quality of the *set* of hypotheses under the assumption of a perfect downstream oracle, and is not equivalent to the performance of a deployed system without such an oracle.

Secondly, the definition of the "independent ensembles" (or "regular ensembles") baseline, while present in the experiments section (line 181), could be introduced earlier and more clearly. These terms are used throughout the paper and figures, and a prominent definition would aid readability.

**Originality:** The idea of minimizing oracle loss via an alternating optimization scheme is not new (Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9]). However, the paper's primary contribution—adapting this idea into a simple, efficient, end-to-end stochastic algorithm suitable for deep learning—is novel and significant. This contribution makes the MCL paradigm practical for the large-scale models prevalent today.

A notable weakness is the omission of related work on diversity regularization in neural networks. Several methods enforce diversity by adding explicit regularization terms to the loss function that penalize similarity between the outputs or representations of different models/components (e.g., work by Chen et al.). While sMCL's implicit approach is different, this body of work shares the same high-level goal and should be discussed to properly contextualize the paper's contribution.

**Significance:** The problem of generating multiple, diverse hypotheses is of high practical importance in many AI systems that interact with users or other components. This paper provides a simple, general, and effective solution. The method is parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and easy to implement, which significantly increases the likelihood that it will be used by other researchers and practitioners. The impressive performance gains across multiple domains demonstrate a clear advance over existing approaches for this problem setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Practicality:** Proposes a simple, scalable, and novel stochastic algorithm that makes the Multiple Choice Learning framework practical for modern deep networks.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is thoroughly evaluated on three different, challenging tasks and is shown to significantly outperform relevant baselines.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The method is general, parameter-free, and agnostic to network architecture and loss function, making it a valuable tool for the community.
4.  **Efficiency:** It offers a massive speed-up (5x or more) compared to the most direct predecessor (MCL), which required multiple full retraining cycles.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Insufficient Analysis:** The claims of emergent specialization, while interesting, are not rigorously justified or analyzed.
2.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss an important and relevant line of research on explicit diversity regularization in neural networks.
3.  **Clarity on Evaluation:** The description of the evaluation protocol could lead to misinterpretation if readers are not already familiar with oracle loss settings.

**Recommendation:**

I am recommending weak acceptance. The paper introduces a simple, elegant, and highly effective method for an important problem. The empirical results are strong and the contribution is practical and significant. However, the weaknesses in the analysis of the specialization phenomenon and the omission of relevant related work prevent a stronger recommendation. The paper would be significantly improved if the authors could provide a deeper analysis of the training dynamics and situate their work within the broader context of diversity-promoting methods. As a suggestion for future work, it would be interesting to see if specialization could be encouraged more explicitly, for instance by incorporating a term into the loss that favors stable assignments.
