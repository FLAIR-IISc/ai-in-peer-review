- Proposes sMCL: a winner-take-gradient training scheme where only the ensemble member with lowest loss for an example receives the gradient; architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, trains members jointly via SGD.
- sMCL interleaves assignment with mini-batch updates (vs MCL’s alternation and retraining), yielding substantial speedups and strong oracle-metric gains on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC, and COCO.
- Paper claims that specialization of ensemble members emerges under sMCL.
- Core idea is technically sound and well-motivated; empirical results are strong and broadly evaluated.
- Major evaluation concern: test-time protocol is unclear and appears to use an oracle to pick the best member; this must be explicitly stated.
- Recommend reporting non-oracle evaluations with a learned selector (e.g., light gating or re-ranking trained on validation) to demonstrate deployability.
- Specialization claim is suggestive but under-supported; add quantitative metrics (mutual information between member ID and class, per-sample assignment entropy, stability across seeds) and systematically ablate the k-winner variant.
- Clarity issues: undefined term “independent/regular ensembles (Indp.)” in figures, missing tie-breaking rules, unclear inference cost scaling with M, and whether batch-norm statistics are per-member.
- Originality: conceptually close to hard-EM, k-means assignment, and hard mixture-of-experts, but contribution lies in adapting concurrent SGD to deep ensembles.
- Missing related work: overlooks diversity-regularization literature (e.g., negative correlation learning, Chen et al.) and DPP-based diversity methods; discussion and comparisons would improve positioning.
- Significance: sMCL could be impactful for multi-hypothesis perception if paired with a practical selector; oracle-only results limit external validity.
- Pros: simple, general algorithm with strong empirical gains and ~5x speedup over prior MCL; applicable across tasks and architectures.
- Cons: unclear test-time protocol and oracle-only evaluation, insufficient evidence for member specialization, missing diversity-regularization comparisons, and unclear baseline definitions.
- Recommendation: Weak accept; paper would become a solid contribution after revisions addressing evaluation, clarity, and related work.
