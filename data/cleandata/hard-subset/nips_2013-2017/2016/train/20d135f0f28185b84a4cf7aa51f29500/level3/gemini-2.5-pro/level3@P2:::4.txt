This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a training procedure for deep network ensembles designed to produce a diverse set of plausible outputs. The core idea is to minimize the "oracle loss," where the loss for a given example is defined by the error of the best-performing member of the ensemble. The proposed method adapts the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8] to stochastic gradient descent (SGD). In each training step, for every example in a mini-batch, the forward pass is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member that produced the lowest loss. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows for concurrent training of all ensemble members, avoiding the costly iterative retraining required by the original MCL algorithm. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on three tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing that sMCL produces ensembles with better oracle performance than independently trained ensembles and other baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating multiple diverse hypotheses from a model. In many real-world systems involving human interaction or downstream verification modules, providing a small set of high-quality candidate solutions is more valuable than a single, potentially incorrect, prediction. This is especially true for tasks with inherent ambiguity.

2.  **Simplicity and Generality:** The sMCL method is simple to understand and implement. It is parameter-free and can be applied to any deep architecture trained with SGD, regardless of the specific loss function. This makes it a potentially attractive and broadly applicable tool.

3.  **Experimental Breadth:** The authors validate their approach across three different and significant problem domains (classification, segmentation, captioning) using corresponding deep architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). This demonstrates the versatility of the proposed method.

4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides interesting qualitative results and analysis showing how the sMCL training procedure encourages ensemble members to specialize in an emergent fashion. The visualizations of class-specialization in classification (Fig. 4c) and diverse outputs in segmentation and captioning (Fig. 6, Fig. 7) are insightful and effectively illustrate the benefits of the approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality:** The primary concern is the limited novelty of the proposed method. The conceptual framework of minimizing oracle loss via an alternating optimization was established by Multiple Choice Learning [8]. The main contribution of this paper is adapting this idea to the SGD setting by making the assignment of examples to models a per-batch, "hard" decision. While this is a practical and necessary adaptation for deep learning, it feels more like an implementation detail or an engineering contribution rather than a fundamentally new machine learning concept. The paper's framing as a "stochastic block gradient descent" approach is also not deeply explored or justified.

2.  **Clarity and Technical Depth:** The paper's writing and organization could be significantly improved. The core technical contribution is described in Section 3, which is exceptionally brief. The section lacks a rigorous derivation or theoretical analysis. For example, the connection to stochastic block gradient descent is mentioned but not formally established. It would be beneficial to discuss the optimization landscape and potential convergence properties, even if informally. Furthermore, the paper does not adequately distinguish sMCL from related concepts like training mixture-of-experts models or other forms of competitive/alternative learning where gradients are selectively routed.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** While the breadth of experiments is a strength, the empirical results are not entirely convincing.
    *   **Weak Baselines:** The baselines used for comparison are not particularly strong. The primary comparison is against independently trained ensembles ("Indp."), which is a standard but simple baseline. The method of Dey et al. [5] consistently underperforms even the independent ensembles for M>2, making it a weak point of comparison. The original MCL method is shown to be slow, but the performance gap between sMCL and MCL on CIFAR-10 is negligible. A stronger case would require comparison against more sophisticated methods for generating diverse outputs from deep models.
    *   **Modest Improvements:** The performance gains over the simple "Indp." baseline are quite modest. For instance, on semantic segmentation, the paper reports a ~7% relative improvement, and on CIFAR-10, the oracle accuracy gain is only a few percentage points. Given the incremental nature of the technical contribution, the empirical gains would need to be more substantial to demonstrate significant impact.
    *   **Choice of Datasets:** For semantic segmentation, evaluation on the PASCAL VOC 2011 dataset is reasonable, but the community has largely moved to the more challenging PASCAL VOC 2012 benchmark. Validating the results on this more standard benchmark would strengthen the paper's claims.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. The proposed sMCL method is simple, general, and easy to implement. The authors demonstrate its applicability across a diverse set of challenging tasks.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The work is of an incremental nature, being a straightforward adaptation of a prior method [8] to SGD-based training. The technical description is sparse and lacks depth. The experimental evaluation relies on weak baselines and shows only modest performance gains, which may not be significant enough to justify the contribution.

In conclusion, while the paper tackles an interesting problem with a simple and elegant solution, its limited novelty and the modest performance improvements shown in the experiments are significant concerns. To be more compelling, the paper would need to either provide a more thorough technical justification for the method or demonstrate more substantial empirical gains against stronger, more relevant baselines on standard, challenging benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL VOC 2012 for segmentation).
