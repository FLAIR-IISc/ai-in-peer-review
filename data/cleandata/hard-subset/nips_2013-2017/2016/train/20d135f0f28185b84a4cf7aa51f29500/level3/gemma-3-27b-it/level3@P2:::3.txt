## Review of Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks for Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The core idea is to leverage a “winner-takes-the-gradient” strategy during stochastic gradient descent, effectively assigning each training example to the ensemble member with the lowest loss. This allows for concurrent training of all ensemble members without the costly retraining steps required by prior MCL methods. The authors demonstrate strong experimental results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showcasing the method’s applicability and efficacy.

The motivation for MCL is well-articulated, framing it within the context of real-world perception systems embedded in larger applications that benefit from multiple plausible hypotheses. The connection to existing work on ensemble learning and generating multiple solutions is clearly established, citing relevant papers such as those by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9], Hinton et al. [12], and Dey et al. [5]. The authors correctly position their work as the first practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles by addressing the limitations of prior methods, particularly the computational cost associated with retraining and sequential learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** The paper tackles a significant challenge in the field – scaling MCL to deep networks – which is highly relevant to the NIPS community given the increasing deployment of deep learning in complex, real-world systems.
* **Simplicity and ease of implementation:** The proposed sMCL method is remarkably simple to implement, requiring only a minor modification to existing stochastic gradient descent training loops. This ease of adoption is a significant advantage.
* **Broad experimental evaluation:** The evaluation across three diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) with different network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach.
* **Competitive performance:** sMCL consistently outperforms baseline methods, including classical ensembles and the previously proposed MCL approach by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9], while also being significantly faster.
* **Interpretable Expertise:** The analysis revealing specialization among ensemble members, particularly in the image classification experiments, is insightful and suggests that sMCL enables the emergence of interpretable expertise within the ensemble.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Implicit, rather than explicit, diversity:** While the paper heavily emphasizes the benefit of diverse solutions, sMCL does not incorporate an explicit diversity term into the loss function or training process. The diversity emerges as a consequence of minimizing the oracle loss, which is a valid approach, but might limit the control over the level of diversity achieved.
* **Incremental contribution:** The contribution, while valuable, feels somewhat incremental relative to the work of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9]. The proposed method can be viewed as a relaxation of their alternating block coordinate descent approach, and the extent to which this relaxation provides a substantial improvement beyond simply enabling training on modern deep architectures could be further explored.
* **Limited diversity quantification:** The evaluation primarily focuses on oracle performance, which is a good measure of overall quality. However, a more direct quantification of the diversity of the generated solutions (e.g., using metrics like average pairwise distance between outputs) would strengthen the analysis.

**Minor Issues:**

* The first sentence of the abstract could be reworded for clarity: “Many practical perception systems…benefit from providing multiple hypotheses…” is slightly awkward.
* The emphasis on “diversity” in the title and throughout the text could be toned down. While diversity is a beneficial outcome, the core contribution is the efficient training of ensembles for MCL, not necessarily the maximization of diversity itself.
* Typo on line 108: the first “f” should be “g.”
* Remove the extra “.” in the middle of the sentence on line 115.
* The paper does not explicitly detail how baseline MCL deep networks were ensured to have converged. Early cutting off of learners in the alternating optimization procedure could potentially harm ensemble performance and should be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the field of deep learning.
* The proposed method is simple, efficient, and produces strong empirical results.
* The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates the generalizability of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, method, and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The contribution is somewhat incremental, building on existing work in MCL.
* The lack of explicit diversity regularization may limit the potential for controlling the diversity of the generated solutions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the contribution is somewhat incremental, the practical benefits of enabling efficient training of deep ensembles for Multiple Choice Learning are significant. The strong experimental results and clear presentation make this a worthwhile addition to NIPS 2016. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the issues outlined above. Specifically, clarifying the convergence criteria for the baseline MCL method and perhaps including a more direct measure of diversity in the evaluation would strengthen the paper.
