This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions, a setting known as Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The core idea is to train M learners in parallel to minimize the oracle loss, where the loss for a given input is defined by the error of the best-performing learner in the ensemble. The paper's main contribution is a novel training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which adapts the MCL framework to be practical for deep networks trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). In contrast to prior work by Guzman-Rivera et al., which used a block coordinate descent approach requiring iterative retraining of models, sMCL integrates the learner-assignment step directly into backpropagation. During the backward pass for each mini-batch, the gradient for each training example is routed only to the single "winner" learner that achieved the lowest loss on that example. This simple "winner-takes-the-gradient" strategy allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks—image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning—showing significant improvements in oracle performance over independently trained ensembles and other baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper tackles an important and highly relevant problem. As deep learning models are deployed in complex, real-world systems, the ability to generate a set of plausible hypotheses rather than a single point estimate is crucial for handling ambiguity and for downstream decision-making processes. This work makes the promising MCL paradigm practical for the kind of large-scale deep networks prevalent today.

2.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed sMCL method is elegant in its simplicity. The "winner-takes-the-gradient" rule is conceptually straightforward and can be implemented with minimal changes to existing deep learning training pipelines, essentially by adding a layer that selectively routes gradients. This ease of implementation makes the method highly accessible and increases its potential for broad adoption.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a major strength of this work. The authors evaluate sMCL across three diverse and challenging domains (classification, segmentation, captioning) using different network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). The method consistently outperforms baselines, including independently trained ensembles and a boosting-style approach. This comprehensive evaluation strongly supports the claim that sMCL is a general and effective technique. The qualitative analyses showing how different learners specialize on different data subsets (e.g., specific classes or caption styles) are also very insightful.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Mismatch in Motivation vs. Method:** The paper's title, abstract, and introduction heavily motivate the work around the concept of "diversity." However, the proposed objective function (the oracle loss) does not include an explicit term to enforce diversity. Diversity is presented as an emergent property of the optimization process. While this is an interesting result, the framing is somewhat misleading, as a reader might expect a method that explicitly models or encourages diversity (e.g., via a repulsion term). The connection between minimizing oracle loss and maximizing diversity could be discussed more formally.

2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The contribution, while practical and effective, can be viewed as an incremental step from the MCL framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The core idea of assigning each example to a single best learner is the same. The main novelty here is the adaptation of this idea to the SGD setting, which makes it tractable for deep networks. While this is a significant engineering and empirical contribution, the conceptual leap from the original block coordinate descent algorithm to this stochastic relaxation is fairly direct.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

-   The comparison against the original MCL baseline [8] raises a question about experimental setup. Given that training a deep network to convergence is extremely time-consuming, how was convergence defined for each meta-iteration of the MCL baseline? If the learners in the MCL baseline were not fully trained at each step, this could unfairly handicap its performance relative to sMCL, which performs updates continuously. Clarification on the training budget and convergence criteria for this baseline would strengthen the comparison.

-   I would suggest rewording the first sentence of the abstract for better clarity and impact. The current version is slightly verbose. Something more direct, such as "Practical perception systems are often part of larger pipelines where downstream components or human users can select the best output from a set of multiple predictions," might be more effective.

-   Related to the weakness mentioned above, I recommend toning down the emphasis on "diversity" in the title and text, or at least being more precise that diversity is an emergent property rather than a direct optimization goal. This would manage reader expectations and more accurately reflect the method's mechanism.

-   There are a couple of minor typos that should be corrected:
    -   Line 108: In the equation for `g(x)`, the first `f` should likely be `g`.
    -   Line 115: There is an extra period "." in the middle of the sentence.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting a simple, effective, and broadly applicable method for an important problem. The strengths in its practicality and extensive empirical validation outweigh the weaknesses regarding its incremental novelty and the framing of diversity. The work is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to many researchers and practitioners at NIPS.
