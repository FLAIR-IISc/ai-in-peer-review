Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based procedure for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss. For each minibatch, the method forwards each example through all M learners, identifies the lowest-loss model per example, and backpropagates only through that winner (“winner-take-gradient”). This directly instantiates the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which introduced an alternating optimization over assignments and model parameters but required repeated retraining to convergence. The main advance here is to “SGD-ize” MCL so it scales to deep networks and structured tasks. The paper evaluates sMCL on CIFAR-10 classification, FCN-based semantic segmentation, and CNN+RNN image captioning, showing consistent gains in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, Dey et al. [5], and (within feasible schedules) the original MCL procedure. Qualitatively and quantitatively, the authors show emergent specialization/diversity among ensemble members and explore a k-winner variant that interpolates between oracle training (k=1) and standard ensembles (k=M).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation connecting the constrained MCL objective to the gradient routing rule is correct and intuitive. The algorithm is straightforward to implement with modern deep-learning frameworks. While there is no convergence analysis, this is common for nonconvex deep learning with hard assignments; the empirical evidence is solid.
- Empirical support: Results span three modalities/architectures, with consistent oracle improvements and useful qualitative analyses (class-wise assignment patterns, segmentation variations, caption n-gram diversity). Runtime comparisons support the scalability claim (≈5x faster than MCL under their setup).
- Completeness and care: The paper is careful to position against MCL [8], Dey et al. [5], and classical ensembles. One limitation is that MCL is not compared under settings that could partially mitigate its retraining cost (see suggestions below). The ablations on k-winners are helpful; additional diversity analysis would strengthen the story (see below).

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to reproduce in spirit. Pseudocode for sMCL is helpful. Figures effectively communicate specialization. Minor suggestions: clarify tie-breaking policy; discuss effects of batch size and learning-rate schedules on assignment stability; and describe any measures taken to avoid early-collapse (if any).

Originality
- Conceptually, sMCL is an incremental but important extension of MCL [8]: it replaces offline alternating retraining with an online minibatch assignment-and-update scheme. This is a small algorithmic change, but it is exactly what makes MCL practical for deep, structured models. Related work on ensembles, mixture-of-experts, and diverse M-best inference is appropriately cited.

Significance
- The contribution is practically significant. A parameter-free, architecture-agnostic procedure that reliably improves oracle metrics and induces interpretable diversity is likely to be adopted. The demonstration on segmentation and captioning is compelling, where multiple plausible hypotheses are particularly useful.

Strengths
- Simple, parameter-free algorithm that drops into existing SGD training.
- Scales to deep networks and structured outputs; concurrent training of members.
- Consistent oracle gains across tasks; clear qualitative specialization.
- k-winner variant illuminates the diversity–performance trade-off.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Algorithmic novelty is modest relative to MCL [8]; essentially a stochastic block coordinate version with per-batch hard assignments.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., on convergence or assignment balance), and limited analysis of when/why specialization emerges versus collapses.
- Comparisons to MCL are constrained by cost; it remains unclear how MCL would fare under more frequent (batched/partial) reassignment schedules.
- Diversity is mostly shown qualitatively; diversity–accuracy trade-offs deserve deeper quantitative treatment.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare to a batched/online variant of MCL: alternate T epochs of training with reassignment using only the current training subset (or even per-minibatch alternating with short inner loops). This would probe whether sMCL’s advantage is due to stochasticity/winner-take-gradient per se or to reassignment frequency.
- Add quantitative diversity analyses:
  - Disagreement/diversity metrics: pairwise prediction disagreement, Q-statistic, inter-model KL divergence (for classifiers), IoU diversity for segmentation, n-gram overlap entropy for captions.
  - Diversity–performance curves: vary k in the k-winner scheme and report oracle vs marginal diversity; optionally try soft assignments pi,m ∝ exp(−`/τ) with temperature annealing to study the hard/soft boundary.
  - Assignment balance: histogram of per-model sample counts over training epochs; correlation with per-class frequency; impact on per-class oracle accuracy.
- Analyze sensitivity: impact of batch size, learning rate, and initialization on assignment dynamics; effect of tie-breaking policies; whether early “winner lock-in” occurs and if a brief warm-up with k>1 or soft assignments reduces brittleness.
- Broader baselines: mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network; snapshot ensembles; deep ensembles with diversity-promoting regularizers (e.g., negative correlation learning) where applicable.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated problem where multiple hypotheses matter.
- Practical and widely applicable algorithm; easy to adopt.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains and interpretable specialization.
- Solid writing and positioning; helpful ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental algorithmic novelty relative to [8].
- Limited theoretical analysis; some comparisons to [8] could be more balanced via batched variants.
- Diversity characterization could be more systematic.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is sMCL to batch size and learning rate? Does larger batch size reduce assignment noise and help or hurt diversity?
- Did you observe assignment imbalance or sample neglect? Any benefits from soft or k>1 assignments early in training?
- How are ties handled in practice, and does random tie-breaking noticeably affect outcomes?
- Could you report calibration and per-member accuracy to better understand specialization?

Overall recommendation
Despite incremental novelty, the SGD formulation makes MCL practical for deep, structured settings and yields consistent empirical benefits. The paper is interesting, clearly written, and likely to be impactful in practice. I favor acceptance, contingent on adding a batched MCL comparison and deeper quantitative analysis of diversity.
