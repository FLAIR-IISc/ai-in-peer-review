This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by framing the problem as minimizing oracle loss. The core idea is elegant: during training, for each example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss (as determined by an oracle) receives a gradient update, effectively encouraging specialization among members. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showcasing sMCL's broad applicability and advantages over existing ensemble methods like independent training, MCL, and a boosting-based approach. The paper is well-written, thoroughly experiments, and offers compelling visualizations illustrating the emergent expertise within the trained ensembles.

The work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8, 9] but crucially addresses its limitations in the context of deep networks, namely the computational cost of iterative retraining. sMCL achieves a significant speedup (5x compared to MCL in the experiments) by enabling concurrent training of ensemble members via a stochastic gradient descent approach. The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of ensemble learning, highlighting how it differs from methods focused on decorrelation or joint loss minimization. The connection to work on generating multiple solutions [3, 15, 16, 23, 24] is also well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The derivation of the gradient update rule (Equation 2) is clear and concise. The approach leverages the efficiency of stochastic gradient descent while effectively addressing the oracle loss minimization problem.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results across diverse tasks and architectures provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of sMCL. The consistent improvements over strong baselines (including a proper implementation of MCL) are convincing.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly articulates the motivation and problem setting. The method is described in a straightforward manner, and the experimental setup is detailed.
*   **Originality:** While building on the MCL framework, the adaptation of this concept to deep networks via a stochastic gradient descent approach is novel and addresses a significant practical limitation of previous work. The emergent specialization observed in the ensembles is a particularly interesting and insightful finding.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of sMCL is considerable. Diverse ensembles are valuable in scenarios where ambiguity or multiple plausible solutions exist, common in real-world applications. The ability to train such ensembles efficiently opens up new possibilities for building robust and reliable perception systems. The interpretability gained from observing specialization within the ensemble is also a significant benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Inference Strategy:** The most significant weakness is the lack of discussion regarding how to effectively *use* the trained ensemble at inference time *without* access to an oracle. The experiments focus solely on training and evaluating the ensemble with an oracle present. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it severely restricts the practical applicability of the work. Exploiting the diversity of the ensemble for improved performance requires a downstream re-ranking or selection strategy, which is not explored. It's unclear how to best combine the outputs, and the optimal approach is likely problem-dependent, a fact the authors concede.
*   **Figure Quality:**  The inclusion of Figure 2 as a raster image is a minor but frustrating issue.  Code snippets and algorithms should be rendered as text or vector graphics for readability and scalability.
*   **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors state sMCL is parameter-free, the underlying training of the individual networks (learning rate, batch size, etc.) requires tuning. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of sMCL to these underlying hyperparameters would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong technical contribution with a novel and effective algorithm.
*   Compelling empirical results demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
*   Excellent clarity of presentation and writing.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of discussion regarding inference strategies limits the practical usability of the method.
*   The minor issue with Figure 2.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. While the lack of discussion regarding inference strategies is a significant weakness, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh this limitation. I recommend acceptance, with a strong encouragement to the authors to address the inference issue in future work. I rate this as a **Strong Accept**.
