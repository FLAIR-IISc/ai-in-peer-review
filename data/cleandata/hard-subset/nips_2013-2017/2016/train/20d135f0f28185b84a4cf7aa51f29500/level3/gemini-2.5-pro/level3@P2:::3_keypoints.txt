- Paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL): training ensembles to minimize oracle loss and produce multiple high-quality predictions for ambiguous inputs.  
- Main contribution: Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” algorithm that backpropagates loss only to the best ensemble member per example.  
- sMCL directly optimizes an upper bound on oracle loss, is architecture- and loss-agnostic, and requires no extra hyperparameters.  
- Empirical validation across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning shows sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines on oracle performance.  
- Analysis shows interpretable specialization emerges (e.g., class-based in classification, structural in captioning).  
- Strength: Highly relevant problem with practical importance for handling ambiguity in deployed perception systems.  
- Strength: Method is exceptionally simple and general, easy to implement within standard SGD training.  
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments on diverse tasks and architectures strengthen the empirical claim.  
- Weakness: Framing emphasizes “diversity” despite no explicit diversity term; diversity is emergent from oracle loss minimization.  
- Weakness: Conceptual novelty is incremental—sMCL is an online/stochastic adaption of prior MCL alternating optimization (Guzman-Rivera et al., 2012).  
- Minor point: Abstract’s first sentence could be clearer; consider reframing emphasis from “diversity” to oracle loss minimization.  
- Minor point: Typo on line 108 (should read argmin g,pi,m) and an extra period on line 115.  
- Question: Request more detail on the MCL baseline training protocol—how and when meta-iterations were stopped to avoid unfairly handicapping that baseline.  
- Overall: Well-written, practically significant paper with strong experimental support; strengths outweigh weaknesses despite limited conceptual novelty.
