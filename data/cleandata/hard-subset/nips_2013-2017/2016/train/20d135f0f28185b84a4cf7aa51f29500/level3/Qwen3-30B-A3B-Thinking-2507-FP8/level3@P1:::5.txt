

The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training deep ensembles to minimize *oracle loss*—the error when an external oracle selects the best prediction from multiple outputs. This addresses a critical gap in practical AI systems (e.g., autonomous driving, captioning) where single-prediction models fail under ambiguity, producing generic or mixed outputs (e.g., "horse-cow" segments). The work generalizes prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8,9] to deep networks, overcoming MCL’s inefficiency (requiring multiple retraining cycles) via a stochastic block gradient descent method. sMCL trains ensemble members concurrently by backpropagating gradients *only* to the lowest-error predictor per example during SGD, making it architecture-agnostic, parameter-free, and 5× faster than MCL. The authors validate sMCL across three tasks: CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning, demonstrating consistent oracle accuracy gains (6–10% over baselines) and emergent specialization (e.g., ensemble members clustering by class or semantic expertise).

**Strengths**  
The paper’s core technical contribution—sMCL—is elegantly simple and theoretically grounded. The stochastic gradient update (Eq. 2) directly minimizes oracle loss without auxiliary losses or retraining, enabling seamless integration into existing deep learning pipelines. This addresses a key bottleneck in prior work [5,8,9], which struggled with scalability for deep architectures. The experimental scope is broad and convincing: sMCL outperforms classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. [5] across diverse tasks and networks (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs), with qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization (e.g., segmentation specialists for "cows" or "horses"). The oracle evaluation protocol (e.g., top-k accuracy for classification, top-IoU for segmentation) is standard and well-justified, avoiding confusion with downstream ranking systems. The method’s simplicity and compatibility with SGD make it highly practical for real-world deployment.

**Weaknesses**  
Despite strong results, three weaknesses require clarification. First, the paper does not address the *impact of initialization* on sMCL’s performance. It uses random initialization for baselines but does not compare against pre-trained models (e.g., ImageNet weights), which could significantly affect results in deep learning. For instance, if sMCL’s specialization depends on initialization, the reported gains might not generalize to standard pre-trained settings. Second, baseline comparisons lack critical details: Did MCL and Dey et al. [5] use the same pre-trained initialization as sMCL? The paper states MCL was initialized with "different random weights," but this is ambiguous and could bias results (e.g., if sMCL’s advantage stems from initialization rather than the algorithm). Third, while the paper claims "diverse solutions," it evaluates *only* oracle accuracy without quantifying *actual diversity*. For captioning, it shows qualitative examples of diverse sentences but omits metrics like n-gram uniqueness or entropy—essential to prove the method generates *meaningful* diversity versus redundant outputs. A comparison with Lee et al.’s "Why M Heads are Better than One" (2015) would contextualize how sMCL’s oracle-focused specialization differs from prior multi-head approaches (e.g., Lee et al. optimizes ensemble diversity directly but not oracle loss).

**Overall Assessment**  
sMCL is a high-quality, original contribution that effectively bridges MCL with deep learning. It advances the state of the art by providing a scalable, end-to-end solution for generating multiple hypotheses—addressing a real-world problem with strong empirical validation. The method’s simplicity and broad applicability (agnostic to architecture/loss) are significant strengths. However, the missing analysis of initialization effects and lack of quantitative diversity metrics weaken the claims about *why* sMCL produces specialized ensembles. The baseline comparison also lacks sufficient detail to isolate the algorithm’s contribution from initialization artifacts.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper is **strongly recommend for acceptance** with minor revisions. The core idea is novel, technically sound, and impactful for practical AI systems. To strengthen it, the authors should: (1) clarify initialization protocols for all baselines, (2) include a comparison with Lee et al. [2015] to contextualize the specialization mechanism, and (3) add diversity metrics (e.g., n-gram diversity for captions, class coverage for segmentation) to complement oracle accuracy. These revisions would fully justify the paper’s claims and solidify its significance. The work aligns with NIPS’ focus on principled, scalable methods for real-world problems and advances the field beyond shallow MCL [8,9] into the deep learning era.
