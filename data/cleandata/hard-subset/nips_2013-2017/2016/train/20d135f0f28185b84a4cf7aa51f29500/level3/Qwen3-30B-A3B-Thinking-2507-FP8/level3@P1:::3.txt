

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by modifying the backpropagation process to have the "winner" (lowest error predictor) take the gradient during training. The authors address the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, where the goal is to produce multiple hypotheses rather than a single prediction, which is particularly valuable in real-world applications with ambiguous inputs. The method is simple to implement, agnostic to architecture and loss function, and parameter-free, training all ensemble members concurrently.

The paper makes a clear connection to prior work on Multiple Choice Learning (Guzman-Rivera et al., 2015; Guzman-Rivera et al., 2016), which proposed an alternating block coordinate descent approach requiring multiple retrainings of ensemble members. The authors rightly identify that this approach is infeasible for modern deep networks, which can take weeks to train. Their contribution is the formulation of a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm that avoids this limitation by interweaving assignment and training steps during the backward pass.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across three diverse tasks: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). For each task, sMCL significantly outperforms classical independent ensembles and the MCL baseline (with 5x speedup), while also outperforming the sequential boosting approach of Dey et al. (2015). The results consistently show that sMCL ensembles achieve higher oracle performance (measuring the best output from the ensemble) compared to baselines, with improvements of 6-10% across tasks. The analysis of ensemble specialization is particularly compelling, showing how members naturally develop different expertise (e.g., class-specific specialists in classification, shape/segmentation specialists in segmentation, and n-gram specialists in captioning) without explicit diversity constraints.

The paper's strengths are clear: the problem addressed is highly relevant to practical AI systems, the method is technically sound and simple to implement, and the evaluation covers three diverse domains with convincing results. The authors effectively demonstrate the broad applicability of their approach.

However, there are notable weaknesses. The paper heavily emphasizes "diversity" in the title and throughout the text, yet the method does not explicitly enforce diversity through any regularization or constraint. The authors show that members become specialized, but this is a consequence of minimizing oracle loss rather than an explicit diversity objective. This is a significant point of confusion given the paper's title and emphasis on diversity. The method appears to be a practical implementation of the MCL framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. (2015), rather than a fundamentally new approach. The key novelty is the adaptation to deep learning with a stochastic optimization approach, but the core MCL objective is not new. This makes the contribution appear somewhat incremental rather than transformative.

The paper has several minor issues that should be addressed: the first sentence of the abstract could be reworded for clarity, the title and text could tone down the emphasis on diversity (since it's not explicitly enforced), and there are a few minor typographical errors (line 108: "f" should be "g"; line 115: extra period). Additionally, the paper doesn't clarify how the baseline MCL ensembles were ensured to have converged—since the authors note that MCL performance drops substantially on segmentation and captioning tasks, it's unclear whether this is due to insufficient meta-iterations or the inherent limitations of the method.

In terms of the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound with strong empirical results, but the lack of explicit diversity enforcement is a concern.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but the diversity emphasis and title could be better aligned with the actual method.
- Originality: The core MCL objective is not new, and the main contribution is the adaptation to deep learning with a stochastic optimization approach, which is a practical but incremental contribution.
- Significance: The problem is important for real-world applications, and the method provides a simple, effective solution that works across multiple domains.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the practical application of Multiple Choice Learning in deep learning, but the emphasis on diversity without explicit enforcement creates a disconnect between the title and the actual method. The authors should clarify this in the revision. The method is simple, effective, and broadly applicable, making it a useful tool for practitioners. While not a breakthrough, it addresses a genuine practical limitation in ensemble learning for deep networks.

The paper should be accepted for NIPS 2016 as it presents a useful, well-evaluated method for an important problem, though the authors should address the diversity emphasis in the title and text to better reflect their actual contribution. The minor issues noted should be fixed in the final version.
