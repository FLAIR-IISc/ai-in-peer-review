## Review of “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles”

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training ensembles of deep networks to produce diverse outputs suitable for scenarios requiring multiple hypotheses. The core idea is to adapt the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8] to deep learning by using a stochastic gradient descent approach, avoiding the costly retraining steps of prior MCL methods. The authors demonstrate sMCL’s applicability across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks, reporting improvements over standard ensembles and comparison to existing methods. 

While the motivation for producing diverse outputs – anticipating downstream oracle evaluation – is clear and relevant, the technical contribution feels incremental. The proposed sMCL reads largely as a straightforward adaptation of the existing MCL framework [8] to a neural network setting, with the key innovation being the use of stochastic gradient descent to circumvent the retraining issue. This is a practical consideration, but doesn’t represent a substantial algorithmic breakthrough. The paper lacks a deeper exploration of *why* this adaptation works well, or what specific properties of deep networks enable its success beyond simply avoiding retraining.

The writing quality is a significant weakness. The paper is often verbose and lacks clarity, with sections, particularly Section 3, being surprisingly short given the technical nature of the proposed method. The distinction between sMCL and standard multi-task learning or regularization techniques that also encourage diversity isn’t sufficiently clarified. The experimental evaluation, while covering a range of tasks, relies on relatively weak baselines. The reported improvements are small, and the lack of comparison to stronger, contemporary methods limits the impact of the results. 

A major concern is the limited scope of the experiments. The authors do not evaluate on the PASCAL VOC 2012 dataset, a standard benchmark for semantic segmentation, despite claiming performance on a related task. Further, comparing against more recent and powerful baselines, such as DeepLab or Mask R-CNN for segmentation, would significantly strengthen the validation of sMCL’s effectiveness.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses a relevant problem (diverse predictions); offers a practical improvement to existing MCL methods by avoiding retraining.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Incremental novelty; poor writing quality; weak baselines and limited experimental validation; lack of clear distinction from related techniques.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents a potentially useful practical adaptation, but the limited novelty, weak experimental validation, and poor presentation do not justify acceptance to a competitive conference like NIPS. The authors should significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, clarify the technical contribution, and improve the writing quality before resubmission.
