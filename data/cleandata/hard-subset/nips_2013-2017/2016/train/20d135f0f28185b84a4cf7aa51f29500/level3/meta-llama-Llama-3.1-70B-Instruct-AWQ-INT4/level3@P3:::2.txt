This paper proposes a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which encourages specialization among ensemble members and yields diverse outputs. The key idea is to use an oracle per instance to select the optimal member and backprop only on that network, effectively implementing a 'winner-take-gradient' training strategy.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the proposed method. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work, highlighting the limitations of traditional single-prediction learners in the face of ambiguity. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, including ensemble learning and multiple-choice learning.

The technical contribution of the paper is the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach, which is well-suited to minimizing the oracle loss in ensembles of deep networks. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The experimental results are impressive, with sMCL significantly outperforming classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

One major omission in the paper is the lack of discussion on how to exploit or use the trained ensemble at inference without the oracle. The authors do not provide any guidance on how to select the optimal member or combine the outputs of multiple members, which is a crucial aspect of deploying the proposed method in practice.

Additionally, the presentation of the pseudocode in Figure 2 as a raster image instead of real text or a vector graphic is a minor issue that detracts from the overall quality of the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to training ensembles of deep networks, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also provide a unique perspective on the problem of ambiguity in perception systems and how to address it through multiple-choice learning.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and the results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications, including computer vision, natural language processing, and robotics.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. However, the lack of discussion on how to exploit or use the trained ensemble at inference without the oracle is a major omission that needs to be addressed.
