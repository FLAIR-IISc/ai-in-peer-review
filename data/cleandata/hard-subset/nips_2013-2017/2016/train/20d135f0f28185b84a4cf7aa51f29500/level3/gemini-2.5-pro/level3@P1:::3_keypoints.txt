- Addresses Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for producing multiple high-quality hypotheses by minimizing oracle loss.  
- Proposes sMCL: a simple SGD-friendly method that trains ensemble members concurrently and backpropagates gradients only to the per-example "winning" model ("winner-takes-the-gradient").  
- sMCL avoids costly retraining of prior MCL methods by performing data-point-to-model assignment on a per-batch basis.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, with significant oracle-performance gains over independent ensembles and baselines.  
- Strength: tackles an important, practical problem where multiple plausible outputs are useful for downstream selection.  
- Strength: method is simple, architecture- and loss-agnostic, introduces no new hyperparameters, and is easy to implement.  
- Strength: comprehensive empirical evaluation with relevant baselines and insightful qualitative analysis of emergent specialization.  
- Weakness: framing emphasizes "diversity" despite no explicit diversity term—diversity is an emergent byproduct of specialization to minimize oracle loss.  
- Weakness: conceptual novelty is incremental—core idea of assigning data to best learner comes from original MCL [8], with sMCL mainly being a stochastic/efficient adaptation.  
- Argument for acceptance: practical, parameter-free, broadly applicable method with strong results and substantial efficiency gains (e.g., ~5x speedup).  
- Argument for acceptance: insightful analysis of specialization patterns (e.g., class-based or n-gram-based) helps explain why the method works.  
- Argument for rejection: limited conceptual novelty relative to original MCL and potentially misleading emphasis on "diversity."  
- Minor comment: rephrase the first abstract sentence for clarity.  
- Minor comment: tone down "diversity" emphasis and present it as an emergent property of oracle-loss minimization.
