- Proposes sMCL: a simple stochastic extension of Multiple Choice Learning that trains ensembles by backpropagating gradients only to the lowest-error predictor per example.  
- Implementation is practical and easy to add to standard deep learning frameworks.  
- Method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and evaluated on classification (CIFAR-10), segmentation (Pascal VOC), and captioning (MSCOCO).  
- Empirically outperforms classical ensembles, prior MCL, and Dey et al.'s method by ~6–10% oracle accuracy and is ~5× faster than MCL.  
- Qualitative analysis shows ensemble members automatically develop interpretable specializations (label clustering, class-specific expertise).  
- Technical approach is sound with clear theoretical motivation and reproducible algorithm descriptions.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and figures effectively illustrate results.  
- Original contribution: novel "winner-take-gradient" training adapted to deep networks, extending MCL in a practical way.  
- Significant practical impact: useful for systems needing multiple hypotheses and broadly applicable across perception tasks.  
- Major weakness: initialization importance is not addressed or clarified across sMCL and baselines.  
- Evaluation limited to oracle accuracy and lacks quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., entropy, coverage).  
- Missing direct comparison with Lee et al. (2015) ("Why M Heads are Better than One"), weakening novelty claims.  
- Recommendation: address initialization details, add diversity analyses, and include comparison to relevant deep-M heads work.  
- Overall: strong, practical contribution with convincing results but needs deeper diversity evaluation and fuller related-work comparison.
