The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" presents a novel approach to training deep neural networks that produce multiple outputs. The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], but propose a stochastic gradient descent (SGD) based algorithm that enables the method to scale to deep neural networks.

The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach, which the authors call Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). This approach is applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent and is agnostic to the form of the task-dependent loss function. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its advantages over existing approaches. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance on various tasks, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning. The results show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the MCL procedure.

One of the major concerns with the paper is that the algorithmic novelty may be only incremental compared to [8]. However, the reviewer is convinced that the small change makes the algorithm more useful and favors acceptance.

To further improve the paper, the reviewer suggests comparing the sMCL algorithm with the MCL algorithm when using batches (rather than the entire dataset). This comparison would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's performance and its advantages over existing approaches.

Additionally, the reviewer requests more analysis of the algorithm's "diversity" effect. The authors provide some qualitative results showing the diversity of the outputs produced by the sMCL ensemble, but a more quantitative analysis would be beneficial in understanding the algorithm's behavior.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and useful approach to training deep neural networks that produce multiple outputs. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance and its advantages over existing approaches. With some additional analysis and comparisons, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of machine learning.
