### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and elegant method for training an ensemble of deep neural networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss" for each training instance. During each step of stochastic gradient descent, a mini-batch is passed through all M members of the ensemble. For each training example, the loss is computed for every member, but the gradient is backpropagated *only* to the single "winner" network that achieved the minimum loss for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" approach encourages individual networks in the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or modes of the data distribution, thereby fostering diversity in their outputs.

The proposed method is a stochastic, parallelized adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. (2012). Unlike the original MCL, which requires costly iterative retraining of models, sMCL integrates the selection step into the standard SGD training loop, making it practical for modern deep learning architectures. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO, showing significant improvements in oracle performance over standard ensembles and other baselines.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed sMCL algorithm is sound, simple to implement, and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is a major strength; it is thorough, well-designed, and spans multiple domains with different network architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs). The results convincingly demonstrate that sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other relevant baselines in terms of oracle metrics. The analysis of the emergent specialization of the ensemble members is particularly insightful, providing clear evidence that the method works as intended (e.g., class-based specialization in classification, n-gram diversity in captioning).

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for generating multiple diverse hypotheses is clearly articulated with compelling examples. The sMCL algorithm is explained precisely and its connection to prior work is well-established. The figures and tables are effective in conveying the main results and analyses. A minor point on clarity is that the pseudocode in Algorithm 2 appears to be a low-resolution raster image rather than typeset text, which detracts from the otherwise professional presentation.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes a solid contribution. While the concept of Multiple Choice Learning is not new, this paper is the first to formulate a practical, stochastic algorithm for applying it to the training of deep ensembles. This is a significant step forward, as previous methods were computationally prohibitive for modern deep networks. The idea of a "winner-take-gradient" update rule within the SGD framework is simple yet novel and powerful.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem: how to generate a diverse set of plausible outputs, which is crucial for applications involving human-in-the-loop systems or downstream re-ranking components. The proposed sMCL method is general, parameter-free, and architecture-agnostic, giving it the potential for wide impact.

However, the paper has one major weakness that significantly limits its practical significance. The entire framework—both training and evaluation—relies on an oracle that identifies the best prediction among the M outputs. The paper provides no discussion or methodology for how to exploit the trained ensemble at inference time in a real-world scenario where the ground truth, and thus the oracle, is unavailable. For any practical application, one needs a mechanism to either select the best hypothesis from the M options or to combine them into a single, improved prediction. Without this crucial component, the proposed method feels incomplete. How is a user or a downstream system supposed to make use of the diverse set of segmentations or captions? This omission leaves a critical gap between the proposed training method and its deployment.

### Arguments for acceptance
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a simple, novel, and effective algorithm (sMCL) for training diverse deep ensembles, which is a significant improvement over the computationally expensive MCL framework.
*   **Pro:** The empirical evidence is strong and comprehensive, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across three different, challenging tasks and architectures.
*   **Pro:** The analysis of how sMCL leads to interpretable, task-dependent specialization among ensemble members is insightful and strengthens the paper's contribution.
*   **Pro:** The problem of generating diverse hypotheses is important for many real-world AI systems.

### Arguments against acceptance
*   **Con:** The paper completely omits the critical question of how to use the trained ensemble at inference time without an oracle. This is a major limitation that hinders the practical applicability and impact of the work, making the contribution feel more theoretical than applied.

### Comments to authors
This is a well-written paper with a clever idea and strong supporting experiments. The sMCL algorithm is an elegant and practical solution for training ensembles to produce diverse outputs by directly optimizing the oracle loss.

My main concern is the lack of discussion regarding inference. You demonstrate that you can train an ensemble that performs well under an oracle, but you do not address how a practitioner would use this ensemble in a real system where no oracle exists. This seems like a critical missing piece. I would strongly encourage you to add a section discussing potential strategies for inference-time deployment. For instance, could one train a separate "re-ranker" or "selector" model? Could uncertainty estimates from the models be used to choose a prediction? Or is the primary use case one where all M outputs are presented to a human user for final selection? Even a preliminary discussion of these possibilities and their trade-offs would make the paper's contribution much more complete and impactful.

Minor suggestion: Please typeset the pseudocode in Algorithm 2 using a proper LaTeX environment (e.g., `algorithmic`) instead of embedding it as an image.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution that is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. Addressing the issue of inference would elevate it from a good paper to an excellent one.
