This paper, "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles," proposes a novel ensemble training algorithm that minimizes oracle loss by training ensemble members concurrently using stochastic gradient descent. The algorithm, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on various tasks and architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand their contributions. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a novel solution to the problem of training diverse deep ensembles. The authors' approach is simple yet effective, and their experiments demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the results, including qualitative and quantitative evaluations, which helps to build confidence in the authors' claims.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. For example, the testing phase is unclear, and the authors' use of ground truth to select the best member is not well-explained. Additionally, the terms "independent ensembles" and "regular ensembles" are not well-defined, and their relationship to the authors' proposed method is not clear.

Furthermore, the authors' claim that their method induces specialization among ensemble members is not convincingly justified. While the authors provide some evidence of specialization, it is not clear whether this is a result of the training algorithm or other factors. The authors could strengthen their argument by providing more rigorous analysis of the specialization phenomenon.

Finally, the authors do not discuss related work on diversity regularization in neural networks, which is a closely related and important area of research. This omission is notable, as the authors' method could be seen as a form of diversity regularization.

Overall, I recommend weak acceptance of this paper. While it makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, there are some areas where it could be improved. With some revisions to address the issues mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a major impact in the field.
