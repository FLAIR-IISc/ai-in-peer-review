- Introduces sMCL, a method that trains deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss by letting an oracle pick the best prediction from multiple outputs.  
- Generalizes prior MCL to deep networks using a stochastic block SGD that avoids costly retraining cycles.  
- sMCL updates gradients only for the lowest-error predictor per example during SGD, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members.  
- Method is architecture-agnostic, parameter-free, and reported to be about 5× faster than MCL.  
- Validated on CIFAR-10 (classification), Pascal VOC (segmentation), and MSCOCO (captioning) with consistent oracle accuracy gains of 6–10% over baselines.  
- Demonstrates emergent interpretable specialization (e.g., ensemble members clustering by class or semantic expertise).  
- Strength: technically simple, theoretically grounded, and integrates seamlessly with standard SGD/no auxiliary losses.  
- Strength: broad, convincing experiments across CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNNs, with standard oracle evaluation metrics.  
- Weakness: paper omits analysis of initialization effects and does not test with/pretrained weights (e.g., ImageNet) which may affect specialization.  
- Weakness: baseline initialization details are ambiguous (MCL and Dey et al.) and may bias comparisons if not matched.  
- Weakness: claims of "diverse solutions" lack quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., n-gram uniqueness, entropy, class coverage).  
- Missing comparison with Lee et al. (2015) to contextualize differences from other multi-head/diversity-focused methods.  
- Overall: high-quality, scalable, original contribution bridging MCL and deep learning, but missing analyses weaken some claims.  
- Recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions—clarify baseline initialization, compare to Lee et al., and add quantitative diversity metrics.
