This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a set of multiple, high-quality hypotheses for a given input. This setting, known as Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), aims to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best prediction within the set. The authors propose a novel and simple training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), which is well-suited for deep networks trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea is to train all ensemble members concurrently. During the backward pass for each training example, the gradient of the loss is backpropagated only to the single "winning" model that achieved the lowest loss on that example. This "winner-takes-the-gradient" strategy effectively performs the data-point-to-model assignment step of traditional MCL on a per-batch basis, avoiding the costly retraining steps required by previous methods like Guzman-Rivera et al. [8]. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of sMCL across three diverse and challenging tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing significant improvements in oracle performance over independently trained ensembles and other baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses an Important Problem:** The task of generating multiple plausible outputs rather than a single one is highly relevant for many real-world applications where ambiguity is inherent and a human-in-the-loop or downstream module can select the best option. Adapting the MCL framework to the context of modern, computationally expensive deep networks is a timely and valuable contribution to the community.

2.  **Simple and General Method:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant in its simplicity. It is easy to implement (conceptually a single layer that routes gradients), introduces no new hyperparameters, and is agnostic to the specific network architecture or loss function. This generality is a major strength and is convincingly demonstrated by its successful application to CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNN models.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper's empirical evaluation is a key strength. The authors test their method on three distinct and important problems, showcasing its versatility. The experiments are well-designed, including relevant baselines such as independently trained ensembles, the original MCL algorithm [8], and a more recent boosting-style approach [5]. The results consistently show that sMCL outperforms these baselines, often by a significant margin, while being much more computationally efficient than the original MCL. The qualitative results and analysis of emergent model specialization provide compelling insights into *why* the method works.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Framing vs. Mechanism:** The paper places heavy emphasis on "diversity" in its title and motivation. However, the sMCL objective does not explicitly enforce diversity between the outputs. Instead, it encourages specialization by minimizing the oracle loss, from which diversity emerges as a byproduct. While the outcome is desirable, this framing could be seen as slightly misleading. Other works on generating diverse solutions often include explicit diversity-promoting terms in the objective function. A more precise framing might focus on "training specialized ensembles to minimize oracle loss."

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The core idea of assigning each data point to the best-performing learner is the central concept of the original MCL framework [8]. The main technical contribution of sMCL is to adapt this from a block coordinate descent procedure (alternating full assignment and retraining steps) to a stochastic, online version integrated into SGD. While this adaptation is crucial for making the approach feasible for deep learning and represents a significant practical and algorithmic advance, the underlying conceptual leap from prior work could be viewed as incremental.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a simple, practical, and effective solution to an important problem: training deep ensembles for multiple-choice prediction.
*   The method is general, parameter-free, and widely applicable, as demonstrated by strong results across three different domains and architectures.
*   The efficiency gains over prior MCL work are substantial (e.g., a reported 5x speedup), making this the first truly practical algorithm for this task in the deep learning era.
*   The analysis of how different forms of specialization emerge (e.g., class-based for classification, n-gram-based for captioning) is insightful.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The conceptual novelty over the original MCL formulation [8] is arguably limited; the main contribution is an efficient, stochastic optimization strategy for the same core idea.
*   The framing around "diversity" is not fully backed by the technical mechanism, which is about specialization to minimize oracle loss.

**Minor Comments & Questions for Authors:**

*   The first sentence of the abstract is slightly convoluted. Consider rephrasing for clarity, for instance: "Many practical perception systems are components of larger processes that can evaluate multiple hypotheses. In such contexts, it is beneficial..."
*   Given the weakness discussed above, I would suggest slightly toning down the emphasis on "diversity" as the primary goal throughout the text and instead highlighting it as a positive emergent property of training specialized experts that minimize oracle loss.
*   Typo on line 108: The function being learned is `g`, so the definition should start with `g(x) = ...`, not `f(x) = ...`.
*   Typo on line 115: There is an extra period in the middle of the sentence in the objective function definition.
*   **Question:** The original MCL [8] requires models to be trained to convergence in its inner loop. How was convergence determined for the MCL baseline in your deep learning experiments? Deep networks can take a very long time to fully converge. If the training for each meta-iteration was stopped early, this could have unfairly handicapped the MCL baseline's performance. Could you please clarify the training schedule used for this baseline?

Overall, I believe this is a strong paper. The weaknesses are relatively minor compared to the strengths. The simplicity, practicality, and excellent empirical results make this a valuable contribution that is likely to be useful to and built upon by many researchers. I recommend acceptance.
