This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training ensembles of deep networks to generate multiple, diverse outputs for scenarios involving an oracle (e.g., human-in-the-loop systems). The core idea is to directly minimize oracle loss via a stochastic block gradient descent approach, offering a computationally efficient alternative to previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over classical ensembles and other baselines.

The work builds on the MCL paradigm introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. (2014, 2015) but addresses a critical limitation of their approach: the impracticality of retraining models multiple times, especially with deep networks. sMCL cleverly bypasses this by formulating the problem in a way that allows for concurrent training of ensemble members using standard stochastic gradient descent. This is a significant contribution, as it makes MCL tractable for modern deep learning architectures. Related work in ensemble learning, such as resampling methods (Liu & Yao, 2007) and diversity-inducing losses, are acknowledged, but sMCL distinguishes itself by directly optimizing for oracle performance. Approaches generating multiple solutions from a single model (e.g., Kirillov et al., 2016) are also discussed, highlighting that sMCL’s focus on an ensemble provides additional benefits in terms of diversity and specialization.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. The derivation of the stochastic gradient descent update rule is clear, and the algorithm’s simplicity is a major advantage.
* **Novelty:** sMCL presents a genuinely novel approach to training diverse ensembles by directly optimizing for oracle loss in a computationally efficient manner. It bridges a gap between the theoretical benefits of MCL and the practical constraints of deep learning.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling, consistently demonstrating the superiority of sMCL over classical ensembles and existing baselines across diverse tasks and architectures.
* **Interpretability:** The analysis revealing interpretable specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class-wise clustering in image classification) adds valuable insight and demonstrates the method’s potential for understanding model behavior.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts, algorithms, and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The authors acknowledge the importance of initialization but do not thoroughly investigate its effect. It is unclear whether the observed performance gains are consistent across different initialization schemes (e.g., random vs. pre-trained weights).  This is a crucial aspect, as the sensitivity of deep networks to initialization is well-known.
* **Baseline Comparison:** A key concern is the comparability of the baseline experiments. The paper does not explicitly state whether the classical ensembles and MCL baselines used the *same* pre-trained initialization as the sMCL ensembles. If sMCL benefited from a more favorable initialization, the reported performance gains could be overstated.
* **Lack of Diversity Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate improved oracle accuracy, they provide limited quantitative analysis of ensemble diversity. Metrics such as pairwise distance between outputs or entropy of the ensemble’s predictions would offer a more comprehensive understanding of the generated solutions' diversity.  The qualitative examples are helpful but not sufficient.
* **Related Work Discussion:** The comparison to Lee et al. (2015) ("Why M Heads are Better than One") is warranted.  Lee et al. also explore multiple outputs, albeit through a different mechanism (multiple output heads in a single network). A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between sMCL and such approaches would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning: generating diverse and informative predictions for systems that involve human interaction or oracle feedback.
* The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and computationally efficient.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches.
* The interpretability analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the learned ensembles.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a thorough investigation of initialization sensitivity raises concerns about the robustness of the results.
* The comparability of the baseline experiments is unclear, potentially inflating the reported performance gains.
* The limited quantitative analysis of ensemble diversity leaves a gap in the evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL method is a practical and effective way to train diverse deep ensembles for scenarios involving oracle feedback. While the concerns regarding initialization, baseline comparison, and diversity analysis are valid, they do not fundamentally undermine the paper’s contributions.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing these points – specifically, a more detailed discussion of the initialization procedure and a quantitative assessment of ensemble diversity. The authors should clearly state whether baselines utilized the same initialization as sMCL.
