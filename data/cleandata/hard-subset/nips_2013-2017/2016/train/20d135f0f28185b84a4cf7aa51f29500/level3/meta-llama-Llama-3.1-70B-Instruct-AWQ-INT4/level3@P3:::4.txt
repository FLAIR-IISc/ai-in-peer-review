This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient descent based approach to minimize the loss with respect to an oracle, introducing Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) for training diverse deep ensembles. The main idea is to pose the task of producing multiple outputs as a learning problem over an ensemble of deep networks, which is beneficial in practical perception systems that include interactions with users or additional components capable of evaluating the quality of predicted solutions.

The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, highlighting the limitations of traditional supervised learning in scenarios where the goal is to generate a set of plausible hypotheses for a downstream process. They also provide a comprehensive review of related work, including ensemble learning and generating multiple solutions, and position their approach as a generalization of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm.

The technical contribution of the paper is the formulation of a stochastic block gradient descent optimization approach, which is applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent and is agnostic to the form of the task-dependent loss. The authors demonstrate the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The experiments show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles and other strong baselines, including the 5x slower MCL procedure. The authors also provide analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

However, the paper has some limitations. The method reads like a neural-network adaptation of prior work [8] with minor adjustments, and the distinction between the proposed framework and widely used alternative/multi-task training is not clarified. The technical section (Sec. 3) is too short, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the optimization approach and its advantages over existing methods.

Additionally, the experiments cover a range of tasks, but the baselines used are weak, and the improvements shown are relatively small. It would be beneficial to evaluate the method on more challenging datasets, such as PASCAL VOC 2012, and compare against stronger baselines to validate its effectiveness.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the optimization approach and its convergence properties. The authors are careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work, but the paper could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the approach.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but some sections could be improved. The introduction and related work sections are clear and concise, but the technical section could benefit from more detail and explanation. The experimental section is clear, but the results could be presented in a more concise and easy-to-understand manner.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles, but the method is not significantly different from prior work [8]. The authors do provide a new perspective on the problem, but the approach is not revolutionary.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of computer vision, and the results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential impact and applications of the approach.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear contribution to the field of computer vision. However, it could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the optimization approach, stronger baselines, and a more critical discussion of the limitations of the approach.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The method is applicable to a wide range of problem domains and network architectures.
* The experiments show significant improvements over classical ensembles and other strong baselines.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The method is not significantly different from prior work [8].
* The technical section is too short and lacks detail.
* The experiments use weak baselines and show relatively small improvements.
* The paper could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the approach.

Recommendation:

* Evaluate the method on more challenging datasets, such as PASCAL VOC 2012.
* Compare against stronger baselines to validate the effectiveness of the approach.
* Provide a more detailed analysis of the optimization approach and its convergence properties.
* Discuss the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future work.
