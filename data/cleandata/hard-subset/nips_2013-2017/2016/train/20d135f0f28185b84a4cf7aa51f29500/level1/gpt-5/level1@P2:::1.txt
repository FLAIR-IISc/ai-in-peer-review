Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) problem—training an ensemble to minimize the oracle loss, i.e., the per-example minimum loss across M predictions. Prior MCL approaches (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al.) alternate between assigning each example to its best model and retraining members to convergence, which is impractical for modern deep nets. The paper introduces Stochastic MCL (sMCL), a simple, SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” approach: for each example in a minibatch, only the lowest-loss model receives the gradient; others get zero. This is essentially a hard-EM/block-coordinate descent variant implemented at the stochastic batch level, making it practical for deep ensembles and structured losses.

The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, and is demonstrated on three tasks: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM). sMCL outperforms independent ensembles, a boosting-like diversity method (Dey et al.), and a 5-iteration MCL baseline (with large speedup over MCL). Qualitative analyses show emergent specialization (by class for classification; by structure and class tendencies for segmentation; and by n-gram/style for captioning). The paper relates to ensemble learning for diversity, diverse M-best inference in structured models, and “network of experts” designs; its main novelty is a practical optimization for oracle loss in deep nets.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The objective reformulation via assignment indicators is standard; the gradient masking to the minimum-loss predictor per example is a straightforward stochastic approximation to minimizing the upper bound. The approach is simple and implementable. While there is no convergence analysis (and the min is non-smooth), the method is consistent with hard-EM/k-means style updates and shows stable empirical behavior.
- Empirical support: Results span three representative tasks/architectures, show consistent oracle metric gains (typically 6–10% relative over independent ensembles), and substantial training speed advantages vs. alternating MCL. Analyses of specialization and a k-winner variant (interpolating between oracle and standard ensemble) are informative. However:
  - Statistical variability is not reported (no error bars or multiple runs).
  - Some baselines are missing or only partially addressed (see weaknesses).
  - Runtime overhead vs. independent ensembles is not quantified (only vs. MCL).
- Experimental details are mostly sufficient for reproduction (architectures, datasets, training schedules) though more clarity on segmentation splits and tie-breaking would help.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for oracle loss is compelling, the algorithm is concisely described (pseudocode helpful), and qualitative figures are illustrative. Minor clarifications suggested: how ties are broken; safeguards against expert starvation; exact loss used per task; and more detail on the Pascal subset and initialization protocol for fair baseline comparison.

Originality:
The core idea—SGD with per-example hard assignment so only the best expert gets the gradient—is conceptually close to hard mixture-of-experts/gating and to prior MCL’s alternating assignments. The novelty lies in bringing this to deep ensembles with a simple, concurrent, parameter-free training rule applicable to arbitrary differentiable losses and structured outputs. This fills a practical gap left by retraining/sequential methods. Overall, moderate novelty with high practical value.

Significance:
The setting (producing multiple plausible hypotheses) is important for ambiguous, structured tasks. A general, simple method that reliably improves oracle metrics across vision and language tasks is useful and likely to be adopted. Demonstrated emergent specialization is an appealing byproduct. The main limitation is that improvements are shown on oracle metrics without downstream selection/reranking or human-in-the-loop evaluation; nevertheless, oracle performance is a standard proxy in this literature. The captioning comparison indicates a single-model beam search can match/exceed oracle in some settings; still, ensemble diversity and unique n-grams suggest complementary benefits.

Strengths:
- Simple, general, parameter-free training rule; easy to retrofit into existing SGD pipelines.
- Concurrent training with large wall-clock savings over alternating MCL; avoids sequential/boosting retraining.
- Consistent improvements across classification, segmentation, and captioning; qualitative diversity and interpretable specialization.
- Insightful analysis (k-winner ablation, specialization visualizations).

Weaknesses and questions:
- Relation to mixture-of-experts with learned gating (including hard gating) is not deeply compared; a baseline with a learned router (e.g., generalist + experts) would be informative.
- Risk of expert starvation or collapse early in training; no explicit mechanism or quantitative analysis of assignment entropy/coverage. Did authors observe/mitigate starvation (e.g., warmup, epsilon-assignments)?
- Limited baselines: no comparison to multi-head shared-trunk models, snapshot ensembles, or diversity-inducing regularizers (e.g., negative correlation). For captioning, diverse beam search or diverse M-best decoding from a single model would be relevant.
- Evaluation focuses on oracle metrics; no downstream selection module experiments (human or learned reranker). This would strengthen practical significance claims.
- Missing variance/runtime details and larger-scale classification (e.g., ImageNet top-k).
- In captioning, a single-model beam sometimes achieves higher oracle; fairness of beam sizes across methods could be discussed further, and combining sMCL with beam search should be quantified.

Pro acceptance:
- Practical, broadly applicable method with clear empirical gains and speed advantage.
- Addresses a relevant problem (multi-hypothesis prediction) with a clean idea and solid demonstrations.
- Emergent interpretable specialization is compelling.

Con acceptance:
- Incremental conceptual novelty relative to hard MoE/MCL; some important baselines missing.
- Oracle-only evaluation; limited analysis of training dynamics and robustness (starvation, initialization).
- Mixed captioning results vs. beam search; limited scale and statistical reporting.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept). The method’s simplicity, generality, and consistent empirical benefits outweigh the incremental novelty and missing baselines. With minor revisions addressing comparisons to gating/MoE, starvation analysis, and expanded evaluations, this would be a strong contribution.
