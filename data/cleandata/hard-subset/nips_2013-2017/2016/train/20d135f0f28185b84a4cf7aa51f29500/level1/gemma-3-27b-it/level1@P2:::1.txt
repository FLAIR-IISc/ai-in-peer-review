This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss. The core idea is elegantly simple: during stochastic gradient descent, gradients are only backpropagated to the ensemble member that achieves the lowest loss on each example. This contrasts with previous methods like Guzman-Rivera et al. (2016) [8, 9], which require iterative retraining or sequential learning, making them impractical for large deep networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing improvements over existing methods and insightful visualizations of emergent expertise within the ensemble.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a compelling solution to a relevant problem. The theoretical justification for sMCL – deriving the gradient update rule and showing its equivalence to a constrained optimization problem – is clear and convincing. The experimental results across diverse tasks and architectures strongly support the claims. The authors carefully evaluate the method against appropriate baselines (independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al. [5]) and acknowledge the limitations of each. The ablation studies and qualitative analysis (e.g., visualization of specialization in segmentation) add further depth to the evaluation. The paper is a complete piece of work, going beyond simply proposing an algorithm and thoroughly investigating its properties.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of multiple-choice learning and explains the benefits of diverse ensembles. The technical details of sMCL are presented clearly, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, allowing for reproducibility. However, some sections (especially the related work) could benefit from a more concise presentation. For instance, the description of related work feels somewhat lengthy and could focus more tightly on the most relevant contributions.

**Originality:** The problem of learning diverse ensembles is not entirely new, but sMCL offers a significant contribution in terms of practicality and scalability. The key innovation lies in the formulation of a stochastic gradient descent approach that avoids the computational bottleneck of previous methods.  The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing approaches (Guzman-Rivera et al., Dey et al.) and clearly articulate the advantages of sMCL. The application of this technique to a broad range of tasks and architectures further highlights its originality. While the core idea of "winner-take-all" gradient updates may have parallels in other contexts, its application to this specific problem and the resulting benefits are novel.

**Significance:** The results are important because they provide a practical solution for training diverse deep ensembles, which are valuable in scenarios where multiple plausible hypotheses are desired (e.g., robotics, human-computer interaction). The demonstrated improvements in oracle performance across different tasks suggest that sMCL could have a tangible impact on real-world applications. The insight that ensembles trained with sMCL exhibit emergent specialization is also significant, as it suggests a mechanism for building more interpretable and robust AI systems. The method’s generality – applicability to any SGD-trained model and loss function – further enhances its potential impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty & Practicality:** sMCL offers a significant improvement over existing methods for training diverse ensembles, addressing a key limitation in scalability.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across diverse tasks and architectures.
*   **Clear Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a well-justified and clearly explained algorithm.
*   **Interpretability:** The analysis of emergent specialization provides valuable insights into the behavior of the trained ensembles.
*   **Generality:** Applicable to a wide range of models and loss functions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Related Work could be more focused:** The related work section is a bit verbose and could benefit from a tighter focus on the most relevant contributions.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the paper demonstrates strong performance overall, there is limited discussion of scenarios where sMCL might not be effective or where its benefits are diminished.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and deserves to be accepted to NIPS 2016. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a novel and effective solution, supported by thorough experimental evaluation and insightful analysis. The clarity of the presentation and the significance of the results make this a valuable addition to the NIPS program.  I would rate this as a **Strong Accept**.
