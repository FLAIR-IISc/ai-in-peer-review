This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), for training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss" over the ensemble, where for each training example, the loss is defined by the single best-performing member of the ensemble. This is a direct adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8], which used a block coordinate descent approach requiring full model retraining. The key contribution of this work is a practical algorithm for the deep learning setting: a simple "winner-take-gradient" rule within Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). For each example in a mini-batch, only the model that produces the lowest loss receives a gradient update. This simple mechanism encourages different models to specialize on different parts of the data distribution, fostering diversity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and generality of sMCL across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO, showing significant improvements in oracle performance over standard ensembles and other relevant baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL method is remarkably simple and elegant. It is easy to implement, introduces no new hyperparameters, and is agnostic to the network architecture and the specific loss function. This makes it a highly practical and broadly applicable tool for practitioners and researchers.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a convincing and comprehensive set of experiments across three very different domains (classification, segmentation, captioning). The consistent and significant improvements over standard independent ensembles, the original MCL algorithm, and a sequential boosting-like approach [5] robustly demonstrate the method's efficacy.
*   **Novelty and Significance:** While the concept of minimizing oracle loss comes from prior work [8], its adaptation into a stochastic, concurrent training algorithm for deep networks is a critical and novel contribution. The original MCL was computationally infeasible for modern deep learning. This paper makes the idea practical and accessible. Generating diverse hypotheses is a fundamentally important problem for systems that interact with humans or other components, and this paper provides a powerful, general-purpose solution that can directly model ambiguity in data.
*   **Insightful Analysis of Specialization:** A major strength of the paper is the analysis of the "interpretable expertise" that emerges from sMCL training. The visualizations and statistics showing how ensemble members specialize (e.g., on specific classes in CIFAR-10, or on different sentence structures in MSCOCO) provide valuable insight into the learning dynamics and demonstrate that the method works as intended. This analysis elevates the paper beyond a simple presentation of improved metrics.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is described concisely, and the connection to prior work is well-established. The experimental setup and results are presented in a clear and easy-to-follow manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Handling of Loss Ties:** The algorithm states that ties for the minimum loss predictor are broken arbitrarily. It would be helpful to discuss how frequently ties occur, especially early in training when models may be similar, and whether the tie-breaking strategy has any noticeable impact on the final performance or specialization patterns.
*   **Initialization for Segmentation:** The semantic segmentation experiment initializes the sMCL ensemble from a standard ensemble that has already been trained for 50 epochs. While this is a reasonable fine-tuning strategy, it would strengthen the paper to clarify if this pre-training step is necessary for good performance or simply a way to accelerate convergence. A comparison with training from random initialization (even for a reduced number of epochs) would help assess the robustness of the method.
*   **Inference Cost:** While the training-time speedup over MCL is a key advantage, the paper does not discuss the inference-time cost. Like any M-member ensemble, the proposed method requires M forward passes, which is a significant computational consideration for deployment. A brief mention of this trade-off would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Comparison with Beam Search in Captioning:** In the image captioning results (Table 1), a single model using beam search with a beam width of 5 achieves a higher oracle CIDEr score than the sMCL ensemble of size 5. The authors rightly point out that sMCL produces more diverse n-grams. However, the comparison could be made more direct and compelling by also applying beam search to each member of the sMCL ensemble and then reporting the oracle score over the union of all generated captions. This would demonstrate the combined power of sMCL's model diversity and beam search's decoding diversity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a simple, novel, and highly effective method for a significant problem in machine learning.
*   **Pro:** The method's generality is convincingly demonstrated on a diverse set of challenging tasks and deep learning architectures.
*   **Pro:** The experiments are thorough, with strong baselines and insightful analysis that goes beyond just reporting metrics.
*   **Pro:** The work is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to understand, making it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The core conceptual idea of minimizing oracle loss is not new, though its practical adaptation for deep learning is.
*   **Con:** Some experimental details (e.g., initialization strategy, handling of ties) could be further clarified to assess the method's full robustness.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a valuable and practical contribution. The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple, elegant, and demonstrably effective. The strengths, particularly the method's generality, strong empirical results, and insightful analysis, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This work is likely to be influential and widely used by researchers and practitioners working with deep learning models in ambiguous, real-world settings. I strongly recommend acceptance.
