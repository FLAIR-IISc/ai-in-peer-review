

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple high-quality predictions rather than single outputs. The authors extend the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8,9] to deep networks by proposing a stochastic gradient descent-based training algorithm that avoids the costly retraining required by previous methods. Unlike existing ensemble approaches that focus on diversity through data sampling or joint loss functions [2, 12, 19, 21, 26, 27], sMCL directly optimizes for oracle loss (minimum loss over ensemble members) through a "winner-take-gradient" mechanism, where only the lowest-error predictor receives gradient updates for each example.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across three distinct domains: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). sMCL consistently outperforms classical ensembles, MCL [8], and Dey et al. [5] (6-10% improvement) while being 5x faster than MCL. The authors provide compelling evidence that sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members—e.g., classification specialists for specific object classes, segmentation experts for particular object types, and captioning models with distinct linguistic patterns.

Strengths: The method is technically sound, broadly applicable, and well-validated across diverse tasks. The "winner-take-gradient" approach is elegantly simple yet effective. The paper provides clear analysis of ensemble specialization, demonstrating how sMCL naturally discovers task-specific expertise without manual design.

Weaknesses: While the paper shows sMCL is 5x faster than MCL, it doesn't compare computational costs against standard ensemble training (which trains all models in parallel). The analysis of why sMCL induces specialization could be deeper—e.g., through theoretical analysis of the optimization dynamics.

Quality: High—rigorous experiments with strong baselines, clear evaluation metrics (oracle loss), and honest discussion of limitations.

Clarity: Excellent—well-organized, clearly written, with effective figures illustrating key concepts.

Originality: Significant—first practical method for training diverse deep ensembles via direct oracle loss minimization, distinct from prior work on ensemble diversity.

Significance: High—addresses a practical need in real-world AI systems where multiple plausible predictions are valuable, with broad applicability across vision and NLP tasks.

Pro: Novel, practical, well-validated method with clear advantages over existing approaches; provides interpretable ensemble specialization.

Con: Limited discussion of computational cost relative to standard ensemble training; theoretical understanding of specialization emergence could be strengthened.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to ensemble learning with practical implications for real-world AI systems, meeting all NIPS quality criteria with strong technical merit and significance. Accept.
