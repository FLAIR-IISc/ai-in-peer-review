This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training approach for deep ensembles designed to produce diverse outputs, particularly beneficial when a downstream oracle evaluates the quality of predictions. The core idea is to leverage stochastic gradient descent to minimize oracle loss directly, avoiding the costly retraining steps of prior methods like Guzman-Rivera et al. (2015). The approach is presented as parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, and demonstrably effective across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of needing multiple plausible hypotheses in real-world perception systems is clearly articulated and relatable. The introduction effectively contrasts Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) with standard supervised learning, highlighting scenarios where diversity in predictions is crucial – implicit class confusion, ambiguous evidence, and mode bias. The framing of the problem as minimizing oracle loss is sensible.

The technical contribution, sMCL, is significant. Formulating the oracle loss minimization as a stochastic block gradient descent problem is a clever and practical solution. The key insight that gradients are effectively zeroed out for incorrect predictions enables concurrent training of ensemble members, a substantial improvement over the iterative retraining of previous MCL approaches (Guzman-Rivera et al., 2015). The paper clearly explains the mathematical derivation and the algorithm itself (Algorithm 2).

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors demonstrate sMCL's effectiveness on three distinct tasks—image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO)—using different network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN).  Comparison with baselines, including independent ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. (2015), is thorough and shows consistent improvements with sMCL. The inclusion of qualitative results, particularly the visualization of specialization in image classification and the diverse segmentations and captions, adds valuable insight. The analysis of oracle assignments to showcase emergent expertise is also compelling.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the claim of being "parameter-free" is largely true, the standard SGD hyperparameters (learning rate, momentum, weight decay, batch size) still need tuning, and their impact on sMCL’s performance isn't thoroughly explored. A more detailed ablation study focusing on these hyperparameters could strengthen the analysis. The comparison to Dey et al. (2015) feels a bit cursory. The authors state that it saturates quickly, but providing a more detailed comparison of training curves and computational costs would be more convincing. Furthermore, the explanation of *why* sMCL leads to specialization could be expanded. While the authors observe this phenomenon, the underlying mechanisms aren’t deeply investigated. Does the initialization, the specific architecture, or the loss function play a crucial role?

The writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from dense mathematical notation without sufficient intuitive explanation. For instance, the derivation of the gradient in Equation (2) could benefit from more elaboration. While the related work section is adequate, it doesn't fully contextualize sMCL within the broader literature of diversity-promoting ensemble methods beyond directly minimizing oracle loss. Approaches that encourage diversity through techniques like dropout, adversarial training, or variational inference are not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** sMCL presents a genuinely novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss via a stochastic gradient descent framework.
*   **Significance:** The method addresses a practically relevant problem (multiple hypothesis generation) and demonstrates consistent performance improvements across a variety of tasks and architectures.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation and algorithmic implementation appear well-reasoned and supported by experimental results.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is largely well-written and explains the core concepts effectively.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The impact of standard SGD hyperparameters on sMCL's performance is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Superficial Comparison to some Baselines:** The comparison to Dey et al. (2015) could be more detailed.
*   **Incomplete Discussion of Specialization Mechanisms:** The paper lacks a deeper investigation into *why* sMCL leads to the observed specialization patterns.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and should be accepted. The proposed method, sMCL, is a clever and effective solution to a relevant problem. The experimental results are convincing, and the paper is generally well-written and clear. While there are some areas for improvement (e.g., a more thorough ablation study and a deeper analysis of specialization mechanisms), these weaknesses do not outweigh the significant contributions of the work. This paper presents a practical and theoretically sound approach to training diverse deep ensembles, and it has the potential to be highly impactful in areas where multiple plausible hypotheses are required.
