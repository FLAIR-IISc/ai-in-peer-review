- Introduces sMCL, a stochastic SGD-based method where only the lowest-error predictor per example receives gradient updates to train diverse deep ensembles producing multiple hypotheses.  
- Solves key limitations of prior MCL methods by enabling concurrent training without costly retraining or sequential procedures.  
- Builds on Guzman-Rivera et al. (MCL) and Dey et al., but is the first practical approach for deep networks under the MCL paradigm.  
- Derivation of the gradient update rule (Equation 2) is correct and well-justified.  
- Thorough experiments on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO show consistent 6–10% oracle metric improvements over baselines.  
- Ablation study on k provides insight into the tradeoff between specialization and accuracy.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear notation, reproducible experimental details, and effective figures (e.g., class-wise specialization).  
- Novelty lies in the simple, elegant "winner-take-gradient" mechanism that is architecture- and loss-agnostic.  
- Significant practical impact: applicable across tasks, parameter-free, efficient (≈5× faster than MCL), and yields interpretable ensemble specializations.  
- Strengths: solves a meaningful problem simply, demonstrates broad gains, provides specialization analysis, and is easy to implement.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical analysis explaining why specialization emerges, unclear scaling to very large ensembles (M≫100), and some MCL comparisons limited by compute.  
- Pro arguments: important problem, novel/simple/effective solution, broad applicability, clear evidence and practical utility.  
- Con arguments: limited theoretical justification, constrained comparisons due to compute, no exploration of very large ensemble scaling.  
- Overall: technically sound, novel, significant, and well-evaluated work that advances training diverse deep ensembles and is well-suited for NIPS.
