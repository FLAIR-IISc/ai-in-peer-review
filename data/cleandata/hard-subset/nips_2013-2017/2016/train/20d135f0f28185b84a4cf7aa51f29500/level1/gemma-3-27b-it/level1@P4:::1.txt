The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss. The key idea is to implement a ‘winner-take-gradient’ approach during stochastic gradient descent, effectively focusing updates only on the ensemble member that performs best for a given example. The authors claim this is parameter-free, agnostic to architecture and loss function, and more efficient than previous methods like MCL. They demonstrate its effectiveness on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

This work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [Guzman-Rivera et al., 2015, 2016], which seeks to learn a mapping that produces multiple hypotheses to minimize oracle loss.  Prior work on MCL involved iterative retraining of ensemble members, which is computationally expensive for deep networks. sMCL addresses this by formulating the problem as a stochastic block gradient descent, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members.  Related work includes ensemble learning techniques focused on diversity [Liu & Yao, 2000], generating multiple solutions from single models [Kirillov et al., 2016], and submodular optimization approaches [Dey et al., 2017].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The sMCL approach provides a novel and efficient solution to training diverse deep ensembles for minimizing oracle loss, a problem previously hampered by the computational cost of iterative retraining methods. The formulation as a stochastic block gradient descent is a significant contribution.
*   **Generality:** The method is claimed to be agnostic to both architecture and loss function, making it broadly applicable to different deep learning tasks. The experiments support this claim with results across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
*   **Efficiency:** The concurrent training of ensemble members significantly reduces training time compared to previous MCL approaches, as highlighted by the 5x speedup compared to the original MCL.
*   **Empirical Results:**  The experimental results consistently demonstrate that sMCL outperforms classical ensembles and other baselines (including MCL and Dey et al.’s method) across all three tasks.
*   **Interpretability:** The analysis reveals emergent specialization among ensemble members, offering insights into how the diversity is achieved. The examples provided showing specialized expertise are compelling.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, clearly explaining the problem, proposed method, and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the authors compare to several baselines, the comparison to more recent and advanced ensemble diversity techniques could be strengthened.  Specifically, exploring methods incorporating dropout variations or adversarial training for diversity might provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Although the authors claim the method is parameter-free, the standard training parameters for the underlying models (learning rate, batch size, etc.) are hyperparameters that still require tuning. This is not explicitly acknowledged as a limitation.
*   **Detailed Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies could help understand the specific contributions of each component of the sMCL approach. For example, an analysis of the impact of the number of ensemble members (M) on performance and diversity would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for why sMCL performs well. While the authors provide an intuitive explanation based on the gradient update rule, a more formal analysis of the optimization landscape could provide further insights.
*   **Figure Clarity**: While generally good, some figures (e.g. 4b) are difficult to interpret without careful attention. Larger font sizes and clearer labelling would improve readability.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   The proposed method addresses a significant challenge in multiple-choice learning and offers a practical solution.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the superiority of sMCL over existing approaches.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is limited, and a more formal justification for the method's effectiveness would be beneficial.
*   The comparison to strong baselines could be expanded to include more recent ensemble diversity techniques.
*   The paper does not fully address the sensitivity of the underlying model hyperparameters.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL method is novel, efficient, and effective, and the experimental results are compelling. While the paper could benefit from a stronger theoretical analysis and a more comprehensive comparison to baselines, the strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
