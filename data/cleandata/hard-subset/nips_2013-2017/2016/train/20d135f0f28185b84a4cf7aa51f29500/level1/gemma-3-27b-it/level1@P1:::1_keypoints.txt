- Proposes sMCL: a “winner-take-gradient” SGD method for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss.  
- Avoids costly retraining of prior MCL methods, making MCL practical for modern deep networks.  
- Well-motivated framing: diverse predictions are important for oracle/human-in-the-loop applications.  
- Related work is adequately covered, positioning sMCL relative to ensemble and multiple-choice learning literature.  

Strengths:
- Novelty: sMCL is a clever, practical adaptation of MCL to deep networks.  
- Generality: architecture-agnostic and applicable to various loss functions.  
- Empirical results: significant improvements over baselines on classification, segmentation, and captioning.  
- Interpretability: ensemble members show emergent specialization rather than averaging.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and methods are clearly presented.  
- Parameter-free claim: no extra hyperparameters beyond standard SGD settings.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparison to recent diversity-inducing techniques (e.g., adversarial or information-maximizing methods).  
- Implicit hyperparameter sensitivity: standard SGD hyperparameters likely affect performance but are not discussed.  
- Tie-breaking in oracle selection is done arbitrarily; impact of tie strategies is not analyzed.  
- Scalability: experiments only up to M=6, leaving behavior for much larger ensembles unexplored.  
- Missing detailed ablation studies to isolate contributions of sMCL components.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, practical method addressing a real-world challenge in ensemble training.  
- Strong empirical evidence across challenging tasks and architectures.  
- Clear exposition and insightful analysis; potentially influential work.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient comparison to modern diversity methods.  
- Lacks discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and tie-breaking effects.  
- Scalability to very large ensembles not demonstrated.  

Overall assessment:
- Strong paper with significant contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: expand related work to include recent diversity techniques, discuss SGD hyperparameter sensitivity, and analyze tie-breaking strategies.
