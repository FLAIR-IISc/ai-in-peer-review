Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the problem of producing multiple plausible outputs for ambiguous perception tasks by minimizing oracle loss (min over M predictions per input). The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a winner-take-gradient training procedure that integrates Multiple Choice Learning [8,9] into standard SGD for deep networks. For each mini-batch, the loss for each example is backpropagated only through the ensemble member with the lowest loss on that example; other members receive no gradient. This realizes a stochastic block coordinate descent on the MCL objective without repeated full retraining.

The method is architecture-agnostic and loss-agnostic. Experiments on three settings—CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM)—show improved oracle metrics over independent ensembles, MCL with alternating retraining [8], and a submodular/boosting-style sequential approach [5]. The paper also reports qualitative “emergent expertise” where ensemble members specialize (by classes in classification; by structure/shape biases in segmentation; by n-gram patterns in captioning). On captioning, the method is compared to beam search from a single model; sMCL yields higher diversity (unique n-grams) with competitive oracle CIDEr.

Relative to prior work: unlike classic ensemble diversity approaches (bagging/resampling [27], artificial data [21], joint losses [19,26]) and expert architectures informed by a generalist [12,2], sMCL directly optimizes oracle loss and discovers specialization without an explicit gating network. Compared to methods generating multiple diverse solutions in probabilistic models [3,15,16,23,24], sMCL is applicable to modern deep architectures. It is closest to MCL [8,9] but replaces alternating retraining with an efficient SGD-based “winner-take-gradient” update. The paper could better position itself relative to classic mixture-of-experts with hard gating (e.g., Jacobs et al., Jordan & Jacobs), which also routes examples to experts, though sMCL optimizes an oracle objective without a learned gate.

Strengths:
- Simple, general training algorithm that drops into SGD pipelines; no extra loss-specific machinery.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across three modalities and architectures.
- Consistent gains in oracle metrics over strong baselines; substantial training-time savings vs. [8].
- Clear empirical evidence of emergent specialization; ablation showing k-winners vs M provides insight into specialization/accuracy trade-offs.
- The oracle-loss viewpoint aligns with many practical scenarios that utilize downstream selection.

Weaknesses:
- Limited theoretical analysis: no convergence guarantees or discussion of stability of the hard assignment dynamics beyond empirical results; the discontinuity from winner-take-all could cause collapse in some settings.
- Baseline fairness: MCL [8] is run for five meta-iterations and deemed infeasible to train to stronger convergence for segmentation/captioning; results may reflect computational budget rather than algorithmic limits. A compute-matched comparison (equal wall-clock or FLOPs) would strengthen claims.
- Diversity quantification is limited (e.g., unique n-grams on captioning); more principled diversity metrics (pairwise distance, coverage, determinantal measures) would provide a clearer picture.
- Missing comparisons to related routing/expert paradigms (mixture-of-experts) and to inference-time diversity methods for sequences (e.g., diverse beam search) that also improve oracle metrics.
- Details and ablations: tie-breaking strategy, assignment imbalance across members, effect of batch size, learning rate, and M on specialization and performance are not systematically analyzed. The “parameter-free” claim is somewhat overstated since M must be chosen, and a k-winners variant introduces an additional parameter.
- Impact beyond oracle metrics: while appropriate for the objective, some applications require end-to-end gains with a real selector; demonstrating downstream selection or human-in-the-loop would improve practical significance.

Quality:
- Technically sound at the algorithmic level; the gradient derivation for the indicator assignment (Eq. 2) is correct as a subgradient of the min. However, the paper lacks formal treatment of optimization properties and potential pathologies (e.g., dead experts).
- Experimental methodology is generally solid with multiple domains. Some design choices (shared CNN for captioning due to memory limits) could limit diversity; still, improvements are observed.
- Claims about speed-ups are plausible but would benefit from precise runtime/compute reporting.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated; algorithm description is succinct.
- Suggestions to improve clarity: explicitly specify tie-breaking for equal losses; include pseudocode with complexity; report assignment distributions over training; clarify evaluation splits for VOC; provide exact runtime comparisons; detail how the “sMCL layer” integrates into frameworks.

Originality:
- Moderate. The central idea—SGD with hard per-example routing to minimize oracle loss—is a practical and nontrivial adaptation of MCL to deep learning, but conceptually related to hard-EM/hard gating and expert routing. The novelty lies in the specific objective (oracle loss), the simple stochastic training rule, and its demonstrated applicability across tasks.

Significance:
- Practical significance is high: an easy-to-implement method that reliably improves oracle metrics and induces specialization across diverse deep architectures. The idea is likely to be adopted by practitioners needing multiple hypotheses. Broader impact could be enhanced with comparisons to additional diversity mechanisms and downstream selection results.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and effective method with consistent improvements across tasks.
- Addresses a known bottleneck in prior MCL methods by enabling efficient, concurrent training.
- Empirical evidence of emergent, interpretable specialization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical grounding and analysis of training dynamics.
- Some baseline comparisons may be underpowered due to compute constraints; missing comparisons to closely related expert/routing methods and diverse decoding.
- Diversity and downstream utility are not comprehensively evaluated.

Overall, this is a solid, practically useful contribution that advances training for diverse deep ensembles by directly optimizing oracle loss via an efficient SGD-compatible procedure.
