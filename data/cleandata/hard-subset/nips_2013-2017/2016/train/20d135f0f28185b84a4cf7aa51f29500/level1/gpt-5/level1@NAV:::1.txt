This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-friendly method to train deep ensembles that minimize oracle (top-k) loss by a winner-take-gradient rule: for each example, only the lowest-loss predictor in the ensemble receives gradient signal. The authors relate their formulation to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) of Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS) which uses alternating assignments and retraining, and to diverse M-best approaches in graphical models, showing that prior procedures are impractical for modern deep nets. Experiments across image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC with FCN), and image captioning (COCO with CNN+LSTM) demonstrate consistent gains in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, boosting-style sequential training (Dey et al., ICCV), and a 5x speedup over MCL with comparable or better accuracy. Qualitatively, the method induces interpretable specialization (class clusters in CIFAR, shape/frequency biases in segmentation, n-gram/style diversity in captions).

Quality: The optimization is simple and technically sound; Eq. (2) follows directly from the block objective. The empirical evaluation is careful, with multiple architectures and clear oracle evaluation. However, theoretical analysis of convergence/stability under hard assignment is absent, and ties/assignment imbalance are handled heuristically.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized, with intuitive motivation, pseudo-code, and thorough qualitative analysis. Suggested improvements: clarify initialization sensitivity, tie-breaking, and provide more training details for reproducibility (e.g., batch composition under sMCL, per-epoch reassignment rates).

Originality: The main novelty is an SGD-based, parameter-free, concurrent training procedure that makes MCL practical for deep networks. While the winner-take-all idea echoes hard-EM and mixture-of-experts, applying it to oracle-loss minimization across diverse deep tasks is a fresh and useful contribution.

Significance: The approach is broadly applicable, easy to implement, and yields substantial oracle gains and specialization. That said, the paper largely evaluates oracle metrics; downstream task impact (re-ranking, human-in-the-loop) and non-oracle metrics (calibration, single-prediction accuracy) are not fully explored.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm with consistent improvements and speedups over MCL.
- Broad validation across tasks with convincing qualitative diversity/specialization.
- Clear connection to prior NIPS work on MCL/diverse M-best.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited analysis of convergence, stability, and load balancing across ensemble members.
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited evidence that gains translate to end-to-end system utility.
- Missing comparisons to alternative diversity-inducing deep methods (e.g., soft assignment, disagreement regularizers) and to learned gating (mixture-of-experts).
