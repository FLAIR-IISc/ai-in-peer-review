- Proposes sMCL, a training procedure for deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss to produce diverse hypothesis sets.
- Core technique: compute forward passes for all models, but backpropagate the loss only to the model with the lowest loss per example ("winner-take-gradient").
- sMCL is a stochastic, online adaptation of prior MCL that makes the paradigm practical for deep networks without iterative retraining.
- Evaluated on three tasks (CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC segmentation, MSCOCO captioning) with significant improvements in oracle performance.
- Compares to independently trained ensembles, original MCL, and a boosting-like method (Dey et al.); sMCL consistently outperforms baselines.
- Qualitative analyses show emergent specialization of ensemble members, supporting claims of increased diversity.
- Technically sound and well-supported empirically, using appropriate architectures for each task.
- Exceptionally clear writing and presentation; algorithm description is implementation-ready and figures effectively support claims.
- Originality is moderate: oracle-loss objective comes from prior MCL work; novelty is the stochastic winner-take-gradient adaptation for deep learning.
- High practical significance: parameter-free, loss- and architecture-agnostic, easy to implement, and likely to be widely adopted.
- Strengths (PRO): simple/elegant solution, broad empirical validation, practical impact, clear presentation, notable performance gains.
- Weaknesses (CON): incremental novelty over prior MCL and lack of theoretical convergence or formal analysis.
- Overall assessment: strengths outweigh weaknesses; strong accept recommendation for NIPS.
