This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of models to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions, a setting known as Multiple Choice Learning (MCL). The motivation is that for many perception tasks with inherent ambiguity, it is more useful to provide a downstream process or human operator (an "oracle") with several plausible hypotheses rather than a single prediction. The core contribution is a novel and efficient training algorithm, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), designed specifically for deep neural networks.

The proposed sMCL method directly optimizes the oracle loss for an ensemble. For each training example within a mini-batch, the algorithm identifies the single model in the ensemble that yields the lowest task-specific loss. During the backpropagation step, the gradient for that example is passed only to this "winning" model, while all other models receive a zero gradient for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy encourages different models in the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution. This approach is presented as a stochastic block gradient descent optimization of the MCL objective introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2012). Unlike the prior work, which required costly iterative retraining of models, sMCL trains all ensemble members concurrently in a single pass, making it practical for deep architectures. The efficacy of sMCL is demonstrated across three distinct domains: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO, using appropriate CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality and Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a well-motivated and practical adaptation of the MCL objective to the stochastic gradient descent framework used for deep learning. The claims of superior performance and emergent specialization are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The evaluation across three different and challenging tasks, each with a different network architecture and loss function, convincingly demonstrates the generality and effectiveness of the method. The analysis of the resulting ensembles, showing how they develop interpretable specializations (e.g., class-based specialization in classification, diverse sentence structures in captioning), adds significant value and provides insight into the algorithm's behavior.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated in the introduction with illustrative examples. The proposed sMCL algorithm is described simply and is effectively contrasted with prior, less efficient methods. The experimental setup and results are presented with a high degree of clarity, using well-designed figures and tables that are easy to interpret. The qualitative results, in particular, provide compelling visual evidence of the diversity generated by sMCL compared to standard ensembles.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—the sMCL training algorithm—is novel and significant. While the concept of MCL is not new, this paper presents the first practical and efficient method for applying it to large-scale deep learning models. Previous methods involving iterative retraining or sequential boosting were computationally prohibitive for modern network architectures. By developing a simple, parameter-free, and general algorithm that can be easily integrated into standard training pipelines, the authors provide a valuable tool to the community. The problem of generating diverse predictions is of high practical importance in fields where models face ambiguity, and this work provides a powerful and accessible solution. Given its simplicity and demonstrated effectiveness, the method is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers and practitioners.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the method is empirically strong and intuitively appealing, the paper provides limited theoretical analysis. The connection to stochastic block gradient descent is made, but a more formal discussion of the optimization dynamics or convergence properties of the proposed "hard-assignment" stochastic procedure would have strengthened the contribution.

*   **Applicability is Constrained:** The method's utility is inherently tied to the MCL problem setting, which presupposes the existence of a downstream oracle capable of selecting the best prediction from the generated set. While this is a valid and important setting, the paper does not discuss scenarios where such an oracle is unavailable or expensive to query, which may limit the direct application of the trained ensemble.

**Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Practical Algorithm:** Proposes sMCL, a novel, simple, and computationally efficient algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles, solving a major limitation of prior work in this area.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** Demonstrates significant improvements in oracle performance over multiple strong baselines across three distinct and challenging tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning).
3.  **High Significance and Impact:** Addresses an important problem of generating diverse hypotheses and provides a general, parameter-free tool that is likely to be widely adopted by the community.
4.  **Excellent Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making the concepts and results easy to understand and reproduce.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding:** The theoretical justification for the algorithm's convergence and optimization properties is brief.
2.  **Specific Problem Setting:** The method's value is primarily demonstrated within the specific context of oracle loss minimization, which requires a downstream selection mechanism.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for a significant problem. The strengths, particularly the impressive empirical validation and the high potential for impact, substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper represents a solid contribution to the field of ensemble learning for deep networks.
