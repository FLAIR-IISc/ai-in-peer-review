- Proposes sMCL, a parameter-free, architecture-agnostic training method for deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss to produce diverse outputs.  
- Key idea: use stochastic gradient descent and stochastic block gradient descent to directly minimize oracle loss, avoiding costly iterative MCL retraining.  
- Insight: gradients for non-selected (incorrect) predictions become zero, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members.  
- Well-motivated framing: diversity in predictions is important for ambiguous inputs, class confusion, and downstream oracle evaluation.  
- Mathematical derivation and Algorithm 2 are clearly presented, though some steps are dense.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness across tasks (CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, MSCOCO captioning) and architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN).  
- Comprehensive comparisons to baselines (independent ensembles, MCL) show consistent improvements; qualitative visualizations reveal emergent specialization.  
- Comparison to Dey et al. (2015) is included but feels cursory and lacking detailed training curve and computational cost analysis.  
- Claim of being "parameter-free" is overstated because standard SGD hyperparameters still require tuning and their effects are not fully explored.  
- Lacks a detailed ablation study on SGD hyperparameters (learning rate, momentum, weight decay, batch size) and their impact on sMCL.  
- Insufficient analysis of why specialization occurs—role of initialization, architecture, or loss function is not deeply investigated.  
- Writing is generally clear but occasionally dense in mathematical notation (e.g., gradient derivation in Eq. (2) needs more intuition).  
- Related work coverage is adequate but omits broader diversity-promoting methods (dropout, adversarial training, variational inference) for fuller contextualization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, significant practical relevance, sound theory and implementation, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited ablation, superficial comparison to some baselines, incomplete explanation of specialization mechanisms.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with convincing experiments; recommended for acceptance despite areas needing further analysis.
