Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets the multiple predictions setting where a downstream oracle selects the best among M outputs. The authors recast Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al. NIPS’12; AISTATS’14] for deep networks and propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL): during SGD, for each training example, only the “winner” (the model with lowest loss on that example) receives gradient updates (“winner-take-gradient”). This realizes a stochastic block coordinate optimization of the standard hard-assignment MCL objective without costly retraining phases.

The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and is demonstrated on three families of deep models: CNNs on CIFAR-10 classification, FCNs on PASCAL VOC segmentation, and CNN+LSTM on MSCOCO captioning. Across tasks, sMCL improves oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr-D) versus independent ensembles, the original MCL (with multiple meta-iterations), and the submodular/boosting approach of Dey et al. The authors qualitatively analyze emergent specialization (e.g., class-specific experts on CIFAR-10, shape/frequency biases for segmentation, n-gram/style diversity for captions). The work is related to ensemble learning and diversity-inducing approaches (bagging, negative correlation), structured-output diverse M-best and diversity-inducing inference (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.), and to mixture-of-experts/competitive learning, but differs in directly optimizing the oracle loss with a simple SGD-compatible procedure and without auxiliary gating.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and practical training recipe: a minimal modification to backprop enables learning diverse ensembles optimizing oracle loss, with no extra hyperparameters.
- Broad empirical validation across tasks and architectures (classification, segmentation, captioning), with consistent oracle improvements and interpretable diversity.
- Clear connection to the classical MCL objective and a compelling argument for efficiency: training in one pass vs. repeated retraining or sequential boosting.
- Useful analysis: emergence of specialization; ablation with top-k assignment interpolating between oracle training and standard ensembles; qualitative outputs that illustrate ambiguity capture.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited theoretical understanding: no convergence or approximation analysis beyond the intuitive gradient surrogate; no discussion of stability (e.g., collapse of multiple learners to a single winner) beyond empirical results.
- Baselines and fairness of comparisons could be strengthened:
  - Captioning: comparing oracle CIDEr of sMCL (M models) vs. beam search of one model conflates training and inference; equalizing compute budgets (e.g., beam for each ensemble member or diverse beam search for single model) would be more informative.
  - Segmentation: sMCL is initialized from a standard ensemble and then fine-tuned; ensure independent ensembles receive the same fine-tuning budget to rule out schedule effects.
  - MCL: only 5 meta-iterations reported; it is acknowledged that more could improve performance (though costly). A partial ablation (e.g., early stopping vs. more iterations on a subset) would help.
- Metrics focus on oracle performance; the paper does not report standard single-prediction accuracy of individual members or average/aggregate performance when the oracle is imperfect. This matters in practice when downstream selection is noisy.
- Scope of diversity: Aside from captioning n-gram counts, diversity is mostly implicit via oracle metrics. Quantitative diversity/correlation measures (e.g., error correlation, pairwise disagreement, calibration differences) would strengthen claims.
- Sensitivity and robustness: No analysis of sensitivity to initialization, M, batch size, or tie-breaking; no report of “dead” learners or strategies to avoid it (e.g., temperature, epsilon-greedy, soft top-k). The top-k variant is promising; more systematic exploration would be useful.
- Computational cost: Although faster than MCL in wall-clock meta-iterations, training cost scales with M forward passes per batch. A cost-normalized comparison (oracle gain per unit compute) would be informative.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of an algorithmic contribution; the gradient derivation from the hard-assignment surrogate is straightforward and consistent with hard-EM/competitive learning. Experiments are well executed on standard datasets and strong architectures. Claims about speed vs. MCL are reasonable, though compute normalization is not fully addressed. The work is honest about limitations of prior methods and reports practical details. Some missing analyses (stability, sensitivity, diversity quantification) limit the depth.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The connection from the MCL objective to the sMCL update is explained succinctly. Algorithms are easy to implement. Figures effectively illustrate specialization and qualitative diversity. Minor suggestions:
- Explicitly specify the training loss used as `(.)` for each task (e.g., pixel-wise cross-entropy for segmentation vs. IoU at eval).
- Clarify whether sMCL for CIFAR-10 is trained from scratch or after pretraining.
- Describe tie-breaking, batch-level assignment details, and any regularization used to avoid early collapse.

Originality
The winner-take-gradient approach is a natural but novel adaptation of MCL to deep SGD. While conceptually related to mixture-of-experts with hard gating/competitive learning, the specific focus on oracle loss and its simple SGD instantiation for arbitrary architectures is new in the deep learning literature of the time. The empirical breadth is also a contribution.

Significance
Multiple-hypothesis outputs are highly relevant (top-k recognition, multimodal segmentation, captioning). sMCL is likely to be adopted due to simplicity and applicability. The improvements in oracle metrics and the qualitative specialization suggest real utility for downstream systems that can re-rank/select. The paper advances practical training for diverse deep ensembles.

Questions and suggestions
- Report individual-member accuracy and average/majority-vote performance to show the trade-off with oracle gains.
- Provide diversity metrics (pairwise disagreements, error correlation, KL divergence of predictive distributions).
- Analyze stability: distribution of assignments over epochs; fraction of data per expert; any “dead” experts? Does top-k mitigate collapse?
- Compute-normalized comparisons (e.g., same total FLOPs between sMCL M=5 and single-model beam or snapshot ensembles).
- Discuss relation to classical mixture-of-experts (Jacobs et al., 1991) and competitive learning; could a learned gating network improve performance while retaining oracle-optimized training?

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general algorithm with strong empirical gains on diverse tasks.
- Addresses a practical and important setting (multiple hypotheses, oracle evaluation).
- Interpretable emergent expertise and insightful analyses.
- Improves upon prior MCL methods in both performance and training efficiency.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis and ablation on stability/sensitivity.
- Some baseline comparisons could be more balanced; lack of compute-normalized results.
- Focus on oracle metrics without reporting potential degradation in single-prediction performance or calibration.

Overall, this is a solid, practically impactful contribution; with minor additions on analysis and baselines, it would be very strong.
