- Introduces sMCL: a stochastic SGD method that trains diverse deep ensembles by updating only the lowest-loss model per example to minimize oracle loss.  
- Overcomes MCL's costly alternating retraining by enabling concurrent training of ensemble members.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO, showing consistent improvements over baselines (classical ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.).  
- Reports 6–10% relative improvement in oracle metrics and faster convergence (≈5x) compared to MCL.  
- Technically sound with a rigorous problem formulation and well-motivated solution.  
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across architectures, tasks, and dataset sizes with reproducibility details.  
- Well-written, clearly organized, and accompanied by effective figures illustrating specialization.  
- Novel contribution: first practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles for deep networks without costly retraining.  
- Method is simple to implement, parameter-free, and broadly applicable to multiple domains.  
- Limited theoretical analysis explaining why sMCL induces specialization among ensemble members.  
- Insufficient exploration of how different loss functions affect specialization patterns.  
- Unclear scaling behavior for very large ensemble sizes (tested only up to M=6) and potential diminishing returns.  
- Could more explicitly contrast sMCL with other recent ensemble diversity methods and "generalist" approaches.  
- Broader real-world impact is suggested but not concretely demonstrated with specific application scenarios.  
- Pro accept arguments: novel, efficient, well-validated, interpretable specializations, simple to implement, significant gains.  
- Con accept arguments: limited theory, limited large-ensemble exploration, uncertain generalization to other structured prediction tasks.  
- Overall assessment: a technically solid, practically significant, and well-evaluated contribution deserving acceptance despite theoretical and scalability gaps.
