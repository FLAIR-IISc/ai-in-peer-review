Summary and relation to prior work: The paper addresses the problem of producing multiple plausible predictions by directly minimizing oracle loss across an ensemble of deep networks. The main claims are: (1) a simple, parameter-free, SGD-compatible training algorithm (sMCL) that performs “winner-take-gradient” assignments per example and batch, and (2) consistent reductions in oracle error across classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC, FCN), and image captioning (MSCOCO, CNN+LSTM) compared to independent ensembles, MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS], and a submodular/boosting-style method [Dey et al., ICCV]. The approach is positioned as a practical variant of Multiple Choice Learning that avoids costly re-training/block-coordinate alternation, akin to hard-EM/k-means-style updates within SGD. It is also related to “M-Best diverse” and diversity-inducing inference in structured prediction (ECCV/NIPS), but extends to general deep architectures.

Quality: The method is technically sound and clearly derived from the surrogate assignment objective. The experiments are broad (three modalities/architectures) and use appropriate oracle metrics; sMCL yields sizable gains (often 6–10%) and 5× training speedup over MCL. Qualitative analyses convincingly show emergent specialization. Limitations: evaluation focuses on oracle metrics; downstream selection/reranking benefits are not quantified. Comparisons to modern mixture-of-experts with learned gating and diversity-regularized ensembles are missing. Convergence/assignment stability and sensitivity to batch size/tie-breaking are not analyzed.

Clarity: The paper is well written, with a clear algorithmic description and compelling figures. Some references look incomplete in the provided list, but related work coverage is otherwise adequate.

Originality: While rooted in prior MCL and hard-assignment ideas, the stochastic “winner-take-gradient” formulation that trains deep ensembles jointly and efficiently is novel and practically important. The cross-domain demonstration strengthens the contribution.

Significance: Multiple-hypothesis prediction is widely relevant; a simple, architecture-agnostic recipe likely sees uptake. The idea should be useful to practitioners dealing with ambiguity and top-k objectives.

Constructive suggestions:
- Report end-to-end results with a simple oracle (e.g., learned reranker or user-study) to validate practical impact beyond oracle metrics.
- Compare against learned-gating mixture-of-experts and diversity-promoting regularizers (e.g., DPP/NC-ensembles), controlling for compute.
- Provide ablations: effect of M, batch size, tie-breaking, curriculum (anneal k from M to 1), and training stability; include per-member top-1 trade-offs and calibration.
- Discuss convergence properties and when reassignment oscillations occur.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and effective algorithm; strong empirical gains across tasks; practical speed advantage; insightful analysis of specialization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; missing comparisons to gating/diversity baselines; limited ablations and theory on stability/convergence.
