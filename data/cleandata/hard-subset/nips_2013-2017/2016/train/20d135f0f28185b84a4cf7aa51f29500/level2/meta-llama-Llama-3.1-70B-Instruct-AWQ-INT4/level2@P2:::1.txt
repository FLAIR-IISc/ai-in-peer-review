The paper "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles" proposes a novel approach to training deep neural network ensembles that can produce multiple diverse solutions for a given input. The authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) that minimizes the oracle loss, which is the minimum loss among all ensemble members.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods.

The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of the sMCL algorithm, which is a significant improvement over existing methods for training diverse deep ensembles. The algorithm is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, and parameter-free. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on a wide range of tasks and deep architectures, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning.

The experimental results are impressive, with sMCL outperforming existing baselines and producing sets of outputs with high oracle performance. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

The paper has several strengths, including:

* The introduction of a novel algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes the oracle loss.
* A thorough analysis of the related work, highlighting the differences between the proposed approach and existing methods.
* Impressive experimental results on a wide range of tasks and deep architectures.
* A detailed analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the oracle loss is the most important metric for evaluating the performance of the ensemble. While this is a reasonable assumption, it may not always be the case in practice.
* The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the sMCL algorithm, which may be important for large-scale applications.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the robustness of the sMCL algorithm to hyperparameter tuning and other forms of noise.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The sMCL algorithm is a promising approach for training diverse deep ensembles, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness on a wide range of tasks and deep architectures.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The experimental results are impressive, with sMCL outperforming existing baselines and producing sets of outputs with high oracle performance.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the related work, highlighting the differences between the proposed approach and existing methods.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes that the oracle loss is the most important metric for evaluating the performance of the ensemble, which may not always be the case in practice.
* The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the sMCL algorithm, which may be important for large-scale applications.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the robustness of the sMCL algorithm to hyperparameter tuning and other forms of noise.
