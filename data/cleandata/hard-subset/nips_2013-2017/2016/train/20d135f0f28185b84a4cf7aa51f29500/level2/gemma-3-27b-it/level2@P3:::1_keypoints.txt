- Introduces sMCL: a stochastic block SGD algorithm to train ensembles minimizing oracle loss without costly retraining.  
- Enables generation of diverse solutions for systems where downstream oracles select among hypotheses.  
- Builds on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) but addresses impractical retraining by proposing a stochastic approach.  
- Demonstrates applicability across architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs) and tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning).  
- Reports improved oracle performance and shows interpretable specialization of ensemble members.  
- Strength: well-motivated, relevant problem setting for systems using multiple hypotheses.  
- Strength: clever, practical adaptation of SGD that is parameter-free and works with varied loss functions.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments showing consistent gains across diverse tasks.  
- Strength: analysis of ensemble specialization provides insightful, interpretable behavior.  
- Weakness: lacks a detailed ablation study isolating the effect of the winner-take-gradient mechanism vs. alternative diversity losses.  
- Weakness: brief discussion of limitations and potential failure cases or hyperparameter sensitivities.  
- Weakness: "interpretable expertise" claim relies mainly on qualitative examples; needs quantitative specialization/diversity metrics (beyond class histograms and CIDEr-D).  
- Weakness: comparisons to Dey et al. are somewhat cursory; deeper analysis of why sMCL outperforms sequential methods is needed.  
- Minor: acknowledgment section is overly long and contains a disclaimer that should be condensed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and practical method, strong empirical results, well-motivated problem, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited ablation and analysis of limitations, specialization claims largely qualitative.  
- Overall: high-quality, significant contribution to ensemble learning and a strong candidate for acceptance pending more detailed analysis.
