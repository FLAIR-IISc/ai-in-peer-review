- Proposes sMCL, a stochastic SGD-based algorithm for training deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss.  
- Uses a "winner-take-gradient" rule: backpropagate loss only to the ensemble member with the lowest-loss prediction per example.  
- Trains all M ensemble members concurrently, making MCL practical for large deep networks without costly retraining.  
- sMCL is simple, parameter-free, and agnostic to network architecture and loss function.  
- Evaluated on three tasks (CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC segmentation, MSCOCO captioning) using modern architectures.  
- Empirical results show sMCL improves oracle performance over standard ensembles, a boosting-style baseline, and original MCL, while being faster.  
- sMCL naturally induces emergent, interpretable specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class-wise partitioning).  
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and strongly supported by comprehensive experiments and analyses.  
- Writing, organization, and presentation (algorithms, figures, tables, qualitative examples) are clear and effective.  
- The approach is highly original as a practical, parallel adaptation of MCL for deep learning.  
- Method is significant and likely to have broad impact due to simplicity, efficiency, and generality.  
- Suggest reporting sMCL oracle performance when each member uses beam search to compare against captioning beam-search diversity.  
- Suggest clarifying whether pre-training the ensemble is necessary for segmentation or if random initialization suffices.  
- Recommend discussing the effect of ensemble size M (saturation of benefits and impact on specialization).  
- Acceptance arguments: novelty, strong empirical evidence, clarity, and high practical impact.  
- Rejection caveats: need for more direct beam-search comparison in captioning and minor implementation clarifications (segmentation initialization).
