

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training deep neural network ensembles to produce diverse multiple predictions that minimize oracle loss (the loss of the best prediction among the ensemble). The authors position this as a practical solution to the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm formalized by Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2015), which previously required computationally prohibitive retraining cycles for deep networks. sMCL replaces this with a stochastic gradient descent approach where, for each training example, gradients are backpropagated *only* to the ensemble member producing the lowest loss for that example. This eliminates the need for alternating training and enables concurrent training of all ensemble members. The paper demonstrates sMCL across image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements over baselines (classical ensembles, MCL, Dey et al. 2016) with up to 10% relative oracle accuracy gains and 5x speedup over MCL.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Contribution & Practicality:** sMCL is elegantly simple, parameter-free, and agnostic to architecture/loss function. It directly addresses a critical bottleneck in applying MCL to deep learning (retraining cycles), making diverse ensemble training feasible for modern architectures. The concurrent training approach is a significant practical advance over prior work.  
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** Results across three distinct tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) convincingly demonstrate sMCL’s efficacy. The oracle metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy, oracle IoU) are well-motivated and standard for this problem. The 6–10% improvements over strong baselines are substantial and consistent.  
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The "interpretable expertise" analysis (Figs. 4c, 5b, 7) is compelling. It shows how sMCL automatically induces specialization (e.g., networks becoming class specialists in classification, segment shape experts in segmentation) without manual design—this emergent behavior is a key strength and directly links to the method’s success.  
4. **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-written, with clear motivation (Fig. 1), concise technical formulation (Sec. 3), and thorough experiments (Sec. 4). The connection between oracle loss minimization and handling ambiguity (e.g., implicit class confusion, ambiguous evidence) is effectively argued.

**Weaknesses & Constructive Suggestions:**  
1. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper focuses on empirical results but lacks theoretical analysis of convergence or guarantees for sMCL. While the empirical success is strong, a brief discussion of why the "winner-takes-gradient" strategy converges (e.g., relation to stochastic optimization with non-smooth objectives) would strengthen the contribution. *Suggestion:* Add a short paragraph (e.g., in Sec. 3) discussing how sMCL relates to existing stochastic optimization frameworks for non-convex problems.  
2. **Comparison with Recent Ensemble Work:** The related work (Sec. 2) covers MCL and Dey et al. but misses more recent deep learning ensemble approaches. For example, Kirillov et al. (NIPS 2015) and Ahmed et al. (ICML 2016) propose methods for inducing diversity in deep ensembles via loss modifications or data augmentation. While sMCL is distinct, a brief comparison would clarify its niche. *Suggestion:* Add 1–2 sentences in Sec. 2 contrasting sMCL with these methods (e.g., "Unlike Kirillov et al. (2015), which enforces diversity via submodular optimization, sMCL learns specialization implicitly through oracle loss minimization").  
3. **Ablation on Gradient Strategy:** The paper does not explore how the gradient strategy (backprop to *only* the winning model) affects specialization. Would relaxing this (e.g., backprop to top-*k* models) improve diversity without sacrificing oracle accuracy? *Suggestion:* Include a small ablation study (e.g., varying *k* in Fig. 4d–e) to show sensitivity to this design choice.  
4. **Computational Cost:** While sMCL is 5x faster than MCL, the absolute training time for large ensembles (e.g., M=6) isn’t compared to standard ensembles. *Suggestion:* Clarify that sMCL trains all M models *concurrently* in a single pass (vs. MCL’s sequential retraining), so total time is roughly equivalent to training one model, not M. This is a key advantage worth emphasizing.

**Significance & Originality:**  
The work is highly significant for real-world AI systems where multiple predictions are valuable (e.g., autonomous driving, assistive tech). It makes MCL practical for deep learning—a gap in prior work. Originality is clear: while MCL was introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. (NIPS 2015), sMCL is the first SGD-based, concurrent training method for deep ensembles. The paper explicitly states this novelty ("we are the first to formalize... in the context of deep networks"), and the experiments confirm it outperforms all prior methods. The emergence of interpretable specialization is a novel insight beyond just performance gains.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong, well-executed paper with a clear contribution to a meaningful problem. The method is simple, practical, and effective across diverse tasks. The weaknesses are minor and addressable with small additions. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on practical, impactful advances in machine learning. It is technically sound (experiments are thorough), well-motivated, and demonstrates clear significance for real-world applications. I recommend acceptance.
