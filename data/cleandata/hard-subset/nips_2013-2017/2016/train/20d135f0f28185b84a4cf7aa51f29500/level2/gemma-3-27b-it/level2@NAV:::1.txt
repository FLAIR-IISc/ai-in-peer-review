## Review of “Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles”

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles designed to produce multiple plausible hypotheses, particularly useful when a system interacts with an oracle for evaluation. The core idea is to leverage a “winner-take-gradient” strategy during stochastic gradient descent, effectively minimizing the oracle loss by only backpropagating errors through the ensemble member providing the best prediction for each example. The authors demonstrate the method’s applicability to image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks with CNN, FCN, and CNN+RNN architectures, showing improved oracle performance compared to existing techniques like standard ensembles, MCL, and a boosting-like approach.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical need for systems that interact with human evaluators or verification components. The proposed method is conceptually simple, yet effective, and avoids the costly retraining inherent in previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches. The experiments are comprehensive, covering diverse architectures and tasks, and the results consistently show sMCL outperforming baselines. A significant strength is the qualitative analysis which reveals interpretable specialization emerging within the ensemble members, demonstrating how the method captures task ambiguity. The parameter-free nature and broad applicability to any SGD-trained model add to its practical appeal.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing diversity-promoting techniques beyond those explicitly tested. While the authors acknowledge the challenges of applying these techniques, a more nuanced discussion would strengthen the contribution. While the ablation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, a quantitative analysis of the computational cost compared to standard training would be valuable. Finally, the presentation of results in Figure 4 (classification) could be improved for clarity, particularly with regards to the visualization of label-space clustering. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective method for training diverse ensembles.
*   Strong empirical results across multiple tasks and architectures.
*   Conceptual simplicity and practical applicability.
*   Emergence of interpretable specialization within ensemble members.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to other diversity-promoting techniques.
*   Lack of detailed computational cost analysis.
*   Clarity could be improved in some figures.

**Overall:** This paper presents a strong contribution to the field. Despite minor shortcomings, the novelty, empirical validation, and practical appeal of sMCL make it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
