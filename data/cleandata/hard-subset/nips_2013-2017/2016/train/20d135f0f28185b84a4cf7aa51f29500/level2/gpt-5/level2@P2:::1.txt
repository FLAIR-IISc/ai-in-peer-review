Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles the problem of training deep ensembles to produce multiple plausible outputs such that an external oracle can select the best one (oracle loss). Building on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm (Guzman-Rivera et al.), the authors propose Stochastic MCL (sMCL): a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme that, per minibatch example, backpropagates the loss only through the ensemble member that currently incurs the smallest loss on that example. This yields a stochastic block coordinate descent approximation to optimizing the upper bound on oracle loss used in prior MCL, but without repeated retraining phases. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and parameter-free (beyond M). Experiments on three domains—CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM)—show consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, a boosting-like sequential method (Dey et al.), and a multi-iteration MCL baseline (with much lower training time). The paper also analyzes emergent specialization across ensemble members (e.g., class-wise experts, shape/frequency experts, and n-gram/style experts), and compares to single-model beam search for captioning.

The approach closely relates to: (i) MCL’s alternating assignment/training with hard example-to-expert assignments, (ii) mixture-of-experts with hard gating/EM-like updates, and (iii) diverse prediction methods for structured outputs (M-best/M-Best-diverse, DPP/diverse decoding). The main novelty is an efficient, concurrent SGD realization of MCL suited to deep architectures.

Quality:
- Soundness: The gradient derivation for the min-ensemble objective (using indicator variables) is straightforward; sMCL implements the subgradient of the min by routing gradients only through the current argmin branch. This is a reasonable and commonly used trick in nondifferentiable objectives (ties aside).
- Empirics: The breadth of tasks is a strength. The reported oracle improvements are consistent and practically meaningful. The qualitative analyses convincingly show diverse predictions and interpretable specialization.
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: Independent ensembles and Dey et al. are appropriate baselines. The MCL baseline is limited by computational constraints (only 5 meta-iterations), which likely understates MCL’s attainable oracle performance; the paper is transparent about this and emphasizes the efficiency advantage. For captioning, comparing to single-model beam search is informative; showing that sMCL produces more lexical diversity even when oracle CIDEr is lower than wide beam search on a single model is useful. A few important baselines are missing or underexplored (see Weaknesses).
- Reproducibility: Many training details are given, but some critical specifics (exact numbers for Figures/Tables, data splits for segmentation, tie-breaking policy, measures to prevent expert starvation) should be clarified to enable reproduction.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The motivation for oracle loss is compelling and the algorithm is easy to grasp and implement. Figures and qualitative examples are helpful. The references list has formatting issues and missing metadata. Some implementation details (pseudocode, batching/tie-breaking, load balancing) should be made explicit.

Originality:
The core idea—hard routing gradients to the argmin predictor—is a natural SGD instantiation of prior MCL/EM-like approaches. The conceptual novelty is moderate, but the contribution is valuable: a simple, practical training scheme that scales to modern deep models, demonstrated across multiple modalities.

Significance:
The work addresses a common need in perception systems: returning multiple plausible hypotheses. sMCL’s simplicity, generality, and consistent oracle gains make it likely to be adopted and built upon. The emergent specialization is interesting and potentially useful beyond oracle loss (e.g., model introspection or expert allocation).

Strengths:
- Simple, parameter-free, SGD-compatible algorithm applicable to many architectures and losses.
- Consistent oracle metric improvements across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Substantial training-time advantage over prior MCL, with concurrent training of all members.
- Insightful analyses of emergent specialization; qualitative results are compelling.
- Clear motivation for oracle loss in downstream, human-in-the-loop, or re-ranking settings.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Relation to mixture-of-experts and hard EM: The method is very close to classic hard gating without an explicit gating network. A more thorough discussion comparing sMCL to MoE (e.g., training dynamics, load balancing, overfitting) would strengthen the positioning.
- Potential expert starvation/imbalance: Winner-take-all can cause some models to receive few or no updates early on. The paper doesn’t report safeguards (e.g., warm-up, temperature/soft assignments, load-balancing regularizers) or statistics on assignment imbalance during training.
- Oracle vs non-oracle trade-offs: It is unclear whether optimizing oracle loss degrades single-prediction or averaged-ensemble performance. Reporting standard metrics (top-1 accuracy, mIoU, standard caption scores) would help assess trade-offs.
- Baselines and decoding: For captioning, diverse beam search or other diversity-promoting decoders are relevant comparators. For classification/segmentation, MC dropout or test-time augmentation sampling could provide alternative multiple-hypothesis baselines.
- Fairness of MCL comparison: The MCL baseline is likely undertrained; while the efficiency benefit is real, a small-scale setting where MCL can be fully optimized would make the accuracy comparison more conclusive.
- Experimental details: Some choices (e.g., “subset” of VOC val set, tie-breaking strategy, exact oracle numbers) are not fully specified. Reference formatting is incomplete.

Questions and suggestions for improvement:
- Please report assignment distributions over time and per-expert data volumes to show that all experts receive sufficient updates; discuss any measures used to prevent collapse.
- Provide non-oracle metrics (single best prediction) to quantify trade-offs induced by sMCL.
- Add ablations: tie-breaking policy; soft top-k routing (k>1) beyond the brief analysis; effect of initializations; starting from scratch vs fine-tuning from independent ensembles.
- For captioning, compare to diverse beam search or nucleus sampling; also evaluate beam search on top of sMCL experts to show complementarity.
- Include a small-scale experiment where MCL can be fully iterated to convergence to isolate methodological differences from computational ones.
- Discuss connections and differences to mixture-of-experts with learned gating; could a learned gating network using features (rather than per-sample loss) further improve oracle performance?
- Clean up and complete the references; specify exact dataset splits and all hyperparameters; consider releasing code.

Pros for acceptance:
- Practical, general, and simple method with consistent gains on diverse tasks.
- Addresses a timely and important problem (multiple hypotheses for downstream oracles).
- Clear empirical story and insightful qualitative analyses.

Cons against acceptance:
- Conceptual novelty is incremental relative to prior MCL/hard-EM/MoE ideas.
- Missing baselines and incomplete analysis of training dynamics (expert starvation), and lack of standard metric reporting.
- MCL comparison may not be entirely fair due to limited iterations.

Overall recommendation:
A solid and useful contribution with strong empirical results and clear practical impact, despite moderate novelty and some missing analyses. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the concerns above.
