Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the problem of training ensembles to produce multiple plausible outputs such that an oracle (downstream selector) can pick the best one, i.e., minimizing oracle loss. Building on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm of Guzman-Rivera et al., the authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL): a simple “winner-take-gradient” variant that integrates the assignment step into minibatch SGD. For each training example, the loss is computed for all M ensemble members; only the lowest-loss predictor receives gradient for that example (ties broken arbitrarily). The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, requires no additional hyperparameters, and trains all members concurrently.

The authors evaluate across three settings: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM), reporting oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr-D). sMCL is compared against independently trained ensembles, the alternating MCL procedure (with a limited number of meta-iterations), and a boosting-like sequential approach (Dey et al.). They also analyze emergent specialization (e.g., class-level clustering in classification, qualitative segmentation modes, diverse n-grams for captioning). The work is related to diverse M-best/M-Best-Diverse inference for structured models, ensemble diversity methods, and diverse decoding for structured prediction; its main novelty is a practical SGD formulation enabling deep ensembles to directly optimize oracle loss without costly retraining.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The objective directly follows the upper-bounded oracle loss formulation. The gradient behavior is clear: for each example, backprop only through the current argmin member. The algorithm is easy to implement within standard deep-learning frameworks.
- Empirical support: Experiments on three distinct tasks and architectures show consistent gains on oracle metrics over strong baselines. Analyses include training curves (faster oracle loss reduction), a k-winners ablation bridging sMCL and independent ensembles, and qualitative specialization.
- Limitations: (1) Comparisons to MCL may understate MCL performance because only 5 meta-iterations were feasible; although practical, it complicates the fairness of the accuracy comparison. (2) The method and evaluation focus exclusively on oracle metrics; the impact on single-prediction performance and on downstream selection modules is not reported. (3) No analysis of potential assignment collapse (one model dominating), tie-breaking effects, or strategies to encourage balanced usage (e.g., entropy regularization) is provided beyond qualitative evidence. (4) Computational overhead is discussed qualitatively (5x faster than MCL) but not quantified relative to independent ensembles (e.g., wall-clock, memory, forward/backward costs as M grows).

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The formulation, gradient intuition, and algorithmic description are easy to follow. Experimental settings are generally described; a few details could be tightened (e.g., exact data splits for VOC subset, memory/compute budgets, and tie-breaking policy). The specialization analyses are informative and intuitive.

Originality:
While the objective and idea of MCL are not new, the key contribution is a simple, general, and practical SGD variant that makes MCL feasible for modern deep networks. To my knowledge, this specific “winner-take-gradient” formulation for minimizing oracle loss across diverse deep architectures is novel and fills an important practical gap left by earlier alternating/sequential methods.

Significance:
Multiple-hypothesis outputs are important in settings with ambiguity and downstream selection. The proposed approach is broadly applicable, easy to adopt, and empirically effective across diverse tasks. The emergent specialization behavior is compelling and potentially useful for interpretability and downstream routing. The work is likely to be adopted and built upon.

Strengths:
- Simple, parameter-free algorithm that plugs into any SGD-trained architecture and loss.
- Consistent improvements over independent ensembles and sequential/alternating MCL variants on oracle metrics across three tasks.
- Joint training yields faster oracle loss reduction than independent ensembles; sMCL is much faster than alternating MCL.
- Clear analyses of emergent specialization (class clusters, segmentation modes, language diversity).
- Useful k-winners ablation that illuminates the diversity–oracle tradeoff.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Fairness of MCL comparison: Given the high cost of retraining, the accuracy comparison may not reflect MCL’s best achievable oracle performance. Consider reporting a controlled small-scale setting where both methods can be run to convergence, or emphasize the practical trade-off (speed vs. accuracy) more explicitly.
- Report non-oracle metrics: Include single-prediction accuracy/IoU/CIDEr for the best member and for the ensemble averaged output to assess whether sMCL sacrifices standard performance for oracle gains.
- Compute/memory overhead: Provide wall-clock and memory comparisons to independent ensembles for different M, and a breakdown of the per-iteration cost (forward losses for all M, backward for one).
- Stability/robustness: Analyze whether assignments collapse; report the distribution of per-member data assignments over training, sensitivity to initialization, and effects of tie-breaking. A soft assignment variant (e.g., top-k with temperature annealing) or a balancing regularizer could be explored.
- Captioning comparisons: In addition to beam search width, consider diverse decoding baselines (e.g., diverse beam search) or nucleus/top-k sampling to disentangle training- vs. inference-time diversity.
- Downstream selection: While oracle is a clean abstraction, a small user study or simulated selector (e.g., learned reranker) could strengthen the claim that sMCL provides practically useful hypothesis sets.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Practical, general technique with clear utility and minimal complexity.
- Strong empirical results and insightful analyses across multiple modalities.
- Addresses a recognized limitation of prior MCL-style methods for deep nets.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Comparisons to prior MCL may not be fully conclusive due to computational constraints.
- Evaluation centered on oracle metrics; limited evidence regarding standard performance and downstream integration.
- Limited theoretical analysis or guarantees beyond the intuitive gradient argument.

Overall evaluation:
A solid, practical contribution that enables training diverse deep ensembles to directly optimize oracle loss, with compelling empirical evidence and broad applicability. Despite some evaluation gaps and missing ablations, the paper is well executed and likely to influence practice. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying comparisons, adding basic non-oracle metrics, and providing compute/memory statistics.
