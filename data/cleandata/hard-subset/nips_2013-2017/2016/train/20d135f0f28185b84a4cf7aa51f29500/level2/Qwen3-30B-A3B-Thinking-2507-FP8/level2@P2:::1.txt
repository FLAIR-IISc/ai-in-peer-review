

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss. The authors frame the problem as producing multiple hypotheses rather than single predictions, which is particularly valuable in practical systems where downstream oracles can select the best solution from a set. The key innovation is a stochastic gradient descent-based training approach that backpropagates gradients only to the lowest-error predictor for each example, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members without the costly retraining required by prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of diverse predictions in practical applications, with compelling examples from computer vision and NLP. The method is well-motivated and the technical contribution is clear: sMCL avoids the iterative retraining of MCL while achieving similar or better results. The authors demonstrate the approach across three different tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning) with multiple architectures, showing consistent improvements over classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s sequential boosting approach. The results are particularly convincing for classification (6-10% relative improvement) and semantic segmentation (7% relative improvement), with qualitative analysis showing interpretable specializations among ensemble members.

The paper has several strengths: it addresses a meaningful problem with practical relevance, the method is simple to implement and broadly applicable, the experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the results are clearly presented with appropriate metrics. The claim that sMCL "automatically generates specializations among ensemble members" is well-supported by the class-wise distribution analysis in Figure 4c and the qualitative examples in Figures 5b and 7.

However, there are several important weaknesses that need addressing. First, the paper lacks theoretical justification for why the method works. While the empirical results are strong, the paper doesn't explain why the "winner-take-gradient" approach leads to specialization and diverse predictions, nor does it provide any convergence guarantees or analysis of the optimization landscape. This is particularly concerning given the method's simplicity and apparent effectiveness.

Second, the comparison with MCL is not sufficiently fair. The authors state that "retraining the MCL ensembles a sufficient number of times proved infeasible" for segmentation and captioning, but don't explain why this is the case or how many meta-iterations would be required for MCL to match sMCL's performance. Without this information, it's difficult to assess whether the speedup is truly due to the method's design or simply because MCL wasn't run long enough.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address limitations or potential failure cases. For example, how does the method perform when the oracle is imperfect or unavailable? What happens when the number of ensemble members grows large? The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free," but doesn't discuss how the method handles cases where the oracle might not be available or when the oracle is imperfect.

Fourth, the paper doesn't provide enough detail about the computational cost of sMCL. While it mentions being 5x faster than MCL, it doesn't quantify the absolute time savings or memory requirements compared to standard ensemble training.

The paper is well-written and the related work is appropriately discussed, but the lack of theoretical grounding and incomplete comparison with MCL are significant concerns. The authors should address these points in the revision. They should provide more analysis of why the method works, run MCL for a sufficient number of iterations to make a fair comparison, and discuss the limitations of the approach.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of training diverse deep ensembles. The method is simple, effective, and broadly applicable. With the suggested revisions to address the theoretical gaps and improve the comparison with MCL, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. As it stands, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the lack of theoretical understanding, but the empirical results are compelling enough that this paper would likely be of interest to the NIPS community.
