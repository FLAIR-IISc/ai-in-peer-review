

The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss. Building upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s Multiple Choice Learning framework [8], the authors address the key limitation of their method—costly retraining of models for deep networks—by proposing a simple SGD-based algorithm that enables concurrent training of ensemble members. The core insight is that gradients should only be backpropagated to the lowest-error predictor for each example, creating a "winner-take-gradient" mechanism.

The paper's main claims are strongly supported through comprehensive experiments across three distinct domains: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). sMCL consistently outperforms classical independent ensembles and other baselines (including MCL [8] and Dey et al. [5]) in oracle metrics, achieving 6-10% relative improvements while being 5x faster than MCL. The qualitative analysis demonstrating how ensemble members develop interpretable specializations (e.g., class-specific expertise in classification, segmentation pattern specialization in semantic segmentation) is particularly compelling.

The work makes a significant contribution to ensemble learning for deep networks by providing a practical, generalizable solution to a problem with clear real-world relevance. The method is simple to implement, architecture-agnostic, and parameter-free, making it broadly applicable. The paper demonstrates excellent understanding of the field, properly situating their work relative to existing literature on ensemble diversity and multiple-choice learning.

While the experimental validation is strong, the paper could better address limitations. It would be valuable to explore how sMCL scales to very large ensembles (e.g., M=100) or handles extremely high-dimensional outputs. Additionally, while claiming agnosticism to loss functions, the paper could provide more concrete examples of applying sMCL to different loss functions beyond those tested.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that effectively bridges theoretical insights with practical applications. The work advances the state of the art in ensemble learning for deep networks, provides clear evidence of its benefits across multiple domains, and offers a method with immediate practical utility for perception systems requiring multiple hypotheses. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
