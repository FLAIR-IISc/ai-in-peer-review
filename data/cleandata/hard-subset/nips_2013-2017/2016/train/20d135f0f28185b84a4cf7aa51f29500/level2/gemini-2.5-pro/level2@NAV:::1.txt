This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and efficient method for training ensembles of deep networks to produce a diverse set of plausible outputs. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," where for each training sample, only the ensemble member with the lowest error receives a gradient update. This "winner-take-gradient" approach is implemented within a standard stochastic gradient descent framework. The work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm but makes it practical for deep learning by replacing MCL's costly iterative retraining with a concurrent training scheme, resulting in a significant speed-up.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

The paper's primary strength lies in its **originality and significance**. It provides an elegant, simple, and computationally feasible solution to the important problem of generating diverse predictions. This is highly relevant for real-world applications where a set of hypotheses is more valuable than a single, potentially overconfident prediction.

The technical **quality** is high, supported by a strong and comprehensive experimental evaluation across three distinct, challenging tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) using modern deep learning architectures. The results convincingly demonstrate that sMCL outperforms relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles and prior MCL methods, in terms of oracle performance. The analysis of the "emergent expertise" of ensemble members is particularly insightful, providing clear qualitative and quantitative evidence of meaningful specialization. The paper is also written with excellent **clarity**, making the motivation and method easy to follow.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses. First, the discussion of limitations is sparse. While sMCL is much faster than iterative MCL, it still requires training M models in parallel, which has significant memory and computational costs compared to a single model. Acknowledging this trade-off would strengthen the paper. Second, the comparison against beam search in the image captioning experiment feels incomplete. While sMCL produces more unique n-grams, a single model with beam search achieves a higher oracle CIDEr score. A more direct comparison, for instance by applying beam search to each sMCL member, would provide a clearer picture of the complementary benefits.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and practical algorithm for an important problem. The method is simple, general, and supported by extensive, high-quality experiments demonstrating its effectiveness and providing valuable insights into its behavior.
*   **Con:** The discussion of computational limitations and one of the baseline comparisons could be slightly improved.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This is a solid contribution that is likely to be impactful and useful to the community.
