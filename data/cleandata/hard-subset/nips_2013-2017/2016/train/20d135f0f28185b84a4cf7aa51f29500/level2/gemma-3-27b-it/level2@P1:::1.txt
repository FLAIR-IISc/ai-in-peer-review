This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss. The core idea is to adapt stochastic gradient descent to a multi-output setting where only the lowest-loss prediction contributes to the gradient update. The authors demonstrate the applicability of sMCL across various architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) and tasks (image classification, segmentation, captioning), showing improvements over existing methods and qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization within the ensemble.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the benefits of generating multiple hypotheses in real-world perception systems. The connection to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) is well-established, and the authors effectively position their work as a practical solution to the limitations of previous MCL approaches (specifically, the computationally expensive retraining requirement). The proposed sMCL algorithm is elegant and conceptually simple, and its parameter-free nature is a significant advantage. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating improvements over baselines on diverse tasks and providing compelling visualizations of ensemble specialization. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of stochastic gradient descent to directly minimize oracle loss in deep ensembles is a novel contribution.
* **Practicality:** sMCL avoids the retraining bottlenecks of previous MCL methods, making it suitable for training large deep networks.
* **Generality:** The method is architecture and loss function agnostic, expanding its applicability.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experiments consistently demonstrate improvements across different tasks and network architectures.
* **Interpretability:** The qualitative analysis reveals interpretable specialization within the ensemble members, providing insights into the learned representations.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Modern Diversity-Inducing Techniques:** While the authors compare against several baselines, the landscape of diversity-inducing techniques in deep learning is rapidly evolving. It would be beneficial to compare against more recent approaches like variational autoencoders for generating diverse samples or adversarial training methods aimed at improving robustness and diversity.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (Implicit):** While presented as parameter-free, the standard hyperparameters for the underlying SGD optimization (learning rate, momentum, weight decay) still require tuning. The impact of these parameters on the diversity and performance of the ensemble isn’t explicitly investigated.
* **Scalability Discussion:** The paper mentions computational challenges with the captioning experiments (limited by memory to tree-like structures). A more thorough discussion of the scalability of sMCL to very large ensembles and datasets would be valuable.
* **Oracle Loss as Sole Metric:** While oracle loss is a natural metric for this problem, it doesn't fully capture the utility of diverse predictions in a downstream application. Evaluating performance with a simulated "downstream" component or user study would provide stronger evidence of the practical benefits of sMCL. 
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** It would be helpful to have an ablation study to understand the contribution of different aspects of the sMCL algorithm. For example, what is the effect of breaking ties randomly during the gradient update?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and practical approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The method is well-motivated and addresses a relevant problem in the field of perception.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to the broader landscape of diversity-inducing techniques is limited.
* The implicit hyperparameter sensitivity of the underlying SGD optimization is not thoroughly investigated.
* The evaluation relies primarily on oracle loss, without a direct assessment of downstream utility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a practical and effective solution for training diverse deep ensembles, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, particularly regarding a more comprehensive comparison to recent diversity-inducing techniques and a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
