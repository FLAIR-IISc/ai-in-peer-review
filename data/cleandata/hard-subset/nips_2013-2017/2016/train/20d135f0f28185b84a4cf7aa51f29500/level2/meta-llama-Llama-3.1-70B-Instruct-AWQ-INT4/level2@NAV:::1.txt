This paper proposes a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles, called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The authors introduce a stochastic gradient descent-based algorithm that minimizes the oracle loss directly, which is applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent. The proposed method is simple to implement, agnostic to both architecture and loss function, parameter-free, and time-efficient.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it generalizes the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm to jointly learn ensembles of deep networks. Second, it presents a novel training algorithm that avoids costly retraining, making it suitable for modern deep architectures. Third, it demonstrates the broad applicability and efficacy of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles on a wide range of problem domains and network architectures.

The experimental results show that sMCL significantly outperforms classical ensembles, the method of Dey et al., and the 5x slower MCL procedure. The authors also provide qualitative analysis of the training and output behaviors of the resulting ensembles, demonstrating how ensemble member specialization and expertise emerge automatically when trained using sMCL.

Strengths of the paper:

* The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, addressing a significant shortcoming in existing approaches to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the experimental results, including qualitative and quantitative evaluations.
* The authors demonstrate the broad applicability of sMCL across different problem domains and network architectures.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other existing methods, such as ensemble learning techniques that use different loss functions or regularization methods.
* The authors could provide more insights into the interpretability of the emergent specializations among ensemble members, particularly in the context of structured prediction problems.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of sMCL, including potential issues with overfitting or mode collapse.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL across different problem domains and network architectures.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the experimental results, including qualitative and quantitative evaluations.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other existing methods.
* The authors could provide more insights into the interpretability of the emergent specializations among ensemble members.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of sMCL.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and ensemble methods. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
