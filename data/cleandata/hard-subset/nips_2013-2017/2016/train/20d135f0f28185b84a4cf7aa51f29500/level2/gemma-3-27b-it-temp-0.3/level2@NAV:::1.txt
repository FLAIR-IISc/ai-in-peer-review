## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss using stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is to assign gradient updates to the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each example, effectively encouraging specialization. The authors demonstrate the method’s applicability across various tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning) and architectures, showing improvements over existing ensemble methods and baselines.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the practical need for diverse predictions in scenarios where downstream processes evaluate solutions. The formulation of MCL as a stochastic optimization problem is a significant contribution, particularly given the impracticality of previous iterative approaches like those of Guzman-Rivera et al. The method’s simplicity – being parameter-free and applicable to any SGD-trained model – is a major strength. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating consistent improvements across different tasks and providing qualitative examples of emergent expertise within the ensemble members. The analysis of label-space clustering in the classification task is particularly insightful. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations. While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of training large ensembles, they don’t delve into the potential scalability issues with very high-dimensional data or extremely large ensemble sizes.  A more detailed comparison to recent diversity-inducing techniques beyond those explicitly mentioned (e.g., adversarial training for diversity) would also strengthen the work. Furthermore, the impact of tie-breaking during gradient assignment isn’t fully explored.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective approach to training diverse ensembles.
*   Strong empirical results across multiple tasks and architectures.
*   Simple to implement and broadly applicable.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of scalability and potential limitations.
*   Could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to related work on diversity induction.
*   Tie-breaking strategy during gradient assignment could be further investigated.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL method is a practical and effective way to train diverse deep ensembles, and the experimental results are compelling. Despite some minor limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
