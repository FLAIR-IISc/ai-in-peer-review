- Introduces sMCL, a training method that assigns gradients to the lowest-error ensemble member per example to minimize oracle loss.  
- sMCL avoids costly retraining required by prior MCL, is architecture-agnostic, parameter-free, and works for any task-dependent loss.  
- Formulates sMCL as stochastic block gradient descent, enabling concurrent training of deep ensemble members.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10 (classification), Pascal VOC (segmentation), and MSCOCO (captioning).  
- Outperforms classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.’s sequential boosting with consistent oracle accuracy gains (6–10%).  
- Achieves a ~5× speedup over prior MCL approaches.  
- sMCL induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members (e.g., class-specific segmentation, n-gram-focused captioning) without manual design.  
- Technical derivation and gradient-assignment rule are sound and clearly justified.  
- Experimental validation is thorough, diverse, and uses meaningful oracle metrics for multi-output performance.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear; algorithm is concise and well-motivated for real-world use cases.  
- Novel relative to prior work by making MCL feasible for deep networks; correctly positions against related probabilistic and sequential methods.  
- High significance and practical impact for applications needing multiple hypotheses (e.g., autonomous vehicles, accessibility).  
- Minor weaknesses: limited scalability testing for ensembles larger than M=6.  
- Minor weaknesses: lack of ablation study on intermediate k values beyond k=1 and k=M.  
- Minor weaknesses: captioning experiments freeze the CNN backbone, limiting evidence for end-to-end fine-tuning applicability.  
- Suggested (minor) improvement: analyze how specialization patterns correlate with dataset properties (e.g., class imbalance).  
- Overall assessment: meets NIPS quality criteria and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
