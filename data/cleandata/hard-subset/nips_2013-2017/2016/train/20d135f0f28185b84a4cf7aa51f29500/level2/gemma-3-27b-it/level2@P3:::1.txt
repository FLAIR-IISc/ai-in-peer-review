This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for ensembles of deep networks designed to minimize oracle loss. The core idea is to enable diverse solution generation, beneficial when downstream processes evaluate and select from multiple hypotheses. The work builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm but addresses the impracticality of previous methods (requiring retraining) by proposing a stochastic block gradient descent approach. The paper demonstrates the applicability of sMCL across various architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs) and tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning), reporting improved oracle performance and interpretable specialization of ensemble members.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The problem setting – providing multiple plausible solutions for systems interacting with oracles – is well-motivated and increasingly relevant. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a clever adaptation of stochastic gradient descent to address the oracle loss minimization, offering a practical solution compared to existing MCL approaches. The parameter-free nature of sMCL and its applicability to various architectures and loss functions are significant advantages. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent performance gains across diverse tasks. The analysis of ensemble member specialization is insightful, showcasing that the method induces interpretable behavior. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, approach, and results.

However, several areas could be strengthened. While the paper extensively compares sMCL to baselines, a more detailed ablation study would be valuable. For example, isolating the impact of the ‘winner-take-gradient’ approach versus simply training an ensemble with a diverse loss function. The discussion of limitations is relatively brief. Addressing potential scenarios where sMCL might *not* be beneficial, or be sensitive to hyperparameter settings beyond those mentioned, would enhance the paper.  The claim of "interpretable expertise" is made frequently. While examples are provided, a more quantitative measure of specialization (beyond the class distribution visualization in the classification experiment) would be helpful. For instance, evaluating the diversity of generated captions using metrics beyond CIDEr-D.  The comparisons to Dey et al. [5] appear somewhat cursory; a deeper discussion of why sMCL outperforms this method, particularly in light of its sequential training, would be informative. Finally, the acknowledgment section is quite extensive; condensing it and removing the disclaimer would improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   Strong empirical results across multiple tasks and architectures.
*   Well-motivated problem setting with increasing real-world relevance.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited ablation study to isolate the contribution of different components.
*   Brief discussion of limitations and potential failure cases.
*   The claim of "interpretable expertise" relies largely on qualitative examples.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a valuable tool for training diverse deep networks, and the experimental results are compelling. The suggested revisions, primarily focused on a more detailed analysis and discussion of limitations, would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.  This work is a strong candidate for acceptance.
