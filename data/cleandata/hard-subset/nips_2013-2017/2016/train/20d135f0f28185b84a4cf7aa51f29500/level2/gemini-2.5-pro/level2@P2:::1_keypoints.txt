- Problem: generating diverse, high-quality predictions in ambiguous settings using the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) objective (minimize oracle loss).
- Main contribution: Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple winner-take-gradient training algorithm that backprops only to the ensemble member with minimum loss per example.
- Practicality: sMCL trains all M models concurrently in a single pass, is parameter-free, and agnostic to network architecture or loss.
- Empirical evaluation: thorough experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning showing large oracle-performance gains over independent ensembles and other diversity methods.
- Emergent specialization: sMCL naturally induces interpretable specialization among ensemble members (e.g., class-based in classification), with clear visualizations and analysis.
- Significance & originality: makes a previously impractical MCL paradigm feasible for modern deep networks; conceptually simple and impactful.
- Technical quality & clarity: method is well-motivated, technically sound, clearly presented, and experimentally convincing across diverse tasks and architectures.
- Interpretability strength: visualization of class-wise assignments and specialization provides strong intuition for why the method works.
- Computational cost weakness: training M models incurs roughly M-fold computation/memory increase; mitigation strategies (parameter sharing, smaller models) should be discussed more generally.
- Beam search comparison weakness: single-model beam search (beam=5) outperformed M=5 sMCL on CIDEr-D; authors should evaluate sMCL ensemble members combined with beam search for a fairer comparison.
- Segmentation initialization concern: sMCL models for segmentation were initialized from a pre-trained ensemble (50 epochs); clarify whether pretraining is necessary or if training from scratch achieves similar results.
- Handling ties: ties for minimum-loss predictor are broken arbitrarily—report tie frequency and consider/justify alternative strategies (e.g., averaging gradients).
- Overall verdict: excellent, influential paper with strong evidence and insightful analysis; weaknesses are minor and mainly call for additional experiments and discussion.
