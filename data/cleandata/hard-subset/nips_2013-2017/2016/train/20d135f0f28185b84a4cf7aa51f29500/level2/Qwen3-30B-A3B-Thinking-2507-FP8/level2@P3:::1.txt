

The paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training method for diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss by assigning gradients only to the lowest-error ensemble member per example during SGD. This avoids the computationally prohibitive retraining required by prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches [8, 9], while being architecture-agnostic, parameter-free, and applicable to any task-dependent loss. The work extends MCL to deep networks by formulating a stochastic block gradient descent strategy, enabling concurrent training of ensemble members. Experiments across image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO) demonstrate sMCL's superiority over classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s sequential boosting method, with consistent oracle accuracy gains (6-10%) and a 5× speedup over MCL. The paper further shows that sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations among ensemble members (e.g., class-specific segmentation specialists or n-gram-focused captioning models), without manual design.

The primary strength lies in the methodological innovation: sMCL directly addresses a critical gap in deep ensemble learning by making MCL computationally feasible. The technical derivation (Section 3) is sound, with clear justification for the gradient assignment rule. The experimental validation is thorough and well-motivated, covering diverse tasks and architectures, and the oracle metrics provide a meaningful evaluation of multi-output performance. The emergence of interpretable specializations (Figures 4c, 5b, 7) is compelling evidence of the method’s practical value in capturing task ambiguity. The paper’s clarity is exceptional—the algorithm is concise (Algorithm 2), and the narrative connects the technical contribution to real-world use cases (e.g., autonomous vehicles, accessibility systems).

The work demonstrates significant novelty relative to prior NIPS contributions. Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9] introduced MCL but required iterative retraining, which is infeasible for deep networks; sMCL overcomes this limitation through stochastic gradient adaptation. Related work on generating multiple solutions [3, 5, 15, 16, 23, 24] focuses on probabilistic models, not deep architectures, and Dey et al. [5] uses sequential training. The paper properly situates itself against these works, with no unsupported novelty claims. The significance is high: sMCL provides a practical, plug-and-play solution for diverse deep ensembles, addressing a tangible need in applications where downstream oracles benefit from multiple hypotheses (e.g., reducing mode-seeking bias in captioning or handling ambiguous segmentation).

Minor weaknesses include limited scalability testing (e.g., ensembles beyond M=6) and a lack of ablation on the "k" parameter (Section 4.1) beyond the reported k=1 vs. k=M comparison. While the paper notes that k=1 maximizes oracle accuracy, it does not quantify how performance degrades for intermediate k values. Additionally, the captioning experiments freeze the CNN backbone (Section 4.3), which may limit the method’s applicability to end-to-end fine-tuning scenarios. These are reasonable to address but not critical to the core contribution.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: technically sound (SGD adaptation supported by theory and experiments), clearly written, significantly novel (overcoming a key barrier in MCL), and impactful for practitioners building multi-output systems. The claimed advantages over MCL (speed, scalability) are well-documented with empirical evidence. The only suggestion for improvement is to include a brief analysis of how sMCL’s specialization patterns correlate with dataset properties (e.g., class imbalance in segmentation), which could further strengthen the interpretation of emergent expertise. However, this is a minor enhancement rather than a necessary revision.

The work is well-suited for NIPS 2016, addressing a timely problem in deep learning with a simple yet effective solution. It advances the state of the art in ensemble learning for structured prediction and provides immediate utility to the community. The paper’s clarity, breadth of evaluation, and practical focus make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
