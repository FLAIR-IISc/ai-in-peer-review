This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel method for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss. The key idea is to perform a ‘winner-take-gradient’ update during stochastic gradient descent, effectively assigning each example to the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss and backpropagating the gradient only through that member. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over existing methods and highlighting the interpretability of the resulting ensembles.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details of sMCL. The connection to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) is clearly established, and the authors effectively position their contribution as a practical solution to the limitations of previous MCL approaches, particularly the computational cost associated with retraining. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent improvements across different tasks and architectures. The inclusion of qualitative examples and analyses of ensemble member specialization adds significant value.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The sMCL approach is a clever and efficient way to address the problem of training diverse ensembles for multiple-choice learning. The formulation as a stochastic block gradient descent is insightful and allows for concurrent training of ensemble members.
* **Practicality:** Unlike previous MCL methods requiring retraining or sequential training, sMCL is easily integrated into existing deep learning pipelines using standard SGD-based optimizers, making it readily applicable to modern deep architectures.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across the three diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) consistently demonstrate the superior performance of sMCL compared to baselines, including the more computationally expensive MCL method.
* **Interpretability:** The analysis of ensemble member specialization (e.g., class-wise clustering in image classification) provides valuable insights into the learned representations and enhances the understanding of the model’s behavior.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the technical details are presented in a concise and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Diversity-Inducing Techniques:** While the comparison to MCL and a simple independent ensemble is effective, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to other techniques designed to promote diversity in ensembles (e.g., dropout, adversarial training, data augmentation strategies tailored for diversity).  This would strengthen the claim that sMCL is superior due to its direct optimization of oracle loss rather than simply promoting diversity.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions sMCL is parameter-free, the baselines (and potentially sMCL itself) have hyperparameters that need tuning. A brief discussion of sensitivity to those hyperparameters would be beneficial.
* **Oracle Loss as Proxy:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes oracle loss. However, in a real-world application, an oracle may not always be available or perfectly accurate. A discussion of how performance under imperfect oracles might differ could strengthen the paper.
* **Qualitative Analysis Depth:** While the qualitative examples are helpful, a more in-depth analysis of the differences in predictions made by different ensemble members could further illuminate the benefits of sMCL. For example, quantifying the disagreement between members and relating it to uncertainty in the input data.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and practically useful algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across a range of tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, technical details, and benefits of the proposed method.
* The interpretability analysis adds significant value and insights into the learned representations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to other diversity-inducing techniques is limited.
* A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would improve the paper.
* The reliance on oracle loss as the primary evaluation metric might not fully reflect real-world applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL method is a clever and practical approach to training diverse ensembles, and the experimental results are compelling.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed with further experimentation and analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest adding a comparison to other diversity-inducing techniques and including a brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
