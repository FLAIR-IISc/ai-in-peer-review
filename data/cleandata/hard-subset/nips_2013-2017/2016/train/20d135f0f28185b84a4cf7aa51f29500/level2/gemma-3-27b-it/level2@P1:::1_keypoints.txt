- Introduces sMCL, a method that trains diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss via SGD where only the lowest-loss prediction contributes to the update.  
- Adapts SGD to a multi-output setting, avoiding costly retraining required by prior MCL approaches.  
- Demonstrates applicability across architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) and tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) with improved performance.  
- Provides qualitative evidence of interpretable specialization among ensemble members.  
- Well-motivated connection to Multiple Choice Learning and positioned as a practical solution to MCL limitations.  
- sMCL is conceptually simple, parameter-free in formulation, and generally applicable across losses and architectures.  
- Strong empirical results and compelling visualizations across diverse tasks and models.  
- Clear, well-written presentation and experimental setup.  
- Limited comparison to more recent diversity-inducing techniques (e.g., VAE-based or adversarial methods).  
- Implicit hyperparameter sensitivity: underlying SGD hyperparameters (learning rate, momentum, weight decay) still require tuning and aren’t analyzed.  
- Scalability concerns: captioning experiments faced memory limits and broader scalability to large ensembles/datasets isn’t fully discussed.  
- Reliance on oracle loss as the primary metric may not reflect downstream utility; no simulated downstream evaluation or user study.  
- Missing ablation study to isolate contributions of sMCL design choices (e.g., tie-breaking behavior).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, practical, well-motivated method with strong experiments and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited comparisons, unexamined hyperparameter effects, and evaluation focused mainly on oracle loss.  
- Overall: strong contribution recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to expand comparisons and discuss hyperparameter sensitivity and scalability.
