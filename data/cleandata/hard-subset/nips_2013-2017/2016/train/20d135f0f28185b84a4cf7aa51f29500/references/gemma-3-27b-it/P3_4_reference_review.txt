## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach, Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), for training diverse deep ensembles. The core idea of directly minimizing oracle loss via a stochastic gradient descent procedure is elegant and practical, addressing a significant limitation of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods. The experimental results across diverse tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning) are strong and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of sMCL.  I believe this is a solid contribution and recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of training ensembles where diversity is key – a scenario relevant to real-world applications requiring a set of plausible hypotheses rather than a single prediction. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional ensemble methods which often don't explicitly encourage diversity. They build upon the foundation of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al.], which directly optimizes for oracle loss, but crucially overcome the computational bottleneck of the original alternating block coordinate descent approach. sMCL introduces a "winner-take-gradient" strategy that allows for concurrent training of ensemble members using standard stochastic gradient descent.

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature. It clearly differentiates sMCL from existing ensemble methods (e.g., resampling, artificial data generation) and related work on generating multiple solutions from a single model. The contrast with Guzman-Rivera et al.’s MCL is particularly well highlighted – the inability to scale to deep networks being a major motivation.  The authors also acknowledge and compare against recent work by Dey et al. [5] which takes a boosting approach and suffers from similar scalability issues.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Practicality & Scalability:** The primary strength of sMCL is its ability to scale to modern deep networks. The SGD-based approach enables concurrent training, making it far more efficient than previous MCL methods. This is a significant contribution, opening up MCL to a wider range of applications.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results across three diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) are consistently positive.  sMCL outperforms both classical ensembles and other baselines, including the more computationally expensive MCL.
* **Emergent Expertise:** The analysis showing the emergence of specialized expertise in ensemble members is fascinating and supports the theoretical motivation. The visualization of specialization in the classification experiment (Figure 4c) is particularly insightful. The segmentation and captioning experiments also show interesting forms of specialization.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the stochastic gradient update is clear and logically sound. The connection to existing optimization techniques is well-explained.
* **Well-Written & Clear:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-established, the method is explained in a concise manner, and the results are presented effectively.
* **Parameter-Free & Agnostic:** The method is elegantly parameter-free and applicable to any model trained with SGD, making it widely adaptable.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the results are strong, a more thorough ablation study could further strengthen the paper. For example, it would be useful to explore the impact of the ensemble size (M) on performance and specialization. Is there a point of diminishing returns? 
* **Tie-Breaking Strategy:** The paper mentions that ties in identifying the lowest-error predictor are broken arbitrarily.  Could different tie-breaking strategies (e.g., random selection, round-robin) influence the results? A brief discussion of this would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost of Oracle Evaluation:** While sMCL reduces training costs compared to MCL, the oracle evaluation itself (during both training and testing) remains computationally expensive, especially for complex tasks like segmentation and captioning. A discussion of this practical consideration would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Though the authors claim the method is parameter-free, the baseline methods require tuning. A sensitivity analysis on those hyperparameters, even briefly, would be good to establish a clear advantage of sMCL.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant improvement over existing methods for training diverse ensembles.
* Addresses a crucial limitation of previous MCL approaches (scalability).
* Strong and consistent empirical results across diverse tasks.
* Clear and well-motivated methodology.
* Potential for broad impact in applications requiring multiple hypotheses.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited ablation study.
* Tie-breaking strategy could be further explored.
* Oracle evaluation remains computationally expensive.

**5. Recommendation:**

**Accept.**  Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the concerns. sMCL is a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated contribution that addresses an important problem in machine learning.  It has the potential to significantly impact the field, particularly in applications where diverse predictions are crucial. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
