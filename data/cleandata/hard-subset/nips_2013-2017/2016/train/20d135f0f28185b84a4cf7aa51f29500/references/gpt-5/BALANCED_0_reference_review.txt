Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) setting, where the learner outputs M hypotheses and performance is measured by oracle loss (min over hypotheses). Prior MCL methods (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS’12; AISTATS’14) relied on alternating optimization and repeated retraining, which is impractical for deep nets. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme: for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives the gradient. This interleaves assignment and optimization at minibatch scale and trains all members jointly.

They evaluate across three domains and architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (small CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (VGG+LSTM). sMCL consistently improves oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, a sequential boosting-like approach (Dey et al., ICCV’15), and a limited retraining-based MCL baseline, while being faster than MCL. The paper also analyzes emergent specialization: in classification, members specialize by class; in segmentation, specialization is subtler (shape/frequency biases); in captioning, members diversify n-grams and sentence structures.

The work connects to ensemble learning and diversity-inducing methods (bagging, negative correlation, joint losses), multi-solution inference in structured prediction (M-Best/diverse MRFs, submodular selection), and mixture-of-experts style specialization. The main novelty is a practical SGD-based procedure for oracle loss minimization in deep ensembles.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy to implement; integrates with any differentiable model and loss.
- Trains all ensemble members concurrently; substantial speed advantage over retraining-based MCL.
- Demonstrated across heterogeneous tasks with clear, consistent oracle improvements and insightful analyses of specialization.
- Parameter-free core algorithm (k=1 winner), with a useful top-k generalization that shows specialization-performance trade-offs.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is almost entirely in terms of oracle metrics. There is no demonstration that gains translate to end-to-end performance with a realistic selector/reranker.
- Limited theoretical analysis: no convergence guarantees, stability analysis, or discussion of conditions under which specialization emerges vs. collapses (e.g., expert starvation).
- Baselines could be strengthened: (i) smooth min (log-sum-exp) surrogate training, (ii) diversity-promoting losses (e.g., negative correlation), (iii) stronger deep ensembles with bagging/augmentations, (iv) diverse inference methods for captioning beyond small-beam search.
- Fairness concerns in captioning: sharing a single CNN for multiple LSTMs due to memory may confound results; beam search width is small; diversity is assessed primarily via unique n-grams rather than more principled diversity metrics.
- No ablation on sensitivity to M, initialization seeds, capacity imbalance, or data order; no load-balancing mechanism to prevent early dominance of a subset of members.

Quality
Technically sound as an optimization heuristic for the discrete-assignment MCL objective; the gradient derivation is straightforward. The empirical work is careful within scope, showing consistent improvements on oracle metrics and providing qualitative analyses. However, the lack of rigorous analysis (or even empirical probes) of failure modes (e.g., dead experts, oscillations), and of practical downstream benefits, weakens the claims. Baseline coverage is good w.r.t. prior MCL-style training, but could be broader with smooth-min surrogates and diversity losses.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The motivation for oracle loss and the algorithm are clearly presented, and the specialization analyses are informative. A few details could be clearer: tie-breaking strategy; steps taken to mitigate expert starvation; training-time/memory comparisons normalized per wall-clock or flops; completeness of reference formatting.

Originality
The core idea—winner-take-all gradient routing to optimize an oracle objective—is simple but nontrivial in its impact: it turns an impractical alternating procedure into a scalable SGD method applicable to deep nets. While related to hard-EM/K-means style alternation and mixture-of-experts specialization, the application to oracle loss for deep ensembles with concurrent training appears novel and practically important.

Significance
Practical significance is high: many applications benefit from multiple hypotheses and oracle-style evaluation. A broadly applicable, lightweight training trick that yields diverse, specialized ensembles is valuable. The lack of end-to-end evaluation with a selector limits the immediate impact, but the oracle improvements and qualitative diversity are compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a smooth-min baseline: train with L_eps = -eps log sum_m exp(-l_m/eps), annealing eps; compare both accuracy and diversity. This offers a principled soft-assignment alternative and may mitigate starvation.
- Report end-to-end results with a simple selector/reranker (e.g., per-class priors for CIFAR, CRF or boundary-based reranker for segmentation, LM or retrieval-based reranker for captions), to show that oracle gains can translate to practical gains.
- Quantify diversity explicitly (pairwise disagreement for classification/segmentation; self-BLEU/self-CIDEr or embedding diversity for captions). Include calibration/disagreement analyses to understand complementarity.
- Probe stability: multiple seeds; effect of M; capacity asymmetries; curriculum or temperature schedules; examine whether some experts receive near-zero gradients and add a light load-balancing regularizer if needed.
- Strengthen baselines: independent ensembles with bagging/stronger augmentations; larger beam and diverse beam/inference methods for captions; multi-head shared-backbone ensembles to control for parameter counts.
- Discuss computational cost fairly: wall-clock and GPU-hours for each method normalized by total forward/backward passes; clarify memory trade-offs.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general, and effective method that addresses a known bottleneck in MCL for deep nets.
- Consistent improvements across tasks/architectures with insightful analyses of emergent specialization.
- Clear practical value to the community; easy to adopt.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics without downstream selection experiments.
- Limited theoretical and empirical analysis of optimization dynamics and failure modes.
- Baselines and diversity metrics could be expanded; some fairness concerns in the captioning setup.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a practical and broadly useful training procedure with solid empirical evidence and clear exposition. Addressing the suggested evaluations (soft-min baseline, diversity metrics, and a simple selector) would strengthen the case further.
