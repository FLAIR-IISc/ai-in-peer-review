- Proposes sMCL, a stochastic SGD-based training method that optimizes oracle loss by applying gradients only to the ensemble member with lowest error ("winner-take-gradient").

- Builds on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) but removes costly alternating retraining, enabling concurrent training for deep networks.

- Demonstrates strong empirical gains (6–10% relative improvements) across CIFAR10 classification, Pascal VOC segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning.

- Shows ensemble member specialization ("interpretable expertise") as qualitative evidence of why sMCL works.

- Method is simple, parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic, and easy to implement in practice.

- Clear motivation: diverse prediction sets are needed for ambiguous or multi-modal problems; connects to human-like multiple guesses.

- Novel technical contribution: practical "winner-take-gradient" strategy that makes MCL feasible for deep learning.

- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple domains and architectures.

- Paper is well-written, organized, and presents math and figures clearly.

- Lacks theoretical analysis explaining why the stochastic approach converges to better solutions than standard ensembles.

- Specialization analysis is superficial; more detailed study of how and why specialists emerge is needed.

- Comparisons to other diversity-inducing ensemble methods are limited; broader contextual baselines would strengthen claims.

- Does not discuss potential failure cases or limitations (e.g., very large ensembles, extreme class imbalance).

- Evaluation: technically sound experiments, clear methodology, and well-supported empirical claims.

- Originality: meaningful advance over prior MCL work by enabling practical deep-network training.

- Significance: addresses an important problem, likely useful to practitioners in vision and NLP.

- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance (NIPS) despite theoretical gaps.

- Suggested improvements: add theoretical justification for convergence, deepen specialization analysis, and discuss limitations/failure cases.
