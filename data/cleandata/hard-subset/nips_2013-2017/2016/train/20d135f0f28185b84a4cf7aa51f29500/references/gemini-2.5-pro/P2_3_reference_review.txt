### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep neural networks to produce a diverse set of plausible outputs for a given input. Instead of minimizing the average loss across the ensemble, the goal is to minimize the *oracle loss*—the loss of the single best prediction within the set. The authors frame this under the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm and propose a novel, efficient training algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The core idea of sMCL is simple yet effective: during each step of stochastic gradient descent (SGD), for each training example, only the ensemble member that currently yields the lowest loss (the "winner") receives a gradient update. The gradients for all other members are zeroed out for that example. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy encourages different models in the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or modes of the data distribution.

The work is well-situated within existing literature. It directly builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework proposed by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8, 9]. However, the original MCL algorithm relies on an iterative, block coordinate descent approach that alternates between re-assigning data points to models and training each model to convergence. This is computationally infeasible for modern deep networks. The primary contribution of this paper is to adapt this idea into a stochastic, parallel training scheme that is practical for deep learning. This distinguishes it from standard ensemble techniques that aim to reduce variance and improve the accuracy of a single, aggregated prediction, as well as from methods that generate diverse outputs from a single model (e.g., beam search, M-best decoding).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The claims are substantiated by extensive and convincing experiments across three distinct and challenging domains: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). The use of diverse architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed method. The empirical results consistently show that sMCL significantly outperforms relevant baselines, including independently trained ensembles, the original (and much slower) MCL, and a boosting-like sequential training method [5]. The analysis of the "interpretable expertise" that emerges from sMCL training is a major strength, providing valuable insight into the behavior of the algorithm. For instance, the visualization of class-wise specialization in the CIFAR10 experiment (Fig. 4c) is particularly compelling.

*   **Weaknesses:** The theoretical justification for the algorithm is more intuitive than formal. The authors correctly formulate it as a stochastic gradient-based approach to minimizing the upper-bound objective from prior work (Eq. 1). However, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of the optimization dynamics or convergence properties. While the empirical results are strong, a more formal treatment would elevate the work further.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation is established upfront with clear, intuitive examples (the "horse-cow" in segmentation, generic captions). The proposed method, sMCL, is described with precision and is simple to understand and implement. The connection to the original MCL and the clear articulation of why MCL is impractical for deep learning makes the contribution of sMCL obvious and well-motivated. The figures and tables are informative and effectively support the paper's narrative. Algorithm 2 provides a concise and unambiguous description of the method.

*   **Weaknesses:** The claim that the method is "parameter-free" is a slight overstatement. While sMCL does not introduce new optimization hyperparameters (like learning rates or momentum terms), the ensemble size, *M*, is a critical hyperparameter that must be chosen by the user and directly impacts performance and computational cost. This is a minor point but could be stated more precisely.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and practical contribution.

*   **Strengths:** While the high-level goal of minimizing oracle loss is not new, the proposed method for achieving this with deep ensembles is. The key innovation is the formulation of a concurrent, SGD-based optimization strategy that elegantly sidesteps the crippling computational cost of the original MCL's iterative retraining. This adaptation makes the entire paradigm of oracle loss minimization practical for the deep learning era. This is a significant and original contribution that bridges the gap between the MCL concept and state-of-the-art models.

*   **Weaknesses:** The core idea is an adaptation of an existing framework [8] rather than a completely new paradigm. However, in the context of deep learning, such practical and effective adaptations are highly valuable contributions.

**Significance:** The paper has high potential for impact.

*   **Strengths:** The problem of generating diverse, high-quality hypotheses is crucial for many real-world AI systems where ambiguity is inherent and a single "average" prediction can be useless or even misleading (e.g., autonomous driving, medical diagnostics, interactive systems). sMCL provides a simple, general, and effective tool to address this. Its agnosticism to the specific loss function and network architecture makes it broadly applicable. The ease of implementation (essentially a custom layer that routes gradients) lowers the barrier to adoption. The phenomenon of emergent specialization could also inspire new research into modular network design and learning interpretable representations.

*   **Weaknesses:** A practical limitation is the memory and computational cost, as it requires training *M* models simultaneously. The authors acknowledge this by sharing the CNN backbone in the captioning experiment. While this is a reasonable engineering solution, the resource intensiveness could limit its application for extremely large models.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective solution to the important problem of training diverse deep ensembles. The method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and thoroughly validated with impressive results across multiple challenging tasks. The adaptation of the Multiple Choice Learning framework into a practical, stochastic algorithm for deep networks is a significant and novel contribution. The paper is well-written and the findings are likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Practicality:** Provides the first practical algorithm for directly minimizing oracle loss for deep ensembles, solving a major limitation of prior work.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** Demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines on three diverse and relevant tasks.
3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed method is easy to understand, implement, and apply to new problems.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** Addresses a fundamental problem in machine perception and prediction under uncertainty, with broad applicability.
5.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of emergent specialization provides valuable insights into the learning dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is primarily empirical, and a more formal analysis of the optimization would strengthen it.
2.  **Practical Scalability Concerns:** The memory/compute cost of training M models in parallel could be a limitation for some users.

The strengths overwhelmingly outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper.
