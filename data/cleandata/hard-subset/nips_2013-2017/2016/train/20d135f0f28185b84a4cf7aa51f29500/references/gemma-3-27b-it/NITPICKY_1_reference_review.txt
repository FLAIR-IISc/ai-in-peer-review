This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss using a stochastic gradient descent approach. The core idea – a ‘winner-take-gradient’ strategy – is conceptually straightforward and aims to address the practical limitations of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods that required costly retraining. The paper demonstrates the efficacy of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over independent ensembles and, where comparable, over existing MCL approaches.

However, despite the potentially valuable contribution, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor but persistent flaws, ultimately undermining its persuasiveness. While the core idea is sound, the execution and presentation lack the meticulousness expected of a NIPS submission. 

First, the writing quality is inconsistent. While the introduction provides sufficient background, it’s verbose and occasionally repetitive. Phrases like “implicit class confusion” could be articulated more concisely. The transitions between paragraphs are sometimes abrupt. More critically, the notation is occasionally unclear. For example, the use of both `(yi, ŷi)` and `(yi, ŷm i)` for slightly different concepts introduces confusion. A consistent notation throughout would drastically improve readability.

Second, the experimental setup, while covering a reasonable range of tasks, lacks thoroughness in its comparison to baselines. The justification for choosing specific baseline configurations (e.g., the number of meta-iterations for MCL) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more exhaustive comparison, including a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters for each baseline, is needed. The claim that MCL “performs similarly to sMCL on small classification tasks” is vague; providing quantitative results would be more convincing. The statement regarding the Dey et al. [5] method “saturating quickly” is not fully supported by the provided data; the performance drop appears gradual, not sudden.

Third, the analysis of *why* sMCL works is superficial. While the paper observes specialization within ensembles, the explanation remains largely descriptive.  The authors claim "automatic emergence of different interpretable notions of specializations," yet the analysis provided (e.g., Figures 4c, 5b) feels limited. A more detailed examination of the learned representations or activation patterns within ensemble members would provide stronger evidence for this claim. Specifically, merely stating that networks specialize in "cows, tables, and sofas" lacks depth.  What specific features or layers are driving this specialization? 

Fourth, the paper suffers from minor technical inconsistencies. Equation (2) is correct, but the immediately following explanation ("Notice that if fm is the minimum error predictor…") could be misinterpreted to mean *only* the minimum error predictor’s gradient is computed, not that only its gradient is *propagated*.  This subtle distinction is important for understanding the algorithm.  Furthermore, the references are inconsistently formatted. Some are full citations, others are just arXiv IDs. 

Fifth, there are minor formatting issues. The text occasionally overflows the margins. Figure 1 is referred to in the introduction but isn't properly labeled or described in the caption.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of stochastic gradient descent to directly minimize oracle loss in ensembles.
*   Demonstrated improvements over existing methods across diverse tasks.
*   Potential to address limitations of previous MCL approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Inconsistent writing quality and occasionally unclear notation.
*   Insufficiently thorough experimental comparison to baselines.
*   Superficial analysis of the mechanisms driving sMCL’s performance.
*   Minor technical inconsistencies and formatting issues.
*   The improvements, while statistically significant, aren't overwhelmingly large, and the added complexity of sMCL compared to a standard ensemble feels disproportionate to the gains.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the idea behind sMCL is promising, the paper’s execution falls short of NIPS standards. The numerous minor flaws, particularly regarding clarity, thoroughness of analysis, and attention to detail, collectively weaken the paper’s impact.  A major revision addressing these issues would be necessary for reconsideration. As it stands, I recommend **rejection**. The paper demonstrates a reasonable idea but lacks the polish and rigor expected of a NIPS publication.
