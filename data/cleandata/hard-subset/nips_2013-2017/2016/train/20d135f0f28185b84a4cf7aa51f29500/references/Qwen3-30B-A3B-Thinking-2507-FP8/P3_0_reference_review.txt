

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

## Summary
This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss directly. The authors address a gap in existing Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods that require costly retraining of ensemble members for deep networks. sMCL uses a "winner-take-gradient" strategy where, for each example in a batch, gradients are only backpropagated to the ensemble member that produced the lowest error prediction for that example. The method is simple, parameter-free, and applicable to any deep learning architecture trained with SGD. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing significant improvements over classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al.'s method while being substantially faster than MCL.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution by solving a practical bottleneck in Multiple Choice Learning for deep networks. The "winner-take-gradient" approach is elegant and effectively addresses the key limitation of prior MCL methods (retraining), making it feasible for modern deep architectures.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments are thorough and well-designed, covering three distinct tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) with appropriate baselines. The oracle metrics provide a clear and meaningful evaluation of the ensemble's ability to produce diverse high-quality outputs.

3. **Insightful Analysis**: The paper's analysis of specialization (Figures 4c, 5b, 7) is particularly strong, demonstrating how sMCL automatically induces interpretable expertise among ensemble members without explicit design. The transition from k=1 (strict oracle loss minimization) to k=M (traditional ensemble) provides valuable theoretical insight.

4. **Practicality**: sMCL is remarkably simple to implement (requiring only a minor modification to the training loop) and is agnostic to architecture and loss function, making it broadly applicable to the deep learning community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: While the paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free," it uses k=1 for all experiments without exploring how results vary with different k values. The authors should provide a sensitivity analysis showing how oracle performance changes as k increases from 1 to M.

2. **Incomplete Computational Comparison**: The paper states sMCL is 5x faster than MCL but doesn't compare training time to standard independent ensembles (which train all models concurrently). It's unclear whether sMCL offers any computational advantage over classical ensembles, which would be critical for adoption.

3. **Lack of Failure Case Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where sMCL might fail or where the diversity it produces might be detrimental. For instance, in some applications, producing multiple incorrect predictions could be worse than a single moderately correct prediction.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper doesn't address whether the sMCL approach converges to the global minimum of the oracle loss objective or how the "winner-take-gradient" strategy affects the optimization landscape compared to standard SGD.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (Strongly Recommended)

This is a well-executed, technically sound paper with significant practical value. The contribution addresses a genuine limitation in the Multiple Choice Learning literature, and the proposed method is both simple and effective. The experiments are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the approach's superiority across diverse tasks.

The paper makes a clear contribution to ensemble learning that will be valuable to practitioners and researchers. The method is immediately applicable to many deep learning applications where producing diverse, high-quality predictions is important (e.g., autonomous systems, assistive technologies). The paper's clarity, thoroughness, and practical significance outweigh the minor weaknesses noted.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a sensitivity analysis for the k parameter in the main text (Figure 4d-e provides a hint but needs more discussion).

2. Add a training time comparison between sMCL and classical independent ensembles to clarify computational benefits.

3. Discuss potential failure cases and limitations of the approach in the conclusion.

4. Provide a brief theoretical analysis of convergence properties or optimization dynamics.

The paper is well-positioned to make a meaningful contribution to the field. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent candidate for publication at NeurIPS. The work demonstrates how a simple modification to the training procedure can yield significant improvements in ensemble diversity and performance, which is exactly the kind of practical, impactful research NeurIPS values.
