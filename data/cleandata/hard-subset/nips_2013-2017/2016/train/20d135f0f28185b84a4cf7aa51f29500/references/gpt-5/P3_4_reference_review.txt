Summary and contributions
The paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in deep networks by introducing Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple and general “winner-take-gradient” training scheme. For each mini-batch example, only the ensemble member that incurs the lowest loss receives gradient signal; others receive zero for that example. This implements an online hard assignment that approximates the MCL objective (min over members of per-example loss), but avoids the prohibitive retrain-to-convergence cycles of prior MCL formulations. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic (as long as SGD applies) and parameter-free.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate improved oracle metrics (top-k/IoU/CIDEr-oracle) across three settings: CIFAR-10 classification with CNNs, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO captioning with CNN+LSTM. They report consistent gains over independent ensembles, MCL with a fixed number of meta-iterations, and the sequential boosting-style method of Dey et al. The analysis highlights interpretable emergent specialization (class specialists in CIFAR-10; object/category tendencies and shape/frequency biases in segmentation; n-gram/semantic variety in captioning). A k-winners variant reveals a trade-off between specialization and oracle performance.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds squarely on MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al.], replacing alternating retrain/reassign with interleaved hard assignments within SGD. It complements work on generating diverse solutions from a single model (e.g., M-best/Div-M-best, diverse MRF inference), which does not directly transfer to general deep architectures. It also relates to ensemble diversity methods (bagging, negative correlation, joint losses), and sequential training maximizing marginal oracle improvement (Dey et al.). Conceptually, sMCL is very close to hard mixture-of-experts (MoE) with a non-parametric gating function defined by “argmin loss on the fly”; the lack of discussion and comparison to classical MoE (hard/soft gating, load-balancing) is a notable omission. Connections to later diversity-promoting decoding (e.g., diverse beam search) and determinantal/diversity regularizers could be discussed; at the time, analogous ideas existed in structured prediction and ensembles.

Quality
- Methodological soundness: The derivation from the upper-bound MCL objective and the gradient expression is correct; the stochastic optimization scheme is reasonable and easy to implement. However, the optimization has discontinuous, data-dependent hard assignments; no convergence guarantees or stability analyses are provided. In practice, winner-take-all can lead to gradient starvation for some members; the paper partially mitigates this by showing specialization emerges, and explores a k-winners variant, but does not analyze conditions under which collapse occurs or how to prevent it (e.g., load balancing, entropy/temperature).
- Experimental rigor: The breadth across three tasks is a strength. The baselines include independent ensembles, MCL, Dey et al., and beam search for captioning. Results consistently favor sMCL on oracle metrics. That said:
  - Evaluation is exclusively on oracle metrics; there is no demonstration of a downstream selector (human, verifier, learned re-ranker) that would justify the central motivation. An ablation showing performance with a simple learned selector would strengthen claims.
  - Fairness and thoroughness of baselines: MCL is limited to five meta-iterations due to cost; while this supports the practicality argument, the comparison under equal wall-clock budget would be more informative. Missing comparisons include MoE with a learned (even simple) gating network and explicit diversity regularizers (e.g., negative correlation, DPP-style or determinantal objectives).
  - For captioning, beam search is used (width 2) and “oracle CIDEr” vs. “unique n-grams” are reported; diversity measures could be more principled (distinct-n, Self-BLEU, semantic diversity), and beam widths matched across methods. The CNN freezing/fine-tuning constraints due to memory are pragmatic but complicate fairness.
  - Reporting would benefit from individual member accuracy vs. oracle trade-offs, wall-clock training times, and sensitivity to M, batch size, tie-breaking, and the choice of loss.
- Reproducibility: High-level hyperparameters are given, but some operational details are missing (e.g., tie-breaking policy, assignment smoothing, whether any exploration/noise is used early on).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The core idea and algorithm are easy to follow, and the qualitative analyses support the specialization narrative. Minor missing details (above) could be added for full reproducibility. The claim that sMCL is “agnostic to the loss” should be tempered: one still needs a per-example differentiable training loss; evaluation metrics (IoU/CIDEr) are not used directly in training.

Originality
The main novelty is pragmatic: pushing MCL into the deep learning regime via an online, per-example hard assignment within SGD. Conceptually, this is close to hard EM/K-means/MCL and to hard MoE without a learned gate. The idea is simple but useful; the cross-domain application breadth and emergent specialization analysis add value. Positioning relative to MoE and diversity-promoting training/decoding would clarify the incremental vs. novel aspects.

Significance
Practically significant: simple, general, and effective for producing diverse, high-oracle ensembles in settings with intrinsic ambiguity. Likely to be used as a baseline and to inspire follow-ups (e.g., soft assignments, load balancing, learned gating, hybrid decoding). The exclusive focus on oracle metrics limits immediate applicability to end-to-end systems; adding results with a selector would increase impact.

Pros
- Simple, general, parameter-free method; easy to add to existing SGD pipelines.
- Consistent improvements in oracle metrics across three tasks/architectures.
- Emergent, interpretable specialization without architectural changes.
- Useful k-winners ablation illuminating diversity–performance trade-offs.
- Strong practical motivation and speed/performance advantages over retraining-based MCL.

Cons
- No theoretical analysis of convergence/stability; potential for gradient starvation/collapse not systematically addressed.
- Evaluation limited to oracle metrics; no downstream selection experiments.
- Missing or weak baselines: hard/soft MoE with learned gate, explicit diversity regularizers, diverse decoding baselines for captioning.
- Some fairness and reporting gaps (wall-clock budgets, ablations, sensitivity).
- Claim of loss-agnosticism somewhat overstated; training still relies on standard differentiable surrogates.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you report wall-clock training times and compare methods under equal compute budgets?
- How often do members die or get negligible gradients? Do simple load-balancing terms or soft assignments help?
- How sensitive is sMCL to M, batch size, initialization, and tie-breaking?
- Can you include a comparison to a hard/soft MoE with a small gating network and to a negative-correlation ensemble?
- For captioning, can you report distinct-n and Self-BLEU, and match beam widths across methods?
- Provide a small study with a simple learned selector to demonstrate end-to-end utility beyond oracle metrics.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The method is simple, well-motivated, and effective across domains, with clear practical significance. Strengthening baselines and adding downstream selection results would elevate the work further. Confidence: high.
