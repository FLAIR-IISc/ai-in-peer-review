This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss. The core idea is to perform stochastic gradient descent where only the ensemble member with the lowest loss on a given example receives a gradient update. The authors demonstrate the applicability of sMCL to image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing improvements over baseline ensembles and related approaches like MCL.

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation behind multiple choice learning – the need for diverse predictions in scenarios with ambiguity or implicit class confusion. The authors effectively connect their work to prior research in ensemble learning, diversity generation, and MCL, properly citing relevant publications (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al., Dey et al.). The technical contribution, the sMCL algorithm, is presented with sufficient detail and mathematical rigor. The experimental results are comprehensive, showcasing performance gains across different tasks and network architectures. The inclusion of qualitative examples (e.g., segmentation outputs, captions) strengthens the presentation and provides intuitive understanding of the benefits of diversity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** sMCL offers a practical solution to the problem of directly minimizing oracle loss in deep ensembles, overcoming the limitations of existing methods like MCL which require costly retraining.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm is theoretically well-motivated, with a clear derivation of the gradient update rule. The experiments provide strong empirical evidence of its effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the concepts and methodology. The use of diagrams and tables aids in understanding the results.
*   **Significance:** The demonstrated improvements in oracle performance, coupled with the automatic emergence of specialized ensemble members, suggest that sMCL has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in diverse prediction tasks. The method’s generality – applicable to any model trained with SGD – is a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares sMCL to several baselines, a more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., ensemble size, learning rate) on performance would be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:** Although sMCL is presented as more efficient than MCL, the computational cost of training multiple deep networks concurrently is still substantial. A more detailed discussion of the practical resource requirements would be beneficial.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a formalization of the oracle loss minimization and the sMCL algorithm. However, further theoretical analysis of the convergence properties and the conditions for specialization to emerge would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss potential sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter tuning or initialization. It would be valuable to address this limitation.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and technically sound algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods across various tasks.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   The proposed method addresses a challenging problem in machine learning with practical implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of extensive ablation studies limits the understanding of the algorithm's behavior and sensitivity.
*   The computational cost of training large ensembles may be a barrier to adoption.
*   The theoretical analysis is limited, leaving open questions about the convergence and specialization properties of sMCL.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is novel, technically sound, and empirically effective. While some areas could be further strengthened through additional analysis and discussion, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
