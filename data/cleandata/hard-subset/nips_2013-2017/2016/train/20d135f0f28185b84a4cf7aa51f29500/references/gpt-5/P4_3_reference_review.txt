Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses multiple-choice learning (MCL), where an ensemble of M predictors produces M outputs per input and performance is measured by an oracle that selects the minimum-loss output. Prior MCL approaches (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9]) optimize an assignment-and-train objective via alternating block coordinate descent, which is impractical for deep networks due to repeated retraining. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” variant that interleaves assignment and learning within SGD: for each example in a minibatch, only the model with the lowest loss on that example receives a gradient update; others receive zero. This can be viewed as a stochastic block gradient method optimizing the same upper-bound objective as [8], but without meta-iterations. The method is loss- and architecture-agnostic.

They evaluate sMCL on three tasks and architectures: CNNs on CIFAR-10 classification, fully-convolutional networks on PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation, and CNN+LSTM image captioning on MSCOCO. In all cases they report oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr) and compare against independent ensembles, MCL [8] (with five meta-iterations), and the sequential boosting-like method of Dey et al. [5]. sMCL shows consistent oracle improvements and emergent specialization across ensemble members (by classes for CIFAR-10, by object categories/shape priors for segmentation, and by n-grams/sentence structures for captioning). They also discuss the effect of updating the top-k predictors per example (k-winners), finding a trade-off between specialization and oracle accuracy.

The work relates to ensemble learning and diversity (negative correlation [19], data resampling [27,21], joint training [19,26]), M-best/diverse inference [3,15,16,23,24], and MCL [8,9]. It also connects to hard mixture-of-experts and competitive learning, though classical MoE/gating literature is not cited.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation follows the standard MCL upper bound with latent assignment variables. The derivative in Eq. (2) and the stochastic training rule are correct for fixed assignments; the method performs online hard-EM-like updates by recomputing assignments per minibatch. As with deep latent-variable optimization, convergence guarantees are not provided, but the approach is standard and empirically effective.
- Empirical support: Experiments cover multiple modalities and architectures with clear oracle metrics. The baselines are relevant: independent ensembles, MCL [8] (limited by cost), and the sequential method [5]. Reported gains are consistent (often 6–10% relative). The captioning study also compares to beam search (single-model inference).
- Limitations: Oracle-only evaluation omits the trade-off with single-prediction (“top-1”) performance and downstream re-ranking costs. The MCL baseline is run for only five meta-iterations (arguably necessary but potentially disadvantageous). Stability issues (e.g., early winner-take-all collapse, tie-breaking effects), sensitivity to M, batch size, and k-winners are not fully ablated. Captioning experiments share a CNN across models due to memory, which may reduce ensemble diversity compared to independent CNNs.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The problem setting and objective are well motivated. The sMCL algorithm is easy to understand and implement. Experimental setups and metrics are described with sufficient detail for reproduction, including datasets, architectures, and training schedules. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Provide the sMCL “layer” pseudo-code and clarify tie-breaking and implementation details for backprop gating.
- Include numerical tables (not just figures) with absolute oracle scores and standard deviations.
- Report top-1 (single-prediction) performance to quantify the accuracy/diversity trade-off.

Originality
The core idea—online assignments with winner-take-gradient—is conceptually simple and closely related to MCL [8] and hard assignment methods (e.g., hard-EM, competitive learning, mixture-of-experts). The novelty lies in making MCL practical for deep networks through a minimalist SGD-compatible training rule, demonstrating generality across tasks and architectures. The paper differentiates from sequential/retraining approaches by concurrent updates and no extra hyperparameters. Citing classical mixture-of-experts/gating work (e.g., Jacobs et al., Jordan & Jacobs) would better position the contribution relative to prior hard routing.

Significance
The contribution is practically valuable: sMCL is parameter-free, drop-in, and yields consistent improvements in oracle metrics with emergent interpretability. This is relevant for applications where downstream selection exists (e.g., human-in-the-loop, verification, planning). The demonstrated speed and simplicity relative to MCL [8] make it likely to be adopted. While conceptually incremental, the breadth of experiments and generality across loss functions/architectures increase impact.

Pros
- Simple, general, and efficient algorithm; easy to integrate with SGD-based deep nets.
- Consistent oracle gains across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Emergent and interpretable specialization without explicit gating.
- Solid baselines and analyses (including k-winners ablation, qualitative diversity).

Cons
- Oracle-only focus; lacks analysis of single-prediction accuracy and practical selection costs.
- Limited MCL baseline due to computational cost; fairness could be discussed more explicitly.
- Potential instability/degeneracy (early dominance, dead experts) not deeply analyzed.
- Missing connections to classical mixture-of-experts and competitive learning literature.
- Captioning comparison to beam search mixes training vs inference diversity; further disentanglement would help.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Report the effect of sMCL on top-1 performance and calibration; quantify the accuracy–diversity trade-off.
- Analyze stability: fraction of data per expert over epochs; effect of random seeds; strategies to avoid dead experts (e.g., entropy regularization, soft assignments, temperature annealing).
- Expand ablations: vary M, batch size, k-winners, and tie-breaking; report assignment entropy.
- Clarify whether ensemble members share any parameters besides the shared CNN in captioning; discuss memory/computation trade-offs.
- Cite and discuss classical mixture-of-experts/gating work and contrast with sMCL (no gating network, hard routing via loss).

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a clear, technically sound, and broadly applicable training procedure that makes MCL practical for deep ensembles, with convincing empirical gains and useful qualitative insights into emergent expertise. While the idea is simple and related to known paradigms, the demonstrated generality, efficiency, and utility justify publication.
