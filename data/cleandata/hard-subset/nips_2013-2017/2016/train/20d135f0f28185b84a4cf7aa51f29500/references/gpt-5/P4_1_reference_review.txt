Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where a learner outputs M predictions per input and is evaluated by oracle loss (the minimum task loss over the M outputs). The contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL): a simple, SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” training scheme for deep ensembles. For each training example, only the model in the ensemble that incurs the lowest loss receives the gradient update; others get zero gradient on that example. This can be viewed as an online version of the assignment step in prior MCL, interleaved with mini-batch SGD rather than alternating full retraining. The method is loss-agnostic and applies to standard deep architectures.

The paper situates itself among: (1) ensemble learning methods aimed at inducing diversity (bagging, negative correlation, joint losses), (2) methods for producing multiple diverse solutions for structured prediction (M-Best diverse, DPP-based diversity), and (3) the MCL framework of Guzman-Rivera et al., which alternates between assignment and retraining. The main novelty is a practical, concurrent SGD-based optimization for deep networks that avoids costly retraining and yields emergent specialization. Results are shown on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation with FCN, and MSCOCO image captioning with CNN+LSTM, consistently improving oracle metrics over independent ensembles, Dey et al. (submodular/boosting-like), and a multi-iteration MCL baseline (with notable training speedups). The paper also analyzes emergent specialization patterns across tasks.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The sMCL update is a straightforward subgradient method for the min-over-experts objective: the derivative wrt fm(xi) is that of the loss for the argmin expert and zero otherwise (ties arbitrarily broken). This is consistent with optimizing the upper-bound assignment objective with hard one-hot assignments at each step. While convergence guarantees are not provided (non-convex deep nets), the approach is standard in spirit (hard-EM/K-means-like) and compatible with SGD practice.
- Experimental support: Three domains and architectures are covered. sMCL improves oracle top-k accuracy (CIFAR-10), oracle IoU (VOC segmentation), and oracle CIDEr-D (MSCOCO captioning), and exhibits faster oracle loss reduction vs independent ensembles. The analysis of specialization (class-wise assignments for CIFAR, qualitative diversity for segmentation and captioning) is informative. However:
  - Fairness/controls: In segmentation, sMCL is fine-tuned from an independently trained ensemble; it is unclear whether MCL and other baselines receive identical initialization/fine-tuning. For captioning, comparisons mix beam search breadth across methods; controlling for total number of hypotheses (e.g., M×beam) would strengthen claims. Reporting non-oracle metrics (e.g., single-prediction or average prediction quality) would clarify trade-offs.
  - Stability/degeneracy: The winner-take-gradient rule can, in principle, starve some learners early. The paper shows emergent specialization, but does not quantify per-model sample loads over time or propose safeguards (e.g., load balancing or entropy regularization). An ablation on initialization sensitivity would help.
  - Compute/overhead: Training M networks jointly with per-example assignment is practical, but the paper could better quantify wall-clock, memory, and throughput vs baselines beyond the “5× faster than MCL” claim tied to a specific number of meta-iterations.
- Reproducibility: High-level hyperparameters are provided; pseudocode clarifies the algorithm; use of public architectures/tooling helps. More training details (batch size effects, tie-breaking policy, learning rate schedules per task, code availability) would further aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The problem setting, objective, and algorithm are concisely described; the relation to prior MCL is explicit. Figures effectively illustrate specialization and output diversity. Suggestions:
- Explicitly note that the sMCL update is a subgradient of the min loss and discuss tie handling.
- Clarify initialization and training schedules across all baselines for fairness.
- Report per-expert assignment distributions over training time to illustrate stability and prevent starvation.
- Provide a brief complexity/runtime analysis and memory footprint.

Originality
The central idea—integrating MCL’s hard assignment into minibatch SGD for deep nets—is simple but practically impactful. It is closely related to classic mixture-of-experts with hard gating (e.g., Jacobs, Jordan, Nowlan, Hinton, 1991–1993) and winner-take-all training, which are not cited and should be discussed to properly position the contribution (hard routing vs learned gating; absence of balancing; application to structured outputs). Within deep structured prediction, the concurrent, parameter-free implementation for standard architectures is novel for the time. The k-winner ablation (interpolating between k=1 oracle and k=M independent) is a nice addition.

Significance
The method is broadly applicable, easy to implement, and demonstrates consistent improvements on diverse tasks, with interpretable emergent specialization. Given the cost of retraining deep models, a practical MCL-compatible SGD procedure is valuable. The impact is primarily practical rather than theoretical, but likely to be adopted by practitioners interested in multi-hypothesis outputs and ensemble diversity.

Pros
- Simple, general, parameter-free algorithm compatible with SGD and standard architectures.
- Consistent gains on oracle metrics across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Concurrent training avoids costly retraining/sequential procedures; empirical speedups.
- Clear analysis of emergent specialization; k-winner interpolation study.

Cons
- Limited discussion of connections to classic mixture-of-experts and winner-take-all literature; missing citations.
- Potential instability/imbalance not analyzed; no mechanisms to prevent expert starvation.
- Some evaluation fairness concerns (initialization for baselines; controlling total number of hypotheses/beam).
- Focus exclusively on oracle metrics; no analysis of non-oracle performance or downstream selection/reranking.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add related work and discussion on mixture-of-experts (Jacobs et al., Jordan & Jacobs) and winner-take-all training; clarify how sMCL differs (no learned gate; hard assignment; application to structured losses).
- Report per-expert load curves, parameter utilization, and an ablation on initialization sensitivity; consider simple balancing regularizers or temperature-scaled soft assignments as a comparison.
- For captioning, compare methods under equal candidate budgets (e.g., report oracle scores for the same total number of sentences via M×beam and for beam-only baselines).
- Provide uniform initialization/fine-tuning protocols across baselines or clearly justify differences; add wall-clock and memory comparisons.
- Include non-oracle metrics and a brief discussion of how sMCL outputs can be integrated with downstream reranking.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed paper with a practical and impactful idea, with room to improve positioning relative to classic MoE and to strengthen evaluation controls.
