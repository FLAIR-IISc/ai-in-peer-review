

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss directly through a "winner-take-gradient" approach. The authors claim their method avoids costly retraining of previous MCL approaches, is applicable to any SGD-trained model, and demonstrates emergent specialization in ensemble members across classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies an important gap in ensemble learning for structured output tasks where multiple plausible predictions are valuable.
- The sMCL algorithm is conceptually simple and well-motivated by the oracle loss minimization objective.
- The experimental results across three different tasks demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in oracle accuracy metrics.
- The paper provides clear evidence of emergent specialization in ensemble members, which is a compelling finding.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Technical Implementation Details:** The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free" but doesn't explain how this is achieved across different tasks. For instance, the learning rate schedule for the CIFAR10 experiments is described as "initial learning rate of 0.001 which drops to 0.0001 after 4000 iterations" but doesn't specify whether this was tuned for sMCL or used as-is from standard training protocols.

2. **Reproducibility Issues:** The paper states "we train on the Pascal VOC 2011 training set augmented with extra segmentations provided in [10]" but [10] is not properly cited in the references (it appears as "and A, M. Everingham, L. Van Gool, C.K.I. Williams, J. Winn, Zisserman. The PASCAL Visual Object Classes Challenge 2011 , , " with multiple formatting errors). This makes reproduction impossible without additional context.

3. **Misleading Speed Claims:** The paper claims sMCL is "5x faster than MCL" but doesn't provide sufficient context. The speedup factor of 5 appears to be derived from the number of meta-iterations (5) in MCL, not actual wall-clock time comparisons. Without concrete timing results on comparable hardware, this claim is unsubstantiated.

4. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses "MCL" to refer to both the Multiple Choice Learning paradigm (a general concept) and the specific algorithm by Guzman-Rivera et al. (Algorithm 1), creating significant confusion for readers. The authors should consistently distinguish between the general framework and their specific algorithm.

5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Multiple instances of inconsistent formatting: "th Conference" (should be "The Conference"), inconsistent spacing in equations (e.g., "n∑ i=1"), and reference formatting errors (e.g., extra commas in references). These minor errors undermine the paper's professionalism.

6. **Theoretical Justification:** While the paper presents a convincing empirical case, it lacks any theoretical analysis of why sMCL converges to a good solution, or how it relates to the optimization landscape of the oracle loss. The claim that "sMCL is generalizable to any learner trained by stochastic gradient descent" isn't supported by theoretical analysis.

7. **Comparison Baselines:** The comparison to Dey et al. [5] is problematic since the authors themselves state this method "is not directly applicable to general deep architectures." This creates a false comparison that doesn't reflect the actual applicability of the methods.

8. **Missing Statistical Significance:** The paper reports "typical improvements of 6-10%" but doesn't provide statistical tests to confirm these differences are significant. Without this, the claimed improvements are difficult to evaluate.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea with promising empirical results, the numerous technical and formatting issues, combined with unsubstantiated claims and insufficient theoretical justification, prevent it from meeting NeurIPS standards. The paper's core contribution is modest—it's a straightforward adaptation of existing multiple-choice learning to a stochastic gradient framework with limited theoretical novelty. The experimental validation is generally good but suffers from reproducibility issues and lacks sufficient statistical rigor.

The paper would benefit from:
- Correcting all formatting and citation errors
- Providing detailed hyperparameter settings for all experiments
- Clarifying the relationship between MCL (paradigm) and MCL (algorithm)
- Adding statistical significance tests to the reported improvements
- Providing concrete wall-clock time comparisons for the speedup claim

While the paper has some interesting empirical findings, these minor but numerous flaws significantly undermine the paper's technical credibility and reproducibility, which are essential for a NeurIPS publication. The authors have made a good start but have not delivered a sufficiently rigorous contribution to merit acceptance. The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the flaws in presentation and execution are too numerous to overlook.

**Recommendation:** Reject
