## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss. The core idea is a stochastic block gradient descent approach where, during backpropagation, gradients are only applied to the ensemble member achieving the lowest loss for each example. This contrasts with previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches that require iterative retraining of ensemble members. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across various tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning) and network architectures, showing improved performance and the emergence of interpretable specialization within the ensemble.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the foundation of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al., 2014, 2016] and connects it to the broader field of ensemble learning. It positions itself as a practical solution to the computational challenges associated with existing MCL methods, which rely on iterative retraining. The authors carefully contrast sMCL with related approaches like diversity-inducing resampling, submodular optimization [Dey et al., 2017], and methods for generating multiple solutions from single models.  The work acknowledges and addresses the limitations of these existing techniques, particularly their inability to scale to modern deep learning architectures.  The paper correctly positions itself as the first to formalize MCL ideas in the context of deep networks and provide a scalable training algorithm.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** sMCL offers a significant practical advancement over existing MCL methods by enabling concurrent training of ensemble members, overcoming the retraining bottleneck.  The proposed stochastic gradient descent approach is clever and well-motivated.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling experimental evidence across diverse tasks and architectures.  Consistently, sMCL outperforms classical ensembles, MCL, and other baselines, demonstrating its broad applicability.
* **Interpretability:** The authors provide insightful analysis of the learned ensembles, demonstrating the emergence of specialization among members. This is particularly evident in the classification experiments where clear label-space clustering is observed. The qualitative examples for segmentation and captioning also support this claim.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the gradient update rule is clear and concise, grounding the method in sound mathematical principles.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the contributions. Algorithm 2 clearly outlines the sMCL training procedure.
* **Parameter-Free & Agnostic:** The method’s parameter-free nature and independence from specific loss functions add to its versatility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Modern Ensemble Techniques:** While the comparison to MCL and Dey et al. is fair, the paper would benefit from a comparison to more recent ensemble techniques that promote diversity, such as those leveraging adversarial training or knowledge distillation for diversity.  It's unclear how sMCL compares to, say, training ensembles with different dropout masks or weight perturbation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters like batch size, learning rate, and momentum. While these are mentioned in the CIFAR-10 experiment description, a more systematic analysis of their impact would be valuable.
* **Scalability Concerns (Implicit):** While sMCL addresses the retraining issue, the memory requirements of training a large ensemble of deep networks concurrently can still be substantial. The captioning experiment notes limitations due to memory constraints, which hints at potential scalability issues as ensemble size and model complexity increase.
* **Theoretical Analysis is Limited:** The theoretical connection between minimizing oracle loss and the emergence of specialization could be further explored. A more formal analysis might provide insights into *why* sMCL leads to the observed specialization patterns.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective algorithm (sMCL) that addresses a key limitation of previous Multiple Choice Learning approaches.
* The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the broad applicability of sMCL across diverse tasks and architectures.
* The emergence of interpretable specialization within the ensembles is a significant and insightful finding.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and grounded in sound theoretical principles.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to the broader landscape of ensemble diversity techniques is somewhat limited.
* The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity is lacking.
* The scalability concerns, while not fully prohibitive, are worth acknowledging.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning.  The proposed sMCL algorithm is a practical and effective solution for training diverse deep ensembles, and the results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them considerably.  I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS and recommend **acceptance**.

**Confidence:** 8/10
