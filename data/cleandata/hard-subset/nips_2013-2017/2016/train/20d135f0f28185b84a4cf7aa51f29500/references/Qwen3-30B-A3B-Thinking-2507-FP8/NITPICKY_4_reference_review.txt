

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning" (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss. The authors argue that for many perception tasks, producing a set of plausible hypotheses (rather than a single prediction) is more useful for downstream applications. Their key contribution is a stochastic optimization approach that avoids the costly retraining required by previous MCL methods. The paper demonstrates sMCL on image classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks, claiming significant improvements over baselines with 5x speedup.

**Strengths:**
- The motivation for multiple-choice learning is compelling, with clear examples of ambiguous real-world scenarios where single-prediction models fail.
- The technical idea of "winner-take-gradient" is elegant and well-motivated, addressing a genuine limitation in existing MCL approaches.
- The experimental results show consistent improvements across multiple tasks and architectures, with a clear demonstration of emergent specialization.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inaccuracies:** The paper claims "the gradient of the loss for each example is backpropagated only to the lowest error predictor" (Section 3). This is misleading - the gradient is scaled by the indicator variable (pi,m), which is 1 for the lowest error predictor and 0 otherwise. This distinction is important for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

2. **Overstated Claims:** The paper states "sMCL is parameter-free" but fails to acknowledge that ensemble size M is a hyperparameter requiring tuning. The claim that "sMCL is agnostic to the exact form of loss function" is also problematic - the method requires differentiable loss functions, which excludes many structured prediction settings.

3. **Experimental Rigor:** The paper reports "typical improvements of 6-10%" without statistical significance testing. In machine learning, such claims require p-values or confidence intervals to be scientifically valid. The claimed 5x speedup over MCL is also not quantified (wall-clock time? iterations?).

4. **Formatting and Consistency Issues:**
   - "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" (Introduction) should be "The Conference..."
   - "Intepretable" (Section 4.3) is misspelled as "Interpretable"
   - "Oracle Evaluation" section lacks a precise definition of what constitutes an "oracle version" of a metric
   - References to figures (e.g., "Figure 1," "Figure 3") appear throughout but are not included in the provided text

5. **Methodological Ambiguity:** The paper states "sMCL is simple to implement... and simply involves introducing one new sMCL layer into existing ensemble architectures" but provides no details on how this layer would be implemented or how it would interact with common deep learning frameworks.

6. **Inadequate Comparison:** The paper claims MCL is "not feasible with training deep networks, since modern architectures can take weeks or months to train a single model once," but doesn't provide specific timing data to support this claim. The comparison to MCL should include actual training times, not just a qualitative statement.

7. **Technical Nuances:** The paper doesn't address whether the "winner-take-gradient" approach could lead to extreme specialization where some ensemble members become useless for most inputs. The claim that "the exact same algorithm leads to the automatic emergence of different interpretable notions of specializations" requires stronger evidence.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a novel technical approach, the numerous minor technical inaccuracies, overstatements, and formatting inconsistencies significantly undermine its scientific rigor. The claim of being "the first to formalize these ideas in the context of deep networks" is not sufficiently justified, as the paper doesn't clearly distinguish its method from previous work beyond the stochastic optimization aspect.

The paper's technical claims require more precise mathematical formulations and experimental validation. The lack of statistical significance testing for the reported improvements is particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, where such rigor is expected.

Given these issues, I recommend rejection. The paper presents a potentially valuable idea but fails to deliver the technical precision and experimental rigor expected at NIPS. The minor flaws accumulate to a point where the paper's contribution is significantly diminished, making it difficult to evaluate the true merits of the approach.

The authors should address these technical inaccuracies, clarify their claims, and provide more rigorous experimental validation before resubmission to a venue like NIPS.
