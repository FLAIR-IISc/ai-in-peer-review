Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where a learner produces M outputs per input and performance is measured by the oracle loss (min over outputs). The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple, SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” training strategy for deep ensembles: for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient updates. This mirrors the hard-assignment step of prior MCL but interleaves assignment with minibatch SGD, enabling joint, concurrent training of deep models.

The approach is evaluated on three tasks/architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM). Across tasks, sMCL improves oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr-D) relative to independently trained ensembles, sequential/boosting-like training [Dey et al.], and the original alternating MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al.], with substantial training-time savings versus MCL. Analysis shows “emergent expertise” where ensemble members specialize (e.g., class-wise specialization for CIFAR-10, more nuanced structural specialization for segmentation, diverse n-gram usage for captioning). An extension selecting the bottom-k losses (k-of-M) interpolates between oracle-loss training (k=1) and standard ensembling (k=M), empirically trading specialization and diversity.

This work builds directly on MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS/AISTATS], and relates to ensemble diversity methods (bagging, negative correlation), M-best/diverse inference in structured models, and multi-modal regression. Conceptually it is also close to (hard) mixture-of-experts with per-example assignment, but differs in using per-example instantaneous loss as the (non-learned) gating signal.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and gradient structure are correctly derived from the MCL upper-bound with hard assignments. The proposed training rule is a straightforward and sound SGD heuristic for this non-smooth objective. There is no convergence analysis (expected, given non-convex deep nets and non-differentiable min), but empirical behavior is consistent and stable across tasks.
- Empirical evidence: The paper reports consistent oracle metric gains across three domains and architectures, and meaningful speedups versus MCL by avoiding repeated retraining. The analyses of specialization (class-wise distributions, qualitative examples) are informative. The k-of-M ablation is useful.
- Experimental limitations: (i) Comparisons to MCL are curtailed by computational cost; a compute-budget-matched comparison would be more informative than a fixed number of meta-iterations. (ii) Baselines do not include learned gating mixture-of-experts or other diversity-promoting training losses for deep nets; such comparisons would strengthen the case that improvements derive from oracle-loss training rather than implicit regularization. (iii) Captioning comparisons focus on oracle CIDEr-D and unique n-grams; more diversity measures (e.g., distinct-n, self-BLEU) and decoding baselines (e.g., larger beam, diversity-promoting decoding) would better contextualize the gains. (iv) The risk of model collapse (one expert winning most examples) is not theoretically analyzed; although empirically specialization emerges, some monitoring or balancing mechanism could be discussed. (v) Most architectures/datasets are mid-scale and somewhat dated; stronger backbones were available for classification by the time (e.g., ResNet), though the focus is on training strategy rather than SOTA.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, the problem is well-motivated by downstream oracle selection use-cases, and the method is concisely described. The connection to the MCL objective and the resulting gradient rule is transparent. Figures illustrating specialization and qualitative outputs are helpful. Implementation details are mostly sufficient, though additional specifics would aid reproducibility (tie-breaking policy for equal losses; measures to prevent expert starvation; batch balancing; exact training schedules for all baselines; how gradients are aggregated through the shared CNN in captioning).

Originality
The main novelty is a practical, SGD-based formulation of MCL for deep networks that enables concurrent training and scales to modern architectures. Relative to prior MCL (alternating retraining) and sequential/boosting-style approaches, this is a meaningful algorithmic contribution. Conceptually, the approach is closely related to hard-EM and mixture-of-experts style per-sample assignment; acknowledging and contrasting to learned gating MoE would better position the contribution. The k-of-M extension is a minor but useful variation.

Significance
Producing multiple hypotheses is important for many applications with downstream re-ranking/oracles. sMCL is simple, parameter-free, widely applicable across architectures and losses, and delivers consistent oracle metric improvements and training speedups over prior MCL. Given the broad relevance of ensembles and multi-hypothesis prediction, this has potential impact as a practical training recipe.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy to implement; works with any SGD-trained model and loss.
- Concurrent training avoids costly retraining; substantial speedup vs. prior MCL.
- Consistent oracle metric gains across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Emergent specialization is demonstrated and analyzed; k-of-M ablation is informative.
- Clear motivation for oracle loss in downstream systems.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing comparisons to mixture-of-experts with learned gating and other deep diversity-promoting training objectives; adding such baselines would strengthen claims.
- Limited theoretical discussion of convergence and collapse avoidance; report assignment entropy over training, fraction of data per expert, and any balancing strategies.
- Fairness of MCL comparison: include results under equal wall-clock or gradient update budgets.
- Captioning evaluation could include stronger decoding baselines (larger beam, diversity-promoting search) and additional diversity metrics.
- Scope is limited to mid-scale settings and older backbones; including a stronger classifier would show scalability.

Questions for authors
- How often do experts collapse/starve in practice? Any safeguards (e.g., epsilon-greedy assignment, load balancing)?
- How sensitive are results to tie-breaking and batch size?
- Can you report compute-matched MCL results?
- How does sMCL affect single-prediction performance (non-oracle metrics)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable training method for a well-motivated objective (oracle loss).
- Broad applicability across tasks and architectures with consistent gains.
- Simplicity and speed relative to prior MCL make it useful to the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lacks comparisons to learned gating MoE and other diversity-inducing training paradigms.
- Limited theoretical analysis and potential for expert collapse not deeply examined.
- Some baselines and evaluations (captioning) could be strengthened.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The contribution is incremental relative to MCL but practically significant, clearly presented, and supported by multi-domain experiments.
