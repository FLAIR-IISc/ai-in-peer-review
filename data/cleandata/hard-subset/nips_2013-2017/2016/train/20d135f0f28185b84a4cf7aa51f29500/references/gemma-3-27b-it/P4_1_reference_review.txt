This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep ensembles designed to minimize oracle loss. The core idea is to leverage the gradients from all ensemble members, but only backpropagate the error to the member that provides the lowest loss for each example. This avoids the computationally expensive retraining steps required by previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showcasing improved oracle performance and emergent specialization among ensemble members.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, technical details, and experimental results. It effectively positions sMCL within the context of MCL and ensemble learning, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the advantages of their proposed approach. The mathematical formulation is concise and easy to follow. The experiments are comprehensive, covering a diverse set of tasks and network architectures. The analysis of ensemble member specialization is insightful, providing qualitative evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The related work section is also well-curated, acknowledging relevant prior research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The sMCL algorithm presents a significant improvement over existing MCL methods by enabling concurrent training of ensemble members using stochastic gradient descent, avoiding costly retraining.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and logically sound. The algorithm is well-defined and appears to be a practical solution for training diverse ensembles.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on a variety of tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning) and with different network architectures. The results consistently show improvements over baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, even for readers unfamiliar with MCL. The use of diagrams and tables enhances clarity.
*   **Significance:** The ability to train diverse deep ensembles efficiently has the potential to improve performance in a wide range of applications, particularly those requiring robust and reliable predictions. The emergent expertise among ensemble members is also a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the comparison to MCL is relevant, the authors could benefit from a more thorough comparison to other diversity-inducing techniques like Deep Ensembles with different initialization and data augmentation techniques. Specifically, a comparison to techniques utilizing methods from recent works on model diversity would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the sensitivity of sMCL to hyperparameters such as learning rate and batch size. A brief analysis of hyperparameter effects would strengthen the paper.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are compelling, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of sMCL would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** While faster than previous MCL methods, the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of sMCL compared to training a standard ensemble. A more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between training time and performance would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and technically sound algorithm that addresses a significant challenge in ensemble learning.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across a range of tasks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and findings.
*   The potential impact of sMCL is significant, as it could lead to improved performance in a variety of applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The comparison to other diversity-inducing techniques is limited.
*   The paper lacks a detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
*   A more rigorous theoretical justification of the algorithm would be desirable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The sMCL algorithm is a novel and effective approach for training diverse deep ensembles, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above, particularly a more comprehensive comparison to strong baselines and a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
