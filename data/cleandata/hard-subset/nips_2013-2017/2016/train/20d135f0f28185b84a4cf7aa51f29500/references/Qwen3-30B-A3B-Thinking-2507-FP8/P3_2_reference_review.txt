

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that directly optimizes for the oracle loss (the loss of the best prediction among M outputs). The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9], which requires costly alternating training of ensemble members, by proposing a stochastic gradient descent-based approach that assigns gradients only to the lowest-error predictor per example. The paper demonstrates sMCL's effectiveness across image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements over baselines including classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. [5].

## Quality Assessment
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a clear, correct derivation of the gradient update rule (Equation 2) that enables the "winner-take-gradient" strategy. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering multiple architectures and tasks, and the results are well-presented with appropriate oracle metrics. The authors responsibly acknowledge limitations, such as MCL's computational infeasibility for complex tasks.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in theoretical grounding and experimental comparison. The theoretical justification for why sMCL converges to the same solution as MCL is insufficiently developed. The comparison with MCL is problematic: the authors admit they couldn't run MCL to convergence for segmentation and captioning tasks due to computational constraints, and only used 5 meta-iterations for MCL. This makes the comparison unfair, as MCL would likely perform better with sufficient meta-iterations. The paper also fails to explore the impact of tie-breaking strategies in the "winner-take-gradient" approach, which could significantly affect results.

## Clarity and Presentation
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the problem and method. Figures 4-7 effectively illustrate the emergence of specialization in ensemble members. The technical description of sMCL (Algorithm 2) is concise and accessible. The paper adequately explains how to implement sMCL, making it easy for practitioners to adopt.

## Originality
While the stochastic extension of MCL to deep networks is novel in the context of deep learning, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from the extensive literature on ensemble diversity. The authors mention diversity as a goal but don't deeply engage with the rich literature on diversity-inducing techniques (e.g., [26, 27] in the related work). The connection to "emergent expertise" is interesting but not sufficiently contrasted with existing work on ensemble specialization.

## Significance
The practical advantage of sMCL (5x faster than MCL) is significant for deep learning applications where training time is prohibitive. The consistent improvements across multiple domains (6-10% over baselines) demonstrate the method's broad utility. However, the paper doesn't convincingly argue why sMCL is superior to simpler approaches like training multiple models with different random seeds (the "Indp. ensembles" baseline), which already produces some diversity.

## Strengths
1. Practical, efficient solution to a meaningful problem in ensemble learning
2. Strong empirical results across multiple domains and architectures
3. Clear evidence of automatic specialization in ensemble members
4. Simple implementation that's agnostic to architecture and loss function

## Weaknesses
1. Unfair comparison with MCL due to insufficient meta-iterations
2. Insufficient theoretical justification for the stochastic approach
3. Superficial engagement with ensemble diversity literature
4. Failure to address potential pitfalls of the "winner-take-gradient" strategy

## Recommendation
I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to ensemble learning, particularly for deep networks where training time is prohibitive. The core idea of sMCL is sound and practically significant, and the results are compelling across multiple tasks.

However, the authors should address the following points before publication:
1. Provide a more thorough theoretical comparison between sMCL and MCL
2. Run MCL for sufficient meta-iterations (or acknowledge the computational limitations more explicitly) to make a fair comparison
3. Expand the discussion of related work on ensemble diversity to better position their contribution
4. Address the potential impact of tie-breaking strategies in the "winner-take-gradient" approach

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it addresses an important practical problem in deep learning with a simple, effective solution that's applicable across multiple domains. The significance of the work is demonstrated through consistent improvements across diverse tasks, and the method is simple enough to be widely adopted.

This work represents a meaningful step toward practical training of diverse deep ensembles, with the potential to impact both research and industry applications where multiple plausible predictions are valuable. The authors have provided a strong foundation for future work in this area.
