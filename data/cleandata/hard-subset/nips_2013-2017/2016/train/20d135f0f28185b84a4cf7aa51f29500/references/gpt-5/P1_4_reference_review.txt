Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple, parameter-free, SGD-compatible procedure to train deep ensembles to minimize oracle loss (min over M predictions per input). The method replaces the alternating assignment/retraining of prior MCL with a per-minibatch “winner-take-gradient” update: for each example, only the predictor with the lowest loss backpropagates gradients; others receive zero gradient. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, trains all ensemble members concurrently, and induces emergent specialization. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation, and MSCOCO image captioning show consistent gains in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, MCL (with limited meta-iterations), and a boosting-like approach [Dey et al.], with qualitative analyses indicating interpretable specialization (class clusters for classification, shape/frequency biases for segmentation, and diverse n-gram structures for captioning). Compared to beam search over a single captioning model, sMCL achieves higher diversity at competitive oracle scores.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on Multiple Choice Learning [Guzman-Rivera et al.] and diversity-aware prediction in MRFs (M-Best Diverse); unlike those, it targets deep architectures trained with SGD, eliminating costly sequential retraining. The update rule is closely related to hard-EM/K-Means, latent SVM/mixture-of-experts style hard assignment (winner-take-all), and negative correlation/diversity in ensembles, but is instantiated here for minimizing oracle loss with deep nets. Relative to expert routing or gating (mixture-of-experts), sMCL dispenses with a learned gate and uses task loss to drive specialization.

Quality
– Technical soundness: The method is a straightforward stochastic block coordinate descent for the MCL upper bound, with correct gradient reasoning (the min induces a subgradient equal to the gradient of the argmin model). The simplicity is a strength; however, theoretical treatment is light. There is no analysis of convergence, specialization stability, or starvation avoidance (dead experts). Empirically, specialization emerges, but safeguards are not discussed.
– Empirical support: Results across three tasks and architectures show consistent improvements in oracle metrics, faster training than MCL, and convincing qualitative diversity. The captioning comparison to beam search is fair and highlights complementary benefits. For segmentation, the MCL baseline is limited by feasibility (only five meta-iterations); this weakens the strength of the head-to-head comparison but is understandable.
– Experimental details are mostly sufficient; hyperparameters and training setups are described. Some reproducibility details are missing (e.g., tie-breaking policy, batch composition when some experts starve early, per-expert update counts), and code availability is not mentioned.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The objective, algorithm, and intuition are presented succinctly, and figures effectively illustrate specialization and diversity. Minor issues: the related work section could more explicitly position sMCL against mixture-of-experts with hard gating; captions and oracle metric definitions are clear but could benefit from a brief note on subgradient interpretation for the min loss.

Originality
The core idea—stochastic hard assignment to minimize oracle loss with deep ensembles—is incremental but novel in its practicality and scope for deep learning. The contribution is primarily an optimization/engineering insight that makes MCL feasible for modern architectures, plus empirical demonstrations of emergent expertise across tasks. Connections to long-standing ideas (hard-EM, mixture-of-experts) are acknowledged implicitly but could be more fully discussed.

Significance
The work addresses a practically important need: producing diverse sets of plausible hypotheses for downstream selection. The proposed method is simple, broadly applicable, and yields measurable gains with minimal code changes. This has clear impact for structured prediction and ambiguous tasks. While oracle metrics do not capture end-to-end system performance, they are standard for this setting. The method is likely to be adopted and extended.

Strengths
– Simple, general, and efficient algorithm that makes MCL practical for deep nets; trains all members in one pass.
– Strong empirical results across classification, segmentation, and captioning; consistent oracle gains and qualitative diversity.
– Emergent, interpretable specialization without explicit gating or extra parameters.
– Clear exposition and useful analyses (e.g., k-winners ablation showing specialization-performance tradeoff).

Weaknesses and suggestions
– Theory: No convergence or stability analysis; discuss subgradient view of min loss and conditions for avoiding expert starvation. A soft-assignment variant (e.g., softmax over negative loss with temperature annealing) or load-balancing regularizer could be explored to mitigate collapse; you partially study top-k but not soft k.
– Baselines: Include a mixture-of-experts baseline with a learned gate (hard or soft) and/or latent SVM-style alternating training to better situate sMCL. For captioning, compare to larger beam sizes and to diverse beam search to further contextualize diversity gains.
– Metrics: Oracle metrics are necessary but insufficient. Add diversity measures beyond unique n-grams (e.g., pairwise BLEU/ROUGE distance; segmentation mask disagreement) and report non-oracle downstream selection (e.g., re-ranking with a learned scorer or simple heuristics) to demonstrate practical end-to-end benefit.
– Fairness/compute: Clarify compute parity across methods. Since sMCL backpropagates through only one expert per example, ensure independent ensembles receive comparable total gradient steps per model.
– Implementation details: Document tie-breaking, initialization sensitivity, and handling of dormant experts. The segmentation setup initializes from a standard ensemble; report whether training from scratch with sMCL works equally well.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is sMCL to initialization and learning rate? Do you observe persistent dead experts, and if so, how do you mitigate this?
2) What is the effect of soft assignments (e.g., temperature-controlled probabilities over experts) on specialization and performance?
3) Can you report results with a learned gating network trained post hoc to pick among experts (non-oracle selection)?
4) How is tie-breaking implemented? Does randomized tie-breaking improve robustness?

Reasons to accept
– Practical, broadly applicable method that is easy to adopt.
– Consistent empirical improvements and insightful analyses of emergent expertise.
– Addresses an important setting (multiple hypotheses for ambiguous tasks) with a compelling solution.

Reasons to reject
– Limited theoretical depth and lack of analysis on convergence/starvation.
– Some baselines are incomplete (no MoE), and MCL comparisons are constrained by compute.
– Oracle-only evaluation; limited evidence of downstream end-to-end benefit.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The contribution is pragmatic but impactful: a simple training rule that unlocks MCL for deep ensembles and yields clear gains and diversity across tasks. Strengthen by adding a MoE baseline, soft-assignment ablation, diversity metrics, and a small non-oracle selection experiment.
