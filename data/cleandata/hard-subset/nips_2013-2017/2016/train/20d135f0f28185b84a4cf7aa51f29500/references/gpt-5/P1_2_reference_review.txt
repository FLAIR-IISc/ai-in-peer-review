Summary
This paper tackles the multiple predictions setting, where the system produces M hypotheses and an oracle selects the best one (oracle loss). Building on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) (Guzman-Rivera et al.), the authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-based “winner-take-gradient” training scheme for deep ensembles: for each training example in a minibatch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss on that example receives gradient updates; others receive zero gradient. This interleaves assignment and learning and obviates the costly alternation/retraining of prior MCL methods. The approach is architecture-agnostic and loss-agnostic.

They empirically demonstrate sMCL on three domains and models: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM). Across tasks and ensemble sizes, sMCL improves oracle metrics (top-k accuracy/IoU/CIDEr) over independent ensembles, submodular-boosting-style methods (Dey et al.), and classical MCL (with far lower training time). Analysis suggests emergent specialization of ensemble members: class specialization in classification, more nuanced structural/shape specialization in segmentation, and n-gram/language structure diversity in captioning. The method is presented as parameter-free, easy to implement, and efficient (training members concurrently).

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself well with respect to: (i) MCL and related diverse M-best inference in probabilistic models (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.), (ii) ensemble learning and diversity-inducing strategies (bagging, negative correlation, resampling), and (iii) deep learning ensembles. The key difference is practical training for deep networks under oracle loss without sequential retraining. The approach is closely related to hard-EM/block coordinate descent used in MCL, competitive learning/winner-take-all and online k-means, and to mixtures-of-experts with a hard implicit gate (Jacobs et al., 1991/1993) but without an explicit gating network. Clarifying these connections would strengthen the positioning.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm that directly optimizes oracle loss with standard backprop; easily dropped into existing deep architectures.
- Clear empirical benefits across three very different tasks and architectures; strong and consistent improvements over reasonable baselines.
- Convincing qualitative analyses showing emergent specialization and diversity that align with intuition about ambiguity and multi-modality.
- Training efficiency: avoids the prohibitive retraining schedule of MCL; concurrent optimization fits modern deep learning practice.
- Insightful ablation (k-winners) connecting degree of specialization to oracle performance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical grounding: There is no convergence analysis or discussion of stability of the stochastic hard assignment procedure. Winner-take-all can be brittle; early dominance may starve other experts. In segmentation, authors warm-start from a trained ensemble, suggesting sensitivity; an ablation on cold-start vs warm-start would be valuable.
- Load balancing: No mechanism prevents one model from “hogging” data. Diagnostics on assignment entropy, fraction of data per expert over time, and mitigation strategies (e.g., soft assignments, entropy/balancing regularizers, epsilon-greedy exploration) would strengthen the work.
- Evaluation scope: Oracle metrics are appropriate for multiple-hypothesis settings, but the paper does not evaluate downstream selection/reranking or human-in-the-loop scenarios. Even a simple learned selector could demonstrate practical gains beyond oracle metrics.
- Captioning comparison: A single-model beam search achieves stronger oracle in some settings (albeit with less diversity). It would be informative to report sMCL+beam (and independent+beam) to separate training-time diversity from inference-time search.
- Computational cost: While sMCL is faster than MCL, training M models still costs about M× a single model (plus per-batch routing). A wall-clock comparison to independent ensembles, and memory/throughput considerations, would be helpful.
- Clarity/reproducibility details: The core algorithm is easy to describe, but some experimental details are sparse in the text (e.g., exact hyperparameters for all tasks, handling of ties, initialization scheme for each domain, how often assignments are recomputed, batch size effects). The reference list also appears incomplete/malformed in places.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. The derivation from the MCL upper bound to the stochastic update is correct and intuitive. Claims about speed and performance are broadly supported, though more quantitative details would help (e.g., exact numbers in the main text; ablations on warm-starts, batch size, M). The limitations (e.g., reliance on oracle metric, potential for unbalanced experts) are not discussed in depth.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful figures that illustrate specialization (as described). Adding a brief discussion comparing sMCL to mixtures-of-experts (soft/hard gating), competitive learning, and EM would improve clarity. Ensure the references are complete and consistent; some entries are truncated.

Originality
The idea of interleaving assignment with SGD updates (hard-EM style) is not new per se, but its application to deep ensembles under oracle loss, with a simple winner-take-gradient layer and broad empirical validation, is novel and useful. The work differentiates itself clearly from prior MCL methods by practicality and from diversity-inducing ensembles by directly optimizing oracle loss.

Significance
High practical significance: diverse deep ensembles are widely used, and producing multiple high-quality hypotheses is central to many applications. The method is likely to be adopted due to its simplicity and demonstrated gains across tasks. Conceptually, it provides a clean baseline for oracle-loss optimization in deep settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Analyze assignment dynamics: show per-expert data share over epochs; report any collapse and remedies.
- Explore soft-assignments (e.g., top-k weighted gradients or temperature-controlled softmin) and load-balancing regularizers; include a small ablation.
- Report sMCL+beam for captioning and top-k for classification to fully compare with inference-time diversity.
- Provide wall-clock and GPU memory comparisons to independent ensembles.
- Discuss failure modes and when sMCL may hurt (e.g., when ambiguity is low).
- Add related citations to mixtures-of-experts and competitive learning; discuss relations and differences.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method with clear practical impact.
- Strong empirical gains across diverse tasks/architectures.
- Insightful analysis showing emergent specialization.
- Addresses a known limitation of prior MCL approaches (training cost).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis; potential brittleness of hard assignments not fully explored.
- Reliance on oracle metrics without downstream selection evaluation.
- Some missing experimental details and ablations (load balancing, warm-start sensitivity, inference-time search).

Recommendation
Weak accept to accept. The method is pragmatic, broadly applicable, and empirically compelling, and the paper will be useful to the community despite limited theory.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
