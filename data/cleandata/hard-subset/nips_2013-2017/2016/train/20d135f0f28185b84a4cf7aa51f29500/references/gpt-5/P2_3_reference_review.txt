Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple, SGD-friendly procedure to train deep ensembles to directly minimize “oracle loss” (the loss of the best prediction among M outputs). The core idea is a winner-take-gradient rule: for each training example in a batch, losses are computed for all M ensemble members and the gradient is backpropagated only through the model with the smallest loss on that example (ties broken arbitrarily). This can be seen as a stochastic block gradient approximation to the MCL objective of Guzman-Rivera et al. (2012, 2014), which previously required alternating assignment and full retraining to convergence. sMCL is claimed to be architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and parallelizable. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM) show higher oracle performance than independent ensembles, MCL (with far less wall-clock time), and the boosting-like method of Dey et al. The authors also analyze emergent specialization (class specialization for classification, more nuanced specialization for segmentation, n-gram specialization for captioning) and a top-k variant that interpolates between oracle training and standard ensembling.

Relation to prior work
- Closest to MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.), which formulates oracle-loss minimization with hard assignments and alternates assignments with retraining. sMCL contributes a stochastic, concurrent alternative enabling practical deep training.
- Distinct from “diverse M-best” and submodular selection methods, which are tailored to probabilistic structured models and/or require sequential training/selection.
- Related to mixture-of-experts and hard-EM style training; however, sMCL does not introduce a gating network or explicit diversity regularizer—diversity and specialization emerge from oracle-loss optimization.
- The paper positions itself clearly vs. independent ensembling, boosting-like reweighting (Dey et al.), and beam search for captioning.

Strengths
- Practicality: The winner-take-gradient rule is simple, parameter-free, and integrates directly into standard SGD pipelines. Parallel training across experts makes it far more feasible than alternating retraining.
- Generality: Demonstrated across three quite different tasks and architectures, including structured outputs. The method is agnostic to the underlying loss.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements in oracle metrics over strong baselines across tasks; faster optimization of oracle loss than independent ensembles.
- Insightful analysis: Clear evidence of emergent specialization; top-k ablation shows a trade-off between specialization and oracle performance; qualitative segmentation/captioning examples are convincing.
- Clarity of presentation: The objective connection to MCL is transparent, and the training recipe is easy to reproduce.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical guarantees: No analysis of convergence or stability (e.g., conditions under which all experts receive sufficient gradient to avoid “dead experts”). While common for deep training, some discussion would help.
- Initialization and dead experts: For segmentation, sMCL is initialized from a pre-trained independent ensemble, which likely mitigates collapse. It is unclear how sensitive sMCL is to random initialization, batch size, and early tie-breaking. Reporting training dynamics (e.g., fraction of data assigned to each expert over epochs) and failure modes would strengthen confidence.
- Fairness of baselines and compute budget: 
  - MCL is run for only five meta-iterations and reported infeasible beyond that; this makes relative performance somewhat inconclusive. A stronger comparison could use partial retraining or warm-started fewer-epoch inner loops to match compute.
  - For captioning, the sMCL ensemble shares the CNN trunk due to memory constraints, which could reduce diversity; ensuring comparable parameter counts or reporting with/without shared CNN would clarify.
  - For segmentation, sMCL fine-tunes after training independent models; ensure comparisons use matched total training budgets.
- Evaluation scope: Almost all results are oracle metrics. While appropriate for the MCL setting, practitioners often need a selection mechanism. A small study showing that a simple reranker (e.g., learned or heuristic) can recover gains in standard metrics would improve practical significance. For captioning, reporting single-output metrics and human judgment in addition to oracle CIDEr-D would be useful.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters: sMCL is presented as parameter-free, but the tie-breaking policy, batch composition, and the top-k variant could materially affect outcomes. A brief sensitivity analysis would help.
- Related work breadth: The paper could more explicitly discuss connections/differences to classical mixture-of-experts (e.g., Jordan & Jacobs) and hard-EM/gating variants, and clarify why sMCL is preferable when no gating signal is available.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound derivation from the MCL surrogate with a correct gradient argument; thorough empirical validation across domains; some questions about stability, sensitivity, and fairness of compute remain but do not undermine the core claims.
- Clarity: Clear and well organized. Algorithms and training protocols are described, though details on tie-breaking, dead-expert avoidance, and exact compute budgets could be expanded.
- Originality: Moderate. The underlying objective is not new (MCL), but the stochastic, concurrent training scheme for deep nets is a practical and nontrivial contribution, and the breadth of applications and analyses are compelling.
- Significance: High practical significance. The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners needing multi-hypothesis outputs for ambiguous tasks. The demonstration of emergent specialization is insightful and may inspire further work.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm enabling oracle-loss training for deep ensembles.
- Consistent empirical gains and insightful analyses across diverse tasks.
- Strong relevance to multi-hypothesis prediction problems prevalent in vision and language.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical/stability analysis; potential for dead experts not fully addressed.
- Comparisons to MCL and other baselines could be more rigorously matched in compute and architecture.
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics without demonstrating downstream selection performance.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- How sensitive is sMCL to initialization, batch size, and tie-breaking? Do you observe dead experts without pretraining?
- Can you report per-expert data assignment over time to show balanced learning?
- For segmentation/captioning, can you include results with matched compute and with/without shared backbones?
- Can you provide a simple reranking experiment to translate oracle gains into standard metrics?
- Please discuss links to mixture-of-experts and whether a soft or learned gating variant of sMCL helps.

Overall recommendation: Accept (strong practical contribution with broad applicability and solid empirical support; weaknesses are addressable and do not negate the value of the method).
