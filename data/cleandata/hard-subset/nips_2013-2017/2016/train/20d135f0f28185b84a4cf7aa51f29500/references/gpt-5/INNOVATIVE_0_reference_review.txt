Summary
This paper addresses the problem of producing multiple plausible predictions for ambiguous perception tasks by directly minimizing oracle loss over an ensemble’s outputs. The key contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL): a simple, general, SGD-friendly “winner-take-gradient” training rule. For each minibatch example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss on that example receives gradient signal; others receive zero. This interleaves assignment and learning, avoids the costly alternating retraining of prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [Guzman-Rivera et al.], and applies to arbitrary differentiable architectures and losses. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation, and MSCOCO captioning show consistent improvements in oracle metrics and reveal emergent specialization among ensemble members (by classes in classification, by structure and object types in segmentation, and by n-grams in captioning).

Relation to prior work
- MCL [8,9] and subsequent boosting/submodular formulations [5] optimize oracle loss via alternating or sequential training but are impractical for deep nets. sMCL is a concurrent, SGD-based instantiation of the same objective’s block structure.
- Diverse M-best for probabilistic structured models [2,14–16,21,23] does not directly apply to general deep architectures; sMCL fills this gap.
- Traditional ensemble diversity (bagging, resampling, negative correlation) and joint training do not target oracle loss nor induce task-specific specialization.
- Conceptually, sMCL resembles hard-EM/“winner-take-all” or (hard) mixture-of-experts without a learned gating network: examples are assigned to the best-performing expert and only that expert updates. The paper’s positioning relative to MoE could be clarified, but the oracle-loss objective and deep, concurrent SGD realization are distinctive.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea: a minimal change to SGD that directly optimizes oracle loss across an ensemble.
- Practical and general: parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic, concurrent training; simple to implement as a layer/routing rule.
- Empirical breadth: image classification, dense prediction, and captioning, with consistent, meaningful gains in oracle metrics and qualitative diversity.
- Insightful analysis: emergent specialization and controllable trade-off via k-winner variation.
- Speed: large wall-clock savings vs MCL’s multi-pass retraining, making oracle-loss ensembles feasible for deep models.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty vs hard MoE: The method is very close to hard assignment in mixture-of-experts; the paper would benefit from a direct comparison to (i) a learned gating network (soft/hard) MoE and (ii) hard-EM MoE training, both evaluated on oracle metrics.
- Stability and collapse: Winner-take-gradient can lead to load imbalance where one model dominates early. Provide assignment entropy/load-balance curves, collapse cases, or simple regularizers (e.g., entropy or load-balancing penalties), and ablate initialization sensitivity and tie-breaking strategies.
- Oracle-only evaluation: While oracle metrics are justified for multi-hypothesis settings, it would be useful to report non-oracle performance (expected loss, calibration) to show sMCL does not catastrophically harm single-prediction quality and to quantify the accuracy-diversity trade-off.
- Baselines breadth: Include diversity-promoting regularizers (negative-correlation, diversity penalties, DPP-like losses) and multi-head single-network baselines. For captioning, compare to diverse beam search and nucleus sampling; the current single-model beam results sometimes approach/beat sMCL oracle.
- Scalability/detail: Results stop at M≤6. How does performance and specialization evolve for larger M? Provide wall-clock/compute usage to substantiate the 5× speedup beyond “5 meta-iterations.”
- Shared-backbone effects: In captioning, the shared CNN may limit diversity; discuss how sMCL behaves with fully separate backbones vs shared lower layers, and how gradients are routed in shared trunks.
- Theoretical footing: Beyond the subgradient view of the upper bound, some discussion of convergence properties under SGD (even informal) would strengthen the technical story.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the optimization rule is correct for the surrogate and easy to implement. Empirical evidence is compelling across tasks. Some baselines are missing (MoE), and analysis of stability/load balancing is light.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Algorithm description is clear. Please add details on tie-breaking, gradient routing with shared layers, training budgets, and the precise k-winner relaxation.

Originality
- Moderate-to-high. While the idea aligns with classic hard-EM/MoE intuitions, casting oracle-loss ensemble training as a stochastic, concurrent, parameter-free procedure for deep nets is a useful conceptual and practical advance.

Significance
- High practical significance. Many deployed systems need sets of plausible hypotheses. sMCL offers a simple, extensible tool for that purpose, and the emergent specialization phenomenon is interesting and potentially impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add MoE (soft/hard gating) baselines and compare oracle and non-oracle metrics.
- Report assignment balance, collapse rates, and effect of an entropy/load-balancing regularizer.
- Provide larger-M experiments and compute/time comparisons.
- For captioning, include diverse beam search/top-p sampling baselines and human evaluation of diversity/utility.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some missing baselines and limited theoretical analysis, the paper introduces a simple, broadly applicable training primitive for oracle-loss ensembles, demonstrates clear empirical gains and emergent specialization across diverse tasks, and opens a useful direction for multi-hypothesis deep prediction.
