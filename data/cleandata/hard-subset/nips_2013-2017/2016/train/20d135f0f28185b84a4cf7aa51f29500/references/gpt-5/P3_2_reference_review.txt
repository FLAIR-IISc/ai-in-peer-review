Summary
The paper tackles the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) setting where the training objective is oracle loss: for each input, an oracle selects the best among M predictions. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” rule: for each example in a minibatch, only the lowest-loss model among the M ensemble members receives gradient signal for that example; other members receive zero. This interleaves assignment and optimization, avoiding the costly alternating retrain-to-convergence cycles of prior MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.). The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic and is applied to CNNs for CIFAR-10, FCNs for Pascal VOC segmentation, and CNN+LSTMs for MSCOCO captioning. Across settings, sMCL improves oracle performance over independent ensembles, a boosting-like diverse-ensemble baseline (Dey et al.), and (given compute constraints) over retrained MCL; it also exhibits emergent specialization (e.g., class clusters for CIFAR, qualitative structural diversity for segmentation and captioning). A k-winner variant interpolates between full oracle (k=1) and standard ensembles (k=M).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al., 2012/2013], and connects to diverse M-best inference in structured models [Batra et al., Kirillov et al.], diversity-promoting ensembles (negative correlation learning), and recent “network of experts” and ensemble-of-experts architectures. Methodologically, sMCL is closely related to hard-assignment mixture-of-experts and hard-EM/K-means: an E-step assigns an example to the best expert and the M-step updates that expert; here this is done stochastically per minibatch. Unlike diverse M-best which operates at inference with a single model (often in MRFs), sMCL learns multiple models end-to-end.

Strengths
- Simple, general, parameter-free training rule that plugs into standard SGD loops; clear subgradient intuition for the min-over-experts objective (Eq. 2).
- Concurrent training avoids the major practical limitation of prior MCL methods that require sequential retraining.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across three tasks and architectures; consistent gains in oracle metrics (6–10% relative over strong baselines).
- Insightful qualitative analyses showing emergent specialization and diversity; the k-winner ablation illuminates the specialization–accuracy tradeoff.
- Practical engineering considerations discussed (e.g., shared CNN trunk for captioning due to memory limits).

Weaknesses and questions
- Oracle-only evaluation: The paper optimizes and reports oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr). While appropriate for the MCL setting, downstream systems typically need a selector; no learned or heuristic selector is evaluated, so it remains unclear how gains translate to end-to-end performance. At minimum, an analysis with a simple selector (e.g., a reranker or per-expert confidence) would strengthen the case.
- Missing comparison to classical mixture-of-experts with learned gating (e.g., Jacobs et al., Jordan & Jacobs). sMCL is essentially hard-assignment MoE without a parametric gating network; a baseline with a soft/learned gate (or hard gate trained with REINFORCE/EM) would be informative on both oracle and non-oracle metrics.
- Optimization dynamics and stability: Winner-take-gradient can induce “rich get richer” dynamics where early stochastic wins lead to brittle specialization. The paper shows extreme class partitioning on CIFAR but lacks analysis of sensitivity to initialization, batch size, and class imbalance, or regularizers (e.g., load balancing) to prevent collapse.
- Computational overhead: Though faster than retraining-based MCL, each batch still requires M forward passes and per-example selection. Memory overhead necessitated sharing the CNN trunk in captioning; scalability to larger M and heavier backbones warrants discussion.
- Limited diversity quantification: Beyond oracle score and n-gram counts, diversity is not quantified (e.g., pairwise disagreement, KL between predictive distributions, diversity–accuracy frontier).
- Theoretical framing: The derivation mixes the upper-bound MCL objective with the oracle min. Since the subgradient of min_m l_i,m selects any active expert, the proposed update is a valid subgradient step on the oracle loss; explicitly stating this would sharpen the theory. Convergence behavior under stochastic, non-smooth assignments could be discussed.

Quality
Technically sound and well-supported empirically across tasks. The method is straightforward to implement; experimental protocols are mostly clear. Some baselines (MoE with gating; DPP/diversity regularizers) are missing, and end-to-end downstream evaluation is absent.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for oracle loss is compelling. Provide more implementation details for segmentation and captioning (exact losses, data splits, learning-rate schedules per head) to aid reproducibility. Explicitly state computational cost relative to independent ensembles.

Originality
The key novelty is adapting MCL to deep networks via an in-batch, winner-take-gradient rule that is trivial to implement and practically efficient. The conceptual connection to hard-assignment MoE is known, but the application to deep ensembles with oracle-loss optimization across diverse tasks and the empirical study are new and valuable.

Significance
Useful and likely to be adopted: many applications benefit from high-recall sets of hypotheses. The method advances practical training for multiple-prediction systems in deep learning, and the analyses provide insight into emergent specialization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a learned selector experiment to bridge oracle metrics to practical use.
- Include a mixture-of-experts baseline with a learned gating network (shared trunk + per-expert heads).
- Study stability: anneal k from M to 1; add load-balancing or entropy regularizers; report sensitivity to M and class imbalance.
- Quantify diversity beyond oracle (e.g., disagreement, feature diversity).
- Report computational/memory cost and wall-clock vs independent ensembles.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, practically impactful algorithm with consistent gains across tasks.
- Addresses a real limitation of prior MCL: training efficiency on deep nets.
- Clear analyses of emergent specialization; good qualitative results.

Cons against acceptance
- Oracle-only evaluation without downstream selector.
- Missing comparison to learned gating MoE and other diversity-inducing baselines.
- Limited analysis of optimization dynamics and scalability.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a practical, broadly applicable contribution with solid empirical support, despite some missing baselines and downstream evaluation.
