This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and elegant training algorithm for deep ensembles. The goal is not to improve the average performance of the ensemble, but rather to train its members to produce a diverse set of plausible hypotheses, such that the *best* prediction among the set (as chosen by an oracle) is correct. This is framed as minimizing the "oracle loss." The work builds directly upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al., which uses a block coordinate descent approach that requires iteratively retraining models to convergence. This iterative retraining is prohibitively expensive for modern deep networks. The key contribution of this paper is a stochastic, online version of this idea: for each example in a mini-batch, the gradient is backpropagated only to the single ensemble member that currently has the lowest loss on that example. This "winner-take-gradient" strategy allows for concurrent training of all ensemble members in a single pass, making the optimization of oracle loss practical for deep learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its novelty. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a simple yet brilliant idea. It provides a highly effective and computationally feasible way to optimize for a non-traditional but very important objective (oracle loss). While building on the MCL concept, the adaptation to the stochastic gradient descent setting is non-trivial and represents a significant conceptual leap. It moves beyond the standard paradigm of training ensembles for average accuracy and opens a new direction for training specialist models that collectively cover a complex, multi-modal solution space. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-impact idea that NIPS should champion.

*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is of great practical importance. Many deep learning models, especially in structured prediction, suffer from "mode-seeking" behavior, producing safe, average, but ultimately uninformative or even nonsensical outputs (e.g., the horse-cow hybrid in segmentation). The ability to generate a diverse set of high-quality hypotheses is critical for systems where a human or a downstream module can perform final verification. sMCL provides a general-purpose, parameter-free, and easy-to-implement tool to achieve this. Its applicability across classification, segmentation, and captioning—all with different architectures—demonstrates its broad potential impact.

*   **Quality:** The experimental validation is extensive and convincing. The authors apply sMCL to three distinct and challenging tasks, demonstrating its robustness and generality. The chosen baselines (independently trained ensembles, MCL, and a boosting-style method from Dey et al.) are appropriate and serve to highlight the effectiveness of sMCL, which consistently outperforms them. A particularly strong aspect of the paper is the analysis of the emergent specialization. The visualizations clearly show how different ensemble members automatically learn to become experts on different subsets of the data (e.g., specific classes in CIFAR-10 or different captioning styles), providing valuable insight into the learning dynamics of the method.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clearly articulated with compelling examples in the introduction. The sMCL algorithm is explained with admirable simplicity, and the contrast with the prior, computationally expensive MCL method makes the contribution immediately obvious. The figures and tables are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The computational cost at inference is M times that of a single model, as all M models must be run to generate the set of hypotheses. This is an inherent trade-off of using an ensemble and is not a flaw of the method itself, but it is a practical limitation worth keeping in mind. The paper is transparent about the training speedup over MCL, but less so about the inference cost compared to single-model approaches.

*   In the image captioning experiment, the comparison against a single model using beam search is insightful but not perfectly controlled. As the authors note, beam search is an inference technique. A more direct comparison might involve applying beam search to each member of the sMCL ensemble. However, the reported result—that sMCL produces more unique n-grams—still strongly supports the central claim of generating diversity, so this is a minor point.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty:** Proposes a simple, clever, and fundamentally new training strategy for deep ensembles.
2.  **High Significance:** Addresses the important problem of generating diverse predictions and makes a previously intractable learning framework (MCL) practical for deep learning.
3.  **Strong Empirical Support:** Demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines on a wide variety of tasks and architectures.
4.  **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is a model of clear scientific communication and provides fascinating insights into emergent model specialization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The work does not address the increased inference-time cost inherent to ensemble methods.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that introduces a highly novel, significant, and practical method. It prioritizes a new direction—optimizing for a diverse set of outputs—over simply achieving an incremental improvement in a standard metric. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is well-executed, clearly presented, and its ideas are likely to be widely used and built upon. I strongly recommend acceptance.
