This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a training method for deep ensembles aiming to minimize oracle loss. The core idea – training ensemble members concurrently by assigning gradients only to the best-performing member for each example – is a pragmatic attempt to address the computational cost of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches. The authors demonstrate sMCL’s efficacy across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for MCL, especially in scenarios with ambiguous data or implicit class confusion, is well-articulated. The mathematical formulation of sMCL is concise and accessible, and the algorithm itself is straightforward. The experimental setup appears comprehensive, with comparisons to independent ensembles, the original MCL method, and a more recent boosting-like approach. The presentation of results is generally clear, and the inclusion of qualitative examples for segmentation and captioning enhances understanding.

However, despite the clarity and thorough experimentation, I remain unconvinced of the novelty and significance of this work, particularly for a NeurIPS publication. While the authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of prior MCL methods, the proposed solution – stochastic gradient descent with selective gradient application – feels more like a clever engineering trick than a fundamental algorithmic advance. This "winner-take-all" gradient scheme, while effective, doesn’t fundamentally alter the underlying optimization landscape, and feels intuitively similar to techniques like hard example mining or curriculum learning. The authors don’t provide a strong theoretical justification for *why* this approach works well beyond the stated observation that it approximates the original MCL objective.

Furthermore, the performance gains, while statistically significant, are not overwhelmingly large. In many cases, the improvements over independently trained ensembles are modest (e.g., a few percentage points in accuracy). The comparison to MCL is hampered by the fact that the authors only compare to MCL run for a limited number of iterations, acknowledging its higher computational cost. A more thorough comparison with a fully converged MCL solution would be crucial. The authors also downplay the limitations of their comparison to Dey et al. [5], noting its saturation issues but not fully exploring why sMCL might avoid these. 

The claim of “interpretable expertise” – the emergence of specialized ensemble members – is interesting, but the demonstrated specialization in the classification experiment (class-wise clustering) feels somewhat obvious given the task and architecture. The specialization observed in the segmentation and captioning experiments is less pronounced and more difficult to interpret. More rigorous analysis of the learned representations and the reasons *why* these specializations emerge would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Pragmatic solution to a well-defined problem (computational cost of MCL).
*   Demonstrated empirical improvements across multiple tasks and architectures.
*   Clear writing and thorough experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the core idea feels more like an engineering optimization than a fundamentally new algorithm.
*   Modest performance gains – the improvements over strong baselines are not dramatic.
*   Incomplete comparison to the original MCL method.
*   Lack of strong theoretical justification for the effectiveness of sMCL.
*   The "interpretable expertise" claim is not fully substantiated.



Overall, I believe this paper is a solid piece of engineering work, but falls short of the bar for acceptance at NeurIPS. The contributions are incremental rather than transformative, and the theoretical underpinnings are weak. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a more applied venue. A more detailed theoretical analysis, a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods (including fully converged MCL), and a deeper investigation of the learned specializations would significantly strengthen the paper.
