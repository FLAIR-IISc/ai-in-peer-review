**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep ensembles. The goal is to produce a diverse set of M plausible outputs for a given input, such that the oracle loss (the loss of the best prediction among the M outputs) is minimized. This is a generalization of the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework to deep networks. The core technical contribution is a simple yet effective "winner-take-gradient" strategy integrated into Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). For each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all M ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only to the member that produced the lowest loss. The authors claim this approach is efficient (training all members concurrently), parameter-free, and agnostic to the loss function and network architecture. They demonstrate its effectiveness on three diverse tasks: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing that sMCL outperforms baselines like independently trained ensembles, the original MCL algorithm, and a boosting-style method by Dey et al. A key finding highlighted is the "emergent specialization" of ensemble members on different facets of the data distribution.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simple and Elegant Method:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is highly intuitive and straightforward to implement. The "winner-take-gradient" idea is a simple modification to a standard training pipeline for ensembles and avoids the costly and complex alternating optimization of prior work like MCL.

2.  **Broad Empirical Validation:** The authors have commendably evaluated their method on three distinct and challenging deep learning tasks, using different architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). This provides strong evidence for the general applicability of sMCL.

3.  **Strong Oracle Performance:** In most experiments, sMCL demonstrates significant improvements in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles and other baselines. This shows its effectiveness at the stated goal of generating a diverse set of high-quality solutions.

4.  **Interesting Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis of emergent expertise (e.g., class specialization in CIFAR10, n-gram specialization in captioning) provides valuable insight into the behavior of the learning algorithm and the resulting models.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

While the core idea is appealing and the results appear promising, the paper is beset by a number of minor-to-moderate issues related to clarity, experimental rigor, and presentation. The accumulation of these flaws substantially detracts from the quality of the submission and raises questions about the work's carefulness.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**
*   **Bibliography:** The reference section is in a state of disarray. It is not formatted correctly (e.g., [1] is missing from the list, [17] is just a name, authors are missing from [6], and the formatting is inconsistent throughout). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
*   **Floating Header:** There is a misplaced header "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." at the bottom of the first page.
*   **Missing Methodological Details:** In Section 4.1, an experiment is described where the constraint of penalizing only the single best predictor is loosened to penalize the lowest `k` error predictors. This is an interesting ablation, but the paper fails to specify the update rule for `k > 1`. Is the gradient averaged over the top-k? Is it backpropagated to all of them? This crucial detail is missing, making the experiment irreproducible.
*   **Imprecise Language:** The paper states that ties for the lowest error predictor are "broken arbitrarily". For a deterministic and reproducible algorithm, this should be specified (e.g., by lowest model index). The claim of being "parameter-free" is also slightly misleading; while sMCL introduces no new hyperparameters, the overall training process is still governed by numerous parameters (learning rate, momentum, etc.).

**2. Experimental Rigor and Comparisons:**
*   **Unfair Speed Comparison:** The claim that sMCL is "5x faster than MCL" is based on running MCL for 5 meta-iterations. This number is arbitrary. A different number of iterations might be required for other tasks, making the speed-up factor not a fundamental property of the method but an artifact of a specific experimental choice.
*   **Confounding Factors in Experiments:**
    *   In the semantic segmentation experiment (Sec 4.2), the models are initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. This is a critical detail. It is unclear if sMCL performs as well from a random initialization on this complex task. This pre-training step complicates the narrative of a simple, end-to-end training procedure.
    *   In the image captioning experiment (Sec 4.3), the authors state that due to memory restrictions, a single CNN backbone was shared among all LSTM models. This is a significant deviation from a true ensemble where each member is an independent `f_m(x)`. It is an ensemble of decoders, not of full models. This hardware-constrained design choice is a major confounder, and its impact on the results is not discussed.
*   **Weak Comparison in Captioning:** In Table 1, the single-model beam search baseline (M=5) achieves a higher oracle CIDEr score than the sMCL ensemble (M=5). The authors dismiss this by noting that beam search is an inference method and could also be applied to sMCL. This is speculative and does not negate the reported result. A proper comparison would have been to apply beam search of width `B` to each of the `M` sMCL models and report the oracle score over the resulting `M*B` candidates, or at least acknowledge that a strong inference-time baseline outperforms their training-time method on the primary metric.

**3. Technical Soundness:**
*   The connection to "stochastic block gradient descent" is tenuous. The update in Eq. 2 relies on hard indicator variables `p_i,m` which are determined by a `min` operation. While it is a block-wise update (updating parameters of one model `f_m` per example), the hard-assignment nature makes it more akin to a self-paced learning or hard-EM-style update within a stochastic setting, rather than a formal gradient-based block descent method. The formal justification could be stronger.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While this paper presents a promising and simple idea for training diverse ensembles, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The core contribution is valuable, but its impact is undermined by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from poor presentation (e.g., the bibliography) to a lack of methodological rigor and confounding factors in the experimental design. The accumulation of these issues erodes confidence in the reported results and the overall carefulness of the work. A major revision addressing the detailed points above would be necessary to make this paper a strong candidate for acceptance.
