Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning deep ensembles that output multiple plausible hypotheses per input and are evaluated by an oracle loss (min over members). Building on Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), the authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL): a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme that, for each training example, backpropagates the loss only through the ensemble member that currently incurs the smallest loss. This interleaves assignment and learning at the mini-batch level, removing the need for expensive alternating retraining in prior MCL work. The approach is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and supports concurrent training of all members.

Empirically, sMCL is evaluated on image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC FCN), and image captioning (VGG16+LSTM on COCO). Across tasks, sMCL consistently improves oracle metrics over independent ensembles, sequential “boosting-like” diverse methods, and is competitive with or better than the alternating MCL baseline while being substantially faster. A notable qualitative finding is that sMCL induces interpretable specialization of ensemble members (e.g., class-wise specialization in classification; structural/shape biases in segmentation; n-gram/style diversity in captioning). The authors also present a k-winners variant that reveals a trade-off between specialization and oracle performance.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-positioned against: (i) classic ensemble diversity methods (resampling, negative correlation, joint losses), (ii) structured multi-solution inference (M-best/diverse), and (iii) MCL and submodular boosting-like approaches that require retraining or sequential training. The key novelty is adapting MCL to deep networks via an SGD-friendly stochastic block descent that performs example-to-expert assignment on the fly. The connection to hard-EM/k-means and to mixture-of-experts (hard routing without a learned gating network) is implicit and mostly appropriate; a slightly deeper contrast to classical MoE with gating would strengthen the positioning.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and bold idea: winner-take-gradient per example yields specialization and diversity without extra parameters or retraining loops.
- Generality: Applicable to arbitrary differentiable losses and architectures; demonstrated on three distinct modalities (CNN classification, FCN segmentation, CNN+RNN captioning).
- Practical significance: Substantially more efficient than alternating MCL, avoiding multi-pass retraining; easy to drop into existing training codebases (effectively a routing layer during backprop).
- Empirical evidence: Consistent oracle improvements over strong baselines; analysis of specialization and a k-winners ablation that connects diversity to oracle gains.
- Interpretability: Emergent specialization is clear and compelling across tasks, supporting the intended use-case of multi-hypothesis perception for downstream selection.

Weaknesses / questions
- Evaluation is primarily on oracle metrics. While this matches the problem setting, it would help to report downstream selection proxies (e.g., simple rerankers, or sensitivity to an imperfect oracle) to establish end-to-end utility.
- Fairness and budgets: Clarify whether total compute and parameter budgets are matched across methods, especially for captioning (beam search vs ensembles) and for MCL (meta-iteration count vs wall-clock).
- Load imbalance: Winner-take-gradient can starve some members early. Do you observe severe imbalance? Any mitigation (e.g., warm-up with k>1, entropy/temperature on assignments, or occasional exploration)?
- Comparisons: A baseline with a learned gating network (mixture-of-experts) or soft assignment variant would be informative. Even a small-scale comparison would contextualize the hard assignment design choice.
- Stability/implementation details: How are ties broken? Sensitivity to batch size, M, and initialization? Any signs of mode collapse? More quantitative ablations would strengthen the claims.
- Scale: Classification is on CIFAR-10 and segmentation on a VOC subset. Demonstrating on a larger-scale classification task (e.g., ImageNet top-k oracle) would bolster significance.

Quality
Technically sound at the algorithmic level; the gradient derivation is straightforward (Eq. 2), and the optimization scheme is consistent with hard-EM intuition. Empirical support spans three domains with both quantitative and qualitative analyses. The main limitations are the reliance on oracle metrics and missing ablations on stability, compute, and assignment dynamics; nonetheless, the presented results substantiate the central claims.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The objective, algorithm, and training procedures are described with sufficient detail to implement. The analysis of emergent expertise is accessible and insightful. Minor suggestions: explicitly discuss tie-breaking and assignment imbalance; tabulate training time/compute across methods; clarify captioning setup where CNN is shared vs fine-tuned.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. Prior MCL established the oracle-loss objective and alternating optimization, but the key contribution here is a practical, SGD-compatible stochastic block descent that enables deep ensembles to be trained once, in parallel, with per-example routing. The emergent specialization and cross-domain validation underline the novelty. The idea relates to (but is distinct from) classical mixture-of-experts and diversity-inducing ensembles.

Significance
High practical significance. Many perception systems benefit from multi-hypothesis outputs, and this work offers a simple, scalable way to train such ensembles that demonstrably improves oracle performance and diversity. I expect practitioners and researchers to adopt and extend this training strategy (e.g., soft assignments, learned gates, uncertainty-aware routing).

Pros
- Simple, general, and efficient algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles under oracle loss
- Strong and consistent empirical gains on three tasks; compelling qualitative diversity/specialization
- Parameter-free, architecture-agnostic, easy to implement

Cons
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited evidence of end-to-end downstream impact
- Missing comparisons to gating-based MoE and deeper ablations on assignment dynamics and compute
- Limited scale in classification/segmentation experiments

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a clean and practically impactful training paradigm that advances the state of the art in multi-hypothesis deep learning and is likely to influence subsequent work. The minor experimental gaps are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution.
