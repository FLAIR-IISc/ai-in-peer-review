Summary
The paper revisits Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) with oracle loss, where an ensemble produces M hypotheses and evaluation selects the best per input. The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a winner-take-gradient training scheme that interleaves the MCL assignment step with stochastic gradient updates: for each example in a minibatch, only the lowest-loss predictor receives gradient signal, others receive zero. This is motivated by the block-coordinate view of the MCL upper-bound objective with binary assignments. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all ensemble members concurrently.

Empirical results span CIFAR-10 classification with a small CNN, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC with FCNs, and image captioning on MSCOCO with a CNN+LSTM. sMCL consistently improves oracle metrics over independent ensembles, a prior MCL retraining scheme, and a submodular boosting approach, and does so with lower training time than MCL. The paper also analyzes emergent specialization: class-wise specialization for classification, more nuanced shape/frequency biases in segmentation, and diverse n-gram usage in captioning.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated with respect to: (i) MCL and oracle-loss training for structured prediction (Guzman-Rivera et al.), (ii) diverse M-best solutions in probabilistic models (Batra et al., Kirillov et al.), (iii) ensemble diversity and decorrelation (bagging, negative correlation, artificial data), and (iv) sequential/boosting-style ensemble construction for oracle metrics (Dey et al.). The novelty here is adapting MCL to deep networks with a simple SGD-compatible procedure that avoids costly alternating retraining, and demonstrating its breadth across different architectures. The approach is related in spirit to hard EM/k-means and to mixture-of-experts with hard routing, but uses the task loss for routing and optimizes oracle loss directly.

Strengths
- Simple and general algorithm: winner-take-gradient can be plugged into any SGD-trained deep model and any loss.
- Strong empirical gains on oracle metrics across three different tasks and architectures, with qualitative analyses of diversity and specialization.
- Practicality: concurrent training, no extra hyperparameters, and substantially faster than prior MCL alternating schemes.
- Insightful analysis of emergent expertise and a k-winners ablation that connects specialization to oracle performance.
- Clear articulation of the motivation for oracle loss in ambiguous, multi-modal prediction settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited theoretical grounding: the method is justified by the block-coordinate view, but there is no convergence analysis under stochastic assignments, nor discussion of when winner-take-gradient is stable or may cause dead experts.
- Evaluation scope: CIFAR-10 and a subset of VOC11 are relatively small; there is no large-scale classification (e.g., ImageNet top-k) where oracle metrics are standard. Captioning uses an older neuraltalk2 stack and beam width 2.
- Oracle-only focus: while appropriate for the setting, there is little discussion of how sMCL affects non-oracle metrics (e.g., top-1, mean IoU of the best single predictor), or how to integrate with downstream selection/reranking.
- Comparisons to related paradigms are missing: mixtures of experts with learned gating and conditional computation could be competitive baselines when trained to promote diversity; likewise, diversity-promoting losses (e.g., repulsive regularizers) are not compared.
- Some experimental details are light: tie-breaking policy, initialization strategy across tasks (e.g., segmentation uses fine-tuning from a standard ensemble), sensitivity to batch size/M, and wall-clock training time beyond the “5× faster” claim tied to meta-iterations.
- Diversity measures are task-specific and partly superficial (unique n-grams). Human evaluation for caption diversity/quality is absent; for segmentation, diversity-coverage trade-offs beyond class-wise histograms could be quantified.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of an algorithmic contribution built on a well-motivated objective. The gradient derivation is correct given fixed assignments; using hard assignments within each minibatch is a reasonable stochastic block update. Claims are supported by consistent empirical improvements on oracle metrics and by qualitative evidence of diversity. However, the paper would benefit from:
- Additional ablations (warm-start vs. training from scratch; effect of k-winners/soft assignments/temperature; dead-expert mitigation).
- Runtime and compute comparisons in absolute terms.
- A discussion of failure modes (e.g., early collapse, class imbalance) and remedies.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with intuitive motivation and helpful qualitative figures. Algorithm description is straightforward. Some references are malformed/incomplete in the provided version; please fix citation formatting. For reproducibility, consider including pseudocode details (tie-breaking, batching, handling ties), hyperparameters per task, and code.

Originality
The core idea—integrating the MCL assignment step into SGD via winner-take-gradient—appears novel in the deep learning context and addresses a practical limitation of prior MCL approaches. The connection to known paradigms (hard EM, hard routing) is acknowledged implicitly; making these links explicit would clarify the conceptual landscape.

Significance
Producing diverse, high-recall sets of hypotheses is important across vision and language tasks. A simple, general approach that improves oracle metrics and induces interpretable specialization is likely to be adopted and built upon. The contribution is practical and broadly applicable, though stronger large-scale evaluations would bolster impact.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is sMCL to initialization and batch size? Do you observe “dead” experts, and if so, how do you mitigate (e.g., warm-up with independent training, entropy regularization, k-winners annealing)?
- Can you report effects on non-oracle performance (e.g., top-1/top-5 for classification, mean IoU of the best single predictor), and the diversity–accuracy trade-off when M varies?
- How does sMCL compare to a mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network trained to minimize oracle loss, or to diversity-promoting regularizers across ensemble members?
- For captioning, can you increase beam width and combine with sMCL at inference? How do human judgments of diversity/quality compare?
- Please provide absolute wall-clock times and compute usage for all methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practically useful algorithm
- Consistent gains on multiple tasks and architectures
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization
- Addresses a known pain point in prior MCL (retraining cost)

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis and discussion of stability
- Evaluation scope modest; lacks large-scale benchmarks and stronger baselines (MoE, diversity regularizers)
- Oracle-only focus without downstream selection integration or non-oracle metrics

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clear, broadly applicable idea with empirical benefits and good insights, despite some missing analyses and broader evaluations. Strengthening comparisons and ablations would further improve the work.
