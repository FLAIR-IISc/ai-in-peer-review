### Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep neural networks to produce a diverse set of plausible outputs, rather than a single "best" prediction. The authors frame this under the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, where the objective is to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble for any given input. The key challenge identified is that prior MCL methods, like the one by Guzman-Rivera et al. (2012), rely on an alternating optimization scheme that requires retraining models to convergence, making them infeasible for modern deep networks that take weeks to train.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel training algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). sMCL adapts the MCL objective to the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) setting. The proposed method is simple and elegant: during each training step, for each example in a mini-batch, only the ensemble member that yields the lowest loss (the "winner") has its parameters updated via backpropagation. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently in a single training run, making it dramatically more efficient than previous methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and broad applicability of sMCL on three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). Their experiments show that sMCL not only significantly outperforms baselines like independently trained ensembles in terms of oracle performance but also leads to the automatic emergence of interpretable, task-dependent specializations among the ensemble members.

**Evaluation**

This is a well-written, well-executed, and impactful paper that presents a simple, effective, and practical solution to an important problem.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a sound and clever adaptation of block coordinate descent to a stochastic setting. The claims of efficiency, improved oracle performance, and emergent specialization are thoroughly supported by a strong set of experiments across three diverse domains. The authors are careful to compare against relevant baselines, including standard ensembles, the original MCL algorithm, and a more recent boosting-style method by Dey et al. The analysis of the "interpretable expertise" that emerges is particularly insightful and provides compelling qualitative evidence for the method's effectiveness.

One minor weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis. While the algorithm is intuitive and empirically successful, a discussion of its convergence properties, even at a high level, would strengthen the paper. For instance, how does this stochastic "hard-EM" like procedure behave, and what guarantees can be made about its convergence compared to standard SGD?

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The motivation for generating diverse outputs and the limitations of prior work are laid out perfectly in the introduction. The sMCL algorithm is explained concisely and intuitively in Section 3, with Algorithm 2 providing a clear pseudo-code implementation. The experimental sections are well-structured, and the results, including figures and tables, are easy to interpret. The paper is written in a way that would allow an expert to easily reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The core idea of minimizing oracle loss is not new and builds directly on the MCL framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. However, the key contribution—the sMCL algorithm—is novel and significant. To my knowledge, this is the first work to propose a practical and efficient SGD-based method for directly minimizing the oracle loss for deep network ensembles. This adaptation is non-trivial and solves a major roadblock that prevented earlier MCL methods from being applied to deep learning. The analysis of emergent, task-dependent specialization is also an original and valuable contribution that provides new insights into the behavior of such ensembles.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Many real-world problems in perception and language are inherently ambiguous, and the standard practice of training models to produce a single, low-expected-loss output can lead to undesirable behaviors like "averaging" (e.g., the horse-cow) or generating generic, uninformative content. This paper provides a simple, parameter-free, and computationally efficient tool to address this problem. Because sMCL is agnostic to the specific network architecture and loss function, it is broadly applicable. Researchers and practitioners working on tasks with multi-modal outputs (e.g., future prediction, dialogue systems, creative generation, structured prediction) are likely to find this method immediately useful and build upon it. It advances the state of the art in training diverse ensembles in a demonstrable and practical way.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Solves a Practical Problem:** The paper provides an elegant and, crucially, computationally feasible solution for training deep ensembles to produce diverse outputs, a long-standing challenge. The 5x speedup over the most relevant baseline (MCL) makes this a practical contribution.
2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The method's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated across three distinct and important tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning), showcasing its generality.
3.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The core "winner-take-gradient" idea is simple to understand and implement, lowering the barrier to adoption.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond just reporting performance metrics and provides a fascinating analysis of how different forms of interpretable specialization emerge automatically depending on the task.
5.  **High Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow, making it a strong submission for the NIPS audience.

**Arguments for Rejection / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Grounding:** As mentioned, the paper is purely empirical. While the results are strong, some discussion on the optimization properties of sMCL would make the work more complete.
2.  **Scalability with Ensemble Size (M):** The experiments are limited to M<=6. It would be useful to discuss how the method scales to larger M. Does specialization continue to increase, or does performance plateau? Are there optimization stability issues with larger ensembles?
3.  **Comparison with Beam Search in Captioning:** In the image captioning results, a single model with a beam width of 5 achieves a higher oracle CIDEr score than the M=5 sMCL ensemble. While the authors correctly point out that sMCL produces more diverse n-grams, this performance gap deserves a slightly deeper discussion. Could the two approaches be combined (e.g., running beam search on each sMCL member)? This seems like a natural extension to explore.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper are overwhelming. It introduces a novel, simple, and highly effective algorithm that solves a significant practical problem in training deep learning models. The experimental validation is thorough and the analysis is insightful. This is a high-quality contribution that will likely be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
