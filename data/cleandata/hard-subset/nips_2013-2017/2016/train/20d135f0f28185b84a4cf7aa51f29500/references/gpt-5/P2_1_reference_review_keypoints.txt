- Paper proposes sMCL: a simple, SGD-compatible "winner-take-gradient" training rule for multiple-choice learning in deep ensembles, equivalent to an online hard-EM/hard MoE without an explicit gating network.  
- Experiments on CIFAR-10, PASCAL VOC, and MSCOCO show consistent oracle-metric improvements, faster oracle-loss optimization, and interpretable emergent specialization; a top-k variant reveals specialization vs. oracle-performance trade-offs.  
- Technical soundness: gradient routing derivation is correct for fixed assignments, but the method is heuristic with no convergence or monotonicity guarantees due to non-differentiable, changing assignments.  
- Empirical support is strong across diverse tasks with qualitative analyses reinforcing diversity claims.  
- Baseline fairness: the prior MCL baseline is limited to five meta-iterations for practicality, which weakens strict accuracy comparisons though sMCL’s speed advantage is clear.  
- Missing analyses include non-oracle selection performance (rerankers/selectors), stability/load balancing (expert collapse), and sensitivity to batch size, tie-breaking, M, and data imbalance.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with implementable pseudocode and compelling figures, but should clarify tie-breaking policy and computational overhead vs independent ensembles.  
- Originality: incremental but meaningful—practical adaptation of MCL to deep nets—closely related to classic mixture-of-experts and online hard-EM, so literature connections should be strengthened.  
- Significance: simple, general, and likely to be adopted where oracle metrics matter, though lack of end-to-end selection experiments limits immediate applicability.  
- Pros: parameter-free, broadly applicable, consistent oracle improvements, ~5x speedup over alternating MCL, and strong qualitative/top-k analyses.  
- Cons: no theoretical guarantees, focus on oracle metrics without selector experiments, potentially undertrained MCL baseline, limited sensitivity analyses, and missing explicit MoE citations.  
- Suggestions: evaluate simple selectors to bridge oracle to end-to-end performance, analyze stability and load balancing (or regularizers), add sensitivity studies (M, batch size, shared vs independent backbones), expand MoE/conditional computation citations, and release code.  
- Overall assessment: a well-executed, practical contribution with broad empirical support and speed advantages; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
