This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss using stochastic gradient descent. The core idea – a “winner-take-gradient” approach – is conceptually simple and aims to address the practical limitations of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods requiring costly retraining. The paper presents experimental results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, demonstrating that sMCL outperforms classical ensembles and other baselines.

While the central concept is promising, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor, yet consistently present, flaws that detract from its overall quality and ultimately lead me to recommend rejection. These flaws, while individually small, accumulate to create an impression of carelessness and insufficient attention to detail.

Firstly, the notation is inconsistent. For example, the set notation [n] is defined but then inconsistently used; sometimes it refers to indices, other times it's unclear. Similarly, the definition of `(y, ŷ)` as a loss function lacks specificity – is it symmetric? Euclidean distance? This ambiguity hinders understanding.

Secondly, the technical justification for sMCL, while intuitively appealing, feels somewhat hand-wavy. Equation (2) provides a partial derivative, but the rationale for *why* simply zeroing gradients for non-minimizing predictors accurately approximates the oracle loss minimization objective is not sufficiently elaborated. A more rigorous analysis, perhaps connecting it to Lagrangian relaxation or similar optimization techniques, would be beneficial. The claim that it “lends itself to a straightforward optimization strategy” is an understatement; it *is* the optimization strategy, and its validity requires greater scrutiny.

Thirdly, the experimental section, despite its breadth, lacks sufficient depth. The hyperparameters used for each experiment are not consistently reported. For example, the CIFAR10 experiment mentions a batch size and learning rate schedule, but doesn’t detail the optimizer used (SGD, Adam, etc.) or the momentum value beyond stating “momentum of 0.9.” The captioning experiments suffer from a similar lack of detail regarding the specific beam search parameters beyond beam width. The justification for choosing these parameter values is entirely absent. 

Furthermore, the comparison to existing methods is uneven. While MCL is included as a baseline, the authors acknowledge it was impractical to fully train on the segmentation and captioning tasks. This raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison. The claim that sMCL is "5x faster than MCL" needs qualification – it's 5x faster *for the limited MCL training performed*. 

The discussion of "interpretable expertise" feels overly strong given the evidence. While class-wise clustering is observed in the CIFAR10 experiment, the segmentation and captioning results show more nuanced, less clear-cut specializations. The qualitative examples, while illustrative, are cherry-picked and don't represent a comprehensive analysis of the ensemble's behavior. The claim that sMCL induces “different interpretable notions of specializations” needs stronger support.

Finally, there are numerous minor formatting and stylistic issues throughout the paper: inconsistencies in font sizes, awkward phrasing ("...widens the scope of some sentences…"), and several instances of missing punctuation. The references are not uniformly formatted, and the inclusion of a disclaimer regarding funding sources at the end feels oddly placed and verbose.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of stochastic gradient descent to the MCL paradigm.
*   Demonstrated improvements over baseline ensembles on multiple tasks.
*   Potential for generating diverse and informative outputs.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous technical justification for the proposed method.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup and hyperparameter reporting.
*   Uneven comparison to existing methods.
*   Overstated claims about "interpretable expertise."
*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, writing, and formatting.



In conclusion, while the core idea of sMCL is interesting, the paper is ultimately marred by a lack of rigor, detail, and polish. The accumulation of these flaws undermines the credibility of the presented results and prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper would benefit significantly from a more thorough technical analysis, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and a careful revision to address the numerous minor issues.
