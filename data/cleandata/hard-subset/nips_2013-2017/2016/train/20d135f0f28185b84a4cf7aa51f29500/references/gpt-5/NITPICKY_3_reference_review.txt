Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-friendly variant of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training ensembles to minimize oracle loss. The key idea is a “winner-take-gradient” scheme: for each sample in a minibatch, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss backpropagates gradients. This interleaves assignment and optimization (akin to hard-EM with per-batch reassignments), aiming to avoid the costly retraining cycles of prior MCL methods. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC segmentation (with FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM) show improved oracle metrics compared to independent ensembles, the alternating MCL of Guzman-Rivera et al., and a boosting-style approach (Dey et al.). The paper emphasizes emergent specialization (e.g., label-space clustering in CIFAR-10, class/shape biases in segmentation, and n-gram diversity in captioning).

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al. ’12/’13], replacing alternating retraining with a stochastic hard-assignment during SGD. It is related to mixture-of-experts (MoE) and winner-take-all/hard-EM style training (e.g., Jordan & Jacobs ’94; not cited) where experts specialize via latent assignments—here the gating is implicit (lowest instantaneous loss). It is also related to “diverse M-best” inference in structured prediction (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.) and diversity-promoting ensembles (negative correlation learning, DPPs; some references missing). The captioning comparison to beam search highlights inference-time diversity vs training-time diversity.

Strengths
- Simple, general, easy-to-implement training rule applicable across architectures and losses.
- Concurrent training of ensemble members; no explicit meta-iterations/retraining.
- Empirical evidence of specialization emerging without architectural gating.
- Consistent gains on oracle metrics across three tasks; speed advantage over alternating MCL.
- Interesting qualitative diversity (segmentation variations; varied caption structures).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Novelty is incremental: the algorithm is essentially hard-EM/winner-take-all gating inside SGD. There is no theoretical analysis of convergence, stability, or variance properties of the stochastic hard assignment—particularly important for non-smooth reassignment dynamics.
- No mechanism to prevent collapse/dead experts. The paper does not report the frequency of expert starvation or any balancing (e.g., entropy/regularizers, load-balancing constraints, annealing, soft assignments). It claims “parameter-free” but relies on arbitrary tie-breaking and batch dynamics; this can materially affect training.
- The comparison to MCL is potentially unfair: “5x faster” is attributed to 5 meta-iterations, but compute per step still scales with M; alternating MCL could adopt early stopping/fewer iterations. No wall-clock/compute-normalized comparison is reported (e.g., same total FLOPs).
- Baseline coverage is incomplete. Standard MoE with a learned gating network is not compared; such a baseline is highly relevant. For captioning, comparing to beam search over a single model (and not a comparable beam over each ensemble member) entangles training vs inference diversity. The Dey et al. method may not be designed for small classification tasks, yet is used as a baseline there.
- Oracle-only evaluation is limiting. For captioning, CIDEr-D oracle is reported, but no SPICE, METEOR, or human evaluation, and “unique n-grams” is a weak proxy for semantic diversity/quality. For segmentation, evaluation is on an unspecified subset of VOC 2011 val; no standard test or comprehensive tables; no per-class metrics; no statistical significance.
- Methodological details are missing/ambiguous:
  - How are gradients aggregated through the shared CNN in the captioning setup? Averaged or summed? Any gradient scaling across members? Any impact on feature drift?
  - What loss functions are used per task (e.g., cross-entropy for classification/segmentation), with what class balancing/weighting?
  - How are ties resolved? Does tie-breaking randomness cause instability?
  - Does batch size affect assignment stability? Any annealing schedule (e.g., start with k>1 and move to k=1)? The paper shows a k-variant only for CIFAR-10 but does not explore it elsewhere.
  - Initialization: segmentation sMCL is initialized from an independently trained ensemble; classification is not. This inconsistency can bias results. No ablation on initialization sensitivity.
- Scope/scale: CIFAR-10 is small; VOC subset is not standard; image captioning uses a relatively old pipeline with small beam (2). Lack of results on larger-scale settings (e.g., ImageNet) weakens claims of broad applicability.
- Claim of “parameter-free” is overstated: M must be chosen; tie-breaking and potential balancing hyperparameters (even if omitted) matter; the generalized k-penalty introduces a tunable parameter.
- Clarity: Algorithm 2 is not shown; definitions of the “sMCL layer” are vague. The derivative in Eq. (2) is written w.r.t. outputs; parameter gradients and the effect of reassignment are not discussed.

Questions for authors
- Provide compute-normalized comparisons (same total FLOPs/wall-clock) versus alternating MCL and an MoE baseline with a learned gating network.
- Report expert utilization statistics (assignment entropy, sample counts per expert over time). Do any experts collapse? Any mitigation?
- Clarify gradient aggregation through the shared CNN in captioning. Did you try independent CNNs (perhaps with gradient checkpointing or model parallelism) to avoid gradient interference?
- Report standard evaluation splits and full metrics (segmentation per-class IoU; captioning SPICE/METEOR; statistical significance).
- Explore soft assignments/entropy regularization or annealed k>1 schedules to stabilize training and prevent starvation. Any empirical differences?
- Analyze sensitivity to batch size, learning rate, and initialization.

Minor issues and formatting
- Numerous reference formatting errors/truncations (e.g., “and A,” “and L,” missing years/venues/pages).
- “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)” missing the ordinal number.
- Inconsistent dataset naming (Pascal/PASCAL; VOC 2011 vs the more standard 2012).
- Typo: “Intepretable” → “Interpretable.” Hyphenation inconsistencies (task-dependent, fine-tune).
- Figures/algorithms frequently referenced but not shown here; ensure captions and axes specify exact numbers, not just trends.

Pros
- Simple, broadly applicable idea; easy drop-in training procedure.
- Demonstrated qualitative diversity and emergent specialization.
- Empirical improvements on oracle metrics across tasks; practical speedups over alternating MCL.

Cons
- Incremental contribution without theoretical backing; closely related to hard MoE/EM.
- Limited, sometimes non-standard experimental protocol; weak baselines (missing learned gating MoE).
- Lack of robustness analysis and ablations; reproducibility details missing.
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited evaluation on standard large-scale benchmarks.
- Numerous formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is practical and results are promising, the contribution is incremental relative to established hard-assignment expert training, the evaluation is insufficiently rigorous and incomplete, and the presentation has multiple clarity/formatting issues. Strengthening baselines (including MoE with learned gating), adding robustness/ablation studies and larger-scale evaluations, and improving clarity would significantly improve the paper.

Confidence
High.
