This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for diverse deep ensembles. The core idea is to minimize oracle loss – the loss incurred by selecting the best prediction from an ensemble – by allowing only the best-performing network in the ensemble to receive gradients during training. This approach, framed as a stochastic block gradient descent, avoids the costly retraining steps of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over standard ensembles and existing MCL approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the practical need for diverse predictions in real-world applications where a single "best" answer may not always exist. The introduction clearly articulates the benefits of MCL and sets the stage for the proposed sMCL algorithm. The connection to existing work on ensemble learning and multiple solution generation is appropriately discussed, and the limitations of prior approaches (specifically the computational cost of retraining) are effectively highlighted.  The technical contribution, formulating MCL as a stochastic block gradient descent, is significant and addresses a key bottleneck in applying MCL to deep learning models.  The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of tasks and architectures, and the results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of sMCL. The inclusion of interpretable expertise analysis (demonstrating specialization within the ensemble) adds valuable insight. The clarity of the paper is generally good, with a logical flow and clear explanations of the proposed method.  The mathematical formulation is concise and accessible.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The sMCL algorithm offers a significant improvement over existing MCL methods by enabling efficient training of deep ensembles.
* **Effectiveness:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent performance gains across diverse tasks and architectures.
* **Efficiency:**  sMCL avoids costly retraining steps, making it practical for large-scale deep learning models.
* **Interpretability:** The analysis of ensemble member specialization provides valuable insights into the learned representations.
* **Generality:** The method is applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent and agnostic to the task-specific loss function.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Modern Diversity Techniques:** While comparisons to classical ensembles and previous MCL methods are provided, the paper lacks a comparison to more recent and sophisticated techniques specifically designed for inducing diversity in ensembles, such as those based on adversarial training or mutual information maximization. A comparison to techniques like Deep Ensemble with data augmentation or MixUp would be useful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of sMCL to hyperparameters, such as ensemble size (M).  How does performance vary with different M values? Are there optimal ranges for M depending on the task or architecture?
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper presents sMCL as a stochastic approximation of an upper bound on the oracle loss. While intuitive, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties and approximation quality of this stochastic approach would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL, an ablation study examining the contribution of different components (e.g., the stochastic gradient descent aspect) could provide further insights.
* **Captioning Results:** The claim of significant improvement in captioning is less compelling than the results in image classification and segmentation.  Qualitative examples are provided, but quantitative results (beyond CIDEr-D) would be beneficial, and a more detailed analysis of the generated captions is needed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear performance gains.
* The method is efficient, practical, and applicable to a wide range of tasks and architectures.
* The interpretable expertise analysis provides valuable insights into the learned representations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to related work is not exhaustive, particularly regarding modern diversity-inducing techniques.
* The lack of theoretical analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis weakens the paper.
* The captioning results require further investigation and more comprehensive evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, effectiveness, and efficiency of sMCL make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Include a comparison to more recent diversity-inducing techniques (e.g., adversarial training, MixUp).
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of sMCL to hyperparameters, particularly ensemble size (M).
3.  Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed stochastic approach.
4.  Consider conducting an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of sMCL.
5.  Provide more detailed analysis and quantitative results for the image captioning experiments.
