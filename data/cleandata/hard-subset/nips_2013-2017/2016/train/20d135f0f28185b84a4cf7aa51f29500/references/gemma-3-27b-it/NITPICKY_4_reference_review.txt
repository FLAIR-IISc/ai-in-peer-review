This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss. The authors frame this as a generalization of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and argue that their stochastic gradient descent-based approach overcomes the computational limitations of previous MCL formulations requiring costly retraining. The core idea—allowing gradients to flow only to the best-performing ensemble member for each example—is elegantly simple. The paper presents experimental results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, demonstrating performance gains over classical ensembles and other baselines, including the original MCL.

While the idea is compelling and the results appear positive, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that collectively detract from its impact. The writing, though generally understandable, is often verbose and repetitive. The introduction, for example, rehashes the motivation for multiple-choice learning multiple times without significant added value. 

Specifically, the notation is inconsistent. The use of both `LO(D)` and Eq. (1) to represent the oracle loss is confusing. The set notation `[n]` is introduced but not consistently used. More importantly, the mathematical derivation leading to Eq. (2) is presented without sufficient justification. While the intuition is clear, a more rigorous derivation would strengthen the argument. 

The experimental section is also problematic. The comparisons to baselines are not always fair or complete. For instance, the claim that MCL performs similarly to sMCL on small classification tasks but degrades on segmentation and captioning lacks a thorough investigation of hyperparameters. The authors mention that MCL became infeasible for the latter tasks but do not provide details on why – was it memory limitations, convergence issues, or simply computational cost? 

The descriptions of the baselines are brief and sometimes vague. The specifics of the “classical ensembles” are not always clear (e.g., how were initializations varied?).  The method of Dey et al. [5] is dismissed too quickly – a more in-depth analysis of its limitations in these specific contexts would be more constructive. The qualitative results (Figures 5 and 7) are presented without sufficient quantitative justification. While visually appealing, these examples do not provide conclusive evidence of the superiority of sMCL.

Furthermore, several minor formatting errors are present:  The citation style is inconsistent. Some references are fully formatted, while others are not. The figure captions are sometimes incomplete or awkwardly worded.  The phrase "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" is oddly placed within the introduction and feels out of place. The acknowledgements section is excessively long and reads like a list of funding sources rather than a concise acknowledgement of contributions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of stochastic gradient descent to minimize oracle loss in deep ensembles.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements across diverse tasks (image classification, segmentation, captioning).
*   The simplicity and generality of the proposed sMCL algorithm.
*   Potential for significant impact on ensemble learning and structured prediction.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in writing, notation, and experimental setup.
*   Lack of rigorous mathematical justification for key derivations.
*   Incomplete comparison to baselines and limited analysis of hyperparameters.
*   Overly verbose and repetitive presentation.
*   Formatting inconsistencies and excessively long acknowledgements.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and potential significance of the core idea, the paper is plagued by a surprising number of minor issues that detract from its overall quality. While none of these flaws are individually catastrophic, their cumulative effect creates a sense of carelessness and undermines the credibility of the work. I believe that the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the aforementioned issues and resubmit in the future. A thorough revision addressing the clarity, rigor, and completeness of the presentation would significantly strengthen the paper.
