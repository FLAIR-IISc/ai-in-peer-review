Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL): training an ensemble to minimize the oracle loss, i.e., the loss of the best prediction among M outputs. The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” SGD procedure that, for each training example, backpropagates only through the ensemble member with the lowest loss. This replaces the alternating retrain-and-reassign procedure of prior MCL work (Guzman-Rivera et al.) with an online, parallelizable variant suitable for deep networks.

The paper convincingly argues that oracle loss is appropriate in downstream systems where a subsequent module (human, verifier, reranker) can pick among multiple hypotheses. It relates to several lines of work: (i) MCL and diverse M-best in structured prediction (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.), (ii) ensemble diversity methods (bagging, negative correlation, artificial data), (iii) mixture-of-experts and expert routing, and (iv) diverse decoding for structured outputs. Compared to prior MCL approaches that require repeated retraining or sequential boosting-like procedures (Dey et al.), sMCL is computationally attractive and broadly applicable to modern deep architectures.

Empirically, sMCL is demonstrated on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning with CNN+LSTM. The core results show consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, sequential reweighting baselines, and (within feasible iterations) the original MCL, while also inducing interpretable specialization across ensemble members.

Strengths:
- Simple, general, parameter-free algorithm that integrates seamlessly with SGD and arbitrary losses/architectures.
- Trains all ensemble members concurrently; avoids the prohibitive retraining of prior MCL and is reported ~5x faster than MCL under chosen schedules.
- Broad empirical coverage (classification, segmentation, captioning) with consistent improvements in oracle metrics and qualitative diversity.
- Nice analysis of emergent specialization (class-wise for CIFAR-10; more nuanced, shape/frequency specialization for segmentation; n-gram/style diversity for captioning).
- Clear motivation for oracle loss in practical pipelines.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited reporting of non-oracle metrics (e.g., per-model accuracy, ensemble average, majority vote). It remains unclear how much individual model quality degrades and what the trade-off is versus diversity.
- Missing comparisons to closely related alternatives:
  - Mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network (soft or hard) that can also promote specialization.
  - Diversity-regularized deep ensembles (e.g., negative correlation, disagreement/ambiguity penalties).
  - For captioning, union of beam-search outputs from multiple independently trained models (beam per model + union), which is arguably a stronger oracle baseline than single-model beam.
- Limited theoretical characterization. The “winner-take-gradient” is a subgradient of a non-smooth upper bound, but there is no convergence analysis, and potential instability, dead experts, or assignment imbalance are not deeply studied.
- Scalability and compute: computing M forward passes per example is required; memory constraints are acknowledged (shared CNN trunk in captioning), but a more thorough complexity/memory analysis as M grows is missing.
- Fairness/consistency of training budgets: in segmentation, sMCL applies additional fine-tuning; it should be clarified whether baselines received equivalent training schedules to rule out gains from extra epochs.
- Sensitivity/ablations: batch size, tie-breaking policy, effect of k-winners (k > 1) beyond the brief CIFAR-10 study, initialization sensitivity, and robustness to different losses are not fully explored.
- Dataset and architectures are not SOTA, though this is reasonable for demonstrating a training principle; still, at least one large-scale top-k classification (e.g., ImageNet) experiment would strengthen claims about practical impact.

Quality:
Technically sound and well motivated. The gradient rule follows a valid subgradient of the min operator; the algorithm is implementable and efficient. However, more careful treatment of stability (e.g., preventing expert starvation via load-balancing or entropy terms) and more comprehensive baselines would strengthen the empirical evidence.

Clarity:
Overall clear and well organized. The problem setup and algorithm are easy to follow, and qualitative analyses are helpful. Some details would benefit from clarification: tie-breaking strategy; how gradients are handled in the shared-CNN setting for captioning under sMCL; exact training schedules for baselines to ensure fairness. A brief complexity/memory discussion would improve clarity.

Originality:
The idea of optimizing oracle loss is not new (MCL), but the contribution of an SGD-friendly, concurrent training procedure that avoids costly retraining is a meaningful and practical advance for deep networks. The empirical observation of emergent specialization across diverse tasks is compelling.

Significance:
Practical significance is high for applications requiring multiple plausible outputs. The simplicity and generality of sMCL make it likely to be adopted. Demonstrated gains across tasks support its usefulness, though additional baselines and larger-scale experiments would further solidify impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report non-oracle performance: per-model accuracy, ensemble vote, and calibration to quantify trade-offs between diversity and individual quality.
- Add baselines: mixture-of-experts with learned gating; diversity-regularized deep ensembles.
- For captioning, compare against union of beams from multiple independent models and sMCL+beam to decouple decoding diversity from training diversity.
- Provide ablations: tie-breaking strategies; batch size sensitivity; load-balancing or soft-assignment variants (temperature-controlled softmin); impact of k-winners on all tasks.
- Analyze assignment distributions and dead experts; consider a small regularizer to balance expert load when necessary.
- Include complexity/memory scaling with M and wall-clock comparisons at equal training budgets.
- If feasible, add a large-scale classification study (e.g., ImageNet top-k oracle) to emphasize significance.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
This is a simple, general, and effective method with solid empirical evidence and clear motivation. While additional baselines and analyses would strengthen the paper, the contribution is practical and timely, and the paper is well written.
