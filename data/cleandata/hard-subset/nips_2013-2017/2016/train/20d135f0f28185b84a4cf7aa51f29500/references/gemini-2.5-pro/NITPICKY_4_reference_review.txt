### Summary

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training ensembles of deep networks to produce a diverse set of outputs. The training objective is to minimize the "oracle loss," which is the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble on any given data point. Building on prior work in Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8], which used a block coordinate descent approach infeasible for deep networks, sMCL introduces a simple and efficient stochastic optimization strategy. In each training step, for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is computed for all ensemble members, but the gradient is backpropagated only through the member with the lowest loss (the "winner"). The authors demonstrate this "winner-take-gradient" approach on three distinct tasks: image classification with CNNs, semantic segmentation with FCNs, and image captioning with CNN+RNNs. The results show that sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines in terms of oracle performance, and leads to the emergence of interpretable specialization among the ensemble members.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating diverse sets of high-quality predictions, which is more useful than a single, potentially ambiguous output in many real-world applications. The motivation provided in the introduction is clear and compelling.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Generality:** The proposed sMCL algorithm is exceptionally simple to implement, intuitive, and computationally efficient. Its agnosticism to the model architecture and the specific loss function makes it broadly applicable, as demonstrated by the variety of tasks tackled.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method shows consistent and significant improvements over baselines (independently trained ensembles, Dey et al. [5]) across all three experiments. The qualitative results, especially for segmentation and captioning, effectively illustrate the increased diversity of the generated outputs.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of emergent specialization is a key strength. The clear label-space partitioning observed in the CIFAR-10 experiment (Fig. 4c) provides a compelling visualization of the method's behavior.

### Weaknesses and Detailed Comments

Despite the promising core idea, the paper suffers from numerous issues in experimental rigor, clarity, and presentation that, in aggregate, significantly undermine the contribution.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.
    *   **Formatting:** There are glaring formatting errors. The conference header ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") is inexplicably placed under the introduction. The bibliography is highly inconsistent and incomplete: reference [6] contains stray text ("and A,"), [17] is just an author's name, [13] is only a URL, and many entries lack proper venue or year information. Figure 3 lacks an overarching caption. Such sloppiness suggests a rushed preparation.
    *   **Vagueness:** The description of the experiment in Section 4.1 where "the lowest k error predictors are penalized" is too vague. It is unclear how this penalty is implemented. Is the gradient distributed among the top-k, or is it simply zeroed out for them? This experiment is presented as a key analysis of specialization, but the lack of detail makes it irreproducible.

2.  **Experimental Rigor and Design Flaws:** Several key experimental choices and comparisons are questionable.
    *   **Compromised Captioning Model:** In Section 4.3, the authors state that due to "memory restrictions," a single shared VGG16 network was used for all LSTM models in the ensemble. This is a major methodological flaw. It violates the paper's premise of training an ensemble of distinct models `f_m`, as a substantial portion of the parameters (the entire CNN feature extractor) is shared. This setup is more akin to a single model with multiple heads than a true ensemble, and it is unclear how this confounding factor affects the results and the emergence of specialization. Claims of generality are weakened when the reported experiments require such significant architectural compromises.
    *   **Weak Baselines and Unfair Comparisons:**
        *   The comparison to MCL [8] is not entirely fair. The authors state that retraining MCL ensembles for the segmentation task was "infeasible" and thus only report results after five meta-iterations. While this highlights sMCL's efficiency, it also means the comparison is against a baseline that was not run to its full potential, potentially inflating the relative performance of sMCL. The claim of a "5x speedup" is arbitrary and tied to this specific choice of 5 iterations.
        *   In the captioning experiment (Table 1), sMCL is compared against beam search from a single model. This is an apples-to-oranges comparison. Beam search is an inference-time decoding strategy, whereas sMCL is a training-time methodology. The authors note that beam search could also be applied to sMCL models but fail to report these crucial results. A fair comparison would be between a single model with a beam of size M and a single sMCL member with a beam of size M, or an sMCL ensemble where each member uses beam search. As presented, the comparison is incomplete and potentially misleading.
    *   **Model/Dataset Choices:** The use of the "CIFAR10-Quick" network, a very small and dated architecture, for the classification task limits the impact of the findings. While suitable for a proof-of-concept, demonstrating the method's efficacy on a more challenging dataset (e.g., ImageNet) with a modern architecture (e.g., ResNet [11], which was contemporary) would have made the results far more convincing.
    *   **Unusual Initialization:** For the segmentation experiment, the models were initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble. This is an atypical procedure. Does sMCL struggle to converge from a random initialization on this more complex, structured-output problem? This detail is not justified and raises questions about the stability and robustness of the training procedure.

3.  **Overstated Claims:**
    *   The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free." This is inaccurate. The number of ensemble members, `M`, is a critical hyperparameter that directly influences model capacity, diversity, and computational cost.
    *   The claim of "interpretable expertise" is strong but not consistently supported. While compelling on CIFAR-10, the authors concede it is "much less evident" for segmentation. For captioning, the evidence is anecdotal and qualitative. The paper would be stronger if it proposed a quantitative metric for specialization and evaluated it across tasks.

**Conclusion:**

The paper introduces sMCL, an elegant and simple idea for training diverse deep ensembles. However, the work in its current form is undermined by a combination of methodological compromises (most notably the shared CNN in the captioning experiment), weak or unfair baseline comparisons, and a general lack of rigor and polish in its presentation. While the core concept is valuable, the supporting evidence is not strong enough to warrant publication at NIPS without substantial revision to address these fundamental flaws.
