

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by minimizing oracle loss. The authors formalize Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) [8,9] for deep networks, introducing a stochastic gradient descent approach that backpropagates gradients only to the lowest-error predictor for each example. The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free," "agnostic to model architecture," and "5x faster than MCL" while outperforming baselines on classification, segmentation, and captioning tasks.

## Strengths

The paper addresses an important problem in ensemble learning - producing diverse, high-quality predictions rather than a single mode-seeking output. The "winner-take-gradient" approach is conceptually simple and elegant, with promising empirical results across multiple domains. The demonstration of automatic specialization in ensemble members (e.g., classifiers focusing on specific classes) is particularly compelling. The experimental setup is well-designed, with appropriate baselines including classical ensembles, MCL [8,9], and Dey et al. [5].

## Weaknesses

### Methodological Gaps

1. **Ambiguous Tie-Breaking**: The paper states "ties broken arbitrarily" but fails to specify how this affects results. In practice, tie-breaking strategy could significantly impact the specialization observed. Without this detail, the reproducibility of the results is questionable.

2. **Overstated Claims**: The claim that sMCL is "parameter-free" is misleading. While the algorithm itself has no additional hyperparameters, the number of ensemble members M must be chosen, and the paper doesn't discuss how this choice affects performance. This should be clarified.

3. **Speedup Claim**: The "5x faster" claim lacks context. The paper states "the factor 5 is the result of choosing 5 meta-iterations" for MCL, but doesn't provide training times or hardware specifications. A fair comparison should include actual wall-clock time measurements under identical conditions.

### Experimental Details

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The paper reports performance improvements (e.g., "6-10%") without statistical significance tests. For instance, in Table 1, the oracle CIDEr-D scores don't include error bars or p-values, making it impossible to determine if improvements are statistically meaningful.

2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The paper states MCL "performs similarly to sMCL on small classification tasks" but then says "MCL performance drops substantially on segmentation and captioning tasks." Without showing the actual performance numbers for MCL on segmentation/captioning (only noting it's "infeasible" to train sufficiently), this claim lacks empirical support.

3. **Inconsistent Evaluation**: The captioning experiments use "beam search with beam width of two" but don't justify this choice. The paper claims "beam search from a single model achieves greater oracle" but doesn't explain why the same beam search strategy isn't applied to sMCL ensembles.

### Technical Presentation

1. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses both "M" and "M" for ensemble size (e.g., "g(x) = {f1(x), f2(x), . . . , fM(x)}" vs "M ∈ [M]"). This inconsistency could confuse readers.

2. **Missing Theoretical Justification**: The paper states that "different ensemble members are free to specialize on subsets of the data distribution" but provides no theoretical basis for why this occurs with sMCL but not with standard ensembles. The observed specialization is empirically demonstrated but not theoretically justified.

3. **Overstated Novelty**: The claim that "we are the first to formalize these ideas in the context of deep networks" is problematic. Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9] explicitly addressed MCL for deep networks in their original work, though they used a different training approach. The paper should more clearly distinguish its contribution from prior work.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising empirical results, the methodological gaps and unclear claims undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The "5x faster" claim lacks proper context, the tie-breaking strategy is unaddressed, and statistical significance is missing from key results. The overstatement of novelty in the context of prior MCL work is particularly concerning for a NIPS submission.

The paper makes a modest contribution to the problem of diverse ensemble learning, but the lack of technical precision and incomplete experimental reporting prevent it from being a strong NIPS submission. While the problem is important and the approach has merit, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish its technical novelty or provide the level of detail required for reproducibility.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Providing statistical significance for all reported improvements
2. Clarifying the tie-breaking strategy and its impact
3. Adding hardware specifications for the speedup claim
4. More precisely distinguishing from prior MCL work
5. Including a theoretical justification for the observed specialization

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The paper's technical shortcomings outweigh its modest empirical contributions.
