This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss. The core idea – a ‘winner-take-gradient’ scheme during SGD – is remarkably simple yet potentially impactful. It sidesteps the expensive retraining required by previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods and offers a practical path to building ensembles that explicitly aim for diverse, high-quality predictions, rather than simply averaging independent models. The framing of the problem within a broader context of real-world applications needing multiple plausible solutions is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The experimental results across image classification, segmentation, and captioning are compelling, consistently demonstrating sMCL’s superiority over baselines, including the more computationally intensive MCL. The observation that specialization emerges organically during training, without explicit regularization, is particularly exciting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The sMCL algorithm is a genuinely new approach to ensemble training. The stochastic block gradient descent formulation for oracle loss minimization is a clever and effective simplification over prior work.
*   **Practicality:** Addressing the computational bottleneck of existing MCL methods is a significant contribution. The ability to train ensembles concurrently using standard SGD makes sMCL accessible and scalable.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The consistent outperformance of sMCL across diverse tasks and architectures is strong evidence of its efficacy.
*   **Interpretability:** The emergent specialization of ensemble members is fascinating and provides valuable insights into the learning process. The visualizations support this claim effectively.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation and technical details are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors provide the gradient derivation, a more in-depth theoretical understanding of *why* sMCL works so well would be beneficial. For example, exploring the relationship between oracle loss and other ensemble objectives would strengthen the argument.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively explore the sensitivity of sMCL to hyperparameters. Although stated as parameter-free, a discussion on batch size or learning rate effects within the sMCL framework would be valuable.
*   **Comparison Depth:** While the comparison to MCL is important, the limited number of meta-iterations for MCL makes the comparison somewhat unfair. A more thorough comparison, even if computationally expensive, would be more convincing. Similarly, the comparison to Dey et al. could be strengthened with more exhaustive tuning.
*   **Limited Ablation:** Ablation studies on the impact of ensemble size (beyond the ranges shown) and the effect of different loss functions would be insightful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The novelty and simplicity of sMCL are highly appealing.
*   The demonstrated improvement over existing methods is significant.
*   The potential impact on areas requiring diverse predictions (e.g., robotics, autonomous systems) is substantial.
*   The emergent specialization observed is a valuable discovery.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex idea.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of deeper theoretical analysis.
*   The relatively limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The potentially incomplete comparison to some baselines.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and convincing experimental results. While some theoretical and experimental refinements could further strengthen the work, the novelty and practical significance of sMCL outweigh these minor shortcomings. I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning and warrants acceptance. The core concept is truly novel, and the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness in a robust manner. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws in favor of the significant advancement this work presents.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
