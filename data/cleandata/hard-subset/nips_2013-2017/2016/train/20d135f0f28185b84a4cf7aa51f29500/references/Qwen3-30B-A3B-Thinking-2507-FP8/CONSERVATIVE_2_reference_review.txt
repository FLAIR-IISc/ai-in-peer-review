

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning" (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that produce multiple plausible predictions per input, rather than a single prediction. The authors claim their method avoids the need for costly retraining in previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches by using a "winner-take-gradient" training strategy. The paper demonstrates results across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

**Quality**

The paper presents a technically sound algorithm (Algorithm 2) that implements a stochastic variant of the MCL framework. The method is conceptually straightforward: during training, only the ensemble member that produces the lowest loss for a given example receives gradient updates. The experiments show modest improvements (6-10%) over baselines on three different tasks.

However, the paper lacks critical elements for a rigorous scientific contribution. There is no theoretical analysis of why this approach should work better than existing methods. The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free" but fails to acknowledge that M (the ensemble size) is a hyperparameter that requires tuning. The speed claim (5x faster than MCL) is weakly supported, with the justification being that the authors chose 5 meta-iterations for MCL, which could be increased in other applications. The paper also does not provide statistical significance tests for the reported improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the observed gains are meaningful.

The most concerning quality issue is the lack of ablation studies. The authors claim that the winner-take-gradient approach is responsible for the observed specialization, but they provide no evidence that this is the key innovation rather than other factors. For example, the paper does not compare sMCL to a variant where all ensemble members receive gradient updates (as in standard ensemble training), which would isolate the effect of the winner-take-gradient strategy.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem statement is well-motivated with practical examples of when multiple predictions are preferable to single predictions. The algorithm is described clearly, and the experiments are well-organized. The paper adequately explains the oracle evaluation metric, which is crucial for evaluating multiple-prediction systems.

**Originality**

The paper builds on the MCL framework from Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9], which the authors cite. The main innovation appears to be the stochastic adaptation of MCL that avoids retraining. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from related work. Vondrick et al. [29] and Dey et al. [5] already proposed methods for training multiple-prediction systems that are similar to MCL, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how sMCL differs from or improves upon these approaches.

The paper claims to be the "first to formalize these ideas in the context of deep networks," but the related work section already cites previous work on MCL that could be extended to deep networks (e.g., Vondrick et al. [29] and Dey et al. [5]). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why these previous methods aren't applicable to deep networks or why sMCL is a necessary improvement.

**Significance**

The problem of producing diverse predictions is indeed important for applications where ambiguity exists. However, the significance of the paper is limited by the modest improvements (6-10%) over baselines, which are not particularly large or consistent across tasks. The paper claims to "significantly outperform" baselines, but this is an overstatement given the magnitude of the improvements.

The paper also fails to demonstrate that the method is necessary for achieving diversity. Standard ensemble methods (e.g., training multiple models with different initializations) already produce diverse predictions, and the paper doesn't convincingly show that sMCL produces significantly better diversity than these standard methods. The paper's claim that sMCL "produces sets of outputs with high oracle performance" is not particularly novel, as this is the primary goal of the MCL framework.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution. The proposed sMCL method appears to work well in the limited experiments presented, but the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support the claims of superiority over existing methods. The lack of theoretical analysis, ablation studies, and statistical significance testing undermines the paper's scientific rigor.

The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from previous work on Multiple Choice Learning, and the improvements claimed are modest. The paper's focus on "diverse deep ensembles" is important, but the specific contribution of sMCL is not clearly established.

I recommend rejection. The paper does not meet the high standards expected for NIPS in terms of rigor, novelty, and significance. The claims made in the paper are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. While the problem is interesting, the paper does not provide a compelling new method that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The authors would need to provide more thorough validation, including ablation studies, statistical significance tests, and a clearer comparison to the most relevant prior work, before this could be considered for publication.
