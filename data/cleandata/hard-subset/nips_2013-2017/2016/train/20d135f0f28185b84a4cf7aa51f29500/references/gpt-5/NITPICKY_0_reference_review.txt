Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), an SGD-friendly variant of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for training deep ensembles to optimize oracle loss. The core idea is “winner-take-gradient”: for each example in a minibatch, backpropagate the loss only through the ensemble member that currently incurs the smallest loss on that example (ties broken arbitrarily). This interleaves assignment and parameter updates, avoiding the costly alternating retraining used in prior MCL. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic (in practice relying on standard differentiable surrogates). Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM) suggest improved oracle metrics and emergent specialization across ensemble members compared to independently trained ensembles, prior MCL (at fixed meta-iterations), and a boosting-like method.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned relative to MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.), diverse M-best for structured models, and ensemble diversity techniques. However, the connection to mixture-of-experts (hard/soft gating; Jordan & Jacobs, Jacobs et al.) and more recent diversity-inducing inference methods (e.g., diverse beam search for captioning) is not adequately developed. The proposed procedure is very close to online hard-EM for a mixture model with an unlearned gate (argmin loss). This relationship should be explicitly analyzed.

Strengths
- Simple idea, easy to implement within SGD frameworks; plausible to use in practice.
- Concurrent training avoids repeated retraining; wall-clock speed-up over alternating MCL is reasonable.
- Empirical evidence of emergent, interpretable specialization for different tasks (class-wise clustering in CIFAR-10; qualitative segmentation and caption diversity).
- Method does not require designing task-specific diversity losses.
- Demonstrates applicability across three modalities (classification, segmentation, captioning).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory: No convergence guarantees or analysis of the biased, discontinuous gradient estimator induced by the argmin assignment; ties and instability are not addressed. The proposed gradient is not an unbiased estimator of the true gradient of the oracle objective. Risk of assignment “starvation” (one model monopolizes data) is not studied, nor are mechanisms to prevent collapse.
- Comparison to mixture-of-experts: Missing a strong baseline with a learned gating network (soft/hard), which is the most natural competitor. sMCL can be seen as MoE with a zero-capacity gate; this must be discussed and compared.
- Experimental rigor: Results are reported almost exclusively in oracle metrics, with limited standard metrics and no statistical significance, variance, or multiple seeds. For segmentation, evaluation is on a “subset” of VOC11 val (not specified), not standard test splits; per-class IoU and absolute numbers are missing. For captioning, only CIDEr-D oracle is reported; no BLEU/SPICE, no human evaluation of diversity. “Unique n-grams” is a weak proxy for semantic diversity.
- Baselines: MCL is capped at 5 meta-iterations with admitted suboptimal performance; no assessment under enough iterations to reach parity. The boosting-like method saturates as ensemble size grows, but implementation and hyperparameters are under-specified. For captioning, beam search is compared but capacity/parameter-count differs; also diverse beam search is not included.
- Fairness/compute: Claims of 5x speedup are relative to an arbitrarily chosen number of MCL iterations; absolute runtimes, FLOPs, and GPU memory are not reported. sMCL requires M forward/backward passes per batch; in captioning, models share a single CNN due to memory, which changes the hypothesis class and may dampen diversity—this is not controlled for against fully independent ensembles.
- “Loss-agnostic” claim: For segmentation and captioning, training uses cross-entropy/likelihood surrogates, not the non-differentiable oracle metrics (IoU, CIDEr). The claim should be qualified.
- Hyperparameters and reproducibility: Key details are missing (batch sizes and schedulers for segmentation/captioning; beam search settings beyond width; tie-breaking policy; initialization; data preprocessing; seeds). No code commitment.
- Scope of benchmarks: CIFAR-10 uses a very small “Quick” network; no ImageNet or modern architectures (even though ResNet is cited). It is unclear if gains persist at larger scales.
- Methodological gaps: No ablation on ensemble size M, batch size, tie-breaking, soft vs hard assignment, or the proposed k-best relaxation beyond a single plot; no analysis of assignment balance; no downstream selection module to show end-to-end task gains beyond oracle.
- Writing/formatting: Numerous reference errors and incomplete citations; figures/tables are referenced but not fully presented here; minor typos (“Intepretable”), inconsistencies in notation (LO vs Eq. 1 objective), and a malformed constraint in Eq. 1 (summation index missing).

Quality
Technically plausible but under-theorized. The empirical section shows encouraging trends but lacks rigor (standardized splits/metrics, error bars, strong baselines like learned gating). Some claims (parameter-free, loss-agnostic) are overstated.

Clarity
High-level motivation and algorithmic description are clear, but many experimental details are omitted. The related work underplays the MoE connection. Presentation suffers from numerous citation/formatting issues.

Originality
The idea of interleaving hard assignment with SGD (“winner-take-gradient”) is a straightforward adaptation of MCL/hard-EM to deep networks. Novelty resides more in packaging and demonstrating practicality rather than a fundamentally new objective.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners seeking diverse hypotheses, but current evidence is limited by non-standard evaluation and missing baselines. Impact would be stronger with MoE comparisons and end-to-end improvements beyond oracle metrics.

Questions and suggestions
- Compare against a learned gating mixture-of-experts (soft/hard) with identical capacity and report both oracle and standard metrics.
- Provide convergence/instability analysis; study collapse and assignment balance; consider a soft assignment with temperature and annealing; report ablations.
- Use standard splits and report absolute metrics (with error bars) and multiple seeds. For VOC, evaluate on the standard val/test; include per-class IoU.
- For captioning, include SPICE/BLEU/METEOR and human judgments of diversity; compare to diverse beam search; control model capacity (independent CNNs vs shared).
- Report wall-clock time, FLOPs, memory, and scaling with M; clarify tie-breaking; release code.
- Fix references and typos; clarify Eq. 1 and Algorithm details.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea; practical to implement.
- Clear qualitative specialization and diversity.
- Concurrent training is attractive; likely to interest the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baselines (MoE) and rigorous evaluation; reliance on non-standard oracle-only metrics.
- Limited theoretical grounding; potential training pathologies unaddressed.
- Incomplete experimental details; non-standard splits; no statistical robustness.
- Overstated claims and formatting/citation issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While promising, the paper in its current form lacks theoretical analysis, key baselines, standardized and rigorous evaluation, and careful presentation. Addressing the above could make it a strong submission.

Confidence
High.
