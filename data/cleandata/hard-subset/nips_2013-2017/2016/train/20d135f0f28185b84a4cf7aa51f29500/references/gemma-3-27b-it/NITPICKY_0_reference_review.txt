This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse ensembles of deep networks by directly minimizing oracle loss. The authors frame this as a generalization of Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) and address a key limitation of prior MCL approaches – the computational expense of retraining models. They present a stochastic block gradient descent optimization strategy that enables concurrent training of ensemble members. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showcasing improved oracle performance and emergent specialization within the ensembles.

While the core idea of sMCL is appealing and addresses a practical bottleneck in MCL, the paper suffers from a surprising number of issues, ranging from minor inconsistencies to more significant shortcomings in experimental detail and clarity. The writing, while generally understandable, is often verbose and repetitive.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a practical problem:** The computational cost of retraining in previous MCL formulations is a significant barrier to adoption. sMCL offers a compelling solution by enabling concurrent training.
*   **Demonstrated effectiveness:** Results across diverse tasks and architectures suggest the method is broadly applicable and performs well. The emergent specialization observed is a positive outcome.
*   **Clear motivation:** The introduction effectively articulates the motivation for multiple-prediction learning in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational inconsistencies:** The use of both *Ŷᵢ* and *ŷ 1 i , . . . , ŷ M i* to represent the same thing is confusing.  Consistency in notation is crucial.
*   **Lack of detail in Algorithm 2:** The algorithm description is too high-level. It states that gradients are backpropagated "only to the lowest error predictor," but doesn’t specify *how* ties are broken. The impact of tie-breaking on results is not discussed.  Is it random? Deterministic based on network index? This detail matters.
*   **Missing justification for hyperparameters:** The experiments section provides limited justification for the chosen hyperparameters (batch sizes, learning rates, epochs). A more thorough ablation study or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Unclear comparison to beam search:** In the captioning section, the comparison to beam search is confusing. The authors state beam search achieves "greater oracle," implying it's superior, yet claim sMCL produces “diverse sentences.” A more nuanced discussion is needed – are the benefits orthogonal?  Why not evaluate sMCL *with* beam search?
*   **Limited discussion of failure cases:** The paper focuses heavily on successes. A discussion of scenarios where sMCL fails or underperforms would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Redundancy in experimental setup descriptions:** The description of the models and datasets feels repetitive across the different tasks. This could be consolidated.
*   **Figure 1 is unhelpful:** The figure illustrating the problems with single-prediction learners is overly simplistic and doesn't convey a strong argument. It feels like filler.
*   **Reference formatting inconsistencies:** Some references are formatted correctly, while others (e.g., [28]) lack complete information.
* **Minor Typo:** The caption of Figure 4f mentions "standard ensemble" but it refers to the same figure as 4c which describes an sMCL ensemble.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The core idea is novel and addresses a practical issue in MCL. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Con:** The paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws, including notational inconsistencies, lack of experimental detail, and unclear comparisons. The writing could be more concise and focused. The lack of a robust ablation study and discussion of failure cases is concerning.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, the numerous minor flaws and lack of thoroughness detract from its overall quality. The combination of imprecise writing, incomplete experimental details, and missing analysis raises concerns about the reproducibility and robustness of the results. I believe that these issues, while individually small, collectively represent a substantial weakness.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.

I recommend rejection at this time. The authors should address the issues raised in this review, focusing on improving clarity, providing more detailed experimental results, and addressing the concerns regarding hyperparameters and comparisons to existing methods. A revised version with these improvements would be worthy of reconsideration.
