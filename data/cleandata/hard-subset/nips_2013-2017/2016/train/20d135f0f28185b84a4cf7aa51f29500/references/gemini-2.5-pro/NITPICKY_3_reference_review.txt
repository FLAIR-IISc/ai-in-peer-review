### Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a training algorithm designed to produce diverse ensembles of deep neural networks. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss," where for any given input, the loss is calculated only with respect to the single best-performing member of the ensemble. sMCL implements this within a stochastic gradient descent framework by forwarding each example in a mini-batch through all M ensemble members, but only backpropagating the gradient for that example through the single member that achieved the lowest loss (a "winner-take-gradient" approach). This method is presented as a practical and efficient alternative to prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) techniques that required costly iterative retraining. The authors demonstrate the method's efficacy and generality across three distinct tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on PASCAL VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO. A key result highlighted is the automatic emergence of interpretable specialization among ensemble members, which is driven by the oracle loss optimization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the important problem of generating a diverse set of high-quality predictions, which is crucial for applications involving ambiguity or downstream human-in-the-loop systems. While building on the MCL framework of Guzman-Rivera et al., the proposed stochastic, parallel training algorithm is a novel and critical contribution that makes the concept viable for modern deep learning architectures. The method is simple, elegant, and intuitive.

2.  **Broad Applicability and Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the method's versatility by applying the exact same algorithm to three very different tasks and network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). In all cases, sMCL shows significant improvements in oracle performance over independently trained ensembles and other baselines. The analysis of emergent specialization is particularly insightful and well-demonstrated.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, and the proposed algorithm is explained well, with a clear connection to the objective function it aims to optimize.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, technical ambiguities, and signs of carelessness that collectively undermine its quality and polish.

1.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It is not alphabetized or sorted numerically, and the citation style is inconsistent. For instance, the first citation in the list is [12], followed by [2]. Reference [13] is just a URL. This lack of attention to a fundamental component of a scientific paper is concerning and suggests a rushed submission.
    *   **Template Artifacts:** The footer on the first page contains leftover template text: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." This should have been removed.
    *   **Figure Captions:** Captions are minimal. Figure 3 merely lists model types without context. The caption for Figure 4 is awkwardly split across its sub-figures (a, b, c, f, d, e in that order), hindering readability.

2.  **Methodological and Experimental Nuances:**
    *   **Ambiguous Experimental Details:** Several key details are vague. In Section 4.1, the batch size for CIFAR-10 is "350". Is this the total batch size, with examples distributed among the M models? Or does each model process 350 examples? In Section 4.2, the FCN models for segmentation are initialized from a "standard ensemble trained for 50 epochs". This detail is critical. If the baseline models do not receive the same pre-training, the comparison is not entirely fair, as sMCL benefits from a potentially stronger starting point.
    *   **Image Captioning Comparison:** The comparison against beam search in Section 4.3 is problematic. Beam search is an inference-time decoding strategy for a single model, whereas sMCL is a training strategy for multiple models. The authors themselves note that beam search could be applied to each sMCL member. More importantly, they concede that for M=5, a single model with beam search achieves a *higher* oracle CIDEr-D score. This is a significant result that weakens the paper's claims, yet it is mentioned only briefly. It suggests a potential trade-off between diversity (unique n-grams) and peak oracle performance that is not adequately explored.
    *   **Practical Costs Overlooked:** The paper emphasizes the time efficiency of sMCL over iterative methods like MCL. However, it largely ignores the significant memory cost. Training M large models in parallel requires M times the GPU memory, a constraint so severe that the authors had to implement a "tree-like model structure" for the VGG16 backbone in the captioning experiment. This is a major practical limitation that should be discussed as a primary trade-off of the approach.
    *   **Minor Technical Gaps:** The paper states that ties for the minimum-loss predictor are "broken arbitrarily". While likely a minor point, the exact mechanism (e.g., random, lowest index) can affect training dynamics, and this lack of specificity is uncharacteristic of a top-tier paper. Furthermore, the "k-sMCL" experiment in Section 4.1, while interesting, feels like a loosely-connected afterthought and is ambiguously described ("the lowest k error predictors are penalized").

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.**

While the core contribution of this paper—a practical SGD-based algorithm for Multiple Choice Learning—is valuable and the results are strong, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The sheer number of minor issues, ranging from a deeply flawed reference section to ambiguous experimental details and questionable baseline comparisons, suggests a lack of rigor and care in the paper's preparation. The strengths of the work are evident, but they are overshadowed by these pervasive weaknesses. A paper with a strong central idea deserves a presentation that is equally strong. I would encourage the authors to perform a thorough revision to address these points and resubmit to a future conference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a simple, novel, and effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   Addresses an important problem with wide-ranging applications.
*   Demonstrates impressive results across three challenging and distinct domains.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Extremely poor formatting of the bibliography and other signs of carelessness.
*   Vague experimental details make reproducibility and fair comparison difficult.
*   The comparison to beam search in the image captioning task is weak and potentially misleading.
*   The significant memory cost of the parallel approach is downplayed.
*   Several minor technical details (e.g., tie-breaking) are left unspecified.
