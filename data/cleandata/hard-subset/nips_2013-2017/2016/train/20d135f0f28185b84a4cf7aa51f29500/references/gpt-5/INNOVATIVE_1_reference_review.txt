Summary
This paper tackles the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) setting, where a system outputs M predictions and a downstream “oracle” selects the best. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple, parameter-free SGD-compatible training scheme for deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss: for each training example, only the minimum-loss predictor (“winner”) receives gradient signal (“winner-take-gradient”). This interleaves assignment and learning at minibatch time, avoiding the costly alternating retraining of prior MCL methods. The method is task-agnostic and applies to standard deep architectures.

The paper situates sMCL against: (i) prior MCL work that required repeated retraining or sequential boosting-like procedures; (ii) ensemble learning methods that induce diversity (bagging, artificial data, negative correlation); and (iii) approaches that extract multiple diverse solutions from a single structured model (M-Best/M-Best-diverse), which are not readily applicable to general deep nets. Empirically, sMCL is demonstrated on CIFAR-10 (classification), Pascal VOC (semantic segmentation with FCNs), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM), showing consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, Dey et al. (boosting-like), and MCL (with large speedups), and revealing interpretable specialization across ensemble members.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and broadly applicable idea: “winner-take-gradient” training to optimize oracle loss in deep ensembles. This makes MCL practical for modern deep nets.
- Novelty and direction: While reminiscent of hard-EM/mixture-of-experts ideas, the explicit focus on oracle loss without a learned gating network and the concurrent, SGD-based training for arbitrary deep architectures is a meaningful step that opens a practical path for multi-hypothesis learning.
- Strong empirical coverage across modalities (classification, dense prediction, captioning), with consistent gains in oracle metrics and compelling qualitative analyses of emergent expertise/diversity.
- Implementation practicality: no extra hyperparameters beyond ensemble size, loss-agnostic, parallelizable, and faster than prior MCL. The k-winners ablation nicely connects specialization/diversity to oracle performance.
- The work addresses a real need in downstream systems that require a set of plausible hypotheses rather than a single point estimate.

Weaknesses and questions
- Relation to mixture-of-experts (MoE): The proposed procedure is close to hard assignment in MoE/mixture models but without a gating network. Please clarify distinctions and trade-offs: when would learned gating (or soft assignment) be preferable, and can your approach benefit from combining with a gating head at test time?
- Evaluation emphasizes oracle metrics. For completeness, please report single-prediction performance (e.g., top-1 accuracy, standard IoU, single best caption) to characterize trade-offs; optimizing oracle loss may harm single-output quality or calibration.
- Limited analysis of stability and collapse: Are results robust to random seeds? Does one model sometimes dominate and starve others? How often do ties occur and how are they broken? Any mitigation (e.g., stochastic tie-breaking, temperature, or entropy regularization)?
- Captioning baselines: A comparison to stronger multi-output inference baselines (e.g., diverse beam search) and/or diversity-promoting decoding would situate the gains better. Likewise, broader structured baselines (e.g., DPP-based diversity for n-best lists) could strengthen claims in that domain.
- The “parameter-free” claim is mostly true, but ensemble size M is a key knob and the k-winners variant suggests further trade-offs; an ablation on M, batch size, and the effect of k on both diversity and oracle performance would be valuable.
- Theoretical perspective is minimal. While not necessary for acceptance, even a short convergence/consistency discussion or conditions under which the stochastic block updates approximate the alternating MCL objective would add rigor.

Quality
Technical idea is sound and well-motivated. The gradient derivation is straightforward and correct. Experiments are broad and reasonably thorough for oracle metrics, with sensible baselines and clear qualitative analyses of specialization/diversity. Some baselines could be stronger in captioning, and additional diagnostics (stability, collapse, single-output performance) would improve completeness.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well-organized; the motivation for oracle loss is compelling. The algorithmic description is easy to follow. Some references appear truncated/garbled in the provided text; please ensure citation formatting is correct. Including pseudocode and a short complexity discussion would help practitioners.

Originality
The main contribution is a practical, SGD-based instantiation of MCL for deep networks with a simple “winner-take-gradient” rule. While related in spirit to hard-EM/MoE and earlier MCL, the paper’s framing around oracle loss for deep ensembles, its task-agnostic training recipe, and empirical breadth constitute a novel and useful advance.

Significance
High. Many real systems need diverse hypothesis sets. This work offers a simple, general recipe that meaningfully improves oracle metrics and induces interpretable specialization across tasks. I expect practitioners to adopt this approach and researchers to build on it (e.g., combining with learned gating, soft assignments, or diversity-aware decoding).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, broadly applicable, and practical training method for multi-hypothesis deep ensembles.
- Consistent empirical gains and qualitative insights across varied domains.
- Advances the field’s ability to train for oracle objectives efficiently.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on oracle-only evaluation; limited analysis of single-output performance, calibration, and training stability.
- Captioning baselines could be stronger; theory is light.

Suggestions
- Report single-output metrics and calibration for at least one task.
- Add robustness results (variance over seeds; analysis of assignment distribution over epochs).
- Compare with a stronger diverse decoding baseline in captioning.
- Discuss integration with gating networks or soft k-winner variants, and whether that helps avoid starvation.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a simple and impactful idea that makes multi-hypothesis training with deep networks practical, and the empirical evidence across tasks is compelling. The novelty and potential to influence practice outweigh the noted limitations.

Confidence
High.
