This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by adapting the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework to stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is to assign gradient updates only to the ensemble member achieving the lowest loss for each example, effectively encouraging specialization. The authors demonstrate the approach on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over independent ensembles and a comparison to the original MCL algorithm, highlighting sMCL’s efficiency.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for MCL, particularly addressing ambiguities and mode-seeking behavior in single-prediction models, is well-articulated. The technical contribution, adapting MCL to SGD, is presented logically and the mathematical formulation is concise. The experimental setup seems comprehensive, including comparisons to relevant baselines across different architectures and tasks. The inclusion of qualitative examples, especially for segmentation and captioning, strengthens the analysis.

However, while the idea is sound and the experimental results are positive, I harbor significant skepticism regarding the true novelty and impact of this work. The core concept – selectively updating network weights based on per-example performance – feels intuitively plausible and not particularly groundbreaking. Existing ensemble methods already implicitly encourage diversity through techniques like bagging or dropout. While sMCL provides a more direct mechanism for fostering specialization, its advantage over these simpler approaches isn't convincingly demonstrated. 

Furthermore, the claim of being "the first practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles" feels overstated. The original MCL, while computationally expensive, wasn't fundamentally impractical. The authors acknowledge this, but frame sMCL’s speedup as a breakthrough. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging the trade-offs between efficiency and potentially more thorough optimization (as in the original MCL) would be valuable.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive, lacks a critical comparison to more recent ensemble diversity techniques. For example, comparing against methods employing adversarial training or knowledge distillation to promote diversity would provide a stronger benchmark. The ablation studies, though present, could be more detailed—e.g., analyzing the impact of different batch sizes or learning rate schedules on specialization. The authors mention specialization emerging "automatically," but a more quantitative analysis of this specialization (e.g., measuring the correlation between ensemble member predictions) would be beneficial. 

The discussion of "interpretable expertise" is somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors present examples of specialization, the analysis remains largely qualitative. A more rigorous assessment—perhaps using techniques from explainable AI—would lend more credibility to this claim. Also, the observation that specialization is less evident in segmentation seems to contradict the claim of general applicability. Why is this the case, and what does it suggest about the limitations of sMCL?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a technically sound method for training diverse ensembles.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements across a range of tasks and architectures.
*   Clearly articulates the motivation for MCL and the limitations of single-prediction models.
*   The algorithm is relatively simple to implement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea lacks significant novelty.
*   The claim of being "the first practical algorithm" is overstated.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks comparison to more recent diversity techniques.
*   The analysis of ensemble specialization is primarily qualitative.
*   The significance of the improvement isn't fully justified, especially considering the added complexity.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While technically solid and clearly presented, it lacks the groundbreaking originality and impact typically expected of a NIPS publication. The improvements over existing methods, while demonstrable, aren't substantial enough to warrant strong endorsement. The authors need to more convincingly demonstrate the advantages of sMCL over simpler ensemble diversity techniques and provide a more rigorous analysis of the emergent specialization observed in their experiments. I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing these concerns.
