Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles multiple-choice learning (MCL) for deep ensembles, where the objective is to minimize the oracle loss over M predictions (i.e., the loss of the best prediction per input). Prior MCL methods alternated between assigning examples to models and retraining models to convergence, which is impractical for modern deep nets. The authors propose Stochastic MCL (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme: for each training example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives the gradient. This can be seen as a stochastic, online version of hard-EM/K-means for the MCL upper-bound objective and is closely related to hard mixture-of-experts without a learned gating network (the “gating” is induced by per-example losses). The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parallelizes across ensemble members, and is parameter-free.

Experiments span three modalities/architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM). Using oracle metrics (top-k, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr), sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles, a prior MCL implementation (with fewer meta-iterations due to cost), and a submodular/boosting-style sequential method. Qualitative analyses show emergent and interpretable specialization (class-wise for classification, structural/shape for segmentation, and n-gram/sentence-structure diversity for captioning). A top-k generalization (routing gradient to k lowest-loss models) demonstrates a trade-off between specialization and oracle performance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation from the MCL upper bound to the per-example gradient routing is correct at the level of partial derivatives with fixed assignment. The method is simple and well-motivated. However, there is no convergence or monotonicity guarantee because assignments depend non-differentiably on parameters and change during training; the approach is thus heuristic but reasonable, akin to online hard-EM/MoE.
- Empirical support: Results across three tasks consistently show notable gains in oracle metrics and faster optimization of oracle loss. Analyses of emergent expertise strengthen the claims about diversity. The top-k ablation is informative.
- Baseline fairness: The MCL baseline is limited to five meta-iterations due to cost; while this reflects real-world feasibility, it weakens the conclusion that sMCL is strictly better than MCL in accuracy (though the speed advantage is clear). For captioning, a single-model beam search can outperform ensembles in oracle CIDEr at M=5, though with lower diversity; this nuance is reported.
- Missing analyses: No study of non-oracle performance (e.g., how well a simple reranker, learned selector, or uncertainty heuristic can pick the right candidate) is provided, which is important for end-to-end systems. Stability and load balancing (e.g., one expert dominating) are not deeply analyzed. Sensitivity to batch size, tie-breaking, M, and data imbalance is not reported.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow. The core algorithm is concise; pseudocode and the “winner-take-gradient” description make it implementable.
- Figures illustrating specialization are compelling. Some details could be clarified: tie-breaking policy and its impact, computational overhead versus independent ensembles, and explicit training-time comparisons beyond the 5x factor against MCL.

Originality
- The contribution is incremental but meaningful: it brings MCL to deep networks via an SGD-compatible training rule, avoiding costly retraining. Conceptually it is close to hard mixture-of-experts/conditional computation with implicit gating by loss, and to online hard-EM; acknowledging classic MoE (Jordan & Jacobs, 1994) would strengthen positioning. Compared to prior M-best/diverse inference for structured models and sequential/diversity-enforcing ensemble methods, the key novelty is practicality and breadth across deep architectures.

Significance
- The approach is simple, general, and likely to be adopted in practice when oracle metrics matter (e.g., perception modules feeding downstream selectors). The emergent specialization is interesting scientifically. Empirical gains on diverse tasks suggest impact. Lack of end-to-end selection performance limits immediate applicability claims, but the method’s utility as a training primitive is clear.

Pros
- Very simple, parameter-free, and broadly applicable training scheme.
- Consistent improvements on oracle metrics across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- 5x speed advantage over alternating MCL while training all experts in parallel.
- Strong qualitative analyses of emergent specialization; informative top-k ablation.
- Clear writing and straightforward reproducibility.

Cons
- No theoretical convergence guarantees; heuristic nature not deeply analyzed.
- Primary focus on oracle metrics; no experiments with a practical selector/reranker.
- MCL baseline potentially undertrained due to cost; comparisons framed partly by feasibility.
- Limited sensitivity analyses (batch size, tie-breaking, M, load balancing).
- Missing explicit connections to classic mixture-of-experts literature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report selection performance using simple selectors (e.g., confidence, learned gating on features) to bridge oracle to end-to-end accuracy.
- Analyze stability and load balancing; consider mild regularizers to avoid expert collapse.
- Provide sensitivity studies (M, batch size, shared vs. independent feature backbones).
- Expand related work to include classic MoE and recent conditional computation.
- Release code to aid adoption.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, practical contribution that enables MCL for deep networks with a simple and effective training rule. Despite limited theory and a focus on oracle metrics, the breadth of experiments, speedup over prior MCL, and emergent specialization make it a valuable addition. I lean toward acceptance.

Recommendation: Accept.
