Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) setting where a learner outputs M hypotheses and performance is measured by an oracle loss that selects the best hypothesis per example. Prior MCL methods (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al.) alternate between assigning examples to experts and retraining models to convergence, which is impractical for modern deep nets. The authors propose Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” variant that interleaves assignment with SGD: for each example in a minibatch, only the model with the lowest loss receives gradient updates. This yields a parameter-free, architecture- and loss-agnostic training procedure for deep ensembles that can be trained once, jointly. Experiments span CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM), consistently improving oracle metrics over independent ensembles, the boosting-style method of Dey et al., and (within feasible meta-iterations) MCL, while being ~5× faster than the latter. A notable qualitative finding is emergent specialization: different ensemble members become class or structure specialists.

This work is well-situated within diverse-M best and ensemble literature: M-best diverse solutions in structured models (e.g., MRFs), MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.), diversity-inducing ensembles (bagging/negative correlation), and expert architectures (mixtures of experts, generalist/specialist networks). The contribution is to adapt the MCL objective to deep nets via a practical SGD-compatible algorithm and to demonstrate broad applicability.

Strengths
- Novelty and practicality: A simple, elegant algorithm that makes oracle-loss training feasible for deep ensembles. No extra hyperparameters or complex scheduling.
- Breadth of applicability: Works across disparate tasks/architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN) and losses; consistent gains in oracle metrics and training speed.
- Emergent specialization: Clear evidence of interpretable expertise in classification and meaningful diversity in segmentation and captioning.
- Clarity of core idea: The “winner-take-gradient” gradient derivation and the connection to hard-EM/K-means are intuitive and convincing.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory and stability: There is little analysis of convergence or stability of the stochastic block gradient procedure on a non-smooth min objective. Early winner-locking or data starvation could cause collapse. A discussion or empirical safeguards (e.g., warm-up, epsilon-greedy, temperature/soft assignments) would strengthen the work.
- Sensitivity/ablations: Limited analysis of sensitivity to M, batch size, tie-breaking, initialization, and the k-of-M generalization (beyond a single figure on CIFAR-10). More thorough ablations would help practitioners.
- Comparisons: Missing comparisons to classic mixture-of-experts with learned gating (and to deep ensembles with shared backbones), MC-Dropout-type sampling for multi-hypothesis coverage, and stronger contemporary deep baselines (e.g., deeper CNNs/ResNets for CIFAR/ImageNet top-k, if feasible).
- Diversity metrics: Aside from oracle metrics and n-gram counts, more principled diversity measures (e.g., pairwise KL/disagreement, marginal coverage on minority classes) would contextualize the gains and trade-offs.
- Compute reporting: Speedups over MCL are noted, but absolute wall-clock/GPU-hours and memory usage for all methods would help assess practicality at scale.
- Captioning: The beam search comparison favors single-model beam width 5 on oracle but with lower diversity. It would be informative to report ensemble beam search (beam per member and merged candidates), and human judgments on fluency/diversity.

Quality
Technically sound and well-executed empirically. The algorithm is simple and correctly derived from the block-coordinate view of the oracle objective. Experiments are convincing across three modalities, with clear improvements in oracle metrics and insightful qualitative analyses. The main gaps are theoretical guarantees/diagnostics and breadth of ablation/comparisons as noted above.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The problem setting, objective, and algorithm are easy to follow. To improve reproducibility: spell out tie-breaking, any warm-up or learning-rate schedules specific to sMCL, exact training times, and handling of shared CNNs in captioning. A compact pseudo-code block (Algorithm 2) and details on the k-of-M variant would be helpful.

Originality
Moderately high. While related to prior MCL/hard-EM ideas, the “winner-take-gradient” adaptation for deep SGD training is a fresh and practically impactful contribution. The demonstration of emergent expertise across tasks is compelling.

Significance
High practical significance: a drop-in training strategy for producing diverse, high-coverage ensembles under oracle evaluation, relevant to applications with downstream selection/reranking. Likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to soft assignment, load-balancing, or curriculum variants). The idea could influence multi-hypothesis training beyond vision (e.g., ASR, MT).

Suggestions for improvement
- Analyze/mitigate early collapse: report assignment entropy over epochs; try softmin/temperature or epsilon-greedy selection; show effect on specialization and oracle score.
- Add diversity diagnostics: disagreement rates, pairwise KL over logits, calibration of individual members vs oracle.
- Expand comparisons: mixture-of-experts with learned gating; MC-Dropout multi-sample baselines; deeper backbones where feasible; ensemble beam search in captioning.
- Report compute: absolute training time, memory, and scaling with M; include per-epoch reassignment frequency comparison to MCL.
- Explore scalability: results with larger M (e.g., up to 10) and on a larger classification dataset (e.g., ImageNet top-5) if computationally feasible.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, broadly applicable, and effective algorithm addressing a real practical gap.
- Strong empirical gains on diverse tasks with interpretable behavior.
- Parameter-free and easy to integrate into existing deep pipelines.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical grounding and diagnostic analysis of training dynamics.
- Some missing baselines/ablations and compute reporting.
- Oracle metrics emphasize coverage; end-to-end impact with downstream selectors could be discussed.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a simple, novel, and useful training paradigm for diverse deep ensembles with clear empirical benefits and broad applicability. The methodological simplicity and demonstrated impact outweigh the relatively minor shortcomings in theory and ablation depth.

Score: 8/10, Confidence: 4/5
