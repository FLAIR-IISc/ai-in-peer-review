This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse ensembles of deep networks by directly minimizing the oracle loss. The core idea is a “winner-take-gradient” approach within stochastic gradient descent, where gradients are only backpropagated to the ensemble member achieving the lowest loss on each example. The authors position this work within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, addressing limitations of previous approaches requiring costly retraining or sequential training. They demonstrate empirical results on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing improved oracle performance compared to standard ensembles and other baselines.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation and technical details of sMCL. The connection to MCL is clearly established, and the authors accurately highlight the practical challenges of prior work, particularly the scalability issues related to retraining ensemble members. The experimental setup is reasonably thorough, utilizing established datasets and architectures. The reported improvements over baselines are noteworthy, particularly the 5x speedup compared to the original MCL algorithm.

However, despite the promising results, I remain skeptical. While the empirical performance gains are encouraging, the paper lacks a sufficiently critical analysis of *why* sMCL works. The authors attribute the success to ensemble member specialization, but the analysis presented (e.g., the class-wise distribution of oracle assignments) is somewhat superficial. A more in-depth investigation into the learned representations and decision boundaries of individual ensemble members would be valuable.

My primary concern is the lack of extensive comparison to stronger, more recent ensemble diversity techniques. The baselines—independent ensembles and the original MCL—feel somewhat dated. While the authors mention limitations in applying other methods, a more rigorous justification for omitting more modern approaches (e.g., those utilizing adversarial training or information-maximizing techniques for diversity) is needed. The Dey et al. [5] baseline is included, but its rapid saturation suggests it isn't a particularly competitive comparator.

Furthermore, the paper feels somewhat descriptive rather than analytically rigorous. The theoretical justification for sMCL is limited to stating that the gradient calculation is equivalent to training a single model when its predictor is the minimum error predictor. A more thorough theoretical analysis of the optimization landscape and convergence properties of sMCL would strengthen the contribution. The claim of being "parameter-free" is also somewhat misleading, as standard SGD parameters (learning rate, momentum, etc.) still require tuning.

The image captioning results, while positive, are presented with less detail than the other tasks. The comparison to beam search is not entirely fair, as beam search is an inference technique that can be applied *to* sMCL ensembles. A more direct comparison of ensemble diversity metrics (e.g., distinct n-grams generated *without* beam search) would be more informative.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of SGD to directly minimize oracle loss in deep ensembles.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance over relevant baselines across diverse tasks.
*   Addresses a practical limitation of previous MCL approaches (costly retraining).
*   Clear and well-written exposition of the method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis and justification.
*   Insufficiently rigorous comparison to modern ensemble diversity techniques.
*   Superficial analysis of ensemble member specialization.
*   The claim of being “parameter-free” is debatable.
*   The method’s advantage isn’t convincingly demonstrated beyond empirical observation – what makes it work?



**Overall:** I believe this paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, but its current form falls short of NIPS standards. The empirical results are promising, but the lack of theoretical depth and comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the aforementioned weaknesses through further analysis and experimentation. The authors should focus on a more thorough theoretical understanding of sMCL and a more rigorous evaluation against stronger baselines to solidify its contribution to the field.
