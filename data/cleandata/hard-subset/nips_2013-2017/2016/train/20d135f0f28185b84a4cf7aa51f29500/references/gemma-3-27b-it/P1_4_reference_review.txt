## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and efficient training algorithm for diverse deep ensembles. The core idea is to directly minimize the oracle loss—the loss of the best prediction from an ensemble—using a stochastic gradient descent approach. Unlike previous methods that require costly retraining or sequential training, sMCL allows for concurrent training of all ensemble members. The authors demonstrate the applicability of sMCL to various tasks (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning) and network architectures, showcasing improved performance and the emergence of interpretable specialization within the ensemble.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [Guzman-Rivera et al., 2015, 2016], which aims to learn models that produce sets of good predictions.  However, it addresses a key limitation of prior MCL methods: their computational cost, especially when applied to deep networks.  The authors also position their work within the broader context of ensemble learning (e.g., resampling, decorrelation) and methods for generating multiple solutions from a single model.  They clearly differentiate sMCL from these approaches by emphasizing its efficiency, generality, and ability to discover specialization without explicit regularization or sequential training.  The connection to related work on submodular optimization [Dey et al., 2017] is also appropriately highlighted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  sMCL provides a genuinely novel and practical approach to training diverse deep ensembles, bridging the gap between theoretical MCL and scalable implementation.
* **Efficiency:** The concurrent training aspect significantly reduces training time compared to existing methods like alternating block coordinate descent (MCL). The 5x speedup over MCL is impressive, particularly for deep networks.
* **Generality:** The method is agnostic to the network architecture and loss function, making it highly adaptable.  The successful application to CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNNs demonstrates this flexibility.
* **Empirical Results:**  The experimental results across three diverse tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) are strong and consistently show improvements over baseline ensembles and other state-of-the-art methods. The use of oracle metrics is appropriate for evaluating multi-solution systems.
* **Interpretability:**  The analysis of ensemble member specialization is a significant strength.  The observation that members develop expertise in different classes or segmentation patterns provides valuable insights into the learning process.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the explanation of sMCL is relatively straightforward. The mathematical formulation is clear, and Algorithm 2 provides a concise description of the training procedure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions some hyperparameters (e.g., batch size, learning rate), there's limited discussion on their sensitivity or how they were chosen. Providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter settings would strengthen the reproducibility and generalizability of the findings.
* **Comparative Depth:** While comparisons to baselines are present, the depth of comparison could be improved. For example, a more detailed analysis of the performance of the Dey et al. [2017] method, including a discussion of why it saturates quickly, would be helpful.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper focuses primarily on the practical implementation and empirical results. A more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of sMCL, or a deeper understanding of why the ‘winner-take-gradient’ approach works so effectively, could enhance the paper's impact.
* **Oracle Metrics:**  While appropriate, relying solely on oracle metrics abstracts away the downstream process that would ultimately select from the ensemble's outputs. A brief discussion of how performance might translate to real-world applications with varying oracle capabilities (e.g., imperfect selection mechanisms) would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the superiority of sMCL over existing methods.
* The method is generally applicable and easy to implement.
* The interpretability analysis provides valuable insights into the learning process.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning (learning to produce diverse and robust predictions).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks a thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* The theoretical justification is limited.
* Comparative analysis could be more in-depth.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The proposed sMCL algorithm is both novel and practical, and the experimental results are compelling.  The weaknesses mentioned above are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the paper's overall quality. I believe that this paper is well-suited for acceptance to NIPS.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10
