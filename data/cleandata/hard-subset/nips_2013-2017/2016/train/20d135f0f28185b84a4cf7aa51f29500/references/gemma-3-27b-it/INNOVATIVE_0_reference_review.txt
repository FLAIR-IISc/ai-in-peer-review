This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by directly optimizing for oracle loss. The core idea – a stochastic gradient descent variant where gradients are routed only to the best-performing ensemble member for each example – is elegant and surprisingly simple. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of diverse ensembles in settings with ambiguity or inherent label noise, and successfully demonstrate sMCL's performance across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks. The connection to Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) is clearly articulated, and the authors effectively position sMCL as a practical solution to the scalability issues plaguing previous MCL approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The sMCL algorithm is a genuinely novel contribution. While the concept of optimizing for oracle loss isn't new, the proposed stochastic gradient descent implementation is a clever and practical workaround to the challenges of training diverse ensembles, particularly with deep networks.
* **Practicality:** The authors highlight, and demonstrate, that sMCL is computationally efficient and readily applicable to existing deep learning architectures without requiring significant modifications. This is a major advantage over previous MCL methods.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and showcase consistent improvements over strong baselines across diverse tasks. The visualizations, particularly those illustrating emergent specialization in the classification task, are compelling.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The notation is standard and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.
* **Significance:** The concept of directly optimizing for oracle loss addresses a core challenge in real-world applications where generating a *set* of plausible solutions is more valuable than a single best guess.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors provide a derivation of the gradient, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of sMCL would be beneficial. Is there a guarantee of convergence, and under what conditions?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively explore the sensitivity of sMCL to hyperparameters beyond those common to standard SGD. A more thorough analysis could reveal potential limitations or best practices.
* **Limited Comparison to Modern Diversity Techniques:** The baselines are reasonable, but the landscape of ensemble diversity methods is rapidly evolving. A comparison to more recent techniques like variational ensembles or adversarial training for diversity could further strengthen the claims.
* **Interpretability beyond Classification:** While the specialization observed in the classification task is well-documented, the interpretability of specialization in the segmentation and captioning tasks is less clear, despite the qualitative examples. More quantitative metrics to assess the nature of diversity would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and practical algorithm for training diverse deep ensembles.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements across a variety of tasks.
* The algorithm is conceptually simple and easily implemented, making it likely to be adopted by the research community.
* The paper addresses a significant challenge in real-world machine learning applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly explored.
* The comparison to more recent diversity techniques is lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling contribution. The novelty of sMCL, coupled with its practicality and strong empirical results, outweighs the minor weaknesses. While a more thorough theoretical analysis and hyperparameter study would be valuable, the current presentation is sufficient to demonstrate the significance of this work. I believe this paper has the potential to influence future research in ensemble learning and structured prediction. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.
