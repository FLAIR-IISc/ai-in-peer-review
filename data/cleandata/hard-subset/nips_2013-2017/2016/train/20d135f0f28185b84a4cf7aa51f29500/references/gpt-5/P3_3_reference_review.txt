Summary
This paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) in deep networks, aiming to minimize oracle loss over an ensemble g(x) = {f1(x), …, fM(x)} by training members to specialize on different parts of the data distribution. The key contribution is Stochastic MCL (sMCL): an SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme where, for each example in a minibatch, only the model with the smallest loss receives gradient. This realizes a stochastic block coordinate descent on the standard MCL upper bound without costly retraining and allows concurrent training of all ensemble members. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and is demonstrated on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM), consistently improving oracle metrics over independent ensembles, the alternating retraining MCL baseline, and the sequential method of Dey et al. Qualitative analyses show emergent specialization (class clusters in classification; class/shape/frequency biases in segmentation; n-gram/style diversity in captioning).

Relation to prior work
- MCL: Extends Guzman-Rivera et al. by replacing alternating re-train/assign loops with a per-batch hard assignment, removing retraining costs.
- Diversity in ensembles: Related to negative correlation learning and data resampling, but here diversity emerges from optimizing the oracle objective rather than explicit decorrelation penalties.
- Multiple diverse outputs: Distinct from M-best/diverse inference for structured models; broader applicability to deep nets.
- Mixture-of-Experts and winner-take-all training: Methodologically close to hard mixture-of-experts (Jacobs, Jordan & Hinton, early 90s) with implicit, loss-based gating. The paper should cite and contrast with these classics; the absence is a gap.

Quality
Strengths:
- Simple, elegant training rule grounded in the gradient of the MCL upper-bound objective.
- Applicable across tasks and architectures; implementation is minimal (a routing of per-example gradients).
- Empirical results across three domains show consistent gains in oracle metrics and compelling qualitative diversity; speed advantage vs MCL is practically important.
- Insightful analyses of emergent specialization and a k-winner variant that elucidates diversity–performance tradeoffs.

Weaknesses/limitations:
- Theory: No convergence or stability analysis beyond intuition. Hard assignment introduces non-smoothness; starvation of some experts is possible. A discussion or empirical check on “load balancing” over epochs would strengthen the paper.
- Gating vs loss mismatch: The “winner” is chosen by training loss (e.g., cross-entropy), whereas oracle metrics may be IoU or CIDEr. This mismatch could yield suboptimal assignments; ablations where the winner is chosen by a proxy aligned to the oracle metric would be informative.
- Fairness of baselines: MCL is capped at five meta-iterations “due to cost,” which may disadvantage it. A wall-clock-matched comparison or learning curves would be fairer. For captioning, a single-model beam search can outperform in oracle CIDEr; applying beam search to each sMCL member (or diverse decoding) is a natural stronger baseline not fully explored.
- Impact on single-prediction performance: Since sMCL optimizes oracle loss, top-1 (or standard single-output) performance may degrade; results are not reported.
- Shared backbones: In captioning, gradients from multiple LSTMs are “aggregated” into a shared CNN for memory reasons; this partially breaks the winner-take-gradient premise and may reduce diversity. Details on how gradients are combined and an ablation with separate CNNs (smaller backbones) would clarify.
- Hyperparameters and stability: Tie-breaking, batch size sensitivity, and safeguards against expert collapse are under-specified. The k-winner extension is interesting; a soft-assignment variant (e.g., softmin over losses with temperature) could mitigate instability.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized with clear problem setup and motivation. Algorithm 2 (sMCL) should be explicit in the paper (pseudo-code); clarify tie-breaking and how backprop is handled with shared components.
- Provide more training details for segmentation and captioning (optimizer settings, data augmentation, early stopping) and report variance over multiple seeds.
- State compute budgets and wall-clock times for all methods to contextualize the 5x speedup claim.

Originality
- The core idea is a practical and effective instantiation of MCL for deep nets via an SGD-friendly hard assignment. While the training dynamic is closely related to classic hard mixture-of-experts/winner-take-all, applying loss-based per-example assignment without an explicit gating network, across diverse deep architectures, is a useful and timely contribution in 2016. The lack of citation to MoE work should be remedied.

Significance
- The method is simple, broadly applicable, and yields clear oracle improvements and interpretable specializations. This is likely to be used by practitioners who need multi-hypothesis outputs and by researchers studying emergent specialization. The contribution is more pragmatic than theoretical, but impactful.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Report single-output performance (top-1, mean IoU, CIDEr of best single member) and ensemble-averaged performance to quantify tradeoffs vs oracle.
- Provide wall-clock-matched comparisons to MCL and Dey et al.; include learning curves.
- Analyze expert utilization: distribution of assignments over epochs, per-class/per-attribute load, and mechanisms (if any) to prevent collapse; consider entropy regularization or soft assignments with temperature.
- For captioning, compare to stronger decoding baselines (e.g., larger beam, diverse beam search if contemporaneous), and apply the same to sMCL members.
- Explore aligning the “winner” selection to the evaluation metric (e.g., structured losses or IoU surrogates for segmentation).
- Add discussion contrasting sMCL to hard mixture-of-experts and gating networks; consider a hybrid with a learned gating signal.

Pros
- Simple, general, effective; strong empirical gains in oracle metrics across tasks.
- Concurrent training; substantial practicality over prior MCL/boosting-like methods.
- Nice qualitative and quantitative analyses of diversity and specialization.

Cons
- Limited theoretical treatment; potential instability/starvation not deeply analyzed.
- Some baseline comparisons and reporting choices could be stronger/fairer.
- Missing discussion of relation to classic mixture-of-experts and decoding baselines.

Recommendation: Accept (weak to clear accept). This is a solid, practical contribution with broad applicability and convincing empirical evidence, despite limited theory and some evaluation gaps.

Confidence: High.
