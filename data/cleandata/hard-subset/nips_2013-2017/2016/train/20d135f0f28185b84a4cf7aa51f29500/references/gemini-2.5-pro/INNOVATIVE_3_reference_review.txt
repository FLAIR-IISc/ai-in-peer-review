This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and practical algorithm for training ensembles of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality outputs. The core idea is to directly optimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member of the ensemble for any given data point. Building on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which is computationally infeasible for deep networks due to its reliance on full retraining cycles, this work proposes a simple and elegant stochastic optimization strategy. During training, for each example in a mini-batch, the gradient of the loss is backpropagated only to the single "winner" model that achieved the lowest error. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently, making the concept of MCL practical for modern deep learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL on three distinct tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing significant improvements over independent ensembles and other baselines, and revealing an intriguing emergent property of member specialization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold Approach:** The primary contribution of this paper is a conceptually simple yet powerful new training paradigm. While the idea of minimizing oracle loss is not new, the formulation of a stochastic, parallel training algorithm to achieve it for deep networks is a significant and novel contribution. It fundamentally rethinks how to train an ensemble not just for accuracy, but for diversity. This is a bold departure from standard ensemble methods that often focus on bagging or boosting and represents a creative solution to a challenging problem. The sMCL algorithm is elegant in its simplicity.

2.  **Significant and Broadly Applicable Contribution:** The problem the paper addresses is of fundamental importance. Many real-world perception problems are inherently ambiguous, and forcing a model to produce a single "best guess" can lead to undesirable mode-seeking behaviors (e.g., generic captions, averaged-out segmentations). Training models to produce a set of plausible hypotheses is a much more powerful and useful approach. The proposed sMCL method is domain-agnostic, parameter-free, and applicable to any architecture trainable with SGD and any task-specific loss function. This generality makes it a highly significant contribution that researchers across many subfields are likely to use and build upon.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Insightful Analysis:** The paper's claims are backed by a strong and diverse set of experiments. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on classification (CNN), segmentation (FCN), and captioning (CNN+RNN) convincingly showcases its broad utility. The results consistently show that sMCL significantly outperforms relevant baselines, including independent ensembles and the prohibitively slow original MCL algorithm. Furthermore, the analysis of emergent specialization is fascinating. The discovery that ensemble members automatically learn to become experts on different subsets of the problem space (e.g., certain object classes, segmentation styles, or n-gram patterns) without any explicit supervision is a key insight and a compelling result of the proposed training scheme.

**Weaknesses:**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few minor points that could be strengthened.

1.  **Limited Theoretical Discussion:** The paper is primarily an empirical contribution. While the motivation for the sMCL algorithm as a stochastic version of block coordinate descent is clear, the paper lacks any theoretical analysis of its convergence properties. While not strictly necessary given the strong empirical results, some discussion on this front would have made the work more complete.

2.  **Minor Ambiguities in Experimental Setup:** In the semantic segmentation experiments, the models are initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble and then fine-tuned. It is unclear if this pre-training step is essential for good performance or simply a practical choice to speed up training. Similarly, in the image captioning experiments, the comparison with beam search is not entirely straightforward, as sMCL is a training procedure while beam search is an inference procedure. A more direct comparison might involve applying beam search to each member of the sMCL ensemble. However, these are minor points and do not detract from the main conclusions.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper presents a novel, elegant, and highly effective method for a significant and practical problem. It moves beyond the standard paradigm of seeking a single best prediction and provides a practical tool for training deep ensembles to capture the multimodality inherent in many real-world tasks. The "winner-take-gradient" idea is a conceptually clean and bold contribution that makes a previously impractical learning framework (MCL) viable for the deep learning era. The strong and broad empirical results, combined with the insightful analysis of emergent specialization, make this a compelling paper. While the theoretical analysis is light, the novelty of the approach and its potential for high impact strongly merit acceptance. This is precisely the type of creative and direction-setting work that should be highlighted at NIPS.
