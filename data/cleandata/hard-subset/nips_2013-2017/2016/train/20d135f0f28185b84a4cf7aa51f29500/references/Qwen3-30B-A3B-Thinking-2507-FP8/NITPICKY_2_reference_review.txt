

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes oracle loss by using a "winner-take-gradient" strategy. The authors claim sMCL is parameter-free, applicable to any deep network trained with SGD, and significantly faster than existing Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches. The method is evaluated on image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a novel training approach for deep ensembles that effectively produces diverse predictions. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines (classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. [5]) across three different tasks. The paper provides convincing evidence of "interpretable expertise" emergence in ensemble members, with clear visualizations showing specialization patterns. The authors make a strong case for the practical benefits of diverse outputs in ambiguous prediction scenarios, which is relevant to real-world AI applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Inconsistencies:** The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free" but later introduces a "k" parameter (in section 4.1) that controls how many lowest-error predictors are penalized. This contradicts the parameter-free claim, and the paper doesn't explain how to set k or justify its selection. The authors state "k = 1 netting highest oracle accuracy" but don't discuss whether this holds across different tasks or dataset sizes.

2. **Lack of Algorithmic Details:** The paper references "Algorithm 2" but fails to include it in the submission. This is a critical omission for reproducibility, as the key innovation (the stochastic gradient update rule) isn't properly described. The paper states "the gradient of the loss for each example is backpropagated only to the lowest error predictor" but doesn't specify how ties are broken or how this differs from other approaches.

3. **Inadequate Speed Claims:** The paper claims sMCL is "5x faster than MCL" based on "5 meta-iterations" but provides no actual timing data. Without concrete training times or comparisons of wall-clock time, this claim lacks empirical support. The paper doesn't explain why 5 meta-iterations are sufficient or how this factor scales with dataset size or network complexity.

4. **Theoretical Gaps:** The paper makes strong claims about sMCL being "applicable to any model trained with stochastic gradient descent" without any theoretical justification. There's no convergence analysis or formal argument showing why this approach would work better than standard ensemble training. The paper states sMCL is "agnoistic to the form of loss function" but doesn't provide examples of non-standard loss functions it would handle.

5. **Experimental Limitations:** The captioning experiments involve "freezing or fine-tuning the CNN" but don't justify why both approaches were necessary or how the fine-tuning procedure differs from standard practice. The paper claims sMCL "significantly outperforms" baselines but provides no statistical significance tests for these results. The paper also fails to address whether the observed improvements are due to the training method or merely due to different hyperparameters.

6. **Formatting and Writing Issues:** The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies, including "th Conference" instead of "The Conference" in the header, inconsistent capitalization, and a typo in the "Oracle Evaluation" section ("oracle versions" should be "oracle versions"). The paper refers to "Figure 1" without describing its content, making it difficult to understand the motivation for the approach.

7. **Related Work Gaps:** The related work section doesn't adequately address Kirillov et al. (2015) which also deals with finding diverse solutions in structured prediction. The paper cites Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9] as the first to formalize MCL but doesn't clearly distinguish their approach from the current work beyond claiming sMCL avoids "costly retraining."

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea with promising experimental results, it contains several critical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution. The lack of algorithmic detail (missing Algorithm 2), contradictory claims about parameters, and unsupported speed claims make the technical contribution difficult to evaluate. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical foundation, clear algorithmic description, and empirical validation of the speed claims.

The experimental results are compelling but lack statistical significance testing and detailed hyperparameter analysis. The paper's claim of "broad applicability" isn't sufficiently supported by the limited range of tasks and architectures tested. The formatting and writing issues further diminish the paper's credibility.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the idea of training diverse ensembles has value, the paper fails to provide a rigorous, reproducible technical contribution. The numerous methodological inconsistencies, unsupported claims, and missing algorithmic details prevent this work from meeting NIPS's standards for a high-quality scientific contribution. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for acceptance.
