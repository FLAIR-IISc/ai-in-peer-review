Summary
This paper revisits Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) for deep models and proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a winner-take-gradient training procedure that directly minimizes the oracle loss over an ensemble. Instead of the alternating assignment/retraining of prior MCL (which is impractical for deep nets), sMCL performs per-mini-batch hard assignment: for each example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free (beyond M), and trains all members concurrently. Experiments on three tasks—CIFAR10 classification, PASCAL VOC segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning with CNN+LSTM—show consistent gains in oracle metrics and interpretable specialization of ensemble members. The paper relates to prior work on MCL and diverse inference (DivMBest and variants), and distinguishes itself by providing a practical SGD-based training scheme for deep ensembles.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with a principled connection to the oracle loss objective; easy to implement in modern frameworks.
- Practical contribution over prior MCL: concurrent training with substantial wall-clock savings; avoids multiple full retrainings.
- Strong, multi-domain empirical evaluation (classification, segmentation, captioning), showing improvements in oracle metrics and qualitative diversity.
- Emergent specialization is well-illustrated and aligns with the intended goal of covering multiple modes.
- Parameter-free in practice (no diversity hyperparameters), broadly applicable to differentiable losses and architectures.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation focuses almost exclusively on oracle metrics. For many applications, utility depends on a downstream selector/reranker; no analysis of how sMCL interacts with such components, or trade-offs with non-oracle accuracy.
- Baselines are limited: independent ensembles and prior MCL (with only 5 meta-iterations), and one boosting-style approach. Missing comparisons to alternative diversity-inducing ensemble methods for deep nets (e.g., negative-correlation learning, disagreement/diversity regularizers, mixture-of-experts with gating).
- Risk of expert collapse/starvation with hard assignment is not studied in depth; no ablations on initialization, tie-breaking, or balancing.
- Captioning: a single-model beam search can match/exceed oracle scores at similar M in some settings; sMCL’s advantage is more in diversity (unique n-grams) than raw oracle. It would be important to report sMCL + beam search to disentangle training vs inference gains.
- Limited scale of classification experiments (CIFAR10-Quick rather than stronger modern backbones like ResNets).

Quality
Technical soundness: The optimization is well-motivated by the indicator-variable upper bound to oracle loss. The gradient routing rule follows directly: non-winners receive zero gradient, turning the method into a hard-EM/mixture-of-experts-style learner with an implicit, non-parametric gating function (argmin loss). This is sensible, but raises stability questions (non-differentiability at assignment boundaries, oscillations, expert starvation). The paper provides empirical evidence of stable training and emergent specialization, but lacks diagnostics (e.g., per-expert load, entropy of assignments, convergence behavior, impact of batch size). The “top-k” variant partially explores the spectrum from oracle loss to standard ensembles, but deeper analysis (e.g., sensitivity to k, soft versions with temperature, or balanced assignment constraints) would strengthen the claims.

Empirical support: Improvements in oracle accuracy/IoU/CIDEr-D are consistent across tasks and ensemble sizes, with qualitative examples that convincingly show mode coverage. However, fairness of baselines could be improved (stronger backbones, more diversity baselines, and a more thorough MCL run if feasible). Claims of a 5x speedup are tied to choosing 5 meta-iterations; wall-clock numbers and hardware details would clarify the practical gain.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The core algorithm (Algorithm 2) is simple and clearly described. Experimental details are mostly adequate, though some specifics could be clarified:
- Exact per-batch assignment protocol and tie-breaking policy.
- Any regularization to prevent collapse (none is claimed; report if not needed).
- Full dataset splits and sizes (the segmentation experiment uses a subset of VOC11 val).
- Wall-clock training times for all methods, and detailed hyperparameters for each task.
- Whether gradients are synchronized when multiple experts tie or if top-k gradients are averaged.

Originality
The contribution is incremental but meaningful: reformulating MCL for SGD via a per-example hard routing rule that is trivial to implement and effective across tasks. Prior MCL/diverse-M-best work did not provide a practical joint training recipe for deep nets; this fills that gap. The connection to classic mixture-of-experts with hard gating is implicit; citing and contrasting to MoE (with learned gate) would contextualize the novelty: sMCL uses the task loss as a non-parametric gate without extra parameters or supervision. The empirical demonstration across different structured outputs adds value.

Significance
High practical significance: practitioners can immediately adopt sMCL to train diverse ensembles with improved oracle performance and interpretable specialization, especially useful where downstream oracles exist (e.g., human-in-the-loop, verification modules, rerankers). Conceptually, it provides a simple baseline for learning multi-modal predictors without changing architecture or loss. The work is likely to be used and extended (e.g., with soft/top-k routing, balancing constraints, or learned gating).

Specific suggestions and questions
- Report load balancing metrics (fraction of samples per expert) over training; analyze collapse/starvation and remedies if observed.
- Include sMCL + beam search for captioning to compare apples-to-apples with single-model beam; report diversity metrics (distinct-n) and oracle jointly.
- Add stronger baselines: negative-correlation ensembles, diversity-promoting regularizers, multi-branch networks, and a mixture-of-experts with a learned gate (even if small-scale).
- Provide wall-clock time and GPU counts for all methods; clarify the “5x” speedup beyond meta-iteration count.
- Discuss limitations: reliance on oracle metrics, potential degradation in non-oracle metrics, and when sMCL may not help (unimodal tasks).

Pros
- Simple, general, and effective algorithm with strong empirical results and clear intuition.
- Concurrent training makes MCL practical for deep nets.
- Emergent expertise and diversity are well-demonstrated qualitatively and quantitatively.

Cons
- Limited baselines and ablations; missing analysis of stability and expert balance.
- Oracle-only evaluation may not reflect end-to-end system utility.
- Classification experiment uses relatively weak backbones.

Overall assessment
A solid, practical contribution that advances learning diverse deep ensembles by making oracle-loss training feasible. Despite some missing comparisons and analyses, the method’s simplicity, generality, and consistent gains across tasks warrant acceptance.
