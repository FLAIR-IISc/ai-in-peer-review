

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach to training diverse deep ensembles by directly optimizing for the oracle loss (minimum loss over multiple predictions). The authors address a significant gap in the literature: previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches [8,9] required costly retraining of models, making them infeasible for deep networks. sMCL solves this by using a "winner-take-gradient" strategy within stochastic gradient descent, where only the model with the lowest loss for each example receives gradient updates. This simple but powerful modification allows concurrent training of ensemble members, is parameter-free, and works with any SGD-trained model.

The paper's core novelty lies in its direct application of MCL to deep networks with a practical, efficient algorithm. Previous ensemble work [2, 12, 21, 26] focused on diversity as a means to reduce error correlation, not as a direct optimization objective. The authors convincingly demonstrate that sMCL leads to automatic emergence of interpretable specializations in ensemble members across three diverse domains: image classification (CIFAR10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). The method achieves significant improvements (6-10%) over baselines including classical ensembles, MCL [8,9], and Dey et al. [5], while being 5x faster than MCL.

What makes this paper stand out is the bold conceptual shift: instead of training models to minimize standard loss, they train them to collectively produce a set of outputs where the best one (selected by an oracle) has minimal loss. This directly addresses a fundamental limitation in many perception systems where single predictions fail to capture ambiguity (e.g., in semantic segmentation where objects are predicted as mixtures of classes, or in captioning where generic descriptions are produced). The paper effectively demonstrates how this approach aligns with human-like reasoning when faced with ambiguous evidence.

The technical contribution is elegant in its simplicity—sMCL requires only a minor modification to existing SGD implementations. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple domains, showing consistent improvements in oracle performance. Their analysis of "interpretable expertise" is particularly compelling, revealing how ensemble members naturally specialize on different classes or aspects of the problem without any explicit design.

The paper has minor weaknesses that don't undermine its significance: the tie-breaking strategy for equal losses is not fully explored, and the comparison to more recent ensemble diversity work could be expanded. However, these are minor issues given the paper's strong novelty and practical impact.

This work is significant because it solves a practical problem (producing diverse, high-quality predictions) with a simple yet powerful approach that's immediately applicable across many domains. The authors demonstrate broad applicability without requiring task-specific modifications, and the method's speed advantage (5x faster than MCL) makes it feasible for real-world deep learning applications.

Compared to previous NIPS work on ensembles [2, 12, 26] and multiple solution generation [3, 5, 8, 9, 15, 16, 23, 24], this paper makes a clear conceptual leap by directly optimizing for oracle loss in a way that's practical for deep networks. It's not merely an incremental improvement on existing methods—it establishes a new paradigm for training diverse deep ensembles.

The paper's strengths—novel conceptual framework, simple yet powerful algorithm, strong empirical validation across diverse domains, and clear demonstration of interpretable specializations—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. As a reviewer who prioritizes bold, novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Pro:
- Novel conceptual framework for training diverse deep ensembles
- Simple, elegant algorithm that avoids costly retraining
- Significant improvements over baselines (6-10% on oracle metrics)
- Broad applicability across multiple domains and architectures
- Clear demonstration of interpretable specializations
- Practical speed advantage (5x faster than MCL)

Con:
- Minor details about tie-breaking strategy not fully explored
- Some recent ensemble diversity work not compared

This paper introduces a significant new direction in ensemble learning with clear practical benefits and strong theoretical grounding. It's the kind of paper that could spark new research directions and has immediate applicability to real-world perception systems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
