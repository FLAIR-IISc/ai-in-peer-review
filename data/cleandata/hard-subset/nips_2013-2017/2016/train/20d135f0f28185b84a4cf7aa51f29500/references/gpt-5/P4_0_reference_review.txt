Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where a system outputs M predictions per input and is evaluated by an oracle loss that selects the best prediction for each example. Prior MCL approaches (e.g., Guzman-Rivera et al.) alternate between assigning each training example to the currently best-performing model and retraining each model on its assigned data to convergence. This is impractical for modern deep nets.

The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” procedure: within each minibatch, for each example, only the lowest-loss model receives the gradient for that example (ties broken arbitrarily). This implements a stochastic block-coordinate descent on the upper-bound objective that replaces the min with binary assignments, but interleaves assignment and parameter updates per batch. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and trains all ensemble members in parallel.

Empirically, sMCL is evaluated on three settings: CNNs for CIFAR-10 classification, FCNs for PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation, and CNN+LSTM models for MSCOCO image captioning. The metric is the oracle version of the task metric (e.g., top-k accuracy for classification, oracle IoU for segmentation, oracle CIDEr for captioning). sMCL consistently improves oracle performance over independently trained ensembles, a sequential boosting-like approach (Dey et al.), and a reimplementation of MCL with multiple meta-iterations (while being ≈5× faster than MCL). The paper also analyzes emergent specialization: in classification, members specialize by class; in segmentation, by class and shape/frequency patterns; in captioning, by n-gram styles and content.

This work relates to: (i) MCL and M-best diverse inference in structured models (e.g., Batra et al.), (ii) ensemble diversity methods (bagging, negative correlation), and (iii) mixture-of-experts and hard-EM style assignment (e.g., Jordan & Jacobs), but differs by assigning examples based on true loss and backpropagating only through the best expert, without a learned gating network, and by applying the idea broadly to deep architectures and structured outputs.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy to implement. The “winner-take-gradient” rule is compatible with any SGD-trained model and any loss, with no extra hyperparameters.
- Practicality and speed. Trains all members concurrently, avoiding repeated retraining; reported ≈5× speedup over MCL.
- Consistent empirical benefits across diverse tasks and architectures, with meaningful gains in oracle metrics (typically 6–10% over strong baselines).
- Insightful qualitative analyses showing interpretable specialization and output diversity.
- Solid baselines for the setting considered: independent ensembles, MCL, and a sequential diversity-boosting method. For captioning, comparison to beam search from a single model provides a useful reference point.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Missing comparisons to closely related alternatives: mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network (soft or hard assignment) is a natural baseline and could clarify whether loss-based assignment brings advantages over learned routing. Similarly, “hard example mining”/online EM-style training analogies could be discussed more rigorously.
- Limited theoretical analysis. The approach corresponds to block-coordinate updates with assignments fixed within a batch, but there is no analysis of convergence or conditions preventing degenerate solutions (e.g., one expert dominating or experts undertrained due to severe class imbalance in assignments).
- Load balancing and diversity control. The method has no explicit mechanism to prevent unbalanced assignments. While specialization emerges, a simple entropy/balancing regularizer could be important in some regimes; an ablation would be informative.
- Evaluation focuses on oracle metrics only. Since downstream applications require a selector (human or algorithmic), assessing how sMCL ensembles interact with simple selection/reranking heuristics would strengthen significance. For captioning, comparison to diverse inference methods (e.g., diverse beam search) would be informative.
- Scale and detail. ImageNet-scale classification or larger segmentation backbones would test scalability. Some experimental details (tie-breaking policy, assignment statistics over training, per-member data exposure) could be expanded to aid reproducibility.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and gradient derivation are straightforward given the assignment variables; the stochastic block-coordinate interpretation is reasonable for nonconvex deep nets. Claims are supported by experiments on three tasks, with careful oracle evaluation and qualitative analyses. However, stronger baselines (MoE/gating) and any convergence or stability analysis are missing.
- Experimental rigor: Clear improvements over chosen baselines; speedup evidence provided; some ablations (varying k winners) included.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Problem setup, algorithmic idea, and experimental protocols are clearly presented. Including pseudocode (referenced) is helpful. Minor areas for improvement: more explicit description of tie-breaking, assignment frequency/statistics during training, and a concise complexity/overhead analysis.

Originality
- The specific “winner-take-gradient” formulation for deep ensembles minimizing oracle loss appears novel relative to prior MCL that required repeated retraining, and distinct from classic MoE by using ground-truth-loss-based assignment without a gating network. Conceptually related ideas exist (hard-EM, MoE), but this paper adapts and demonstrates them effectively for modern deep structured tasks.

Significance
- Practical impact is high: the method is simple, fast, and broadly applicable; improvements in oracle metrics and demonstrated diversity are likely to be useful for multi-hypothesis systems and downstream selection modules. Wider adoption would benefit from additional baselines and larger-scale experiments but the contribution is already valuable.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How are ties handled in practice, and how sensitive are results to tie-breaking?
- Do you observe assignment imbalance over time (e.g., one model getting most examples)? If so, can you report per-member assignment shares and training dynamics? Would a balancing regularizer help or hurt oracle performance?
- How does sMCL compare to a mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network (soft or hard) trained end-to-end? This seems a crucial baseline.
- For captioning, how does sMCL compare to diverse beam search or other diversity-promoting inference methods?
- Can you report diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise disagreement/KL, segmentation boundary differences) in addition to oracle metrics?

Pros/Cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, general, and efficient algorithm with clear empirical gains across tasks
- Strong practical relevance for multi-hypothesis perception
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization

Cons:
- Missing key baselines (mixture-of-experts with gating; diverse inference for captioning)
- No theoretical or empirical analysis of load balancing/stability
- Limited scale of experiments and focus solely on oracle metrics

Ratings
- Quality: Good
- Clarity: Good
- Originality: Good
- Significance: Good

Recommendation: Accept (contingent on addressing missing baselines and adding assignment dynamics analyses).
