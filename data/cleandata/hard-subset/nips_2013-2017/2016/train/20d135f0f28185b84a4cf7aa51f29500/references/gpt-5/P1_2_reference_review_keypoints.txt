- Summary: Proposes sMCL, a simple SGD "winner-take-gradient" scheme for deep ensembles that optimizes oracle loss by updating only the lowest-loss ensemble member per example; demonstrates improved oracle metrics across classification, segmentation, and captioning.

- Relation to prior work: Positions sMCL relative to MCL, diverse M-best, ensemble/diversity methods, and mixtures-of-experts/competitive learning, but could clarify connections further.

- Strengths: Simple, architecture- and loss-agnostic, efficient to train concurrently, yields consistent empirical gains and interpretable emergent specialization across tasks.

- Theoretical concerns: Lacks convergence/stability analysis; hard winner-take-all assignments may be brittle and enable early dominance that starves experts.

- Load balancing issue: No mechanism to prevent one model from hogging data; paper lacks diagnostics (assignment entropy, per-expert data share) and mitigation strategies.

- Evaluation scope limits: Focuses on oracle metrics without testing downstream selection/reranking or human-in-the-loop selection; needs experiments showing practical gains beyond oracle.

- Captioning comparison gap: Single-model beam search can outperform sMCL oracle in some cases; should report sMCL+beam and independent+beam to disentangle training vs. inference diversity.

- Computational cost: Although faster than MCL, training M models still costs roughly M× a single model; wall-clock, memory, and throughput comparisons to independent ensembles are missing.

- Clarity/reproducibility: Algorithm is clear but several experimental details are sparse (hyperparameters, tie handling, initialization, assignment frequency, batch effects) and references contain errors.

- Quality: Technically sound derivation and empirical support, but needs more quantitative details and ablations (warm-starts, batch size, M) to back claims.

- Originality: Interleaving assignment with SGD is not new, but applying it as a practical, parameter-free method for deep ensembles under oracle loss with broad validation is novel and useful.

- Significance: High practical impact—simple baseline for oracle-loss optimization likely to be widely adopted given demonstrated gains.

- Suggested experiments/improvements: Analyze per-expert assignment dynamics; explore soft/top-k assignments and balancing regularizers; report sMCL+beam; provide wall-clock/GPU memory comparisons; discuss failure modes and add missing citations.

- Pros for acceptance: Practical, simple method with strong empirical gains and insightful analysis that addresses MCL's training cost.

- Cons for acceptance: Limited theoretical analysis, reliance on oracle metrics without downstream evaluation, and missing experimental ablations/details.

- Recommendation: Weak accept to accept — pragmatic, broadly applicable, and empirically compelling despite limited theory.

- Reviewer confidence: Medium.
