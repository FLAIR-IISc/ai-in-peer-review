Summary
The paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL): training an ensemble of M predictors to minimize oracle loss, i.e., min over m of the task loss for each example. Building on Guzman-Rivera et al. (MCL), which alternates between hard assignment of examples to models and retraining to convergence, the authors propose a stochastic variant (sMCL) amenable to deep learning. The key idea is a “winner-take-gradient” update: for each example in a mini-batch, only the model with the lowest loss receives gradients; others receive zero. This is equivalent to stochastic block coordinate descent on the MCL upper bound with hard per-example assignments interleaved with SGD updates.

They demonstrate sMCL across three tasks/architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM). Compared to independently trained ensembles, Dey et al.’s boosting-like sequential method, and batch MCL (5 meta-iterations), sMCL improves oracle metrics (top-k accuracy, IoU, CIDEr) while being considerably faster than batch MCL. They also analyze emergent specialization: in classification, members become class specialists; in segmentation, more nuanced shape/class biases arise; in captioning, members specialize in different n-grams/structures.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects two lines of work: (1) M-best/diverse inference in structured prediction (e.g., Batra et al., Kirillov et al.) that extract multiple diverse solutions from a single probabilistic model, and (2) ensemble learning/diversity induction (bagging, negative correlation, artificial data). Unlike diverse M-best methods (tailored to specific graphical models/energies), sMCL directly trains multiple deep predictors under oracle loss. Relative to prior MCL formulations (Guzman-Rivera et al.; Vondrick et al.), the contribution is an SGD-compatible, parallel training algorithm that avoids repeated retraining or sequential boosting schedules. The method is also related to hard-EM/k-means and mixture-of-experts with hard gating, with the notable distinction that “gating” is induced by the per-example loss rather than a learned input-dependent gating network.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation is straightforward: the gradient of the surrogate upper bound w.r.t. fm(xi) is the usual loss gradient if fm wins, else zero. While simple, it is correct and immediately implementable. However, the paper lacks analysis of convergence properties, stability, or conditions under which “expert starvation” occurs. There is no load-balancing term; early noise can lock examples to a subset of models.
- Empirics: Results are convincing across three tasks, with consistent oracle improvements and qualitative diversity. The specialization analyses are insightful. That said, several choices limit the strength of the claims:
  - Heavy reliance on oracle metrics without demonstrating downstream selection/reranking effectiveness. Oracle gains do not guarantee end-to-end utility.
  - Comparisons: MCL is only run for 5 meta-iterations (claimed infeasible beyond that), which may disadvantage it; the 5x speedup claim is tied to this choice. More careful compute-matched comparisons (e.g., equal total epochs/updates per member) would be fairer.
  - Captioning comparison to beam search is informative but not apples-to-apples (beam search is inference-time diversification on a single model). The trade-off between oracle score and linguistic diversity is noted but could be quantified more deeply (e.g., self-BLEU, distinct-n metrics, image-conditioned diversity).
  - Limited ablations: sensitivity to M, batch size, learning rate, and initialization; effect of tie-breaking; training from scratch vs. fine-tuning (segmentation); top-k winner variants beyond a single k curve; alternative diversity metrics (pairwise disagreement, coverage).
- Reproducibility: Many training details are provided, but some are missing or fragmented (e.g., exact FCN hyperparameters, dataset splits for VOC subset, captioning fine-tuning configuration). Including pseudocode (Algorithm 2 is referenced) and implementation notes (e.g., handling ties efficiently, backprop masking) would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for oracle loss is clear and supported by qualitative examples. The related work is broad and mostly appropriate. A few suggestions:
- Explicitly connect sMCL to hard mixture-of-experts and discuss differences (no gating network, loss-based gating) and implications (no balancing).
- Discuss computational complexity and memory footprint vs. independent ensembles; clarify whether sMCL’s per-step cost is identical aside from a max-over-M per example.
- Provide a stability discussion and practical tips (e.g., pretraining independently for a few epochs, temperature/soft assignments).

Originality
The core novelty is not the objective (oracle loss with hard assignments) but the training procedure that interleaves assignment with SGD in deep nets. This “winner-take-gradient” perspective is simple yet impactful, and, to my knowledge, was not previously articulated/practiced for deep ensembles optimizing oracle loss across such diverse tasks. The work distinguishes itself from M-best inference (single-model) and classic ensemble methods (not oracle-focused).

Significance
The contribution is practical and likely to be adopted: it is architecture- and loss-agnostic, trivial to implement, and demonstrably effective across tasks. The idea that specialization emerges organically under oracle loss is compelling and useful. While theoretical depth is limited, the empirical breadth and simplicity increase its potential impact.

Pros
- Simple, general, and scalable method; easy to drop into existing training loops.
- Strong, consistent oracle improvements across classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Parallel training; substantial wall-clock speed advantage over batch MCL.
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization.
- Applicable to structured outputs without modeling changes.

Cons
- Evaluation focuses on oracle metrics; lack of downstream selection experiments diminishes practical significance claims.
- No load balancing or soft gating; potential expert starvation and instability are not analyzed.
- Comparisons to MCL may not be compute-matched; beam-search comparison is not strictly comparable.
- Limited ablations on sensitivity to hyperparameters, k-winner variants, and initialization.
- Missing comparisons to classic mixture-of-experts with learned gating and to modern uncertainty/diversity baselines (e.g., MC dropout).

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is sMCL to batch size, learning rate, and M? Any signs of starvation or mode collapse? Do you use any warm-starting?
- Can you report load balance statistics (fraction of examples per member over time) and mitigation strategies (e.g., entropy regularization, soft assignments)?
- Does training from scratch (no independent pretraining) work equally well for segmentation?
- Can you demonstrate downstream selection (e.g., simple rerankers) and calibration?
- How does sMCL compare to a hard/soft mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network?

Recommendation
Accept (7). Despite limited theory and some evaluation caveats, the paper presents a simple, general, and effective training recipe for diverse deep ensembles under oracle loss, with solid empirical evidence and clear practical utility. Addressing the noted evaluation and analysis gaps would further strengthen the contribution.
