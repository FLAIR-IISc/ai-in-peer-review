Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the multiple-choice learning (MCL) setting, where the learner outputs M hypotheses and is evaluated by an oracle loss that selects the best among them. The main contribution is a stochastic training procedure, sMCL, that replaces the alternating assignment/retraining of classical MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.) with a “winner-take-gradient” strategy within SGD: for each example, only the ensemble member with the lowest loss receives gradient signal. The authors argue this is architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free, and substantially faster than prior MCL training. They demonstrate results on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+RNN), reporting improvements in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, Dey et al. (boosting-like), and an MCL baseline trained for 5 meta-iterations. They also present qualitative evidence of emergent specialization across ensemble members.

This work sits at the intersection of ensemble learning, multiple diverse solutions (M-best/M- diverse in structured prediction), and mixture-of-experts (MoE). The formulation is closely related to hard-EM with latent assignment variables, and the proposed sMCL can be seen as an online, hard-gating MoE without an explicit gating network. The paper appropriately references MCL and diverse M-best work for structured models, but does not adequately situate itself with respect to classic MoE (e.g., Jordan & Jacobs, 1994) and related hard assignment/gating ideas.

Strengths:
- Simple, general recipe that can be plugged into standard deep learning training loops and applied across tasks and architectures.
- Empirically yields more diverse ensembles and consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles across three domains.
- Concurrent training removes the expensive retraining cycles of prior MCL implementations; reported speedups suggest better practicality for deep nets.
- Qualitative analyses (class specialization for CIFAR-10; diverse segmentation hypotheses; diverse captions) are insightful and consistent with the method’s inductive bias.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Over-reliance on oracle metrics. While oracle evaluation is standard for MCL, it can mask degradation in individual model quality and calibration. There is no assessment of non-oracle performance (e.g., average per-member accuracy/IoU/CIDEr), nor any simple downstream selection/reranking demonstration. In practical systems, a selection mechanism is necessary; even a basic learned re-ranker or confidence heuristic would provide a more complete picture.
- Missing baselines and fairness issues:
  - Mixture-of-experts: An explicit MoE baseline with a learned gating network (soft or hard) trained end-to-end would be a natural and strong comparator, especially given sMCL’s close relation to hard MoE.
  - For captioning, a single-model beam search already attains higher oracle than sMCL at M=5; this weakens the claim that sMCL is the “first practical algorithm” for diverse deep outputs. Additional comparisons to temperature sampling/top-k/nucleus sampling would be appropriate.
  - For segmentation/captioning, capacity and training budget parity are unclear. In captioning, the shared CNN backbone across ensemble members changes the capacity profile; baselines that also share the backbone should be included, or the total parameter/compute budgets should be equalized and reported.
- Convergence/stability and starvation. The winner-take-gradient rule can starve weaker learners early, leading to collapse or highly skewed assignment. The paper does not report assignment distributions over time, variance across random seeds, or stabilization tricks (e.g., epsilon-greedy assignment, temperature-softened argmin, k-winner variants). The k-winner ablation is suggestive but limited; more systematic analysis is needed.
- Experimental scope and strength:
  - CIFAR-10-Quick is a small, dated model; results would be more convincing with stronger backbones (e.g., contemporary ResNets for the time) and on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet top-k).
  - VOC results are reported on a subset of VOC11 val; details are sparse, and the absolute numbers and standard deviations are not provided. MCL was run for only 5 meta-iterations “due to cost,” so the comparison may be unfair if more iterations would close the gap.
  - Diversity metrics are thin. Counting unique n-grams is coarse; pairwise BLEU/self-BLEU, distinct-n, and calibration/disagreement measures would strengthen claims about diversity-quality trade-offs.
- Theoretical guarantees: While the link to the upper-bound objective is clear, there is no convergence analysis or discussion of stationary points under stochastic hard assignment. Even a brief analysis of conditions under which sMCL approximates block-coordinate descent would help.

Quality: Technically plausible and consistent with prior MCL/EM intuitions, but analysis is empirical and somewhat limited. Claims about efficiency and broad applicability are credible, yet some baselines are missing and fairness/variance are underreported.

Clarity: Generally well-written and easy to follow. However, important experimental details (dataset splits, compute budgets, parameter counts, how ties are broken, assignment dynamics over training, variance across runs) are missing. The relationship to MoE should be explicitly discussed.

Originality: The incremental novelty is in making MCL practical for deep nets via stochastic hard assignment in SGD. The idea itself is a natural extension of prior MCL/hard-EM/MoE, but the application to deep ensembles is useful.

Significance: Potentially useful to practitioners needing multiple hypotheses. However, without stronger baselines (MoE, stronger backbones), non-oracle evaluations, and more rigorous analysis, the impact is somewhat limited.

Questions and requests for the authors:
- Provide comparisons to a MoE baseline with a learned gating network (soft/hard). How does sMCL compare in oracle and non-oracle metrics?
- Report non-oracle per-member performance and a simple selection mechanism’s performance (e.g., confidence-based or a lightweight re-ranker).
- Clarify training budget parity and parameter counts across methods; for captioning, include baselines with shared CNN backbones.
- Report variance across seeds and assignment distributions over epochs; any evidence of predictor starvation?
- Detail tie-breaking strategy and its effect; did you try softened assignments (e.g., temperature-softmin) or epsilon-greedy?
- Provide absolute numbers with standard deviations for all plots and tables; expand VOC evaluation to standard splits if possible.
- Add stronger diversity metrics (self-BLEU, distinct-n, pairwise disagreement).

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, broadly applicable method with compelling qualitative behavior.
- Consistent oracle gains across tasks; practical training speedup over prior MCL.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing key baselines (MoE), limited/non-robust experimental validation, and heavy reliance on oracle metrics.
- Potential instability and starvation not analyzed; fairness of comparisons unclear.

Recommendation: Borderline reject (would move to accept if key baselines and non-oracle evaluations are added, and fairness/variance concerns are addressed).

Confidence: High.
