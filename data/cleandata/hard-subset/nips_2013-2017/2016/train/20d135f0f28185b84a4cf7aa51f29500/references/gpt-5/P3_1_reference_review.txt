Summary
The paper tackles the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) setting, where an ensemble produces M outputs per input and performance is measured by an oracle loss that selects the best output per example. The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” SGD procedure: in each minibatch, compute each model’s loss per example and backpropagate only through the model with the minimum loss for that example. This is an SGD-friendly surrogate to the alternating retrain-and-reassign procedure of classical MCL, making MCL practical for deep networks. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM) show consistent improvements in oracle metrics over independent ensembles, MCL (with limited meta-iterations), and a boosting-like sequential method, with interpretable emergent specialization.

Relation to prior work
The work is an SGD instantiation of the MCL paradigm (Guzman-Rivera et al.). It is also closely related to hard-EM training and mixture-of-experts with hard gating (Jacobs et al., Jordan & Jacobs), though the gating here is implicit (argmin of current loss) rather than learned. It contrasts with diversity-promoting ensemble methods (e.g., resampling, negative correlation, artificial data) that do not optimize oracle loss and often do not extend to structured outputs. It differs from “diverse M-best”/DPP-style inference-time diversity, focusing instead on training-time specialization across multiple networks.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: a one-line change to the backward pass makes MCL feasible for deep nets without expensive alternating retraining; concurrently trains all members; parameter-free in its core form.
- Generality: applicable across architectures and losses; demonstrated on classification, segmentation, and captioning.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements in oracle metrics, faster optimization of oracle loss vs. independent ensembles; interpretable emergent specializations (class-wise for CIFAR-10; more nuanced, shape/frequency biases for segmentation; stylistic and content diversity for captions).
- Clear motivation: many real systems need sets of plausible hypotheses; oracle loss aligns with such use-cases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty vs. prior art: sMCL is essentially hard assignment to the argmin expert followed by standard backprop—a natural extension of MCL and hard-EM/MoE to minibatch SGD. The paper would benefit from a tighter connection and explicit comparison to mixture-of-experts with learned gates (including load balancing) and to soft versions (e.g., temperature-weighted k-winners).
- Stability and collapse: winner-take-all can lead to early dominance and starvation of other experts. The paper does not quantify how often collapse happens, nor propose balancing regularizers. Some experiments suggest healthy specialization, but a diagnostic (e.g., assignment entropy, per-expert data share over time) and mitigation strategies would strengthen the work.
- Experimental scope and fairness:
  - MCL baseline: only five meta-iterations reported; likely insufficient for convergence. A discussion of computational budget parity and performance-time trade-offs would be fairer.
  - Captioning: the CNN is shared due to memory, and authors “aggregate and backpropagate” gradients from all LSTMs through the CNN. This deviates from winner-take-gradient and could dampen specialization; please clarify whether per-example CNN gradients come only from the winning LSTM or from all.
  - Beam search comparison: a single model with wider beam yields higher oracle in one setting but lower diversity. It would be informative to report sMCL+beam to separate training- vs. inference-time diversity.
  - Non-oracle metrics: optimizing oracle loss can degrade the average single-prediction quality. Reporting standard top-1/top-5 accuracy, mean IoU, and CIDEr for individual members and the ensemble’s averaged prediction would quantify this trade-off.
- Missing ablations: sensitivity to M, batch size, initialization, tie-breaking policy; k-winners training (not just evaluation) to interpolate diversity vs. coverage; computation overhead vs. independent ensembles.
- Related work gaps: classical MoE literature is not cited; diversity-regularized deep ensembles (e.g., determinantal or repulsive priors) could be discussed for contrast.

Quality
Technically sound at the algorithmic level; the gradient expression is correct for the upper-bound formulation with hard assignments. However, there is no theoretical analysis (e.g., convergence, bounds on oracle loss vs. M, or conditions preventing collapse). Empirical results are solid but would benefit from the additional fairness checks, non-oracle metrics, and stability diagnostics mentioned above.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The core algorithm is easy to understand and implement. Some details need clarification: tie-breaking policy; any normalization of losses across models; use of k-winners during training vs. only for analysis; precise treatment of shared CNN gradients in captioning; compute/memory costs.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea is a straightforward and useful SGD realization of MCL with hard assignments, closely related to longstanding MoE/hard-EM ideas. The novelty lies in recognizing that winner-take-gradient yields a practical, parameter-free training recipe for modern deep ensembles and demonstrating its broad applicability.

Significance
High practical significance: many applications benefit from diverse hypothesis sets; the method is simple and likely to be adopted. The empirical evidence across three tasks supports the claim. The work could catalyze further research on load-balanced, differentiable variants and on integrating training-time specialization with inference-time diversity.

Questions for the authors
1) How often do you observe expert collapse? Can you report per-expert data shares and assignment entropy over epochs? Do you use any balancing tricks (e.g., warm start with shared pretraining, temperature annealing, epsilon-greedy assignments)?
2) For captioning with a shared CNN, are per-example CNN gradients taken only from the winning LSTM or aggregated across all LSTMs? If the latter, how does this affect specialization?
3) Can you report individual-model (non-oracle) performance to quantify the trade-off?
4) Did you try sMCL+beam? How do gains compound?
5) How sensitive is sMCL to M, batch size, and initialization? Any guidance to avoid degenerate assignments?

Pros
- Simple, general, and effective training procedure for oracle loss
- Strong empirical improvements and interpretable specializations
- Makes MCL practical for deep architectures; concurrent training and speedups

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to MCL/hard-EM/MoE
- No theoretical analysis; potential instability/collapse not quantified
- Some fairness and clarity issues in experimental setup and baselines
- Missing non-oracle metrics and broader ablations

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The method is simple, practical, and empirically useful across multiple domains. While the conceptual novelty is incremental, the contribution is significant for enabling MCL with deep models. Addressing the clarity and experimental concerns would further strengthen the paper.

Reviewer confidence: high.
