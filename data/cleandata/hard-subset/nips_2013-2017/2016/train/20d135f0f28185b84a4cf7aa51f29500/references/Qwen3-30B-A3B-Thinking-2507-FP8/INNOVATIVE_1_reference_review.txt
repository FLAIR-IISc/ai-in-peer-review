

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles by directly optimizing for the oracle loss—the minimum loss among multiple predictions. The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm [8,9], but make a significant practical contribution by adapting it to deep networks through a stochastic gradient descent approach that avoids the costly retraining required by previous MCL methods. The key insight is that during training, only the gradient of the lowest-error predictor for each example should be backpropagated, enabling concurrent training of all ensemble members.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of diverse predictions in real-world applications where ambiguity is common (e.g., object classification with overlapping categories, image captioning with ambiguous scenes). The authors demonstrate that sMCL leads to automatically emerging specializations among ensemble members across multiple domains: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). The results consistently show sMCL outperforms classical ensembles, the previous MCL method (by 6-10% in oracle metrics), and the sequential boosting approach of Dey et al. [5], while being 5x faster than MCL.

The paper's core contribution—transforming MCL into a practical deep learning algorithm—is novel and impactful. Previous work on MCL [8,9] required alternating between training models and reassigning examples, making it infeasible for deep networks that take weeks to train. sMCL elegantly sidesteps this issue with a simple modification to the standard SGD procedure: during backward pass, only the gradient of the lowest-error predictor for each example is backpropagated. This is a bold, elegant solution that requires no additional parameters, is agnostic to loss function or architecture, and works with standard deep learning frameworks.

The paper excels in demonstrating the broad applicability of sMCL across three distinct domains with different network architectures and output spaces. The analysis of emergent specializations (e.g., ensemble members becoming specialists for particular classes in image classification, or for specific objects in segmentation) provides compelling evidence that the method is not just improving performance but fundamentally changing how ensembles operate. The qualitative results in Figure 6 and 7 vividly illustrate how sMCL produces diverse, contextually appropriate predictions where classical ensembles produce repetitive, mode-seeking outputs.

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could be improved. The paper states that MCL is "not feasible with training deep networks" but doesn't provide specific timing comparisons for a single MCL iteration versus sMCL. A brief example (e.g., "training a single ResNet-50 model takes 2 weeks, so 5 meta-iterations of MCL would take 50 weeks") would strengthen the practical motivation. Additionally, while the k-parameter analysis (Figures 4d-e) is insightful, the paper could better clarify how this parameter would be chosen in practice.

However, these are minor points in the context of the paper's significant contribution. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation in existing ensemble learning approaches (infeasibility for deep networks) and provided a simple yet powerful solution that works with standard deep learning frameworks. The novelty of the approach is substantial—this isn't just another ensemble method, but a fundamentally new way to train ensembles that directly optimizes for the oracle loss in a way that's practical for modern deep networks.

As a reviewer who prioritizes bold, novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The sMCL approach is conceptually clean, broadly applicable, and significantly advances the state of the art in ensemble learning for deep networks. It provides a practical solution to a real problem in computer vision and natural language processing applications where diverse predictions are valuable. The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of their approach across multiple domains with compelling results.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by bridging the gap between the theoretical MCL paradigm and practical deep learning. It's the kind of paper that opens up new research directions—many researchers will now consider how to apply this "winner-take-gradient" approach to their own problems. Given the significance of the problem, the elegance of the solution, and the strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
