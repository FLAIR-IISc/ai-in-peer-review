Summary
The paper tackles multiple-choice learning (MCL) for deep ensembles, where the goal is to produce M predictions per input and minimize the oracle loss (min over members). Prior MCL methods alternate between assigning examples to the best member and retraining each member to convergence, which is impractical for deep nets. The proposed Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL) interleaves assignment and training within SGD: for each example in a minibatch, only the lowest-loss model receives gradient (“winner-take-gradient”). The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic and trains all members concurrently. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM) report consistent gains in oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles, baseline MCL (with a limited number of meta-iterations), and a boosting-like approach (Dey et al.). The paper also highlights emergent specialization among ensemble members.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on MCL (Guzman-Rivera et al.) and related diverse M-best methods for structured prediction, as well as diversity-inducing ensemble techniques (bagging, negative correlation) and mixture-of-experts / ensemble-of-experts ideas. Unlike diverse M-best inference for probabilistic models, sMCL targets general differentiable deep models. Compared to prior MCL training, sMCL replaces expensive alternating retraining with an SGD-compatible hard assignment within minibatches.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy-to-implement training mechanism that plugs into standard deep architectures with little overhead.
- Targets a clearly defined and relevant objective (oracle loss) for real applications requiring multiple plausible hypotheses.
- Concurrent training of ensemble members addresses a genuine computational bottleneck of prior MCL.
- Cross-domain empirical evaluation (classification, segmentation, captioning) with consistent improvements in oracle metrics over baselines; qualitative evidence of meaningful specialization.
- Clear connection to hard-EM/K-means intuition; presentation is generally readable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited theoretical grounding. The paper optimizes a surrogate (hard assignment upper bound), but provides no convergence analysis, no discussion of stability, and no guarantees relative to the oracle loss—especially given the non-differentiability and tie-breaking.
- Risk of degenerate training dynamics. Winner-take-all can create “dead” experts or imbalanced load. Aside from a brief k-best variation, there is no explicit balancing or regularization; the paper does not quantify assignment entropy, load balance, or sensitivity to initialization.
- Baseline coverage is uneven. For classification and segmentation, comparisons to mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network, top-k loss (or “oracle” cross-entropy variants), or diversity-promoting regularizers (e.g., negative correlation) are absent. For captioning, comparisons emphasize oracle CIDEr-D and unique n-grams but do not examine alternative diverse decoding methods (e.g., diverse beam search) or sampling-based methods that were known at the time.
- Evaluation emphasizes oracle metrics without end-to-end evidence. The paper assumes a downstream “oracle” but does not demonstrate a selection mechanism or the utility to a real system (e.g., re-ranking, human-in-the-loop). Improvements in oracle metrics, while suggestive, do not guarantee practical gains.
- Scale and datasets. CIFAR-10 “quick” is small; segmentation is on VOC11 val subset; captioning uses a beam width of two. The absence of larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet top-k, full VOC/COCO segmentation protocols) limits claims of broad applicability. For MCL, the paper concedes that stronger results might be possible with more meta-iterations but deems them infeasible; this blurs whether the comparison is methodological or computational.
- Reproducibility details are thin. Crucial elements (exact tie-breaking policy, assignment implementation, batch composition effects, impact of M, batch size, and learning rates per member) are not fully specified. No code availability is mentioned.

Quality
Technically sound at the algorithmic level, but lacks theoretical analysis of the surrogate and training dynamics. Experimental support spans three tasks with consistent trends, yet the scope and depth are modest, and some baselines are missing or underpowered. Claims such as “first practical algorithm for diverse deep ensembles” feel stronger than the evidence presented.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-structured. The conceptual link to MCL and the gradient derivation are straightforward. However, several implementation details and experimental settings need more precision for reproducibility.

Originality
The core idea—stochastic hard assignment of examples to the current best predictor within SGD—is a natural and simple adaptation of prior MCL/EM-style methods to deep learning. It is incremental conceptually but novel in its practical instantiation and application breadth across deep architectures.

Significance
Producing diverse sets of plausible outputs is important. If robust and broadly applicable, sMCL could be of practical interest. However, the current evidence (small-scale datasets, oracle-only metrics, partial baselines) limits the strength of the conclusions. Without end-to-end evaluations or stronger baselines at scale, significance remains uncertain.

Questions for the authors
- How often do experts die or collapse to a single dominant member? Can you report assignment distributions over epochs and across seeds?
- How sensitive are results to M, batch size, learning rates, and tie-breaking? Does stochastic tie-breaking matter?
- Can you compare to a mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network trained jointly, and to top-k cross-entropy or other oracle-oriented losses?
- For captioning, how does sMCL compare to diverse beam search or sampling-based decoding for a single model with the same number of outputs?
- Can you show end-to-end benefits with a downstream selector (even a simple learned re-ranker)?
- Provide training time/memory scaling with M and a fair computational budget comparison to MCL (e.g., same total GPU-hours).

Pros
- Simple, general method; easy integration into SGD training.
- Consistent improvements in oracle metrics across tasks.
- Interesting qualitative specialization emerges.
- Addresses real computational limitations of prior MCL.

Cons
- Limited theory; potential instability and degeneracy not analyzed.
- Missing key baselines (mixture-of-experts, top-k loss, diverse decoding).
- Small-scale experiments; oracle-only evaluation without downstream selection.
- Comparisons to MCL confounded by compute limits.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is appealing and could be impactful, but the empirical evidence and baseline coverage are not yet strong enough, and the lack of analysis of training dynamics is concerning. Strengthening baselines, scaling experiments, and providing end-to-end results would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: High.
