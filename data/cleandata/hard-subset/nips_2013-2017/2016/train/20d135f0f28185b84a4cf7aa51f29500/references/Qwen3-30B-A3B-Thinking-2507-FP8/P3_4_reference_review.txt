

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

## Summary
This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that directly minimizes oracle loss (the loss of the best prediction in the ensemble) rather than the standard expected loss. The authors build upon the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9], but propose a stochastic optimization approach that avoids the costly retraining required by prior MCL methods. Instead of alternating between assigning examples to their best predictor and retraining models, sMCL uses a "winner-take-gradient" strategy where only the lowest-error predictor for each example receives gradient updates during training. The paper demonstrates sMCL across three diverse tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (Pascal VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO), showing consistent improvements over baselines including classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. [5].

## Strengths

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the growing literature on ensemble methods and structured prediction. Its key strengths include:

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The authors effectively motivate the need for diverse output sets in ambiguous prediction scenarios, providing concrete examples from computer vision and natural language processing.

2. **Simple yet Effective Algorithm**: sMCL is remarkably simple to implement, requiring only a minor modification to standard SGD training. This practicality is a significant advantage over prior MCL methods that require expensive retraining.

3. **Strong Empirical Results**: The method consistently outperforms baselines across multiple tasks (6-10% improvements over classical ensembles), with particularly impressive results in semantic segmentation where the method reveals interpretable specializations.

4. **Insightful Analysis**: The paper provides valuable insights into how specialization emerges naturally during training, with the class-wise distribution of oracle assignments (Figures 4c-4e) demonstrating clear specialization patterns that emerge without explicit design.

## Weaknesses

While promising, the paper has several limitations that should be addressed:

1. **Theoretical Weakness**: The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of the sMCL algorithm. There is no convergence proof, no analysis of the trade-offs between ensemble size and specialization, and no formal justification for why the "winner-take-gradient" approach leads to better specialization than other diversity-inducing techniques.

2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The comparison with MCL is somewhat misleading. The paper states MCL is 5x slower, but doesn't clarify whether this factor accounts for the number of meta-iterations needed for convergence. A fairer comparison would include MCL run for a sufficient number of meta-iterations to achieve comparable performance.

3. **Superficial Analysis of Specialization**: While the paper shows that specialization emerges, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* certain ensemble members specialize on particular classes or tasks. A more thorough analysis of the relationship between specialization patterns and dataset characteristics would strengthen the paper.

4. **Lack of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where sMCL might fail or underperform, nor does it address potential limitations (e.g., when the optimal specialization is not easily captured by a single loss function).

5. **Limited Connection to Recent Ensemble Work**: The related work section doesn't sufficiently engage with recent advances in deep ensemble methods, such as those that use data augmentation, different initialization strategies, or specific diversity-promoting losses.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of ensemble learning for ambiguous prediction tasks. The sMCL algorithm is simple, effective, and generalizable to a wide range of deep learning architectures and tasks. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper provides insightful analysis of how specialization emerges during training.

However, the lack of theoretical grounding and incomplete baseline comparisons prevent the paper from being a strong candidate for NIPS. While the method is practical and shows clear improvements over baselines, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from other diversity-inducing ensemble techniques that have been developed recently.

The significance of the work is high for practitioners working on structured prediction problems where multiple reasonable outputs exist. However, the paper doesn't advance the theoretical understanding of ensemble learning as much as it could, and the novelty is somewhat incremental given the prior MCL work.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper has strong empirical results and addresses an important problem in machine learning, but it lacks the theoretical depth and comprehensive comparison that would make it a compelling NIPS contribution. The authors should address the theoretical limitations, provide a more thorough analysis of specialization patterns, and conduct a more rigorous comparison with recent ensemble methods before the paper would be suitable for NIPS.

The work is promising and could be strengthened significantly with additional theoretical analysis and more nuanced comparison with related methods. With these improvements, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the field.
