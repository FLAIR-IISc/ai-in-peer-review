### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of training an ensemble of deep networks to produce a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input. This is framed within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, where the objective is to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing prediction among the set of outputs. The authors argue that this is particularly useful for tasks with inherent ambiguity, where generating multiple plausible hypotheses is more valuable than producing a single, potentially averaged-out prediction.

The primary contribution is a novel training algorithm called Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). This method adapts the block coordinate descent approach of prior work on MCL [Guzman-Rivera et al., 2012] to the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) setting used for training deep neural networks. The sMCL algorithm trains all M ensemble members concurrently. For each data point in a mini-batch, a forward pass is computed for all M models. During the backward pass, the gradient of the loss is backpropagated only to the single model that produced the lowest loss for that specific data point. This "winner-take-gradient" approach encourages different models in the ensemble to specialize on different subsets or aspects of the data distribution.

The paper contrasts sMCL with traditional ensemble methods that aim to improve average accuracy and with previous MCL methods that require costly, iterative retraining of models, making them impractical for deep learning. The efficacy of sMCL is demonstrated across three diverse and challenging tasks: image classification on CIFAR-10, semantic segmentation on Pascal VOC, and image captioning on MSCOCO. In each case, sMCL is shown to significantly outperform baselines (independently trained ensembles, MCL, and a boosting-like method) in terms of oracle performance metrics. The paper provides both quantitative and qualitative analysis showing that sMCL leads to the emergence of interpretable specializations among the ensemble members.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel, simple, and effective method for training diverse deep ensembles. While the concept of Multiple Choice Learning is not new, its adaptation to a stochastic, concurrent training regime for deep networks is a significant and timely contribution. The problem of generating diverse outputs for ambiguous tasks is of high practical importance in fields like computer vision and NLP. sMCL provides a general, parameter-free, and architecture-agnostic solution that could be widely adopted.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated, stemming directly from the partial derivative of the MCL objective function. The experimental evaluation is strong and comprehensive. The authors validate their method on three distinct tasks with different network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN), demonstrating its broad applicability. The choice of baselines is appropriate, and the results consistently show that sMCL achieves superior oracle performance, supporting the central claims of the paper.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for generating diverse solutions is clearly articulated with compelling examples in the introduction. The sMCL algorithm is explained concisely and is easy to understand. The experiments are described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented clearly through tables, graphs, and insightful qualitative examples that effectively illustrate the emergent specialization of the ensemble members.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Cost:** While sMCL offers a significant speedup over the original iterative MCL, its training cost should be discussed more transparently. During training, sMCL requires M forward passes for each example to determine the "winner," making it approximately M times slower and requiring M times more memory than training a single model. This cost may be prohibitive for very large models or ensembles. A more explicit discussion of this computational trade-off would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Inference and Practical Application:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on oracle metrics, which effectively measures the quality and diversity of the generated set of predictions. However, in a real-world application, an oracle is not available to select the best output. The paper does not address the downstream problem of how to utilize the diverse set of predictions at inference time (e.g., via a re-ranking model or by passing all hypotheses to a human operator). While this is outside the core contribution of training the ensemble, a brief discussion of potential selection mechanisms would enhance the paper's practical significance.

3.  **Comparison in Image Captioning:** In the image captioning experiment (Table 1), beam search with a beam size of M=5 on a single model achieves a higher oracle CIDEr score than the sMCL ensemble of M=5. Although sMCL generates more diverse captions (more unique n-grams), this result slightly complicates the otherwise clear narrative of sMCL's superiority. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the trade-off between oracle score and diversity in this specific context, or by showing results where beam search is also applied to each member of the sMCL ensemble to ensure a more direct comparison.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to the important problem of generating diverse predictions with deep networks. The technical contribution is sound, the experimental validation is thorough and convincing, and the paper is written with excellent clarity. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate more to the scope of the discussion than to fundamental flaws in the method or evaluation. The work is a significant advance over prior art in making Multiple Choice Learning feasible for modern deep learning architectures and is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
