### Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

**Summary**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel and efficient algorithm for training ensembles of deep neural networks. The goal is not to improve the accuracy of an averaged ensemble prediction, but rather to train the members to produce a diverse set of high-quality outputs. The training objective is to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing member on any given example. The key idea of sMCL is remarkably simple: during stochastic gradient descent, for each example in a mini-batch, the loss is backpropagated only to the single ensemble member that produced the lowest error. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows for concurrent training of all ensemble members and avoids the computationally prohibitive retraining cycles required by previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods, making the paradigm practical for modern deep learning architectures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of sMCL across three distinct and challenging tasks: image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning, showing that it consistently outperforms standard ensembles and other baselines in oracle performance and output diversity. A key finding is that this simple training strategy leads to the automatic emergence of interpretable "specialists" within the ensemble.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold and elegant conceptual contribution. While the idea of Multiple Choice Learning is not new, its application to deep learning was previously impractical due to the high cost of retraining models. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a brilliant and non-obvious solution to this problem. The "winner-take-gradient" rule is simple to understand, easy to implement, and represents a fundamentally new way to think about training deep ensembles. Instead of encouraging diversity through complex regularizers or data partitioning schemes, it arises naturally from optimizing the oracle loss directly. This is precisely the kind of creative and foundational work that NIPS should champion.

2.  **Significant and Important Problem:** The paper addresses a critical, yet often under-explored, aspect of machine perception. Standard models trained with expected loss minimization often exhibit "mode-seeking" behavior, producing averaged or generic outputs when faced with ambiguity. This is a major practical limitation. The sMCL framework provides a direct and effective method for generating a set of plausible hypotheses, which is invaluable for any system that involves a downstream verification component or a human in the loop. The ability to capture multi-modal beliefs about the world is a significant step towards more robust and useful AI systems.

3.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the broad applicability of their method on three very different tasks using diverse network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). The consistent and significant gains over baselines across all tasks strongly support the generality of the approach. The analysis of the emergent specialization is particularly compelling. Seeing the ensemble members automatically partition the label space in the CIFAR10 experiment, or specialize in different semantic concepts and sentence structures in the captioning task, provides powerful evidence that the method works as intended and offers fascinating insights into the learning dynamics of ensembles.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality Depends on a Downstream Oracle:** The evaluation is based on an oracle that always picks the best output. In a real-world application, such an oracle does not exist. The paper does not address the subsequent challenge of how to select the best hypothesis from the generated set without access to ground truth. While this is a known limitation of the entire "generating diverse solutions" line of work, it remains a practical hurdle. However, I view the generation of a high-quality, diverse candidate set as a necessary and significant prerequisite for any re-ranking or selection module, and thus this paper's contribution remains substantial on its own.

2.  **Nuanced Comparison with Baselines:** The comparison with the original MCL method is somewhat constrained by the authors' (understandable) admission that running it properly was computationally infeasible. While sMCL's 5x speedup is a major contribution in itself, this makes the direct performance comparison on the more complex tasks less conclusive. Similarly, the image captioning results show that a single model with a large beam search can achieve higher oracle CIDEr score (albeit with much less diversity). This nuance is interesting but does not detract from the core message, especially since beam search can be combined with sMCL models as well. These are minor points and do not undermine the paper's core claims.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly effective approach to a significant problem. The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple, elegant, and its "winner-take-gradient" dynamic is a genuinely new contribution to the field of deep ensemble learning. The paper prioritizes a powerful new concept over incremental methodological refinement, and backs it up with strong, broad, and insightful experiments. The work is likely to inspire follow-up research into diverse prediction sets, emergent specialization in ensembles, and training dynamics under alternative loss functions. This is an exemplary NIPS paper.
