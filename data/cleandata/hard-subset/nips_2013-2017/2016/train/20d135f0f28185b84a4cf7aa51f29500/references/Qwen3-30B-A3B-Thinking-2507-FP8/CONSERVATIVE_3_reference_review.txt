

# Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper proposes "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning" (sMCL), a method for training deep ensembles that produce diverse outputs by backpropagating gradients only to the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each example. The authors argue that traditional single-output models fail to handle ambiguity in perception tasks, and that ensembles trained to minimize oracle loss (the lowest loss among the ensemble's outputs) better capture the multi-modal nature of real-world problems. The key technical contribution is a stochastic optimization approach that avoids the costly retraining required by previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) methods.

The paper builds on the MCL framework introduced by Guzman-Rivera et al. [8,9], which uses an alternating block coordinate descent approach to minimize oracle loss. The authors' main claim is that sMCL provides a practical alternative that trains all ensemble members concurrently without requiring multiple retraining cycles, while achieving better performance than existing baselines.

**Strengths:**
- The problem of producing diverse, ambiguous predictions is indeed important for many real-world perception tasks.
- The method is technically sound, leveraging standard SGD with a simple modification to the backpropagation step.
- The experiments across three different domains (image classification, semantic segmentation, image captioning) demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines.
- The paper clearly demonstrates how ensemble members develop interpretable specializations (e.g., classifiers focusing on specific classes, segmenters focusing on specific objects).

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper overstates the novelty of the approach. The "winner-takes-gradient" mechanism is conceptually similar to mixture-of-experts training and other ensemble methods that use selective gradient updates. The authors fail to sufficiently distinguish their contribution from prior work on selective training approaches.
- The claimed improvements (6-10% over baselines) are modest and do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods. For example, in image classification, the gains are marginal compared to standard ensemble approaches.
- The paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis to explain why sMCL leads to better diversity and performance. The connection between the stochastic optimization approach and the emergence of interpretable specializations is not well-justified.
- The experimental validation is limited. The paper compares against only three baselines (classical ensembles, MCL, and Dey et al. [5]), but fails to include more recent approaches to ensemble diversity that might be more directly relevant. The comparison against MCL is particularly weak since the authors acknowledge MCL would theoretically perform similarly but is "infeasible" to run for the same number of iterations.
- The paper doesn't adequately address limitations or failure cases. For instance, it doesn't explore how sMCL performs with very large ensemble sizes or on datasets with different levels of ambiguity.
- The paper claims sMCL is "parameter-free," but the experiments use specific hyperparameters (e.g., k in the "k-best" variant) that could significantly impact performance, suggesting the method may require careful tuning.

**Critical Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically plausible approach to training diverse ensembles, it falls short of demonstrating a significant methodological advance. The core idea—using a stochastic approach to avoid retraining in MCL—is not sufficiently novel or rigorously validated. The modest performance gains demonstrated in the experiments do not justify the claim that this represents a "broad applicability and efficacy" of sMCL for training diverse deep ensembles. The paper's emphasis on "interpretable emergent expertise" is somewhat anecdotal, with the analysis of specializations relying heavily on visual inspection of figures rather than quantitative measures.

The paper also fails to convincingly argue why this approach is necessary in the deep learning context. Many existing ensemble methods (e.g., bagging, boosting) already produce diverse outputs, and the authors don't clearly demonstrate that sMCL provides substantial advantages over these established techniques.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and presents a technically sound method, it lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant publication at NIPS. The claims about the method's superiority are not adequately supported by the experimental evidence, and the paper fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from prior work in ensemble learning. The modest performance gains demonstrated do not represent a significant enough advance to justify the new methodological claim.

The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with existing ensemble diversity techniques, stronger theoretical justification for the approach, and a more critical assessment of the method's limitations. Without these improvements, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.
