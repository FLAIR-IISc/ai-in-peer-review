

# Peer Review: Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel approach for training diverse deep ensembles that minimizes the oracle loss—defined as the loss achieved by an oracle selecting the best prediction from the ensemble. The work builds upon Guzman-Rivera et al.'s Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework [8,9], which minimizes oracle loss through an iterative alternating optimization approach. The key innovation of sMCL is its adaptation of this framework to stochastic gradient descent, avoiding the computationally expensive retraining steps required by MCL. Instead, sMCL employs a "winner-take-gradient" strategy where only the ensemble member with the lowest loss for each example receives gradient updates during training. The paper demonstrates the broad applicability of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing significant improvements over standard ensemble methods and MCL while being 5x faster.

## Strengths

1. **Practical and Well-Motivated Problem**: The paper addresses a meaningful problem in real-world applications where diverse predictions are essential (e.g., ambiguous evidence, implicit class confusion). The motivation is well-justified with compelling examples from computer vision and natural language processing.

2. **Technical Innovation**: While the core idea of minimizing oracle loss is not entirely new, the stochastic approach that avoids retraining is a significant practical advancement. The method is simple, generalizable to any SGD-trained model, and parameter-free, making it highly accessible.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The authors provide comprehensive results across three distinct tasks (classification, segmentation, captioning) with appropriate baselines. The oracle evaluation metrics appropriately measure the key contribution of the method. The qualitative analysis of emergent specializations (e.g., class-wise clustering in classification, segmentation specialists in segmentation) is particularly insightful.

4. **Clear Presentation of Emergent Specialization**: The paper demonstrates how sMCL automatically induces interpretable specializations in ensemble members without explicit design or initialization, which is a compelling contribution to understanding ensemble learning.

5. **Practical Impact**: The 5x speedup over MCL while achieving superior performance makes this method highly practical for real-world deep learning applications where training time is a major constraint.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper lacks theoretical justification for why the stochastic approach converges to good solutions. While the empirical results are strong, a brief analysis of convergence properties or comparison to the theoretical guarantees of MCL would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited MCL Comparison**: The paper acknowledges that retraining MCL ensembles for segmentation and captioning tasks proved infeasible, so they only report results after five meta-iterations. This makes it difficult to fully assess the relative performance gains of sMCL over MCL on more complex tasks.

3. **Scalability Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how performance scales with ensemble size beyond M=6. Understanding the diminishing returns of adding more ensemble members would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Comparison with Recent Ensemble Methods**: The paper compares against Dey et al. [5] and classical ensembles, but doesn't address other recent approaches to diverse ensemble learning (e.g., methods based on adversarial training or diversity regularization) that might be relevant.

5. **Implementation Details**: While the method is simple in concept, the paper could benefit from more specific implementation details (e.g., how the "winner" is determined in case of ties, how the gradient is computed exactly) to facilitate reproducibility.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a compelling and practical approach to training diverse deep ensembles that significantly outperforms existing methods while being computationally efficient. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of ensemble learning. The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the method's broad applicability.

The main weaknesses relate to the lack of theoretical analysis and limited comparison with MCL on more complex tasks. However, these do not substantially undermine the paper's core contribution, which is a practical and effective solution to a real problem.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and well-organized. I would recommend the authors to:
1. Add a brief theoretical discussion of the convergence properties of sMCL
2. Provide more details about how the "winner" is determined in case of ties during training
3. Extend the comparison with MCL by including additional meta-iterations for the segmentation and captioning tasks if feasible
4. Discuss potential limitations of the approach (e.g., performance on tasks with highly imbalanced label distributions)

The paper makes a significant contribution to ensemble learning that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners. The simplicity of the approach, combined with its strong empirical performance across multiple domains, makes it a promising direction for future work in training diverse deep learning models.
