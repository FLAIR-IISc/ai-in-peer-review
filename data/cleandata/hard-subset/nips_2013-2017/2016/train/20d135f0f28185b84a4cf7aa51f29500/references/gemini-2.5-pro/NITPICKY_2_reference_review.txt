**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a novel training algorithm for deep neural network ensembles. The goal is to generate a diverse set of high-quality predictions for a given input, which is particularly useful for ambiguous tasks in vision and language. The method optimizes the "oracle loss" of an ensemble, where the loss for a given example is defined by the error of the best-performing member of the ensemble. Building on the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) framework of Guzman-Rivera et al. [8], which is infeasible for deep networks due to its iterative retraining procedure, sMCL proposes a simple and efficient stochastic gradient descent-based alternative. During training, for each example in a mini-batch, the gradient of the loss is backpropagated only to the ensemble member that achieved the lowest error (the "winner"). The authors demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL across three tasks: image classification (CIFAR-10), semantic segmentation (PASCAL VOC), and image captioning (MSCOCO). The results show that sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other baselines in oracle performance, and that the method encourages the emergence of interpretable specialization among ensemble members.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of generating diverse outputs from deep models to handle ambiguity. The proposed sMCL algorithm is an elegant and novel adaptation of the MCL principle to the modern deep learning paradigm. It provides a practical solution to a problem where prior methods [5, 8] were computationally prohibitive.

2.  **Generality and Simplicity:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, easy to implement (described as a "winner-take-gradient" strategy), and broadly applicable. Its agnosticism to the specific network architecture and loss function is a major advantage, as demonstrated by the diverse range of experiments.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper validates its claims with a comprehensive set of experiments on three distinct and challenging tasks, utilizing different network architectures (CNN, FCN, CNN+RNN). The consistent and significant improvements in oracle metrics over baselines, particularly independently trained ensembles, provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The qualitative results and analysis of emergent specialization are insightful.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological inconsistencies, presentation flaws, and a lack of technical depth that undermine its quality.

1.  **Methodological Inconsistencies:**
    *   A central claim is that sMCL trains all members concurrently "to convergence once," avoiding costly retraining. However, in the semantic segmentation experiment (Sec 4.2), the models are initialized from a "standard ensemble trained for 50 epochs" and then fine-tuned with sMCL. This suggests sMCL might function better as a fine-tuning strategy rather than a from-scratch training method in complex settings, which significantly weakens the paper's headline claim of efficiency and simplicity. This critical detail is not adequately justified or discussed.
    *   The method is described as "parameter-free" in the introduction and conclusion. However, Section 4.1 introduces an experiment where the constraint is loosened to penalize the `k` lowest-error predictors, directly introducing a hyperparameter `k`. This experiment, while interesting, contradicts the "parameter-free" assertion.
    *   The comparison to beam search in the image captioning experiment (Sec 4.3) is not on equal footing. Beam search is an inference-time technique for a single model, while sMCL trains an ensemble. A more appropriate comparison would be to report the oracle score over all sentences generated by applying beam search to *each member* of the sMCL ensemble. The claim that "increased beam width could provide similar benefits for sMCL ensembles" is speculative without empirical evidence.

2.  **Lack of Technical Rigor:**
    *   The paper frames sMCL as "stochastic block gradient descent," but this connection is not formally established. The derivation in Eq. 2 shows the gradient of an upper-bound objective, but there is no analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties or theoretical guarantees. The work remains a purely empirical contribution based on a heuristic, albeit an effective one.
    *   Minor details are glossed over. For instance, the tie-breaking rule for selecting the "winner" is described as "arbitrary," but its potential impact is never discussed.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The manuscript suffers from a general lack of polish expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   The reference section is highly unprofessional. The list is not ordered alphabetically or by citation number, and entries are inconsistently formatted. Some lack publication years or venues (e.g., [13] is just a URL). This level of carelessness is concerning.
    *   The claim of a "5x speedup" over MCL is based on an arbitrary choice of 5 meta-iterations for the baseline. While MCL is certainly slower, presenting this as a fixed multiplicative factor is misleading.
    *   The footnote on the first page ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") appears to be a leftover template artifact.
    *   In the captioning experiment, the use of a single shared CNN for all LSTM models due to "memory restrictions" is an experimental confounder that is not adequately discussed. It is unclear how specialization can be fully achieved when the primary feature extractor is shared and updated with an aggregated gradient.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a simple, novel, and highly effective method for training diverse deep ensembles.
*   The problem it addresses is significant and relevant to the NIPS community.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrated across a variety of challenging tasks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper contains significant methodological inconsistencies, particularly the need for pre-training in the segmentation experiment and the contradiction of the "parameter-free" claim.
*   The work lacks theoretical analysis, making it a purely heuristic contribution.
*   Several experimental comparisons are weak or not rigorously controlled (e.g., beam search, MCL on segmentation).
*   The manuscript suffers from numerous and severe presentation flaws, most notably an unacceptable reference list, which suggests a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision**

While the core idea of sMCL is promising and the empirical results are strong, the paper in its current form falls short of the high standards for NIPS. The combination of methodological inconsistencies, lack of theoretical depth, and numerous presentation errors is too substantial to overlook. The work requires a major revision to address these shortcomings. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
