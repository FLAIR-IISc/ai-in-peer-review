Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the “multiple predictions” setting where a system outputs M hypotheses per input and performance is measured by oracle loss (min over the M losses). Prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) alternates between assigning examples to the lowest-loss model and retraining models on their assigned partitions—impractical for deep nets. The authors propose Stochastic MCL (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” scheme: for each example in a minibatch, compute the loss for each of the M learners and backpropagate the gradient only through the current lowest-loss learner (ties broken arbitrarily). This is an online block-coordinate analog of MCL that trains all ensemble members jointly, is parameter-free, and is agnostic to architecture and task loss.

Experiments span CIFAR-10 classification (CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM), reporting oracle metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy, best IoU, oracle CIDEr). sMCL improves oracle performance over independently trained ensembles and over two prior approaches: (i) alternating MCL with retraining; and (ii) a boosting-like sequential method that reweights examples for marginal oracle gain. The paper analyzes emergent specialization: in classification, heads become class specialists; in segmentation, specialization is more nuanced (shapes/frequency bias); in captioning, heads generate more diverse n-grams while improving oracle CIDEr.

The work is related to: MCL for structured prediction (Guzman-Rivera et al., NIPS ’12/AISTATS ’14), M-best diverse inference in graphical models (Batra et al., ECCV ’12; Kirillov et al., NIPS/ICCV), ensemble diversity methods (bagging, negative correlation), mixtures-of-experts and conditional computation. The key novelty is a practical SGD-based optimization for deep ensembles under oracle loss, avoiding expensive retraining and generalizing across architectures.

Strengths
- Simple, general, parameter-free algorithm that integrates seamlessly with SGD and backprop.
- Strong empirical evidence across three tasks/architectures; consistent and sizable oracle gains over reasonable baselines.
- Good analysis of emergent expertise and a useful k-winners ablation connecting specialization and oracle performance.
- Clear motivation for oracle metrics in applications with downstream oracles (human, verification).
- Practical contribution: concurrent training avoids repeated retraining; speedups over MCL reported.

Weaknesses and questions
- Fairness/compute accounting: For segmentation, sMCL is initialized from a standard ensemble and then fine-tuned. Are all baselines allowed the same total compute and initialization stages? Please clarify training budgets, wall-clock, and epochs across methods, and report comparable runtimes for all.
- Stability and “dead experts”: Winner-take-gradient can starve heads early. Did the authors observe head collapse? Any heuristics (warm-up, temperature/softmin, random tie-breaking) used to mitigate this? An annealed soft assignment baseline (e.g., softmin with temperature) would help assess robustness.
- Diversity metrics: Beyond class-assignment histograms and n-gram counts, please report standardized diversity/disagreement metrics (e.g., pairwise prediction disagreement, self-BLEU for captions, pairwise IoU diversity), and the trade-off between per-head quality and ensemble oracle.
- Selection mechanism: Oracle metrics isolate the multi-hypothesis generator but do not ensure utility without a selector/reranker. It would strengthen the paper to show results with a simple downstream selection (e.g., a learned reranker or a simulated oracle with noisy access) to quantify end-to-end benefits.
- Missing baselines relevant to 2016: For captioning, compare to diverse beam search (e.g., Vijayakumar et al., NIPS 2016) and/or coverage-augmented decoding; for classification, compare to top-k training losses or multi-head sharing with diversity regularizers (negative correlation). For segmentation, a mixture-of-experts with learned gating would be a useful control.
- Theoretical perspective: The method is equivalent to hard EM/k-means-like assignments under the MCL upper bound; a short discussion on convergence behavior, non-differentiability (min), and conditions for equitable head utilization would be valuable.
- Reporting: Many references in the bibliography appear incomplete or malformed. Figures are central to claims (e.g., Fig. 4–7) but quantitative numbers, error bars, or variances across seeds are not provided in the text excerpt. Please include numeric results, confidence intervals, and multiple random seeds.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-motivated as an online approximation to MCL’s alternating scheme. The experimental evidence is convincing that the approach scales to deep nets and yields improved oracle metrics. Some concerns remain about baseline completeness, training budget fairness, and lack of statistical variance reporting.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The algorithm is easy to reproduce. Please fix the bibliography, add more training details (tie-breaking, any warm-up), and present numeric tables with standard deviations. A brief pseudocode box (Algorithm 2) is helpful; include it in the main text.

Originality
Moderate. The key idea—SGD-compatible hard assignment under oracle loss—is a natural yet nontrivial adaptation of prior MCL to deep learning. The contribution is primarily practical and unifying rather than deeply theoretical.

Significance
High practical significance: a broadly applicable recipe for training diverse deep ensembles for multi-hypothesis outputs, with demonstrated gains on three domains. The work is likely to be adopted in settings where oracle metrics matter.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, effective method with clear practical value.
- Consistent improvements over prior MCL-style and ensemble baselines; speedups in training.
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization and diversity.

Cons for rejection
- Missing key contemporaneous baselines (e.g., diverse decoding).
- Limited discussion of stability, head collapse, and fairness of compute budgets.
- Oracle-only evaluation without end-to-end selection results; limited diversity quantification.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a practical and impactful training strategy for diverse deep ensembles with solid empirical results. Addressing the baseline and reporting concerns would strengthen it further.

Reviewer confidence: High.
