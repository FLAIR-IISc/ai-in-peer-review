Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” training strategy for deep ensembles aimed at minimizing oracle loss: for each example in a minibatch, only the model with the lowest loss on that example receives gradient updates. This is a stochastic analogue of prior Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) that alternated between hard assignment and full retraining. sMCL is claimed to be architecture- and loss-agnostic, parameter-free (aside from ensemble size M), and much faster than prior MCL since all members train concurrently. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (with FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM) show oracle metric gains over independent ensembles, Dey et al. (submodular/boosting-like), and a limited MCL baseline, and show emergent specialization across ensemble members. The method is related to classic hard-EM/mixture-of-experts without a gating network, MCL for structured prediction, and diverse M-best inference; it differentiates itself primarily by a practical SGD-based training recipe for deep models.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation is straightforward—hard assignments imply that the gradient for a sample flows only to the current best expert. This mirrors hard mixture-of-experts with a non-parametric “gating” based on instantaneous loss. However, the paper lacks analysis of convergence, stability, or conditions under which assignment collapse/imbalance occurs. Tie-breaking, early-stage noise, and gradient starvation are important but not studied in depth.
- Empirical support: Results are given across three tasks with oracle metrics. Gains over independent ensembles and Dey et al. are consistent; MCL (5 meta-iterations) trails sMCL, with the argument that more iterations are infeasible. However, several aspects limit confidence: (i) mostly small/older architectures (CIFAR-10 Quick, FCN for VOC11); (ii) heavy reliance on oracle metrics without downstream selection/reranking experiments; (iii) insufficient compute-parity controls (e.g., segmentation uses fine-tuning from a trained ensemble for sMCL but not clearly for baselines); (iv) no variance or statistical significance across runs; and (v) in captioning, a single model with larger beam sometimes achieves higher oracle scores than sMCL, undercutting the main claim.
- Missing baselines: A major omission is comparison to mixture-of-experts with a learned gating function (e.g., classical Jordan & Jacobs), which is a well-established way to induce specialization and could be trained end-to-end with SGD. A softmin/temperature-controlled variant of the min (log-sum-exp) would also be a natural, differentiable baseline. Diversity-driven inference methods for captioning beyond beam search (e.g., diverse decoding) are also relevant.
- Ablations: Limited exploration of design choices. The “k-winners” variant is interesting but lacks systematic evaluation across tasks; there is no study of tie-breaking, annealing, or assignment regularizers to avoid expert starvation or promote balanced coverage.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The connection to prior MCL and the SGD-based reinterpretation is easy to follow. Nonetheless, several details would benefit from clarification:
- Exact training budgets for sMCL vs. baselines to ensure compute parity.
- Tie-breaking policy and its effect; any annealing/warmup to prevent early collapse.
- Degree of assignment imbalance across experts and its evolution during training (beyond a single histogram).
- For captioning, decoding settings for all methods (beam width, length normalization, etc.) and stronger diversity metrics beyond unique n-grams (e.g., pairwise BLEU, self-CIDEr).

Originality
The core idea—hard-assigning each example to its current best model and backpropagating only there—is conceptually close to existing MCL and to hard mixture-of-experts. The contribution is mainly a practical, concurrent SGD instantiation for deep nets and the empirical observation that it induces specialization. This is a meaningful engineering improvement for deep learning practice, but the methodological novelty is incremental rather than fundamental.

Significance
If robust, a simple, drop-in recipe to train diverse deep ensembles for oracle performance is practically useful. However, the paper’s reliance on oracle metrics, relatively small-scale settings, missing MoE baselines, and limited analysis make it difficult to assess broader impact. Demonstrations on larger-scale, modern architectures and downstream selection pipelines would strengthen the case that practitioners should adopt sMCL.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easy to implement; trains all experts concurrently.
- Consistent improvements in oracle metrics across tasks; emergent, interpretable specialization.
- Clear exposition and positioning relative to prior MCL and diversity work.
- Potentially valuable for applications needing multiple hypotheses.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to MCL and hard MoE; no theoretical analysis.
- Missing comparison to mixture-of-experts with learned gating; no softmin/annealed variant baseline.
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited downstream evaluation and diversity quantification.
- Compute-parity and reproducibility concerns; no multi-seed statistics.
- Some baselines underpowered or not tuned (e.g., captioning beam sizes), and small/dated architectures.

Questions for the authors
1. How does sMCL compare to a mixture-of-experts with a gating network trained end-to-end? Can you include this baseline?
2. Have you tried a softmin/log-sum-exp surrogate with temperature annealing to reduce assignment noise early on?
3. What safeguards exist against expert collapse or extreme imbalance? Can you report assignment entropy and utilization over epochs?
4. Are improvements maintained with stronger backbones (e.g., ResNets) and larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet)?
5. Can you provide downstream selection/reranking results and statistical significance over multiple seeds?
6. For captioning, can you match decoding strength across methods (beam widths, length penalties) and add stronger diversity metrics?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and simple method with consistent oracle gains and interpretable specialization.
- Concurrent training addresses a real limitation of prior MCL approaches.
- Broad applicability across tasks and architectures.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; close to known hard assignment/EM/MoE ideas.
- Incomplete baselines (notably MoE), lack of theory, and limited analysis of stability/imbalance.
- Evaluation leans on oracle metrics with small-scale models; downstream impact not convincingly demonstrated.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is appealing and results are promising, but the paper needs stronger baselines (MoE/gating, softmin), more rigorous experimental controls and analyses, and evidence beyond oracle metrics to justify publication at NIPS.

Confidence
High.
