Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the multiple-choice learning (MCL) setting, where a learner outputs M hypotheses and is evaluated with an oracle loss that selects the best among them. The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” SGD procedure: for each training example, compute the loss across M predictors and backpropagate only through the predictor with the minimum loss (ties broken arbitrarily). This interleaves the assignment and update steps of the original MCL formulation by Guzman-Rivera et al., avoiding repeated retraining and enabling practical training of deep ensembles. The authors demonstrate applicability across diverse architectures/tasks: CIFAR-10 classification, PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation with FCNs, and MSCOCO image captioning with CNN+LSTM. They report consistent oracle improvements over classical independent ensembles, the alternating MCL procedure, and the boosting-like method of Dey et al., with qualitative analyses showing emergent specialization (e.g., class-wise in classification, shape/frequency biases in segmentation, n-gram/style differences in captioning).

This work is well-situated with literature on ensembles (bagging, decorrelation, negative correlation learning), diverse M-best inference for structured prediction, and prior MCL/boosting formulations. Conceptually, sMCL is closely related to hard-EM and mixture-of-experts with hard gating, but without an explicit learned gate; it also connects to multi-hypothesis prediction and diverse decoding (e.g., n-best/beam search) at test time.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: a single loss modification enabling joint training of deep ensembles without meta-iterations or extra hyperparameters.
- Generality: applicable to any differentiable model/loss and demonstrated across three different modalities and architectures.
- Empirical gains: consistent oracle improvements (typically 6–10%) over strong baselines; faster than alternating MCL by avoiding retraining.
- Interpretability: clear analyses showing emergent specialization and a tunable k-winners variant that reveals a diversity–performance tradeoff.
- Relevance: addresses a widely-encountered need for multi-hypothesis sets in ambiguous perception tasks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation scope: Emphasis is exclusively on oracle metrics. While justified for the MCL setting, many applications require a downstream selector (learned re-ranker, human, or verification module). The paper would be stronger with at least one end-to-end study (e.g., simple learned selector or human-in-the-loop) to quantify practical gains.
- Baseline fairness/details: For segmentation, sMCL is initialized from an independently trained ensemble and fine-tuned; it is unclear if independent baselines received the same fine-tuning budget and learning-rate schedule. For captioning, sharing a single CNN across LSTMs due to memory may bias comparisons; please clarify whether baselines share the CNN similarly.
- Missing comparisons: In captioning, diverse beam search and other diversity-promoting decoding methods are natural baselines; the paper compares to varying beam widths but primarily with a single model. Similarly, multi-hypothesis prediction (winner-takes-all losses) and mixture-of-experts with hard/soft gating are highly related and merit discussion/contrast.
- Optimization dynamics: Winner-take-all updates can cause expert starvation/imbalance. The paper shows specialization emerges, but does not quantify assignment imbalance over training or propose remedies (e.g., load-balancing regularizers, temperature/annealing, soft assignments). Stability w.r.t. initialization, batch size, and M is not explored.
- Limited scale: Results are on CIFAR-10, VOC11 subset, and MSCOCO. It would be helpful to see larger-scale classification (e.g., ImageNet top-k oracle) or larger M to understand scalability and diminishing returns.

Quality
The method is technically sound and the empirical evidence supports the claims about oracle performance and emergent specialization. However, the paper would benefit from clearer experimental controls (training budgets, shared components) and a small ablation on tie-breaking, batch size, and k-winners. Runtime comparisons beyond the “5x faster than MCL” (e.g., wall-clock vs independent ensembles) would also help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The core idea and gradient derivation are straightforward. Including precise pseudo-code (Algorithm 2), explicit tie-breaking policy, and training details for each baseline would further improve reproducibility. The figures illustrating specialization are helpful.

Originality
The contribution is incremental conceptually but meaningful: casting MCL’s hard assignment into a stochastic, parallelizable training procedure for deep nets. The connection to hard-EM/mixture-of-experts is natural but not explicitly leveraged with a gate; the simplicity and breadth of applicability are the novelty.

Significance
Given the prevalence of ambiguous, structured prediction tasks, a practical, general method to train multi-hypothesis deep ensembles is useful. The oracle gains and qualitative diversity are convincing, and the approach is likely to be adopted.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general method with strong empirical gains across tasks.
- Clear practical advance over prior MCL/boosting approaches by enabling end-to-end deep training.
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization and a controllable diversity–accuracy tradeoff.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evaluation limited to oracle metrics without downstream selection; missing some key baselines (diverse decoding, related multi-hypothesis training).
- Limited analysis of optimization dynamics and robustness; potential expert starvation not addressed.
- Some ambiguity about fairness of training budgets and shared components in baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add at least one downstream selection experiment (simple learned selector or human study) to complement oracle metrics.
- Include comparisons to diverse beam search (for captions) and, if feasible, to a hard/soft mixture-of-experts with a learned gate.
- Report assignment distributions over time, expert utilization, and sensitivity to initialization/batch size/M; consider a lightweight load-balancing regularizer or annealed soft assignments to mitigate starvation.
- Clarify and equalize training budgets across baselines; specify whether CNN backbones are shared/frozen/fine-tuned identically.
- Provide additional diversity metrics (e.g., distinct-n, self-BLEU) and an ablation on k-winners.
- If space allows, add a larger-scale classification experiment (e.g., ImageNet top-5 oracle).

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: High
