Summary
The paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where an oracle selects the best of M predictions, and proposes a practical stochastic training scheme (sMCL) for deep networks. Instead of alternating between assignment and full retraining as in prior MCL work, sMCL integrates the “assign to lowest-loss predictor” step into SGD: for each example in a mini-batch, only the minimum-loss model receives gradient (“winner-take-gradient”). The approach is loss-agnostic and architecture-agnostic. Experiments on CIFAR-10 classification, Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (CNN+LSTM) report improved oracle metrics over independently trained ensembles and a boosting-like method; sMCL is comparable to (but much faster than) alternating MCL on small classification and claimed to outperform MCL on the more expensive tasks. The paper highlights emergent specialization of ensemble members.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: shows that the constrained MCL objective yields a simple per-example gradient routing rule suitable for SGD.
- Practical contribution: a simple, parameter-free training recipe implementable as a layer that routes gradients; plausible speed benefits over alternating retraining.
- Broad applicability: demonstrated on classification, segmentation, and captioning; qualitative analyses show interpretable specializations.
- Oracle-centric evaluation is appropriate for the stated use-case (downstream re-ranking/selection).

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: the method is essentially online hard-EM/competitive learning/hard mixture-of-experts without a learned gating network, applied to deep ensembles. The technical step from [Guzman-Rivera et al.] to “do the assignment inside SGD” is straightforward.
- Evaluation emphasizes oracle metrics only; little evidence that sMCL helps real downstream selection or that it preserves/improves single-prediction quality. For captioning, a single model with a wider beam achieves higher oracle scores at M=5, undercutting claims of superiority.
- Comparisons to MCL are inconclusive: on segmentation/captioning, MCL is reported infeasible beyond five meta-iterations, so the comparison appears undertrained. This weakens the “5x faster and better” claim.
- Methodological concerns are underexplored: potential for “dead” or monopolizing experts; sensitivity to initialization; batch size and tie-breaking effects; lack of balancing regularization; no quantitative diversity metrics beyond n-gram counts.
- Datasets/architectures are relatively small or not SOTA (CIFAR-10 Quick; FCN on VOC11 subset; neuraltalk2), limiting generality. No ImageNet-scale classification or stronger segmentation/captioning baselines.

Quality
- Soundness: The gradient routing derivation is correct for the upper-bound objective and aligns with competitive learning. However, the paper lacks analysis of convergence behavior, stability, or conditions preventing collapse (few experts winning most examples). The k-winner variant is briefly mentioned but not deeply studied.
- Empirics: Improvements over independent ensembles are plausible and sometimes sizable in oracle terms, but statistical significance and sensitivity analyses are missing. The captioning case shows beam search can exceed sMCL on the same oracle metric. The MCL baseline is not fully optimized on the heavy tasks, making claims about superiority premature.
- Fairness of baselines: Segmentation experiments warm-start sMCL from a trained ensemble; details on whether independent ensembles receive identical fine-tuning and compute budgets are not fully clear. Diversity-focused baselines for classification (e.g., negative correlation learning) are not included.

Clarity
- Generally clear and readable, with useful figures and qualitative examples. The algorithm is specified succinctly. Some important practical details are missing: tie-breaking policy, handling of dead experts, assignment smoothing/annealing, and compute budgets per method. The “parameter-free” claim overlooks the crucial choice of M and (in variant) k.

Originality
- The problem setting (MCL) and objective are not new. The novelty is a practical SGD instantiation of the alternating assignment/retraining loop for deep nets. This is a useful engineering contribution but relatively incremental conceptually, closely related to classic hard mixture-of-experts/competitive learning.

Significance
- If robust, sMCL could be adopted as a simple recipe to train multi-hypothesis deep models with improved oracle performance and emergent specialization. However, lack of stronger baselines, limited scale, and reliance on oracle metrics reduce the demonstrated impact. The absence of a learned selection mechanism limits immediate practical utility.

Questions for the authors
- How do you prevent collapse or dead experts? Do you observe experts that receive near-zero gradient? Any load-balancing regularizer or temperature-annealed soft assignments evaluated?
- How sensitive is sMCL to initialization, batch size, and tie-breaking? Please provide ablations and assignment entropy/load-balance statistics.
- For segmentation/captioning, can you provide a more equitable comparison to MCL (e.g., fewer but stronger meta-iterations with early stopping), or a wall-clock vs. performance curve for both?
- Can you report standard (non-oracle) metrics per member and averaged, to show that sMCL does not degrade single-prediction quality?
- For captioning, beyond unique n-grams, please include diversity metrics (e.g., self-BLEU/mBLEU) and human evaluation on diversity/quality trade-offs. Also compare against larger-beam ensembles and nucleus sampling.
- Can you include a learned gating baseline (mixture-of-experts) that selects one expert at test time, to assess practical deployability vs. oracle?

Pros
- Simple, general training trick; likely easy to reproduce.
- Consistent oracle improvements over naive ensembles across tasks; compelling qualitative specializations.
- Computationally attractive compared to alternating MCL.

Cons
- Conceptually incremental; close to known competitive/hard-EM ideas.
- Heavy reliance on oracle metrics; limited assessment of real-world utility or selection mechanisms.
- Incomplete/possibly unfair baselines (especially MCL) and limited-scale experiments.
- Missing analyses of stability, dead experts, and sensitivity.

Recommendation: Borderline reject
While the idea is practical and the results are suggestive, the contribution feels incremental relative to prior MCL and hard mixture-of-experts, and the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough (in terms of baselines, scale, and analyses) to justify acceptance. Strengthening the comparisons (especially to a stronger MCL and to learned gating MoE/beam search), adding robustness analyses, and demonstrating benefits beyond oracle metrics would substantially improve the case for publication. Confidence: high.
