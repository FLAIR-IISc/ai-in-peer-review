Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses Multiple Choice Learning (MCL), where a model outputs M predictions and is evaluated by oracle loss (min over predictions). The core contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple SGD-compatible “winner-take-gradient” procedure: for each training example, only the ensemble member with lowest loss on that example receives gradient updates. This is derived by viewing MCL’s upper-bound formulation with binary assignment variables and taking gradients that are nonzero only for the argmin predictor. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, requires no additional hyperparameters, and trains all ensemble members concurrently.

Empirically, sMCL is demonstrated on three tasks/architectures: CIFAR10 classification (small CNN), Pascal VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO image captioning (VGG16 encoder + LSTM). The paper compares against: (i) independently trained ensembles, (ii) the original alternating MCL (with few meta-iterations due to cost), and (iii) a sequential submodular/boosting-style method (Dey et al.). sMCL improves oracle metrics consistently and substantially versus independent ensembles and Dey et al., and matches or exceeds MCL while being ~5× faster. The paper also analyzes emergent specialization (e.g., class-wise specialization in CIFAR10 and more nuanced shape/class tendencies in segmentation) and explores a k-winners variant interpolating between oracle training (k=1) and standard ensembles (k=M). In captioning, sMCL increases oracle CIDEr-D and diversity (unique n-grams) relative to baselines; it also compares to beam search from a single model.

This work is closely related to prior MCL formulations (Guzman-Rivera et al.) and diverse M-best inference for structured models, but those methods either require retraining or sequential training and do not scale to modern deep nets. Conceptually, the approach also resembles hard-EM or hard mixture-of-experts (winner-take-all), but applied to optimizing oracle loss across an ensemble without a learned gating network.

Strengths
- Simple, general, and easily implementable training rule that integrates seamlessly with SGD-based deep nets.
- Broad empirical validation across distinct tasks and architectures, with consistent oracle improvements and clear speed advantages over alternating MCL.
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization and a controlled k-winners ablation linking specialization to oracle performance.
- Practical significance for applications where top-k or oracle metrics matter (e.g., ambiguous structured outputs).

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical analysis: no convergence guarantees or characterization of conditions under which winner-take-gradient avoids collapse/starvation.
- Some evidence of required warm-starting (e.g., segmentation starts from independently trained models), suggesting stability/training dynamics deserve deeper study.
- Evaluation is primarily in oracle metrics; downstream selection/reranking or human-in-the-loop utility is not demonstrated.
- Comparisons to alternative ensemble/diversity mechanisms (e.g., learned gating in mixture-of-experts, entropy/diversity regularization, snapshot ensembles) are not included; MCL baseline is constrained by computational budget, complicating fairness.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The gradient rule follows directly from the MCL upper-bound objective with hard assignments; using per-batch assignments yields a plausible stochastic approximation. However, the non-smooth, discrete assignment and arbitrary tie-breaking raise questions about convergence and stability. Potential failure modes (e.g., one model monopolizing gradients) are not theoretically addressed; empirically, some tasks are warmed up with independent training, which helps but is not analyzed systematically.
- Empirical support: Multi-domain experiments are convincing for oracle metrics, with qualitative diversity and specialization analyses. Exact numeric gains, training time comparisons, and variability (multiple runs, confidence intervals) should be reported more completely to assess robustness. The k-winners ablation is useful and supports the central claim that strong specialization correlates with oracle improvements.
- Fairness of baselines: Alternating MCL is only run for five meta-iterations; acknowledging that fully converged MCL is infeasible for deep nets supports the paper’s practicality argument, but it also means the accuracy comparison is not fully controlled. Captioning comparisons include beam search from a single model; combining beam search with sMCL would be informative.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivation and algorithm are easy to follow, and task-specific setups are described. It would help to:
  - Provide pseudocode for the sMCL backward pass and tie-breaking policy.
  - Detail initialization strategies (from-scratch vs warm-start), and report how they affect convergence/diversity.
  - Include full numeric tables (means and standard deviations), not just plots, and wall-clock training times per setting.

Originality
- Extends MCL to deep networks with a concurrent SGD-based algorithm that is practically viable; to the best of my knowledge at the time, this is novel and nontrivial. While related to hard-EM and mixture-of-experts ideas, optimizing oracle loss across an ensemble without gating and doing so in a scalable SGD manner constitutes a distinct and useful contribution.

Significance
- The method is likely to be adopted because of its simplicity, breadth of applicability, and relevance to common top-k/oracle metrics. The empirical gains and speedups over alternating MCL are meaningful in practice. The work can influence subsequent research on multi-hypothesis prediction and diversity-promoting training for deep ensembles.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Training dynamics: Do you observe model starvation or collapse without warm-starting? Can you report assignment entropy over time and the effect of different initializations or batch sizes?
- Balancing: Have you tried mild balancing terms (e.g., entropy regularization over assignments, temperature-softmin) to encourage non-degenerate specialization?
- Captioning: Report results with beam search on sMCL ensembles and analyze diversity vs oracle trade-offs.
- Comparisons: Discuss relationships to hard mixture-of-experts with learned gating and winner-take-all/multiple-hypothesis training; if feasible, include a gating baseline.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general algorithm with broad applicability and clear empirical benefits.
- Addresses a practical bottleneck (costly retraining) of prior MCL methods.
- Compelling analyses of emergent specialization and diversity.
- Strong alignment with tasks where multiple plausible outputs are essential.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical grounding of training dynamics and convergence.
- Heavily oracle-focused evaluation without downstream selection results.
- Some baselines (e.g., fully converged MCL, gating-based MoE) are missing, and warm-start requirements suggest potential stability issues.

Overall recommendation
A solid and practical contribution that advances training of diverse deep ensembles for oracle loss. Despite limited theory and some missing baselines, the method’s simplicity, breadth, and empirical gains warrant acceptance.
