Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles Multiple Choice Learning (MCL): training an ensemble to minimize oracle loss, i.e., the loss of the best prediction among M outputs. The main contribution is Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a simple “winner-take-gradient” procedure compatible with SGD: for each training example, only the predictor with the lowest loss receives gradients (ties broken arbitrarily). This can be seen as a stochastic block gradient descent approximation to the MCL objective, which replaces the min with indicator variables. The method is architecture- and loss-agnostic, requires no additional hyperparameters, and trains all ensemble members concurrently.

The work is positioned against prior MCL approaches that require alternating assignment and full retraining (Guzman-Rivera et al.), boosting-like methods that train members sequentially (Dey et al.), and classical ensembles that rely on independent training or decorrelation tricks. It is also closely related to hard-EM/mixture-of-experts with hard gating and to diverse M-best for structured prediction, but the proposed approach is tailored to deep nets trained with SGD.

Experiments span three domains and architectures: CIFAR-10 classification (small CNN), PASCAL VOC semantic segmentation (FCN), and MSCOCO captioning (CNN+LSTM with beam search). Across tasks, sMCL improves oracle metrics versus independent ensembles and the sequential methods, and is reported to be roughly 5× faster than the retrain-based MCL baseline. The authors further analyze emergent specialization: in classification, clear class-level specialization arises; in segmentation, specialization is more nuanced (shape/frequency biases); in captioning, models produce more diverse n-grams and sentences.

Quality
Technical soundness: The derivation is straightforward and correct: the gradient of the MCL upper-bound wrt fm(xi) reduces to the single-model gradient for the argmin predictor and zero for others (Eq. 2), yielding a simple SGD-compatible training rule. This is a sensible stochastic approximation to the alternating optimization in MCL and is analogous to hard gating in mixture-of-experts. The algorithm is easy to implement.

Empirical support: Experiments cover multiple tasks and architectures and compare to relevant baselines (independent ensembles, retrain-based MCL, and the boosting-like method). Reported gains in oracle metrics (top-k, oracle IoU, oracle CIDEr-D) are consistent and non-trivial (typically 6–10% over independent ensembles). The ablation with k-winners shows a clear accuracy–specialization trade-off, supporting the design choice of k=1. Training curves indicate faster convergence on oracle loss.

Limitations:
- Evaluation focuses almost exclusively on oracle metrics. For many applications, downstream selection/reranking matters; reporting calibration, diversity metrics (beyond unique n-grams), or downstream selection performance would strengthen the case. It would also help to report single-prediction performance per member and the union’s standard top-1 performance to understand trade-offs.
- The MCL baseline is trained for five meta-iterations; authors acknowledge more iterations may help but were infeasible. This makes the speed comparison compelling but leaves some uncertainty about the ultimate accuracy gap.
- No analysis of stability/sensitivity: winner-take-all can starve some members. The paper would benefit from reporting the distribution of assignments over time, failure cases, and sensitivity to ensemble size M, batch size, initialization, and data imbalance.
- No theoretical convergence analysis (reasonable for a practical method, but worth noting).
- Some comparisons could be tighter for captioning: a single model with larger beam width can match or surpass oracle performance while being less diverse; comparing to diverse decoding (if contemporary) or using equalized beam search settings for all methods would clarify trade-offs.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The problem setting, objective, and algorithm are clearly explained, with an intuitive justification and simple gradient expression. The experimental setup is described at a useful level, though some details are sparse (e.g., exact pseudocode for sMCL layer, tie-breaking policy, assignment statistics over training, compute overhead from evaluating M models per batch). Figures illustrating specialization and diversity are helpful.

Originality
The core idea builds directly on MCL and hard-EM/mixture-of-experts style gating, but the contribution of a practical SGD-based training procedure that avoids costly retraining and works across modern deep architectures is novel and useful. Relative to prior MCL/diverse M-best/boosting-like sequential methods, concurrent SGD training for oracle loss is a meaningful advance. The idea is simple, but that is a strength here.

Significance
Producing diverse, high-quality sets of hypotheses is important for many perception tasks and aligns with common evaluation practices (e.g., top-k). The method is simple, broadly applicable, and likely to be adopted. The observed emergent specialization is interesting and may inspire follow-on work (e.g., learned gating, capacity allocation, or curriculum). The main caveat is the heavy reliance on oracle metrics without downstream evaluation; nonetheless, the technique fills a practical gap and advances the state of the art for training diverse deep ensembles.

Pros
- Simple, general, and practical algorithm with a clear connection to the MCL objective.
- Concurrent training; no retraining or sequential stages; parameter-free.
- Consistent improvements on three tasks/architectures; faster convergence and better oracle loss.
- Insightful analysis of emergent specialization; k-winners ablation clarifies behavior.

Cons
- Evaluation centered on oracle metrics; limited downstream or calibration/diversity analysis.
- MCL baseline strength bounded by feasible meta-iterations; fairness caveat.
- Potential model starvation and sensitivity not thoroughly analyzed.
- Captioning comparisons to strong diverse decoding baselines are limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report assignment distributions over training, per-member utilization, and sensitivity to M.
- Include downstream selection or calibrated confidence experiments; report standard accuracy trade-offs.
- Strengthen captioning comparisons (e.g., diverse beam search) and align beam settings across methods.
- Provide implementation pseudocode for the sMCL layer and clarify tie-breaking.
- Discuss strategies to prevent starvation (e.g., epsilon-greedy assignments, temperature-soft min) and possibly include a soft variant.

Overall recommendation
Accept. A well-motivated, technically sound, and practically impactful contribution that makes MCL feasible for deep ensembles, with solid empirical evidence and clear writing. The simplicity and generality outweigh the noted evaluation limitations.
