- Proposes sMCL, a stochastic gradient method that trains diverse deep ensembles by assigning gradients to the lowest-error predictor to optimize oracle loss.  
- Builds on MCL but replaces costly alternating training with a "winner-take-gradient" SGD update (Equation 2).  
- Empirical evaluation across CIFAR10, Pascal VOC, and MSCOCO shows consistent oracle-metric improvements over baselines (classical ensembles, MCL, Dey et al.).  
- Derivation of the gradient update is correct and the experimental design is comprehensive with clear presentation of results.  
- Theoretical grounding is weak: insufficient justification that sMCL converges to the same solution as MCL.  
- Comparison with MCL is potentially unfair because MCL was not run to convergence for complex tasks (only 5 meta-iterations).  
- The impact of tie-breaking strategies in the winner-take-gradient approach is not explored.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and provides accessible implementation details (Algorithm 2).  
- Figures effectively illustrate emergent specialization among ensemble members.  
- Novelty is limited in context of broader ensemble diversity literature and lacks deep engagement with prior diversity-inducing methods.  
- Practical significance is strong: sMCL is ~5x faster than MCL and yields 6–10% improvements across tasks.  
- The paper does not convincingly argue superiority over simpler independent ensembles (random seeds).  
- Strengths: practical efficiency, strong cross-domain empirical results, automatic specialization, and simple architecture-agnostic implementation.  
- Weaknesses: unfair MCL comparison, insufficient theoretical justification, superficial related-work discussion on diversity, and unaddressed tie-breaking pitfalls.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, asking for better theoretical comparison, fairer MCL experiments, expanded related work on diversity, and analysis of tie-breaking strategies.
