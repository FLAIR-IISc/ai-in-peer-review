This paper introduces a compelling and novel approach to training deep ensembles – Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). The core idea of directly optimizing for oracle loss during ensemble training, combined with a clever stochastic gradient descent implementation, is a significant contribution. While the experimental validation isn’t exhaustive, the consistent performance gains across diverse architectures (CNNs, FCNs, CNN+RNNs) and tasks (image classification, segmentation, captioning) strongly suggest a promising direction. The authors successfully sidestep the computational bottlenecks of previous Multiple Choice Learning approaches, making it practical for deep networks.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the work – the limitations of single-prediction learners in ambiguous scenarios. The connection to human reasoning (multiple guesses) is a helpful framing device. The mathematical formulation is concise and the algorithm description (Algorithm 2) is easy to follow. The inclusion of interpretable expertise analysis (showing specialization of ensemble members) adds significant value and insight. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the speedup over MCL is dependent on the number of meta-iterations chosen for MCL) and comparison to related work is thorough.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** sMCL is a genuinely novel approach to ensemble learning, directly addressing the oracle loss optimization problem in a scalable manner.
*   **Practicality:** The stochastic gradient descent formulation overcomes the limitations of previous MCL methods, making it applicable to modern deep learning architectures.
*   **Empirical Results:** Consistent performance gains across diverse tasks and network architectures provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
*   **Interpretability:** The analysis of ensemble member specialization adds valuable insight into the learning process.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the derivation of the gradient is sound, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of sMCL would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are diverse, the datasets used are relatively standard. Investigating performance on more challenging or less-explored datasets could further strengthen the findings.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  More detailed ablation studies could elucidate the impact of specific design choices (e.g., the effect of ensemble size, learning rate, etc.).
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to existing diversity-inducing techniques *applicable* to deep networks are included, a comparison to more sophisticated ensemble methods (e.g., those using adversarial training for diversity) might be insightful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel and impactful idea with strong empirical support.
*   sMCL addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional deep learning approaches – their struggle with ambiguity.
*   The method is practical and scalable, making it readily applicable to a wide range of problems.
*   The interpretable expertise analysis provides valuable insights into the learned representations.
*   The clear writing and organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is limited.
*   The experimental validation, while strong, could be further expanded.
*   The lack of comparison to certain advanced ensemble baselines.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a highly promising new approach to ensemble learning. While some minor weaknesses exist, the novelty, practicality, and strong empirical results significantly outweigh them. I believe this work has the potential to influence future research in ensemble learning and structured prediction. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should consider adding a section discussing limitations and future work, including potential avenues for theoretical analysis and more extensive experimentation.
