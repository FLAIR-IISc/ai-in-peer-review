### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of training deep learning models to produce a diverse set of plausible outputs, rather than a single prediction. This is particularly useful for tasks with inherent ambiguity, where a single output might be an undesirable average of multiple modes in the data distribution. The authors frame this problem within the Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) paradigm, where the goal is to train an ensemble of `M` models to minimize the "oracle loss"—the loss of the best-performing model in the ensemble for each given input.

The paper identifies a key limitation of prior work on MCL [8, 9], which uses a block coordinate descent algorithm requiring models to be retrained to convergence multiple times. This iterative retraining is computationally infeasible for modern deep networks. To overcome this, the paper introduces its primary contribution: Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL). sMCL is a novel, efficient training algorithm based on stochastic gradient descent (SGD). For each example in a mini-batch, sMCL computes the forward pass and loss for all `M` ensemble members. During the backward pass, however, it backpropagates the gradient of the loss only through the single model that achieved the lowest error on that example. This "winner-take-gradient" approach allows all ensemble members to be trained concurrently in a single training run.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and broad applicability of sMCL across three different domains: image classification on CIFAR-10 with CNNs, semantic segmentation on Pascal VOC with FCNs, and image captioning on MSCOCO with a CNN+RNN architecture. In all cases, sMCL outperforms independently trained ensembles and other relevant baselines in terms of oracle performance. A key finding is that this training procedure automatically encourages interpretable, task-dependent specialization among the ensemble members, such as specializing in different object classes (classification), segmentation styles (segmentation), or sentence structures (captioning).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed sMCL algorithm is a well-motivated and direct adaptation of the MCL objective function for SGD-based optimization. The derivation in Equation 2 clearly justifies the "winner-take-gradient" update rule. The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on three distinct and challenging tasks, using appropriate deep learning architectures for each. The evaluation uses suitable oracle metrics (accuracy, IoU, CIDEr) and includes both quantitative and compelling qualitative results that effectively demonstrate the increased diversity and oracle performance of sMCL ensembles. The analysis of emergent specialization, particularly the ablation study in Figure 4 that varies the number of "winning" predictors, provides valuable insight into the mechanism and is a strength of the paper.

A minor weakness is the lack of clarity on certain experimental details. For instance, the semantic segmentation models are initialized from a pre-trained standard ensemble before being fine-tuned with sMCL. It is unclear if this is a necessary step for stability or convergence, or simply for efficiency. A discussion of performance when training from scratch would strengthen the results. Similarly, in the image captioning experiment, the performance of sMCL is slightly below that of a single model with a large beam search width in terms of the oracle CIDEr score, although it produces more diverse outputs. While the authors note this trade-off, it slightly qualifies the claims of superior performance on this task.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides clear motivation for the problem with illustrative examples. The proposed sMCL method is explained with simplicity and precision, both mathematically and algorithmically. The figures and tables are informative and effectively communicate the main results. The qualitative examples in Figures 6 and 7 are particularly helpful in visualizing the diversity generated by the sMCL ensembles compared to baselines. The overall narrative is easy to follow for readers familiar with deep learning and ensemble methods. A small suggestion for improvement would be to provide a diagram for the shared CNN architecture used in the image captioning experiment, as the textual description is slightly difficult to parse.

**Originality:** The core idea of minimizing an oracle loss via MCL was proposed in previous work [8]. The primary novelty of this paper lies in developing a practical and efficient algorithm, sMCL, that makes this learning paradigm feasible for large-scale deep neural networks. Prior methods based on retraining are not scalable, and this work provides the first practical SGD-based solution for concurrently training a deep ensemble directly on the oracle loss. This adaptation is non-trivial and represents a significant methodological contribution. The demonstration of emergent, task-specific specialization as a direct consequence of the objective is also a novel and insightful finding.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem. In many real-world applications, from autonomous systems to assistive technologies, generating a set of high-quality hypotheses is more valuable than producing a single, potentially flawed prediction. The proposed sMCL algorithm is simple, general (architecture and loss-agnostic), parameter-free, and efficient. Its ease of implementation—requiring only a modification to the gradient flow during backpropagation—makes it highly likely that the method will be adopted by researchers and practitioners. The work opens promising directions for future research, including exploring softened versions of the winner-take-all rule or applying the concept to other areas like generative modeling. A potential limitation, not fully discussed, is the increased computational cost (memory and inference time) associated with running an M-member ensemble, which could be a barrier in resource-constrained settings.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper proposes a novel, simple, and effective algorithm (sMCL) to solve the important problem of training diverse deep ensembles.
*   **Pro:** sMCL provides a significant practical improvement over prior MCL methods, making the oracle loss objective feasible for modern deep learning.
*   **Pro:** The claims are backed by strong and comprehensive experiments across three distinct and relevant tasks, demonstrating wide applicability.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the emergent specialization behavior of the trained models.
*   **Pro:** The method's simplicity and generality make it likely to have a significant impact and be widely used.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The core conceptual framework (MCL) is not new; the contribution is primarily an algorithmic one for adapting it to deep learning.
*   **Con:** The computational and memory overhead of training and deploying `M` models is a practical limitation that is not explicitly addressed in the discussion.
*   **Con:** A few experimental details, such as the necessity of pre-training for the segmentation task, could be clarified to further strengthen the empirical claims.
