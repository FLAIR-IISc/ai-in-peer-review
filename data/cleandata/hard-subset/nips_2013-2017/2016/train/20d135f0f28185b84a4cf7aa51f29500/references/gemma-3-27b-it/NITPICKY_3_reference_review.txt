## Review of "Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning for Training Diverse Deep Ensembles"

This paper introduces Stochastic Multiple Choice Learning (sMCL), a method for training diverse deep ensembles by directly minimizing oracle loss through a stochastic gradient descent approach. The core idea – modifying the gradient update to only backpropagate loss through the best-performing ensemble member for each example – is conceptually simple and aims to address the computational cost of previous Multiple Choice Learning (MCL) approaches. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of sMCL across image classification, semantic segmentation, and image captioning tasks, showing improvements over baseline ensembles and comparison to existing MCL implementations.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical limitation of MCL:** The authors correctly identify the retraining bottleneck in previous MCL methods and provide a compelling solution that enables training deep ensembles concurrently.
* **Clear theoretical justification:** The derivation of the gradient update rule is straightforward and well-explained, linking it directly to the objective function and providing intuition for its behavior.
* **Empirical validation across diverse tasks:** Demonstrating the effectiveness of sMCL on CNNs, FCNs, and CNN+RNN architectures lends credibility to its general applicability.
* **Emergent specialization:** The observation of interpretable specialization among ensemble members (particularly in the classification setting) is a valuable and insightful result.
* **Parameter-free nature:** The method's lack of hyperparameters simplifies its application and reduces the risk of overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to modern diversity-inducing techniques:** While comparisons to independent ensembles and the original MCL are provided, the authors do not compare sMCL to more recent methods specifically designed for ensemble diversity, such as those employing techniques from adversarial training or meta-learning. Ignoring these methods weakens the claim of superiority.
* **Insufficient ablation study:** The experiments primarily focus on comparing sMCL to baselines as a whole. There’s a lack of ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components or design choices within sMCL itself. For example, the sensitivity to the batch size or the impact of ties during gradient selection isn’t thoroughly investigated.
* **Overstated claims of “automatic emergence”:** While specialization is observed, the authors consistently attribute this to “automatic emergence.” This phrasing feels overly strong. The method *encourages* specialization through the optimization objective, but doesn't guarantee it in all scenarios (as evidenced by the less pronounced specialization in the segmentation experiments).
* **Implementation details are sparse:** The paper refers to “the Caffe deep learning framework” and “neuraltalk2” without sufficient detail for easy reproducibility. While code may be available, the lack of specific version numbers and key configuration settings hinders independent verification.
* **Figure quality and presentation:** Several figures (e.g., 4b, 5a) have small font sizes, making them difficult to read. The figure captions sometimes lack sufficient detail to fully understand the presented data.
* **Minor inconsistencies in terminology:** The term “oracle performance” is used interchangeably with metrics like IoU and CIDEr-D, which represent *evaluations* of the oracle performance. This slight ambiguity should be clarified.
* **Redundancy in discussion of Related Work:** Certain sections within related work feel repetitive. The limitations of previous MCL approaches are reiterated multiple times.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

* **Tie-breaking during gradient selection:** The paper states ties are broken arbitrarily. Is this truly random, or is there a consistent deterministic tie-breaking rule? The choice could subtly influence the learned specializations.
* **Impact of ensemble size:** While results are shown for ensemble sizes up to 6, the scalability of sMCL to larger ensembles is not investigated.  Are there diminishing returns or practical limitations as M increases?
* **Hyperparameter tuning:**  The authors state sMCL is parameter-free, but the learning rate and other training parameters still require tuning. This qualification should be more prominently acknowledged.
* **Why is MCL performance so poor on segmentation/captioning?** The authors attribute this to MCL’s inability to reassign examples effectively.  A more detailed analysis of the limitations of alternating block coordinate descent in these contexts would be insightful.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant practical problem in ensemble learning.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated method.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results across diverse tasks.
* Offers insights into the emergence of specialization in deep ensembles.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lack of comparison to more recent diversity-inducing techniques.
* Insufficient ablation study and sensitivity analysis.
* Overstated claims regarding "automatic emergence" and generalization.
* Minor presentation and reproducibility concerns.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from a number of relatively minor but persistent flaws. The lack of comprehensive comparisons, the absence of ablation studies, and the slightly overstated claims detract from the overall impact. While the core idea of sMCL is valuable, the paper does not fully convince me that it represents a substantial advancement over existing techniques. 

**Recommendation:**  **Reject with encouragement to revise.**  The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by including more thorough comparisons to state-of-the-art diversity-inducing methods and conducting a more detailed ablation study. Improving the presentation and addressing the reproducibility concerns would also strengthen the paper significantly.
