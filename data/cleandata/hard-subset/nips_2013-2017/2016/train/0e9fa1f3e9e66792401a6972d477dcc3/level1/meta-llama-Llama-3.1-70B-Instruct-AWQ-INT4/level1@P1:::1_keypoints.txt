- Paper introduces a novel method using bidirectional Monte Carlo to measure reliability of MCMC inference by upper-bounding the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence.  
- Method runs AIS chains forward (prior→posterior) and reverse on simulated data to estimate an expected upper bound on divergence.  
- Authors integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan and validate it on multiple models and datasets.  
- Proposes a protocol for diagnosing inference on real data by inferring hyperparameters, simulating data, measuring inference quality on the simulated data, and checking consistency with real-data behavior.  
- Technical contribution 1: shows bidirectional Monte Carlo yields an estimator that upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples.  
- Technical contribution 2: provides a technique to exactly compute true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, showing the bound often matches well.  
- Technical contribution 3: proposes a practical protocol for using bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate approximate inference on real-world datasets.  
- Technical contribution 4: implements and validates the method in both WebPPL and Stan across various probabilistic models.  
- Strength: addresses a challenging problem—measuring MCMC reliability—with a novel, well-explained approach.  
- Strength: paper is well-written, well-organized, and balances theory and practice.  
- Strength: practical validation and tool integration into two PPLs strengthen reproducibility and applicability.  
- Weakness: assumes reader familiarity with MCMC and probabilistic programming, potentially limiting accessibility.  
- Weakness: could include more detailed derivations and explanations of the estimator and real-data protocol.  
- Weakness: lacks more extensive comparisons with existing methods for measuring MCMC reliability.  
- Originality: extends recent bidirectional Monte Carlo work to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference and proposes a real-data evaluation protocol.  
- Significance: offers a potentially impactful new way to quantify approximate posterior accuracy, relevant to probabilistic programming and ML.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear explanations and a balanced theoretical/practical evaluation.  
- Clarity: generally clear and easy to follow, with good explanations of technical aspects and the protocol.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, clear, well-organized method with practical implementations and potential significant impact.  
- Arguments for rejection: accessibility issues for less-expert readers, need for deeper technical details, and insufficient comparisons to prior methods.
