### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The method builds upon and extends the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique. The core idea of BDMC is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both a "forward" direction (from a simple prior to the complex posterior) and a "reverse" direction (from the posterior back to the prior). The forward run provides a stochastic lower bound on the log marginal likelihood (log-ML), while the reverse run, which requires an exact sample from the posterior, provides a stochastic upper bound.

The main theoretical contribution of this work is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the distribution of approximate samples generated by the forward AIS chain and the true posterior distribution. This provides a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality, moving beyond common but often unreliable heuristics for convergence diagnosis.

Since the method in its pure form requires an exact posterior sample, it is only rigorously applicable to simulated data. The authors address this limitation by proposing a practical protocol for real-world data: first, run approximate inference on the real data to find plausible hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated data to assess inference quality. The authors validate this protocol by showing that inference behavior on simulated data closely matches that on real data for several models.

Finally, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of BREAD by implementing it within two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They use it for a compelling case study comparing the effectiveness of collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations for matrix factorization, showing how BREAD can guide modeling decisions. They also report using BREAD to discover and fix a bug in WebPPL, highlighting its utility as a debugging tool.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in computational statistics: how to reliably assess the quality of MCMC samples. Traditional diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) can only detect certain kinds of non-convergence, whereas BREAD provides a rigorous, quantitative upper bound on a meaningful statistical divergence. This is a significant step forward. Its integration into Stan and WebPPL makes it a potentially high-impact tool for both researchers developing new inference methods and practitioners using PPLs for applied modeling.

2.  **Novel and Sound Contribution:** While the work is an extension of BDMC [GGA15], the connection between the log-ML bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and elegant theoretical insight. This repurposing of BDMC from a tool for evidence estimation to one for sample quality assessment is a key conceptual contribution. The derivations are clear and appear technically sound.

3.  **Excellent Experimental Validation:** The empirical work is thorough and convincing. The authors don't just present their method but also carefully validate its key assumptions. The experiments in Section 5.1 showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data, and that the reverse-chain results are robust to using an approximate posterior sample for initialization, are crucial for making the method practical.

4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The case study on model representation (Section 5.2.1) is an excellent demonstration of how BREAD can provide concrete, actionable insights that would be difficult to obtain otherwise. It quantitatively shows the trade-offs between different modeling choices in different PPLs. The anecdote about debugging WebPPL further strengthens the case for the method's usefulness.

5.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The background on AIS and BDMC is explained lucidly, the new derivations are presented clearly, and the experimental setup and results are well-motivated and described. The relationship to prior and concurrent work [CTM16] is also discussed honestly and clearly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Applicability of the Core Method:** The proposed bound is on the quality of samples from AIS (or similar methods like SMC), not on general MCMC algorithms like HMC or Metropolis-Hastings directly. While the authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS can inform the design of other MCMC methods (since they often share transition kernels), the diagnostic is not directly applicable to the output of a standard Stan (NUTS) run, for example. This limitation could be stated more explicitly.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The BREAD procedure is computationally intensive, requiring multiple runs of both forward and reverse AIS chains, where each chain itself involves many MCMC steps. This may limit its routine use, making it more of a tool for deep validation of a final model/sampler rather than a quick, iterative diagnostic during development.

3.  **Heuristic Nature on Real Data:** The proposed protocol for real-world data is pragmatic and well-justified empirically, but it breaks the rigorous mathematical guarantees of the method. Since the reverse chain is initialized from an *approximate* posterior sample, the resulting value is no longer a guaranteed stochastic upper bound on the log-ML, and therefore the gap is no longer a guaranteed upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The authors are transparent about this, but it remains a key limitation.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is of very high quality and makes a strong contribution. A minor point of clarification could be made regarding the use of multiple AIS chains. Footnote 3 notes that the theoretical analysis applies to the log of a single AIS weight, not the more common and lower-variance practice of averaging weights before taking the log. It would be interesting to know if the theoretical analysis could be extended to cover the log-of-average-weights case, which would more closely align the theory and practice.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that is well-suited for NIPS. It combines a neat theoretical idea with strong empirical validation and a clear demonstration of practical utility for the machine learning and statistics communities.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and unsolved problem in probabilistic inference.
*   Provides a novel, theoretically-grounded method (BREAD).
*   Demonstrates high practical value through PPL implementations and compelling case studies.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and technically sound.
*   High potential for impact on both inference research and applied probabilistic modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method's rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data.
*   The direct applicability is restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers.

The arguments for acceptance are significantly stronger. The paper is honest about its limitations and provides a well-reasoned, empirically-validated protocol for extending the method's utility to real-world scenarios. This is a solid piece of work that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
