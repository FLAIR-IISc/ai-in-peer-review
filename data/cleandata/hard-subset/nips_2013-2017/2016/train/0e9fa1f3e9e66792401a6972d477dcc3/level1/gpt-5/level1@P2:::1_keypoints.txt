- Paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior p(θ,z|y) and approximate sample distribution q for AIS/SMC-style samplers.
- The gap between forward AIS lower bound and reverse AIS upper bound gives, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence.
- Method (BREAD) is implemented in WebPPL and Stan and includes a practical protocol for applying the idea to real data via fitted-hyperparameter simulation.
- Empirical validation on five models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures) supports the protocol and includes a matrix-factorization case study.
- BREAD proved useful in guiding modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and detected a real implementation bug in WebPPL.
- Derivation is technically clear, relying on AIS-as-IS over an augmented space and KL contraction arguments with transparent assumptions.
- Practical utility is high due to integrations into mainstream PPLs and a pragmatic protocol bridging simulated and real data.
- Experiments analyze sensitivity to reverse-chain initialization and check distributional similarity between simulated and real AIS estimates.
- Main theoretical guarantee holds only in expectation; single-run gap estimates can be high-variance and heavy-tailed without concentration bounds.
- Rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior draw, so real-data application relies on a heuristic fitted-hyperparameter protocol without theoretical justification.
- Limited evaluation on hard targets: real-model studies lack ground-truth Jeffreys divergence, so bound tightness on challenging multimodal/high-D posteriors is not established.
- Applicability is restricted to samplers expressible as importance sampling on an extended space (AIS/SMC), excluding many common MCMC algorithms unless embedded in AIS/SMC.
- Reporting lacks sufficient detail on temperature schedules, kernel choices, and computational overhead; schedule misspecification effects are not systematically analyzed.
- Assessment: technically sound and clear, original extension of BDMC, with moderate-to-high significance despite expectations-only guarantees and heuristic real-data protocol.
- Suggested improvements: provide guidance on number of runs and variance reduction, report wall-clock vs. bound tradeoffs, analyze sensitivity to schedules/kernels, and strengthen or justify the fitted-hyperparameter protocol.
- Recommend additional experiments with cases where true Jeffreys divergence is known (e.g., controlled Gaussian or multimodal mixtures) to assess bound tightness.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled, practical diagnostic integrated into PPLs with demonstrated utility and nontrivial empirical studies.
- Arguments against acceptance: expectation-only guarantees, simulated-data requirement, limited evaluation on truly challenging inference settings, and lack of variance/concentration analysis.
- Recommendation: lean accept — meaningful, well-executed contribution with immediate practical use despite limitations.
