- Paper extends BDMC to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior p and AIS/SMC approximate sample distribution q.
- Key identities: E[log R̂] = log(ZT/Z1) − DKL(q||p) and E[log R̂rev] = log(Z1/ZT) − DKL(p||q), so E[gap] upper-bounds DJ(p||q).
- Proposes a practical real-data protocol: simulate datasets at fitted hyperparameters and initialize reverse chain from an approximate posterior.
- Implements method in WebPPL and Stan and validates on linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and GMMs.
- Builds on AIS, Jarzynski’s identity, SMC samplers, and BDMC; relates to concurrent “subjective divergence” work but focuses on Jeffreys divergence expectation.
- Derivations and core inequalities are correct and follow from standard AIS/importance-sampling arguments.
- Result E[log R̂rev − log R̂] ≥ DJ(p||q) is valid; paper is careful about expectation vs. high-probability statements.
- Main guarantee is an expectation bound; the paper lacks per-run guarantees on DJ from finite runs.
- Visualization of quartiles is insufficient; should report sample means and confidence intervals to reflect expectation guarantees.
- Method applies to AIS/SMC and extended-state importance samplers, not plain MCMC unless embedded in such schemes.
- Requiring an exact posterior sample for rigorous guarantees restricts formal certification to simulated data.
- The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data is heuristic but empirically supported by matching forward-AIS behavior.
- Clarify how adaptation (e.g., HMC/NUTS in Stan) was handled to ensure intermediate transitions leave pt invariant.
- Paper is well organized and readable; AIS/BDMC background and notation are clear and consistent.
- Suggested clarity improvements: state assumptions on kernels, provide guidance on estimating E[gap] (runs, variance, CIs), and report computational overhead.
- Extension to expected Jeffreys divergence is novel and practically useful; integration into two systems adds value.
- Approach is significant for evaluating approximate posterior quality and guiding inference/modeling choices.
- Strengths: correct derivations, practical real-data protocol, implementations in WebPPL/Stan, useful empirical insights, and careful related-work discussion.
- Weaknesses: expectation-only guarantees, heuristic real-data initialization, limited tightness evaluation on challenging/multimodal posteriors.
- Suggested experiments/clarifications: add CI estimation procedures, discuss failure modes for real-data protocol, test harder multimodal benchmarks, and clarify AIS kernel/adaptation details.
- Recommend comparisons to standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS) and Stein discrepancies to contextualize sensitivity and cost.
- Request ablations on temperature schedules and transitions per temperature to show how BREAD can guide tuning.
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theory, implementable procedure, fills an important gap, demonstrated utility across systems/models.
- Arguments against acceptance: expectation-only bounds limit per-instance certification, formal guarantees mainly apply to simulated data, and limited complex-posterior experiments.
- Overall: technically sound, practically relevant contribution with clear impact potential despite limitations tied to expectation-only bounds and simulated-data requirements.
