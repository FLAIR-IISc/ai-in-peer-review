Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) from estimating marginal likelihoods to assessing the quality of posterior samples produced by MCMC-like samplers that can be cast as AIS/SMC on an extended space. The key result shows that the gap between forward AIS’s stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1 and reverse AIS’s stochastic upper bound upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence DJ between the approximate sample distribution qfwd(xT) (the terminal state distribution of the AIS chain) and the true target pT. The method, termed BREAD, thus provides a quantitative, asymptotically convergent diagnostic of accuracy for samplers with annealing bridges.

Because reverse AIS needs an exact posterior draw, the paper focuses on simulated data, where posterior draws can be obtained by ancestral sampling from the joint p(θ, z, y). To transfer insights to real data, the authors propose a fitted-hyperparameter protocol: infer hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from those hyperparameters, and assess inference quality on the simulated data, checking that AIS behavior matches between simulated and real data. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan toolchains and demonstrate (i) validation of the expectation bound on toy problems where both sides are exactly computable; (ii) empirical consistency between real and simulated datasets across five models; (iii) robustness of reverse AIS to approximations in the posterior hyperparameter initialization; (iv) use of BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations in matrix factorization; and (v) debugging a bug in WebPPL uncovered when the bounds violated the theoretical ordering.

This work builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), and is closely related to contemporaneous work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), clarifying the relation when the reverse chain is initialized at the true posterior.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: tight link from BDMC bounds to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior distributions. This provides a quantified diagnostic rather than heuristic convergence checks.
- Practical impact: integration in two PPLs (WebPPL, Stan), with empirical case studies; clear demonstration that the method can inform model representation choices and even catch implementation bugs.
- Careful validation: toy settings with exact computations; multiple real-data models; thoughtful protocol to bridge simulated and real data, with empirical checks that forward AIS behavior matches.
- Generality within scope: applies to AIS/SMC-like samplers with reversible kernels; no restrictive assumptions on target (e.g., multimodal targets allowed).

Weaknesses and limitations
- Expectation bound: The upper bound is only in expectation over AIS randomness; per-instance guarantees are not provided. The empirical reporting (quartiles) does not quantify how close finite-sample estimates are to the expected gap.
- Applicability: Requires algorithms amenable to AIS/SMC bridges and access to an exact posterior sample; the latter limits rigorous guarantees to simulated data. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data is heuristic; while the paper shows robustness to the number of MCMC steps S, there is no guarantee the approximate reverse initialization suffices in misspecified or weakly identified models.
- Target of evaluation: The divergence is between pT and the distribution of terminal AIS states qfwd, not the stationary distribution of a generic MCMC kernel after burn-in. The paper argues shared design choices, but the mapping from BREAD’s diagnostic to, e.g., NUTS-in-practice may be indirect.
- Sensitivity and guidance: Limited analysis of sensitivity to annealing schedules, number of temperatures, and per-temperature mixing. Practical guidance for setting these to obtain reliable bounds is minimal. No concentration bounds or confidence intervals around the estimated bound are provided.
- Empirical scope: The toy exact calculations are small; real-data experiments are medium-scale. It would be valuable to stress-test in higher dimensions where reverse AIS can be very heavy-tailed.

Quality
The theoretical derivations are sound and clearly relate the forward/reverse AIS expectations to the two KL terms, yielding an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence. The empirical methodology is careful, and the results are consistent and meaningful. Some statistical reporting could be strengthened (e.g., variability of the bound estimates, diagnostics of AIS weight variance, schedule choices).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with clear exposition of AIS, stochastic bounds, BDMC, and the new BREAD interpretation. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is described clearly, and the limitations are acknowledged. Figures communicate the patterns, though adding error bars or CIs for the gap would help. It would help to explicitly restate that the bound pertains to the distribution of terminal AIS states and discuss implications for widely used samplers like NUTS.

Originality
The connection from BDMC to expected upper bounds on Jeffreys divergence for approximate posterior samples is a novel and useful reframing, distinct from standard convergence diagnostics. The real-to-simulated protocol and integration in PPLs are practical contributions. The relation to “subjective divergence” is acknowledged; this work helps clarify when rigorous guarantees hold.

Significance
A principled, implementable diagnostic that can (i) quantify sample quality, (ii) guide modeling/algorithmic choices, and (iii) detect implementation bugs is valuable for the community and especially for probabilistic programming. While the requirement of simulated data for rigorous guarantees limits scope, the demonstrated insights and tooling are likely to be used and built upon.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report variability of the estimated gap vs. expected bound: e.g., multiple independent reverse AIS initializations where possible, bootstrap CIs, or tail-probability diagnostics.
- Provide guidance on annealing schedules, number of intermediates, and per-temperature mixing, possibly relating the gap to AIS ESS or variance of log-weights.
- Explore correlation with standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS) and with downstream predictive metrics to help practitioners interpret the numbers.
- Clarify how the AIS kernels in Stan were instantiated (HMC/NUTS per temperature?) and discuss any constraints.
- Discuss failure modes (e.g., model misspecification) where simulated data may not reflect real-data behavior, and provide mitigation strategies.

Arguments for acceptance
- Rigorous, insightful repurposing of BDMC to quantify posterior sample quality.
- Practical integration and demonstrations with actionable findings.
- Clear presentation and careful validation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees hold only in expectation and only for simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity and finite-sample behavior; guidance for practitioners could be stronger.
- The diagnostic targets AIS terminal-state distributions, which may not directly reflect all MCMC algorithms users care about.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a principled, useful diagnostic with solid theory, practical integration, and compelling demonstrations; the limitations are acknowledged and suggest fruitful future work.
