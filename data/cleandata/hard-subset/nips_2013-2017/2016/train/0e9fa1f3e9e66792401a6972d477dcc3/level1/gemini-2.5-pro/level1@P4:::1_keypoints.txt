- Proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) to quantitatively measure approximate posterior sample quality from AIS/BDMC-based MCMC.  
- Uses forward AIS (prior→posterior) for a stochastic lower bound and reverse AIS (posterior→prior) for a stochastic upper bound on log p(y).  
- Shows the expected gap between forward and reverse AIS bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between true and approximate posteriors.  
- Requires exact posterior samples for the reverse chain, restricting rigorous guarantees to simulated data where such samples are available.  
- Proposes a heuristic protocol for real data: estimate hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from the model using those estimates, then apply BREAD to the simulated data.  
- Implements and evaluates BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, using experiments to validate the method, compare samplers, and uncover a PPL bug.  
- Theoretical derivation connecting BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence is sound and follows from importance sampling and KL non-negativity.  
- Empirical validation is comprehensive: tests bound tightness on toy problems, checks consistency between real and simulated inference, and assesses protocol robustness.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, with accessible derivations and effective visualizations (e.g., AIS quartile plots).  
- Original contribution: recasts BDMC from marginal-likelihood estimation to a principled inference-validation tool and introduces a practical protocol for real data.  
- High significance for MCMC diagnostics and PPL users, offering a principled quantitative measure where existing diagnostics are heuristic.  
- Practical impact demonstrated via model-comparison use cases and debugging of PPL implementations.  
- Limitations: rigorous guarantees only for simulated data; heuristic for real data may fail under model misspecification.  
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-style algorithms unless other MCMC methods are embedded in an AIS-like framework.  
- Computationally intensive, requiring many forward and reverse AIS runs, which may prohibit routine on-the-fly use by end-users.
