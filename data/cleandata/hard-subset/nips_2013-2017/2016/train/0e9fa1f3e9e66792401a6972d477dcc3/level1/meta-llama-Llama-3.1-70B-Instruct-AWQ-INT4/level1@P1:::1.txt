This paper presents a novel approach to measuring the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The authors integrate their method into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets. They also present a protocol for using their method to diagnose inference quality on real-world data by inferring hyperparameters on the real data, simulating data from those hyperparameters, measuring inference quality on the simulated data, and validating the consistency of the inference algorithm’s behavior between the real and simulated data.

The paper makes four main technical contributions. First, it shows that bidirectional Monte Carlo yields an estimator which upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples from the true posterior. Second, it presents a technique for exactly computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, and shows that the upper bound is often a good match in practice. Third, it proposes a protocol for using bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate the accuracy of approximate inference on real-world datasets. Finally, it extends both WebPPL and Stan to implement the proposed method, and validates it on a variety of probabilistic models in both frameworks.
