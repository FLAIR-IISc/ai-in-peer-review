**Summary**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by certain MCMC-based algorithms. The method builds upon the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which was originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods. The core idea is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both a "forward" direction (from prior to posterior) and a "reverse" direction (from posterior to prior). The authors provide a key theoretical contribution by showing that the expected gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained from the reverse and forward AIS runs respectively, forms an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of AIS samples and the true posterior.

Because the reverse procedure requires an exact sample from the posterior, the method provides rigorous guarantees only on simulated data. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; finally, run BREAD on this simulated data, using an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain. The authors implement their method in two prominent and different probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models. They demonstrate its utility by using it to compare different model representations for matrix factorization and to identify a bug in WebPPL's sampler.

**Relation to Prior Work**

The paper is clearly and correctly situated as an extension of Neal's Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) [Nea01] and, more directly, Grosse et al.'s Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15]. The novelty lies not in the BDMC machinery itself, but in reinterpreting its output—specifically the gap between the bounds—as a measure of posterior sample quality. The authors do an excellent job of contrasting their approach with classical MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., [GR92]), correctly noting that diagnostics can only detect non-convergence, whereas BREAD can provide a quantitative measure of accuracy. The comparison to the work of Gorham and Mackey [GM15] on using Stein's method is also insightful, highlighting that BREAD does not require convexity assumptions and thus applies more broadly, albeit with its own set of constraints (e.g., requiring simulated data). The discussion of concurrent and closely related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16] is honest and valuable, clarifying the relationship between the two approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. Assessing the reliability of inference is a major bottleneck in the practical application of MCMC and the adoption of probabilistic programming languages, especially for non-expert users. Providing a quantitative, automated tool for this purpose is a significant contribution that could have a broad impact on both practitioners and researchers.
2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The derivation showing that the expected BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence is a simple but elegant and novel insight. The analysis in Section 3.1 appears technically sound and is presented with clarity. This provides a principled, information-theoretic measure of sample quality.
3.  **Practicality and Generality:** The implementation and validation in both WebPPL (using trace M-H) and Stan (using NUTS/HMC) demonstrate the method's applicability to different underlying MCMC engines. The case studies are compelling: comparing model representations and debugging a PPL are exactly the kinds of difficult tasks that BREAD is designed to facilitate. This shows the method is not just a theoretical curiosity but a useful engineering tool.
4.  **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors perform a careful set of validation experiments. The check for consistency between real and simulated data (Sec 5.1.1) and the sensitivity analysis of the approximate posterior initialization (Sec 5.1.2) are thoughtful and add significant credibility to the proposed protocol for real-world data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Applicability to Standard MCMC:** The method directly evaluates the sample distribution from AIS, `q_fwd(x_T)`, not the stationary distribution of a standard MCMC sampler. While the authors argue that insights from tuning AIS (e.g., choice of transition operators) will transfer to standard MCMC, this connection is indirect. It would be helpful to clarify this distinction and perhaps discuss how the divergence of the AIS marginal distribution relates to the divergence of samples from a long-run MCMC chain using the final target kernel.
2.  **Computational Cost:** BREAD is computationally intensive, requiring numerous runs of AIS, which is itself more expensive than a standard MCMC chain. This cost may be prohibitive for large-scale models where a single inference run is already at the limit of feasibility. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential trade-offs would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Reliance on a Heuristic for Real Data:** The protocol for real-world data, while pragmatic and well-validated in the paper's experiments, necessarily sacrifices the rigorous theoretical guarantees. The use of an *approximate* posterior sample to seed the reverse chain could be problematic if the initial inference run fails to explore the full posterior (e.g., in cases of severe multi-modality). While the authors' empirical results are reassuring, this remains a key theoretical limitation.
4.  **Variance of the Bound Estimator:** As noted in Footnote 3, the theoretical analysis applies to single-chain AIS estimates (`log w_T`), precluding the standard variance-reduction technique of averaging weights before taking the logarithm. This implies that the estimator for the bound, `\hat{B}`, may have very high variance, a known issue with log-space estimators. The paper visualizes quartiles for this reason, but a more direct discussion of how many BREAD repetitions are needed to obtain a reliable estimate of the expected gap would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in a core area of NIPS.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound theoretical result.
*   Provides a practical tool and demonstrates its utility on real systems (Stan, WebPPL) and for concrete problems (model selection, debugging).
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and the experiments are thoughtfully designed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method's direct applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-like algorithms.
*   The protocol for real-world data relies on a heuristic that breaks the formal guarantees.
*   The computational cost may limit its use in practice.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and timely contribution. It provides a novel, principled, and practical method for tackling the crucial challenge of evaluating MCMC inference quality. The strengths in significance, originality, and practical demonstration far outweigh the inherent limitations of the approach. The work is likely to be influential and to inspire further research in this important area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
