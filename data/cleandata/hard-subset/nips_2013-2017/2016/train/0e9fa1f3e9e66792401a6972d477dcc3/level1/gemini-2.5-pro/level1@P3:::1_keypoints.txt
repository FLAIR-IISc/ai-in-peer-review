- Introduces BREAD, which uses forward and reverse AIS to bound divergence between AIS sample distribution and the true posterior.  
- Proves the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log-marginal bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence.  
- Reverse AIS requires exact posterior samples, so theoretical guarantees hold only on simulated data.  
- Proposes a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters, simulate data, and run BREAD initialized with an approximate posterior sample.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validates on multiple models, showing practical utility (model comparison, bug finding).  
- Positions work as an extension/reinterpretation of BDMC/AIS rather than a new sampling algorithm.  
- Contrasts BREAD with classical MCMC diagnostics (detect non-convergence) and Stein-method approaches (different assumptions and scope).  
- Novel, simple, and technically sound derivation linking BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence.  
- Demonstrates practicality and generality by supporting different MCMC engines (trace M-H, NUTS/HMC).  
- Provides rigorous evaluation including consistency checks between real and simulated data and sensitivity analysis of initialization.  
- Limited direct applicability to standard MCMC since BREAD evaluates AIS marginals, not stationary distributions of generic MCMC samplers.  
- Computationally expensive due to multiple AIS runs, making it potentially impractical for large-scale models.  
- Real-data protocol relies on a heuristic (approximate posterior initialization) that breaks formal guarantees and may fail with severe multimodality.  
- The bound estimator can have high variance (single-chain log-weight estimates), raising questions about the number of repetitions needed for reliability.  
- Overall recommendation: strong, well-written, and practically useful contribution with limitations that do not outweigh its significance; strong accept.
