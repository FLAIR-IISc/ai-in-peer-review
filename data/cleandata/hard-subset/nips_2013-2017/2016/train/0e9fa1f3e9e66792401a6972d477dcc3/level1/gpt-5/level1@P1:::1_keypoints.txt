- Extends BDMC to assess posterior sample quality: shows the expected gap between forward and reverse AIS bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS terminal-state distribution qfwd and true posterior pT.  
- Introduces BREAD: a diagnostic that uses forward/reverse AIS on an extended space to quantify sampler accuracy asymptotically.  
- Requires exact posterior draws for reverse AIS, so rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated-data settings.  
- Proposes a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to transfer insights to real data by simulating from inferred hyperparameters and checking AIS behavior consistency.  
- Integrated BREAD into WebPPL and Stan toolchains to demonstrate usability in practice.  
- Empirical validations include toy problems with exact computations, five real-data models showing consistency, and case studies (collapsed vs uncollapsed, bug debugging).  
- Shows reverse AIS robustness to approximations in posterior hyperparameter initialization in experiments.  
- Clarifies relation to prior work: builds on AIS, BDMC and relates to “subjective divergence” while specifying when rigorous guarantees hold.  
- Strength: provides a principled, quantified diagnostic (expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence) rather than heuristic checks.  
- Strength: practical impact via PPL integrations and demonstrations that inform modeling and reveal implementation bugs.  
- Strength: careful validation across toy and medium-scale real-data experiments with thoughtful protocols.  
- Strength: method is general for AIS/SMC-like samplers with reversible kernels and does not require unimodal targets.  
- Weakness: bound holds only in expectation over AIS randomness; no per-instance guarantees or quantified finite-sample closeness reported.  
- Weakness: applicability limited to algorithms admitting AIS/SMC bridges and needing exact posterior samples; fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic and may fail under misspecification or weak identification.  
- Weakness: diagnostic targets terminal AIS state distribution, which may not map directly to stationary behavior of other MCMC algorithms (e.g., NUTS after burn-in).  
- Weakness: limited analysis and practical guidance on annealing schedules, number of temperatures, per-temperature mixing, and no concentration/confidence bounds for estimates.  
- Weakness: empirical experiments are small-to-medium scale; higher-dimensional stress tests where reverse AIS is heavy-tailed are lacking.  
- Quality: theoretical derivations are sound and clearly relate AIS expectations to KL terms; empirical methodology is careful but statistical reporting could be improved.  
- Clarity: generally well written with clear exposition and protocol description; would benefit from explicit restatement that the bound pertains to terminal AIS states and from error bars/CIs.  
- Originality: novel reframing of BDMC as an expected Jeffreys-divergence bound and practical contributions via PPL integration and real-to-simulated protocol.  
- Significance: offers a principled, implementable diagnostic useful for probabilistic programming, model/algorithm choice, and debugging, though scope is limited by simulated-data requirement.  
- Suggestions: report variability/CI for estimated gap, provide guidance on annealing schedules and mixing, relate to ESS/log-weight variance, compare with standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS), clarify AIS kernel choices, and discuss failure modes under misspecification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous and insightful theoretical repurposing of BDMC, practical tooling and experiments, clear presentation and validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: guarantees only in expectation and mainly for simulated data, limited sensitivity/finite-sample analysis, and indirect mapping to common MCMC practice.  
- Recommendation: Accept — useful principled diagnostic with solid theory, practical integration, and meaningful demonstrations despite acknowledged limitations.
