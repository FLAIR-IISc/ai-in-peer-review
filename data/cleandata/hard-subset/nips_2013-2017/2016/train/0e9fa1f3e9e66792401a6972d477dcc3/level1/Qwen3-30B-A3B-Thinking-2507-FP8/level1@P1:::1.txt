

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to rigorously evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors leverage BDMC to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples, which is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming systems where users need to assess the reliability of black-box inference. The paper extends Grosse et al.'s BDMC framework [GGA15] to provide a theoretically grounded method for measuring inference quality, validated through integration with WebPPL and Stan.

The paper builds upon the BDMC framework by deriving how the technique can bound the Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1), providing both theoretical justification and empirical validation on toy examples (Appendix B). The authors then propose a practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters (Section 3.2), which they validate through experiments on five common models (Section 5.1). The paper demonstrates the method's utility by comparing model representations in matrix factorization (Section 5.2.1) and even discovering a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical derivation showing how BDMC can bound the Jeffreys divergence (Equations 6-12). The validation on toy distributions (Appendix B) demonstrates the bound's accuracy, and the experiments on real models (Section 5) provide convincing evidence of the method's practical utility. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly the need for simulated data and the approximate posterior initialization in Section 3.2. The method is well-motivated and addresses a significant gap in MCMC reliability assessment.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background sections (2.1-2.3) provide a clear foundation for the technical contributions, and the derivation in Section 3.1 is carefully presented. The experimental results are clearly described with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-2). The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience, with sufficient mathematical detail to allow reproduction of the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by extending BDMC to measure MCMC inference quality rather than just estimating marginal likelihoods. While related to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka's [CTM16] work on "subjective divergence," the authors clearly differentiate their approach by showing how BREAD upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence when using the true posterior for reverse AIS initialization. The paper also provides the first practical application of this technique to probabilistic programming systems, demonstrating its utility in model representation comparisons and debugging.

**Significance:** The problem of assessing MCMC reliability is highly significant, especially for probabilistic programming where end-users may not have expertise in MCMC diagnostics. BREAD provides a rigorous, automatic, and general method for monitoring inference quality, which could significantly improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems. The paper demonstrates this through concrete examples: guiding model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and discovering a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2). This work has the potential to impact both practitioners using probabilistic programming and researchers developing inference algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical contribution extending BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence
- Rigorous validation on toy distributions and multiple real models
- Practical protocol for real-world application via hyperparameter fitting
- Demonstrated utility in both model design (Section 5.2.1) and debugging (Section 5.2.2)
- Well-executed integration with WebPPL and Stan
- Clear writing and organization

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational overhead of running both forward and reverse AIS chains
- The assumption that fitted hyperparameters yield representative posterior samples (Section 3.2) is validated only for the five models tested; more extensive validation would strengthen this claim
- The paper doesn't thoroughly compare BREAD's performance to other MCMC diagnostic methods (beyond the brief discussion in Section 4)

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The method is theoretically sound, well-validated, and has clear practical utility. While the computational cost of the method could be better discussed, this doesn't detract from the paper's main contribution. The work is a natural extension of BDMC [GGA15] and directly addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference. The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016 and would be of significant interest to both practitioners and researchers working on probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Novel extension of BDMC to MCMC reliability assessment, strong theoretical foundation, clear practical application with real-world validation, significant potential impact on probabilistic programming reliability.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Limited discussion of computational overhead, need for more extensive validation of the hyperparameter fitting protocol.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference with a theoretically sound and practically useful solution. I recommend acceptance.
