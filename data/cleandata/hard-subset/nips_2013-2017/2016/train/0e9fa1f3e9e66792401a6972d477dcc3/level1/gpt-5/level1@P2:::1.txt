Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. Specifically, it shows that for algorithms expressible as importance sampling on an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC), the gap between forward AIS’s stochastic lower bound on log(ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS’s stochastic upper bound yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior p(θ, z | y) and the distribution q of approximate samples produced by the algorithm. The method, termed BREAD, is integrated into WebPPL and Stan. The paper also proposes a practical protocol to apply the idea to real data by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating synthetic data from those fitted hyperparameters, and validating that the inference behavior (e.g., AIS weight distributions) matches between simulated and real data. Experiments on five models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures) support this protocol; a case study on matrix factorization illustrates how BREAD can guide model representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed), and it also helped discover a bug in WebPPL.

The work connects to thermodynamic integration and Jarzynski/AIS (Neal 2001; Jarzynski 1997), to sample-quality diagnostics (Stein discrepancy; Gorham & Mackey, NIPS 2015), and to concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016). It complements classical MCMC convergence diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin; Brooks-Gelman), offering a quantitative, principled criterion when an exact posterior sample is available (simulated data).

Strengths
- Technical clarity: The derivation cleanly shows that E[log R̂−log R̂rev] ≥ DKL(q||p)+DKL(p||q). The argument rests on AIS-as-IS over an augmented space and KL contractions, making assumptions transparent (reversibility, invariance of kernels).
- Practical utility: Integrations into WebPPL and Stan, with code pathways that many practitioners use. The protocol to bridge to real data is pragmatic and well-validated empirically.
- Empirical validation: Five diverse models, analysis of sensitivity to the number of MCMC steps for the reverse-chain initialization, and consistency checks between simulated and real data (distributional similarity of AIS estimates).
- Insight for modeling/inference choices: The collapsed vs. uncollapsed comparison gives actionable guidance; BREAD also surfaced an implementation bug—demonstrating value as a regression/validation tool.
- Scope beyond a single algorithm: While proofs are for AIS-style samplers, the design choices (transition kernels, collapsing) overlap with general MCMC practice, making insights relevant more broadly.

Weaknesses
- Expectation-level bound: The Jeffreys divergence control holds in expectation over AIS randomness; single-run estimates can be high-variance and heavy-tailed. The paper reports quartiles but offers no concentration guarantees for the gap statistic or guidance on the number of runs needed for reliable conclusions.
- Requirement of an exact posterior draw: Rigorous guarantees apply only on simulated data. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real-data diagnosis is heuristic; while empirically convincing in the tested cases, it lacks theoretical justification (e.g., conditions under which the proxy exactly or approximately upper bounds J).
- Limited exploration of hard targets: Validation of bound tightness is done on toy distributions; the real-model studies do not provide ground-truth J, so tightness remains somewhat anecdotal. More evidence on challenging multimodal or high-dimensional posteriors would strengthen claims.
- Algorithmic scope: Applicability is restricted to samplers that can be cast as IS on an extended space (AIS/SMC). Many widely used MCMC algorithms cannot be directly evaluated unless embedded into AIS/SMC.
- Reporting details: More detail on temperature schedules, kernel choices, and computational overhead would help practitioners reproduce and tune BREAD. The impact of schedule misspecification on the bound (and its variance) is not systematically analyzed.

Assessment by NIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound derivations with correct use of AIS/BDMC machinery. Experimental methodology is careful, though the main theoretical guarantee is expectation-only and the real-data protocol is heuristic. The work is a complete contribution rather than a position paper.
- Clarity: Well-organized and readable. Background and notation are sufficient, with clear derivations (Eqs. 6–12). Suggestions: add practical guidance on run counts, variance control, and schedule design; explicitly quantify computational cost.
- Originality: Novel use of BDMC to upper bound posterior sample divergence, with a new practical protocol and integration into two PPLs. Relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriately discussed.
- Significance: Moderate to high. Offers a principled diagnostic sorely needed in probabilistic programming and MCMC practice, with demonstrated utility for model representation choices and debugging. Limitations (expectation-only, simulated-data requirement) temper impact but do not negate practical value.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you provide empirical or theoretical guidance on the number of forward/reverse AIS runs needed for a stable estimate of the bound, and perhaps combine multiple runs to reduce variance with justified procedures?
- Could you report wall-clock cost vs. bound tightness (e.g., tradeoffs in T, schedule choices, number of intermediate distributions)?
- How sensitive is BREAD to schedule and kernel choices? Any automated tuning recommendations?
- Can the fitted-hyperparameter protocol be strengthened, e.g., via importance weighting, diagnostics to detect mismatch, or conditions under which it preserves an upper bound?
- Show additional cases with known J (e.g., Gaussian with known covariance mismatch, multimodal mixtures) to further characterize bound tightness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of BDMC with a practically useful diagnostic for posterior sample quality.
- Solid integration into mainstream PPLs; nontrivial empirical studies and a compelling use case (model representation choice).
- Fills a recognized gap in MCMC/PPL validation and can improve reliability and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees are expectation-only and require simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic without theoretical guarantees.
- Limited evaluation on truly challenging inference settings and lack of variance/concentration analysis for the diagnostic.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a meaningful, well-executed contribution that the community can use immediately, despite limitations. It advances the state of practice in validating MCMC-based inference and probabilistic programming.
