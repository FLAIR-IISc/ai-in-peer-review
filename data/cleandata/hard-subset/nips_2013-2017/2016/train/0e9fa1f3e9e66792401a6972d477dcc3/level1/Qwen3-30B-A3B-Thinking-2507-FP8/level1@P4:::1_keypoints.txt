- Introduces BREAD, an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure MCMC posterior inference quality.
- Theoretical result: BDMC-based estimator upper bounds the expected Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between true and approximate posteriors.
- Validated on toy problems where exact computation is possible, showing the upper bound is reasonably tight.
- Presents a practical protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters.
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating broad applicability and practical utility.
- Paper is technically rigorous with clear, well-organized derivations and distinction between theory and empirical validation.
- Novelty: repurposes BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to rigorous evaluation of posterior inference and differs from CTM16 by using the true posterior for the reverse chain.
- Significant contribution: provides a general, automatic procedure for monitoring MCMC quality that could benefit probabilistic programming users and influence model representation choices.
- Empirical impact: used to analyze collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and uncovered a bug in WebPPL.
- Strength: clear theoretical contribution, rigorous toy validation, practical protocol, and software integration.
- Weakness: requires simulated data to obtain exact posterior samples for guarantees.
- Weakness: limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended state spaces (e.g., AIS, SMC).
- Weakness: computational cost of running forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for very large models.
- Weakness: lacks extensive comparison of BREAD’s computational cost against other diagnostics.
- Conclusion: technically sound, well-validated, practically useful work with high potential impact; recommendation for acceptance.
