## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples obtained via MCMC algorithms, particularly those amenable to AIS/SMC framing. The core idea extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo [GGA15] from estimating marginal likelihoods to evaluating the quality of posterior inference. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate its utility through experiments, including a bug discovery in WebPPL and insightful comparisons of model representations.

This work builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in MCMC diagnostics (e.g., [CC96, GR92]), importance sampling [Nea01], and marginal likelihood estimation [GGA15]. It addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian inference: reliably assessing the accuracy of approximate posterior samples, especially within “black box” inference systems. The connection to BDMC is well-motivated, and the extension to bounding the Jeffreys divergence is a natural and valuable contribution.  Recent related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16] is acknowledged and appropriately distinguished. 

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The mathematical analysis appears sound, offering theoretical guarantees (for simulated data). The practical implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages is a significant achievement, making the method accessible to a broad audience.  The experimental validation is thorough, demonstrating both the accuracy of the bounds and the utility of BREAD in guiding modeling choices (matrix factorization representations). The bug discovery highlights a crucial benefit of rigorous evaluation tools.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The reliance on simulated data for rigorous guarantees limits direct applicability to real-world problems, even with the proposed protocol for approximating real-world behavior. The analysis of the hyperparameter fitting protocol (Section 3.2) and its impact on the bounds could be strengthened. While the authors acknowledge the potential for the fitted hyperparameters to influence the results, more detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the bounds to this process would be beneficial. Additionally, the presentation of results (e.g., quartiles in Fig. 1) is necessitated by outlier issues, which might warrant further investigation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded extension of BDMC to posterior evaluation.
*   Practical implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Strong experimental validation demonstrating accuracy and utility.
*   Potential for debugging and improving inference software.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on simulated data for rigorous guarantees.
*   Potential sensitivity to the hyperparameter fitting protocol.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference.  The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically useful. While the limitations regarding real-world applicability should be acknowledged, the potential benefits of BREAD for improving the reliability of probabilistic programming systems are significant. I recommend acceptance.
