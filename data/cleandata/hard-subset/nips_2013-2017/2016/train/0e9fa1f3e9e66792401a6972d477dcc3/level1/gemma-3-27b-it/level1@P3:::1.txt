This paper presents a compelling extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea – using the Jeffreys divergence as a metric and bounding it with reverse annealing – is both theoretically sound and practically relevant, particularly in the context of “black box” probabilistic programming systems where diagnostic tools are crucial. The authors demonstrate the method's integration into WebPPL and Stan, providing empirical validation and showcasing its utility in guiding model representation choices.

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous analysis, careful presentation, and practical impact. The derivation connecting BDMC to a bound on the Jeffreys divergence is clearly explained, building logically on previous work [GGA15, Nea01].  The acknowledgement of the limitation – requiring simulated data for exact bounds – and the proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation is a pragmatic and valuable contribution. The experimental validation, particularly demonstrating consistency between real and simulated data, is convincing. The identification of a bug in WebPPL during testing further underscores the practical benefit of this technique.  The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in both WebPPL and Stan provides concrete examples of how BREAD can inform modeling decisions, moving beyond mere diagnostics toward active algorithm design.  The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the work within the broader literature on MCMC diagnostics and marginal likelihood estimation, including a fair comparison with [CTM16].

However, there are areas for improvement. While the authors acknowledge that the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence can overestimate the true divergence, a more detailed exploration of *when* and *why* this occurs would be beneficial.  The empirical analysis focuses heavily on Stan and WebPPL. While this is reasonable given the integration effort, expanding the experiments to include other probabilistic programming languages or MCMC implementations would strengthen the generalizability claims. The discussion around the hyperparameter fitting protocol (Section 3.2) feels somewhat hand-wavy.  The claim that a small number of MCMC steps is sufficient to obtain a plausible posterior sample for initializing the reverse chain isn’t rigorously justified and could be a potential source of error. More thorough sensitivity analysis to the number of MCMC steps would bolster confidence in this aspect of the methodology.  The presentation of results in Figure 1 relies heavily on quartiles due to outliers. While justifiable, a discussion of the cause of these outliers and their impact on the conclusions would add further clarity. Finally, the code used for the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to bounding the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC evaluation is a novel contribution.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified.
* **Practicality:** The method is implemented in two widely used probabilistic programming languages and demonstrates practical utility.
* **Impact:** BREAD offers a principled way to evaluate and improve inference algorithms, potentially benefiting a wide range of researchers and practitioners.
* **Completeness:** The paper addresses limitations and provides a reasonable protocol for applying the method to real-world data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited generalizability:** The empirical evaluation is primarily focused on Stan and WebPPL.
* **Hyperparameter protocol:** The justification for the hyperparameter fitting procedure could be strengthened.
* **Outlier handling:** While addressed, a deeper investigation of outliers in the AIS estimates would be valuable.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The method is theoretically sound, practically useful, and well-presented. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the hyperparameter fitting protocol, outlier analysis, and consideration of broader applicability.  The potential impact of this work on the development and deployment of reliable probabilistic programming systems is substantial.
