This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to use BDMC to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. This is achieved by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validate it on several models and datasets, also using it to guide the design of inference algorithms by comparing different model representations.

The paper builds upon recent work on BDMC [GGA15] and extends it to a novel application – assessing the quality of MCMC samples. The connection to Jeffreys divergence provides a theoretically grounded way to quantify the discrepancy between approximate and true posteriors. The practical implementation within two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) is a significant contribution, enabling wider adoption and usability. The use of simulated data combined with a protocol for extrapolating to real-world data, similar to parametric bootstrap, is a reasonable approach to overcome the limitation of requiring exact posterior samples. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation, extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, appears solid, referencing and building upon existing work appropriately. The use of AIS is well-justified, and the connection to importance sampling is clearly explained.
*   **Novelty:** Applying BDMC to the evaluation of MCMC is a novel contribution.  While BDMC itself is established [GGA15], this application to assess sample quality, and particularly the link to the Jeffreys divergence, provides a new perspective and a useful tool.
*   **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the methodology is explained with sufficient detail. The background section provides necessary context for understanding the proposed method.
*   **Significance:** The ability to rigorously evaluate MCMC performance, particularly within the “black box” setting of probabilistic programming, is highly significant. The successful integration with WebPPL and Stan and the demonstration of its utility in guiding model design decisions are strong indicators of potential impact. The bug detection in WebPPL is a valuable demonstration of the method’s diagnostic capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Applicability:** The method’s direct applicability is limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). This restricts its use to specific types of MCMC algorithms. While the authors acknowledge this and argue the insights can generalize, the limitation should be emphasized.
*   **Real-World Data Protocol:** The protocol for extending the method to real-world data (fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating, and evaluating) relies on the assumption that the fitted hyperparameters are representative of the true data-generating process. This assumption might not always hold, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its potential impact.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains, particularly reverse AIS, can be computationally expensive. The paper briefly acknowledges this but does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead or strategies for mitigating it.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the validation on several models is helpful, the analysis could be strengthened by comparing the results with other existing convergence diagnostics or quality metrics. The focus on Stan and WebPPL might limit the generalizability of the findings.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for evaluating MCMC-based inference.
*   The integration with widely used probabilistic programming languages (Stan and WebPPL) increases its practical impact.
*   The demonstration of its utility in guiding model design decisions and debugging implementations is compelling.
*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic inference – reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The method's direct applicability is limited to specific types of MCMC algorithms.
*   The protocol for extending the method to real-world data relies on potentially questionable assumptions.
*   The computational cost might be prohibitive for some applications.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant and valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The proposed method, BREAD, offers a rigorous and theoretically grounded approach to evaluating MCMC performance. The practical implementation and validation within Stan and WebPPL demonstrate its utility and potential impact.  While the limitations regarding applicability and computational cost are noteworthy, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section. Specifically, elaborating on the computational cost and discussing the limitations of the real-world data protocol would strengthen the paper.
