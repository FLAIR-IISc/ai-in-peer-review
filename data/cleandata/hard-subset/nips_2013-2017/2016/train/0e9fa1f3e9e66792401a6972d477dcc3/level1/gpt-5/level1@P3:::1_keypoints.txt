- Extends BDMC to assess MCMC posterior inference by using forward/reverse AIS gap (BREAD).
- Proves the expected forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior.
- Targets algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended path space (AIS explicitly, SMC by analogy).
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and proposes a pragmatic simulated-data protocol for real-data diagnostics.
- Validates method on toy problems (where true Jeffreys divergence is computable) and on multiple realistic models.
- Shows forward AIS behavior transfers from simulated to real data and demonstrates utility for representation choices and bug detection.
- Derivation is conceptually clear and rigorous, with an intuitive path-space decomposition.
- Practical relevance demonstrated via PPL integration and concrete case studies (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed MF).
- Proposed real-data protocol (fit hyperparameters, simulate, compare AIS) is sensible and empirically validated for robustness to S.
- Main theoretical guarantee holds only in expectation; single-run bounds can be loose and high-probability guarantees are not provided.
- Requires an exact posterior sample for rigorous upper bounds, so guarantees are limited to simulated-data settings.
- Real-data use is heuristic (empirical-Bayes-like) without formal bias analysis; robustness to model mismatch is not guaranteed.
- Experiments use AIS exclusively; limited guidance and few experiments for SMC and for mapping arbitrary MCMC (e.g., HMC) to AIS/SMC.
- Little quantitative study of tightness/variance scaling with dimension, annealing schedules, or kernel choices beyond toy examples.
- Quartile visualizations obscure expectation values that are central to the bound; means/uncertainties should be reported.
- No empirical comparison to other diagnostics (Stein discrepancies, ESS, R-hat) or demonstration that BREAD detects failures those methods miss.
- Computational overhead of reverse AIS is not quantified or compared against diagnostic value.
- Technically sound and carefully argued, with clear presentation; main caveats are expectation-based guarantees and heuristic real-data use.
- Original contribution: converting BDMC log-ML sandwiching into an expected Jeffreys divergence bound and PPL integrations.
- High practical significance for probabilistic programming and MCMC evaluation despite applicability limits.
- Suggested improvements: provide finite-sample/tail guarantees, analyze tightness vs schedule/dimension/kernel choices, include SMC experiments, compare to other diagnostics, explore stronger reverse references, and report computational costs.
- Arguments for acceptance: clear theoretical advance, practical PPL integration, and convincing case studies.
- Arguments against acceptance: guarantees only in expectation and for simulated data, limited tightness/variance exploration, and AIS-focused experiments.
