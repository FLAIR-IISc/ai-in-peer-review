

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to measure the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors show that BDMC can be used to upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. They validate their method on toy distributions where exact computation is possible, present a protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan for practical evaluation. The paper builds upon and extends the BDMC framework, which was previously used to estimate marginal likelihoods, to address the important problem of reliably evaluating MCMC inference quality.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical derivation showing that the BDMC estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is rigorous and well-motivated. The authors carefully validate their method on toy distributions where exact computation is possible, demonstrating that the upper bound is reasonably tight. The experimental section thoroughly evaluates the method across multiple models and datasets, including a practical validation of their protocol for real-world data. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for simulated data to obtain exact posterior samples) and provides a thoughtful discussion of related work, particularly distinguishing their approach from the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16]. The method is validated on both WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating broad applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The background sections provide sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC, and the mathematical derivations are presented with clarity and precision. The paper's structure logically progresses from theoretical foundations to practical implementation and validation. The experimental results are presented with appropriate figures and supporting details, making it easy for readers to understand the methodology and findings. The distinction between theoretical guarantees and empirical validation is clearly articulated.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending BDMC to measure MCMC inference quality rather than just estimating marginal likelihoods. While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, this paper shows how it can be repurposed to evaluate the accuracy of approximate posterior samples. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from related work, particularly [CTM16], by using the true posterior for the reverse chain (providing a rigorous guarantee) rather than an arbitrary reference distribution. The practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data represents a novel contribution to the field of probabilistic inference evaluation. The paper's approach is distinct from traditional convergence diagnostics, which can only identify certain forms of non-convergence but cannot rigorously demonstrate convergence.

**Significance:** This work addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference: the lack of reliable quantitative measures for evaluating MCMC inference quality. The proposed method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality in probabilistic programming languages, which could significantly benefit non-expert users. The paper demonstrates practical value by using BREAD to analyze model representation choices in WebPPL and Stan, and even uncovers a bug in WebPPL. The insights gained from BREAD regarding the trade-offs between collapsed and uncollapsed model representations could inform the design of future probabilistic programming systems and inference algorithms. The method has the potential to become a standard tool for evaluating inference quality in probabilistic modeling.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical contribution showing how BDMC can bound Jeffreys divergence
- Rigorous validation on toy distributions where exact computation is possible
- Practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data
- Integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates broad applicability
- Provides valuable insights into model representation choices
- Uncovered a bug in WebPPL, demonstrating practical utility

**Weaknesses:**
- The method requires simulated data to obtain exact posterior samples (though the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data)
- Limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC), though the authors argue these capture important design choices in MCMC
- Computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for very large models
- The paper doesn't extensively compare the computational cost of BREAD to other diagnostic methods

**Conclusion:** This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference by providing a theoretically sound, practically useful method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The work is technically rigorous, well-validated, and addresses an important problem in the field. The method has significant potential to improve the reliability and usability of probabilistic programming systems. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a distinct contribution from related work. I recommend acceptance.
