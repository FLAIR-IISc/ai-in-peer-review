- Introduces BREAD, a method that bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence to measure MCMC posterior inference quality.
- Extends BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015) from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior-quality evaluation using bidirectional AIS.
- Theoretical derivation is rigorous: BREAD upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Validation on toy distributions shows the bound is reasonably tight in practice.
- Empirical experiments on five real-world models demonstrate practical utility and consistency between real and simulated data.
- Practical implementations provided in WebPPL and Stan, including using BREAD to guide model representation and uncover a WebPPL bug.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and accessible with good explanations of AIS, stochastic bounds, and BDMC.
- One minor clarity suggestion: explicitly define the "fitted hyperparameter" protocol before validation experiments.
- Original contribution: repurposes BDMC to directly evaluate approximate posteriors and distinguishes itself from related work by using exact posterior samples.
- Addresses a significant practical problem by providing a rigorous, general procedure for monitoring inference quality in probabilistic programming.
- Strengths include solid theory, thorough validation, practical implementations, and demonstrated real-world utility.
- Weaknesses: reliance on representativeness of hyperparameter-fitted simulated data for real-data applicability.
- Additional weaknesses: limited discussion of computational scalability and incomplete comparison/possible complementarities with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016).
- Does not address extensions for cases without access to exact posterior samples.
- Recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, practically useful, well-validated, and likely to be adopted in probabilistic programming systems.
