

# Peer Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for rigorously measuring the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The work builds on the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework proposed by Grosse et al. (2015), extending it to evaluate MCMC inference quality rather than just marginal likelihood estimation. The authors demonstrate their method in WebPPL and Stan, using it to guide model representation choices and even uncover a bug in WebPPL.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution. The derivation in Section 3.1 is rigorous and clearly shows that the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The validation on toy distributions (Appendix B) demonstrates that the upper bound is reasonably tight in practice. The experimental validation on five real-world models (Section 5.1) provides strong empirical evidence for the method's utility, particularly the consistency between real and simulated data. The paper properly acknowledges limitations, noting that the method requires exact posterior samples (typically available only for simulated data) and that the real-world application protocol relies on the representativeness of hyperparameter-fitted simulated data. The theoretical guarantees are well-supported, though the paper could have more explicitly discussed the computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides a clear and concise explanation of AIS, stochastic bounds, and BDMC, making the paper accessible to readers unfamiliar with these concepts. The theoretical derivation is presented logically, with helpful intermediate steps. The experimental results are well-presented with appropriate visualizations (e.g., Figure 1 showing the consistency between real and simulated data). The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon. One minor improvement would be to more explicitly define the "fitted hyperparameter" protocol in Section 3.2 before presenting the validation experiments.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior quality evaluation. While Grosse et al. (2015) introduced BDMC for marginal likelihood, this paper demonstrates how the same framework can be repurposed to measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples, which is a more direct concern for practitioners. The paper's key insight—that BDMC can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence—represents a meaningful extension of previous work. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), who also build on BDMC, and clarify how their approach differs (using exact posterior samples rather than an arbitrary reference distribution). This is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers to be considered original.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant: MCMC practitioners lack reliable, quantitative measures of inference quality, especially for black-box inference systems. BREAD provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which could substantially improve confidence in probabilistic programming systems. The paper demonstrates practical utility by using BREAD to guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and to uncover a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2), showing that the method has tangible value beyond theoretical interest. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a method that works without restrictive assumptions (unlike Gorham and Mackey's 2015 method), making it applicable to multimodal or badly conditioned posteriors.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical derivation showing BREAD upper bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation
- Rigorous validation on toy distributions and real-world models
- Practical implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages
- Demonstrated utility for model selection and debugging
- Well-written with clear organization and explanations

**Weaknesses:**
- The method's applicability to real-world data depends on the representativeness of hyperparameter-fitted simulated data, which is not always guaranteed (though the authors validate this for their examples)
- Limited discussion of computational scalability for large models
- The comparison with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) could be more thorough, particularly regarding potential complementarities
- The paper doesn't address how the method might be extended to cases where exact posterior samples aren't available

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It addresses an important practical problem with a theoretically sound and practically useful solution. The method has clear potential for adoption in probabilistic programming systems and for improving the reliability of Bayesian inference. The paper is well-written, well-validated, and makes a clear contribution to the literature.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solves a significant practical problem in probabilistic inference
- Theoretical contribution is rigorous and well-justified
- Practical utility demonstrated through multiple experiments
- Provides a valuable tool for probabilistic programming users
- Advances the state of the art in evaluating inference quality

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Method has limitations in direct applicability to real-world data without the authors' protocol
- Limited discussion of computational scalability
- Could have more thoroughly compared with related work

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The BREAD method provides a rigorous and practical way to measure MCMC inference quality, which has been a longstanding challenge. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates significant practical utility. I recommend acceptance.
