- Contribution: Extends BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding posterior sample quality via AIS forward/reverse.
- Main theoretical result: The BDMC gap upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS sample distribution and the true posterior (E-bound shown in Eq. 8–12).
- Implementation: Integrates the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan.
- Empirical validation: Validated tightness on toy problems where true divergence is computable.
- Practical protocol: Proposes a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to proxy real datasets and checks sensitivity to MCMC warmup steps.
- Applications demonstrated: Consistency checks between simulated and real data, comparing collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization, and finding a WebPPL bug.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to convergence diagnostics, tempered transitions/nonequilibrium work, SMC, Stein discrepancies, and acknowledges concurrent "subjective divergence" work.
- Quality: Derivation is sound and empirical studies are careful and practically relevant.
- Key limitation (theory): Guarantee holds only in expectation; concentration/variance of the estimator is not characterized.
- Key limitation (practice): Use of quartiles in reporting sits uneasily with an expectation-only theoretical bound.
- Method sensitivity: Approach inherits AIS sensitivities (temperature schedule, kernel mixing) which are not systematically explored.
- Clarity: Paper is well written and organized; suggestions include adding pseudocode, guidance on annealing schedules/number of intermediates, and clearer conditions for bound tightness.
- Originality: Novel and useful extension of BDMC to posterior divergence and embedding in PPL toolchains; concurrent work only slightly reduces novelty.
- Significance: High — provides a quantitative, implementation-agnostic diagnostic useful for representation choices, sampler tuning, and debugging.
- Pros for acceptance: Strong theoretical link, practical integration and impactful case studies, and a sensible protocol for bridging simulated and real data.
- Cons for acceptance: Expectation-only bound with unknown variability, applicability limited to settings with reverse initialization and simulated data, and limited analysis of annealing-path sensitivity and computational scaling.
