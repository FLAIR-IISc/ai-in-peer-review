This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The work builds directly upon Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] and Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) [Nea01]. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, produced by running AIS from the prior to the posterior and vice-versa, serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. As the method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, it is rigorously applicable only to simulated data. The authors propose a practical protocol to extend its utility to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters and then generating simulated data. The method is successfully implemented and demonstrated within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the critical and long-standing challenge of MCMC convergence diagnostics. Moving beyond heuristics to a quantitative measure of divergence from the true posterior is a significant advance for both practitioners using black-box inference tools and researchers developing new algorithms.
*   **Originality:** The connection between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and elegant insight. It cleverly repurposes the BDMC framework from a tool for marginal likelihood estimation to one for assessing posterior quality.
*   **Quality & Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-organized. The theoretical derivation is straightforward. The experimental validation is comprehensive, convincingly demonstrating the consistency between real and simulated data under their proposed protocol and the method's utility for practical tasks like comparing model representations and even debugging a PPL implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Applicability to Real Data:** The primary limitation is that the rigorous theoretical guarantee holds only for simulated data. While the proposed protocol for handling real-world data is sensible and empirically validated, it remains a heuristic, as the reverse chain is not initialized from a guaranteed exact posterior sample.
*   **Computational Cost:** The method requires running multiple AIS chains in both forward and reverse directions, which can be computationally intensive, potentially limiting its use as a routine diagnostic for large-scale problems.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated. Despite the inherent limitation regarding real-world data, the work provides a powerful new tool for rigorously analyzing and comparing inference algorithms and will likely be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
