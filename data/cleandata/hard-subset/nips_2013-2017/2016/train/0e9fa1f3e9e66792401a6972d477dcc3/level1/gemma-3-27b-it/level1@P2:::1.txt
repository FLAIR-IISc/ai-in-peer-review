This paper presents a valuable extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea – using BDMC to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the samples obtained from MCMC – is both theoretically sound and practically relevant. The authors demonstrate its application to WebPPL and Stan, and showcase its utility in guiding model representation choices. 

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the BDMC framework introduced by Grosse et al. (GGA15), initially intended for estimating marginal likelihoods. The key innovation is recognizing that the same machinery can be repurposed to assess the *quality* of the samples generated by MCMC, rather than simply estimating the normalizing constant. This is particularly crucial for "black box" inference systems like probabilistic programming languages where diagnosing convergence and accuracy can be challenging.  The work correctly positions itself relative to existing MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996; Gelman & Rubin, 1992) by highlighting that BREAD provides *rigorous* bounds (in the simulated data setting) whereas most diagnostics are heuristics. It also acknowledges related work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (CTM16) which uses a similar BDMC approach but with a different reference distribution.  The connections to annealed importance sampling (AIS; Neal, 2001) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC; del Moral et al., 2006) are appropriately noted, as these are the algorithms to which the technique is directly applicable.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation linking BDMC to an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is clear and well-motivated. The analysis builds logically from existing work on AIS and extends it in a meaningful way.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – evaluating the reliability of MCMC samples – is a significant challenge in Bayesian inference, especially in the context of probabilistic programming. The proposed solution offers a principled approach.
* **Implementation and Validation:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a substantial contribution. The validation experiments, while not exhaustive, provide evidence that the method can detect differences in inference quality based on model representation. The demonstration of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the practical value of the approach as a testing tool.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.  The detailed explanation of AIS and BDMC in the background section is helpful.
* **Addressing Real-World Challenges:** Recognizing the limitation of requiring exact posterior samples and proposing a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (via hyperparameter estimation and simulation) demonstrates a thoughtful approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the practical cost of running BDMC (especially the reverse chain) can be substantial. The paper acknowledges this implicitly, but a more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.
* **Real-World Data Protocol:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on the assumption that the hyperparameters estimated on the real data generate simulated data representative of the real data. This is a reasonable heuristic, but its validity depends on the specific model and dataset.  Further investigation into the robustness of this protocol would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue that insights gained from these algorithms can inform the design of more general MCMC methods, the direct applicability is limited.
* **Discussion of Alternatives:** A more comprehensive comparison with existing methods for assessing MCMC convergence (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size) would strengthen the paper.  How does BREAD complement or surpass these existing techniques?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference – evaluating the reliability of MCMC samples.
* The proposed method is theoretically sound and builds on established techniques.
* The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the practical feasibility of the approach.
* The validation experiments provide encouraging results.
* The discovery of a bug in WebPPL showcases the method’s utility as a debugging tool.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of BDMC may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
* The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on potentially strong assumptions.
* The method’s direct applicability is limited to a specific class of inference algorithms.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method provides a principled and rigorous way to evaluate the quality of MCMC samples, and its implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes it accessible to a wide audience. While the computational cost and limited scope are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above (e.g., a more detailed discussion of computational cost, a more thorough investigation of the real-world data protocol, and a more comprehensive comparison with existing convergence diagnostics).
