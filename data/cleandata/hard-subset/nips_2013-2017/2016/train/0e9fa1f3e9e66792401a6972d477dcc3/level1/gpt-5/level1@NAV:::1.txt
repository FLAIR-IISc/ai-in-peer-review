This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) from marginal likelihood estimation to assessing posterior sample quality. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS; Neal, 2001) forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data, the authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on log p(y) upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS sample distribution and the true posterior. They integrate the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, validate tightness on toy problems where the true divergence is computable, propose a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to proxy real datasets, and demonstrate applications: (i) consistency between simulated and real data behavior across five models, (ii) comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations, and (iii) discovering a bug in WebPPL. The work relates to convergence diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996), tempered transitions and nonequilibrium work (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997), SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006), and sample-quality measures via Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). It also acknowledges concurrent “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Quality: The derivation is sound: Eq. (8–12) clearly show that the BDMC gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. Empirical studies are careful and practically relevant, and the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is validated via sensitivity to the number of MCMC “warmup” steps. However, the guarantee is only in expectation; concentration/variance of the estimator is not characterized, and the use of quartiles (rather than expectations) sits uneasily with the theoretical bound. The approach inherits AIS sensitivities (temperature schedule, kernel mixing), which are not systematically explored.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Suggestions: provide pseudocode for BREAD, practical guidance on choosing annealing schedules/number of intermediates, and a clearer statement of when the bound becomes tight.

Originality: Extending BDMC to bound posterior sample divergence and embedding it in PPL toolchains is novel and useful. Concurrent work reduces novelty only slightly; the positioning is appropriate.

Significance: High. A quantitative, implementation-agnostic diagnostic for black-box inference can guide model representation choices, tune samplers, and aid debugging.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical link from BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical integration and impactful case studies (Stan/WebPPL, representation trade-offs, bug discovery).
- Sensible protocol bridging simulated and real data.

Cons for acceptance:
- Expectation-only bound with unknown concentration; limited guidance on estimator variability.
- Applicability restricted to settings with (approximate) reverse initialization; guarantees require simulated data.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to annealing path and computational cost scaling.
