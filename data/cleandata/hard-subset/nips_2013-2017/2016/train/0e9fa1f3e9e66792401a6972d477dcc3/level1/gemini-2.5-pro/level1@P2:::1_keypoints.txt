- Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to show the expected forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS approximate posterior and the true posterior.  
- Requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse AIS chain, so formal guarantees only hold for simulated data.  
- Proposes a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters with approximate inference, simulate data from fitted hyperparameters, then apply BREAD to the simulated dataset.  
- Provides implementations in WebPPL and Stan and validates the approach across several models.  
- Theoretical derivation connecting the BDMC gap to the Jeffreys divergence is sound and follows from KL properties and AIS as importance sampling on an extended space.  
- Experiments demonstrate the bound is reasonably tight on toy examples.  
- Empirically shows inference behavior (AIS estimates) on real data closely tracks behavior on simulated data from fitted hyperparameters.  
- Validates robustness: results are stable to the number of MCMC steps used to generate the reverse-chain initialization.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, self-contained, and clearly explains AIS, stochastic bounds, BDMC, and the BREAD derivation.  
- Novel reframing of BDMC from evidence estimation to sample-quality assessment, with proper acknowledgment of concurrent related work.  
- Significant practical impact for PPL developers, algorithm designers, and modelers—useful for benchmarking, debugging, and model selection.  
- Demonstrated use cases include comparing model parameterizations and discovering a bug in WebPPL’s multivariate Gaussian sampler.  
- Main limitations: formal guarantees limited to simulated data, and the bound concerns AIS-generated samples rather than arbitrary MCMC stationary distributions (e.g., NUTS).  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept based on novelty, technical soundness, significance, clarity, and completeness.
