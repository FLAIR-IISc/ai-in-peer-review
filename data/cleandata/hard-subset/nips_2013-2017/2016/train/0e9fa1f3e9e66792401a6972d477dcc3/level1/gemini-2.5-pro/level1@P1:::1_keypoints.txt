- Summary: Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC/AIS to bound the Jeffreys divergence between forward-AIS approximate samples and the true posterior via the gap between forward (lower) and reverse (upper) log-ML bounds.

- Theoretical contribution: Proves the expected gap between AIS bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence, repurposing BDMC for sample-quality assessment.

- Practical protocol for real data: Recommend fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data from them, and running BREAD on the simulated data; empirical validation shows similar inference behavior on real and simulated data.

- Implementations and case studies: Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, used to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization and to find/fix a WebPPL bug.

- Strength — significance/impact: Addresses a fundamental problem in assessing MCMC sample quality and provides a rigorous, quantitative diagnostic beyond traditional heuristics.

- Strength — novelty/soundness: The connection between log-ML bound gap and Jeffreys divergence is novel, elegant, and appears technically sound.

- Strength — experimental validation: Thorough experiments validate key assumptions and robustness (e.g., reverse-chain robustness to approximate initialization).

- Strength — practical utility: Demonstrates actionable guidance for modeling choices and debugging via PPL integrations.

- Strength — clarity: Paper is very well-written, organized, and transparent about related work and limitations.

- Weakness — limited applicability: Rigorous guarantees apply to AIS/SMC-style samplers, not directly to general MCMC algorithms like HMC/NUTS.

- Weakness — computational cost: BREAD is computationally intensive, requiring multiple long forward and reverse AIS runs, so it's better for deep validation than routine diagnostics.

- Weakness — heuristic on real data: The real-data protocol uses approximate posterior samples, breaking formal guarantees so the bound is no longer strictly valid.

- Detailed comment: The theory applies to the log of a single AIS weight, not the common log-of-average-weights; extending analysis to the latter would better match practice.

- Argument for acceptance: Solves a critical and unsolved problem with a novel, theoretically-grounded method and strong empirical and practical demonstrations.

- Argument for acceptance: High practical value through PPL implementations and compelling case studies that illustrate model- and sampler-level insights.

- Argument for acceptance: Paper is clear, technically sound, and likely to have strong impact on inference research and applied probabilistic modeling.

- Argument against acceptance: Rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data scenarios.

- Argument against acceptance: Direct applicability is restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers, not general-purpose MCMC outputs.
