Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [Grosse et al., 2015] from marginal likelihood benchmarking to assessing the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. For algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended path space—specifically AIS [Neal, 2001] and, by analogy, SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006]—the authors show that the gap between forward AIS (a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS (a stochastic upper bound, given an exact posterior sample) yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. They call the overall procedure BREAD. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL (MH over execution traces) and Stan (NUTS/HMC), validate tightness on toy examples, and propose a practical protocol to diagnose inference on real data by fitting hyperparameters, simulating datasets, and comparing forward/reverse AIS behaviors. Experiments across five models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR, Gaussian mixtures) show close agreement between real and simulated data behaviors, and a matrix factorization study illustrates how BREAD can inform representation choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed). A debugging anecdote demonstrates utility in catching implementation bugs.

This work connects to: classical MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin [1992], Brooks–Gelman [1998], Cowles–Carlin [1996]), thermodynamic integration and tempered transitions [Neal, 1996], Jarzynski’s non-equilibrium identity [1997], reverse-AIS-based bounds [Burda et al., 2015], Stein discrepancies for posterior sample quality [Gorham–Mackey, 2015], and concurrent “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016]. Relative to BDMC, the novelty is to convert bidirectional log-partition estimates into an expected bound on a symmetric divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and rigor: clean derivation that the expected forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence (Eqs. 6–12), with an intuitive decomposition over the path space. Nicely situates BDMC as a sample-quality assessor rather than only a log-ML tool.
- Practical relevance for PPLs: integration into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstration that BREAD can guide modeling/inference choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and uncover subtle implementation errors.
- Sensible protocol for real data: the fitted-hyperparameter simulation scheme is pragmatic and validated empirically (reverse AIS insensitive to the number of hyperparameter MCMC updates).
- Empirical validation: toy cases where true Jeffreys divergence can be computed; multiple realistic models; comparison of forward AIS behavior on real vs simulated data suggests good transfer.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope of guarantees: the Jeffreys divergence bound holds only in expectation over AIS randomness; single-run realizations can be loose, and high-probability guarantees are not provided beyond standard tail bounds for stochastic lower/upper bounds. Variance of the gap can be large.
- Requirement of an exact posterior sample: rigorous upper bounds are limited to simulated data. The real-data protocol is heuristic (empirical Bayes–like), without a formal bias analysis. The validation via insensitivity to S is reassuring but does not preclude failure in mismatched or weakly identified models.
- Algorithmic coverage: although applicable in principle to SMC, the experiments use AIS exclusively. Many popular MCMC methods (e.g., HMC as deployed in Stan) are evaluated only indirectly via an AIS construction; guidance for mapping arbitrary MCMC to AIS/SMC for BREAD is limited.
- Tightness characterization: beyond toy examples, there is little quantitative analysis of tightness or scaling with dimensionality, annealing schedules, and kernel choices. The quartile visualizations obscure expectations, which are central to the bound.
- Comparisons to other diagnostics: there is no empirical comparison to Stein discrepancies, ESS, or R-hat, nor a demonstration that BREAD detects failures that others miss (aside from the bug anecdote).
- Computational overhead: reverse AIS adds cost; the paper does not quantify overhead versus diagnostic value across settings.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully argued. The derivations are standard but well presented, and the empirical protocol is thoughtfully designed. The main caveats are the expected (not high-probability) nature of the divergence bound, and the heuristic use on real data.

Clarity
Clear and well organized. The connection between path-space AIS and sample quality is explained crisply. Suggestions: state the main result as a theorem with explicit assumptions (ergodicity, reversibility, support conditions); report means and uncertainties of the forward/reverse log-estimates alongside quartiles; detail annealing schedules and kernel tuning; quantify computational overhead.

Originality
The extension from BDMC’s log-ML sandwiching to an expected Jeffreys divergence bound for approximate samples is novel and useful. The real-data protocol and PPL integration are practically original. The relation to concurrent subjective divergence is acknowledged.

Significance
High practical significance for probabilistic programming and MCMC evaluation. While limited to AIS/SMC-like settings and simulated data for guarantees, the method provides a rare rigorous handle on sample quality and can influence model representation choices and system debugging.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample/tail guarantees for the divergence estimator (e.g., via multiple independent forward/reverse runs and concentration inequalities; cf. Gogate et al., 2007).
- Investigate tightness and variance as functions of schedule length, temperature spacing (e.g., geometric vs moment averaging), kernel choices, and dimension.
- Include SMC experiments and guidance for using BREAD with particle methods (e.g., resampling effects).
- Empirically compare with Stein discrepancy, ESS, and R-hat on failure cases (multimodal, funnel, heavy tails) to illustrate complementary strengths.
- Explore stronger reference distributions for the reverse chain on real data (e.g., tempered posterior, amortized approximations) and analyze induced bias.
- Report computational costs and wall-clock tradeoffs for the matrix factorization study.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution connecting BDMC to expected bounds on posterior sample quality.
- Practical integration into major PPLs with convincing case studies and real-data protocol.
- Demonstrated utility for model representation choices and debugging.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees are in expectation and require simulated data; real-data usage is heuristic.
- Limited exploration of tightness/variance and lack of comparative diagnostics.
- Experiments focus on AIS; broader applicability to SMC and generic MCMC not fully demonstrated.

Overall: a solid, well-motivated contribution that advances rigorous evaluation of MCMC-based inference and is likely to impact PPL practice.
