This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The core idea is to leverage the strengths of BDMC – providing upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood – and repurpose it to bound the Jeffreys divergence, a measure of distributional difference. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validating it on several models and datasets, and use it to guide design choices related to model representations.

This work builds upon the recent resurgence of interest in evaluating the reliability of probabilistic inference. Prior work focuses on convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996; Gelman & Rubin, 1992) and, more recently, quantifying approximation error (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016; Gorham & Mackey, 2015).  The authors correctly position BREAD as offering a more rigorous, though computationally demanding, alternative to traditional diagnostics, particularly valuable in “black box” inference settings (Goodman et al., 2008).  The connection to annealed importance sampling (AIS) (Neal, 2001) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) (del Moral et al., 2006) is well-established, and the extension to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and theoretically sound contribution.  The parallel work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) is acknowledged, and the differences are clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound, building on the BDMC framework (Grosse et al., 2015), is mathematically rigorous and well-explained. The connection to stochastic lower bounds (BGS15) is effectively leveraged.
* **Practical Integration:** Integrating BREAD into two widely-used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) is a significant achievement, making the technique accessible to a broad audience.
* **Empirical Validation:** The validation experiments, including the consistency check between real and simulated data, are well-designed and presented, demonstrating the practical utility of BREAD. The exploration of model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) provides a compelling use case.
* **Bug Discovery:** Uncovering a bug in WebPPL through the use of BREAD highlights the method's value as a tool for verifying implementations.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the underlying concepts and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While acknowledged, the significant computational cost of BREAD remains a substantial barrier to its widespread adoption.  The paper doesn’t delve deeply into strategies for mitigating this cost (e.g., variance reduction techniques).
* **Simulated Data Dependency:** The initial requirement for exact posterior samples limits the direct applicability of BREAD to real-world problems without the ability to simulate data. The proposed protocol for real-world data analysis (fitting hyperparameters and simulating) introduces potential biases, though the validation experiments mitigate this concern to some degree.
* **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The experiments, while thorough, are primarily conducted on relatively small models.  The scalability of BREAD to high-dimensional, complex models is not fully explored.
* **Quartile Visualization:**  While justifying the quartile visualization for noisy estimates, it obscures potential information compared to, say, boxplots, and doesn't fully explain *why* the estimates have such large outliers.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the proposed hyperparameter fitting protocol (Section 3.2) is not extensively explored. It would be helpful to understand the sensitivity of BREAD’s results to the choices made in this step.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based inference.
* The integration into WebPPL and Stan is a significant engineering contribution.
* The empirical validation is compelling and demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.
* The potential for identifying bugs in probabilistic programming language implementations is a valuable side benefit.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of BREAD remains a substantial limitation.
* The reliance on simulated data restricts its direct applicability to many real-world problems.
* The scalability to complex, high-dimensional models is not fully demonstrated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical foundations are solid, the implementation is impressive, and the empirical validation is convincing. While the computational cost and reliance on simulated data are legitimate concerns, the authors acknowledge these limitations and demonstrate reasonable strategies for mitigating them. The potential benefits of BREAD – providing a rigorous and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality – outweigh the drawbacks.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors briefly discuss strategies for reducing the computational cost of BREAD (e.g., importance sampling variants, approximations to the KL divergence) and expanding the discussion of potential biases introduced by the hyperparameter fitting protocol.
