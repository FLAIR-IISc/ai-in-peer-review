## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper presents a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for upper-bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples obtained from MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly those leveraging annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea builds upon bidirectional Monte Carlo [GGA15] to provide a quantifiable measure of inference reliability, a long-standing challenge in probabilistic inference. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate its utility in evaluating model representations.

This work addresses a crucial problem in Bayesian inference: assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples. Existing diagnostics often lack rigorous guarantees. The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a significant contribution, offering a more principled approach to evaluating inference accuracy. The practical implementation within two popular probabilistic programming languages is commendable, enhancing the accessibility of this method. The validation on simulated data and the protocol for applying it to real-world data (via hyperparameter estimation and simulation) are well-considered. The application to comparing model representations in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates a concrete use case, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a debugging tool.

However, the method’s reliance on simulated data, even with the proposed protocol, remains a limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the extent to which the results generalize to complex, real-world scenarios requires further investigation. The computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains could also be prohibitive for high-dimensional models. The analysis primarily focuses on models where exact posterior sampling is feasible for simulation, which limits the immediate applicability to all MCMC scenarios. The discussion of related work is thorough, but could benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent advances in convergence diagnostics beyond those explicitly mentioned.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of BDMC to quantify MCMC accuracy.
*   Rigorous theoretical foundation and demonstrable bounds on Jeffreys divergence.
*   Practical implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Demonstrated utility in model comparison and bug detection.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on simulated data and potential limitations in generalizing to real-world problems.
*   Computational cost may be a barrier for complex models.
*   Applicability limited to algorithms amenable to AIS/SMC framework.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The method is theoretically sound, practically implemented, and addresses a critical challenge. While limitations exist, the potential benefits of BREAD for improving the reliability and trustworthiness of Bayesian inference outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance.
