This paper presents a method, termed Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The central problem addressed is the difficulty of reliably diagnosing the convergence and accuracy of MCMC, a challenge that is particularly acute for users of "black-box" probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The proposed method builds upon the recently introduced Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015), which was originally developed for estimating log marginal likelihoods.

The core idea is to use Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to form stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, p(y). The forward AIS chain, running from the prior to the posterior, yields a stochastic lower bound. The reverse AIS chain, running from the posterior to the prior, provides a stochastic upper bound. A key requirement for the reverse chain is an exact sample from the posterior, limiting the rigorous application of the method to simulated data, where one can first sample from the joint distribution p(θ, z, y) to obtain such a sample. The main theoretical contribution of this work is a refinement of the BDMC analysis, showing that the expected gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on log p(y) is itself an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the approximate posterior distribution generated by the forward AIS chain.

To bridge the gap between simulated and real-world data, the authors propose a protocol: first, run approximate inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters; third, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. They provide empirical validation for this protocol. The method is implemented and tested within two prominent PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, and its utility is demonstrated through experiments that validate the approach, compare different model representations for matrix factorization, and uncover a bug in a PPL implementation. The work is appropriately contextualized with respect to MCMC diagnostics, AIS, BDMC, and concurrent related work (Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016).

### Quality
The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the expected BDMC gap to the Jeffreys divergence, is the central theoretical result and appears to be sound. It follows directly from the properties of importance sampling and the non-negativity of KL divergence. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors are careful to evaluate the potential weaknesses of their method. For example, they test the tightness of the bound on toy problems (Appendix B), validate the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data, and assess the robustness of the proposed heuristic for handling real-world data. This represents a complete piece of work, moving from theory to practical implementation and validation.

### Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the background section provides a concise and sufficient overview of AIS and BDMC for a reader to understand the core contributions. The derivation of the main result in Section 3 is presented step-by-step and is easy to follow. The experimental sections are well-structured, with clear descriptions of the models, datasets, and results. The figures, particularly the quartile plots of AIS estimates, are effective at visualizing the performance of the samplers and the behavior of the bounds.

### Originality
The paper's originality lies in its novel application and extension of the BDMC framework. While BDMC itself was introduced by Grosse et al. (2015) for marginal likelihood estimation, the insight to use the gap between the forward and reverse estimates as a bound on the posterior sample quality (specifically, the Jeffreys divergence) is new and significant. This recasts BDMC from a tool for model selection into a tool for inference validation. The proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data, while heuristic, is also a novel practical contribution. The authors are transparent about closely related independent work, which is commendable.

### Significance
The work is highly significant. The problem of assessing MCMC sample quality is fundamental to applied Bayesian statistics and machine learning. Existing convergence diagnostics are largely heuristic and often fail to detect subtle problems, whereas BREAD provides a principled, quantitative measure of accuracy, with rigorous guarantees on simulated data. This is particularly valuable for the growing community of PPL users, who may not be inference experts but need to trust the output of their models. The paper's demonstrations of using BREAD for scientifically-motivated algorithm design (comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers) and for debugging PPL implementations provide compelling evidence of its practical importance. This work has the potential to become a standard tool for developers of PPLs and for researchers designing and validating new MCMC-based inference algorithms.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** It addresses a critical, unsolved problem in MCMC-based inference with a novel and theoretically grounded method. The connection established between the BDMC gap and Jeffreys divergence is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the empirical validation is thorough and convincing.
3.  **Practical Impact:** The implementation in Stan and WebPPL and the demonstrated use-cases for model comparison and debugging show clear practical utility for the machine learning and statistics communities.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

### Arguments against Acceptance
1.  **Limitation to Simulated Data:** The primary weakness is that the theoretical guarantees hold only for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a heuristic, and its reliability may be context-dependent, particularly in cases of severe model misspecification.
2.  **Limited Applicability:** The method applies directly to algorithms like AIS and SMC. While insights may generalize, it is not a direct diagnostic for standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling without embedding them within an AIS-like framework.
3.  **Computational Cost:** The method is computationally intensive, requiring numerous forward and reverse AIS runs, which may limit its use for routine, on-the-fly diagnostics by end-users.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a significant and original contribution to a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference. The limitations are clearly acknowledged and thoughtfully addressed. The work is poised to have a substantial impact on the practice of probabilistic modeling and the development of inference tools.
