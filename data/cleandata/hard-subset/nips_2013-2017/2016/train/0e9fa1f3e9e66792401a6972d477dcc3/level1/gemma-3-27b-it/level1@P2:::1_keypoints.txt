- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and MCMC samples.  
- Applies the method to WebPPL and Stan and uses it to inform model representation choices.  
- Builds on Grosse et al. (GGA15) and repurposes BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to sample-quality assessment.  
- Positions BREAD as providing rigorous bounds (on simulated data) versus heuristic MCMC diagnostics.  
- Acknowledges related work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka) and connections to AIS and SMC.  
- Theoretical derivation linking BDMC to an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is clear and well-motivated.  
- Addresses a practically important problem: evaluating reliability of MCMC in probabilistic programming.  
- Implementation in WebPPL and Stan is a substantial practical contribution.  
- Validation experiments show BREAD can detect inference-quality differences and revealed a real bug in WebPPL.  
- Paper is generally well-written with helpful background on AIS and BDMC.  
- Proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation.  
- High computational cost (particularly running the reverse chain) is a notable practical limitation.  
- The real-data protocol depends on the assumption simulated data from estimated hyperparameters represent real data and needs further validation.  
- Method is directly applicable only to AIS/SMC-like algorithms (importance sampling over extended state spaces).  
- Lacks a comprehensive empirical comparison with standard MCMC diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS) to clarify complementarity or superiority.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a core challenge, is theoretically sound, implemented in major systems, validated experimentally, and uncovered a bug.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational expense, strong assumptions in real-data protocol, and limited direct applicability.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to discuss computational trade-offs, test protocol robustness, and compare to existing convergence diagnostics.
