

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors build upon Grosse et al. (2015), who used BDMC to estimate marginal likelihoods, by deriving a method to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The key insight is that by running AIS chains both forward (from prior to posterior) and backward (from posterior to prior), one can obtain an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence in expectation. The authors integrate their method into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on multiple models, and demonstrate its utility for evaluating model representations and debugging inference implementations.

**Quality** - The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1) is rigorous and builds appropriately on the BDMC framework. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for exact posterior samples on simulated data) and validate their method on toy distributions where the true Jeffreys divergence can be computed exactly. The experimental validation (Section 5.1) demonstrates consistency between inference behavior on simulated and real data, and the protocol for using fitted hyperparameters to simulate data appears well-justified. The paper is thorough in addressing both strengths and weaknesses, and the integration into WebPPL and Stan provides practical evidence of the method's utility.

**Clarity** - The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section (Section 2) provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC, and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly with helpful explanations. The figures (particularly Fig. 1 and 2) effectively illustrate key findings, and the experimental section is well-structured. The authors make a strong effort to provide enough detail for experts to reproduce their results. The connection between the theoretical development and practical implementation is particularly clear.

**Originality** - The paper makes a significant contribution by extending BDMC to a new application domain: measuring the quality of MCMC inference. While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, this paper demonstrates how the same framework can be applied to evaluate posterior samples. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), by showing that their method provides rigorous guarantees for the Jeffreys divergence. The demonstration of how BREAD can guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and uncover bugs (Section 5.2.2) represents novel applications of the technique.

**Significance** - This paper addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference: the difficulty of measuring the reliability of approximate posterior samples, especially in black-box inference systems. The proposed method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming languages targeting non-expert users. The authors demonstrate that BREAD can provide actionable insights for model design (e.g., choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed representations) and debugging inference implementations. This has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of probabilistic programming tools, making them more trustworthy for practitioners.

**Strengths:**
- Solid theoretical contribution extending BDMC to a new application
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan with real-world validation
- Demonstrated utility for model design and debugging
- Clear presentation of technical content
- Thorough validation on both toy and real-world examples

**Weaknesses:**
- The method is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained (simulated data), requiring the authors' protocol for real-world applications
- The computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for large-scale applications
- The paper doesn't deeply explore the trade-offs between the accuracy of the bound and computational requirements

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical development is sound, the implementation is practical, and the results demonstrate clear value for both researchers and practitioners. While the method has limitations (primarily the need for simulated data), the authors provide a well-justified protocol for extending it to real-world applications. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel solution that has direct practical implications for probabilistic programming systems. I believe this work would be of substantial interest to the NIPS community and would make a valuable contribution to the conference.

The paper is technically rigorous, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in evaluating MCMC inference by providing a theoretically grounded method for measuring the reliability of approximate posterior samples. The integration with popular probabilistic programming languages and the demonstration of practical applications (model representation choices, debugging) significantly enhance the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance.
