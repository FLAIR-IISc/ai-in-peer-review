### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by certain MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core contribution is an extension of the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique, previously proposed by some of the same authors [GGA15]. While BDMC was designed to provide tight, sandwiching bounds on the log marginal likelihood, this work cleverly reinterprets the gap between the forward and reverse bounds. The authors show that the expectation of this gap provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and the true posterior.

The method's primary limitation is that it requires an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, restricting its rigorous application to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, run approximate inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; and finally, apply BREAD on this simulated dataset. The paper provides an implementation of BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages (PPLs), WebPPL and Stan, and validates the approach on several models.

The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence is a long-standing and critical one in machine learning and statistics, with classic methods like Gelman-Rubin diagnostics [GR92] being widely used. However, most existing diagnostics are heuristics that can indicate non-convergence but cannot provide a quantitative certificate of quality. BREAD is a step toward such a certificate. The work is also related to Gorham and Mackey [GM15], who use Stein's method for a similar goal but under stronger assumptions (e.g., strong convexity). The authors are also commendably upfront about concurrent, independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which shares the core idea of using a BDMC-like procedure to bound a divergence.

### Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation in Section 3.1, which establishes the connection between the BDMC gap and the Jeffreys divergence, is sound and follows directly from the properties of KL divergence and the formulation of AIS as importance sampling on an extended space. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors are careful to validate the key assumptions and potential weaknesses of their method. For instance:
1.  They show on toy examples (Appendix B) that the derived bound is reasonably tight in practice.
2.  They directly address the "simulation gap" by demonstrating that inference behavior (as measured by AIS estimates) on real data closely tracks the behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters (Section 5.1.1).
3.  They validate their proposed protocol for real-world data by showing that the results are robust to the number of MCMC steps used to generate the approximate posterior sample for the reverse chain initialization (Section 5.1.2).

This is a complete piece of work, moving from theory to implementation and practical demonstration. The authors are honest about the method's main limitation—its formal guarantees only hold for simulated data—and their proposed protocol is a well-reasoned and empirically justified heuristic.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The background section provides a clear and concise overview of AIS, stochastic bounds, and BDMC, making the paper largely self-contained. The logical flow from the BDMC framework to the BREAD divergence bound is presented clearly. The experimental design and results are explained well, and the figures are informative. The central ideas are communicated effectively, allowing an expert to grasp the method's mechanics and implications quickly.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly upon BDMC [GGA15], the idea of using the gap between the forward and reverse estimators to bound a statistical divergence is a novel and insightful contribution. This reframing of BDMC from a tool for evidence estimation to a tool for sample quality assessment is a significant conceptual leap. The application of this idea to the critical problem of evaluating black-box inference in PPLs is both timely and new. The authors properly contextualize their work with respect to concurrent research [CTM16], which reinforces the novelty and importance of the underlying idea.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. The evaluation of probabilistic inference is a major bottleneck for both practitioners and researchers. Practitioners using PPLs often have to rely on weak heuristics or blind faith to trust their results. Researchers developing new inference algorithms lack standardized, quantitative benchmarks. BREAD offers a principled and automated way to address these issues.

The two main use cases demonstrated in the paper highlight its significance:
1.  **Comparing Model Representations:** The experiment in Section 5.2.1 provides a concrete methodology for answering a common and difficult modeling question: which parameterization of a model is better for a given inference engine? The results provide clear, quantitative guidance.
2.  **Debugging Inference Systems:** The anecdote about discovering a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampler is a powerful testament to the method's utility. As PPLs become more complex, such rigorous, automated testing tools will be indispensable.

While the reliance on simulated data for formal guarantees is a limitation for end-users wishing to validate a single analysis, the proposed protocol makes it a powerful tool for system developers, algorithm designers, and sophisticated modelers for the purpose of benchmarking, debugging, and model selection. This work is likely to be built upon and could become a standard tool in the PPL developer's toolkit.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Insight:** Proposes a clever and original method for the important and unsolved problem of quantitatively evaluating MCMC sample quality.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivation is correct, and the claims are rigorously supported by a strong set of validation experiments.
*   **High Significance:** Addresses a critical need in the probabilistic programming and Bayesian inference communities. The demonstrated use cases for comparing models and debugging code are highly compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete package, including theory, implementation in two major frameworks (Stan and WebPPL), and compelling empirical demonstrations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Limitation to Simulated Data:** The primary weakness is that the rigorous guarantees only apply to simulated data. The protocol for real data is a heuristic, though a well-motivated and validated one.
*   **Scope of the Bound:** The derived bound is on the quality of samples from AIS, not necessarily the stationary distribution of a general MCMC algorithm (e.g., the NUTS sampler itself). The authors argue that insights from AIS performance are transferable, which is plausible but not formally established.

**Recommendation:**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, sound, and significant contribution to a difficult and important problem. The quality of the execution, from theory to empirical validation, is very high. This is a clear and strong accept.
