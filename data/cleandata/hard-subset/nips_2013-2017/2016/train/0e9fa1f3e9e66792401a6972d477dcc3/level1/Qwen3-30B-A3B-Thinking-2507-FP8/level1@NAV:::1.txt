

This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors build on Grosse et al. (2015), who introduced BDMC for estimating marginal likelihood, and extend it to provide an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence. The method requires exact posterior samples, which they obtain via simulation from the model distribution, and they propose a practical protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters. The authors integrate their technique into WebPPL and Stan, using it to analyze model representation choices and even uncover a bug in WebPPL.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous theoretical derivation showing that the BDMC estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The experimental validation on multiple models and datasets is thorough, with careful analysis of the accuracy of the bound and the validity of their real-world data protocol. The paper clearly addresses the limitations of traditional MCMC diagnostics, which cannot confirm convergence, while providing a method that can rigorously demonstrate convergence in the simulated data setting.

The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation of the mathematical derivations and experimental results. The paper adequately references related work, including Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), and distinguishes their contribution from prior methods. The originality is significant—extending BDMC to evaluate MCMC inference quality is novel, and the protocol for real-world data application represents a substantial practical contribution. The significance is high, as reliable evaluation of MCMC inference is a fundamental challenge in probabilistic modeling, and the method provides a general, automatic procedure for practitioners.

The main limitation is that the method requires simulated data for rigorous bounds, though the authors provide a reasonable protocol for real-world application. The approach is limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC), but these cover many important inference methods.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, practical integration with popular probabilistic programming languages, novel application to evaluate model representations, and demonstrated utility in debugging implementations.

Con: Limited to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC), requires simulated data for rigorous bounds (though protocol mitigates this), and the paper could better discuss the computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains.

This work makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a rigorous, general method for evaluating MCMC inference quality, which addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling. It should be accepted.
