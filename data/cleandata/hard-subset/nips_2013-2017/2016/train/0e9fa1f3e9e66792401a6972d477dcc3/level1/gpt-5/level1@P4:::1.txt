Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior p and the distribution q of approximate samples produced by annealed importance sampling (AIS) or related extended-state methods (e.g., SMC). The key identities show that E[log R̂] = log(ZT/Z1) − DKL(q||p) and E[log R̂rev] = log(Z1/ZT) − DKL(p||q), where R̂ and R̂rev are the AIS estimates in the forward and reverse directions, respectively. Thus, the expected gap between the reverse and forward log estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence DJ(p||q). The work further proposes a practical protocol to diagnose inference on real data by simulating datasets at hyperparameters fitted to the real data, and by initializing the reverse chain from an approximate posterior over those hyperparameters. The method is implemented in WebPPL and Stan, and validated on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and GMMs). Additional experiments explore model representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and demonstrate BREAD’s utility in debugging a probabilistic programming system.

This contribution builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), Jarzynski’s non-equilibrium work identity (Jarzynski, 1997), SMC samplers (Del Moral et al., 2006), and BDMC’s stochastic upper/lower bounds on log partition functions. It complements convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman) and sample quality measures based on Stein’s method (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). Closest concurrent work is Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), which uses a related “subjective divergence”; the present paper focuses on Jeffreys divergence with exact-posterior reverse initialization and analyzes the expectation bound.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Derivations: The core inequalities are correct and follow from AIS-as-importance-sampling, Jensen’s inequality, and the fact that marginalization cannot increase KL. The result that E[log R̂rev − log R̂] ≥ DJ(p||q) is correct. The paper is careful about what is guaranteed (in expectation) and when stochastic lower/upper bounds apply with high probability (for log partition functions).
- Scope of guarantees: The bound is an expectation bound; per-run guarantees on DJ are not provided. This limits the strength of certification from a finite number of runs unless one also provides uncertainty quantification for E[·]. The paper primarily visualizes quartiles; it would be beneficial to report sample means and confidence intervals for log estimates to directly reflect the expectation guarantees.
- Applicability: The method applies to AIS/SMC or other extended-state importance samplers; it does not directly certify plain MCMC without embedding it in such a scheme. Requiring an exact posterior sample restricts rigorous guarantees to simulated data. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data is well motivated but heuristic; the paper provides empirical support by showing matching forward-AIS behavior between real and simulated datasets and insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps used to obtain the approximate reverse initialization.
- Implementation details: Using Stan’s HMC/NUTS within AIS requires that transitions leave each intermediate pt invariant (no adaptation during transitions). Clarifying how adaptation was handled and whether reversibility/ergodicity conditions were ensured would strengthen the technical presentation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The AIS/BDMC background is clear, and the notation for forward/reverse chains and conditionals is consistent. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- State explicit assumptions on transition kernels (reversibility, ergodicity, no adaptation during AIS transitions).
- Provide guidance on estimating E[gap] in practice: number of forward/reverse runs, variance, and how to form confidence intervals for the expectation bound.
- Report means and standard errors for log estimates in addition to quartiles in figures.
- Summarize computational overhead and wall-clock cost to obtain bounds.

Originality
The extension of BDMC to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the AIS-induced approximate posterior is novel and useful. The proposed real-data protocol and integration into two probabilistic programming systems add practical value. The relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is discussed appropriately.

Significance
Quantitatively evaluating approximate posterior sample quality is important for probabilistic programming and MCMC practice. Providing expectation upper bounds on DJ(p||q) for AIS/SMC can guide algorithmic choices, detect inference failures, and even reveal implementation bugs. The experiments demonstrate applicability across models and show actionable insights (e.g., representation trade-offs in Stan vs. WebPPL). The approach is likely to influence both practitioners and tool builders, though its strongest guarantees remain limited to simulated data.

Strengths
- Clear, correct derivations linking BDMC gaps to Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Practical protocol for real data with empirical validation of behavior transfer from simulated to real datasets.
- Integration into WebPPL and Stan; demonstrated utility for model representation choices and debugging.
- Careful discussion of related work and limitations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Guarantees are in expectation; provide statistical estimation procedures and intervals for E[gap] from finite runs.
- Rigor is limited to simulated data; for real data, the fitted-hyperparameter reverse initialization is heuristic. Discuss conditions under which it might fail (e.g., severe prior–likelihood mismatch) and potential diagnostics.
- Tightness of the bound is shown on toy problems; add more challenging/multimodal benchmarks to assess tightness and variability.
- Clarify AIS kernel details in Stan (no adaptation during AIS, temperature schedules, step sizes) and in WebPPL (reversibility and detailed balance of trace moves at intermediate temperatures).
- Compare BREAD’s diagnostic signal to standard measures (R̂, ESS) and to Stein discrepancies to contextualize sensitivity and cost.
- Provide ablations on temperature schedules (# of intermediates) and transitions per temperature, and discuss how BREAD can be used to tune these choices.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with clear, implementable procedure.
- Addresses an important gap: objective measurement of MCMC sample quality.
- Demonstrated utility across systems/models; can guide modeling/inference choices and catch bugs.
- Well situated in prior literature and timely for probabilistic programming.

Arguments against acceptance
- Expectation-only bound limits per-instance certification; empirical estimation and uncertainty reporting are underdeveloped.
- Core guarantee applies only to simulated data; real-data protocol lacks formal justification.
- Experimental evaluation of bound tightness on complex posteriors is limited.

Overall, this is a technically sound and practically relevant contribution that advances quantitative assessment of MCMC-based inference, with clear potential impact despite the expectation-only nature of the bound and simulated-data requirement for rigorous guarantees.
