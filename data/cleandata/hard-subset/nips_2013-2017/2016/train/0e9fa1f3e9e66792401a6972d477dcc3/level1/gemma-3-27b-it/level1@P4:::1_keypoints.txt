- Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC to upper-bound the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence for evaluating MCMC posterior quality.
- Core method runs annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains forward (prior→posterior) and reverse to obtain the bound.
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, facilitating wider practical adoption.
- Validated on multiple models/datasets and used to compare model representations and guide inference design.
- Theoretical grounding: extends BDMC and links the method to Jeffreys divergence for a principled discrepancy measure.
- Uses simulated-data protocol (similar to parametric bootstrap) to extrapolate bounds to real-world data when exact posteriors are unavailable.
- Strength — technical soundness: AIS-based approach and importance-sampling connections are well-justified.
- Strength — novelty: novel application of BDMC to assess MCMC sample quality via Jeffreys divergence.
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written, motivated, and methodologically clear.
- Strength — significance: useful diagnostic for black-box probabilistic programming; demonstrated by finding a WebPPL bug.
- Weakness — applicability: limited to algorithms representable as importance sampling on extended state spaces (AIS/SMC).
- Weakness — real-world protocol: relies on fitted hyperparameters being representative of true data generation, which may fail.
- Weakness — computational cost: reverse AIS is expensive; paper lacks detailed overhead analysis or mitigation strategies.
- Weakness — limited empirical validation: could strengthen by comparing to existing convergence diagnostics and broader platforms beyond Stan/WebPPL.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practically integrated, useful for model design/debugging, addresses an important inference evaluation challenge.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited algorithmic applicability, questionable real-data assumptions, potentially prohibitive computational cost.
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better analyze computational cost and discuss limitations of the real-data protocol.
