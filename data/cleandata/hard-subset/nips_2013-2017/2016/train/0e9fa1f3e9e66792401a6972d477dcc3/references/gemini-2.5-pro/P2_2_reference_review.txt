### Summary

This paper introduces Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), a method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained by running AIS in the forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions, provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. Since this method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, it is rigorously applicable only to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data, then use these to generate a simulated dataset for which BREAD can be applied. The method is implemented and validated in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and is used to provide practical guidance on model representation choices and to debug an inference implementation.

### Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that addresses a significant and long-standing problem in the MCMC and probabilistic programming communities. The theoretical contribution is elegant, the proposed practical protocol is well-reasoned, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing.

**Quality: (Excellent)**

The paper is technically sound. The central theoretical result, deriving an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence from the BDMC framework (Eqs. 8, 11, and 12), is a simple but clever extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). The logic is clear and well-presented.

The experimental work is a major strength. The authors don't just present the method; they carefully validate its underlying assumptions and demonstrate its utility.
1.  **Validation of the real-data protocol:** The experiments in Section 5.1.1 provide strong evidence that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on the real data itself (Fig. 1). This is crucial for the method's practical relevance.
2.  **Robustness:** The authors astutely check whether using an *approximate* posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain (a necessary step in their real-data protocol) significantly affects the results. The finding that the reverse AIS estimates are insensitive to the number of MCMC burn-in steps (Section 5.1.2) lends confidence to this heuristic.
3.  **Practical Application:** The use of BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is an excellent case study. It yields a non-obvious, platform-dependent conclusion (collapsed is better for WebPPL, uncollapsed for Stan), demonstrating that BREAD can provide concrete, actionable guidance for practitioners.
4.  **Debugging:** The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's value. Rigorous, quantitative tools that can reveal implementation errors are highly valuable to the community.

One minor weakness is that the main result is an upper bound *in expectation*. This means that for any single run, the estimated gap `B_hat` is not guaranteed to be greater than the true Jeffreys divergence `J`. The authors could be slightly more explicit about this limitation and its practical implications, perhaps suggesting that averaging `B_hat` over several runs is advisable to get a more stable estimate of the bound `B`.

**Clarity: (Excellent)**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and summarizes the contributions. The background section provides the necessary context on AIS and BDMC in a concise manner. The core method is derived step-by-step in Section 3.1. The experimental sections are logical and the figures are clear and supportive of the claims. The writing is precise, making it relatively easy for an expert to follow the derivations and understand the experimental setup.

**Originality: (Very Good)**

The paper builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015). However, the key contribution—reinterpreting the BDMC log-likelihood gap as a bound on the Jeffreys divergence—is novel and significant. It reframes BDMC from a tool for estimating marginal likelihoods to a tool for diagnosing posterior inference quality, which is a different and arguably more common problem for MCMC users.

The authors are also commendably honest and thorough in discussing related work, particularly the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016). They clearly explain the relationship between the two papers, noting the equivalence of the core idea when the reference distribution is the true posterior. The proposed protocol for handling real-world data and the extensive validation in Stan and WebPPL appear to be unique contributions of this work.

**Significance: (Excellent)**

The work is highly significant. The lack of reliable, quantitative, and automated convergence diagnostics is a major pain point for both novice and expert users of MCMC and probabilistic programming languages. While existing diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin can detect non-convergence, they cannot confirm convergence or quantify the error in the resulting approximation. BREAD provides a principled step in this direction.

The potential impact is broad:
*   **For PPL users:** It offers a method to gain confidence in the results of a black-box inference engine.
*   **For algorithm/PPL developers:** It provides a powerful tool for regression testing and debugging, as demonstrated by the WebPPL example.
*   **For researchers:** It enables principled comparisons between different MCMC transition operators or model representations, moving beyond simple metrics like effective sample size, which can be misleading.

While the method is currently limited to algorithms like AIS/SMC and is only strictly rigorous for simulated data, the proposed protocol makes it widely applicable in practice. This work is very likely to be built upon and used by others in the field.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Solves an important problem:** Provides a much-needed quantitative measure for MCMC inference quality.
*   **Novel and elegant idea:** The connection between the BDMC likelihood gap and Jeffreys divergence is a valuable insight.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** The experiments are thorough, covering validation of the method's assumptions and demonstrating clear, practical utility in model selection and debugging.
*   **High potential for impact:** The tool could become a standard part of the workflow for developing and using probabilistic programming systems.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The core theoretical guarantee (the bound on divergence) is only rigorous for simulated data, and the real-data application relies on a well-justified but still heuristic protocol.
*   The bound holds in expectation, not with high probability for a single run, which is a slight technical weakness.
*   The method is not universally applicable to all MCMC algorithms (e.g., standard HMC or Metropolis-Hastings), although the authors argue the insights are transferable.

These weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths and are transparently discussed by the authors. The paper represents a complete and significant contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
