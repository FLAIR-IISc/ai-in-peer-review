

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) that provides rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors extend the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015), which was originally designed to estimate marginal likelihoods, to evaluate the accuracy of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The key insight is that BDMC can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, which is particularly valuable for assessing the quality of posterior samples produced by methods like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The paper also proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, which addresses a major limitation of previous approaches that required exact posterior samples.

The paper makes four main technical contributions: (1) a theoretical derivation showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) validation of the bound using exact computations on toy distributions; (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data; and (4) integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan with demonstrations on multiple probabilistic models. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of BREAD by comparing different model representations in matrix factorization models and even uncovering a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampling implementation.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient background for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC. The theoretical derivation is sound, with careful attention to the mathematical details of how the bounds relate to the Jeffreys divergence. The experimental validation is thorough, with five different models across the Stan manual and careful analysis of the consistency between real and simulated data. The application to probabilistic programming languages is particularly significant, as it addresses a major challenge in the field: how to assess the reliability of black-box inference.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work. It acknowledges the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) and explains how their approach differs from BREAD (specifically, BREAD uses exact posterior samples for the reverse chain initialization, while their method uses an arbitrary reference distribution). The paper also distinguishes itself from traditional convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998) by providing rigorous bounds rather than heuristic diagnostics, and from Gorham and Mackey (2015) by making no assumptions about the distribution's structure.

The main limitations are: (1) BREAD is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained, though the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data; (2) the protocol relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters generate representative simulated data, which may not hold for all models; and (3) the computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for very large models.

In terms of novelty, the paper extends the BDMC framework to a new application domain (evaluating posterior samples rather than marginal likelihoods) and provides a practical protocol for real-world use. While the theoretical foundation builds on existing work, the specific application to probabilistic programming and the demonstration of its utility in model selection and debugging represent meaningful contributions to the field.

The significance of this work is high, as it provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference in probabilistic programming systems. This directly addresses a major challenge in making probabilistic programming accessible to non-expert users. The paper demonstrates that BREAD can guide modeling decisions (e.g., choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed representations) and even uncover implementation bugs, which is a compelling practical contribution.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical derivation and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient background to be accessible to NIPS readers. The mathematical derivations are presented logically and the experimental results are well-organized.

Originality: The application of BDMC to evaluate posterior sample quality is novel, as is the protocol for real-world data. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, including the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

Significance: The method addresses a significant problem in probabilistic programming and has clear practical utility for both users and developers of inference algorithms. The demonstration of debugging capabilities in WebPPL highlights the method's value beyond just evaluation.

Strengths: Theoretical contribution is sound, experimental validation is thorough, practical utility is well-demonstrated, and the paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses: The method's applicability to real-world data requires additional assumptions that may not always hold, and the computational cost may be a limitation for large-scale applications.

Pro acceptance: Provides a rigorous, theoretically grounded method for evaluating inference quality in probabilistic programming, with demonstrated practical utility in model selection and debugging.

Con rejection: The limitations regarding real-world applicability (requiring simulated data and fitted hyperparameters) may restrict the method's immediate utility for some practitioners.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in evaluating MCMC inference. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant problem in probabilistic programming. I recommend acceptance.
