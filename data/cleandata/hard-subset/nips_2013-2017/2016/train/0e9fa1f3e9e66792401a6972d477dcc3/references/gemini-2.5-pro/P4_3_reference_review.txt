### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The central problem addressed is the long-standing difficulty of rigorously diagnosing the convergence and accuracy of MCMC samplers. BREAD builds upon the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15], which provides stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood by running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both forward and reverse directions.

The key theoretical contribution of this work is to show that the expected gap between the BDMC upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood serves as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. This provides a direct, quantitative measure of sample quality.

Since the reverse AIS pass required for the upper bound necessitates an exact sample from the true posterior, the method is rigorously applicable only to simulated data. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: 1) perform approximate inference on the real dataset to obtain point estimates of hyperparameters; 2) simulate a new dataset from the model using these hyperparameters; 3) run BREAD on this simulated dataset to measure inference quality; 4) validate that the sampler's behavior is consistent between the real and simulated data.

The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate its utility. They demonstrate that inference behavior on simulated data (using their protocol) closely matches that on real data for several models. They then use BREAD to provide concrete guidance on modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization) and demonstrate its utility as a debugging tool by identifying a bug in WebPPL's sampler.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation extending the analysis of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is sound and clearly presented. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across five different models using the Stan platform. The authors are careful in their evaluation, validating the key assumptions of their proposed protocol for real-world data (consistency of sampler behavior and robustness to the approximate posterior sample). The work is a complete contribution, including theory, a practical protocol, implementation, and compelling use-case demonstrations.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, making the paper accessible. The main theoretical result in Section 3.1 is derived step-by-step and is easy to follow. Figures are informative, and the experimental setup and conclusions are explained thoroughly. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the existing literature on convergence diagnostics.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—connecting the BDMC log-likelihood gap to the Jeffreys divergence—is a novel and insightful extension of the work in [GGA15]. While building on existing methods (AIS, BDMC), the application to quantitatively bound posterior approximation error is new. The proposed protocol for applying this diagnostic to real-world data is also a novel and pragmatic contribution. The authors are also commendably forthright about related independent work [CTM16], clearly explaining the relationship between the two approaches.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance to the machine learning and statistics communities. As probabilistic programming and Bayesian methods become more widespread, the need for reliable, automated tools to assess inference quality is critical. BREAD offers a more rigorous alternative to traditional convergence heuristics. The successful application to compare model representations and to debug a probabilistic programming language implementation strongly demonstrates the potential impact of this work. Researchers developing new inference algorithms and practitioners using PPLs are both likely to find this method useful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Direct Applicability:** The method is directly applicable to inference algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. It cannot be used to directly diagnose a standard MCMC algorithm (e.g., HMC or Gibbs) on its own. While the authors correctly state that insights from diagnosing AIS (which uses MCMC kernels internally) can be generalized, the diagnostic is on the full AIS procedure, not just the transition kernel's mixing properties. This limitation could be stated more explicitly.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data relies on a heuristic. Simulating data from point estimates of hyperparameters assumes the posterior is well-behaved and that this single synthetic dataset is representative. While the authors provide compelling validation (Sec 5.1.1, 5.1.2), this may not hold for complex, multimodal posteriors or models with significant misspecification. This is an inherent difficulty, but it remains a limitation on the rigor of the method for real-world (non-simulated) problems.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires running many forward and reverse AIS chains, which can be computationally expensive, likely much more so than the original inference run one wishes to diagnose. A discussion of the practical wall-clock time trade-offs would strengthen the paper and provide users with a better sense of when the method is practical to apply.

4.  **Use of Individual Chain Estimates:** As noted in footnote 3, the analysis requires reporting statistics over individual AIS chains rather than using the average of the importance weights, which is standard practice to reduce estimator variance. This results in a distribution of bounds (visualized as quartiles in the figures) rather than a single, tighter estimate of the bound. It is unclear how a user should interpret this distribution of bounds in practice. Further discussion on this point or potential extensions to handle averaged estimates would be beneficial.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and long-standing problem in probabilistic inference.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound method for quantitatively bounding posterior approximation error.
*   Provides a practical, well-validated protocol for applying the method to real-world data.
*   The utility of the method is convincingly demonstrated through experiments that guide modeling decisions and identify an implementation bug.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against:**
*   The method's rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data, and its direct applicability is restricted to certain classes of samplers like AIS/SMC.
*   The computational cost may be prohibitive for very large models.
*   The practical interpretation of the resulting distribution of bounds could be clearer.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a significant and original contribution to a fundamentally important problem. The proposed method, BREAD, is a valuable new tool for researchers and practitioners in Bayesian inference.
