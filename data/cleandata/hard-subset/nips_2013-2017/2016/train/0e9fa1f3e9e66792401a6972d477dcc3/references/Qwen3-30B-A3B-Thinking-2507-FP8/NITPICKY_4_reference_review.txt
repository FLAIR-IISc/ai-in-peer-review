

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that BREAD can be used to rigorously evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms like AIS and SMC, and integrate their method into WebPPL and Stan.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, which is a more comprehensive measure of sampling quality than unidirectional divergences. The integration with WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility, and the method successfully identified a bug in WebPPL. The validation experiments in Section 5.1.1 provide evidence that simulated data with fitted hyperparameters can serve as a reasonable proxy for real data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and Reference Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies in citations and references. For example, references cite "GGA15" in the text but "Grosse et al.,? \Q2015\E" in the bibliography. The reference "CGHL+ p" appears inconsistently throughout the text. These are basic presentation errors that undermine the paper's credibility.

2. **Technical Errors:** Equation (7) in Section 3.1 contains a formatting error: "Eqfwd(v,xT ) log fT(xT) qrev(v|xT) Z1 qfwd(v,xT)". The LaTeX formatting is broken, making the equation difficult to parse. Equation (8) incorrectly states "logZT logZ1 DKL(qfwd(xT) qfwd(v|xT) k pT(xT) qrev(v|xT))" where the KL divergence notation should be clearer (e.g., DKL(qfwd(xT) || pT(xT))).

3. **Misrepresentation of Method Scope:** The paper claims BREAD evaluates "MCMC inference" but is only applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). This limitation is underemphasized in the introduction, potentially misleading readers about the method's generality.

4. **Insufficient Validation of Key Assumption:** Section 3.2 proposes using fitted hyperparameters to generate simulated data, but Section 5.1.2 only validates that the reverse AIS curves are statistically indistinguishable for different numbers of MCMC steps (S=10, 100, 1000, 10000). It does not validate that the fitted hyperparameters correspond to the true posterior or that the simulated data truly resembles the real data. The paper claims "we expect even a small number of MCMC steps to produce a plausible posterior sample," but provides no evidence supporting this expectation.

5. **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper fails to provide clear guidance on how to interpret the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in practice. While the theoretical guarantee is valuable, the paper doesn't explain how users of probabilistic programming languages should use this bound to make decisions about model or algorithm choices.

6. **Confusing Terminology:** The paper introduces BDMC as a method, then refers to the new method as "BREAD," which is confusing since BREAD is a specific application of BDMC. The paper also uses inconsistent terminology (e.g., "reverse AIS" vs. "reverse chain").

7. **Inadequate Comparison with Related Work:** The paper references [CTM16] but doesn't clearly explain the differences between their method and [CTM16] beyond the reverse chain initialization. The paper claims "BREAD makes no assumptions about the distribution itself," but this is not rigorously justified.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of BDMC with some practical applications, the numerous technical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and insufficient validation of key assumptions significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The method is more limited in scope than the paper suggests, and the practical utility is not well-explained. The errors in equations and inconsistent presentation are particularly concerning for a paper that claims to provide rigorous bounds.

The paper's strengths (theoretical contribution, integration with probabilistic programming languages, bug identification) are overshadowed by these fundamental issues. For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it must demonstrate both theoretical soundness and careful presentation, which this paper fails to do.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's technical errors and insufficient validation of key assumptions make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
