Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based approximate posterior sampling (not just marginal likelihood estimation). For algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling on an extended state space—primarily AIS and, in principle, SMC—the authors show that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log marginal likelihood estimates yields an estimator whose expectation upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of the AIS terminal state and the true posterior. They term the overall protocol BREAD. Because the reverse run needs an exact posterior sample, they focus on simulated data (via ancestral sampling) and propose a pragmatic “fitted hyperparameters” protocol to assess real-data performance: infer hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data at those values, and compare inference behavior. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate tightness on toy problems, show consistency between simulated and real-data behavior across five models, use BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization, and report uncovering a WebPPL bug.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and its reverse-AIS construction (Neal/Jarzynski), and relates to thermodynamic integration and tempered transitions. The main novelty is to reinterpret the BDMC sandwich as an estimator that upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) and pT(x). It is closely related to the “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which also leverages BDMC-like forward/reverse procedures but allows arbitrary reference initializations for the reverse chain. The paper contrasts with sample-quality diagnostics like Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey), and with standard MCMC convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman), emphasizing that BREAD provides quantitative accuracy guarantees on simulated data.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a simple, principled derivation showing E[log R̂rev−1 − log R̂] ≥ DKL(p||q)+DKL(q||p). This connects marginal likelihood sandwiching to posterior-sample quality in a way that is broadly understandable and useful.
- Practical integration into two platforms (WebPPL and Stan) and use on several canonical models, including a concrete case study (matrix factorization) that demonstrates how BREAD informs modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed).
- Sensible protocol for real-data diagnosis via fitted hyperparameters and simulated data, with evidence that simulated and real-data AIS behavior match well in the reported settings.
- Useful negative capability: the method helped uncover a subtle implementation bug, underscoring its value for PPL testing and verification.
- Good positioning relative to related work; the overlap with subjective divergence is acknowledged and the relationship is clarified.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Expectation-only guarantee: The bound is only in expectation over the AIS randomness (and, strictly, over the reverse initialization from pT). In practice, the paper reports quartiles of single-run estimates, which neither target the expectation nor provide high-probability guarantees on the Jeffreys divergence. Heavy tails of log-weights exacerbate the gap between typical and mean behavior.
- Dependence on exact posterior samples: The rigorous guarantee requires exact posterior initializations, restricting formal claims to simulated data. The “fitted hyperparameters” workaround for real data is heuristic; while the empirical checks across five models are reassuring, there is no theory controlling the additional error when the reverse chain is initialized from an approximate reference. The paper cites subjective divergence but does not derive a bound that accounts for mismatch between the reference and the true posterior.
- Generality beyond AIS: Although the introduction claims applicability to SMC and other extended-state IS algorithms, the experiments and derivations are AIS-centric. SMC resampling and path choices introduce additional complications (e.g., the definition of qfwd(xT)), which are not empirically addressed.
- Sensitivity and robustness: There is little systematic exploration of how the bound behaves under multimodality, poor tempering schedules, or weakly mixing kernels—precisely the regimes where AIS can struggle. The toy examples in Appendix B are mentioned as “often a good match,” but quantitative characterization of tightness and failure modes is limited.
- Estimation practice: Since the estimator is an expectation, the paper should more carefully discuss how many reverse/forward runs are needed, how to estimate E[gap] with uncertainty under heavy tails (e.g., median-of-means, tail-robust estimators), and when to expect reliable conclusions under a fixed computational budget.
- Minor theoretical clarity: The discussion about having only a single sample from p1 (or from the posterior for reverse AIS) could be clearer about the conditions under which unbiasedness/expectation identities hold (unconditional vs. conditional on the fixed initial sample).

Quality
Technically sound derivation for AIS, with correct use of the extended-state argument and data-processing inequality. The empirical evidence is helpful but somewhat limited in scope (no SMC experiments; limited stress tests). The fitted-hyperparameters protocol is well-motivated but lacks theoretical control.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and helpful intuition. A few citation placeholders and minor typos remain. It would help to explicitly state the expectations (over which randomness), clarify the practical estimation of E[gap], and more prominently caution that single-run gaps are not guaranteed upper bounds.

Originality
The core idea—repurposing BDMC’s sandwich to bound the Jeffreys divergence of AIS terminal samples—is a neat, incremental contribution with practical relevance. The integration into PPLs and the real-data protocol are useful engineering contributions. Overlap with subjective divergence is acknowledged; the difference here is the insistence on exact posterior initialization for a guaranteed upper bound.

Significance
Useful for the PPL and MCMC communities: provides a way to quantify sample quality for AIS-like samplers and to compare design choices. The constraints (simulated data; expectation-only) limit the strongest claims, but the tool can still shape practice, expose bugs, and guide model representation choices.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide empirical estimation guidance: number of AIS runs, robust mean estimation under heavy tails, and confidence intervals for E[gap].
- Characterize sensitivity to temperature schedules and transition kernels; include a stress test on a multimodal target where forward AIS is known to be biased/high variance.
- Either include SMC experiments or temper claims of generality; discuss how resampling affects qfwd(xT) and the bound.
- The fitted-hyperparameter protocol: quantify mismatch between the reference and posterior (e.g., via a Stein discrepancy or bridge sampling diagnostics) and relate this to the bias in the reverse bound; or adopt the subjective divergence framing and explicitly report the additional term.
- Clarify whether reverse runs reuse a single exact posterior sample or resample it each time; discuss the implication for unbiasedness and estimation of expectations.

Minor issues
- Several citations show as placeholders; please fix.
- Figure descriptions mention quartiles due to outliers; add means with robust CI if feasible.
- Make code and scripts available to ensure reproducibility.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper makes a clear, useful contribution that will interest the MCMC and PPL communities, despite limitations. Strengthening the empirical methodology around expectation estimation and better scoping the SMC claims would further improve it.
