This paper proposes BREAD, a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via AIS or SMC) and the true posterior, leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo. While the core idea of extending BDMC for this purpose is logical, and the theoretical analysis appears sound, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of compelling evidence beyond toy examples and a significant reliance on simulation for validation. The presented work feels incremental rather than a substantial contribution to the field.

The primary strength of the paper lies in the formalization of BREAD and the derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The connection to existing work on BDMC and the explanation of how to apply it to probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan are clearly articulated. The identification and debugging of a bug in WebPPL is a positive, albeit somewhat serendipitous, outcome. 

However, several weaknesses raise serious concerns. First, the applicability is restricted to algorithms amenable to an importance sampling interpretation, like AIS or SMC. While the authors argue these algorithms share design choices with general MCMC methods, the direct relevance to broader MCMC settings remains unclear. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how insights gained from BREAD on AIS/SMC would generalize to, for example, Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling. 

Second, and more crucially, the empirical validation is limited. While the authors show that the upper bound is "a good match" on toy distributions (Appendix B), the complexity of real-world Bayesian models is far greater. The validation on the five models from the Stan manual (Section 5.1) is primarily focused on demonstrating *consistency* between real and simulated data, rather than the *accuracy* of the divergence bound itself.  The observation that forward AIS chains match, while reassuring, doesn’t speak to the tightness of the Jeffreys divergence bound. The authors acknowledge that the reverse AIS curves are statistically indistinguishable across different MCMC step numbers (S), but this simply indicates robustness of the simulation setup, not a validation of BREAD’s accuracy in capturing divergence. The claim that the results provide a means of choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed model representations feels weak given the limited scope of the experiments.

The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, involving fitting hyperparameters and simulating from them, is a clever attempt to circumvent the need for exact posterior samples. However, it introduces another layer of approximation, and the authors’ assessment that the approximate samples are “sufficiently representative” feels subjective.

The related work section is adequate, but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of existing MCMC convergence diagnostics, beyond simply stating that BREAD offers rigorous bounds that diagnostics do not.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Formalizes a logical extension of BDMC for quantifying MCMC accuracy.
*   Provides a useful tool for debugging probabilistic programming language implementations.
*   Offers a potential framework for automatically assessing the reliability of probabilistic inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited applicability beyond AIS/SMC.
*   Insufficient empirical validation on complex, real-world models to demonstrate the practical value of the Jeffreys divergence bound.
*   Reliance on simulation and fitted hyperparameters introduces significant approximations.
*   The significance of the work feels incremental, and the connection to broader MCMC methods is tenuous.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

While the paper presents a technically sound method, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and limited scope undermine its significance. The claims regarding the practical utility of BREAD for assessing MCMC accuracy are not sufficiently supported. I believe further work is needed to demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on a wider range of models and datasets before it can be considered a substantial contribution to the field. I would encourage the authors to focus on more challenging real-world applications and to provide a more thorough evaluation of the accuracy and tightness of the derived bounds.
