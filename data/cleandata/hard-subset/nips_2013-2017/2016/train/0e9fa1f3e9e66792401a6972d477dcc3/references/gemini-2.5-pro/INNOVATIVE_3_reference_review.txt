**Summary**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The work builds upon the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which was originally developed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key and clever insight of this paper is to show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, produced by forward and reverse AIS runs, provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution of the samples and the true posterior. Since BREAD requires an exact posterior sample to initialize its reverse run, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate a new dataset from this posterior predictive distribution, and then apply BREAD, empirically validating that inference behavior is consistent between the real and simulated settings. The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to provide concrete guidance on model representation choices and even to uncover a bug in an implementation.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and ambition. The problem of validating MCMC output is as old as MCMC itself, and has largely been dominated by heuristic diagnostics that can only flag non-convergence. This paper proposes a completely different and far more powerful approach: a direct, quantitative bound on the error of the approximate posterior, measured by a principled metric (Jeffreys divergence). The conceptual leap of repurposing a marginal likelihood estimator (BDMC) to measure sample quality is both elegant and significant. This is not an incremental improvement on existing diagnostics; it is a new direction for thinking about inference validation.

The significance of this contribution cannot be overstated. With the proliferation of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs), the need for automated, reliable, and interpretable checks on inference quality is more critical than ever. BREAD offers a path toward this "push-button" validation, empowering users to trust the output of black-box inference and guiding developers in designing better models and algorithms. The experiments powerfully demonstrate this potential: the comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization provides clear, actionable insights, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a compelling testament to the method's utility as a rigorous debugging tool.

The paper is also exceptionally clear and well-written. The theoretical derivation is presented cleanly, and the experimental design is thoughtful and convincing. The authors are commendably transparent about the method's main limitation: the requirement of an exact posterior sample, which restricts its formal guarantees to the simulated data setting.

This limitation is also the paper's primary weakness. The protocol for real-world data is a heuristic, and it breaks the very guarantees that make the method so appealing in the first place. A reviewer focused on methodological perfection might see this as a fatal flaw. However, in my view, this is an acceptable and pragmatic compromise. The authors do not simply propose the heuristic; they take pains to validate it, showing that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches that on real data, and that the results are robust to the quality of the approximate posterior sample used for initialization. This is a thoughtful approach to a difficult problem. To have a rigorous tool that works on realistic simulated data is a massive step forward, and the proposed protocol provides a reasonable bridge to practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **High Novelty and Originality:** The central idea of using the BDMC bound gap to measure Jeffreys divergence is a significant and non-obvious conceptual contribution. It opens up a new avenue for research in inference diagnostics.
2.  **Major Significance:** The paper addresses a foundational, difficult, and highly important problem in machine learning and statistics. A tool like BREAD could fundamentally improve the reliability and accessibility of probabilistic programming.
3.  **Strong Practical Utility:** The method is not just a theoretical curiosity. Its implementation and successful application in Stan and WebPPL to guide modeling decisions and find bugs demonstrate its immediate value.
4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is well-written, and the authors are upfront about the limitations of their approach, providing empirical validation for their proposed heuristic solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1.  **Limited Formal Guarantees:** The rigorous bounds only apply to simulated data. The protocol for real-world data is a heuristic, which may not be reliable in all cases (e.g., severe model misspecification).

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty of the core idea and its potential impact on the field far outweigh the limitation of its formal guarantees to simulated data. This is precisely the kind of bold, creative, and insightful work that NIPS should champion. It tackles a grand challenge with a beautiful new idea, provides a practical implementation, and opens the door to a great deal of future research. While the bridge to real-world data is heuristic, it is a well-reasoned and empirically supported first step. This paper provides the community with both a new tool and a new way to think about a fundamental problem.
