Summary
This paper extends BDMC to assess the reliability of AIS/SMC-based posterior inference by showing that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log ratio estimators upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the target posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The method (BREAD) yields rigorous bounds on simulated data (where an exact posterior sample is available via ancestral sampling) and proposes a practical protocol for real data by fitting hyperparameters to real data, simulating datasets from those hyperparameters, and initializing the reverse chain with a short MCMC run. Implementations in WebPPL and Stan are provided. Empirically, the authors (i) validate the similarity of forward AIS behavior on real vs simulated data, (ii) show insensitivity of reverse bounds to the number of MCMC steps used to initialize from fitted hyperparameters, (iii) use BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization in both systems, and (iv) uncover a WebPPL bug.

Relation to prior work: Builds on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and connects to AIS (Neal, 2001), SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006), and convergence diagnostics. Overlaps with concurrent “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension: a simple, useful link between BDMC sandwiching and an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for approximate samples.
- Practical tooling: Integrations with two PPLs; the debugging story is compelling.
- Sensible real-data protocol and initial validation (forward curves align; reverse estimates insensitive to number of MCMC steps used to initialize).
- The analysis uses only mild assumptions (reversibility/invariance of kernels), and the idea is potentially impactful for evaluating automatic inference.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/theoretical
- The key guarantee is only in expectation: E[log Rhat_rev^{-1} − log Rhat] ≥ J. The paper frequently interprets single-run gaps as “rigorous” diagnostics, but a single realization need not upper bound J; high variance/heavy tails of AIS log-weights exacerbate this. Please quantify variability and provide procedures to obtain high-probability bounds (e.g., averaging multiple independent reverse runs with independent exact posterior samples; robust estimators like median-of-means).
- Conditions for the main inequality are not clearly stated. Formalize assumptions (absolute continuity, ergodicity, reversibility, correctness of MCMC kernels for intermediate distributions), and present a theorem with a proof. Eqs. (6–11) compress several steps; clarify the measure with respect to which KL is taken, and justify the marginalization inequality carefully.
- Applicability to SMC is claimed but not demonstrated. No SMC experiments, and no discussion of particle degeneracy effects on the bound’s tightness/variance.
- Real-data protocol lacks theoretical justification. Initializing the reverse chain with an approximate posterior sample discards the BDMC guarantee. The sensitivity study over S steps is reassuring but insufficient: no bias quantification, no failure modes (e.g., weakly identifiable hyperparameters, concentrated priors), and no diagnostics that (η?,θ?) is close to p(η,θ|D).
- Use of NUTS in AIS: How do you ensure each transition leaves p_t invariant? Was adaptation disabled (it must be), step-size fixed across t, and momentum refreshed? If NUTS parameters differ across t, reversibility/invariance may fail. This needs to be explicit.
- Path/schedule choice: No analysis of how temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, or per-stage kernel effort affect bound tightness and variance. This is crucial for practitioners.

Experimental/reporting
- The main quantity of interest (expected bound on Jeffreys divergence) is not directly reported on realistic problems. Instead, plots show distributions of log-marginal estimates, quartiles, and vertically shifted curves. Please report estimates of E[log Rhat_rev^{-1} − log Rhat] with uncertainty, and translate to nats per dimension when appropriate.
- The choice to visualize quartiles (not means) is understandable for heavy tails, but then the paper concludes about divergence bounds without any aggregation strategy aligned with the expectation guarantee. Provide CIs over means (e.g., via repeated exact-posterior draws on multiple simulated datasets).
- Comparisons to SMC are absent despite claims of applicability. The matrix factorization study is informative but limited to one size; no runtime/mixing diagnostics; no acceptance rates or leapfrog counts for HMC/NUTS; no reproducibility details (seeds, K, T, schedule).
- The forward-AIS alignment on real vs simulated data after vertical shift is a weak proxy for inference quality matching. Consider additional diagnostics (e.g., compare posterior summaries, effective sample sizes, or the distribution of final-state distances for matched seeds).
- Single exact posterior sample: to estimate expectations reliably, multiple independent simulated datasets and exact posterior draws are needed. It’s unclear how many were used; please clarify.

Claims/positioning
- “Rigorous, general, and automatic” is overstated for real data; the only rigorous guarantee is on simulated data with exact posterior samples. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic.
- Scope: the method evaluates algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended spaces (e.g., AIS/SMC with bridging). It does not directly assess vanilla stationary MCMC on the posterior; the introduction could be more precise to avoid overgeneralization.

Presentation/clarity
- Several notational/typographical issues hinder readability:
  - Inconsistent/malformed citations (e.g., “GS; CGHL+ p”, “GMRB+08”, missing years).
  - Stray line “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)…” mid-text.
  - Eq. (2): “1 = 0 and T = 1” likely means β1=0, βT=1; clarify notation.
  - Occasional typos: “qfwd(pT)” in Sec. 3.1, missing subscripts/superscripts, u>i vj should be u_i^T v_j.
  - Footnote “we sometimes have access to a sample from p1, but not a sampler” is problematic: AIS requires an initial draw per chain; if only one sample is available, independence across chains is violated—please clarify how you handled this.
- Missing implementation details: number of temperatures, schedule, number of transitions per stage, acceptance rates, total compute, and code/seed availability.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide a formal theorem with assumptions and a complete proof that E[log Rhat_rev^{-1} − log Rhat] ≥ J, and a procedure to obtain high-probability upper bounds via repeated simulated datasets?
2. How were NUTS transitions configured at intermediate temperatures to ensure invariance/reversibility? Was adaptation disabled?
3. How many independent exact posterior samples (simulated datasets) were used to estimate expectations? How were variances estimated?
4. Can you include SMC experiments and discuss how resampling affects bound tightness?
5. Can you report actual estimated divergence bounds (means with CIs) for your realistic models?

Pros
- Simple, appealing connection; potentially useful tool for PPL users.
- Practical systems integration; real bug found.
- Sensible idea to guide modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed).

Cons
- Key guarantee only in expectation; no uncertainty quantification; heavy-tail issues not addressed.
- Real-data protocol heuristic; lacks theoretical backing and deeper validation.
- Missing SMC experiments; limited empirical scope and reporting.
- Clarity/formatting issues and missing implementation details.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is promising and likely impactful with stronger theory-to-practice bridging, fuller experiments (including SMC), rigorous uncertainty quantification, and improved clarity.
