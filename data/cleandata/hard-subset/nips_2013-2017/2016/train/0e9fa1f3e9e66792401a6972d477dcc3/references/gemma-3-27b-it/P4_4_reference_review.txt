This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The core idea builds upon previous work on BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods, extending it to assess the accuracy of inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from hyperparameters estimated from the real data and then evaluating inference quality on the simulated data. They implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate its use for guiding modeling and algorithmic decisions, specifically comparing different representations of a matrix factorization model.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in MCMC and probabilistic programming: reliable validation of inference quality. The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a natural and valuable step. The proposed protocol for real-world data analysis offers a pragmatic approach to overcoming the limitation of requiring exact posterior samples. The implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages increases the accessibility and potential impact of the work.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness, clear presentation, and practical relevance. The mathematical derivation connecting BDMC to the Jeffreys divergence bound appears rigorous, and the empirical validation on toy distributions (mentioned but detailed in the appendix) supports the theoretical claims. The inclusion of experiments in both WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the versatility of the approach. The application to model representation comparison is a concrete illustration of how BREAD can be used to inform modeling decisions. The bug finding in WebPPL highlights the utility of BREAD beyond its primary purpose of quantifying accuracy.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the theoretical connection is sound, the practical sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of the hyperparameter estimation step is not fully explored. The authors acknowledge this in the real-world application section, but a more detailed analysis of the robustness of the method to inaccuracies in hyperparameter estimates would strengthen the paper. The experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks a thorough comparison with existing convergence diagnostics. It would be beneficial to demonstrate how BREAD complements or surpasses traditional diagnostics in detecting problematic inference behavior.  The description of the reverse AIS implementation is relatively brief, and details about the choice of MCMC kernels in the reverse chain would be helpful. The reliance on quartiles to visualize results due to outliers is understandable, but the impact of these outliers on the conclusions is not thoroughly discussed.  The comparison of collapsed and uncollapsed representations is somewhat limited to one specific model and dataset; exploring a wider range of models would strengthen the generalizability of the findings. Finally, the writing could be slightly more concise in places.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound extension of BDMC to assess inference accuracy.
*   Addresses a crucial problem in MCMC and probabilistic programming.
*   Practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data.
*   Implementation in two widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Demonstrated utility in guiding modeling decisions and debugging software.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Sensitivity to hyperparameter estimation accuracy not fully investigated.
*   Limited comparison with existing convergence diagnostics.
*   Limited scope of the model representation comparison.
*   Some sections could benefit from greater conciseness.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The BREAD method offers a rigorous and potentially powerful tool for evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based inference. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper – its technical soundness, practical relevance, and clear presentation – outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above, particularly regarding the robustness to hyperparameter estimation errors and a more thorough comparison with existing diagnostics.
