## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the accuracy of MCMC-based inference, specifically for algorithms amenable to importance sampling interpretations like AIS and SMC. The core idea of leveraging BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is sound and offers a rigorous, albeit computationally intensive, approach to evaluating inference reliability. The implementation in WebPPL and Stan is a significant contribution. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the practical impact is somewhat limited by the computational cost and reliance on simulated data even with the proposed protocol. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarifying the limitations and providing more extensive empirical validation on diverse real-world datasets.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on the existing BDMC framework [GGA15], initially designed for log-marginal likelihood estimation, and ingeniously adapts it to quantify the accuracy of approximate posterior samples. This addresses a long-standing challenge in MCMC: objectively assessing convergence and reliability beyond heuristic diagnostics. The authors correctly identify the Jeffreys divergence as a suitable metric and demonstrate how BDMC can provide an upper bound on it. The work is well-situated within the broader context of MCMC diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and automatic inference evaluation [CTM16].  Crucially, the paper distinguishes itself by providing a *bound* on error, rather than simply attempting to estimate it, offering stronger guarantees. The connection to parametric bootstrap [ET98] through the real-data protocol is insightful.  The related work section is thorough and accurately portrays the landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence upper bound is mathematically sound and provides a solid foundation for the method (BREAD).
* **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC beyond log-marginal likelihood estimation to posterior accuracy assessment is a significant and creative contribution.
* **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into widely used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) dramatically increases its accessibility and potential impact.
* **Real-Data Protocol:**  The proposed protocol for using BREAD with real-world data, while complex, attempts to bridge the gap between the ideal simulated data setting and practical applications.
* **Bug Discovery:** The serendipitous discovery of a bug in WebPPL showcases the potential of BREAD as a debugging tool for probabilistic programming systems.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the topic.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** BDMC, by its nature, is computationally expensive.  The need for exact posterior samples (even for simulated data) and running both forward and reverse AIS chains severely limits its scalability to complex models and large datasets. This is only briefly addressed.
* **Simulated Data Reliance:** Despite the real-data protocol, the fundamental guarantee of the method relies on having access to an exact posterior sample, which is only possible with simulated data. The effectiveness of the protocol relies heavily on the quality of the fitted hyperparameters, and this is not fully explored.  The experiments validating the protocol are limited in scope.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the method's functionality, the number of models and datasets used is relatively small.  A more comprehensive evaluation on diverse models and real-world problems would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Jeffreys Divergence as a Metric:** While theoretically appealing, the Jeffreys divergence might not be the most intuitive or easily interpretable metric for practical users.  The paper doesn't discuss alternative divergence measures.
* **Overstated Claims:** The phrase "rigorous, general, and automatic procedure" in the introduction feels a bit strong, considering the computational cost and reliance on simulated data.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The method provides a theoretically sound approach to quantifying posterior accuracy, a significant challenge in Bayesian inference.
* The implementation in widely-used probabilistic programming languages makes the method accessible to a broad audience.
* The work demonstrates the potential of BDMC beyond log-marginal likelihood estimation.
* The bug discovery showcases the method’s practical value.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and reliance on simulated data limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The empirical evaluation is limited in scope.
* The claims of "rigor" and "generality" may be overstated.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of BREAD and discuss potential optimizations or approximations to reduce its cost?
* How sensitive is the real-data protocol to the choice of prior distributions for the hyperparameters?
* What are the limitations of the Jeffreys divergence as a metric for evaluating posterior accuracy, and have you considered alternative measures?
* Can you provide more detailed results from the experiments on the real-world datasets, including a comparison of computational time and the tightness of the Jeffreys divergence bounds?
* Could you discuss potential applications of BREAD beyond the specific examples presented in the paper?



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the computational cost, reliance on simulated data, and limited empirical evaluation.  Clarifying the limitations of the method and providing a more nuanced discussion of its practical applicability will significantly strengthen the paper.  Adding more extensive experimental results on diverse datasets would further bolster the claims. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and deserves to be published in NIPS.
