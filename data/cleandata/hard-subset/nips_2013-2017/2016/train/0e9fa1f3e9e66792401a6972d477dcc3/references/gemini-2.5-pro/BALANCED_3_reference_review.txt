### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method called BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) to quantitatively measure the reliability of certain MCMC inference algorithms. The method builds directly on Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was introduced by Grosse et al. (2015) to provide stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key technical contribution of this work is to show that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by an algorithm like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and the true posterior distribution.

Since BDMC requires an exact sample from the target (posterior) distribution to initialize the reverse AIS chain, the method is rigorously applicable only to simulated data where such a sample can be generated. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated data to measure inference quality. The authors validate this protocol by showing that inference behavior on simulated data closely mirrors that on real data for several models.

The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to conduct insightful experiments. They demonstrate how BREAD can be used to compare the performance of different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and also show its utility as a powerful debugging tool by discovering a bug in WebPPL's implementation.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a long-standing and critical problem in probabilistic machine learning: how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. While traditional MCMC diagnostics can often detect non-convergence, they typically cannot provide a quantitative certificate of convergence. BREAD offers a principled way to do so (on simulated data) by bounding a standard measure of distributional distance (Jeffreys divergence). Repurposing the BDMC framework from marginal likelihood estimation to divergence bounding is a simple but very clever and significant insight. This tool is particularly valuable for users of probabilistic programming languages, who may not be experts in MCMC diagnostics.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation that the expected gap between the BDMC bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (Equations 8-12) is straightforward and appears correct. The theoretical foundation is solid, building upon the well-established theory of AIS and importance sampling.

3.  **Excellent Experimental Validation:** The paper is supported by a strong and convincing set of experiments.
    *   The validation of the real-world data protocol (Section 5.1) is thorough. The authors convincingly show that inference behavior (as measured by forward AIS curves) is consistent between real and simulated data and that the results are robust to the quality of the approximate posterior sample used to initialize the reverse chain.
    *   The case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model (Section 5.2.1) is a compelling demonstration of BREAD's utility for making concrete modeling and algorithmic decisions.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2) is a powerful, real-world testament to the method's usefulness for ensuring the correctness of inference implementations.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background, motivation, and technical derivation are presented with great clarity. The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the theoretical underpinnings to practical applications and validation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Applicability of the Core Method:** The BREAD method is directly applicable only to inference algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS or SMC. It cannot be used to directly evaluate a standard MCMC sampler like Metropolis-Hastings or NUTS, which are the workhorses of many PPLs (including Stan). While the authors argue that insights from evaluating AIS can inform the design of other MCMC algorithms (since they share transition operators), this connection is indirect. The paper would benefit from a more explicit acknowledgment of this limitation in the main text.

2.  **Potential Failure Modes of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real data is a sensible heuristic but not foolproof. Simulating data from a point estimate of the hyperparameters (`ˆ⌘real`) could potentially generate a dataset that is "easier" for inference than the original real-world data, especially if the real data lies in a tail of the prior or if the posterior over hyperparameters is multi-modal. While the validation in Sec 5.1.1 is good, it primarily relies on the similarity of forward AIS curves, which might not fully capture the difficulty of exploring the entire posterior landscape. A brief discussion of these potential failure modes would make the paper more complete.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Computational Cost:** The method requires running many AIS chains in both the forward and reverse directions, which can be computationally intensive, especially if a large number of intermediate distributions are needed for the bounds to be tight. The paper lacks a discussion of the computational overhead of BREAD and how this cost might limit its practical application for very large models or datasets.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   In Footnote 3, you mention that the analysis does not justify averaging the weights from multiple AIS chains and that you instead report quartiles of the estimates. This is an important point. The theoretical result is that the expectation of the gap, `E[B]`, bounds the Jeffreys divergence. An empirical average of the gap `B` over multiple runs would be a natural estimator for `E[B]`. It is unclear from Figure 1 whether you are plotting quartiles of the log-likelihood estimates themselves or of the gap `B`. Plotting the distribution (e.g., via boxplots) of the estimated gap `B` directly would be more aligned with the paper's main claim. Please clarify this aspect of the experimental reporting.

*   Regarding the real-data protocol, have you considered what happens in the case of model misspecification? The appendix contains an interesting example, and it might be worth briefly mentioning this in the main text as a potential diagnostic feature: a divergence between the forward AIS curves on real vs. simulated data could be a signal of model misspecification.

*   The connection to the concurrent work of [CTM16] is handled very well. Thank you for clearly situating your contribution relative to theirs.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the important problem of evaluating and validating probabilistic inference. The core idea is elegant, the theoretical claims are sound, and the experimental work is thorough and convincing. The proposed method, BREAD, provides a valuable and much-needed tool for both researchers developing new inference algorithms and practitioners using probabilistic programming systems. Despite some limitations in the direct applicability of the method, the overall contribution is strong. The paper is of high quality and will likely be of great interest to the NeurIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept**
