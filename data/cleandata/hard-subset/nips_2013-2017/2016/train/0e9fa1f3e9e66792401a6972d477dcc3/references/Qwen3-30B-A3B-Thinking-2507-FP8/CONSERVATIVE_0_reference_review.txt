

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors claim this provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for evaluating posterior inference quality in probabilistic programming languages. The method leverages BDMC's ability to sandwich the log marginal likelihood with stochastic bounds, extending this to bound the Jeffreys divergence.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) showing BDMC yields an estimator upper bounding the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, (2) presenting exact computation of the bound on small examples, (3) proposing a protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data, and (4) implementing BREAD in WebPPL and Stan.

The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 appears technically sound, building logically on the BDMC framework from [GGA15]. The paper provides a clear mathematical justification for how BDMC can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence. The authors also demonstrate the method's utility through debugging a WebPPL bug and comparing model representations in matrix factorization, which is a useful practical application.

However, the paper suffers from significant limitations that undermine its contribution. The most fundamental issue is that BREAD can only be rigorously validated on simulated data, as it requires exact posterior samples. The paper acknowledges this limitation but fails to adequately address the critical question: how well does performance on simulated data correlate with performance on real-world data? While the authors propose a protocol for generating simulated data from fitted hyperparameters, they only provide limited validation of this protocol (Section 5.1.1-5.1.2), showing only that the forward AIS curves match between real and simulated data for five specific models. This is insufficient to establish general reliability, especially given the paper's claim of being "rigorous" and "general."

The paper's validation is also limited in scope. The authors only test on five models from the Stan manual, all with small datasets (N=50 for the matrix factorization model), and rely on toy distributions for exact Jeffreys divergence computation. There is no comparison with established MCMC convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin statistics, effective sample size, or autocorrelation analysis. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether BREAD provides meaningful improvements over existing methods.

The paper also fails to sufficiently differentiate itself from related work. The authors acknowledge [CTM16] but only briefly discuss it as "independent work" with "strong similarities." In fact, [CTM16] defines an estimator equivalent to BDMC but initializes the reverse chain from an arbitrary reference distribution rather than the true posterior. The authors' claim that "BREAD can provide valuable insights into the tricky question of which representations of models to choose" is not convincingly demonstrated, as their matrix factorization example only compares two representations within a single model.

The paper's significance is questionable given the practical limitations. For real-world applications, the requirement for exact posterior samples (only possible through simulation) makes BREAD impractical as a routine diagnostic tool. The authors' protocol for using simulated data as a proxy for real data is heuristic and lacks theoretical justification, as they admit the simulated data "can be very dissimilar to real datasets."

While the theoretical extension of BDMC is sound, the paper presents it as a solution to a problem that has been addressed by established methods for years. MCMC convergence diagnostics have been extensively studied since the 1990s (e.g., [Gelman and Rubin, 1992]), and the paper fails to engage meaningfully with this rich literature. The authors' claim that "BREAD can rigorously demonstrate convergence" is misleading, as it only provides rigorous bounds in the simulated data setting, not for real-world inference.

In summary, while the theoretical extension of BDMC is technically correct, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims of being "rigorous, general, and automatic." The method's practical utility is limited by its dependence on simulated data, and it fails to demonstrate clear advantages over established MCMC convergence diagnostics. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with existing methods, broader validation across diverse models and datasets, and a clearer justification for why this approach provides new insights beyond what is already known.

Strengths: Clear theoretical derivation, useful debugging example, good presentation of related work.

Weaknesses: Limited empirical validation, inadequate comparison with established methods, failure to adequately address the gap between simulated and real-world data, insufficient justification for the practical utility of the method.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing work but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of being a general, rigorous, and practical solution for evaluating MCMC inference. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the limitations significantly undermine the paper's significance. I recommend rejection.
