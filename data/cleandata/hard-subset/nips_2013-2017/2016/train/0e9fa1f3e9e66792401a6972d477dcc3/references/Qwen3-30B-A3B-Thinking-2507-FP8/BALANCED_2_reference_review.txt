

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method that bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate MCMC samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors build on Grosse et al. (2015) to show that BDMC can provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, which they validate on toy distributions. The paper also proposes a practical protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, and integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan for validation on multiple models.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical soundness:** The derivation in Section 3.1 is clear and rigorous, extending GGA15's analysis to show that the BDMC estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The mathematical arguments are well-supported and correctly build on prior work.

2. **Practical utility:** The paper successfully demonstrates BREAD's value through multiple applications: validating its behavior on real vs. simulated data (Section 5.1), analyzing model representation choices in probabilistic programming (Section 5.2.1), and even uncovering a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2). This practical validation significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

3. **Clear exposition:** The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to practical implementation. The background section (Section 2) provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS or BDMC.

4. **Relevance to probabilistic programming:** The integration with WebPPL and Stan directly addresses a critical need in probabilistic programming: providing users with reliable metrics for inference quality. This connects well with the broader NIPS community's interest in practical probabilistic modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited validation of the real-world protocol:** While the paper validates the consistency between real and simulated data for five models (Section 5.1.1), it doesn't sufficiently address *why* the fitted hyperparameter approach works. The assumption that simulated data from fitted hyperparameters will be representative of real data is crucial but underjustified. The authors should discuss the scenarios where this might fail and provide evidence of robustness across diverse datasets.

2. **Comparison with related work:** The paper mentions related work (CTM16), but doesn't provide a thorough comparison of BREAD with other methods for measuring MCMC reliability. A table comparing the theoretical guarantees, computational requirements, and practical applicability of BREAD versus other approaches would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Scalability concerns:** The paper demonstrates BREAD on small models (matrix factorization with N=50, D=25, K=5), but doesn't address how the method scales to larger problems. For probabilistic programming systems to adopt BREAD, it's critical to understand its computational cost relative to standard inference methods.

4. **Limited experimental scope:** The validation in Section 5.1 is limited to five models. More extensive experiments across diverse model classes would better establish BREAD's general utility.

**Recommendation and Suggestions for Improvement:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the problem of validating MCMC inference, with strong theoretical foundations and practical applications. However, it has significant limitations in its validation of the real-world protocol and lacks sufficient comparison with related methods.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Add a thorough discussion of the limitations of the fitted hyperparameter protocol, including scenarios where it might fail to produce representative simulated data. A more detailed analysis of the relationship between the fitted hyperparameters and the true posterior would be valuable.

2. Include a comparison with other methods for measuring MCMC reliability (e.g., convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin, or other divergence estimators like those proposed by Gorham and Mackey [2015]). A table summarizing the theoretical guarantees, computational requirements, and applicability of different methods would help establish BREAD's unique value proposition.

3. Extend the experiments to larger models to demonstrate scalability. The authors should report computational costs (wall time, memory usage) alongside the divergence bounds.

4. Clarify the relationship between BREAD and CTM16. While the paper notes the connection, a more detailed comparison would help readers understand the specific contributions of this work.

The paper's core idea is valuable and well-executed, but the current version doesn't fully establish its practical utility for real-world applications. With the suggested revisions, particularly the expanded validation and comparison with related methods, this could be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Summary of Pro and Con Arguments:**

*Pro:* Theoretical extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence; practical validation on multiple models; integration with probabilistic programming languages; demonstrated utility in debugging and model selection.

*Con:* Limited validation of the real-world protocol; insufficient comparison with related methods; no scalability analysis; limited experimental scope.

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation and practical demonstration, but with significant gaps in validation and comparison, I recommend **weak acceptance**. With the suggested revisions addressing the limitations in validation and comparison, this paper would make a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
