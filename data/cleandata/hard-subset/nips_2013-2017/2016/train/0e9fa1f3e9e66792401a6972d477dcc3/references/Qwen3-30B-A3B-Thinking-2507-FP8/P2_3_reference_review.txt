

# Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC framework (Grosse et al., 2015) to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The method is applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space. The paper also proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validating consistency between simulated and real data. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility for both evaluating inference quality and debugging probabilistic programming implementations.

The work builds on Grosse et al. (2015), which used BDMC to estimate marginal likelihoods, and relates to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also uses BDMC for inference evaluation but initializes the reverse chain from an arbitrary reference distribution rather than the true posterior.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness and Novelty (Quality/Originality):** The paper's main theoretical contribution is a clear derivation showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. This is a significant extension of the BDMC framework, as it transforms a marginal likelihood estimation technique into a reliability measure for posterior inference. The derivation in Section 3.1 is mathematically rigorous and well-motivated, providing a novel theoretical contribution to the field of probabilistic inference.

**Practical Implementation and Validation (Significance/Quality):** The authors demonstrate the practical utility of BREAD through extensive experiments on both toy models and real-world applications. Their validation protocol (Section 5.1) effectively addresses a critical limitation of BDMC by showing that simulated data from fitted hyperparameters can serve as a reliable proxy for real data. The method was successfully used to debug a subtle bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2), highlighting its practical value beyond mere theoretical interest.

**Integration with Probabilistic Programming (Significance):** The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to practitioners. The authors demonstrate how BREAD can guide modeling decisions (Section 5.2.1), showing that it provides actionable insights about model representation choices. This bridges the gap between theoretical advances and practical implementation, which is particularly valuable given the growing popularity of probabilistic programming.

**Comprehensive Related Work (Clarity/Originality):** The related work section thoroughly discusses connections to previous methods for convergence diagnosis, automatic MCMC configuration, and other inference quality measures. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) by emphasizing the theoretical guarantees of using the true posterior for the reverse chain initialization.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Application (Quality/Significance):** The method requires exact posterior samples, which are only available for simulated data. While the paper proposes a protocol for real-world data, this still requires fitting hyperparameters to real data and generating synthetic data from those hyperparameters. This limitation restricts the method's applicability to cases where the model is well-specified and the hyperparameters can be reliably estimated. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how the method performs when the model is misspecified, which is common in practice.

**Scalability Concerns (Quality):** The experiments focus on relatively small models (N=50, K=5, D=25 for matrix factorization). The paper doesn't address how the computational cost scales with model size or dataset size. For large-scale applications, which are increasingly common in probabilistic modeling, the additional computational burden of running both forward and reverse AIS chains could become prohibitive.

**Limited Discussion of Bound Tightness (Quality):** While the paper shows that the upper bound is reasonably tight on toy examples, it doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how tight the bound is across different model types or under different conditions. A more thorough investigation of the gap between the bound and the true Jeffreys divergence would strengthen the paper's claims.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel and well-executed, and the practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates clear utility for both researchers and practitioners. The method addresses a fundamental problem in MCMC inference—how to rigorously measure the reliability of approximate samples—which has been a persistent challenge in the field.

The paper's main limitations (restricted applicability to simulated data and scalability concerns) are acknowledged by the authors and are common challenges in this area. The validation protocol they propose for real-world data is a thoughtful solution to this limitation. While the paper doesn't fully address how to handle model misspecification, the experiments in Appendix D provide some evidence that the method can still be useful even when models are slightly misspecified.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and reproduce the method. The integration with existing probabilistic programming frameworks makes the work immediately applicable to a broad audience. The debugging example in Section 5.2.2 is particularly compelling as it demonstrates the method's practical utility beyond just theoretical interest.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The theoretical development is sound, the practical implementation is well-executed, and the method has clear potential for widespread adoption in probabilistic programming. The paper addresses a significant problem in a novel way and provides both theoretical guarantees and practical validation.

The minor weaknesses identified (scope limitations and scalability concerns) do not detract from the paper's overall contribution, as they are common challenges in this area and are appropriately acknowledged by the authors. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a valuable tool for practitioners working with probabilistic models.

**Pro Acceptance:** Novel theoretical contribution, practical implementation with real-world validation, clear demonstration of utility for debugging and model selection, well-written and well-organized.

**Con Rejection:** Limited to simulated data (though protocol addresses this), potential scalability issues for large models (though not a fundamental limitation of the method itself).
