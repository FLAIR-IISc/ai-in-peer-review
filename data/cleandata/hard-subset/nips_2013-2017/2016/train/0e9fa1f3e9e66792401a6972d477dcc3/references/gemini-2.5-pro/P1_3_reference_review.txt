### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method called BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) to quantitatively measure the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was previously used to obtain tight bounds on the log marginal likelihood (log-ML). The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log-ML produced by BDMC serves as an estimator that, in expectation, upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by an algorithm (like Annealed Importance Sampling, AIS) and the true posterior.

Since BDMC's reverse pass requires an exact sample from the true posterior, the rigorous bound is only applicable to simulated data. To address this limitation and make the method useful for real-world datasets, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these fitted hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset to measure inference quality. They validate this protocol by showing that inference behavior on simulated data closely matches that on real data for several models.

The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to conduct a case study comparing the performance of collapsed versus uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model. This analysis yields practical insights into modeling choices for different inference engines. As a further testament to its utility, the authors report using BREAD to discover and debug a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL.

This work builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015) and is closely related to concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) on "subjective divergence," which the authors appropriately acknowledge and differentiate.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a fundamental and notoriously difficult problem in probabilistic modeling: how to know if an MCMC sampler has converged and is producing reliable samples. Traditional diagnostics can only detect non-convergence, whereas this method provides a quantitative, rigorous upper bound on a meaningful divergence measure (Jeffreys), at least in the simulated data setting. The theoretical contribution—linking the BDMC gap to an expected bound on Jeffreys divergence—is an elegant and important extension of the original BDMC analysis.

2.  **Practical Utility:** The method is not just a theoretical curiosity. The authors have implemented it in two widely-used PPLs (Stan and WebPPL), demonstrating its practicality. The case studies are highly compelling. The analysis of model representation (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) provides concrete, actionable advice for practitioners and highlights how BREAD can guide modeling decisions. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful demonstration of the method's value as a tool for validating and debugging inference systems.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their method, particularly its reliance on simulated data for rigorous guarantees. They design a sensible set of experiments to validate their proposed protocol for real-world data. They show that (a) inference behavior is consistent between real data and data simulated from fitted hyperparameters, and (b) the reverse bound is robust to using an approximate posterior sample for the hyperparameters.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the background material is presented concisely, the core method is derived step-by-step, and the experiments logically support the claims. The paper is easy to follow for a reader familiar with MCMC and importance sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Applicability of the Rigorous Bound:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is that the rigorous bound only applies to simulated data where an exact posterior sample can be obtained. The protocol for real data is a heuristic. While the validation is encouraging, it may not hold in all cases, especially under significant model misspecification where the posterior given real data could have a very different geometry from the posterior given data simulated from fitted hyperparameters.

2.  **Scope of Applicable Algorithms:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. It cannot be directly applied to measure the quality of samples from a standard Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampler chain. While insights may transfer, this limits its direct use as a general-purpose MCMC diagnostic.

3.  **Computational Cost:** Running BDMC requires executing many AIS chains in both forward and reverse directions, which is computationally expensive. This likely relegates BREAD to a diagnostic tool for model/sampler development and debugging rather than a routine check for every inference run.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, high-impact problem in machine learning and statistics.
*   Provides a novel and theoretically sound method for quantifying inference error.
*   Demonstrates clear practical value through PPL implementations and compelling case studies (model selection, debugging).
*   The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and empirically thorough.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data; the real-data protocol is a heuristic.
*   The method's direct applicability is restricted to a subset of inference algorithms (AIS/SMC-like).

Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper makes a valuable and timely contribution to the field.

### Detailed Comments and Qualitative Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The theoretical derivation is sound. The experimental methodology is well-designed to support the paper's claims, including a careful validation of the real-data heuristic. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach. The work is a complete and polished contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is written with excellent clarity. The progression from background concepts (AIS, BDMC) to the novel contribution (the Jeffreys divergence bound) and its practical application is logical and easy to follow. The figures are informative and support the text well.

*   **Originality:** The work is original. While it builds directly on BDMC, the insight to use the log-ML gap to bound a posterior divergence is a novel and clever contribution. This shifts the focus of BDMC from a tool for model selection (via marginal likelihood) to a tool for inference validation, which is a significant conceptual step. The relationship to concurrent work is handled appropriately.

*   **Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. As probabilistic programming systems become more widespread, the need for automatic, reliable, and quantitative validation of inference results becomes paramount. BREAD provides a principled and practical step in this direction. Researchers developing new inference algorithms, developers of PPLs, and sophisticated practitioners choosing between model representations are all likely to find this work useful and build upon it. The ability to use it for debugging inference code is a particularly impactful application.
