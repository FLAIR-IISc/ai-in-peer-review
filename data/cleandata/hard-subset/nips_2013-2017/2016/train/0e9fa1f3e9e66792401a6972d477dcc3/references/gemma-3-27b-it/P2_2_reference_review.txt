## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior.  It proposes "BREAD" (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) – a method for bounding this divergence using BDMC, and develops a protocol for applying this to real-world data by simulating data from estimated hyperparameters.  The authors implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its use in guiding modeling choices (specifically, representation of a matrix factorization model) and even identifying a bug in WebPPL.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of BDMC:**  Extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to directly assess posterior accuracy is a valuable contribution.  The theoretical connection to bounding Jeffreys divergence is clearly presented.
* **Rigorous Approach:** The work provides *guarantees* (albeit for simulated data) on the quality of inference, a rarity in the often heuristic world of MCMC diagnostics.
* **Practical Implementation:**  Integrating BREAD into widely-used probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan increases its accessibility and potential impact.
* **Demonstrated Usefulness:** The application to matrix factorization, revealing tradeoffs between collapsed and uncollapsed representations, demonstrates the practical utility of the method. The bug discovery is a bonus and highlights a potential quality control benefit.
* **Addressing Real-World Applicability:** The protocol for using BREAD with real data (via hyperparameter estimation and simulation) is a thoughtful attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical use.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Simulated Data Requirement:** The core theoretical guarantee relies on having access to exact posterior samples, limiting direct application to real-world problems without the hyperparameter estimation/simulation workaround. While the authors address this, the validity of the workaround hinges on the quality of hyperparameter estimation and the representativeness of simulated data.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC is computationally intensive. While the paper doesn’t deeply explore this, the overhead compared to traditional convergence diagnostics could be a barrier to widespread adoption.
* **Limited Scope:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms amenable to the AIS/SMC framework (viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space). While the authors argue for broader implications, the direct applicability is limited.  The relevance to more general MCMC methods isn’t fully established.
* **Empirical Validation of Protocol:**  The validation of the hyperparameter estimation/simulation protocol is somewhat limited. While the authors show consistency between real and simulated data for their chosen models, broader evaluation is needed to establish confidence in this approach.  The authors acknowledge this in the discussion.




**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the experimental setup seems well-designed.  The authors are upfront about limitations.  However, the reliance on simulated data and the computational cost raise concerns about practicality.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background material is thorough. However, the mathematical notation can be dense at times, potentially making it challenging for readers unfamiliar with BDMC.
* **Originality:** The application of BDMC to posterior divergence estimation is novel. While BDMC itself is established, the extension and the BREAD framework represent a significant contribution. The related work section is thorough and appropriately cites relevant research, including Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka’s work.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is considerable. A reliable metric for assessing posterior accuracy could greatly improve the trustworthiness of probabilistic programming and Bayesian inference. The demonstration of its utility in guiding modeling decisions and debugging adds to the significance.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a rigorous (albeit limited) method for evaluating MCMC accuracy.
* Offers practical tools for users of probabilistic programming languages.
* Demonstrates real-world utility in model selection and bug detection.
* Well-written and technically sound.

**Con:**

* Reliance on simulated data limits direct applicability.
* Computational cost may be prohibitive.
* Limited scope (AIS/SMC framework).
* Empirical validation of the real-world protocol could be stronger.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The limitations, particularly the reliance on simulated data, are acknowledged and addressed (to a degree) by the authors. The potential benefits of a more reliable metric for assessing MCMC accuracy outweigh the drawbacks.  

**Minor Revisions:**

*   **Expand Discussion of Computational Cost:** Provide more detail on the computational demands of BREAD and how they scale with problem size.
*   **Strengthen Validation of Protocol:**  Include more diverse models and datasets in the validation of the hyperparameter estimation/simulation protocol.
* **Clarify Scope:** More clearly articulate the limitations of the method in terms of the types of MCMC algorithms to which it can be directly applied.
