

# NIPS Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method that extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors build upon the BDMC method from Grosse et al. (2015), which provides stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, to derive an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.

The paper makes four key contributions: (1) a theoretical derivation showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence; (2) a technique for exactly computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples; (3) a protocol for evaluating inference accuracy on real-world datasets by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters; and (4) integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages. The authors validate their approach on several models, including matrix factorization, and demonstrate how BREAD can guide model representation choices and even reveal implementation bugs.

The work is well-positioned in the context of the broader literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics and probabilistic programming. It relates to existing work on convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks and Gelman, 1998), automatic MCMC tuning (Roberts and Rosenthal, 2001), and other methods for assessing inference quality (Gorham and Mackey, 2015; Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016).

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) showing that the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The derivations are clear and well-motivated, building logically on the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015).

2. **Practical Utility**: The method addresses a critical problem in probabilistic modeling—evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference. The authors successfully integrate BREAD into two major probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan), making it immediately useful to practitioners.

3. **Comprehensive Validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation across multiple models (Section 5), including a compelling case where BREAD revealed a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2). The validation of the protocol for real-world data (Section 5.1) demonstrates the method's practical applicability.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Related Work**: The authors carefully distinguish their approach from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), highlighting how their method uses the true posterior for the reverse chain (providing stronger guarantees) while their protocol for real-world data uses approximate posterior samples as a proxy.

5. **Insightful Scientific Findings**: The matrix factorization case study (Section 5.2.1) demonstrates how BREAD can provide actionable insights about model representation choices, showing that the optimal representation differs between WebPPL and Stan.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The method is only directly applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC), which excludes many common MCMC methods. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's worth noting that this restricts the method's applicability to a subset of MCMC inference techniques.

2. **Computational Cost**: Running both forward and reverse AIS chains for bounding the Jeffreys divergence is computationally intensive. The paper doesn't thoroughly address scalability to larger datasets or higher-dimensional models, which would be an important consideration for practical adoption.

3. **Real-World Data Protocol Limitations**: The protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2) relies on fitting hyperparameters to real data and simulating from those. The paper acknowledges that this may not work well for models with very broad priors, but it would be beneficial to have more discussion about when this protocol might fail and how to diagnose such failures.

4. **Lack of Comparative Analysis**: While the paper demonstrates the utility of BREAD, it doesn't compare it against other existing methods for measuring inference reliability (e.g., the subjective divergence method from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016) in a systematic way. A more direct comparison would strengthen the argument for BREAD's advantages.

5. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases**: The paper shows that BREAD works well in the cases they tested, but it would be valuable to discuss potential failure cases or scenarios where the bound might be particularly loose.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The problem of reliably measuring the quality of MCMC inference is important and has been difficult to address with rigorous methods. The BREAD approach provides a theoretically grounded, practical solution that can be immediately integrated into existing probabilistic programming frameworks.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The integration into WebPPL and Stan, along with the demonstration of how BREAD can guide modeling decisions and reveal implementation bugs, makes this work particularly valuable for practitioners.

The main limitations are the restricted scope of applicable MCMC algorithms and the computational cost of the method. However, these are reasonable trade-offs for the rigorous guarantees provided, and the authors acknowledge these limitations.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work addresses an important problem with a novel and technically sound approach, provides thorough validation, and has clear practical utility for probabilistic programming users. The method has the potential to become a standard tool for evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference.

The paper advances the state of the art in probabilistic inference by providing a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality—something that has been lacking in the field. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to a wide range of users, and the case study on model representation choices demonstrates the practical value of the method.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the related work section, more explicitly compare BREAD's theoretical guarantees with those of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) and discuss the practical implications of these differences.

2. Add a brief discussion of the computational cost of BREAD relative to standard MCMC inference, especially for larger models.

3. Include a small case study where the bound is loose to better illustrate the method's limitations.

4. In Section 5.1.2, consider adding a brief analysis of how the number of MCMC steps (S) affects the accuracy of the reverse AIS estimates, rather than just noting that the curves were statistically indistinguishable.

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. I believe it would be a welcome addition to the NIPS proceedings.
