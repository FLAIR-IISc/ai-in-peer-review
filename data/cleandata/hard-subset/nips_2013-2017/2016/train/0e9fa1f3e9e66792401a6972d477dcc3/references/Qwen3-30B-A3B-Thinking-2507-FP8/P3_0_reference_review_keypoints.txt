- Introduces BREAD, an extension of BDMC that bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior.  
- Proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and integrates implementations in WebPPL and Stan.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous derivation and proof that the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Experimental validation: thorough tests across multiple models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.) showing consistency between simulated and real-data inference behavior.  
- Practical utility: used to compare model representations and discovered a real bug in WebPPL, demonstrating debugging value.  
- Limitation: method is restricted to AIS and SMC; applicability to other MCMC algorithms (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings, HMC) is unclear.  
- Assumption concern: protocol assumes inference behavior on simulated data matches real data, but lacks rigorous conditions or diagnostics for when this may fail.  
- Computational concern: paper does not discuss the cost of running forward and reverse AIS chains or compare BREAD’s runtime to standard diagnostics.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and mathematically clear, with effective figures and experiments.  
- Clarity gap: should more explicitly state limitations and temper claims about informing broader MCMC algorithm design.  
- Related work: insufficiently differentiated from Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016); needs a clearer comparison.  
- Originality: novel application of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence and a useful real-data protocol, though built directly on prior BDMC work.  
- Novel applications: using BREAD for model representation comparison and bug detection is practically novel.  
- Significance: provides a rigorous tool for measuring MCMC reliability and is immediately applicable via WebPPL/Stan integration.  
- Comparison gap: lacks a comprehensive comparison with standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Geweke).  
- Recommended improvements: explicitly discuss AIS/SMC limitation, differentiate from related work, analyze computational cost, and compare to existing diagnostics.  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, valuable contribution with reasonable limitations; recommended for acceptance.
