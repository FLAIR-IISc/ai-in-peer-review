- Introduces BREAD, extending bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior.
- Shows theoretically that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Proposes a protocol for real-world data by simulating datasets from hyperparameters estimated on real data to apply BREAD in practice.
- Integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan and validates it on multiple probabilistic models.
- Empirically demonstrates usefulness via model-representation insights (collapsed vs. uncollapsed MF) and by finding a WebPPL bug.
- Theoretical contribution: extends BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior quality assessment with clear derivations.
- Practical validation: thorough experiments on toy and realistic models showing consistent behavior between real and simulated data.
- Real-world utility: provides actionable analysis and debugging capability for probabilistic programming systems.
- Limited applicability: restricted to importance-sampling–view algorithms (AIS, SMC), excluding many standard MCMC methods like Metropolis-Hastings.
- Approximation in protocol: using approximate posterior samples to initialize reverse chains breaks strict theoretical guarantees for real data.
- Computational cost: running both forward and reverse AIS chains is expensive and scalability is not fully addressed.
- Incomplete comparison to related work: insufficient clarification of differences from Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), especially when using approximate posteriors.
- Lacks comparison to standard posterior quality metrics (e.g., ESS, Geweke) to contextualize BREAD’s advantages.
- Strong recommendation for acceptance: technically sound, well-validated, novel, and practically impactful despite acknowledged limitations.
- Suggested improvements: clarify relation to prior work regarding approximate vs. exact posteriors.
- Suggested improvements: discuss computational cost and include a small scalability experiment.
- Suggested improvements: explicitly state method limitations in the conclusion (applicability and cost).
- Suggested improvements: add more context on why collapsed vs. uncollapsed tradeoffs differ between WebPPL and Stan.
