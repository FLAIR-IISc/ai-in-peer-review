

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method that extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC-based inference algorithms (such as AIS or SMC) and the true posterior distribution. The authors build upon the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015), which provides stochastic bounds on marginal likelihoods. They demonstrate that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, and they propose a protocol for applying this technique to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) theoretical derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, (2) exact computation of both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, and (4) integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan.

This work is situated within the important problem of validating MCMC inference, which has been a longstanding challenge in probabilistic modeling. The paper makes a clear connection to related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), who also use BDMC for posterior evaluation but initialize the reverse chain from an arbitrary reference distribution rather than the true posterior.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The derivation showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is mathematically sound and extends the BDMC framework in a meaningful way. The paper correctly identifies the connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence, which is not immediately obvious.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a significant practical contribution. The paper demonstrates how BREAD can be used not just as a diagnostic tool but also as a debugging tool, as evidenced by the discovery of a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampling.

3. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The validation on simulated data (Section 5.1) is well-executed, showing that the behavior of inference on simulated data matches that on real data for multiple models. The analysis of different model representations (Section 5.2.1) provides concrete, actionable insights for probabilistic programming users.

4. **Good Writing and Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear background on AIS, BDMC, and the necessary probabilistic concepts. The figures are informative, and the derivations are presented with sufficient mathematical detail.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Application**: The core contribution (bounding Jeffreys divergence) only applies rigorously to simulated data, with the real-world application being a heuristic extension. The paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why fitting hyperparameters and using approximate posterior samples as a proxy for exact posterior samples will yield valid bounds on real data. While the experiments in Section 5.1.2 suggest it works for the models tested, this doesn't guarantee general applicability.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Standard Convergence Diagnostics**: The paper doesn't compare BREAD with established MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size, Geweke diagnostics). Without such a comparison, it's unclear what unique value BREAD provides beyond what is already available in the MCMC community. For instance, the paper claims BREAD can "rigorously demonstrate convergence," but existing diagnostics can also provide valuable insights about convergence.

3. **Unclear Justification for Using Jeffreys Divergence**: The paper uses Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL) as the metric of interest but doesn't convincingly justify why this is preferable to standard KL divergence or other metrics. The authors mention that both covering high-probability regions and avoiding low-probability regions are important, but they don't demonstrate how Jeffreys divergence better addresses these concerns than other metrics.

4. **Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of BREAD. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains for every model could be prohibitively expensive for large models, which would limit its practical utility. The authors should address this limitation.

5. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees for Real-World Application**: The protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2) is described as a "heuristic scheme" but lacks rigorous theoretical support. The paper doesn't address how the quality of the fitted hyperparameters affects the accuracy of the bounds, which is crucial for real-world applications.

## Comparison with Related Work

The paper appropriately acknowledges Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), who also use BDMC for posterior evaluation. The key difference is that BREAD uses the true posterior (or a proxy) to initialize the reverse chain, while Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka use an arbitrary reference distribution. The authors note that the subjective divergence used by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence only when the reference distribution is the true posterior, which aligns with their theoretical result.

However, the paper could have engaged more deeply with the broader literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks and Gelman, 1998; Cowles and Carlin, 1996; Gelman and Rubin, 1992). It would have strengthened the paper to position BREAD within this established field and demonstrate how it complements or improves upon existing methods.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the Comparison with Existing Diagnostics**: Add a section comparing BREAD with standard MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size) on the same models. Show how BREAD provides complementary information or has advantages in specific scenarios.

2. **Justify the Use of Jeffreys Divergence**: Provide a more detailed argument for why Jeffreys divergence is the appropriate metric for measuring MCMC reliability, rather than standard KL divergence. Perhaps include a discussion of how the symmetrization addresses limitations of one-directional KL.

3. **Address Computational Cost**: Discuss the computational cost of BREAD in detail and provide benchmarks showing how it scales with model size and complexity. This would help readers understand its practical limitations.

4. **Strengthen the Real-World Application Protocol**: Provide more theoretical justification for the protocol in Section 3.2, perhaps by deriving bounds on the error introduced by using approximate posterior samples instead of exact ones.

5. **Include a Broader Discussion of Limitations**: Be more explicit about the limitations of the method, particularly regarding its applicability to models where exact posterior sampling is infeasible, even with simulated data.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical derivation is solid, the implementation is practical, and the debugging example demonstrates real-world utility. However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the restricted applicability of the core technique to simulated data and the lack of comparison with existing convergence diagnostics.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and it makes a clear contribution to the literature on MCMC validation. While the method is not a panacea for all convergence diagnostics challenges, it provides a valuable tool for probabilistic programming users and developers.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper addresses an important problem in probabilistic modeling with a theoretically sound approach. With the suggested revisions to strengthen the comparison with existing methods, clarify the theoretical justification for the real-world application protocol, and better address the computational cost, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

The key improvements needed are:
1. More thorough comparison with existing MCMC convergence diagnostics
2. Better justification for using Jeffreys divergence as the metric
3. Discussion of computational cost and scalability
4. Stronger theoretical grounding for the real-world application protocol

These revisions would significantly enhance the paper's impact and clarify its contribution to the field.
