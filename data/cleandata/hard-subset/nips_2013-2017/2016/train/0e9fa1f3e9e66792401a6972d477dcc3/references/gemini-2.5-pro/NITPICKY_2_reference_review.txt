**Summary**

This paper proposes a method called BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to quantitatively measure the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which provides stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. As the reverse part of BDMC requires an exact posterior sample, the method is only rigorously applicable to simulated data. To bridge this gap for practical use, the authors propose a protocol for real-world data: infer hyperparameters on the real data, simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters, and then apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. The method is implemented and tested in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, on several models. The authors use BREAD to compare different model representations and demonstrate its utility for debugging PPL implementations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing problem in probabilistic modeling: the lack of reliable, quantitative, and general-purpose diagnostics for MCMC convergence. Existing heuristics can fail to detect non-convergence, and BREAD offers a more rigorous alternative, which is a significant contribution.
2.  **Novelty:** The central theoretical contribution—linking the gap in BDMC bounds to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence—is an elegant and novel insight. While building directly on BDMC [GGA15], this re-interpretation for measuring sample quality is a new and valuable perspective.
3.  **Practical Utility:** The authors demonstrate clear practical applications. The comparison of collapsed versus uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization provides concrete guidance on model formulation. Furthermore, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a very compelling demonstration of the method's power as a correctness-checking tool for developers of inference systems.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor and major flaws, ranging from presentation to methodology, which collectively cast doubt on its readiness for publication.

1.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript's preparation is exceptionally careless.
    *   The citations in the introduction are improperly formatted (e.g., `[... GS; CGHL+ p]`). What does the "+ p" signify?
    *   The reference list at the end is completely broken, with extraneous characters like `\.,? \Q2015\E`. This is unacceptable for a publication of this caliber and makes it difficult to verify the relationship to prior work.
    *   A stray line, "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.", is left floating at the bottom of the first page. Such formatting errors suggest a rushed submission.
    *   A typo in Eq. (12) refers to `qfwd(pT)` instead of `qfwd(xT)`, which can confuse the reader about the objects of the divergence measure.

2.  **Major Methodological Limitation:** A critical weakness is buried in footnote 3. The analysis in Section 3.1 provides a bound on the Jeffreys divergence *in expectation*, where the expectation is over a single run of the forward and reverse AIS chains. In practice, AIS estimates have high variance, and it is standard to average the importance weights from many independent runs. The authors state, "the analysis of Section 3.1 does not justify such averaging, so we report estimates corresponding to individual AIS chains." This is a crippling limitation. The bound `B` is itself a random variable, and a single instance of it may be a poor estimate of its expectation, `E[B]`. If the variance of `B` is large, a single estimate is practically meaningless. The paper does not analyze this variance or propose a valid way to create a stable, low-variance estimate of the divergence bound. Displaying quartiles of single-chain estimates (Fig. 1) obscures this problem rather than addressing it.

3.  **Weak Protocol for Real-World Data:** The "rigorous" nature of the bound holds only for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2) is described as a "heuristic scheme" and significantly undermines the paper's claims of rigor. The reverse chain is initialized from an MCMC sample that starts from a point estimate of the hyperparameters. This procedure rests on the strong and unverified assumption that a "small number of MCMC steps" is sufficient to generate a plausible posterior sample. The validation in Section 5.1.2, which shows insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps `S`, is weak evidence; it could simply mean that all tested values of `S` produce equally poor starting points, or that the reverse AIS is not sensitive to this initialization for the specific models tested. The rigor, which is the main selling point of the method, is lost in this crucial step toward practical application.

4.  **Clarity and Technical Details:**
    *   The connection to SMC is mentioned in passing but never developed. Since SMC is cited as a key algorithm to which this method applies, the lack of detail is a notable omission.
    *   The justification for vertically shifting the plots in Figure 1 to align real and simulated data estimates is ad-hoc ("shifted vertically based on the largest forward AIS estimate"). This visual manipulation could be misleading and makes it difficult to judge the claim that inference behavior is truly consistent. A more principled comparison of the distributions' shapes or the convergence rates would be more convincing.
    *   The analysis of the bound's tightness is relegated to an appendix that was not provided. This is a key piece of information needed to assess the quality of the proposed bound.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of this paper is clever and addresses an important problem, the execution is deeply flawed. The combination of sloppy presentation, a severe and under-discussed methodological limitation regarding the variance of the bound estimator, and a weak, heuristic protocol for real-world data applications prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper's promise of a "rigorous, general, and automatic procedure" is not met, as the rigor is lost precisely when moving from simulated to real-world data. I would encourage the authors to significantly revise the manuscript to address these issues, particularly by developing a method to produce low-variance estimates of the divergence bound and by more thoroughly investigating and validating the protocol for real-world data.
