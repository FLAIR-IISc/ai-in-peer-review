## Peer Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the accuracy of MCMC-based inference, specifically for algorithms amenable to interpretation as importance sampling. The core idea of using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is sound and theoretically well-motivated. The empirical validation, including integration into WebPPL and Stan, is a significant contribution. While the method is currently limited in scope, the insights gained are potentially applicable to broader MCMC diagnostics. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focused on clarifying limitations and streamlining the presentation.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the critical problem of validating MCMC inference, a notoriously difficult task. It leverages the existing BDMC framework, originally designed for marginal likelihood estimation, to *bound* the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. This provides a quantitative measure of inference reliability, addressing a long-standing need in the field. The authors correctly position their work within the context of convergence diagnostics and emphasize the distinction between identifying non-convergence and providing guarantees of accuracy (which BDMC, in the simulated data setting, achieves).  The related work section is comprehensive, citing relevant papers on MCMC diagnostics, BDMC, and probabilistic programming.  The connection to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka’s work is particularly well-handled, outlining the subtle differences.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to divergence bounding is a significant and clever contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is rigorous and clearly presented.
* **Practical Implementation:**  Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan enhances its accessibility and demonstrates its practical utility.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, including the consistency check between real and simulated data and the matrix factorization model analysis, provide convincing evidence of BREAD's effectiveness.  The validation of the hyperparameter fitting procedure is particularly strong.
* **Diagnostic Potential:** Even with its current limitations, the method provides a concrete tool for evaluating inference quality and making informed modeling decisions (as demonstrated with the matrix factorization example).
* **Bug Detection:** The identification of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a validation tool for probabilistic programming systems.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Applicability:** The method is currently restricted to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). This excludes many standard MCMC methods. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a significant constraint.
* **Simulated Data Requirement:** The need for exact posterior samples initially limits application to simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a good step, but its reliance on accurate hyperparameter estimation and the assumption of a reasonable approximate posterior is a potential concern.
* **Computational Cost:** Running BDMC can be computationally expensive, especially for complex models. The paper doesn’t fully address this cost, and a discussion of scalability would be valuable.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** While generally well-written, the paper occasionally suffers from dense mathematical notation. Some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  The connection between different sections sometimes feels abrupt.
* **Interpretation of Divergence Bounds:**  While BREAD provides *bounds* on the Jeffreys divergence, the paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of what constitutes a "good" or "bad" bound in practical scenarios.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and claims are well-supported. The analysis is rigorous and the empirical results are compelling. The completeness of the work is good, although further scalability analysis would be beneficial.  The authors are appropriately cautious about the limitations of their approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, but some sections could benefit from simplification and more intuitive explanations.  The equations are generally well-explained, but a more streamlined presentation would improve readability.
* **Originality:** The application of BDMC to divergence bounding is novel and represents a significant contribution. The combination of theoretical analysis and practical implementation adds to the originality of the work.
* **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic inference: evaluating the reliability of MCMC results.  BREAD has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners using probabilistic programming languages. The bug detection example further underscores its significance.



**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous, quantitative measure for assessing MCMC inference quality (in the simulated data setting).
* Extends a valuable existing technique (BDMC) to a new and important application.
* Demonstrates practical utility through integration with WebPPL and Stan.
* Includes strong empirical validation.
* Offers potential for improving the reliability of probabilistic programming systems.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited applicability to a subset of MCMC algorithms.
* Reliance on simulated data or assumptions about approximate posteriors for real-world application.
* Potential computational cost.



**6. Recommendations & Questions for the Authors:**

* **Clarify Limitations:**  More explicitly discuss the computational cost of BREAD and its limitations regarding scalability.
* **Discuss Practical Interpretation of Bounds:** Provide guidance on how to interpret the Jeffreys divergence bounds in practical settings.  What constitutes an acceptable level of divergence?
* **Scalability Analysis:** Include a brief analysis of the method's scalability with increasing model complexity and data size.
* **Address Real-World Applicability:**  Elaborate on potential strategies for mitigating the limitations when applying BREAD to real-world data without exact posterior samples.
* **Streamline Presentation:**  Consider streamlining the mathematical notation and providing more intuitive explanations for key concepts.



**Overall, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference and warrants acceptance. The authors have presented a novel, theoretically sound, and practically validated method for assessing MCMC reliability. Addressing the minor suggestions above would further strengthen the paper.**
