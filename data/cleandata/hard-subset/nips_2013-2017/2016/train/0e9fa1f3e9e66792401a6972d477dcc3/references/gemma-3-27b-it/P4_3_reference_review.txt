This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via AIS or SMC) and the true posterior. The core idea builds upon bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide stochastic upper bounds on the divergence, enabling a quantifiable assessment of inference accuracy, particularly valuable in probabilistic programming. The authors also present a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from inferred hyperparameters, and demonstrate implementations within WebPPL and Stan.

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation, technical details, and experimental validation. It correctly positions the work within the context of existing MCMC diagnostics and marginal likelihood estimation techniques like AIS and SMC. The connection to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka’s independent work based on BDMC is also appropriately discussed. The four main contributions – divergence bounding, exact computation verification, real-world data protocol, and language integration – are clearly stated and addressed throughout the paper. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence appears logically sound, building upon existing BDMC analysis. The demonstration of accuracy on toy examples provides initial confidence in the method's efficacy.
*   **Novelty:** Extending BDMC to measure posterior accuracy, rather than just marginal likelihood, represents a valuable contribution. The protocol for real-world data analysis addresses a crucial practical challenge, and the implementations in WebPPL and Stan make the method accessible.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and easy to follow. The use of examples and visualizations enhances understanding.
*   **Significance:** Reliable assessment of MCMC/inference quality is a long-standing problem in the field. BREAD offers a rigorous, automated approach, potentially reducing reliance on heuristic diagnostics and improving trust in probabilistic programming results. The identification of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the practical value of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Applicability:** The method is currently limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). Its direct application to traditional MCMC methods is not immediately apparent, though the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest broader implications.
*   **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and hence BREAD, can be computationally expensive, particularly for complex models. The paper doesn't fully address the trade-off between the accuracy gains and the increased computational burden.
*   **Real-World Data Protocol:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on fitting hyperparameters. The performance of this approach is sensitive to the quality of the hyperparameter inference, and the paper doesn’t fully explore the impact of inaccurate hyperparameter estimates.
*   **Dependence on Exact Posterior Samples (Simulated Data):** While the simulated data experiments provide valuable validation, the need for exact posterior samples restricts the method’s applicability to models where such samples can be obtained.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for quantifying MCMC inference accuracy.
*   The results demonstrate the potential of BREAD to provide rigorous, automated diagnostics for probabilistic programming.
*   The implementations in WebPPL and Stan significantly enhance the practical impact of the work.
*   The identification of a bug in WebPPL illustrates the utility of the method for software validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited applicability of the method to specific types of algorithms (AIS, SMC) may restrict its immediate impact.
*   The computational cost of BREAD could be a barrier to adoption for complex models.
*   The reliance on hyperparameter inference in the real-world data protocol introduces potential uncertainties.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-executed solution. The technical soundness, clarity, and potential impact of the work are commendable. While the limitations regarding applicability and computational cost should be acknowledged, the benefits of having a rigorous, automated diagnostic tool for MCMC inference outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance.
