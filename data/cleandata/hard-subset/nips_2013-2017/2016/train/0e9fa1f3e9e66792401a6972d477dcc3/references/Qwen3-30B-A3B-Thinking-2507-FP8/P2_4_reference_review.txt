

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method to rigorously measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly those that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC). The key insight is that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence. The authors also propose a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical derivation showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is clear and well-supported. The authors carefully justify their claims through mathematical derivations (Section 3.1) and validate their method on toy examples where both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound can be computed exactly (Appendix B). Their experimental validation across five different probabilistic models in Stan demonstrates that the behavior on simulated data is consistent with real data (Section 5.1.1), addressing a key limitation of the method. The paper is complete and not merely a position paper, as it provides both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the method requires exact posterior samples, which is only available for simulated data) and propose a practical protocol to address this limitation.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section (Section 2) effectively introduces the necessary concepts (AIS, BDMC) without overwhelming the reader. The mathematical derivations are presented with clarity, and the connection between BDMC and the new method is well-explained. The experimental section is well-structured, with clear descriptions of the validation protocols and the scientific findings. The figures (Fig. 1, Fig. 2) effectively illustrate key results. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including model specifications and implementation details.

## Originality

The paper makes a novel contribution by extending BDMC from estimating marginal likelihoods to measuring the reliability of posterior samples. While BDMC was previously used for estimating marginal likelihoods, this paper shows how it can be repurposed to evaluate the quality of approximate posterior inference. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), by noting that their method uses the true posterior to initialize the reverse chain, while the other method uses an arbitrary reference distribution. The paper adequately references related work on convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks and Gelman, 1998) and MCMC tuning (e.g., Roberts and Rosenthal, 2001), making it clear how their work fits within the broader literature.

## Significance

The paper addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic inference: how to reliably measure the quality of MCMC-based posterior approximations. Current convergence diagnostics are heuristic and cannot confirm convergence, while this method provides rigorous bounds on the error between approximate and true posteriors. This is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan, where non-expert users need to trust the inference results. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of their method by using it to guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and even uncover a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2). The integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks makes this method immediately applicable to a wide range of practitioners.

## Weaknesses

The main limitation is that BREAD requires exact posterior samples, which is only feasible for simulated data. While the authors propose a protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2), this relies on the assumption that datasets generated from fitted hyperparameters resemble real datasets. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't fully address potential cases where this assumption might fail (e.g., when the model is misspecified).

Additionally, the computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains might be prohibitive for very large models, though this is a limitation common to many MCMC diagnostics.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference by providing a rigorous, general, and automatic method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates clear utility. The paper addresses a major pain point in probabilistic modeling, particularly for non-expert users of probabilistic programming languages.

The strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The method is novel, well-justified, and has clear practical applications. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a rigorous alternative to heuristic convergence diagnostics.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**

The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level. It is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents a novel application of BDMC (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with practical implications (Significance). The method has the potential to become a standard tool for evaluating MCMC inference in probabilistic programming systems, making it highly relevant to the NIPS community.
