### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel method, Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core contribution is extending the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally proposed by Grosse et al. (2015) for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution (generated by methods like Annealed Importance Sampling, AIS) and the true posterior.

A key requirement of the method is an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, which restricts its rigorous application to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world datasets: first, estimate hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these estimated hyperparameters; and third, apply BREAD on this simulated data. The paper provides empirical validation for this protocol, showing that inference behavior is often consistent between real and appropriately simulated data.

The authors implement BREAD in two prominent probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They demonstrate its utility through a series of experiments, including validating the real-data protocol across five different models and using BREAD to compare the performance of collapsed versus uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model. A notable practical outcome was the discovery of a bug in WebPPL, highlighting BREAD's potential as a debugging tool.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in Bayesian inference: how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. Existing convergence diagnostics are often heuristic and can only flag non-convergence. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality (an expected bound on Jeffreys divergence), which is a significant step forward, particularly for users of probabilistic programming languages who rely on black-box inference.

2.  **Elegant and Sound Technical Contribution:** The central theoretical result—that the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation—is novel, elegant, and clearly derived. It provides a beautiful connection between the problem of marginal likelihood estimation and the problem of measuring posterior approximation quality. The authors also provide evidence on toy problems (Appendix B) that the bound is reasonably tight.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength.
    *   The authors thoughtfully address the main limitation of their method (the need for simulated data) by proposing and validating a protocol for real-world data applications. The experiments across five models successfully demonstrate the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data under this protocol.
    *   The case study comparing model representations for matrix factorization is a compelling demonstration of how BREAD can guide practical modeling and algorithmic decisions, yielding non-obvious, platform-specific insights.
    *   The anecdote about debugging WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's practical utility for developers of inference systems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, the theoretical derivation is concise, and the motivation for the work is compelling.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Direct Applicability of Rigorous Bounds:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD only apply to simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real data is a well-motivated heuristic, but it relies on the model being reasonably well-specified. While the validation is encouraging, the performance of this heuristic in cases of significant model misspecification remains an open question.

2.  **Scope of Applicable Algorithms:** The method is tailored to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, like AIS and SMC. The paper argues that insights gained from evaluating these algorithms (e.g., the effectiveness of transition kernels) can generalize to standard MCMC. This is a reasonable claim, but it is an indirect benefit. BREAD does not provide a direct quality measure for a standard, long-run MCMC chain (e.g., from NUTS or Metropolis-Hastings) that is not embedded within an AIS/SMC scheme.

3.  **Practical Considerations (Computational Cost and Variance):**
    *   The computational cost of running BREAD can be substantial, as it requires running multiple forward and reverse AIS chains. A more explicit discussion of the computational overhead relative to the original inference task would improve the paper's practical guidance.
    *   Footnote 3 mentions a subtle but important point: the theoretical analysis applies to individual AIS chains, and it is unclear if it justifies averaging estimates across chains to reduce variance (as is standard practice with AIS weights). This implies the bound `B_hat` from a single run could be noisy. Expanding on the variance of the bound estimator and the practical implications of this limitation would be valuable.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Addresses a highly significant problem in the field with a novel and elegant theoretical result.
*   Provides a practical tool for researchers, practitioners, and PPL developers to quantitatively assess and debug inference algorithms.
*   The proposed protocol for real-world data is thoughtfully designed and backed by solid empirical validation.
*   The experiments are thorough, compelling, and demonstrate clear scientific and practical utility.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The rigorous theoretical guarantees are confined to the simulated data setting, and the real-data protocol is a heuristic.
*   The method's applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-style algorithms, not all MCMC methods directly.
*   Practical aspects like computational cost and the high variance of single-chain estimates could be explored more deeply.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The strengths—a novel theoretical insight into a critical problem, combined with thorough and convincing empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are transparently discussed by the authors. This work is likely to be highly influential and to spur further research on rigorous diagnostics for MCMC. I strongly recommend acceptance.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Model Misspecification:** While the appendix touches on this, it would be beneficial to add a short discussion in the main text about how the real-data protocol might behave under model misspecification. For instance, you could suggest that a large discrepancy between the forward AIS curves on real and simulated data might itself serve as a diagnostic for misspecification.

2.  **Variance of the Bound:** Could you elaborate on the issue raised in Footnote 3? While deriving a bound for an averaged estimator might be beyond the scope of this work, a brief discussion on the practical variance of the single-chain bound `B_hat` and how many chains might be needed to get a reliable estimate of its expectation would be very helpful for practitioners.

3.  **Clarifying Algorithm Scope:** To avoid potential misunderstanding, it might be helpful to state more explicitly in the introduction or methods section that BREAD evaluates the inference process of AIS/SMC, and that its insights apply to standard MCMC primarily through the evaluation of the transition operators used within the annealing/sequential procedure.
