Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based approximate posterior inference. The key idea is to use annealed importance sampling (AIS) (Neal, 2001) in both forward and reverse directions to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log partition function ratio. The authors show that the expected gap between the reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of AIS approximate samples and the true target pT. This yields a quantitative, computable measure of approximation quality for algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over extended state spaces (AIS, SMC). They call the overall procedure BREAD.

The paper further proposes a practical protocol to apply these bounds to real datasets, where exact posterior samples are not available: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate a dataset from the fitted hyperparameters, and initialize the reverse AIS chain using a short MCMC run to approximate a posterior sample. They integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan, validate that forward-AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data across five models, examine sensitivity to the number of reverse-initialization MCMC steps, use BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations, and report discovering and fixing a sampling bug in WebPPL.

This work builds directly on BDMC’s log marginal likelihood sandwiching and connects to ideas in nonequilibrium work identities (Jarzynski, 1997), tempered transitions (Neal, 1996), and stochastic bounds for log partition functions. It is closely related to the contemporaneous “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which gives an unbiased estimator of a quantity that upper bounds Jeffreys divergence when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior; the current paper’s expectation bound and focus on AIS-based posterior sample quality provide a complementary emphasis and practical protocol for real data.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The derivations are clear and standard for AIS viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space. Writing the joint laws of forward and reverse chains and applying KL decomposition yields E[log R̂] ≤ log(ZT/Z1) − DKL(qfwd(xT) || pT) and E[log R̂rev] ≤ log(Z1/ZT) − DKL(pT || qfwd). The expected gap therefore bounds the Jeffreys divergence DJ(pT, qfwd). The expectation-only nature of the bound is accurately stated. The one-sided variants using any external lower/upper bound are correct.
- Assumptions and caveats: The upper bound on DJ is only in expectation; heavy tails of log-weights can yield high variance estimators, and the paper notes this when discussing visualization. For real data, the reverse initialization is approximate, so guarantees do not hold; the authors provide empirical validation across S ∈ {10,…,10^4}.
- Empirics: Experiments cover five standard models in Stan, showing good alignment between real and simulated forward AIS curves and insensitivity of reverse bounds to S. The matrix factorization case study sensibly illustrates how BREAD informs model representation choices. The bug-finding anecdote is compelling.

Areas to improve technical rigor:
- Concentration/uncertainty: Since the bound holds in expectation, the paper should report estimates with uncertainty (e.g., standard errors, median-of-means, or robust estimators), and discuss the variance/heavy-tail behavior. Given the cited Markov/tail results and Gogate et al. (2007), some finite-sample guidance or aggregation strategy would strengthen practical use.
- Reverse-chain correctness: Clarify the conditions under which the transition kernels (e.g., NUTS) are reversible and leave the tempered distributions invariant, and whether adaptation is disabled during sampling at each temperature.
- Path/schedule: Provide details on temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and per-temperature transitions; explore sensitivity to these choices.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The notation for forward/reverse AIS and the decomposition into xT and v is clear. The background on stochastic bounds is helpful. Suggestions:
- Explicitly state how the empirical estimator of E[·] is formed (per-chain vs. averaging), and provide guidance on the number of AIS chains needed.
- Summarize the toy examples where exact J and the bound B are computed (Appendix B) with a table/figure in the main text to convey tightness more concretely.
- In Section 3.2, clarify the protocol for the number of reverse-initialization MCMC steps and diagnostics to assess the plausibility of the approximate posterior sample.
- State whether SMC is actually evaluated, since applicability is claimed.

Originality
The theoretical contribution—showing that the BDMC forward/reverse log gap upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) and pT—is a neat and natural extension of BDMC. The real-data protocol and integration into WebPPL and Stan are practically valuable. The overlap with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) is acknowledged; this paper’s framing in terms of AIS sample quality and the pragmatic fitted-hyperparameter protocol provide additional value, though the novelty is incremental relative to BDMC.

Significance
Providing a quantitative, broadly applicable measure of MCMC-based posterior sample quality is important. The BREAD procedure can guide model/algorithm design in probabilistic programming and can serve as a regression test for inference engines. The reliance on simulated data and expectation-only guarantees limit the formality on real data, but the protocol and empirical validations are persuasive. If adopted, this could influence practice in PPLs and MCMC benchmarking.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Expectation-only bound: Discuss and, if possible, provide high-probability or finite-sample confidence intervals for DJ via robust averaging or concentration inequalities; at least report variability.
- Scope of experiments: Include an example with pronounced multimodality or higher dimensionality to stress-test the method. If SMC is claimed, include a small SMC evaluation.
- Reporting: Alongside forward/reverse curves, report numerical estimates of the bound on DJ with uncertainties. Explore correlation with standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS) to situate the measure.
- Real-data proxy: Provide a diagnostic to check that the fitted-hyperparameter simulated data are close to the real data (e.g., posterior predictive checks) and that reverse initialization is not overly sensitive to starting points.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical link between BDMC and an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical, tool-supported implementation in two PPLs, with code apparently exercised on multiple models.
- Useful empirical demonstrations: model-representation guidance and detection of a real bug.
- Addresses an important need: quantifying reliability of MCMC-based inference.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty is incremental relative to BDMC and closely related concurrent work.
- Guarantees hold only in expectation and only rigorously on simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Limited exploration of estimator variance and finite-sample behavior; lack of SMC experiments despite claims.

Questions for the authors
- How many AIS chains are used to estimate E[gap], and how do you aggregate? Can you report variability or robust estimates?
- Are NUTS/HMC transitions used at each temperature strictly reversible/invariant without adaptation? What settings were used?
- Do you have results with SMC transitions, and do bounds behave similarly?
- Can you quantify the tightness of the bound on more than toy examples (e.g., via sandwiching with longer runs)?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a technically sound and practically useful extension of BDMC, integrates it into widely used tools, and demonstrates its utility. Addressing variance/concentration reporting and adding more experimental detail would further strengthen the contribution.
