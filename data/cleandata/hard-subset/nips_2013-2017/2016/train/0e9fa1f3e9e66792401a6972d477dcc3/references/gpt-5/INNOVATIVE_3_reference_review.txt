Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-style approximate inference by upper bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. The key idea is to use forward AIS (stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS initialized from an exact posterior sample (stochastic upper bound), and show that the expected gap between these bounds upper bounds DJ(q, p). The authors further show how to obtain one-sided KL bounds if an external upper/lower bound is available. Because exact posterior samples are only available for simulated data, they propose a pragmatic protocol for real datasets: fit hyperparameters to the real data, simulate from those, and initialize the reverse chain using a short MCMC run, empirically validating that this preserves the behavior of the bounds. They implement the method (BREAD) in WebPPL and Stan, validate on several models (regression, AR, mixtures, matrix factorization), use it to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations across PPLs, and even uncover a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
BDMC for marginal likelihood sandwiching (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001) form the foundation. The paper’s main novelty is translating BDMC’s sandwich into an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the target, making the technique directly about sample quality rather than only log evidence estimation. The connection to thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski identities is acknowledged. The method is compared to Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015), which provide distributional diagnostics under stronger assumptions, and to concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), clarifying similarities and differences. The discussion of MCMC diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin, Brooks-Gelman, etc.) is appropriate.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean and useful bridge from BDMC to a bound on Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples. This reframes AIS/SMC as tools for assessing posterior sample quality, not just estimating log-evidence.
- Practical impact: Integrations into Stan and WebPPL, with examples showing how the tool informs modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and detects implementation bugs.
- Protocol for real data: A pragmatic and easy-to-follow procedure that keeps the spirit of BDMC while acknowledging the lack of exact posterior samples in practice; empirical validation suggests robustness.
- Clarity of guarantees: In the simulated-data setting, the guarantees are rigorous; the derivations are straightforward and correct.
- Breadth: The study spans multiple models and two PPLs, and demonstrates consistent forward AIS behavior between real and matched simulated datasets.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees are in expectation: The Jeffreys divergence bound is an expected upper bound. Users need guidance on how to translate this into actionable decisions per run; variance of the gap can be high for heavy-tailed weights. The paper cites ways to tighten tails via multiple samples, but does not provide a concrete recipe in the main text.
- Real-data protocol loses rigor: When using fitted hyperparameters and approximate reverse-chain initialization, the bound is no longer guaranteed. The empirical evidence is encouraging but limited, and there is no theoretical control of the bias introduced by this step.
- Applicability scope: The approach is directly applicable to AIS/SMC-like samplers that can be cast as importance sampling on an extended space and require a reversible kernel family along a temperature path. It does not directly assess generic MCMC (e.g., HMC) run in isolation without an AIS scaffold.
- Experimental scale and coverage: Most experiments are modest-scale and largely AIS-based; SMC is mentioned but not empirically demonstrated. There is little quantitative calibration of bound tightness beyond toy cases, and no comparison to alternative diagnostics (e.g., Stein discrepancy) on the same tasks.
- Practical guidance: The paper does not provide thresholds for “good enough” DJ for practitioners, nor sensitivity analyses on the number/placement of intermediate distributions, or computational overhead versus diagnostic value.

Quality
The theoretical derivations are sound and clearly related to BDMC. The experiments convincingly support the claims about practicality, especially the proxy-simulation protocol and utility for model representation choices and debugging. Some analyses that would strengthen the paper (variance/tail control, SMC experiments, comparison to alternative diagnostics) are missing but not fatal given the novelty.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The exposition of forward/reverse AIS and the factorized distributions is clear. Minor issues: some references and notation formatting look rough in the provided text; a short “user guide” subsection with actionable advice (e.g., number of AIS runs, schedule design, interpreting the bound magnitudes) would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. The conversion of BDMC’s sandwiching into an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for approximate samples is a crisp and impactful conceptual step. The practical integration into PPLs and the real-data protocol further enhance originality beyond a purely theoretical note. The overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” work is acknowledged; this paper’s use of the true posterior for guarantees and its broader empirical validation remain distinct.

Significance
Meaningful for practitioners and PPL designers. Reliable, quantitative diagnostics for posterior sample quality are scarce, and this offers a principled, implementable tool that can influence modeling and algorithmic choices, and even surface implementation bugs. While limited in scope, it opens a direction likely to be built upon.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide a concrete multiple-run procedure to convert the expected bound into a high-probability statement, with guidance on number of runs?
- Show sensitivity to annealing schedule, number of temperatures, and MCMC kernel choice.
- Include an SMC experiment to support the claimed applicability.
- Compare to Stein discrepancy on at least one model to position strengths/weaknesses.
- Discuss how to adapt the method to evaluate pure MCMC runs (e.g., via AIS bridges starting from chain states).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel conceptual contribution linking BDMC to sample-quality diagnostics.
- Practical and useful integrations; clear evidence of utility (model choices, debugging).
- Important problem area; likely to influence PPL practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees are expectation-based; limited guidance on variance and per-run reliability.
- Real-data protocol lacks theoretical guarantees.
- Limited experimental scope (no SMC, small scale, no comparison to alternative diagnostics).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a novel and promising direction with clear practical value, and I am willing to overlook the incomplete validations and scope limitations in light of the conceptual contribution and potential impact.

Confidence
High.
