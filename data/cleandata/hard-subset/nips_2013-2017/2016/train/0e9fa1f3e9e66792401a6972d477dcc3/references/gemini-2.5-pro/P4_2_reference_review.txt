### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD) to quantitatively measure the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method extends the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The central idea is that for algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS), one can obtain a stochastic lower bound on the log marginal likelihood (log-ML) via a "forward" AIS run (annealing from prior to posterior) and a stochastic upper bound via a "reverse" AIS run (annealing from posterior to prior). The reverse run requires an exact sample from the true posterior, limiting its direct application to simulated data.

The paper's main technical contribution is a refinement of the BDMC analysis, demonstrating that the expected gap between these stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log-ML serves as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. This provides a principled, quantitative measure of the quality of the approximate samples.

To bridge the gap between simulated data (where the method is rigorous) and real-world data (where exact posterior samples are unavailable), the authors propose a practical protocol. This involves fitting hyperparameters on the real data, simulating a new dataset from the model using these hyperparameters, and then running BREAD on this simulated dataset. The paper provides empirical validation showing that inference behavior is often consistent between the real and simulated data generated this way.

Finally, the authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They demonstrate its utility by using it to compare different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and to uncover a subtle bug in WebPPL's sampler, highlighting its value as a tool for both model selection and software validation.

The work builds directly upon BDMC [GGA15] and is related to the long-standing field of MCMC convergence diagnostics, but offers a more holistic, quantitative measure of distributional accuracy compared to traditional heuristic methods. The authors also clearly situate their work with respect to concurrent, related research [CTM16].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation in Section 3.1, which establishes the connection between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence, is a clear and sound extension of the analysis in [GGA15]. The claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical analysis and extensive, well-designed experiments. The authors are careful to evaluate the different facets of their proposal:
1.  The tightness of the bound is evaluated on toy problems (Appendix B).
2.  The protocol for real-world data is validated by comparing inference behavior on real and simulated datasets for five different models, showing strong consistency (Section 5.1.1).
3.  The robustness of the protocol to the use of an approximate posterior sample for initializing the reverse chain is tested and validated (Section 5.1.2).

The application of BREAD to debug WebPPL is a particularly strong piece of evidence for the method's correctness and practical utility.

A minor weakness is that the protocol for real-world data remains a heuristic. While the validation is convincing for the models tested, the assumption that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters will mirror behavior on real data may not hold universally, especially in cases of significant model misspecification. The authors honestly acknowledge this by mentioning an example of failure in the appendix, which strengthens the paper.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented concisely and provides all the necessary context. The main theoretical result is derived step-by-step, making it easy to follow. The structure is logical, moving from theory to validation and then to practical applications. The figures effectively visualize the experimental results, for instance by using quartiles to robustly show the distribution of AIS estimates.

A small point for improvement: Footnote 3 mentions that the analysis does not justify averaging weights across AIS chains, and thus individual estimates are reported. However, the main result is on the *expectation* of the bound gap, which is typically estimated by averaging. It would be helpful to clarify in the main text how the empirical bound shown in the plots is computed from the 100 individual chains and how this relates to the theoretical expectation.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel application and extension of the BDMC framework. While BDMC itself is not new, using its bound gap to provide a quantitative measure of posterior sample quality in terms of Jeffreys divergence is a new and insightful contribution. This work moves beyond simply estimating the marginal likelihood to directly assessing the quality of the posterior approximation. The proposed protocol for applying the technique to real-world data is also a novel, practical contribution that significantly enhances the method's applicability.

The originality is slightly tempered by the existence of concurrent work [CTM16] exploring similar ideas. However, the authors do an excellent job of discussing this related work, and their focus on providing a practical protocol for real data and demonstrating its use in PPLs for model comparison and debugging represents a distinct and valuable contribution.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant. The lack of reliable, quantitative, and automated methods for validating approximate inference is a major bottleneck in the broader adoption of probabilistic modeling, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming systems aimed at non-expert users. BREAD offers a principled and practical step toward addressing this critical problem.

The potential impact is large:
-   **For practitioners:** It provides a tool to gain confidence in the results of complex models.
-   **For researchers:** It offers a quantitative criterion for comparing different models or inference algorithms, as demonstrated by the collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization experiment.
-   **For PPL developers:** It serves as a powerful debugging and regression testing tool to ensure the correctness of inference implementations.

The primary limitation on its significance is its scope; the method is directly applicable to AIS and SMC, but not to the most common MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS directly. However, the authors rightly argue that insights gained from analyzing AIS can inform the design of MCMC algorithms more broadly, as many design choices (e.g., transition operators, model parameterization) are shared.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a critical problem:** Provides a novel and principled solution to the long-standing challenge of assessing MCMC reliability.
2.  **Technically sound:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by clear derivations.
3.  **Thorough empirical validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims, including the heuristic protocol for real-world data.
4.  **High practical value:** The demonstrated applications in model comparison and PPL debugging are compelling and highlight the method's immediate utility.
5.  **Excellent presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited applicability:** The method is directly applicable only to a specific class of samplers (AIS/SMC) and relies on a heuristic protocol for real-world data analysis.
2.  **Incremental novelty:** The work is a direct extension of existing work on BDMC, and similar ideas have been explored concurrently by others.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a significant and practical contribution to an important area of research.
