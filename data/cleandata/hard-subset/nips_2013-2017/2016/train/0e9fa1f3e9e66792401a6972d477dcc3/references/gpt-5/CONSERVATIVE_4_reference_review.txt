Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-type samplers that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space. The core theoretical contribution is to show that the gap between forward-AIS and reverse-AIS log-partition estimates upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS output distribution q(xT) and the true target pT. The authors derive inequalities showing E[log R̂rev−1 − log R̂] ≥ DKL(pT||q) + DKL(q||pT). They also propose a heuristic protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters to the real dataset, simulate data from the fitted model, and initialize reverse AIS using an approximately drawn posterior sample for the simulated data. They integrate this into WebPPL and Stan, run experiments on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures), and use BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed representations and to catch an implementation bug in WebPPL.

This work builds directly on BDMC, AIS (Neal, 2001), thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski, and is related to recent work on sample quality metrics (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). It overlaps with concurrent work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) that defines a subjective divergence based on BDMC; the present paper’s bound is equivalent when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior.

Strengths
- Important problem: diagnosing reliability of MCMC-based inference remains hard; providing principled, model-agnostic diagnostics is valuable.
- Clear theoretical link: the expectation of the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence of the AIS sample distribution. The derivation is clean and leverages standard AIS/importance-sampling arguments.
- Practical integration: implementations for WebPPL and Stan; demonstrations across several canonical models; the debugging example is compelling.
- Insight into modeling choices: empirical comparison of collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization is useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Only an expectation bound: The key guarantee is E[gap] ≥ Jeffreys divergence. A single-run gap is a noisy random variable with potentially heavy tails; no high-probability or finite-sample confidence bounds are provided. This limits its utility as a rigorous diagnostic for a single application run.
- Dependence on exact posterior samples: The bound is only rigorous on simulated data where exact posterior samples can be produced. The real-data protocol replaces this with an approximate posterior initialization for reverse AIS; the resulting “bound” loses its theoretical status. The empirical validation (insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps S) is suggestive but hardly definitive.
- Limited empirical scope and calibration: Tightness is shown exactly on toy distributions; on realistic models, the paper reports quartiles of AIS bounds but does not calibrate the estimated divergence against ground-truth divergences in models where these are analytically available (e.g., conjugate Gaussian models) or against downstream estimation error. No study of how the bound tracks known pathologies (multimodality, poor mixing) is provided.
- Variance and estimator practice: The paper acknowledges that log AIS estimates have pathological variance properties and averages are not justified by the analysis. Yet most plots rely on summaries over many chains and quartiles. There is little guidance on how many forward/reverse runs are needed for a reliable diagnostic, or what to report to users.
- Applicability beyond AIS/SMC: Claims that insights transfer to “MCMC more broadly” are not well supported. The method requires AIS/SMC-like constructions and intermediate distributions; many widely used samplers (e.g., pure HMC for posterior expectations) are not naturally evaluated by BREAD without wrapping them in AIS, which changes the algorithm.
- Sensitivity and practicality: The method’s behavior will depend strongly on schedule, choice of kernels at each temperature, and tuning (e.g., NUTS step size/mass matrix) across intermediate distributions. The paper does not study sensitivity, cost, or provide recommendations for robust use. Computational overhead is roughly doubled (reverse direction) and not quantified.
- Relation to concurrent work: The overlap with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) is acknowledged, but a more thorough comparison (empirically and conceptually) is missing, especially since the real-data protocol essentially uses a reference distribution as in subjective divergence.

Quality
Technically sound as an extension of BDMC; the inequalities are standard applications of KL manipulations and AIS correctness. However, the main guarantee is only in expectation, and the empirical evaluation is limited in scale and calibration. The real-data procedure lacks theoretical backing.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The exposition of AIS/BDMC is helpful. However, the paper should emphasize more prominently and early that the bound is in expectation only, and that guarantees do not carry to the real-data protocol. Practical guidance (how many runs, schedules, diagnostics to report) is sparse.

Originality
Incremental. The main idea—using the BDMC sandwich to control divergences—feels like a direct and natural consequence of BDMC’s analysis; similar observations appear in concurrent CTM16. The implementation in PPLs is useful but not conceptually novel.

Significance
Potentially useful as a tool within PPL systems for testing inference and catching bugs. As a general diagnostic for MCMC reliability on real data, the lack of high-probability guarantees and dependence on simulated-data proxies limits impact. Broader claims about informing MCMC design are premature.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide high-probability concentration bounds (even if loose) on the Jeffreys divergence using multiple independent BDMC runs.
- Calibrate the bound on models with analytic posteriors (e.g., Gaussian-linear regression) at nontrivial dimensions; report correlation with actual errors in posterior expectations.
- Analyze sensitivity to temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and choice/tuning of kernels at each temperature (especially with NUTS).
- Quantify computational overhead and provide practical defaults.
- Compare empirically with Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey) and standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS) to show added value.
- Clarify assumptions ensuring that the Stan HMC/NUTS kernels leave each intermediate distribution invariant without adaptation leakage.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, model-agnostic approach.
- Clean theoretical link to Jeffreys divergence (in expectation).
- Practical integration and some informative case studies; demonstrated debugging utility.

Cons
- Expectation-only bound; no finite-sample guarantees.
- Real-data protocol breaks theoretical guarantees; limited validation.
- Empirical evaluation modest; no calibration against ground truth or downstream error.
- Applicability constrained to AIS/SMC-style samplers; sensitivity and cost not studied.
- Overlap with concurrent work; novelty is incremental.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and potentially useful within PPL tooling, but the theoretical guarantee is too weak for a reliable diagnostic, and the empirical evidence is insufficiently thorough to justify the broader claims. Strengthening the validation and providing finite-sample guidance would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
