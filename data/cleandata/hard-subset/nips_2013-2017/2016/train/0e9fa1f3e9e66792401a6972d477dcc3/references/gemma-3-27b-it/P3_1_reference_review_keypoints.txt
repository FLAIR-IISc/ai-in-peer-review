- Overall: Strong accept — BREAD rigorously assesses MCMC reliability despite high computational cost, with solid theory, implementation, and experiments.
- Summary: Extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo to upper-bound the Jeffreys divergence, offering a quantitative measure of posterior approximation quality.
- Related work: Situates BREAD among MCMC diagnostics, marginal likelihood estimation, SMC, and notes concurrent similar work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka.
- Theoretical Rigor: Derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound is mathematically sound and builds on established BDMC results.
- Novel Application: Applying BDMC to directly assess posterior accuracy is an original and significant contribution.
- Practical Implementation: BREAD implemented in WebPPL and Stan, lowering the barrier to adoption.
- Real-data Protocol: Proposes inferring hyperparameters, simulating data, and validating consistency to apply BREAD to real datasets.
- Experiments: Compelling validations include model-representation comparisons and detecting a WebPPL bug.
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written with clear derivations and intuitive explanations.
- Computational Cost: Main limitation — running forward and reverse AIS chains is time-consuming and limits applicability to small models.
- Dependence on Exact Samples: Requires exact posterior samples for simulated data, restricting direct applicability for some models.
- AIS Assumptions: Inherits limitations of AIS and is only applicable to algorithms expressible as importance sampling.
- Scalability Discussion: Lacks detailed strategies (e.g., variance reduction, parallelization) to mitigate computational expense.
- For Acceptance: Provides a rigorous, implemented, and validated tool addressing an important problem in Bayesian inference.
- Against Acceptance: High computational cost and reliance on exact posterior samples limit use in complex, real-world problems.
- Recommendation: Despite limitations, strengths outweigh weaknesses — strongly recommend acceptance.
