This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), to quantitatively measure the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by certain MCMC-like algorithms. The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC can, in expectation, provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution and the true posterior. This method is applicable to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC).

A significant practical limitation is that the reverse pass of the algorithm, which provides the upper bound, requires an exact sample from the target posterior distribution. This restricts the rigorous application of the method to simulated data, where the ground-truth parameters are known. To address this, the authors propose a protocol for real-world data: first, run inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from these estimated hyperparameters; third, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. The paper validates this approach and demonstrates its utility by applying it to models within two probabilistic programming frameworks, WebPPL and Stan, using it to compare model representations and even uncover a bug.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and assessing sample quality is a long-standing and critical challenge in Bayesian inference. Existing methods, such as the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic, are primarily heuristic and designed to detect non-convergence rather than provide a quantitative measure of sample quality. The ambition to develop a more rigorous, quantitative tool is highly commendable.

2.  **Methodologically Sound Derivation:** The core theoretical contribution in Section 3.1, which connects the BDMC bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence, is a clever and elegant extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). The derivation appears to be technically sound and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method *in the simulated data setting*.

3.  **Compelling Use Cases:** The experiments in Section 5.2 effectively demonstrate the potential utility of BREAD. The comparison of collapsed versus uncollapsed matrix factorization models provides a concrete example of how this tool can guide modeling decisions by offering a quantitative trade-off between computational cost and sampler accuracy. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a strong testament to the method's value as a sanity check and debugging tool for inference systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **The "Real Data" Protocol Lacks Rigor:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the disconnect between the rigorous guarantees provided for simulated data and the heuristic nature of the proposed protocol for real-world data. The core appeal of BREAD is its theoretical grounding, but this is forfeited when applied to real data. The protocol relies on several strong and unverified assumptions: (i) that the model is well-specified for the real data, (ii) that the initial hyperparameter estimates are accurate, and (iii) that the sampler's performance on data simulated from a point estimate of the hyperparameters is representative of its performance on the actual dataset. The validation provided (Fig. 1), which relies on visual similarity of AIS estimate distributions, is a plausibility argument at best and does not constitute rigorous evidence for the protocol's reliability. This significantly curtails the practical significance of the method, as the ultimate goal is almost always inference on real, not simulated, data.

2.  **Limited Applicability:** The method is only directly applicable to a specific class of algorithms (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue that "insights which inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly" can be gained, this connection is not explicitly demonstrated. It is unclear how tuning an AIS schedule using BREAD would inform the tuning of a fundamentally different algorithm like NUTS, which is the default in Stan. This limits the method's scope as a general-purpose diagnostic tool for the broader MCMC community.

3.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Diagnostics:** The paper introduces BREAD as a solution to the shortcomings of existing convergence diagnostics but fails to provide any direct comparison against them. Established methods like the Gelman-Rubin statistic (`R-hat`) and effective sample size (ESS) are the standard tools used by practitioners. A crucial missing piece of the evaluation is an experiment showing a scenario where, for example, `R-hat` indicates convergence while BREAD reveals a high Jeffreys divergence, or vice-versa. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical added value of BREAD over the tools that are already widely used and understood. The claim that BREAD can "rigorously demonstrate convergence" needs to be contextualized against the capabilities and failures of existing methods.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting method for bounding the error of certain sampling algorithms. For the specific case of simulated data and AIS-like samplers, the contribution is solid and provides a valuable tool for algorithm developers and researchers testing new models.

However, the paper's claims about its applicability to real-world data are not well-supported. The proposed protocol is a heuristic that abandons the rigor that makes the method attractive in the first place. This central weakness, combined with the lack of comparison to standard MCMC diagnostics, makes me skeptical of its broader impact and utility for the average practitioner. While the work is of good quality and clearly presented, its practical significance feels overstated given the limitations. It is a solid contribution to a niche problem (evaluating AIS on simulated data) but falls short of being the general, rigorous procedure for monitoring posterior inference that it claims to be.

I am borderline on this paper. The theoretical result is neat, but its practical application is fraught with unproven heuristics. Given the high standards of NIPS, the gap between the method's rigorous foundation and its proposed practical use is a significant concern.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in MCMC inference.
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical result connecting BDMC to the Jeffreys divergence.
*   Provides a useful tool for debugging and evaluating inference in probabilistic programming languages, as demonstrated by the experiments.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method's rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data, a major practical limitation.
*   The proposed protocol for real-world data is a heuristic that lacks theoretical justification and is only weakly validated.
*   No comparison is made to standard, widely-used MCMC diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, ESS), making it difficult to judge its practical contribution.
*   Limited applicability to AIS/SMC-like samplers, not general MCMC methods.
