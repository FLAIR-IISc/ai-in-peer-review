This paper presents a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution, extending the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The method is applicable to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors provide theoretical analysis, empirical validation on toy distributions, a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (using hyperparameter inference), and implementations within the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages.

The core idea of extending BDMC to measure posterior accuracy is novel and builds logically on previous work in bounding marginal likelihoods. The derivation of an estimator for the Jeffreys divergence, and showing its accuracy on toy problems, is a valuable contribution. Addressing the challenge of applying the method to real-world data via a hyperparameter fitting protocol demonstrates practical awareness. The implementations in WebPPL and Stan are significant, as they integrate the method into widely-used inference systems.  The identification of a bug in WebPPL while using BREAD highlights the method’s potential for software validation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building on established work in BDMC and KL divergence bounds. The derivation of the divergence bound is clearly presented.
*   **Novelty:** Extending BDMC to measure divergence between approximate and true posteriors is a novel and useful application of the BDMC framework. The approach of using reverse annealing for divergence estimation is insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanation of AIS and BDMC provides necessary background. Mathematical notation is standard and consistently used.
*   **Significance:** The ability to reliably assess the quality of approximate inference is highly significant, especially in the context of probabilistic programming where users may lack expertise in diagnosing convergence issues. The tool has the potential to increase confidence in results and guide model/algorithm design.
*   **Implementation:** Providing implementations in both WebPPL and Stan increases the accessibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Applicability:** The method is currently limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling, which excludes many traditional MCMC methods directly.  While the authors acknowledge this and suggest broader implications, the practical reach remains constrained.
*   **Computational Cost:** BDMC is computationally intensive, requiring exact samples from the target distribution (or a good proxy in the real-world setting). This cost might be prohibitive for complex models or high-dimensional data.
*   **Hyperparameter Protocol:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on fitting hyperparameters, which introduces its own set of challenges and potential biases. The assumption that fitted hyperparameters accurately reflect the true data-generating process may not always hold.
*  **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the validation on toy distributions is helpful, more extensive empirical evaluation on a wider range of real-world problems would strengthen the claims about the method's practical utility.  The examples in Section 5.1.1 are limited to five models.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference—reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples.
*   The proposed method (BREAD) is novel, technically sound, and builds on a strong theoretical foundation (BDMC).
*   The implementations in WebPPL and Stan are valuable contributions that make the method accessible to a wider audience.
*   The identification of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates the method’s practical utility beyond its intended purpose.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited applicability of the method (to importance sampling algorithms only) restricts its immediate impact.
*   The computational cost of BDMC may be a barrier to adoption in complex settings.
*   The hyperparameter protocol for real-world data introduces potential biases that are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound method for assessing the quality of approximate inference. While the limited applicability and computational cost are drawbacks, the potential benefits of reliably quantifying approximation error, particularly in probabilistic programming, are significant. The implementations in WebPPL and Stan are a substantial contribution. I recommend acceptance.
