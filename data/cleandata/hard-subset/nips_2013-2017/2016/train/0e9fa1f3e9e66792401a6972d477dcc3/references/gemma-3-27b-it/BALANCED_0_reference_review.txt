This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the approximate distribution obtained from algorithms like AIS or SMC. The core idea is to leverage bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to obtain stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, and connect this to bounding the divergence. The authors demonstrate the method on toy distributions, propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data (via hyperparameter estimation and simulation), and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan.

The paper is well-motivated. Rigorously validating MCMC and related inference algorithms is a long-standing challenge, and the proposed approach offers a potentially valuable tool. The connection between BDMC and bounding the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and insightful contribution. The extensions to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation are practical and address a crucial limitation of the method. The implementation in both WebPPL and Stan increases the accessibility and potential impact of the work. The use of BREAD to diagnose a bug in WebPPL provides a concrete demonstration of its utility beyond merely quantifying approximation error.

However, there are several weaknesses. First, the theoretical guarantees rely on having an exact sample from the true posterior, which is only readily available in simulated data. While the proposed protocol for real-world data attempts to address this, the validity of this protocol is not fully established. The paper shows *consistency* between real and simulated data, but doesn’t provide a strong theoretical justification for why this consistency should hold beyond specific scenarios. The number of MCMC steps used to approximate the posterior in the real-world protocol seems somewhat arbitrary and could significantly impact the quality of the bounds. More investigation into the sensitivity of the results to this parameter would be beneficial.

Second, the experimental evaluation, while thorough, is limited to a relatively small set of models. It would be valuable to demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a wider range of models, including those with more complex dependencies and non-identifiability issues.  The experiments largely focus on demonstrating that BREAD *works* rather than showcasing substantial insights gleaned *from* using BREAD on challenging inference problems.  The matrix factorization example is illustrative, but the benefit observed isn't dramatically compelling.

Third, the clarity suffers in some sections. The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound in Section 3.1 is dense and difficult to follow without careful reading. A more intuitive explanation of the key steps would improve accessibility. Also, while the related work section is extensive, it could be more focused.  Several cited papers have only a tangential connection to the core contribution.  A more thorough discussion of the connection to Stein's method and related divergence estimation techniques would be helpful.

Finally, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, a more comprehensive discussion of its computational cost would be beneficial. BDMC can be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional models. Understanding the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost is crucial for practical application.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence.
*   Addresses a long-standing challenge in validating probabilistic inference algorithms.
*   Practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data.
*   Implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Demonstrated usefulness in debugging an existing probabilistic programming language.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions (exact posterior samples).
*   Protocol for real-world data lacks strong theoretical justification.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   Some sections lack clarity and require more intuitive explanations.
*   Computational cost is not thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The proposed method has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners alike. However, the limitations outlined above prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with the suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the theoretical justification for the real-world protocol, the scope of the experimental evaluation, and the clarity of the technical presentation. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I encourage the authors to resubmit a revised version in the future.
