Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantitatively assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS and SMC). The central theoretical contribution shows that the gap between forward AIS (a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS (a stochastic upper bound enabled by an exact posterior sample) upper-bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS and the true target. The method, termed BREAD, is implemented in WebPPL and Stan. The authors also propose a practical protocol to approximate the required exact posterior sample on real data by (i) fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, (ii) simulating synthetic data from those hyperparameters, and (iii) initializing the reverse chain using a short MCMC run targeting the posterior given the simulated data. Experiments validate tightness on toy problems, show agreement of forward AIS behavior between real and simulated datasets across several models, compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations in Stan and WebPPL, and demonstrate BREAD’s utility for debugging.

The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), AIS (Neal, 2001), and the use of reverse annealing to obtain stochastic upper bounds. It complements convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman; Cowles–Carlin) and sample-quality measures such as Stein discrepancies (Gorham–Mackey). The manuscript discusses concurrent related work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), clarifying that when initialized from the true posterior the estimators coincide in spirit, while emphasizing BREAD’s focus on bounding Jeffreys divergence and practical integration in PPLs.

Strengths
- Clear, principled bridge from BDMC to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior samples; the derivation is simple and compelling.
- Practical impact: integration into two widely used PPLs (WebPPL, Stan), with demonstrations on multiple models, including a useful case study (collapsed vs. uncollapsed factorization) and a real bug uncovering.
- Sensible and well-motivated protocol to approximate exact posterior samples for real-data assessment via fitted hyperparameters and simulated datasets; empirical evidence suggests behavior transfers.
- The bound is meaningful for multimodal, badly conditioned posteriors, without requiring convexity or strong smoothness assumptions.
- The paper is well written and situates itself appropriately in the literature, including concurrent work.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The key guarantee is in expectation over the randomness of the forward and reverse procedures; per-instance validity is not provided, and high variance is possible. Practical guidance for estimating uncertainty in the bound is limited.
- Requires an exact posterior sample to obtain an upper bound; the real-data protocol necessarily forfeits rigor and relies on empirical similarity between real and simulated data and on short MCMC runs to approximate posterior draws. The conditions under which this surrogate is reliable are not theoretically characterized.
- Applicability is restricted to AIS/SMC-like samplers and settings where one can define a temperature path and reversible kernels at intermediate distributions; it does not directly assess the steady-state quality of, e.g., plain HMC/NUTS without an annealing framework.
- Experimental scope is modest: small to medium-sized models/datasets; no systematic study of scaling with dimension, number of temperatures, or kernel choices; computational overhead and wall-clock comparisons are not deeply analyzed.
- Some implementation details are under-specified (e.g., temperature schedules, NUTS adaptation per temperature, number of reverse initializations), which are crucial in practice.

Quality
The theoretical derivations appear sound and follow straightforwardly from AIS viewed as IS on an extended space. The expectation bounds are correct and clearly stated. The empirical results support the claims qualitatively, including validation on toy cases where exact values can be computed. However, the lack of uncertainty quantification for the bound and limited analysis of variance make it hard for practitioners to know how many runs are needed for reliable assessment.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The presentation of the key inequalities (forward/reverse expectations and their connection to KL/Jeffreys divergence) is concise. It would help to explicitly state a theorem with assumptions (reversibility, ergodicity, existence of an exact posterior sample) and to provide pseudocode for the BREAD protocol on real data.

Originality
The main idea—a Jeffreys divergence bound from the BDMC gap—is a novel and neat extension, though closely related to concurrent “subjective divergence” work. The practical integration into PPLs and use for model representation choices and debugging is original and valuable.

Significance
This has the potential to provide a rigorous, interpretable yardstick for AIS/SMC-based inference and to influence practice in probabilistic programming. The scope is limited by the need for simulated data or approximate posterior samples, but within that scope the impact is meaningful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide confidence intervals or concentration results for the estimated gap (e.g., via median-of-means or bootstrapping across multiple chains), and practical guidance on the number of forward/reverse runs required.
- Analyze sensitivity to temperature schedule, number of intermediate distributions, and kernel choices; include ablations and scaling with dimensionality.
- Report computational overhead (time per bound, time-to-certain-bound) and compare to competing diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS) and Stein discrepancies where applicable.
- Clarify Stan implementation details: whether NUTS adaptation is re-run per temperature, how step sizes/mass matrices are handled across temperatures, and implications for reversibility.
- Explore SMC variants and particle numbers; discuss when SMC-based BREAD may be preferable to AIS.
- Where the real-data protocol is used, add quantitative checks of posterior proximity (e.g., comparing summary statistics or divergences between approximate reverse initializations and posterior under simulated data).

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, correct theoretical connection; practical tool that surfaces real issues and informs modeling choices; integration into widely used systems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees are only in expectation and become heuristic on real data; experimental scope and variance/overhead analysis are limited.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium
