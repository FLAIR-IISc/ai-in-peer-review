

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) techniques. The authors claim to extend the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015) to provide rigorous bounds on inference quality for MCMC-based algorithms like AIS and SMC. The paper presents theoretical derivations, validation on toy examples, and integration into WebPPL and Stan.

## Strengths

The paper has several notable strengths. The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is sound, showing how BDMC can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence. The authors provide a clear protocol for applying the method to real-world data by using fitted hyperparameters to generate representative simulated datasets. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical applicability, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL through their method is a compelling real-world validation of the technique's utility.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty, validation, and practical significance.

First, the novelty is limited. The core idea is a direct extension of the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015), which the authors themselves cite. While they refine the analysis to bound the Jeffreys divergence specifically, this is a natural extension rather than a fundamentally new approach. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work like Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also builds on BDMC for evaluating posterior inference. The authors mention this work in Section 4 but don't adequately compare their method's advantages.

Second, the validation is insufficient. The paper demonstrates the method works on toy distributions where the true Jeffreys divergence can be computed exactly (Appendix B), but this doesn't demonstrate practical utility for real-world problems. The validation on real-world data (Section 5.1) is limited to five models from the Stan manual, and the paper doesn't compare against established convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin, Geweke, or visual diagnostics. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether BREAD provides meaningful advantages over existing methods.

Third, the practical limitations of the method are underemphasized. BREAD only provides rigorous bounds when exact posterior samples are available, which is only possible with simulated data. For real-world data, the authors propose a heuristic protocol using fitted hyperparameters, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this protocol reliably reflects true inference quality on real data. The validation in Section 5.1.1 shows that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data, but this is a weak validation of the method's practical utility.

Fourth, the paper overstates the generality of the method. BREAD is limited to inference algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), which excludes the vast majority of MCMC methods used in practice. The claim that it "provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure" is misleading given these constraints.

## Significance and Originality

The paper's significance is questionable. While the method has theoretical interest, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that it solves a critical problem in MCMC inference. Existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) are widely used, and the paper doesn't show that BREAD provides meaningful improvements over these methods in practice. The paper's focus on probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL, Stan) is narrow, as most practitioners don't use these specific tools.

The originality is marginal. The core technique is a straightforward extension of existing BDMC work, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which appears to have similar goals.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing work, it fails to provide sufficient evidence of practical utility or meaningful advantages over established methods. The validation is limited to toy examples and a narrow set of models, and the paper doesn't adequately address the method's constraints or compare it to existing diagnostics. The claims of "rigorous bounds" and "general" applicability are overstated given the method's limitations.

The paper would be strengthened by more extensive comparisons with established convergence diagnostics, validation on larger, more diverse datasets, and clearer articulation of the method's practical advantages over existing approaches. Without these, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

Given the skepticism toward unproven approaches and the high bar for validation against established baselines, I recommend rejection. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized venue focused on probabilistic programming or theoretical aspects of MCMC rather than NIPS, which emphasizes significant advances in machine learning methodology with broad applicability.
