This paper proposes BREAD, a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between an approximate posterior distribution (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior, leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo. The core idea is sound – utilizing forward and reverse AIS chains to sandwich the log marginal likelihood, and relating this to a bound on the divergence. The authors extend existing BDMC work and apply it to probabilistic programming systems, WebPPL and Stan, offering a practical tool for assessing inference quality. 

However, despite the novelty and potential importance, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor – yet cumulatively significant – flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental validation. While the core concept is interesting, the execution lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to directly bound the Jeffreys divergence is a novel contribution, extending the original BDMC framework.
*   **Practicality:**  Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan makes it immediately useful for practitioners of probabilistic programming.
*   **Potential Impact:**  A reliable method for assessing inference quality is highly valuable, especially for users of "black-box" probabilistic programming systems.
*   **Diagnostic Capability:** The identification of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates the potential of BREAD as a diagnostic tool.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Presentation:** The mathematical derivations often lack sufficient explanation. Equations are presented without clear motivation or context, forcing the reader to reconstruct the logic. For example, the jump from Eq. (6) to (8) relies on a KL divergence property that is not explicitly stated or referenced.
*   **Overly Strong Claims:** The authors repeatedly claim "rigorous" bounds without fully acknowledging the assumptions inherent in the method, particularly the requirement for exact posterior samples for the reverse chain (even in the simulated data setting) and the potential impact of approximating the posterior for hyperparameter estimation.
*   **Insufficient Validation:** While the authors attempt to validate their method, the experiments are not comprehensive enough. The choice of only five models for validation is limiting. Furthermore, the reliance on visual inspection of curves in Fig. 1 is insufficient; quantitative comparisons and statistical tests are needed.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** There are small but pervasive inconsistencies in notation and terminology. For example, the definition of `v` in Section 3.1 is unclear and seems unnecessarily complex. The meaning of "forward" and "reverse" AIS becomes confused at times.
*   **Formatting and Style Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting errors and stylistic inconsistencies (e.g., uneven spacing, inconsistent citation style). Though minor individually, they detract from the overall professionalism of the work.
*   **Unclear Comparison to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (CTM16):** The discussion of CTM16 feels dismissive. While the authors correctly point out differences in initialization, they downplay the conceptual similarities and potential implications of using a reference distribution other than the true posterior.
*    **Missing Appendix Details:** Several references to "Appendix B" and "Appendix D" are made, but the details provided are inadequate to fully assess the claims. For instance, the description of the toy distributions used in Appendix B is insufficient to evaluate the tightness of the bound.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem – assessing the reliability of MCMC inference.
*   The core idea of BREAD is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The integration with WebPPL and Stan provides a practical tool for practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks the technical rigor and polish expected of a NIPS publication.
*   The validation experiments are not comprehensive enough to justify the strong claims made by the authors.
*   The numerous minor flaws in presentation and style detract from the overall quality of the work.
*   The mathematical derivations are often unclear and lack sufficient motivation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a promising idea, the numerous shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and validation prevent it from meeting the standards of a NIPS publication. The authors need to significantly revise the paper, addressing the concerns outlined above, to demonstrate the robustness and reliability of their method. Specifically, more rigorous mathematical derivations, more comprehensive experimental validation, and a more thorough comparison to related work are required. The paper feels premature in its current form, resembling a proof-of-concept rather than a polished scientific contribution.
