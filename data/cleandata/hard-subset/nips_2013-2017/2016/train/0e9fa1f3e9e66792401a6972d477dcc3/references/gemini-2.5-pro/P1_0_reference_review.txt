### Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC also serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior.

A key challenge is that this method, in its rigorous form, requires an exact sample from the true posterior, limiting its direct application to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world datasets: first, estimate hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated data to assess inference quality. The authors validate this protocol by showing that the sampler's behavior is consistent between the real and simulated data for several models.

The method is implemented and tested in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The authors use BREAD to provide practical guidance on model representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers) for a matrix factorization model and demonstrate its utility as a debugging tool for probabilistic programming systems. The work is well-situated with respect to prior work on MCMC diagnostics, sample quality assessment, and the original BDMC paper [GGA15]. The authors also clearly and fairly discuss concurrent, related work [CTM16].

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Contribution:** The paper addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in probabilistic inference: how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. While many heuristic diagnostics exist (e.g., Gelman-Rubin), BREAD provides a more rigorous, quantitative measure (the Jeffreys divergence) that is grounded in theory. The connection between the BDMC log-likelihood gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a simple but powerful insight.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Thorough Validation:** The derivation in Section 3.1, extending the analysis of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, is clear and appears correct. The experimental validation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors don't just present the method but proactively address its potential weaknesses.
    *   They validate the tightness of the bound on toy problems (Appendix B).
    *   They provide strong empirical evidence (Fig. 1) that sampler behavior on simulated data (using their proposed protocol) is a good proxy for its behavior on real data.
    *   They test the sensitivity of the method to the approximate posterior sample used to initialize the reverse chain, showing it to be robust (Section 5.1.2).

3.  **Practical Relevance and Impact:** This work has the potential for significant impact on both practitioners and developers of probabilistic inference systems.
    *   For users of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs), BREAD provides a much-needed tool for gaining confidence in inference results, moving beyond simple heuristic checks.
    *   The case study comparing model representations for matrix factorization in Stan and WebPPL is an excellent demonstration of how BREAD can guide modeling decisions.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights an important secondary use case for BREAD as a powerful tool for testing and debugging inference implementations.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background material on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, making the paper accessible. The core ideas are motivated well, and the logical flow from theory to the real-world data protocol and then to experiments is easy to follow. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Applicability of the Core Method:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is that BREAD is only directly applicable to inference algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). It cannot be directly applied to standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling. While the authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS are transferable, this is an indirect connection. The title "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference" might be slightly too broad, as it primarily measures AIS/SMC-based inference.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is a clever and pragmatic workaround, but it remains a heuristic. Its validity rests on the assumption that the inference algorithm's performance on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters accurately reflects its performance on the actual data. The paper provides good evidence for this in five models, but it may not hold universally, especially in cases of severe model misspecification where the posterior over hyperparameters might be very different from the generating parameters.

3.  **Bound is on the Expectation:** The result is that the *expected* gap E[B̂] bounds the Jeffreys divergence J. A single computed gap B̂ is not a guaranteed upper bound. While averaging multiple B̂ estimates would give a good estimate of E[B̂], this subtlety is important. The paper is careful with its language, but a practitioner might misinterpret a single B̂ value.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, theoretically-grounded method for addressing the crucial problem of MCMC reliability.
*   **Pro:** The proposed method, BREAD, is shown to be practically useful for evaluating, comparing, and debugging inference systems, with implementations in two major PPLs.
*   **Pro:** The experimental validation is thorough, convincing, and directly addresses the main limitations of the approach.
*   **Pro:** The paper is extremely well-written and clear, making a strong contribution to the field.
*   **Con:** The method's direct applicability is limited to a subset of inference algorithms (AIS/SMC).
*   **Con:** The extension to real-world data relies on a reasonable but un-guaranteed heuristic.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution. The problem it addresses is of high importance to the NIPS community, and the proposed solution is elegant, well-validated, and practically useful. The weaknesses are clearly acknowledged and are more limitations on scope than flaws in the work itself. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the core idea and its practical impact on the growing field of probabilistic programming, far outweigh these limitations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
