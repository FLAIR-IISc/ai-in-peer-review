This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantifying the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015), which was originally developed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key insight is that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, produced by forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS), provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution produced by AIS and the true posterior. This provides a quantitative, rigorous measure of sample quality.

Since the method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse AIS chain, its theoretical guarantees are limited to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, run inference on the real data to get point estimates for hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset to diagnose inference quality. The paper validates this protocol and demonstrates the utility of BREAD by integrating it into two popular probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL. Using these implementations, the authors show how BREAD can be used to compare different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even to discover an implementation bug in WebPPL. The work is closely related to the vast literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics and to concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also builds on BDMC.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the fundamental and challenging problem of assessing the quality of approximate posterior inference. While many MCMC convergence diagnostics exist (e.g., Gelman-Rubin), they are often heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative measure—a bound on the Jeffreys divergence—which is a significant step forward. The core theoretical contribution, re-interpreting the BDMC gap as a bound on a statistical divergence (Eqs. 8, 11, 12), is a simple but powerful and novel idea.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical derivation is sound and clearly explained. The paper provides a strong empirical validation of the proposed method. The experiments are well-designed and convincing:
    *   The protocol for applying BREAD to real data is tested by showing that inference behavior (the shape of the AIS estimate curves) is consistent between real data and data simulated from fitted hyperparameters (Fig. 1).
    *   The use of an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain is validated by showing the resulting bounds are insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to generate that sample.
    *   The "scientific findings" section is a compelling demonstration of the method's practical utility. Using BREAD to provide a quantitative comparison between model representations and to find a bug in a PPL powerfully illustrates its value as a tool for both researchers and developers.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear background on AIS and BDMC before presenting their own contribution. The logic is laid out clearly, and the figures are informative. The paper provides enough detail to understand the method and appreciate its contributions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Applicability to Real-World Data:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD apply only to simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a sensible heuristic, and the validation experiments are encouraging. However, it relies on the assumption that inference performance on data simulated from a posterior mean of the hyperparameters is representative of performance on the actual data. This may not hold if the posterior over hyperparameters is broad or multimodal, or if the model is misspecified (as the authors briefly note in the appendix). It would strengthen the paper to discuss these failure modes of the protocol more explicitly in the main text.

2.  **Scope of Applicable Algorithms:** The method is directly applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, primarily AIS and SMC. The authors argue that insights gained from evaluating these algorithms can inform the design of MCMC more broadly. While this is true to some extent (e.g., choice of transition operators), it is not a general-purpose diagnostic for any MCMC sampler (e.g., a standard Gibbs sampler). This limitation on scope should be clearly stated.

3.  **Properties of the Bound:** The derived bound is on the *expectation* of the gap `E[B]`. In practice, one computes an empirical estimate of this gap from a finite number of runs. It would be useful to include some discussion of the variance of the estimator `B`. If the variance is very high, a large number of runs might be needed to get a reliable estimate of the bound, potentially making the method computationally expensive. Furthermore, footnote 3 mentions that the analysis does not justify averaging the importance weights from multiple chains, which is standard practice for reducing the variance of AIS log-ML estimates. This seems like a significant practical limitation. Could the analysis be extended to handle the standard practice of averaging `K` chains (e.g., via a log-sum-exp of weights)? This would likely yield a much more stable estimator and increase the practical utility of the method.

4.  **Tightness of the Bound:** The inequality in Eq. (8) and (11) arises from marginalizing out the intermediate path variables `v`. The tightness of the final bound on the Jeffreys divergence depends on how much information is lost in this marginalization. While Appendix B shows the bound is tight on some toy examples, a discussion of the factors that might affect this tightness (e.g., number of annealing steps, mixing of the transition kernels) would be valuable. A loose bound might be of limited use for diagnosing subtle convergence issues.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in probabilistic machine learning.
    *   Provides a novel, theoretically-grounded, and quantitative measure of MCMC sample quality.
    *   Demonstrates clear practical utility for evaluating, comparing, and debugging inference algorithms in widely-used PPLs.
    *   The paper is technically sound, well-validated, and clearly written.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The method's rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data, and the real-data protocol is a heuristic.
    *   The scope is limited to AIS/SMC-style algorithms.
    *   Practical issues around the variance of the bound estimator and the inability to average samples are not fully addressed.

**Decision**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method, BREAD, is an elegant and practical tool for assessing the quality of approximate inference, a problem of great importance to the NeurIPS community. The strengths—novelty, technical quality, and demonstrated utility—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely inherent limitations of the approach and are handled transparently by the authors. This work is likely to be highly influential for developers of PPLs and researchers developing new inference methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
