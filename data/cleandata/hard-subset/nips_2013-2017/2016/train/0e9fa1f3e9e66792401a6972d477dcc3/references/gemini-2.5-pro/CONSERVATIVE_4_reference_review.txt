**Summary:**

This paper proposes a new method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for quantitatively assessing the quality of samples from certain MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors' primary theoretical contribution is to show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation for the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. This technique is applicable to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC).

Recognizing that the method's rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample (and are thus limited to simulated data), the authors propose a protocol for application to real-world data. This involves fitting hyperparameters on the real data, simulating a new dataset from the model using these parameters, and then applying BREAD to this simulated dataset. The paper validates this protocol on several models and datasets, and demonstrates its utility by comparing different model representations in two probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The difficulty of diagnosing MCMC convergence and assessing sample quality is a long-standing and critical problem in computational statistics. Existing methods are largely heuristic and can fail to detect certain modes of non-convergence. The goal of providing a quantitative measure of divergence from the true posterior is highly valuable.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation showing that the expected gap in BDMC bounds the Jeffreys divergence is a clear and elegant result. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method in the setting where its assumptions are met (i.e., on simulated data with an exact posterior sample).
3.  **Thoughtful Validation:** The authors are careful to validate key aspects of their proposed workflow. The experiments in Section 5.1, which show consistency between inference behavior on real data and simulated data (generated via their protocol), are a crucial step and lend credibility to their proposed heuristic for real-world applications. The investigation into the sensitivity of the reverse chain's initialization is also well-executed.
4.  **Demonstrated Utility:** The application of BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is a compelling use case. It provides concrete, quantitative guidance on a common modeling choice that can significantly impact performance. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL further underscores the potential of this method as a rigorous tool for developers of inference systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The primary weakness of the paper is the significant gap between the rigorous theoretical guarantees and the practical application of the method. The "bound" in BREAD is only a true bound in expectation on simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a multi-step heuristic that relinquishes this guarantee. While the authors are transparent about this, it significantly tempers the paper's claims of providing a "rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference" on real data. What is presented is a sophisticated diagnostic, not a rigorous bound, for real-world problems.
2.  **Limited Applicability:** The method is directly applicable only to a specific class of algorithms (AIS, SMC). The authors suggest that insights gained from evaluating AIS can "inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly," but this assertion is not substantiated. It is unclear how an analysis using BREAD on an AIS sampler would provide concrete guidance for tuning a standard Hamiltonian Monte Carlo or Metropolis-Hastings sampler for the same model.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper fails to compare BREAD against any standard MCMC convergence diagnostics. For a practitioner, the key question is whether this complex new machinery offers advantages over established, simpler methods like the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic (R-hat) or effective sample size (ESS). For the model comparison experiment in Section 5.2.1, would standard diagnostics have led to the same conclusions? Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical significance and added value of the proposed method over the tools already widely used in the community.
4.  **Dependence on Model Specification:** The real-data protocol relies on simulating data from the model. This implicitly assumes the model is well-specified. As the authors note in the appendix, model misspecification can cause the inference behavior on simulated data to diverge from that on real data, invalidating the protocol. This is a major practical limitation for a method intended for general use.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a novel theoretical idea and some interesting experiments, its practical significance is undermined by the limitations of the proposed real-world data protocol and the absence of comparisons to established diagnostic methods. The core contribution—a rigorous bound on divergence—is unfortunately confined to a setting (simulated data) that is of secondary interest to most practitioners. The heuristic for real data is plausible but lacks the rigor that is the paper's main selling point. To be a convincing contribution for a top-tier venue like NIPS, the authors would need to provide stronger evidence of BREAD's practical advantages over existing, well-understood diagnostics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel theoretical connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence.
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in applied Bayesian inference.
*   Careful experimental design and validation of the proposed workflow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The rigorous guarantees do not apply to the proposed real-world data protocol, which is a heuristic.
*   The method has limited applicability to only a subset of MCMC algorithms (AIS/SMC).
*   Crucially, there is no comparison to standard, widely-used MCMC diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS), making it impossible to judge the method's practical added value.

Given my preference for methods that are rigorously validated against established practice, the lack of baseline comparisons is a significant flaw. The paper presents an interesting idea, but does not sufficiently demonstrate that it represents a clear advance over the state of the art in practical MCMC diagnostics. Therefore, I lean towards rejection.
