

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method [GGA15] to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The method is applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). 

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) showing that BDMC yields an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) presenting a technique to compute both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples; (3) proposing a protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters; and (4) integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages. The authors demonstrate that BREAD can guide modeling and algorithmic decisions, as shown in their analysis of matrix factorization models.

This work relates to and extends the BDMC method from [GGA15], which was previously used to estimate log marginal likelihoods. The authors also discuss connections to related work including [CTM16], which also builds on BDMC for evaluating posterior inference, but with different theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Theoretical Contributions**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical derivation showing that BDMC can upper bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The analysis in Section 3.1 is careful and well-justified, building on the BDMC framework while making a novel contribution to bounding the Jeffreys divergence rather than just the log marginal likelihood.

2. **Practical Integration**: The authors successfully integrate BREAD into two widely used probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan), demonstrating its practical utility. This integration makes the method immediately applicable to real-world inference problems.

3. **Validation Protocol**: The proposed protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data (Section 3.2) is thoughtful and addresses a significant practical challenge. The validation experiments in Section 5.1.1-5.1.2 convincingly show that the simulated data approach can effectively proxy for real data.

4. **Scientific Utility**: The paper demonstrates the practical value of BREAD through its application to model representation choices (Section 5.2.1), revealing important differences in how WebPPL and Stan respond to different model representations. The debugging example (Section 5.2.2) further demonstrates BREAD's utility for identifying implementation errors.

5. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical validation on both toy examples and real-world models, providing a well-rounded evaluation of the method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Applicability**: While the authors argue that their method provides insights for broader MCMC inference, the method is only directly applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC. It's not clear how this limitation affects the general applicability of their insights to traditional MCMC methods like Metropolis-Hastings or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the BREAD method. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains for each model could be prohibitively expensive for large-scale applications, which might limit its practical adoption.

3. **Real-World Data Validation**: While the validation in Section 5.1.1 shows promising results, it's limited to only five models. The paper doesn't explore how the method performs on more complex or higher-dimensional real-world datasets that might challenge the assumptions of the protocol.

4. **Comparison with Alternative Methods**: The paper mentions related work [CTM16] but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other methods for measuring MCMC reliability (e.g., convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin). It would be valuable to understand how BREAD compares to these established methods in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and ease of use.

5. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper states that "BREAD can rigorously demonstrate convergence in the simulated data setting," but it doesn't fully address the limitations of this approach. The method requires exact posterior samples, which are only available for simulated data, limiting its direct applicability to real-world inference without the proposed protocol.

## Specific Comments

**Introduction (Section 1):** The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivates the need for rigorous reliability measures in MCMC. The connection to probabilistic programming languages is well-motivated and relevant to current trends in machine learning.

**Background (Section 2):** The background section is thorough and well-written, providing necessary context for the method. The explanation of reverse AIS and BDMC is clear and accessible.

**Methods (Section 3):** The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is the strongest part of the paper. The key insight that the gap between forward and reverse bounds upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is well-motivated and rigorously derived. However, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly discussing the computational cost of obtaining the reverse chain, especially for complex models.

**Experiments (Section 5):** The experiments in Section 5.1 are well-designed and address important concerns about the method's applicability to real-world data. The comparison between WebPPL and Stan in Section 5.2.1 is particularly valuable for practitioners. However, the paper would benefit from including more detailed analysis of the computational resources required for BREAD compared to alternative methods.

**Related Work (Section 4):** The related work section is good but could be strengthened by more detailed comparison with other methods for measuring MCMC reliability. For example, the paper could discuss how BREAD compares to methods like the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic or the effective sample size metric.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and rigorous approach to measuring the reliability of MCMC inference, with significant potential to improve the practice of probabilistic modeling. The theoretical contributions are sound, the method is well-integrated into existing probabilistic programming frameworks, and the experimental validation is thorough. While there are limitations in the scope of applicability and computational cost, these are common challenges for rigorous methods in statistical inference.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, and the proposed BREAD method addresses an important gap in the literature.

## Final Evaluation

**Quality**: 8/10 - The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theory and experiments, though it could better address computational cost and limitations.

**Clarity**: 9/10 - The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and practical implementation.

**Originality**: 8/10 - The extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence is novel, though the core BDMC method is from prior work.

**Significance**: 8/10 - The method has significant potential impact for probabilistic programming and MCMC practitioners, though its direct applicability is limited to certain inference algorithms.

**Overall**: The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While not all limitations are fully addressed, the method is sound, well-motivated, and has clear practical utility. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
