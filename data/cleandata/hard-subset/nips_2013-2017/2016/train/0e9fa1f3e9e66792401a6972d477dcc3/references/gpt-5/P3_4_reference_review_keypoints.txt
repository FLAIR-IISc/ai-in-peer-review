- Paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC-based MCMC via gap B̂ = log R̂rev−1 − log R̂fwd.
- Proposes a pragmatic real-data protocol: fit hyperparameters, simulate data, run BDMC on simulated data, and compare forward-AIS behavior between real and simulated data.
- Main theoretical result: E[B̂] ≥ DJ(qfwd, pT), leveraging AIS’s extended-state importance sampling and KL contraction.
- Practical relevance: provides a quantitative, interpretable sample-quality diagnostic for probabilistic programming systems with actionable uses (representation choices, debugging).
- Solid positioning against related work, connecting to MCMC diagnostics, Stein discrepancies, thermodynamic integration, and “subjective divergence.”
- Implemented in WebPPL and Stan and evaluated on five models, including a real bug found in WebPPL.
- Honest discussion of limitations (need for exact posterior samples for simulated data, stochastic bounds, simulated-data setting) and attempt to bridge to real data.
- Key weakness: bound holds in expectation only (E[B̂] ≥ DJ), not almost surely, risking misinterpretation from single runs.
- Recommends emphasizing estimation of E[B̂] with uncertainty and proposing concentration/aggregation schemes (e.g., median-of-means) for high-probability bounds.
- Real-data protocol is heuristic with no formal guarantees; fitted-hyperparameter reverse initialization may fail under misspecification or weak identification.
- Experimental scope limited to relatively benign models; lacks stress tests on highly multimodal, heavy-tailed, or high-dimensional hierarchical targets.
- Limited empirical comparison to standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS) and Stein discrepancies, missing demonstration of complementarity or superiority.
- AIS/SMC specifics matter: dependence on annealing schedule, intermediate kernels, and adaptation needs clearer discussion and reporting.
- Presentation issues: incomplete references, typos, and notational clarifications needed; figures should distinguish averaging over runs vs. per-chain behavior.
- Theory judged correct and derivation clean; tightness checks on toy models are useful but limited.
- Empirics carefully executed within scope; runtime reporting and uncertainty quantification for E[B̂] are lacking.
- Clarity is generally good with clear delineation of contributions; recommend an explicit algorithm box and caveat discussion for the real-data protocol.
- Originality: novel use of BDMC bounds to upper bound Jeffreys divergence; distinction from “subjective divergence” is clear.
- Significance: high practical impact for PPLs and MCMC diagnostics despite reliance on simulated data.
- Suggested improvements: run multiple independent bidirectional chains, report means/confidence intervals, and explore tail bounds or bootstrap for E[B̂].
- Suggested experiments: harder targets (mixture models with label switching, Ising/Potts, funnel distributions) to validate detection of mixing failures.
- Compare DJ estimates to R̂, ESS, and Stein discrepancy on the same runs and analyze correlations and disagreements.
- Provide more implementation details: annealing schedules, number of temperatures, kernel adaptation per temperature, and wall-clock/performance tradeoffs.
- Explore and compare SMC instantiations given applicability claims.
- Clarify real-data heuristic limitations and propose diagnostics to detect unreliability (posterior predictive checks, sensitivity analyses).
- Pros: elegant, correct theory; implemented in two PPLs; finds real bugs and provides useful diagnostic.
- Cons: expectation-only bound may mislead without aggregation; real-data protocol lacks guarantees; limited stress testing and presentation issues.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — principled and useful diagnostic with solid theory and promising practice, but needs stronger empirical validation and clearer interpretation of the expectation-bound.
