Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), originally designed to sandwich the log marginal likelihood on simulated data, to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key theoretical contribution is showing that, for AIS/SMC-style samplers viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space, the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log-weight estimates upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the sampler’s approximate distribution over final states and the true posterior. The authors term the overall approach BREAD. They also propose a practical protocol for real-world data: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data at those hyperparameters to obtain exact posterior samples (for simulated data), and compare behavior of forward/reverse chains to diagnose inference quality. They implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and provide experiments: exact calculations on toy problems to assess bound tightness; empirical consistency between simulated and real datasets across five models; analysis of collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization in WebPPL and Stan; and a debugging case where BREAD revealed a bug.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001), interpreting the forward/reverse AIS sandwich in terms of sample quality via KL/Jeffreys divergence. It is contemporaneous with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka’s subjective divergence, which coincides with BDMC when the reverse chain is initialized from an exact posterior; the present paper emphasizes the Jeffreys-divergence bound and provides a protocol and implementations in PPLs. It situates itself relative to convergence diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin; Brooks-Gelman) and to Stein discrepancies (Gorham-Mackey).

Strengths
- Novel conceptual step: repurposes BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to a principled, divergence-based measure of sample quality for AIS/SMC, with a clean Jeffreys-divergence interpretation.
- Practical impact: concrete, automated procedure integrated into two PPLs, with evidence that it can guide model representation choices and catch implementation bugs.
- Theory with actionable implications: the expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is simple to compute from forward/reverse AIS runs and tight on toy problems.
- Sensible protocol for real data: fitted-hyperparameter simulation with empirical validation that reverse-AIS estimates are insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the posterior over hyperparameters.
- Breadth of experiments: five diverse models, two systems (Stan/WebPPL), and a substantive case study (matrix factorization) demonstrating how BREAD informs modeling decisions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees in expectation only: ˆB upper-bounds J in expectation, but per-run bounds can be below J. The experiments mostly show distributions of forward/reverse AIS estimates rather than explicit estimates of E[ˆB] with uncertainty; more emphasis on estimating E[ˆB] (with CIs) would strengthen claims.
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers and settings where exact posterior samples are available (simulated data). The real-data protocol uses approximate posterior samples for reverse AIS, breaking rigor; while empirically stable in the reported cases, there is no theoretical guarantee.
- Limited tightness characterization: toy examples suggest good match, but broader empirical assessment of tightness across model classes (e.g., multimodal, heavy-tailed, hierarchical) is limited.
- Comparison to contemporaneous subjective divergence is mostly qualitative. A direct empirical comparison (same models, same budgets) would clarify trade-offs when using non-posterior reference distributions.
- Diagnostic scope: the method is less informative for arbitrary MCMC (e.g., pure HMC/NUTS without AIS tempering), which may limit adoption unless AIS/SMC is already used or can be retrofitted.
- Some practical details are missing or could be clearer: how temperature schedules and kernel choices affect the bound; whether NUTS/HMC transitions at intermediate temperatures are strictly reversible/invariant in their implementations; computational overhead (wall-clock) of reverse chains; and recommendations for the number of runs to estimate E[ˆB] robustly.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with clear derivations and correct use of AIS/BDMC machinery. The real-data protocol is heuristic but responsibly evaluated. I would encourage more rigorous estimation and reporting of E[ˆB] and sensitivity analyses (schedules, kernels, number of AIS steps).

Clarity
Well written and organized. The connection from Eqs. (6–11) to the Jeffreys bound is clearly laid out. Suggestions:
- Provide pseudocode for BREAD on simulated and real data, including defaults for schedule and number of reverse chains.
- Report E[ˆB] with confidence intervals across runs, and give guidance on run counts needed for stable estimates.
- Clarify invariance/reversibility assumptions for the transition kernels used in Stan/WebPPL at intermediate distributions.

Originality
High. While based on BDMC, the reframing to bound Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples and the practical PPL integration constitute a meaningful and novel advance. This is a bold, direction-setting idea for diagnosing inference quality beyond traditional MCMC diagnostics.

Significance
High for the probabilistic programming and approximate inference communities. If adopted, BREAD could become a standard tool to assess and debug samplers and guide modeling choices, especially in PPLs. Even with limitations, the conceptual advance and demonstrated utility justify publication.

Questions for authors
- Can you report E[ˆB] (with uncertainty) versus AIS steps across your experiments, and show how it correlates with downstream metrics (ESS, R-hat)?
- How sensitive is ˆB to the temperature schedule and kernel choices? Any automatic tuning guidance?
- For Stan/NUTS, confirm invariance/reversibility at intermediate temperatures and discuss any adaptations disabled during AIS.
- Could you provide any concentration results or high-probability bounds for ˆB under mild assumptions?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel repurposing of BDMC to quantify sample quality via Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical, implemented, and demonstrably useful in real systems.
- Clear potential impact on PPL reliability and algorithm design.
- Strong, diverse experimental evidence and a compelling case study.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees only in expectation; limited tightness characterization.
- Heuristic real-data protocol without formal guarantees.
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-style methods.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant idea with clear practical value and sufficient validation, and it opens a promising direction for principled inference diagnostics in probabilistic programming.
