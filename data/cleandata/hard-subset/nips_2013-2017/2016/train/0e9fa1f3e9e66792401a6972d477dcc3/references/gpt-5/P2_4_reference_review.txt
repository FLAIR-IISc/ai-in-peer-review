Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-style posterior inference by upper bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The main mechanism uses forward and reverse AIS to form stochastic lower and upper bounds on log marginal likelihood; the expected gap between these bounds equals or exceeds the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) (the AIS terminal-state distribution) and pT. The authors:
- derive the bound and show how one-sided KLs can be bounded given any additional upper/lower bound on logR,
- verify tightness on toy distributions where true Jeffreys divergence is computable,
- propose a practical protocol to diagnose inference on real data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, and initializing reverse AIS from an approximate posterior sample,
- integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate use on several models and a matrix factorization case study, including uncovering a bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on BDMC and AIS, and is closely related to contemporaneous “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which also uses BDMC-like constructions to upper bound Jeffreys divergence when initialized from the true posterior and relaxes to a reference distribution otherwise. The present paper emphasizes the expectation bound from the AIS gap, proposes a concrete real-data protocol via fitted hyperparameters, and offers system integrations in Stan and WebPPL. The positioning vis-à-vis CTM16 is acknowledged; the paper would benefit from a sharper delineation of theoretical and empirical differences.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations leveraging the AIS extended-state formulation, Jensen, and KL monotonicity are sound. The result that E[log Rhat^{-1}_rev − log Rhat] ≥ DJ(pT,qfwd) is correct and useful. It is important, however, that this is an expectation bound, not a high-probability bound; the paper is clear on this point but could stress implications for practical estimation (variance, required number of chains).
- Empirics: The toy problems with exact Jeffreys computations support tightness. The real-data protocol is evaluated on five models in Stan; the observed alignment of forward AIS curves between real and simulated data is compelling heuristic evidence. The hyperparameter-initialization approximation (varying S MCMC steps) shows stability in reverse AIS, which is reassuring. The matrix factorization case study is informative and the debugging example is a strong practical endorsement.
- Limitations: For real datasets, guarantees are lost; the method becomes diagnostic and relies on similarity between real and simulated data. The bound is only in expectation; there are no tail guarantees for the gap, and heavy-tailed AIS weights complicate estimation. The paper reports quartiles rather than means and discourages averaging, but then uses multiple chains; a more systematic treatment (e.g., median-of-means or concentration results for the gap) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The exposition of AIS/BDMC and the new derivation is generally clear and well motivated. The paper is well organized, with methods before applications, and appendices provide details. There are formatting/citation glitches and minor typos that should be cleaned up. It would help to explicitly state the assumptions under which the expectation bounds hold, and to summarize the computational protocol to estimate E[gap] with confidence intervals.

Originality
Moderate. The key conceptual step—relating the forward/reverse AIS bound gap to Jeffreys divergence—is closely tied to BDMC and overlaps with CTM16. The contributions are:
- a refined derivation and emphasis on expected Jeffreys bounds,
- a practical real-data protocol using fitted hyperparameters,
- system integrations and case studies demonstrating utility.
These are valuable, albeit incremental theoretically; the practical angle is novel and useful.

Significance
High practical significance for probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics. A quantitative, model-agnostic measure of sample quality that can be automated in Stan/WebPPL can influence practice, help choose model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed), tune samplers, and detect implementation bugs. The theoretical guarantee is limited to simulated data; nevertheless, the proposed protocol offers a reasonable bridge to real applications.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical connection between AIS gap and Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical, system-level integration in two major PPLs.
- Thoughtful real-data protocol with empirical validation.
- Useful case studies and real debugging impact.
- Careful discussion of limitations and related work.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Expectation-only bound; no finite-sample or high-probability control for the gap. Estimation may be noisy, especially with heavy-tailed weights.
- Real-data protocol lacks guarantees; depends on similarity between simulated and real data and on approximate initialization quality.
- Overlap with CTM16; differences could be articulated more sharply, including empirical comparisons on identical tasks.
- Limited scale and diversity of experiments; more challenging, high-dimensional or multimodal posteriors would further validate robustness.
- Citation and formatting issues.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide variance estimates or confidence intervals for E[gap], and recommend a practical number of AIS chains? Consider robust estimators (median-of-means) for heavy-tailed settings.
- Can you leverage existing multi-sample tail bounds (e.g., Gogate et al., 2007) to get a concentration statement for the gap?
- How sensitive are results to the temperature schedule and MCMC kernels? A sensitivity study would be valuable.
- Could you compare against Stein discrepancy metrics on the same problems to triangulate sample quality?
- Please clarify distinctions from CTM16 (both theory and practice) and, if possible, include a brief empirical comparison.
- Clean up references and minor typos.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-motivated paper with a correct derivation, meaningful system integrations, and convincing practical value, despite theoretical limitations on real data and expectation-only guarantees. It meets NeurIPS criteria on quality, clarity, and significance; originality is moderate given related concurrent work, but the practical contributions are strong. I lean toward acceptance after clarifying the relation to CTM16 and strengthening the empirical estimation protocol for the expectation bound.
