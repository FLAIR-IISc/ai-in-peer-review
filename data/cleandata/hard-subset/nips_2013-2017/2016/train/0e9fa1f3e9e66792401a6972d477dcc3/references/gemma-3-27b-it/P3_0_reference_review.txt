## NIPS 2016 Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally designed for estimating log marginal likelihoods, to provide a rigorous upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior.  The core idea is leveraging the reverse AIS chain, initialized with a true posterior sample (available in simulated data settings), to bound the divergence.  The authors propose “Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing” (BREAD), validate its accuracy on toy examples, develop a protocol for applying it to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation, and implement it within the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming frameworks.  A significant contribution is recognizing and addressing the challenge of applying BREAD to real data by proposing a workflow for simulating data informed by initial inference on the real dataset.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the broader landscape of MCMC diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998), log-likelihood estimation (Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015), and recent advancements in using BDMC for inference assessment (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).  The authors accurately highlight the difference between their approach – aiming for a *bound* on divergence – and methods focused on convergence diagnostics that can only *detect* potential issues. The connection to tempered transitions and non-equilibrium statistical mechanics (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997) is also appropriately noted.  The acknowledgement of related work (CTM16) is particularly commendable.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Approach:** BREAD provides a theoretically sound, quantifiable measure of posterior accuracy.  Unlike many heuristics, it offers an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, providing a level of confidence unattainable with traditional diagnostics.
* **Practical Applicability:** Extending BREAD to real-world data via the hyperparameter estimation and simulation protocol is a clever and crucial contribution.  This addresses a significant practical limitation of methods relying on exact posterior samples.
* **Implementation in Probabilistic Programming Languages:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan greatly increases its accessibility to a wide range of researchers and practitioners.
* **Demonstrated Utility:** The paper convincingly demonstrates how BREAD can be used to inform modeling decisions (matrix factorization representation) and even identify bugs in software implementations.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The technical details are presented clearly, and the motivation for each step is well explained.
* **Thorough Validation:** The authors perform a series of experiments to validate their approach, addressing potential issues such as the accuracy of the bound and the representativeness of simulated data.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While theoretically powerful, running BREAD appears computationally demanding, requiring both forward and reverse AIS chains.  The paper doesn’t fully address the scaling of this method to very high-dimensional problems.
* **Limitations of the Protocol:** The hyperparameter estimation and simulation protocol, while clever, introduces its own potential biases. The success of the method hinges on the quality of the initial hyperparameter estimates.  A more thorough analysis of the robustness of this protocol to different prior specifications would be beneficial.
* **Dependence on Simulated Data (Even with Protocol):** Despite the protocol, the ultimate validation still relies on generating data from a model fit to the real data.  Model misspecification could lead to misleading results, as hinted at in Appendix D.
* **Jeffreys Divergence as a Metric:** While mathematically convenient, the Jeffreys divergence may not be the most intuitive measure of posterior accuracy for all applications. A discussion of alternative divergence measures and their suitability for different inference tasks would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison with Existing Diagnostics:** While BREAD is presented as a more rigorous approach than existing diagnostics, a more direct comparison (e.g., applying both BREAD and traditional diagnostics to the same models and datasets) would strengthen the paper’s claims.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for assessing the reliability of MCMC-based inference.
* The practical extension to real-world data through the hyperparameter estimation and simulation protocol is a significant contribution.
* The implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes the method readily accessible to a broad audience.
* The authors demonstrate the utility of BREAD with compelling examples.
* The paper is well-written and thoroughly validated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of BREAD could limit its applicability to complex models.
* The reliance on simulated data, even with the proposed protocol, introduces potential biases.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing diagnostic tools.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the computational cost and dependence on simulated data are valid concerns, the theoretical rigor, practical applicability, and demonstrated utility of BREAD outweigh these weaknesses.  The authors have addressed a critical challenge – evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference – with a novel and effective approach.  

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept**. 

I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and has the potential to significantly impact the way researchers and practitioners evaluate the accuracy of their probabilistic models. The ability to confidently assess inference quality is crucial for the widespread adoption of probabilistic programming and Bayesian methods.
