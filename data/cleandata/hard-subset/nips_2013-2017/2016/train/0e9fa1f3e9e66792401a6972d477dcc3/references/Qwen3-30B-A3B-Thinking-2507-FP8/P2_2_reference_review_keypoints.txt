- Introduces BREAD, a method that bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors using the gap from bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC)/AIS.  
- Extends BDMC (previously for marginal likelihoods) to measure MCMC inference quality rather than just estimate marginal likelihoods.  
- Theoretical contribution: proves the expected BDMC gap provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence with clear mathematical derivations.  
- Analyzes bias in stochastic bounds and extends prior work [GGA15] to the divergence-bounding setting.  
- Validated on toy distributions where true divergence and bounds can be computed exactly, showing reasonably tight bounds.  
- Proposes and validates a protocol for applying the method to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters, tested on five models.  
- Demonstrates practical integrations into WebPPL and Stan and shows utility for guiding model representation choices.  
- Used BREAD to debug a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL, illustrating practical debugging value.  
- Clearly written and well-organized paper with accessible background, effective figures, and helpful appendices.  
- Novelty: conceptual advance applying BDMC to posterior-sample quality and clear differentiation from related work (e.g., [CTM16]).  
- Significance: provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality with immediate applicability in probabilistic programming.  
- Limitations: applicable only to algorithms representable as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS/SMC).  
- Practical limitations: requires exact posterior samples for simulation and relies on fitted hyperparameters producing representative simulated data.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, practically useful, well-validated, and impactful.
