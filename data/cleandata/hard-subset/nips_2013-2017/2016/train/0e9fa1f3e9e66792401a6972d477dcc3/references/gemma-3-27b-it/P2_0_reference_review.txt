## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally designed for log-marginal likelihood estimation, to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior. The authors derive an estimator that, in expectation, upper bounds this divergence and demonstrate its accuracy on toy examples. They also propose a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validate their approach by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan, using it to analyze matrix factorization model representations.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the BDMC work of Grosse et al. (2015) and extends it to a different, but related, application – assessing sample quality rather than marginal likelihood. It acknowledges related work in MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998), sample quality estimation (Gorham & Mackey, 2015), and other BDMC-based methods for evaluating probabilistic programming inference (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).  The paper appropriately positions itself within this landscape, highlighting its unique contribution of directly bounding the Jeffreys divergence.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC to measure sample quality is a clever and potentially impactful application.  The Jeffreys divergence is a sensible metric for evaluating the overall quality of an approximate posterior.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the estimator and its expectation bound on the Jeffreys divergence provides a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Practical Implementation & Validation:**  Implementing BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL & Stan) and validating it on diverse models significantly strengthens the work. The empirical demonstration of its utility in guiding model representation choices is particularly compelling.
* **Addressing Real-World Applicability:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, is a pragmatic and important addition. The validation of this protocol is also a strength.
* **Bug Discovery:** The accidental discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a robust testing tool for probabilistic programming systems.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Applicability:**  The method is currently limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). This restricts its direct applicability to standard MCMC methods, although the authors correctly point out that insights from AIS can inform MCMC design.
* **Computational Cost:**  BDMC, even for log-marginal likelihood estimation, can be computationally expensive. Applying it to assess sample quality adds further computational overhead, potentially limiting its scalability to very complex models. This isn't fully discussed.
* **Assumptions for Real-World Application:** The protocol for real-world data relies on the assumption that hyperparameters fitted on real data adequately capture the underlying data generating process. While the authors validate this, it's a potential limitation that needs careful consideration in practice.
* **Quartile Visualization:** The use of quartiles for visualization, while addressing outlier issues, makes it harder to directly compare performance across different conditions.  A discussion of why means aren’t appropriate is good, but alternative visualizations (e.g., box plots) might be more informative.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous derivation and thorough validation.  The claims are well-supported, and the analysis is careful. It’s a complete piece of work, although the limitations regarding computational cost and scope could be more explicitly discussed.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear and well-organized.  The mathematical notation is standard and accessible.  The description of the experimental setup and the interpretation of results are also clear.
* **Originality:**  The application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and significant contribution.  While building on existing work, the authors present a new perspective and valuable extension.
* **Significance:** The ability to rigorously assess the reliability of approximate posterior inference is crucial for advancing the field of probabilistic modeling. BREAD has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners alike, particularly those working with probabilistic programming languages. The demonstration of its use in guiding model design is particularly promising.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a critical problem (assessing MCMC reliability).
* Strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation.
* Practical implementation in popular probabilistic programming languages.
* Potential to significantly impact the field of probabilistic modeling.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of applicability to specific classes of algorithms.
* Computational cost may limit scalability.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The authors present a novel and theoretically sound method for assessing the reliability of approximate posterior inference. The practical implementation and validation, particularly the integration with WebPPL and Stan, are impressive. While the scope is limited and computational cost is a concern, the potential benefits of BREAD outweigh these drawbacks.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on a more thorough discussion of the computational costs and scalability limitations.
