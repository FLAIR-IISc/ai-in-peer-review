- Extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior.
- Uses BDMC’s upper and lower log-marginal-likelihood bounds to derive an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Demonstrates exact computation of the Jeffreys divergence and the bound on toy examples with good accuracy.
- Proposes a practical protocol for real data by simulating data from hyperparameters inferred on the real dataset (analogy to parametric bootstrap).
- Implements BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) in WebPPL and Stan and validates on several models.
- Methodology uncovered a real bug in WebPPL, demonstrating practical utility.
- Builds on Grosse et al. (2015) BDMC work and contrasts with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) regarding reverse chain initialization.
- Situates BREAD among MCMC diagnostics and related techniques (tempered transitions, importance sampling), offering rigorous bounds vs. heuristic diagnostics.
- Paper is well-written, logically organized, and uses equations appropriately.
- Matrix factorization experiments show BREAD can guide modeling choices and expose tradeoffs between representations.
- Method is mathematically rigorous and provides an objective measure of MCMC performance.
- Practical protocol for real data addresses the need for exact posterior samples and is a notable contribution.
- Implementation in popular probabilistic programming languages increases accessibility and impact.
- Computationally expensive: requires forward and reverse AIS chains and exact samples for simulated data, limiting applicability to complex models.
- Applicable only to algorithms representable as importance sampling on extended state spaces (AIS, SMC), restricting scope.
- Protocol relies on simulated data being representative of real data; hyperparameter uncertainty may compromise validity.
- Visualizations using quartiles reduce outlier influence but may obscure distributional details.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practical protocol, usable implementations, and empirical insights.
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost, limited algorithmic scope, and reliance on simulated data.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with valuable contributions despite computational and applicability limitations.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept for NIPS 2016.
- Minor suggestions: discuss computational-cost reductions (approximations/parallelization), analyze sensitivity to hyperparameter estimation, provide visualizations that retain more info, and include runtime comparisons with typical diagnostics.
