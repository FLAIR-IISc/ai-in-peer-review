- Summary: Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence between approximate MCMC posteriors and true posteriors, with protocol for real data and integrations in WebPPL and Stan.

- Strength 1: Theoretical soundness — BDMC derivation gives an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence with clear connections to prior work.

- Strength 2: Practical relevance — Addresses MCMC validation, guides modeling choices, and can uncover implementation bugs.

- Strength 3: Comprehensive validation — Validated on toy models (with exact divergence) and five real-world models using a simulation-based protocol.

- Strength 4: Tooling integration — Implemented in WebPPL and Stan, making the method accessible to practitioners.

- Strength 5: Clear presentation — Well-organized exposition from theory to applications.

- Weakness 1: Limited real-data applicability — Requires exact posterior samples, so protocol depends on assumptions that may not hold for all models or datasets.

- Weakness 2: Computational cost — Running forward and reverse AIS chains may be expensive for large-scale models; cost analysis is lacking.

- Weakness 3: Limited validation scope — Only five models tested; broader validation on multimodal or high-dimensional problems is needed.

- Weakness 4: Comparison with diagnostics — Lacks a thorough comparison to existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin).

- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and practical contribution with rigorous theory and useful implementations, despite noted limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Specific comment 1: Add a detailed discussion and comparison of BREAD's computational cost versus other methods.

- Specific comment 2: Discuss expected performance on more challenging multimodal or high-dimensional problems.

- Specific comment 3: Include a brief comparison with existing convergence diagnostics to position BREAD among validation tools.

- Specific comment 4: Expand the WebPPL bug example to emphasize practical utility.
