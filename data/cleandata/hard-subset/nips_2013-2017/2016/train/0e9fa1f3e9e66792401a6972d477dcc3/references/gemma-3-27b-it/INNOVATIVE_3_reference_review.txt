This paper presents a novel and promising method, BREAD, for evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based inference, extending the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea – leveraging BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence – is a significant contribution. While the method currently applies directly to algorithms amenable to importance sampling (AIS, SMC), the authors convincingly argue its broader relevance to MCMC design choices, and propose a pragmatic protocol for application to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation. The integration with WebPPL and Stan is a strong practical demonstration.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though some sections (particularly the background material on AIS) are quite dense. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need for exact posterior samples in the simulation setting. The experimental validation, though limited in scope, provides initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness and reveals a bug in WebPPL – a testament to its diagnostic potential. 

The strength of this work lies in its boldness and potential impact. Reliable quantitative measures for MCMC convergence are sorely needed, and BREAD offers a principled, albeit computationally demanding, approach.  The authors don’t shy away from addressing a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference. The framing of the problem in terms of bounding the Jeffreys divergence is a sophisticated choice.  I particularly appreciate the attempt to bridge the gap between simulated and real-world data through hyperparameter estimation and simulation.

However, there are areas for improvement. The computational cost of BREAD is a major concern. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of scalability and potential approximations would be beneficial.  The empirical validation, while demonstrating the method’s utility, feels somewhat limited. The five models used are representative but more diverse examples, especially those pushing the boundaries of current inference techniques, would strengthen the claims. The connection between the Jeffreys divergence bounds and practical inference performance (e.g., prediction error) could be better articulated. The proposed protocol for real-world data relies heavily on the quality of the hyperparameter estimates. A sensitivity analysis examining the impact of imperfect hyperparameter estimation on the BREAD results would be valuable. While the analysis of the toy distributions is helpful, a more in-depth comparison with existing convergence diagnostics would be insightful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is genuinely novel and addresses a critical need in the field.
*   **Significance:**  Successful development of reliable MCMC diagnostics would be a substantial advance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, and the implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical feasibility.
*   **Potential Impact:** BREAD could become a standard tool for evaluating and improving MCMC algorithms. The debugging application is compelling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The method is likely to be computationally expensive, limiting its applicability to large-scale models.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation could be more extensive and diverse.
*   **Real-World Protocol Concerns:** The reliance on accurate hyperparameter estimation introduces potential biases.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns regarding computational cost and the scope of the empirical validation, I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution to the field of MCMC diagnostics. The potential impact of BREAD is substantial, and the authors have provided a solid foundation for future work. I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and the methodological imperfections can be addressed in future iterations. This is a paper that pushes the boundaries of current research and offers a new perspective on a long-standing problem. The reviewers should also note the authors proactively identified and fixed a bug in WebPPL which shows the method’s practical utility.
