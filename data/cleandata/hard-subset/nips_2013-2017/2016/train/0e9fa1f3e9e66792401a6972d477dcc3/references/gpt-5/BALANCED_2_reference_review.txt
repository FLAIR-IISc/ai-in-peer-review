Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al. 2015) from bounding log marginal likelihoods (log-ML) on simulated data to measuring the reliability of posterior inference for certain MCMC-based algorithms, notably AIS and SMC. The key theoretical result is that the gap between forward and reverse AIS log-ML estimates, ˆB = log R̂rev^{-1} − log R̂, upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS and the true target pT. This turns BDMC’s sandwich on logZ into a quantitative measure of sample quality. The authors dub the approach BREAD and provide: (i) the derivation of the expected Jeffreys bound; (ii) exact computations on toy distributions suggesting the bound is often tight; (iii) a protocol to use simulated data anchored at hyperparameters fitted on real data to diagnose real-data inference; (iv) implementations in WebPPL and Stan; and (v) empirical case studies validating behavior across real vs simulated data, comparing collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization representations, and revealing a bug in WebPPL.

This work is well grounded in prior literature: AIS (Neal 2001), thermodynamic integration and Jarzynski’s equality, BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015), classical MCMC diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin; Brooks-Gelman; Cowles-Carlin), and alternative sample-quality measures (Stein discrepancy; Gorham & Mackey 2015). It also acknowledges contemporaneous subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016), which is conceptually related; when initialized from the true posterior, their estimator coincides with the BDMC-based bound here.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations connecting E[log R̂] and E[log R̂rev] to one-sided KLs are sound, and the conclusion that E[ˆB] ≥ DJ(pT, qfwd) is clear. The paper is careful about the distinction between stochastic bounds on logZ and the expectation bound on Jeffreys divergence. A limitation is that the divergence guarantee is in expectation; there is no high-probability or finite-sample control of DJ from observed gaps.
- Algorithms/scope: The guarantees require an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, which is available for simulated data via ancestral sampling; for real data the authors propose a fitted-hyperparameter proxy and empirically validate its stability.
- Experiments: The empirical section is thoughtfully designed. The real vs simulated curves align well across five Stan models, supporting the fitted-hyperparameter protocol. The collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization comparison is a nice demonstration of how BREAD can inform modeling choices. The toy examples indicating bound tightness are mentioned but relegated to the appendix; more quantitative analysis would strengthen the case. Most results are for AIS; SMC is stated as applicable but not evaluated.
- Practicality: Integrations into WebPPL and Stan and the debugging use case are valuable.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and generally well organized. The BDMC-to-divergence derivation is presented accessibly. The limitations and assumptions are stated. Some notational density (qfwd vs qrev conditionals) may challenge non-experts, but the narrative is coherent. The real-data protocol is described candidly as heuristic.

Originality
Using BDMC’s bidirectional logZ estimates to upper-bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence of AIS/SMC approximate samples is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel perspective relative to BDMC’s original aim. The contemporaneous subjective divergence work overlaps conceptually; the paper positions its contribution appropriately and still contributes a concrete, rigorous diagnostic and practical integration in PPLs.

Significance
The ability to quantify sample quality with a principled, implementation-independent criterion is important for probabilistic programming and for practitioners tuning AIS/SMC pipelines. The method’s scope is limited (requires extended-state-space importance sampling structure and exact posterior samples for rigorous guarantees), and the expectation-only nature of the bound reduces per-dataset certification value. Nevertheless, as a diagnostic for simulated-data benchmarking and for guiding modeling and kernel choices, the approach is useful.

Pros
- Clear theoretical link from BDMC to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical protocol to relate simulated to real data; empirically validated across diverse models.
- Implementations in two widely used systems; actionable insights (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and a demonstrated bug find.
- Good discussion of relation to prior/concurrent work.

Cons
- Guarantee is in expectation; no finite-sample or concentration bounds for DJ from observed gaps.
- Rigorous certification requires exact posterior samples; real-data protocol is heuristic without error quantification.
- Empirical evaluation focuses on AIS; little to no SMC results despite claims of applicability.
- Limited exploration of sensitivity to annealing schedule, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels; limited analysis of bound tightness beyond small toys.
- The measure pertains to the distribution of AIS terminal states, not general-purpose MCMC chains without an AIS/SMC wrapper.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical calibration of the observed gap versus true DJ on more challenging targets, and report confidence intervals or variance estimates for ˆB. If possible, derive or adapt concentration results to translate observed gaps into probabilistic statements about DJ.
- Include SMC experiments to substantiate the claimed generality.
- Analyze sensitivity to path design (temperatures, number of stages) and kernel choices; offer practical guidance for achieving informative bounds.
- Explore correlations between the bound and downstream quantities (e.g., predictive log likelihood, posterior expectations) to aid practitioner interpretation.
- Clarify how multiple AIS runs should be aggregated for estimating E[ˆB] and discuss the impact of heavy-tailed weight distributions.
- Expand the discussion contrasting your approach with subjective divergence beyond the idealized posterior-initialized case.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a solid theoretical insight with practical impact for evaluating AIS/SMC-based inference in probabilistic programming, accompanied by convincing implementations and case studies. The main limitations (expectation-only bound, reliance on exact posterior samples) are acknowledged and partially mitigated by the proposed protocol. With the suggested additions, the contribution would be even stronger.
