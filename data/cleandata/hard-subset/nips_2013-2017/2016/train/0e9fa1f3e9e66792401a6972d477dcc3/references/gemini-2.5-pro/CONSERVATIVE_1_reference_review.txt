This paper proposes BREAD, a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed for estimating log marginal likelihoods, to provide a quantitative measure of inference quality. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution of samples and the true posterior. As this technique requires an exact sample from the posterior, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters, then simulating a new dataset, and finally applying BREAD to this simulated data. The method is implemented in WebPPL and Stan and used to compare different model representations for matrix factorization.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The primary technical contribution—deriving an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence from the BDMC framework—is a clever and well-founded extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). It provides a formal, quantitative measure of sample quality, which is a desirable goal in a field that often relies on heuristics.
2.  **Demonstrated Utility:** The application of BREAD to compare collapsed versus uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model in Stan and WebPPL is a compelling use case. It provides concrete, quantitative guidance for a common modeling decision that often depends on intuition and trial-and-error. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the potential of this method as a rigorous tool for validating probabilistic programming systems.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the technical arguments are presented clearly. The background on AIS and BDMC is sufficient for understanding the main contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Validation of the Real-Data Protocol:** The most significant weakness lies in the proposed protocol for handling real-world data. While the rigorous bounds for simulated data are compelling, their practical relevance hinges on whether performance on simulated data accurately reflects performance on real data. The protocol to bridge this gap is heuristic and relies on strong assumptions. Using a point estimate of hyperparameters to generate a single simulated dataset may not be representative, especially if the hyperparameter posterior is broad or multimodal. The validation provided (Figure 1) shows consistency for five well-behaved models, but this is far from a comprehensive demonstration of the protocol's reliability. The procedure breaks the formal guarantees that are the main selling point of the method, and the degree to which this approximation impacts the conclusions is not thoroughly explored. For instance, how does the procedure fare under model misspecification, a common scenario with real data?
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper positions BREAD as a superior alternative to standard MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin R-hat, effective sample size). However, it provides no empirical comparison to these widely used and understood methods. A crucial missing piece of this work is an analysis of how BREAD's assessment of convergence compares to that of traditional diagnostics. Do the methods agree on when a sampler has converged? Is BREAD more sensitive to particular modes of failure? Without this comparison, it is difficult for a practitioner to justify the considerable computational expense of BREAD over existing, much cheaper diagnostics. The claim that BREAD "can rigorously demonstrate convergence" while diagnostics cannot is only true in the limited (and often less interesting) simulated data setting.
3.  **Limited Applicability:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which can be framed as importance sampling on an extended space. It cannot be used to directly assess the quality of samples from a standard MCMC run (e.g., a long chain from Stan's NUTS sampler). The authors argue that insights from evaluating AIS can inform the design of general MCMC algorithms, as they share design choices (e.g., MCMC kernels). While plausible, this is an indirect argument and limits the method's scope. The primary tool for posterior inference in systems like Stan is NUTS, not AIS, yet the tool cannot directly evaluate its output.
4.  **Unanalyzed Computational Cost:** The computational overhead of running BREAD appears to be substantial. It requires running multiple forward and reverse AIS chains, each of which involves many MCMC steps. The paper does not provide a clear analysis of this cost or a discussion of the trade-off between the cost and the insights gained, particularly in comparison to the alternative of simply running standard MCMC for more iterations and applying traditional diagnostics.

**Conclusion and Recommendation:**
This paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound idea for quantifying the error of certain sampling algorithms. However, the work in its current form feels incomplete as a contribution to the practice of MCMC validation. The claims of practical utility on real-world data rest on a heuristic protocol that is not sufficiently validated. More importantly, the complete absence of comparison to standard, established diagnostic tools is a major omission. While the theoretical contribution is elegant, the paper does not provide enough evidence to convince a practitioner to adopt this complex and computationally intensive method over existing tools. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper would be much stronger if it included a thorough empirical comparison with standard diagnostics on a range of problems, and a more rigorous investigation into the failure modes of the proposed real-data protocol.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, theoretically-grounded method for obtaining a quantitative bound on the Jeffreys divergence.
*   The application studies show clear utility for model/sampler configuration and for debugging inference systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The proposed protocol for real-world data is heuristic and its reliability is not established beyond a few simple examples.
*   The paper fails to compare its proposed method against standard, widely-used MCMC convergence diagnostics.
*   The method has limited applicability and cannot directly diagnose the output of standard MCMC algorithms like HMC/NUTS.
