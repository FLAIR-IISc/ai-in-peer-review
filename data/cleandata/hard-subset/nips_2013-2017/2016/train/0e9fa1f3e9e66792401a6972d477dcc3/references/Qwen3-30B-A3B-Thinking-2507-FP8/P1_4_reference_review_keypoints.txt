- Summary: Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to upper-bound Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors, validates on toy and simulated real-data protocols, and integrates into WebPPL and Stan.

- Strength — Theoretical contribution: Extends BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence with a rigorous derivation showing the AIS gap upper-bounds the divergence.

- Strength — Practical implementation: Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and used it to find a WebPPL bug, demonstrating practical utility.

- Strength — Comprehensive validation: Experiments show simulated-data protocol matches real-data inference behavior across multiple models.

- Strength — Clarity and organization: Well-written paper with clear background, logical flow, and effective figures.

- Weakness — Limited applicability: Method applies only to algorithms representable as importance sampling on extended state spaces (e.g., AIS/SMC), excluding many MCMC methods.

- Weakness — Heuristic for real data: Protocol relies on simulating from fitted hyperparameters, a heuristic that may fail in some settings and lacks analysis of failure modes.

- Weakness — Scalability concerns: Requires forward and reverse AIS chains, potentially computationally prohibitive for large complex models, with no empirical scalability study.

- Weakness — Comparison with CTM16: Comparison to Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) is superficial; needs deeper analysis of subjective vs. Jeffreys divergence.

- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and well-executed contribution with practical implementations and acknowledged limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Suggestion 1: Discuss scenarios where the real-data simulation heuristic might fail (e.g., broad priors or model misspecification).

- Suggestion 2: Add analysis of computational costs to clarify practical limitations.

- Suggestion 3: Expand comparison with CTM16 to clarify differences between subjective divergence and Jeffreys divergence.
