Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper-bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC-style algorithms and the true posterior. The core idea: forward AIS provides a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1, reverse AIS (initialized from an exact posterior sample) provides a stochastic upper bound, and the expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds DJ(pT, qfwd). The method (BREAD) is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrated on toy examples (where exact values are computable), and used on five realistic models to (i) validate simulated-data proxies for real-data behavior via a fitted-hyperparameter protocol, (ii) assess modeling/algorithmic choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) across systems, and (iii) debug a subtle implementation bug.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001). The new theoretical contribution is to link the BDMC bound gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions.
- Related to concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which coincides with the BDMC-based bound when initialized from the true posterior; this paper emphasizes the expectation-bound interpretation and provides a protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters.
- Complementary to sample quality diagnostics like Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015): BREAD is algorithm-aware (AIS/SMC style) but offers stronger guarantees on simulated data without convexity assumptions.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations for E[log R] and E[log Rrev] via extended-state importance sampling are sound and standard; the use of the data-processing inequality to obtain inequalities after marginalizing auxiliary variables is correct. The conclusion E[gap] ≥ DKL(q||p) + DKL(p||q) is well-justified. The guarantee is in expectation over AIS randomness and requires an exact posterior sample to get a stochastic upper bound on log ZT/Z1.
- Limitations of guarantees: The Jeffreys divergence bound is not a high-probability bound; it holds in expectation. Estimating this expectation from a finite number of AIS runs introduces Monte Carlo error and sensitivity to AIS weight outliers. The paper acknowledges heavy tails and visualizes quartiles, but for the stated guarantee one ultimately needs estimates of the mean and its uncertainty; providing means with MC standard errors would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Real-data protocol: The fitted-hyperparameter simulation is a reasonable and pragmatic parametric-bootstrap-style approach. The empirical consistency checks across five models are convincing, and the insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps used to refine the hyperparameters is encouraging. However, there is no general guarantee; the approach can fail under strong misspecification or concentrated priors far from the fitted point (the appendix notes one such mismatch).
- Experiments: Integrations into Stan and WebPPL, five diverse models, and an illustrative comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations are solid. The discovery of a WebPPL bug demonstrates practical utility. It would be useful to report results normalized by compute time as well as steps, and to show sensitivity to annealing schedules and transition kernels. The paper claims applicability to SMC but does not empirically evaluate SMC.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized. The exposition of AIS/BDMC and the derivation of the expected bound are clear. The practical protocol for real-data analysis is described plainly.
- Suggestions:
  - When using quartile plots due to outliers, also provide the sample means and confidence intervals, since the theory is about expectations.
  - State preconditions prominently (reversibility of kernels; availability of exact posterior samples for simulated data).
  - Provide more details on temperature schedules and kernel choices; sensitivity analyses would help practitioners.

Originality
- Extends BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding posterior sample quality via Jeffreys divergence; this is a natural but nontrivial insight. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data and the PPL integrations are practical contributions. Overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriately acknowledged; the paper’s emphasis on expectation bounds and extensive practical validation distinguishes it. Overall, moderate originality.

Significance
- Addresses a real need: principled, quantitative assessment of approximate posterior samples, especially for black-box inference in PPLs. The method yields actionable insights (e.g., representation choices in Stan vs. WebPPL) and can catch implementation errors. Likely to be adopted by PPL developers and researchers comparing inference schemes. The scope is limited to AIS/SMC-like algorithms and simulated data for rigorous guarantees, but even the heuristic real-data protocol is useful in practice.

Strengths
- Clear, correct theoretical link between BDMC gap and expected Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical, system-level integration into two PPLs.
- Convincing empirical demonstrations across multiple models; useful case study on collapsed vs. uncollapsed models.
- Demonstrated utility for debugging and validation.

Weaknesses
- Bound holds only in expectation; no high-probability or finite-sample guarantees on DJ. Empirical reporting relies on quartiles rather than means/MCSE, which is misaligned with the theoretical guarantee.
- Applicability restricted to algorithms that can be framed as AIS/SMC with reversible kernels and that can provide an exact posterior sample (or a close proxy).
- Real-data protocol is heuristic; while empirically validated here, it lacks general guarantees and can fail under misspecification.
- No empirical evaluation of SMC despite claims of applicability; limited sensitivity analyses (temperature schedules, kernel choices), and limited runtime-normalized comparisons.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report sample means of the forward–reverse log-gap with confidence intervals, alongside the quartiles.
- Provide compute-time-normalized plots and sensitivity to annealing schedules and transition kernels.
- Include at least one SMC experiment to substantiate applicability beyond AIS.
- Discuss whether variance-reduction techniques or combined-sample tail bounds (e.g., Gogate et al., 2007) can deliver tighter or more reliable finite-sample estimates of the expectation.
- Clarify how many reverse chains are needed in practice and how to choose the number of steps to obtain stable estimates.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is technically sound, tackles an important and under-served problem, offers a practical tool with demonstrated utility in mainstream PPLs, and provides new theoretical and empirical insights, despite limitations in the scope of guarantees and breadth of empirical evaluation.
