Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-style approximate posterior inference that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space, notably AIS and SMC. The key theoretical contribution is to show that the expected gap between the reverse and forward AIS log normalizing constant estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by the algorithm and the true target posterior. The authors dub the resulting protocol BREAD and argue it provides a quantitative measure of posterior sample quality.

They also propose a practical protocol for real datasets: fit hyperparameters to the real data, simulate synthetic datasets from those fitted hyperparameters, and run BREAD there; additionally, initialize the reverse chain for simulated data from an approximate posterior over hyperparameters obtained via a brief MCMC run. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, evaluate on several models, compare collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization representations, and demonstrate BREAD’s utility for debugging by uncovering a WebPPL bug.

This work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and reverse AIS/Jarzynski-type arguments (Neal 2001; Jarzynski 1997), and relates to convergence diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin; Brooks-Gelman), sample quality metrics (Stein discrepancies; Gorham & Mackey 2015), and automatic configuration of MCMC (Roberts & Rosenthal 2001). It is contemporaneous with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka’s subjective divergence, which is closely related; the paper positions its contribution as an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence when using the true posterior to initialize reverse AIS and discusses the subjective divergence variant with arbitrary reference distributions.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation is sound and clearly ties the bias of forward and reverse AIS log-estimates to KL terms. The core result—that E[log R̂rev^{-1} − log R̂] ≥ DKL(q||p) + DKL(p||q)—is well-motivated and correct given AIS’s extended-state importance sampling view and the availability of an exact posterior sample.
- Scope of guarantees: The bound is only in expectation; single-run gaps need not bound the divergence. The paper acknowledges this and reports distributions over runs. One-sided bounds with an external U/L are a nice addition.
- Practical protocol: The “fitted hyperparameter” procedure for real data is heuristic and removes the rigorous guarantee (no exact posterior sample). The empirical checks (reverse curves insensitive to number of MCMC steps S; similarity of forward AIS distributions on simulated vs real data) are reassuring but indirect.
- Experiments: A good variety of models and two PPLs; however, quantitative tightness is demonstrated rigorously only on toy problems. For larger models, no ground-truth divergence is available, and tightness is assessed qualitatively.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized. The extended-state derivations and the connections to KL terms are clear.
- It would help to state assumptions more explicitly (reversibility/ergodicity, details of temperature schedules) and to summarize practical guidance (number of temperatures, kernel settings per temperature, variance control).
- Reporting: Because single-run logs are heavy-tailed, the choice to visualize quartiles rather than means is sensible, but confidence intervals on E[gap] would make the “in expectation” guarantee more actionable.

Originality
- The main conceptual step—repurposing BDMC/AIS sandwiching into an estimator of posterior sample quality via Jeffreys divergence—is novel and useful.
- The applied protocol for real-world data and integration into two PPLs add practical novelty. Relation to subjective divergence is properly discussed.

Significance
- Addresses a long-standing gap: rigorous, quantitative assessment of approximate posterior samples, at least on simulated data. This is valuable for both PPL users and algorithm designers.
- The method informs model representation and algorithm choices (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed) and can detect implementation bugs.
- Limitations in scope (AIS/SMC-style samplers; need for exact posterior sample for rigor; expectation-only bound) constrain impact but still leave a substantial use case.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical link between AIS bidirectional estimates and Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical, implementable protocol; integration into WebPPL and Stan.
- Empirical validation across diverse models; insightful case study on matrix factorization representations.
- Demonstrated utility for debugging and for comparing design choices.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Expectation-only bound: Provide guidance on estimating E[gap] with uncertainty (e.g., via many runs and concentration bounds), and discuss the number of runs needed for a reliable decision.
- Applicability: Emphasize when AIS/SMC framing is valid in Stan (NUTS per temperature; step-size/metric adaptation across temperatures) and how adaptation interacts with invariance.
- Tightness: Beyond toy problems, quantify correlation between the bound and downstream performance (predictive log-likelihood, ESS/R-hat) to build practitioner trust.
- Real-data protocol: The fitted-hyperparameter reverse initialization removes rigor; consider sensitivity analyses where the reference distribution is intentionally misspecified, and report how the gap changes. If feasible, explore SMC variants where resampling and backward kernels may tighten reverse estimates.
- Variance reduction: Discuss strategies to tighten the sandwich (more temperatures, better schedules, symmetrized AIS paths, Rao-Blackwellization, or combining multiple runs via log-mean-exp with tail bounds).
- Clarity: Include more implementation details in the main text (temperature schedules, kernel choices, computational cost vs bound tightness).

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with clear derivation.
- Practical tool likely to be adopted in PPLs and by practitioners.
- Broad relevance to MCMC evaluation; compelling experiments and case studies.
- Clear relation to and advancement beyond BDMC.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees require simulated data and exact posterior samples; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Bound is only in expectation; empirical estimation and uncertainty treatment could be developed further.
- Limited quantitative assessment of tightness on complex models.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a meaningful theoretical and practical advance for assessing MCMC-based inference, with good empirical support and clear utility for the NIPS community, despite limitations that could be addressed in future work.
