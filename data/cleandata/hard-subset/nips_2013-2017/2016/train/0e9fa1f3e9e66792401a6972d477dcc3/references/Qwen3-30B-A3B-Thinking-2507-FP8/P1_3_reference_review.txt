

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to evaluate inference algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space. BREAD provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, which the authors validate on toy distributions. They also propose a practical protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and comparing inference behavior between real and simulated data. The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, with applications demonstrating how it can guide model representation choices.

The paper builds on recent work by Grosse et al. (2015) on bidirectional Monte Carlo for estimating marginal likelihoods, and relates to independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) on subjective divergence. The authors clearly distinguish their contribution from this related work by showing how BREAD's mathematical guarantees apply to Jeffreys divergence with exact posterior samples, while the subjective divergence in CTM16 requires the true posterior to be used.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical soundness**: The paper provides a careful theoretical derivation showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The mathematical development is thorough and well-motivated, with clear connections to existing literature on partition function estimation.

2. **Practical relevance**: The problem addressed—validating MCMC inference—is fundamental to probabilistic modeling and has significant practical implications. The authors demonstrate how BREAD can be used to guide modeling decisions (e.g., model representation in matrix factorization) and even uncover implementation bugs (as in WebPPL).

3. **Comprehensive validation**: The authors validate their method on both toy models (where exact Jeffreys divergence can be computed) and real-world applications across multiple probabilistic models. Their protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data through simulated data from fitted hyperparameters is well-justified and validated across five different models.

4. **Integration with major frameworks**: The implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to practitioners, which is a significant contribution to the field.

5. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear exposition of background material and a logical progression from theory to validation to application.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited applicability to real-world data**: While the authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, the method fundamentally requires access to exact posterior samples, which is only feasible for simulated data. Their protocol relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters will yield datasets where the true posterior is well-approximated by samples from the approximate posterior after a small number of MCMC steps. This assumption might not hold for all models or datasets.

2. **Computational cost**: The paper does not discuss the computational cost of running BREAD, which involves running both forward and reverse AIS chains. For large-scale models, this could be prohibitively expensive, limiting the practical applicability of the method.

3. **Limited scope of validation**: While the authors validate their method on five models, this is still a relatively small set. More extensive validation across a wider range of models and problem sizes would strengthen their claims.

4. **Comparison with other diagnostic methods**: The paper briefly mentions convergence diagnostics but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with existing methods (e.g., Gelman-Rubin statistics, trace plots). A more detailed comparison would help position BREAD within the broader landscape of MCMC validation techniques.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical development is rigorous, the practical implementation is well-executed, and the validation demonstrates clear utility. The method addresses a genuine need in the field—rigorous validation of MCMC inference—and provides a practical tool for probabilistic programming users.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it combines theoretical insights with practical applications in a relevant domain. The contribution is novel in its specific application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC validation, and it provides a useful tool for practitioners. While the limitations regarding real-world application are acknowledged, the authors provide a thoughtful protocol for addressing them.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The theoretical development is sound, the validation is thorough, and the practical implementation in major probabilistic programming frameworks makes it immediately useful. While the method has limitations regarding computational cost and applicability to real-world data (which are acknowledged), these do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for replication. The comparison with related work (particularly CTM16) is thoughtful and helps position the contribution within the field.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of running BREAD, including a comparison of the time required for BREAD versus other validation methods.

2. While the validation on five models is helpful, a brief discussion of how the method might perform on more challenging multimodal or high-dimensional problems would strengthen the paper.

3. Consider adding a brief comparison with existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) to help readers understand the relative advantages of BREAD.

4. The paper mentions a bug discovered in WebPPL using BREAD, which is a compelling example of the method's utility. This could be expanded slightly to emphasize the practical value of the approach beyond just validation.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, with clear theoretical foundations and practical utility.
