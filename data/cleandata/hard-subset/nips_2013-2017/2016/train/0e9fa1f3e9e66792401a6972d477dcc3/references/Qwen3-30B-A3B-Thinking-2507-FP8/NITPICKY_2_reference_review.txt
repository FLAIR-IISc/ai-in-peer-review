

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The method leverages BDMC to sandwich the log marginal likelihood, using the resulting bounds to estimate the Jeffreys divergence. The paper also introduces a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

**Strengths:** The paper identifies a meaningful problem in MCMC inference validation, and the technical approach is theoretically grounded. The experimental validation on WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility, and the method has potential for probabilistic programming systems. The comparison of model representations in matrix factorization provides concrete evidence of the method's value.

**Weaknesses:** The paper contains multiple significant technical errors and inconsistencies that undermine its claims:

1. **Critical mathematical error in Section 3.1:** The paper states "marginalizing out variables cannot increase the KL divergence," which is incorrect. Marginalization can increase KL divergence (e.g., if the marginal distribution is more spread out). This error invalidates the derivation of the upper bound.

2. **Incorrect statement about posterior sampling (Section 2.3):** "If one ancestrally samples ✓, z, and y, this is equivalent to first generating a dataset y and then sampling (✓, z) exactly from the posterior." This is false. Ancestral sampling from p(✓,z,y) produces samples from the joint distribution, not the posterior. The posterior is p(✓,z|y) = p(✓,z,y)/p(y), so sampling from the joint does not produce samples from the posterior unless conditioned on y.

3. **Mischaracterization of the BREAD bound (Section 3.1):** The paper claims "in expectation, ˆB upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence," but the correct statement is that E[ˆB] upper bounds J. The bound itself is not guaranteed to be an upper bound, only its expectation.

4. **Inconsistent notation and citations:** The paper uses inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "GGA15" vs "Grosse et al., 2015"), and the citation for Stan is incomplete ("CGHL+ p" should be "CGHL+14"). The paper also inconsistently uses "Jeffreys divergence" when referring to KL divergence in some sections.

5. **Flawed real-world data protocol (Section 3.2):** The paper assumes "the true hyperparameters ˆ⌘real which generated D ought to be in a region of high posterior mass," but this is not necessarily true. Model misspecification could cause the posterior to be concentrated away from the true hyperparameters.

6. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims BREAD "provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference," but this is only rigorously true for simulated data. For real-world data, the protocol is heuristic and not proven to generalize.

7. **Formatting issues:** The paper contains multiple inconsistent formatting issues in references (e.g., "Brooks and Gelman.,? \Q1998\E" should be "Brooks and Gelman, 1998"), inconsistent use of mathematical notation (e.g., "t 2" should be "t ≥ 2"), and typographical errors (e.g., "th Conference" should be "The Conference").

8. **Unsubstantiated claims:** The paper claims BREAD can "provide valuable insights into the tricky question of which representations of models to choose," but the matrix factorization experiment only uses one specific problem size (N=50, K=5, D=25), making generalization questionable.

**Conclusion:** While the paper addresses an important problem and has some practical value, the technical errors are severe enough to undermine the core claims. The mathematical errors in Section 3.1 invalidate the main theoretical contribution, and the overstatement of BREAD's applicability to real-world data without proper justification is problematic. The numerous formatting inconsistencies and citation errors further diminish the paper's quality.

The paper does not meet the high standards of NIPS for technical soundness and clarity. The theoretical contribution is not properly established, and the experimental validation is limited to specific problem sizes without sufficient generalization. The authors should address these fundamental errors before the work can be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
