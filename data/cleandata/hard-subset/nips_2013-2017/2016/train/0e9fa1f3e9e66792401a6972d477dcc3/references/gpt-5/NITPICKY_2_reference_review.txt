Summary
The paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), an application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of AIS/SMC-style MCMC inference. The main theoretical result shows that, for AIS viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space, the gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS log partition estimates upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution q of the AIS final state and the target p. The authors (i) formalize this expected upper bound, (ii) show tightness on toy problems where the true Jeffreys divergence can be computed, (iii) propose a pragmatic protocol for “real data” by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating from the fitted model, and initializing reverse AIS from an approximate posterior sample, and (iv) integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan. Experiments validate qualitative consistency between real and simulated data across five models, illustrate sensitivity to model representation (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization), and show a debugging use case.

Strengths
- Clear and useful reinterpretation of BDMC as an expected bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC-style samplers, with a clean derivation.
- Practical orientation: integration into two PPLs; examples across several models; compelling debugging anecdote.
- The “fitted-hyperparameter” protocol is pragmatic and may help practitioners relate simulated-data guarantees to real datasets.
- The paper raises the important question of quantitatively evaluating approximate posterior sampling quality beyond standard convergence diagnostics.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/methodological
- Expectation vs. instance-wise guarantees: The main bound controls E[gap] ≥ J, not the realized gap for a single run. No concentration or finite-sample inequalities are provided for the gap itself, unlike the one-sided tail controls for stochastic lower/upper bounds of log-partition functions. Several claims and plots read as if per-run “bounds” are being interpreted, which is not justified by the analysis. Please clarify what is guaranteed (expectation only) and provide guidance on how many runs are needed to estimate E[gap] with confidence; ideally, add concentration results or empirical variance analyses.
- Use on real data breaks guarantees: Section 3.2 initializes the reverse chain from an approximate posterior sample over hyperparameters and parameters. This invalidates the BDMC guarantee. While the empirical invariance to S (number of MCMC steps) is reassuring, the protocol remains heuristic. The paper should clearly label results obtained this way as “diagnostic” rather than “rigorous,” quantify the bias induced by approximate reverse initialization, and give failure cases (beyond the single misspecification example in Appendix D).
- AIS kernel invariance and adaptation: The theoretical justification requires each transition kernel T_t to leave p_t invariant. In Stan, NUTS is typically adaptive; adaptation breaks detailed balance/invariance during warmup. The paper does not specify whether adaptation was disabled during AIS (and how tuning was performed per temperature). WebPPL’s MH kernels under tempered targets also need to be specified precisely to ensure invariance. This is a major omission; without these details, the validity of the estimator and comparisons are unclear.
- Initial distribution sampling: AIS requires exact sampling from p1. Footnote indicates that sometimes only a single sample (not a sampler) is available; that would break unbiasedness and the stochastic lower-bound property. Please clarify exactly how p1 was sampled in all experiments and whether fresh p1 samples were drawn for each AIS run.
- Independence and reuse of reverse initializations: Many reverse AIS runs share the same initial exact posterior sample (or approximate sample in the real-data protocol). This induces dependence across estimates; yet the paper visualizes quartiles and does not report uncertainty accounting for dependence. Please clarify and, if applicable, adjust the statistical summaries.
- Temperature schedules and kernel design: Performance of AIS depends critically on the schedule, number of intermediates, and transition strength per temperature. The paper gives little detail on schedule selection, kernel sweep length, acceptance rates, or diagnostics across temperatures. Without these details, it is hard to assess the generality of the findings or reproduce them.
- SMC claims vs. experiments: The method is said to apply to SMC, but experiments are exclusively AIS. At least one substantive SMC example (with the same analysis) would strengthen the scope claims.
- Overclaim about “rigorously demonstrate convergence”: Even on simulated data, the result is an expectation bound on the divergence of the AIS final-state distribution, not a verification that a generic MCMC has converged. The distinction from classical MCMC convergence diagnostics should be described more cautiously.
- Evaluation metrics and reporting: For the five real/simulated comparisons, results are presented qualitatively via quartiles of log-ML estimates shifted vertically, with no numeric divergence estimates, confidence intervals, or wall-clock times. The matrix factorization case discusses per-iteration vs. per-time tradeoffs, but timing details (hardware, implementation, step sizes) are missing. This limits the practical impact and reproducibility.

Novelty/positioning
- The link between BDMC’s bidirectional estimates and symmetric divergences is closely related to Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (subjective divergence). The paper acknowledges this but should delineate more sharply what is technically new (beyond “refining the analysis”) and what is essentially an alternative presentation of known results. A formal proposition/theorem with precise conditions and proof sketch would help.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous formatting and citation issues: e.g., “[GS; CGHL+ p]”, “[Nea+11]”, “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems …” mid-text, broken equation (1), inconsistent notation (D vs y; η vs ⌘), missing years in references, footnote “1” placement. These hinder readability and require careful editing.
- Some claims are imprecisely worded (e.g., “forward and reverse AIS chains yield lower and upper bounds on log p(y) with high probability” without stating the one-sided tail behavior and the conditions).
- Important implementation details (kernel invariance, adaptation, temperature schedule) are omitted.

Pros
- Addresses an important, practical problem.
- Clean theoretical insight; potentially useful diagnostic tool.
- Integration into popular PPLs; interesting case studies, including a real bug found.

Cons
- Guarantees are in expectation; the paper sometimes over-interprets per-run behavior.
- Real-data protocol invalidates the guarantee; evaluation largely qualitative.
- Critical methodological details missing (kernel invariance/adaptation, AIS setup).
- Limited novelty relative to existing BDMC/subjective divergence work.
- Presentation issues and numerous formatting/citation errors.

Recommendation: Reject
The idea is promising and practically relevant, but the current manuscript has substantial methodological ambiguities, overstatements of guarantees, limited quantitative evaluation, and significant clarity/formatting problems. I encourage the authors to (i) tighten theoretical claims (including concentration or estimation procedures for E[gap]), (ii) clearly separate rigorous bounds from heuristic diagnostics, (iii) provide full implementation details (especially for Stan/NUTS and schedule design), (iv) add at least one SMC experiment, and (v) substantially improve the presentation and referencing. With these addressed, the work could make a valuable contribution.
