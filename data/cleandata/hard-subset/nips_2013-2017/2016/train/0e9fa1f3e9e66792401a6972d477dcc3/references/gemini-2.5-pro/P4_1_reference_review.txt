This paper introduces a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD) for quantitatively measuring the reliability of certain MCMC inference algorithms. The method extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015), which was originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. BDMC uses Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both a forward and a reverse direction to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key theoretical contribution of this paper is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

Because the method requires an exact sample from the posterior to initialize the reverse chain, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data where such a sample can be generated. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; and third, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset to evaluate inference quality. The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, and use it to validate the proposed protocol. They further demonstrate its utility with a case study comparing different model representations for matrix factorization and by identifying a bug in the WebPPL implementation. This work is closely related to the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), a relationship the authors clearly discuss.

### Strengths

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in probabilistic modeling and inference: how to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. Existing MCMC convergence diagnostics are often heuristic and can only detect non-convergence, whereas this work provides a quantitative upper bound on a formal divergence measure. This is a significant step forward for building more reliable and automated inference systems, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming where users may not be inference experts.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—connecting the BDMC marginal likelihood bounds to the Jeffreys divergence—is novel, insightful, and theoretically elegant. While building directly upon BDMC [GGA15] and AIS [Nea01], the paper repurposes these tools for a different and arguably more impactful goal: evaluating sample quality rather than just estimating evidence. The proposed protocol for applying the technique to real-world data is also a valuable, pragmatic contribution.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation in Section 3.1 that establishes the Jeffreys divergence bound is clear and appears sound. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors are careful to validate the key assumptions of their proposed protocol for real-world data, showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data (Sec 5.1.1) and that the reverse chain is not overly sensitive to the initialization from an approximate posterior sample (Sec 5.1.2). The case study on model representation provides a compelling demonstration of how BREAD can yield actionable insights for practitioners.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The background material on AIS and BDMC is explained clearly, the new theoretical results are derived step-by-step, and the experimental setup and results are presented lucidly. The organization is logical, guiding the reader from the theoretical foundation to practical application and validation.

### Weaknesses

**Limited Applicability of Guarantees:** The rigorous mathematical guarantees of BREAD are confined to the simulated data setting, as it requires an exact posterior sample. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a heuristic. While the authors provide compelling empirical validation for its use, this remains a fundamental limitation. The performance of the protocol may degrade if the generative model is severely misspecified for the real-world data, a point acknowledged by the authors in the appendix.

**Scope of Evaluated Algorithms:** The method directly evaluates the sample distribution produced by algorithms like AIS and SMC, which can be viewed as importance sampling on an extended space. It does not directly assess the quality of samples from a standard, long-run MCMC chain (e.g., from NUTS or Metropolis-Hastings after burn-in). The authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS can inform the design of general MCMC algorithms since they share components (e.g., transition operators). However, this is an indirect benefit, and the method cannot be used "off-the-shelf" to diagnose a standard HMC run.

**Computational Cost:** BREAD requires running both forward and reverse AIS chains, potentially multiple times to get stable estimates of the expected bounds. This can be computationally intensive, especially for complex models or long annealing schedules. A more detailed discussion of the computational overhead would be beneficial for potential users to gauge the practicality of the method for their specific problems.

**Clarity on Estimation:** Footnote 3 states that the analysis "does not justify" averaging estimates and that quartiles of individual chain estimates are reported instead. This is slightly confusing. The theoretical result is that the *expectation* of the bound `E[B_hat]` upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. A standard Monte Carlo approach to estimate this expectation would be to average `B_hat` over many independent runs. It is unclear why this would not be justified. Clarifying this point would strengthen the paper's presentation of how the bound should be estimated in practice.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a highly significant and long-standing problem in the field.
2.  Provides a novel and sound theoretical result connecting BDMC bounds to the Jeffreys divergence.
3.  Proposes and validates a practical protocol for applying the method to real-world problems.
4.  Demonstrates clear utility through extensive experiments, including model selection and debugging.
5.  The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The method’s rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data, and its application to real data is heuristic.
2.  The technique is directly applicable only to AIS/SMC-like samplers, not general MCMC algorithms.
3.  The computational cost may be a barrier to adoption in some settings.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It makes an important and novel contribution to the critical area of inference validation. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and its practical utility is convincingly demonstrated.
