- Paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors using reverse AIS initialized from true posterior samples.
- Introduces BREAD and a protocol to apply it to real data by estimating hyperparameters and simulating datasets, plus implementations in WebPPL and Stan.
- Positions work within MCMC diagnostics and log-likelihood estimation literature, distinguishing bounds from heuristic convergence diagnostics and acknowledging related BDMC work.
- Connects method to tempered transitions and non-equilibrium statistical mechanics (Neal, Jarzynski) and properly cites prior work (CTM16).
- Strength: provides a rigorous, theoretically grounded upper bound on posterior inaccuracy rather than just diagnostic signals.
- Strength: practical protocol to extend the method to real-world data addresses a key limitation of requiring exact posterior samples.
- Strength: implementations in popular probabilistic programming languages increase accessibility and potential impact.
- Strength: demonstrated utility through experiments that guide modeling choices and detect software bugs.
- Strength: paper is clearly written, well-organized, and includes thorough validation experiments.
- Weakness: high computational cost due to running both forward and reverse AIS chains and unclear scalability to very high dimensions.
- Weakness: protocol depends on quality of initial hyperparameter estimates and lacks thorough robustness analysis to prior misspecification.
- Weakness: reliance on simulated data risks misleading validation if the fitted model is misspecified (noted in Appendix D).
- Weakness: Jeffreys divergence may not be the most intuitive or appropriate metric for all applications; alternatives deserve discussion.
- Weakness: limited direct empirical comparison with existing MCMC diagnostics to quantify relative advantages.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practically extended to real data, implemented broadly, and well-validated.
- Arguments against acceptance: computational expense, dependence on simulated-data protocol with potential biases, and need for more comparisons to standard diagnostics.
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution whose benefits outweigh concerns about cost and simulated-data dependence.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept.
