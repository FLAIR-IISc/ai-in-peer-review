- Main result: Extends BDMC to show the forward–reverse AIS log-marginal-likelihood gap upper-bounds the expected Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between AIS/SMC sample distribution and the true posterior.

- Method/protocol: Proposes BREAD — use forward/reverse AIS on simulated data (from hyperparameters fitted to real data) and an approximately posterior-initialized reverse chain to obtain practical diagnostics for real datasets.

- Implementation: Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validates across five models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, GMM).

- Empirical findings: Demonstrates tightness on toy cases, consistency between real and simulated data for tested models, compares collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization across platforms, and discovers a WebPPL bug.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on BDMC, AIS, and reverse annealing; clarifies relation to contemporaneous subjective-divergence work (equivalent when reverse chain starts from true posterior).

- Theoretical novelty: Interprets BDMC sandwich as an expected Jeffreys divergence bound and decomposes it into one-sided KL controls after marginalizing auxiliaries.

- Strength — theory: Clear, elegant derivation that the AIS gap controls Jeffreys divergence in expectation.

- Strength — practical: Provides an automated, general diagnostic for PPL users with real-world integration and empirical protocol bridging simulated and real data.

- Strength — scientific utility: Useful for model representation comparisons and debugging (e.g., finding implementation bugs).

- Weakness — expectation-only: The bound holds only in expectation; no high-probability or finite-sample guarantees and single runs can underestimate J.

- Weakness — exact sample requirement: Requires an exact posterior sample for rigor; the real-data workaround (fitted hyperparameters) is heuristic and loses formal guarantees.

- Weakness — limited SMC evaluation: Experiments focus on AIS despite claims of SMC applicability; SMC and SMC-preferred models are underexplored.

- Weakness — limited tightness study: Tightness assessments restricted to small toy cases; lacks systematic analysis across schedules, intermediate distributions, and multimodality.

- Weakness — reporting/uncertainty: Some reporting choices (quartiles for AIS weights, shared reverse initial state) complicate interpretation; run-to-run variability and uncertainty of B are not emphasized.

- Weakness — presentation details: Notation/citations have rough spots and some implementation details (schedules, kernels, compute budgets) are sparse.

- Quality assessment: Technically sound and experiments support main claims, but would benefit from more quantitative analysis of bound tightness and variability.

- Clarity suggestions: Paper is generally clear; recommend adding pseudocode, detailed annealing/kernel choices, sensitivity analyses, and guidance on number of runs and uncertainty summarization.

- Originality: Moderately original — primarily a repurposing of BDMC results but with a novel interpretative insight and practical protocol/PPL integrations.

- Significance: High practical impact for benchmarking and validating MCMC-based inference in PPLs, despite limitations on real-data guarantees and experiment breadth.

- Actionable questions/suggestions: Provide concentration/CI methods for J, analyze sensitivity to temperature schedules/kernels, include SMC experiments, investigate multimodal behavior, consider complementary estimators, and release reproducible code.

- Recommendation and confidence: Weak accept; reviewer has high confidence in theoretical assessment and medium confidence in empirical generality.
