Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper-bounding (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples (from AIS/SMC-style samplers) and the true posterior. The key idea: forward AIS gives a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1, reverse AIS (initialized from an exact posterior sample) gives a stochastic upper bound, and the expected gap between the two is an upper bound on DKL(q||p)+DKL(p||q). The authors (i) formalize this bound, (ii) show tightness on toy problems where exact divergences are computable, (iii) propose a practical “fitted hyperparameter” protocol to apply the method to real datasets by simulating data from hyperparameters inferred on the real data, and (iv) integrate the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, using it to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and to uncover a subtle implementation bug. The paper relates to BDMC, RAISE, thermodynamic integration, SMC, and recent subjective divergence work.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), which sandwiches marginal likelihood; here the novelty is to bound the sample quality (Jeffreys divergence) induced by AIS/SMC.
- Connects to RAISE/Burda et al. (stochastic bounds via reverse annealing).
- The independent “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) is closely related; this paper’s analysis matches the case where the reverse chain uses the true posterior. Authors acknowledge and contrast the reference-distribution case.
- Goes beyond standard MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman) by providing a quantitative guarantee (on simulated data).
- Touches on Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey) as an alternative sample quality metric.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation that E[log R̂rev^{-1} − log R̂] ≥ DJ(pT, qfwd) is clear and correct, and the inequality steps are standard (marginalization reduces KL). The “in expectation” qualifier is important; there are no finite-sample, high-probability guarantees for the divergence bound.
- Scope: Requires a single exact posterior sample to run reverse AIS; hence strict guarantees hold for simulated data (or rare settings with exact posterior samplers). The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data is sensible, though heuristic.
- Empirics: Demonstrations across five models in Stan, with consistent behavior between real and simulated data; robustness of reverse AIS to number of MCMC steps when approximating the reverse initialization; insightful comparison of collapsed vs uncollapsed formulations across Stan/WebPPL; and a compelling debugging anecdote. However, stress tests in very high dimensions or strongly multimodal posteriors are limited, and comparisons to alternative diagnostics (e.g., Stein discrepancy) are missing.
- Practicalities: The authors note heavy-tailed AIS log-weight behavior and report quartiles of per-chain estimates because the analysis does not justify simple averaging; this is honest but highlights practical variance/concentration issues that could use more discussion or remedies.

Clarity
- Well-written and organized; background is sufficient for a NeurIPS audience. The BREAD method and its assumptions/limitations are stated plainly. Some minor notational slips (e.g., “qfwd(pT)”) could be cleaned; more explicit guidance on choosing annealing schedules and transition kernels would help practitioners.

Originality
- While close to BDMC, the conceptual reframing—using the bidirectional log-evidence gap to upper bound posterior-sample divergence—is novel and useful. The protocol for real data and integration into two PPLs reflect an ambitious and practical step beyond marginal-likelihood benchmarking.

Significance
- Provides a rare, quantitative, semi-rigorous diagnostic for sample quality that can influence model/algorithm design in PPLs, and even catch bugs. Likely to be adopted by the PPL community and spur further work on bounded diagnostics for MCMC.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Only an expectation bound; no finite-sample concentration results for the divergence. Suggest exploring aggregation schemes (e.g., median-of-means on log-weights) or existing tail-strengthening techniques and reporting empirical coverage.
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers requiring an annealing path and reversible kernels; broader MCMC (e.g., pure NUTS on the posterior) is only indirectly informed. Clarify how to adapt to SMC with resampling and to nonreversible transitions.
- The real-data protocol lacks guarantees and could fail under misspecification or highly diffuse priors; show a more systematic sensitivity analysis and failure case exploration.
- Missing comparisons to Stein discrepancies or other sample quality measures; even a small-scale side-by-side would help position BREAD’s strengths/weaknesses.
- Provide guidance on schedule length, temperature placement, and transition kernel design in terms of the resulting divergence bound.

Questions for the authors
- Can multiple independent exact posterior initializations for reverse AIS materially tighten the empirical bound or its variance?
- How does the bound behave under severe multimodality where AIS often misses modes? Can you show a case where DJ is large and BREAD detects it?
- Any path to a high-probability upper bound on DJ using repeated runs?

Pros
- Conceptually novel, bridges BDMC to posterior-sample quality
- Practical, implemented in major PPLs, with real insights and bug-finding
- Clear analysis and honest discussion of limitations

Cons
- Expectation-only bound; variance/concentration issues unresolved
- Limited empirical stress-testing and limited comparisons to alternative diagnostics
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-like algorithms

Recommendation: Accept (7/10)
This is a bold and useful extension of BDMC with clear practical impact on probabilistic programming and MCMC validation. Despite some limitations (expectation-only guarantee, reliance on simulated data), the conceptual advance and demonstrated utility merit inclusion.
