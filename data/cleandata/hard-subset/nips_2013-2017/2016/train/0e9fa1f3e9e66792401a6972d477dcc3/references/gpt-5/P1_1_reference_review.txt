Summary and context
The paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based approximate posterior sampling. Focusing on algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC), the authors show that the expected gap between a forward AIS stochastic lower bound and a reverse AIS stochastic upper bound on the log partition ratio upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior p and the distribution q of approximate samples produced by the algorithm. They term the practical procedure BREAD. They further (i) provide a derivation that refines prior BDMC analyses to obtain bounds on DKL(q||p), DKL(p||q), and their sum DJ, (ii) show on small toy problems that the bound can be tight by computing J and the bound exactly, (iii) propose a pragmatic protocol for real datasets based on simulating from hyperparameters fit to real data and initializing the reverse chain from an approximate posterior sample over (hyperparameters, parameters), and (iv) implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, validating the proxy protocol across five models and using BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations. As a by-product, BREAD revealed a subtle WebPPL bug.

Relation to prior work: The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015) and AIS (Neal 2001), and connects to stochastic bounds on log normalizers (Burda et al. 2015), SMC (Del Moral et al. 2006), convergence diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin 1992; Brooks & Gelman 1998), and Stein discrepancies for sample quality (Gorham & Mackey 2015). It is closely related to concurrent work on subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016); when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior, their estimator and this paper’s analysis coincide in upper-bounding Jeffreys divergence.

Strengths
- Clear, simple theoretical result: the expected forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds DJ(p||q) for the marginal distribution of the final state. This provides a rigorous, interpretable quantity for sample quality.
- Practical impact: implementation in two widely used probabilistic programming systems and a concrete protocol for using simulated data to assess real-data performance. The debugging anecdote underscores utility.
- Breadth: applies to AIS and, by extension, SMC-style algorithms; analyses in terms of one-sided KLs with one direction plus any external bound are also useful.
- Empirical validation: consistency between real and simulated data across diverse models; insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the reverse initialization over hyperparameters.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Guarantees hold only on simulated data with exact posterior samples. The real-data protocol (fit hyperparameters, simulate data, approximate reverse initialization) is heuristic; no theory ensures that diagnostic generalizes to the real dataset.
- The bound is in expectation. The paper does not provide concentration results or recommended procedures to estimate the expectation with confidence intervals; instead it plots quartiles to avoid outlier issues. Given the expectation guarantee, reporting mean and uncertainty would be preferable.
- Tightness is demonstrated only on small toy distributions; no quantitative assessment of tightness in higher-dimensional, realistic settings.
- Sensitivity analyses are limited. The quality of AIS bounds depends on temperature schedules, transition kernels, and numbers of steps/particles; these design choices are not systematically explored.
- Despite claiming applicability to SMC, experiments focus on AIS; SMC demonstrations would strengthen the case.
- Presentation/citation issues (typos, dangling citations, formatting artifacts) detract from clarity.

Quality
The derivations are sound and extend BDMC in a natural yet useful way. The key inequalities (Eqs. 6–12) are standard KL manipulations on path distributions and their marginalization, leading to a clean expectation bound on DJ. The empirical sections are careful about the limitations (e.g., approximate reverse initialization) and provide some validation. However, the statistical treatment of the estimators could be stronger: since the guarantee is about the expected gap, the paper should report means with error bars or provide variance/concentration analysis. The real-data proxy is plausible and validated empirically but remains heuristic.

Clarity
Generally well written and pedagogical background on AIS/BDMC. The high-level narrative is easy to follow. That said, the manuscript contains several formatting/citation glitches (e.g., [GS; CGHL+ p], stray “th Conference…” line, misformatted footnotes), and a few places would benefit from more explicit guidance for practitioners (recommended number of chains, temperatures, diagnostics to detect when the bound is informative). The choice to visualize quartiles rather than expectations should be justified more clearly with a discussion of the estimator one would report in practice.

Originality
The central theoretical observation is a direct refinement of BDMC and overlaps with concurrent “subjective divergence” work; novelty is moderate but nontrivial and useful. The proposal to use fitted-hyperparameter simulation as a proxy for real-data diagnostics is pragmatic and, to my knowledge, new in this context. The cross-system implementation and case study provide additional novelty in practice.

Significance
Practically significant for probabilistic programming and Bayesian computation: a quantitative, actionable measure of sample quality for AIS/SMC that can guide modeling choices (e.g., collapsing) and detect implementation bugs. While the scope excludes generic MCMC without an importance-sampling structure or exact posterior samples, AIS/SMC are widely used, making the method relevant. The lack of high-probability bounds and the simulated-data requirement temper impact but do not negate utility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report sample means of the gap with confidence intervals or bootstrap, and discuss how many chains are needed for reliable estimation. Consider variance reduction or tail-robust estimators consistent with the expectation guarantee.
- Add sensitivity studies to temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels. Provide practical recommendations.
- Include at least one SMC experiment.
- Quantify tightness on moderately high-dimensional models where ground truth KL can be approximated (e.g., via tempering with very long runs).
- Clarify the relationship and differences with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) beyond the exact-posterior case; discuss risks when the reverse initialization is approximate.
- Fix citation/formatting issues and provide implementation details (code, seeds, number of AIS steps/temperatures, time budgets) for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, correct theory yielding a useful and interpretable metric.
- Practical implementations and evidence of real utility (modeling choices, debugging).
- Valuable to the NIPS community working on probabilistic programming and Bayesian computation.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees limited to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Expectation-only bound without uncertainty quantification or concentration results.
- Limited empirical breadth (no SMC) and tightness assessment in realistic regimes.
- Some presentation polish needed.

Overall recommendation
A strong, pragmatic contribution that meaningfully advances our ability to assess MCMC-based inference in practice. Despite limitations, I lean toward acceptance after minor revisions focused on empirical breadth and clarity.
