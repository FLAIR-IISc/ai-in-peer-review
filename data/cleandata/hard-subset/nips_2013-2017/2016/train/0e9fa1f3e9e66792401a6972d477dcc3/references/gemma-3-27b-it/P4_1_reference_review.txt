This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained, for example, from AIS or SMC) and the true posterior, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming. The core idea builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) by refining the analysis to derive an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The authors also present a protocol for applying this to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation, and implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its use for model comparison and bug detection.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in MCMC-based inference: reliable validation of results. The use of BDMC and the focus on bounding the Jeffreys divergence provide a rigorous approach, moving beyond heuristic convergence diagnostics. The extension to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation is a practical and valuable contribution. The implementation in two prominent probabilistic programming languages further enhances the paper’s impact and accessibility. The authors clearly relate their work to existing literature, acknowledging related efforts like [CTM16] and thoroughly citing relevant prior work on MCMC diagnostics and related techniques like AIS and SMC.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. The technical analysis appears sound, building logically on the BDMC framework. The validation experiments, particularly the demonstration that the method can detect a bug in WebPPL, are compelling. The clear presentation of the method and its application to concrete examples (matrix factorization) makes it easy to understand. The authors are careful to discuss limitations, such as the requirement for exact samples in the reverse chain for simulated data. The extensive experimental evaluation with different models and platforms bolsters confidence in the method’s robustness.

However, some weaknesses exist. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the connection between the Jeffreys divergence bound and practical inference performance isn’t fully explored. Does a small Jeffreys divergence *guarantee* reliable inference in a practical sense? The paper doesn't definitively answer this.  The protocol for applying BREAD to real data relies on fitting hyperparameters, which could introduce its own biases.  While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter fitting procedure would be beneficial. The computational cost of BREAD, especially the reverse AIS chain, is not fully discussed; a comparison to standard convergence diagnostics in terms of computational effort would be valuable.  The presentation of experimental results, while clear, often relies on quartiles due to outliers.  While justified, more detailed discussion of the nature and impact of these outliers would be helpful. Finally, the paper primarily focuses on Stan and WebPPL.  Expanding the implementation or demonstration to other probabilistic programming languages would broaden its appeal.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is based on a solid theoretical foundation (BDMC) and provides rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence.
*   **Novelty:** The extension of BDMC to measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples is a novel contribution.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method has clear applications for validating inference in probabilistic programming languages and guiding model selection.
*   **Implementation & Validation:**  The implementation in Stan and WebPPL, along with the validation experiments (including bug detection), demonstrates the method's practicality and effectiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** The connection between the Jeffreys divergence bound and real-world inference reliability could be explored further.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational cost of BREAD, particularly for the reverse AIS chain, isn’t fully addressed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter fitting procedure requires further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The method is technically sound, novel, and has clear practical applications. While some weaknesses exist, they do not outweigh the significant strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the comments regarding computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and the discussion of the practical implications of the Jeffreys divergence bound.
