

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for rigorously measuring the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, rather than just estimating marginal likelihoods as in previous work. The key insight is that by running AIS in both forward and reverse directions (with exact posterior samples), one can sandwich the log marginal likelihood and derive a bound on the divergence.

The paper makes four significant contributions: (1) a theoretical derivation showing BDMC yields an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) validation of this bound on toy distributions where exact calculations are possible; (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters; and (4) integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, with practical applications including debugging a WebPPL bug.

The core novelty of this work lies in recognizing that BDMC can be repurposed not just for marginal likelihood estimation, but for directly measuring the quality of posterior samples. While related work (such as Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016) also builds on BDMC, this paper provides a mathematically rigorous bound on the divergence itself, rather than a related quantity. This represents a significant conceptual leap from previous approaches to MCMC validation.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with strong technical development of the theoretical framework. The experimental validation demonstrates practical utility, particularly the debugging application in WebPPL, which shows the method's value beyond just theoretical contributions. The integration into two major probabilistic programming languages significantly enhances the paper's practical relevance.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its bold conceptual shift: moving from heuristic convergence diagnostics to a rigorous, quantitative measure of inference quality. While the method is currently limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained (via simulated data), the authors provide a practical protocol for applying it to real-world data, and the validation experiments demonstrate that this approach works well in practice.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that it requires exact posterior samples (hence the focus on simulated data), and that the real-world protocol relies on the assumption that simulated data from fitted hyperparameters resembles real data. However, these limitations don't detract from the novelty of the core contribution.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference: how to reliably assess the quality of posterior samples. Current convergence diagnostics cannot confirm convergence, whereas BREAD provides rigorous bounds on the error. This is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming systems where users need to trust the inference results without deep statistical expertise.

Strengths:
- Novel core idea: repurposing BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence rather than estimate marginal likelihoods
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous bounds
- Practical implementation in two major probabilistic programming languages
- Demonstrated utility in debugging a real implementation
- Clear presentation of the method and validation

Weaknesses:
- The method is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained (though the authors provide a practical protocol for real-world data)
- The computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be significant
- Limited discussion of how this compares to other validation approaches in terms of computational efficiency

Arguments for acceptance:
- The core idea is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in MCMC validation
- The method provides a rigorous measure of inference quality where none existed before
- The practical implementation and debugging example demonstrate real-world utility
- The paper is well-written and technically sound

Arguments against acceptance:
- The method's applicability is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained
- The computational overhead of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for large-scale problems
- The real-world protocol relies on the assumption that simulated data from fitted hyperparameters resembles real data

Despite these limitations, the novelty and conceptual significance of this work outweigh the methodological constraints. The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to a critical problem in Bayesian inference, with clear practical applications. The authors have demonstrated that BREAD is not just a theoretical curiosity but has tangible value in practice, as evidenced by the WebPPL bug discovery.

Given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, this paper deserves strong consideration for acceptance. It represents a significant step forward in the field, offering a rigorous, general method for evaluating MCMC inference quality that can be immediately applied in probabilistic programming systems. While not perfect, the paper's novel contribution is substantial enough to overcome its minor limitations.

I recommend acceptance as it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, addresses a difficult problem (validating MCMC inference), and provides a unique theoretical and practical approach to a fundamental challenge in probabilistic modeling.
