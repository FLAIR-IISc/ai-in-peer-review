This paper presents a novel and intriguing extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC inference, specifically by bounding the Jeffreys divergence. The core idea – leveraging BDMC’s strengths in bounding marginal likelihoods to assess posterior accuracy – is a bold and potentially impactful contribution. While the experimental validation is not exhaustive, the initial results are promising, and the implementation in WebPPL and Stan significantly increases the accessibility and potential impact of the method. I value the novelty of this approach and its potential to address a long-standing challenge in Bayesian inference: rigorous validation of MCMC results.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to quantify posterior accuracy, rather than solely marginal likelihood estimation, is a genuinely novel idea. It re-purposes a powerful technique in a new and insightful way.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence is well-motivated and presented clearly. The connection to stochastic lower/upper bounds and the use of reverse AIS are cleverly exploited.
* **Practical Implementation:** Extending BREAD to WebPPL and Stan is a significant contribution, making it accessible to a broad range of users. This is especially valuable given the growing adoption of probabilistic programming.
* **Potential Impact:** A reliable measure of MCMC convergence and accuracy has been a longstanding need. BREAD offers a promising step towards filling that gap and building trust in probabilistic programming systems.
* **Diagnostic Application:** The identified bug in WebPPL demonstrates the practical utility of BREAD as a debugging tool – a testament to the rigor of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS/SMC). While the authors acknowledge this, the practical implications are significant. The extent to which insights gained from AIS/SMC generalize to broader MCMC methods requires further investigation.
* **Simulated Data Dependency:** The core method relies on access to an exact posterior sample, necessitating simulated data. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (hyperparameter fitting and data simulation) introduces potential biases, and the assumption that the fitted hyperparameters accurately reflect the true underlying data-generating process is crucial.  While validated to some extent in the experiments, this remains a concern.
* **Computational Cost:** Running both forward and reverse AIS chains, particularly for complex models, can be computationally expensive. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost, or strategies for mitigating it.
* **Experimental Scope:** The validation experiments are primarily focused on a relatively small set of models.  A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of problem complexities and model structures would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Quartile Visualization:** While justifiable given outliers, visualizing quartiles obscures some information about the distribution of AIS estimates. Reporting additional statistics (e.g., medians, interquartile ranges) might be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is substantial. It tackles a critical problem in Bayesian inference with a creative and theoretically sound solution.
* The implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes the method accessible to a wide audience and facilitates further research and application.
* The potential impact of a rigorous, automated procedure for monitoring MCMC reliability is significant.
* The identified bug in WebPPL demonstrates the practical utility of the method beyond just convergence diagnostics.
* While not perfect, the experimental validation provides encouraging evidence that the method performs as expected.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of applicability (AIS/SMC only) restricts the immediate impact of the method.
* The reliance on simulated data and the associated assumptions introduce potential biases.
* The computational cost could be prohibitive for large-scale problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. While the paper has some limitations, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The authors have presented a compelling case for BREAD as a valuable tool for assessing MCMC reliability, and the implementation in popular probabilistic programming languages makes it readily available to the research community.  The paper doesn’t deliver a complete solution to the problem of MCMC validation, but it offers a significant step forward and opens up exciting new avenues for research.  I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to NIPS and has the potential to influence future research in Bayesian inference and probabilistic programming.  Future work could focus on extending the method to a wider class of MCMC algorithms, developing strategies for reducing computational cost, and providing more rigorous guarantees for the real-world data application protocol.
