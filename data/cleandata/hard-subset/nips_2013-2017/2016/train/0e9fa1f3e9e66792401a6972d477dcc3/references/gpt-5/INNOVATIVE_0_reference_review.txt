Summary
The paper builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide a quantitative measure of the reliability of MCMC-based inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-style samplers that can be viewed as importance sampling over extended state spaces. The main theoretical contribution shows that the gap between reverse and forward AIS log-normalizer estimates upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors also propose a pragmatic protocol to apply the method to real datasets by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data from those hyperparameters, and then using a reverse chain initialized from an approximate posterior over hyperparameters. They implement the method (“BREAD”) in WebPPL and Stan, validate the approach on toy cases where the true Jeffreys divergence can be computed, compare inference representations (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) across systems, and show a debugging use case that uncovered a bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) provided stochastic upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood using forward and reverse AIS given an exact posterior sample on simulated data. This paper leverages that mechanism to bound a posterior-sample quality metric (Jeffreys divergence) and analyzes the gap explicitly. It also situates itself relative to contemporaneous “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), clarifying that when the true posterior is used for the reverse initialization, their objective coincides with an upper bound on Jeffreys as shown here. The paper also discusses connections to classic convergence diagnostics, thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski equality, SMC, and Stein-based sample quality metrics.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Recasting BDMC’s log-ML sandwich as a quantitative certificate of posterior sample quality is a fresh and compelling idea. The Jeffreys divergence focus is principled and aligns with two-sided notions of “good sampling.”
- Practical impact: Implementations in Stan and WebPPL, with a clear protocol for real-data diagnostics, make this useful for practitioners and for probabilistic programming systems. The demonstration that BREAD can surface bugs is particularly convincing.
- Theoretical clarity: The derivation connecting the expected bidirectional gap to Jeffreys divergence is clean and builds naturally on the extended-state AIS view. The one-sided bounds with auxiliary upper/lower bounds are also useful.
- Empirical validation: Although modest, the toy problems where true Jeffreys is known support tightness, and the suite of Stan experiments (five models) supports the “simulated-from-fitted-hyperparameters” protocol as a proxy for real data. The matrix factorization case study provides actionable insight into representation choices across systems.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Expectation-only guarantee: The Jeffreys divergence bound holds in expectation over AIS randomness, not per run. The paper acknowledges heavy-tailed AIS weights and reports quartiles, but stops short of providing statistical confidence intervals or concentration results for the estimator of the expected gap. This weakens the direct “certificate” interpretation in finite computation.
- Scope restriction: The approach targets AIS/SMC-type methods with a tractable sequence and at least one exact posterior sample (available only for simulated data). While the real-data protocol is pragmatic, it is heuristic; there is no guarantee the approximate initialization does not bias the reverse chain in a way that meaningfully loosens the bound.
- Limited tightness assessment: Tightness is examined on small toy problems; beyond that, there is no quantitative assessment of how loose the bound can get in challenging regimes (e.g., strongly multimodal posteriors, high dimension) or how it scales with the annealing schedule and kernel choices.
- Experimental reporting: For the main diagnostic quantity (expected gap), the paper avoids averaging due to analysis concerns but still needs to present an estimator of the expectation with uncertainty. Using multiple runs to estimate E[gap] with error bars and possibly small-sample corrections (e.g., median-of-means) would make the claims more compelling.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The main derivation is correct and clearly extends BDMC’s framework. The empirical protocol is careful and the debugging story underscores rigor. The reliance on expectations and the lack of finite-sample uncertainty quantification is the main technical gap.

Clarity
Well-written and well-organized. The exposition of AIS/BDMC/BREAD is clear. Suggestions:
- Include a concise algorithm box for BREAD (forward and reverse runs and the diagnostic computation).
- Be explicit early that the Jeffreys bound is in expectation and explain how the reported numbers estimate that expectation.
- Summarize in the main text (not only appendices) the toy-problem tightness results with concrete numbers.

Originality
The idea to use the BDMC sandwich to upper bound Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples is original and timely. The real-data protocol and dual implementations in PPLs further contribute novelty in practice.

Significance
High practical significance for PPLs and MCMC practitioners: a broadly applicable, model-agnostic diagnostic for posterior sample quality in settings where calibration is notoriously difficult. The ability to guide model representation choices and catch implementation errors is impactful.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors provide a practical estimator for E[gap] with confidence intervals across multiple AIS runs? Even a non-rigorous bootstrap or MOM estimator would help users interpret results.
- How sensitive is the bound to the annealing schedule and transition kernel mixing? A short ablation (e.g., number of temperatures, HMC vs MH kernels) would be informative.
- Can the approach be demonstrated with SMC explicitly, including resampling, to emphasize generality beyond AIS?
- For the real-data protocol, can the authors bound or empirically assess the bias introduced by initializing the reverse chain from the fitted-hyperparameter posterior approximation?

Pros
- Clear, novel connection between BDMC and posterior sample quality (Jeffreys divergence).
- Useful, implementable tool for PPLs; demonstrated across Stan/WebPPL with concrete insights.
- Pragmatic real-data protocol; evidence that behavior matches simulated data.
- Reveals implementation bugs; actionable for system developers.

Cons
- Expectation-only bounds without finite-sample uncertainty quantification.
- Heuristic nature of real-data reverse initialization; no guarantees.
- Limited assessment of tightness in complex/high-dimensional scenarios.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some limitations in finite-sample guarantees and breadth of empirical tightness evaluation, the conceptual contribution is strong, broadly useful, and likely to influence practice in probabilistic programming and MCMC. My enthusiasm is driven by the novelty and practical utility of turning BDMC into a quantitative sample-quality diagnostic.

Confidence
High.
