- Extends BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence between AIS/SMC final-state distribution and the true posterior via gap ˆB = log(ˆRrev−1) − log(ˆR).
- Key theoretical result: E[ˆB] upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence J between qfwd(xT) and pT.
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates on regression, time series, clustering, and matrix factorization.
- Practical protocol for real data: simulate datasets at fitted hyperparameters and compare forward-AIS behavior with sanity checks.
- Honest framing: guarantees hold for simulated data with exact posterior samples; real-data use is heuristic.
- Conceptual strength: clean, elegant extension of BDMC linking biases of log ˆR and log ˆRrev to KLs and Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical relevance: exposes modeling and representation tradeoffs (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed) and helped find a WebPPL bug.
- Protocol strength: sensible parametric-bootstrap–style approach and useful sanity checks for real-data proxies.
- Theory quality: core inequalities and derivations (Eqs. 6–12) are correct and well-motivated.
- Implementation quality: convincing integrations with two PPLs and a practical debugging use case.
- Empirics: experiments are relevant but would benefit from Monte Carlo estimates of E[ˆB] with confidence intervals and discussion of run counts.
- Main limitation: the bound is in expectation only (E[ˆB] ≥ J); single-run gaps or quartiles are not formal guarantees.
- No high-probability/finite-sample CIs: variance of log-AIS estimators can be large, risking over-interpretation of single runs.
- Applicability restricted: requires IS-style reverse procedure and an exact posterior sample, so it does not directly validate standard MCMC like vanilla NUTS.
- Real-data protocol undermines guarantees when reverse chain is initialized from approximate posterior (brief MCMC), especially in multimodal/weakly identified/misspecified settings.
- Reverse SMC treatment is underspecified: precise reverse-with-resampling construction and proofs are missing.
- Limited tightness analysis: empirical tightness shown only on small toys; no conditions or bounds predicting when E[ˆB] tracks J.
- Sensitivity and tuning: gap depends on annealing schedule, number of temperatures, and kernels; practical guidance or adaptive optimization is lacking.
- Clarity suggestions: provide concise algorithmic summary, explicit statement that qfwd(xT) is AIS final-state distribution, and how many runs/CIs are needed.
- SMC applicability should be fully specified or deferred to appendix with proofs and algorithms.
- Suggested improvements: provide high-confidence bounds via multiple runs/concentration inequalities or one-sided CIs.
- Suggested improvements: offer guidance or optimization strategies for temperature schedules and step counts to control the gap.
- Suggested improvements: analyze effect of initialization quality and consider stronger reference distributions for real data.
- Suggested improvements: elaborate reverse SMC construction and test resampling-heavy SMC scenarios.
- Suggested improvements: include challenging multimodal/high-dimensional examples where standard diagnostics fail but BREAD detects divergence.
- Pros: solid theoretical contribution, practical implementations, diverse experiments, and clear utility for PPLs and debugging.
- Cons: expectation-only guarantees without finite-sample CIs, limited applicability beyond AIS/SMC and simulated-data settings, sparse theory on tightness and SMC reverse construction.
- Overall recommendation: strong, practically impactful paper recommended for acceptance, with suggested clarifications and additional empirical/theoretical development.
