

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method that bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors claim their method can be used to rigorously evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms like AIS and SMC, and integrate it into probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan. The paper builds on the work of Grosse et al. (2015) who introduced BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods, extending it to bound the Jeffreys divergence.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in probabilistic modeling: the difficulty of validating MCMC inference algorithms. The idea of using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is reasonable and well-motivated. The integration into WebPPL and Stan provides practical utility, and the authors demonstrate a concrete application in debugging a probabilistic programming language. The experimental validation on toy models and real-world datasets is a strength, showing the method can be applied in practice.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Citation Inconsistencies
The paper exhibits multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism. Citations are inconsistently formatted throughout (e.g., "GGA15" vs "Grosse et al., 2015"), and some references have unusual notations like "CGHL+ p" that appear to be typos. In the references section, journal names are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics" vs "Journal of the American Statistical Association"). The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript to ensure consistent citation and formatting throughout.

### Technical Inconsistencies
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that weaken its credibility. In Section 2.3, the authors state "it is typically infeasible to generate exact posterior samples" but then describe how exact posterior samples can be generated for simulated data, creating a contradiction. Equation (7) contains unclear notation: "DKL(qfwd(xT ) qfwd(v |xT ) k pT (xT ) qrev(v |xT ))" lacks proper punctuation to clarify the joint distribution being referenced. The definition of J in equation (12) as "DJ(pT(xT), qfwd(xT))" is confusing since DJ typically denotes the symmetric Jeffreys divergence, but the notation suggests a directional measure.

### Methodological Concerns
The paper claims BREAD is applicable to "algorithms which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC)," but fails to explain how the method extends to SMC. The relationship between AIS and SMC is mentioned but not developed, leaving a significant gap in the paper's scope. The protocol for using BREAD on real-world data (Section 3.2) makes a strong claim that "the true hyperparameters ˆ⌘real which generated D ought to be in a region of high posterior mass" without providing evidence or justification for this assertion.

### Missing Details
The paper states "We show that this upper bound is quite accurate on some toy distributions for which both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound can be computed exactly" (Section 1), but fails to provide specific details about these toy distributions or the accuracy of the bound. The paper references "Appendix B" but doesn't provide it in the submission, making it impossible to evaluate their claims about the toy distributions. The paper also claims "the reverse AIS curves were statistically indistinguishable" in Section 5.1.2 but provides no statistical tests or evidence to support this claim.

### Presentation Issues
The acronym "BREAD" is introduced without consistent spelling out (sometimes as "Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD)" and sometimes just as "BREAD"). The paper states "the prior is typically easy to sample from" without defining what "typically" means, which is problematic as this is not universally true. The paper also contains a grammatical error: "It is required that one can obtain one or more exact samples from p1.1" (the ".1" appears to be a typo).

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem and has practical value, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and lack of sufficient detail significantly undermine its credibility. The paper's claims about the accuracy of the bound on toy distributions cannot be verified due to missing details, and the methodological extensions to SMC and real-world data are not sufficiently justified. The technical flaws are substantial enough to prevent me from recommending acceptance at NIPS.

The paper has potential but requires significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. The authors should provide more precise technical details, resolve all inconsistencies, provide the missing appendix information, and strengthen the justification for their claims about the applicability to SMC and real-world data.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical flaws that prevent proper evaluation of the method's validity and significance.
