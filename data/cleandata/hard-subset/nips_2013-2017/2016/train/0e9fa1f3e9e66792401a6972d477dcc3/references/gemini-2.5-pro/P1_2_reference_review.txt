### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms. The work extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC provides an estimator that, in expectation, upper bounds the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior.

The method is directly applicable to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). A major limitation is that the reverse part of the procedure, which provides the upper bound, requires an exact sample from the target (posterior) distribution. This restricts the rigorous application of BREAD to simulated data, where such a sample can be obtained by sampling from the joint distribution. To bridge this gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; third, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset to diagnose inference quality.

The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They conduct experiments to validate their real-data protocol and use the tool to provide concrete scientific insights, such as comparing the efficiency of different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) for matrix factorization. As a powerful demonstration of its utility, they also report using BREAD to uncover a subtle bug in WebPPL's sampler.

This work builds directly on Neal's AIS (2001) and Grosse et al.'s BDMC (2015). It positions itself as a more rigorous alternative to traditional MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin), which can only detect non-convergence, and distinguishes itself from other sample quality measures (e.g., Gorham & Mackey, 2015) by not requiring assumptions like convexity. The authors also clearly situate their contribution with respect to the concurrent and closely related work of Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation in Section 3.1, connecting the expected BDMC bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence, is elegant and impactful. It transforms BDMC from a tool for estimating marginal likelihoods into a principled diagnostic for posterior approximation quality. This provides a much-needed rigorous foundation for MCMC evaluation, moving beyond heuristics.

2.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** Assessing the reliability of approximate inference is a fundamental and notoriously difficult problem for both practitioners and researchers. The lack of reliable diagnostics is a major bottleneck in the broader adoption of probabilistic programming. This paper offers a concrete, automated, and theoretically-grounded solution.

3.  **Thoughtful and Well-Validated Practical Protocol:** The paper is commendably transparent about the limitation of requiring simulated data. The proposed protocol to handle real-world data is sensible and well-motivated. The validation experiments in Section 5.1 are convincing; showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data (Sec 5.1.1) and that the reverse chain is robust to the approximate posterior initialization (Sec 5.1.2) significantly strengthens the paper's practical claims.

4.  **Compelling Experimental Results and Use Cases:** The experiments are not just toy validations. The analysis of collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization in Stan and WebPPL (Sec 5.2.1) is a perfect example of how BREAD can guide difficult modeling and algorithmic decisions. Furthermore, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL (Sec 5.2.2) is a powerful testament to the utility of having rigorous, mathematically-grounded tools for testing complex inference machinery.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is clear and concise, the core method is derived step-by-step, and the experimental setup and results are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Direct Applicability:** The primary weakness, which the authors acknowledge, is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD apply only to a specific class of algorithms (AIS/SMC) and only on simulated data. It cannot be directly used to bound the divergence of samples from a standard HMC or Gibbs sampler run on a real-world dataset. While the proposed protocol is a clever workaround, it relies on the assumption that inference on the simulated data is representative of inference on the real data, which may not always hold (e.g., in cases of severe model misspecification, as noted in the appendix).

2.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires running many forward and reverse AIS chains, which can be computationally intensive, potentially much more so than the original inference task. The paper does not analyze this overhead in detail. A discussion of the computational trade-offs would be valuable for practitioners considering adopting this method.

3.  **Minor Lack of Clarity on Experimental Reporting:** Footnote 3 states that because the theoretical analysis applies to individual chains, the authors report quartiles of estimates rather than averaging weights, which is standard practice for AIS. While this is a minor point, a brief justification in the main text for why the expectation of the average log estimate cannot be used, or why the bound on a single run's expectation is the most relevant quantity, would clarify the experimental methodology.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The central theoretical claim is well-supported by a clear derivation. The experimental work is thorough and carefully designed to validate the method's assumptions and demonstrate its utility. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured, making a complex topic accessible. Reproducibility is aided by the integration into open-source frameworks (Stan, WebPPL) and the provision of model details in the appendix.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using the BDMC gap to bound Jeffreys divergence is novel and significant. While building on existing work, this extension provides a new and powerful application for these tools. The paper also does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related and concurrent work.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. It provides a foundational tool for the rigorous evaluation of probabilistic inference systems, which is of great importance to the NIPS community. Researchers developing new inference algorithms, developers of probabilistic programming languages, and expert practitioners can all benefit from BREAD. The demonstrated use cases for model selection and debugging are compelling and likely to inspire follow-up work.

### Recommendation: **Accept**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. It addresses a critical, long-standing problem with a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical approach. The strengths—a clear theoretical insight, compelling experimental validation, and high potential impact—far outweigh the inherent limitations of the method, which the authors address transparently and thoughtfully. This work will be of great interest to the NIPS community and has the potential to become a standard tool for validating and debugging MCMC-based inference systems.
