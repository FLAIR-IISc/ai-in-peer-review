Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference in algorithms that can be seen as importance sampling on an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC). The main theoretical contribution shows that the gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS log normalizing constant estimators, B̂ = log R̂rev−1 − log R̂fwd, upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal sample distribution qfwd(xT) and the true target pT(x). This yields a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality for simulated datasets, where an exact posterior sample can be obtained by ancestral simulation. The paper also proposes a pragmatic protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on real data; simulate data from the fitted model; run BDMC on the simulated data; and check that forward-AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data. The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and evaluated across five models; it is used to study collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization and to detect a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical bridge from BDMC to a sample-quality metric: deriving E[B̂] ≥ DJ(qfwd, pT) is elegant, simple, and useful. The derivations (Eqs. 6–12) correctly leverage AIS’s extended-state importance sampling representation and KL contraction under marginalization.
- Practical relevance: a quantitative, interpretable measure to monitor approximate posterior sampling in probabilistic programming systems, with actionable insights (e.g., representation choices; debugging).
- Sound positioning vs. related work: connects to classical MCMC diagnostics (R̂, autocorrelations), Stein discrepancies, thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski equality, and concurrent “subjective divergence.”
- Implementation in two distinct PPLs and multi-model empirical exploration lend credibility and potential impact in practice.
- Honest discussion of limitations (need for exact posterior samples; only stochastic bounds; simulated-data setting) and an attempt to bridge to real data.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Expectation-only bound: The key guarantee is E[B̂] ≥ DJ, not B̂ ≥ DJ almost surely. Many plots appear to show single-run curves or quartiles. Using single realizations does not provide a rigorous bound on DJ; practitioners may misinterpret this. The paper should emphasize estimation of E[B̂] (with uncertainty), and ideally provide concentration bounds or aggregation schemes (e.g., median-of-means) to produce high-probability upper bounds on DJ from multiple runs.
- Real-data protocol lacks guarantees: The fitted-hyperparameter reverse initialization is heuristic. While the insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps S is reassuring on the tested models, there are no formal conditions under which the subjective reference approximates the true posterior closely enough for B̂ to remain informative. Failure modes (e.g., misspecification or weakly identified hyperparameters) are only lightly discussed.
- Experimental scope: Most models are relatively benign. Stronger evidence on overtly challenging targets (highly multimodal posteriors, heavy tails, high-dimensional hierarchical models) would better validate that DJ tracked by BREAD reflects mixing failures meaningfully.
- Limited comparison to alternative diagnostics: The paper does not empirically relate DJ estimates to standard metrics (R̂, ESS) or Stein discrepancies, missing an opportunity to show complementarity or superiority.
- AIS/SMC specifics matter: The bound pertains to the AIS terminal distribution qfwd, which depends on the annealing schedule, number and quality of intermediate kernels, and adaptation. The paper could be clearer on how kernels are tuned per temperature (especially for Stan/NUTS), the number of leapfrog steps, and the impact of path choices (geometric vs. moment averaging) on both estimator variance and DJ.
- Presentation issues: Several references are incomplete, typos remain, and some notational choices (tilde chains, “qfwd(xT)” as a marginal) could be clarified. The interplay of averaging over runs vs. per-chain behavior should be made explicit in figures.

Quality
- Theory is correct and builds directly on BDMC/AIS. The expectation inequality follows cleanly and the tightness investigation on toy models is a useful sanity check (though limited).
- Empirics are carefully executed within scope; the debugging example is compelling. However, stronger, more varied stress tests, runtime reporting, and uncertainty quantification for E[B̂] would improve the evidentiary basis.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with a good background on AIS, stochastic bounds, and BDMC. The new contribution is clearly delineated. The real-data protocol is clearly described but could benefit from an explicit algorithm box and discussion of caveats. Reference formatting and minor typos should be fixed.

Originality
- The use of BDMC’s bidirectional bounds to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples is a novel and valuable extension. The overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” is acknowledged; the distinction (true posterior vs. arbitrary reference) is clear.

Significance
- High practical significance for PPLs and MCMC practice: a quantitative, interpretable measure to monitor sampler reliability, guide modeling/algorithmic choices, and catch implementation bugs. The reliance on simulated data is a limitation, but the proposed protocol makes the approach usable for real applications in a diagnostic capacity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Emphasize and estimate E[B̂]: run multiple independent bidirectional chains, report mean and confidence intervals, and consider tail bounds (e.g., via Markov/Bennett or bootstrap) to provide high-probability statements about DJ.
- Add experiments on harder targets (e.g., mixture models with pronounced label switching, Ising/Potts models, funnel distributions) and show that BREAD detects non-convergence where standard diagnostics may be over-optimistic.
- Compare against R̂, ESS, and Stein discrepancy on the same runs; analyze correlations and cases of disagreement.
- Provide more implementation details: annealing schedules, number of temperatures, kernel adaptation per temperature, costs per run, and wall-clock/performance tradeoffs.
- Explore SMC instantiations, given the stated applicability, and compare against AIS.
- Clarify the limitations of the real-data heuristic; suggest diagnostics to detect when the fitted-hyperparameter strategy is unreliable (e.g., posterior predictive checks, sensitivity to priors).

Pros
- Elegant, correct theory; practically useful diagnostic.
- Implemented in two PPLs; produces scientific insights and finds real bugs.
- Clear relation to and extension of BDMC.

Cons
- Expectation-only bound may mislead if not aggregated properly.
- Real-data protocol lacks guarantees; limited stress testing and comparisons.
- Some presentation and reference issues.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and useful diagnostic with solid theory and promising practice. Strengthening empirical validation and clarifying the expectation-bound interpretation would make it an even more impactful contribution.
