### Summary
This paper proposes a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD) to quantitatively measure the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by MCMC algorithms. The method extends the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The key idea is that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the marginal likelihood, obtained from forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) runs, provides an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution and the true posterior. A significant limitation is that this procedure requires an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, which is only feasible for simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a protocol for real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters to obtain an exact posterior sample, and finally use BREAD on this simulated dataset, arguing that the inference behavior is consistent between the real and simulated settings. The method is implemented and tested in two probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, and used to compare different model representations for matrix factorization.

### Strengths
1.  **Principled Metric:** The paper provides a method to bound a meaningful and rigorous measure of sample quality—the Jeffreys divergence. This moves beyond traditional convergence diagnostics which can often only detect non-convergence rather than confirm the quality of the resulting approximation.
2.  **Sound Derivation:** The derivation in Section 3.1, showing that the expected gap between the BDMC bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, is clear and technically sound. It is an elegant extension of the analysis in the original BDMC paper [GGA15].
3.  **Practical Use Case:** The application of BREAD to compare the collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model (Section 5.2.1) is a compelling demonstration. It shows how the method can provide quantitative guidance on important modeling and algorithmic choices that are often made based on heuristics.
4.  **Implementation and Validation:** The authors have implemented their method in two widely used probabilistic programming frameworks (Stan and WebPPL) and performed a reasonable set of validation experiments on several models.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Fundamental Limitation to Simulated Data:** The primary weakness of this work is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD apply only to simulated data, where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a heuristic. It relies on the strong assumption that data simulated from point-estimates of hyperparameters will induce a posterior landscape that is representative of the one for the actual data. The validation in Section 5.1.1, based on visual similarity of AIS curves, is suggestive but not conclusive proof that this assumption holds in general. Model misspecification or complex posterior geometries in the real data could easily be missed by this protocol.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper positions itself as a new tool for validating MCMC inference, yet it completely omits any comparison or discussion of its relationship to standard MCMC convergence diagnostics, such as the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic ($\hat{R}$) or effective sample size (ESS). These methods are the bedrock of applied MCMC. A thorough evaluation should demonstrate what BREAD can uncover that these methods cannot, and vice versa. Without this context, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed method for practitioners who already rely on an established toolkit.
3.  **Incremental Novelty:** The core theoretical insight is a relatively straightforward extension of the BDMC framework presented in [GGA15], a recent paper by some of the same authors. While the application to bounding divergence is new and useful, the conceptual leap from the original work is modest.
4.  **Limited Applicability of the Core Method:** The proposed method is only directly applicable to algorithms like AIS or SMC. While the authors argue that insights gained from evaluating these algorithms can inform the design of more general MCMC methods, the tool itself cannot be directly applied to diagnose a standard Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampler run, limiting its direct scope.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a clever idea for quantifying the quality of approximate posterior distributions from certain MCMC methods. The theoretical foundation for the simulated data case is solid, and the experimental demonstration of its utility for model comparison is well-executed.

However, the work is hampered by significant limitations. The reliance on simulated data for rigorous guarantees is a major drawback, and the proposed heuristic for real-world data is not validated with sufficient rigor to inspire confidence. An inference diagnostic that works reliably only when we already know the answer (i.e., on simulated data where the ground truth is known) is of limited practical utility. The most significant failing of the paper is the absence of any comparison with standard, widely used convergence diagnostics. This omission makes it impossible to judge where BREAD fits into the existing landscape of tools and whether it offers a substantial advantage over established practice.

While the idea is interesting and could be valuable for researchers developing new inference algorithms or PPLs (as evidenced by the debugging example), its immediate value to the broader community of practitioners is questionable. The work feels more like a proof-of-concept for an interesting property of BDMC rather than a fully-fledged and validated diagnostic tool ready for widespread use. Given the unproven nature of its application to real-world data and the lack of comparison to standard baselines, the paper does not currently meet the high bar for acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, quantitative metric (an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence) for MCMC sample quality.
*   The derivation is sound and the method is demonstrated to be useful for algorithm/model design choices in a controlled setting.
*   Implementation in major PPLs is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The method's guarantees are confined to simulated data; the proposed protocol for real data is a heuristic with insufficient validation.
*   The paper fails to compare against or even discuss its relationship to established MCMC diagnostics (e.g., $\hat{R}$, ESS).
*   The novelty is somewhat incremental over the prior BDMC work.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
