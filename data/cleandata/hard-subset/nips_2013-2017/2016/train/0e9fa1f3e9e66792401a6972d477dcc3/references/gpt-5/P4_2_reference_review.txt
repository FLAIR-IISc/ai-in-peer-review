Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based approximate inference. The core idea is to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence (sum of forward and reverse KL) between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC-like algorithms and the true posterior, when an exact posterior sample is available (as on simulated data). The bound is the expected gap between stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood obtained via forward and reverse AIS. The authors also propose a practical protocol for real-world datasets: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data from the fitted model, and apply BDMC, initializing the reverse chain from an approximate posterior sample over hyperparameters obtained by a short MCMC run.

Contributions include: (1) deriving that the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate-sample distribution and the target posterior; (2) exact computation of both the true Jeffreys divergence and the bound on toy cases, showing reasonable tightness; (3) a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to transfer insights from simulated to real data; (4) integration into WebPPL and Stan, with empirical studies across five models and a case study comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization, and the discovery of an implementation bug in WebPPL.

The work is closely related to prior BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) which sandwiched the log marginal likelihood, AIS (Neal, 2001), and non-equilibrium work relations (Jarzynski, 1997). It complements convergence diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin, Brooks-Gelman) and recent sample-quality measures such as Stein discrepancies (Gorham and Mackey, NeurIPS 2015). Concurrent work (Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016) proposes “subjective divergence,” equivalent to the BDMC gap when initialized from the true posterior; this paper’s analysis shows the Jeffreys divergence upper bound in expectation for that special case and contrasts the use of reference initializations.

Quality:
- Theory: The derivation that E[log R_hat] differs from log ZT/Z1 by DKL terms, and that the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate-sample and target distributions, is sound and follows standard KL properties (invariance under marginalization). The guarantees are rigorous for simulated data when reverse AIS starts from an exact posterior sample.
- Practical protocol: For real data, the fitted-hyperparameter procedure is heuristic; the reverse chain starts from an approximate posterior sample rather than an exact one, so no formal guarantees hold. The empirical validation across five models suggests robustness: forward AIS behavior aligns between real and simulated datasets; reverse AIS estimates are insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to initialize hyperparameters.
- Empirics: The experiments are diverse (linear/logistic regression, AR, mixture of Gaussians, matrix factorization) and implemented in both WebPPL and Stan. The matrix factorization study demonstrates that BREAD provides actionable guidance on representation choices and can reveal implementation bugs. However, quantitative analysis of estimator variance/concentration is limited; the bound is only in expectation, and the paper notes heavy-tailed behavior of AIS log-weights, reporting quartiles rather than means.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The background on AIS/BDMC is concise, and the derivation is readable. The real-data protocol is explained, and the experimental setup is described, with pointers to appendices for details and code snippets. Suggestions for clarity: provide more guidance on annealing schedules, number of intermediates, and number of AIS repeats required to estimate the expectation reliably; summarize toy examples where the true Jeffreys divergence is computed exactly in the main text.

Originality:
The extension of BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples is novel and useful. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol and integration into two PPLs enhance practical relevance. Concurrent related work on subjective divergence is acknowledged; the present paper’s framing and empirical validation are still distinct and valuable.

Significance:
The method provides a principled, quantitative diagnostic for AIS/SMC-style approximate inference and for design choices in probabilistic programming. It can detect bugs, compare model representations, and offer a measure aligned with posterior approximation quality. Limitations are notable: applicability requires AIS/SMC-like samplers and exact posterior samples (or approximate ones without guarantees) and yields only an expected bound. Still, the potential for informing practice in PPLs and MCMC tuning makes this contribution meaningful.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical result: expected BDMC gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence for simulated data.
- Practical protocol to relate simulated to real data; validation across diverse models.
- Demonstrated utility: guides representation choice; reveals implementation bugs.
- Integration in major PPLs (WebPPL, Stan); relevance to automatic inference.

Cons:
- Guarantee is only in expectation; no concentration analysis or finite-sample confidence guidance.
- Real-data protocol lacks formal guarantees; reliance on fitted hyperparameters and approximate reverse initialization.
- Tightness of the bound is shown only on toy problems; no quantitative tightness assessment on realistic, high-dimensional models.
- Limited discussion on sensitivity to annealing schedules, transition kernels, and computational cost.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide empirical or theoretical concentration results for the estimator of the expected gap (e.g., number of AIS runs needed for a reliable estimate; variance reduction strategies).
- Analyze sensitivity to annealing schedules and propose automatic tuning.
- Quantify tightness on more challenging benchmarks where approximate ground-truth divergences are available (e.g., tempered posteriors or small exact models).
- Clarify computational costs and report wall-clock results alongside step counts.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce all experiments.

Recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper presents a sound and useful extension of BDMC, with practical impact for evaluating MCMC-based inference in PPLs. Despite expectation-only guarantees and heuristic aspects for real data, the empirical validation and demonstrated utility justify acceptance.
