- Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between approximate MCMC samples and the true posterior.  
- Shows theoretically that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Proposes a practical protocol for real-world data: simulate data from fitted hyperparameters and use approximate posterior samples to initialize reverse chains.  
- Validates BREAD by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan and testing on multiple probabilistic models.  
- Demonstrates practical utility by using BREAD to detect a bug in WebPPL’s multivariate Gaussian sampling.  
- Builds on and differentiates from prior work on BDMC, marginal likelihood estimation, and inference evaluation.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical derivations supporting the expected upper bound on divergence.  
- Strength: addresses a key practical problem—assessing approximate posterior quality, especially for probabilistic programming users.  
- Strength: real-world validation and concrete experiments increase credibility and utility.  
- Strength: provides a clear, validated protocol for applying the method to real datasets.  
- Strength: novel use of BDMC for measuring inference quality rather than only marginal likelihoods.  
- Strength: thoughtful comparison with related work (e.g., CTM16).  
- Weakness: applicable only to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended state spaces (e.g., AIS/SMC), excluding many MCMC methods.  
- Weakness: potentially high computational cost from running both forward and reverse AIS chains, which may limit scalability.  
- Weakness: relies on simulated data to obtain exact posterior samples, posing challenges for complex models.  
- Weakness: insufficient quantification of how using approximate posterior samples in reverse AIS affects the upper bound.  
- Weakness: sparse implementation details for integrating BREAD into probabilistic programming frameworks.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear derivations and comprehensive experimental validation.  
- Clarity: well-written, well-organized, with clear background and reproducible experimental details, though some implementation specifics are missing.  
- Originality: a significant and novel extension of BDMC to measure inference reliability and a new protocol for real-world application.  
- Significance: addresses an important practical problem and provides a useful tool for probabilistic programming and model design choices.  
- Recommendation: strong accept—contribution outweighs limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a practical problem, has rigorous guarantees for simulated data, validated in real frameworks, and is well-differentiated from prior work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited algorithmic applicability, potential computational expense, dependence on simulated data, and limited implementation detail.  
- Suggested improvements: analyze computational cost scaling in Section 3.2 and generalization of representation tradeoffs in Section 5.2.1.  
- Suggested improvements: discuss extensions for when exact posterior samples are unavailable and potential directions for future work.  
- Suggested improvements: provide more implementation details on initializing reverse AIS with approximate posterior samples in WebPPL and Stan.
