

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework (Grosse et al., 2015) to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, applicable to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The work is motivated by the challenge of rigorously validating MCMC inference, which remains difficult despite its widespread use in probabilistic modeling.

The main technical contribution is a refined analysis of BDMC that derives an estimator upper bounding the Jeffreys divergence. The authors demonstrate the accuracy of this upper bound on toy distributions where the true Jeffreys divergence can be computed exactly. They also propose a practical protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data: fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data from these hyperparameters, and evaluating inference quality on the simulated data. This protocol is validated across multiple probabilistic models in both WebPPL and Stan, showing consistent inference behavior between real and simulated data.

The paper makes clear distinctions from related work, particularly the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which uses a similar BDMC framework but with a different reference distribution. The authors also discuss connections to convergence diagnostics for MCMC, noting that BREAD provides rigorous guarantees where traditional diagnostics can only identify particular forms of non-convergence.

The paper demonstrates significant practical value through integration with two probabilistic programming frameworks. BREAD is used to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) in matrix factorization models, revealing language-specific performance differences. The authors also identify a bug in WebPPL through BREAD's theoretical guarantees, demonstrating its utility for debugging inference implementations.

In terms of technical soundness, the theoretical derivations are clear and well-motivated. The experimental validation is thorough, addressing key concerns about the accuracy of the upper bound and the validity of the real-world data protocol. The authors carefully consider potential pitfalls, such as the use of approximate posterior samples for reverse chain initialization, and validate this approach across five different models.

The novelty lies in extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to measuring inference reliability, and developing a practical protocol for real-world data. While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, the authors clearly demonstrate how their extension provides a new tool for evaluating posterior inference quality. The paper distinguishes itself from previous NIPS papers on convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks and Gelman, 1998) by providing rigorous bounds rather than heuristic diagnostics.

The significance of the work is substantial. Probabilistic programming languages have the potential to democratize sophisticated probabilistic modeling, but their adoption is hindered by the inability to assess inference reliability. BREAD provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, directly addressing this limitation.

Potential weaknesses include the method's limitation to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC), which may exclude some common MCMC approaches. Additionally, the real-world data protocol relies on an approximation (using approximate posterior samples for reverse chain initialization), though the authors validate this approximation across multiple models. The paper could better address scalability to high-dimensional problems, which is a common challenge in MCMC.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with strong connections between theoretical contributions and practical implementation. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the integration with probabilistic programming frameworks demonstrates real-world utility.

Strengths: Strong theoretical foundation, clear practical application, thorough experimental validation, meaningful contribution to probabilistic programming.

Weaknesses: Limited to specific inference algorithms, real-world protocol involves approximation.

Pro/Con for acceptance:
- Pro: Novel extension of BDMC to measure inference reliability, strong theoretical basis, practical implementation in popular frameworks, thorough validation.
- Con: Limited to AIS/SMC algorithms, real-world protocol relies on approximation.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for evaluating MCMC inference quality, addressing a fundamental challenge in probabilistic modeling. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical utility. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
