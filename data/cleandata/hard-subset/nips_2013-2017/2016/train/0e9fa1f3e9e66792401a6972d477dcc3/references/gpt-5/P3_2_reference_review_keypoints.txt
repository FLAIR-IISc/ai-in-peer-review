- Extends BDMC to quantify MCMC posterior-sample reliability via the forward/reverse AIS log-marginal-likelihood gap (BREAD) that upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Requires exact posterior samples for reverse runs, so focuses on simulated data and proposes a “fitted hyperparameters” heuristic for real data.  
- Integrated BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validated on toy problems and five models, used to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization, and uncovered a WebPPL bug.  
- Builds on BDMC, reverse-AIS, thermodynamic integration, and is closely related to subjective divergence work, but emphasizes exact posterior initialization for guarantees.  
- Contrasts with Stein discrepancies and standard MCMC diagnostics by providing quantitative accuracy guarantees on simulated data.  
- Theoretical contribution: shows E[log R̂_rev−1 − log R̂] ≥ DKL(p||q)+DKL(q||p), connecting marginal likelihood sandwiching to posterior sample quality.  
- Practical contribution: implementation in two PPLs and case studies that demonstrate how BREAD can inform modeling choices.  
- Empirical finding: simulated-data behavior matched real-data AIS behavior in reported settings, supporting the fitted-hyperparameters protocol empirically.  
- Negative capability: method is useful for debugging and verification, as evidenced by finding a subtle implementation bug.  
- Key limitation: the bound holds only in expectation over AIS randomness, so single-run quartiles do not provide high-probability guarantees and heavy tails can widen the gap.  
- Reliance on exact posterior samples limits formal claims to simulated data; the fitted-hyperparameters approach for real data is heuristic and lacks theoretical error control.  
- Claimed generality to SMC and other extended-state IS algorithms is not demonstrated empirically, and SMC-specific complications (resampling, path definitions) are unaddressed.  
- Limited robustness analysis: insufficient exploration of multimodality, poor tempering schedules, or weakly mixing kernels where AIS typically struggles.  
- Estimation practice underexplored: the paper doesn’t specify how many forward/reverse runs are needed or how to obtain robust estimates and uncertainties under heavy tails.  
- Minor theoretical clarity issues: need clearer statements about expectations (over which randomness) and the role of single fixed posterior samples in unbiasedness arguments.  
- Technical quality: sound AIS derivation using extended-state arguments and data-processing inequality; experiments helpful but scope-limited.  
- Clarity: generally well-written with clear notation and intuition, but contains citation placeholders, typos, and needs clearer guidance on expectation estimation.  
- Originality: an incremental but neat repurposing of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence, with useful engineering contributions; overlap with subjective divergence is acknowledged.  
- Significance: valuable to PPL and MCMC communities for quantifying AIS-like sampler quality and guiding choices, though constrained by expectation-only and simulated-data requirements.  
- Suggested improvements: give empirical guidance on number of runs and robust mean estimation, provide confidence intervals, and stress-test on multimodal/poorly tempered targets.  
- Recommend either add SMC experiments or temper generality claims and discuss how resampling affects q_fwd(x_T) and the bound.  
- For fitted-hyperparameters: quantify mismatch between reference and true posterior (e.g., Stein discrepancy) or adopt subjective-divergence framing to report extra error terms.  
- Minor fixes requested: repair citation placeholders, add means with robust CIs in figures, and release code/scripts for reproducibility.  
- Overall recommendation: lean accept, contingent on strengthening empirical methodology for expectation estimation and better scoping of SMC/generalization claims.
