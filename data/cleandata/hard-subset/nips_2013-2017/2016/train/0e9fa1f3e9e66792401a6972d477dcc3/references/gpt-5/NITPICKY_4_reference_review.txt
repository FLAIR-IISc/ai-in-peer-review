Summary
The paper proposes BREAD, an application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the accuracy of MCMC-based inference (notably AIS/SMC viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space). The key theoretical claim is that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log-partition estimates upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the target posterior pT and the AIS output distribution qfwd. The paper integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, introduces a practical protocol for real data via simulation from fitted hyperparameters, validates behavior on several models, analyzes representation choices for a matrix factorization model, and reports a bug caught in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
Builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and RA (Burda et al., 2015). The connection to Gorham & Mackey (Stein discrepancies) and classical MCMC diagnostics is discussed. The overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) is acknowledged; the main difference is using an exact posterior sample for tightness and guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual extension: from marginal likelihood benchmarking to expected bounds on Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples.
- Practical insight: an implementable procedure for AIS/SMC in two PPLs, with a sensible protocol to mimic real data via fitted hyperparameters.
- Potential impact: a rare attempt at providing quantitative guarantees (albeit in expectation) for sample quality; could guide model representation choices and catch implementation bugs.
- Honest discussion of limitations and related work, including simultaneous independent work.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality/technical rigor
- The main guarantee is only “in expectation” over AIS randomness. The paper uses single-run or small-sample empirical summaries (quartiles) but does not provide finite-sample, high-probability bounds for the Jeffreys divergence or for the estimator gap. Without concentration results, practitioners cannot know when a realized gap is actually informative. Given the heavy tails of AIS weights, this is a substantial limitation.
- The paper discourages averaging weights for the BREAD estimator but then relies on quartiles/visual alignment. This is ad hoc. A rigorous estimator of E[ˆB], confidence intervals, or a tail bound (perhaps via multiple independent runs, Markov/Chebychev bounds, or self-normalized importance-weight stabilizations) is needed.
- The theoretical development for SMC is claimed but not detailed. Assumptions for kernels, resampling, and the exact form of the reverse chain (including path distributions) should be stated explicitly; a formal SMC variant of Eqs. (6)–(11) is missing.
- The “fitted hyperparameters” protocol breaks the guarantee (no exact sample). The empirical check (varying S) is suggestive but not a proof. You should either (a) provide conditions under which this induces a controlled bias, (b) propose a principled reference distribution with a bound on the additional error, or (c) use models with conjugacy where exact posterior sampling is feasible to demonstrate guarantees on realistic problems.
- No analysis of schedule design (temperatures), number of intermediates, or transition kernels’ effect on bound tightness. Since tightness depends critically on annealing, this omission is nontrivial.
- The paper compares only qualitatively with standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS, autocorrelation) and Stein discrepancies; quantitative comparisons are absent. Even if scopes differ, showing practical complementarity would strengthen significance.
- Evaluation of bound tightness is limited to “toy distributions” (Appendix B). No quantitative tightness results are reported on the realistic models; figures are described but not numerically summarized (e.g., average ˆB vs. J or surrogate).

Significance
- The scope is limited: guarantees hold only for simulated data with an exact posterior sample; the proposed real-data workaround is heuristic. This constrains applicability.
- The novelty relative to BDMC and RA is incremental: the main inequality is a straightforward application of KL decomposition and data processing (marginalization), and the practical contribution is more systems/engineering. Given concurrent work (subjective divergence), the distinct added value should be sharpened.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical/formatting errors impede readability and credibility:
  - Broken citations placeholders: “[GS; CGHL+ p]”, “[LTBS00; GMRB+08]”, “\Q2015\E”, escaped punctuation “\.”, inconsistent author-year formats; missing years and venues.
  - Equation (1) typesetting mangled (missing equals, subscripts/spaces: “wt wt 1 ft(xt 1)/ft 1(xt 1)”).
  - Multiple notation issues: e.g., “qfwd(pT)” (should be qfwd(xT)); “p̃t = pT t+1” unclear; “t 2” for t ≥ 2; commas as assignment (e.g., “J , DJ(…) , …”).
  - Footnote 1 references an exact sampler but is not properly placed; dangling notes.
  - References list contains placeholders, inconsistent capitalization, missing details, and inconsistent abbreviations.
- Experimental details are incomplete: exact T, number of temperatures, schedule, kernel choices, acceptance rates, ESS, wall-clock vs. iteration runtime, number of repeated runs for reverse AIS, seeds, and computational budgets are not systematically reported. The reliance on figures without numerical tables makes reproducibility difficult.

Experimental evaluation
- Validation on five models is welcome, but the main outcomes are qualitative (overlapping quartiles) rather than quantitative bounds on divergence or convergence.
- The matrix factorization study is interesting but reports conclusions without sufficient runtime accounting and without sensitivity analyses (e.g., K, N, D scaling, different priors). It’s unclear how general the conclusion is.
- The detection of a WebPPL bug is a nice anecdote, but it also underscores how brittle the method can be if exact posterior sampling is compromised.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide finite-sample high-probability bounds for J based on multiple BDMC runs, perhaps via concentration inequalities for log AIS estimators and a union bound?
2. For SMC, what is the precise reverse-chain construction and unbiasedness statement you rely on? Please include a formal derivation.
3. Can you report quantitative tightness (E[ˆB] vs. J) on moderately sized, realistic models with conjugacy to compute J?
4. How sensitive are your results to the temperature schedule and number of intermediates? Can BREAD be used to adapt schedules?
5. For the fitted-hyperparameter protocol, can you bound the bias in terms of TV/KL between the true posterior and the reference distribution used to initialize the reverse chain?

Pros
- Clear conceptual framing and useful extension of BDMC.
- Implementations in two PPLs; practical relevance; found a real bug.
- Potentially useful for model representation choices.

Cons
- Only expectation-level guarantees; no finite-sample control.
- Heuristic real-data protocol without theoretical support.
- Limited and mostly qualitative experimental validation; no quantitative tightness in realistic settings.
- Significant clarity/formatting/notation issues and incomplete methodological details.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising, but the current paper lacks rigor in finite-sample guarantees, has incomplete experimental quantification, and suffers from substantial presentation problems. With stronger theoretical control of the estimator, clearer SMC derivations, quantitative tightness studies on realistic models, and cleaned-up writing and references, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence: High.
