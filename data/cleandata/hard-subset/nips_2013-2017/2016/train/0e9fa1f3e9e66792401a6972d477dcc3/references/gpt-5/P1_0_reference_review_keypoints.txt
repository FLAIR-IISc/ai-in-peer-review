- Paper extends BDMC to assess MCMC-based approximate posterior sampling via bidirectional AIS (BREAD), producing stochastic lower and upper bounds on log partition ratios.
- Main theoretical result: the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS’s sample distribution and the true posterior.
- Practical protocol: infer hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from those hyperparameters, validate AIS behavior matches between simulated and real data, and initialize reverse AIS from an approximate hyperparameter posterior.
- Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validated on five models (regression, time series, mixture models, matrix factorization).
- Empirical findings include collapsed vs. uncollapsed representation comparisons and a debugging case that uncovered a WebPPL bug.
- Builds directly on BDMC and AIS, leveraging standard importance-sampling identities and KL contraction arguments.
- Related to prior “subjective divergence” work and connects to thermodynamic integration, Jarzynski equality, and convergence diagnostics.
- Strength: provides a clear, general expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence linking partition-function estimation to sample-quality assessment.
- Strength: practical integration into popular PPLs with a usable protocol for real-data validation via simulation.
- Strength: empirical validation across multiple models and a robustness study for approximate hyperparameter posterior initialization.
- Strength: yields actionable insights (e.g., representation choices) and proved useful for debugging implementations.
- Weakness: guarantee is in expectation only—single-run gap need not upper-bound Jeffreys divergence and there is no high-probability control.
- Weakness: applicability limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers and requires simulated data/exact posterior samples for tight upper bounds.
- Weakness: limited prescriptive guidance on choosing annealing schedules, number of steps, and kernels to obtain tight/stable estimates.
- Weakness: limited quantitative characterization of bound tightness across regimes like high dimension or multimodality.
- Weakness: experiments focus on AIS; empirical extension to SMC variants and complex bridging schemes is unclear.
- Quality: theoretical derivation is sound and concise, experimental methodology is careful, but lacks concentration analyses and tighter statistical treatment of repeated runs.
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized; suggests adding full citations, pseudocode for BREAD, and explicit tuning guidance.
- Originality: incremental theoretical novelty over BDMC but a useful and practical twist bounding sample quality via Jeffreys divergence.
- Significance: offers an implementable, model-agnostic diagnostic tool for AIS/SMC inference with practical utility despite expectation-only guarantees.
- Suggestions: provide concentration results or empirical intervals for E[log R], systematic tightness studies across synthetic families, guidance/heuristics for schedule design (e.g., adaptive spacing), study alternative reference distributions, and report computational overheads.
- Pros summary: clear theoretical link, practical PPL integration, empirical validation, and real utility for debugging and modeling choices.
- Cons summary: expectation-only guarantees, restricted applicability, limited tuning guidance, and modest analysis of tightness.
- Overall recommendation: Accept (score 7/10) with confidence 4/5.
