### Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of certain Monte Carlo inference algorithms. The core problem it addresses is the long-standing difficulty of diagnosing the convergence and accuracy of MCMC samplers, for which current methods are largely heuristic. The proposed method builds upon Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique for estimating log marginal likelihoods by sandwiching the true value between stochastic upper and lower bounds derived from forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) runs.

The key theoretical contribution is to show that the gap between these upper and lower bounds provides an estimator that, in expectation, upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution produced by AIS and the true posterior. This provides a quantitative measure of sample quality. Since the reverse AIS procedure requires an exact sample from the posterior, this guarantee is strictly valid only for simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world datasets: first, estimate hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters; and finally, run BREAD on this simulated data to assess inference quality, validating that the sampler's behavior is consistent between the real and simulated datasets.

The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They conduct a thorough set of experiments to validate the approach and demonstrate its utility on several models. The experiments show that the bound is reasonably tight, that inference behavior on simulated data can be a good proxy for real data, and that the method can be used to provide concrete guidance on modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and for debugging inference implementations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation connecting the BDMC log-likelihood gap to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1) is sound, elegant, and clearly presented. The experimental work is thorough and convincing. The authors don't just present their method but also carefully validate its key assumptions: that the bound is informative in practice (Appendix B), that inference on realistically simulated data is a good proxy for real data (Section 5.1.1), and that their proposed heuristic for initializing the reverse chain on real-data problems is robust (Section 5.1.2). The use cases are strong evidence of the method's practical value.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear background on AIS and BDMC before introducing their own contribution. The motivation is clear, the methods are described precisely, and the experimental results are presented with informative figures and clear takeaways. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand the contributions and their context.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the key insight—reinterpreting the log-likelihood gap as a bound on posterior approximation error—is novel and significant. This shifts the focus of BDMC from a tool for model comparison (via marginal likelihoods) to a powerful diagnostic tool for inference algorithms. The protocol for applying the method to real-world data is also a novel and pragmatic contribution. The authors appropriately situate their work relative to classical MCMC diagnostics and concurrent work [CTM16].

**Significance:** The paper's potential impact is very high. The problem of assessing inference quality is fundamental to the entire field of probabilistic machine learning and its application. BREAD represents a major step forward from existing heuristic diagnostics (like R-hat), providing a more principled and quantitative measure of accuracy. By integrating the method into popular PPLs like Stan and WebPPL, the authors have created a tool that can be immediately used by a large community of researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated applications in comparing model representations and debugging a PPL implementation highlight its practical utility for improving both the practice and the engineering of probabilistic inference.

Despite these significant strengths, the work has a few limitations, which are generally well-acknowledged by the authors:

1.  **Limited Applicability to Algorithm Class:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which can be framed as importance sampling over an extended space and produce unbiased marginal likelihood estimates. It does not apply to more standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS directly. While the authors argue that insights can be transferred, this remains a significant limitation.
2.  **Reliance on Simulated Data for Guarantees:** The rigorous theoretical guarantees hold only for simulated data where an exact posterior sample is accessible. The proposed protocol for real data is a sensible and well-motivated heuristic, but it is a heuristic nonetheless. Its success depends on the model being well-specified and on the initial hyperparameter estimates being reasonable.
3.  **Computational Cost:** The method is computationally intensive, requiring multiple runs of AIS in both forward and reverse directions. AIS itself is often much more costly than a standard MCMC run. This high cost might limit its routine use, restricting it to smaller models or final validation checks rather than interactive development.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   Addresses a fundamental and long-standing problem in probabilistic inference with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   Provides a quantitative measure of sample quality (an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence), which is a significant advance over existing heuristic diagnostics.
*   The work is validated with a strong and comprehensive set of experiments.
*   The demonstrated use cases for comparing model representations and debugging PPLs are compelling and show high practical value.
*   Implementation in widely-used PPLs makes the contribution immediately accessible and useful to the community.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

### Arguments against Acceptance
*   The rigorous guarantees are confined to simulated data, and the application to real data relies on a heuristic protocol.
*   The method is only directly applicable to a specific class of inference algorithms (AIS/SMC), not the most commonly used MCMC methods.
*   The computational cost could be a barrier to widespread adoption for large-scale problems.

### Constructive Suggestions

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance. The limitations are inherent to the problem's difficulty and are clearly articulated. My suggestions are minor and intended to further strengthen the paper.

1.  **Clarification on Averaging Samples:** Footnote 3 mentions that the analysis in Section 3.1 does not justify averaging weights across multiple AIS chains and instead reports quantiles of estimates from individual chains. This is an important practical detail. Could the authors elaborate on this point? For estimating `log Z`, one typically computes `log(mean(w_T))` over multiple runs. It would be helpful to discuss if a similar multi-sample estimator for the Jeffreys divergence bound `E[B]` exists and what its properties would be. If not, a brief explanation of the theoretical obstacle would be valuable.
2.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the computational cost is an implicit limitation, a brief, explicit discussion of the trade-offs would be helpful for practitioners. For example, some guidance on the number of intermediate distributions and chains required to get a reliable estimate of the bound would be useful context.
3.  **Model Misspecification:** The real-data protocol implicitly assumes the model is well-specified. The authors briefly mention an example of this failing in the appendix. It might be worth highlighting in the main text that a failure of the forward AIS curves to match between real and simulated data could itself be a useful diagnostic for model misspecification.
