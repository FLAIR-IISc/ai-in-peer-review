### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantifying the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The central idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally used for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The authors derive a key theoretical result: the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior.

Since this method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse AIS chain, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset to diagnose inference quality. The paper validates this protocol and demonstrates the utility of BREAD by integrating it into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They use it to compare different model representations for matrix factorization and to uncover a bug in WebPPL's implementation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Problem:** The paper addresses a fundamental and long-standing challenge in Bayesian inference: the lack of reliable, quantitative diagnostics for MCMC convergence. Existing methods like Gelman-Rubin are heuristic and can fail to detect certain types of non-convergence. BREAD provides a more principled measure of sample quality, directly tied to a standard statistical divergence.

2.  **Elegant Theoretical Contribution:** The core technical result in Section 3.1 is simple but powerful. The derivation showing that the expected gap `E[B]` between the forward and reverse AIS log-ML estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is a clever and non-obvious extension of the original BDMC analysis. It elegantly connects the difficulty of partition function estimation to the quality of the samples produced by the algorithm.

3.  **Practical Utility and Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The authors go beyond theory by implementing BREAD in two popular PPLs (Stan and WebPPL). The experiments are well-designed and convincing.
    *   The validation of the "real-data" protocol (Section 5.1) is thoughtful and provides confidence that insights from simulated data can be relevant to real-world problems. The consistency between forward AIS curves on real and simulated data is a compelling piece of evidence.
    *   The use case in Section 5.2.1 (comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers) is an excellent example of how BREAD can guide practical modeling and algorithm design decisions, which are often made based on intuition or expensive trial-and-error.
    *   The debugging example in Section 5.2.2 is a powerful testament to the method's utility. A diagnostic that is sensitive enough to find subtle implementation bugs is extremely valuable for developers of PPLs.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background on AIS and BDMC is clear and concise. The derivation of the main result is easy to follow, and the motivation for each step of the work is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Applicability of the Core Method:** The rigorous bound only applies to algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. It does not directly apply to more standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS run in their typical "long-run" mode. The authors acknowledge this, arguing that insights will generalize, but this is a significant limitation on the direct applicability of the method.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data application (Section 3.2) breaks the rigorous guarantees of BREAD. Initializing the reverse chain from an *approximate* posterior sample means that the reverse estimate is no longer a guaranteed stochastic upper bound on the log-ML. While the experiments in 5.1.2 suggest this approximation is robust in practice (the reverse estimates are insensitive to the number of burn-in steps S), it is a crucial departure from the theory. The paper is honest about this being a "heuristic scheme," but the implications could be discussed more deeply. For instance, it's not clear under what conditions this approximation might fail.

3.  **Potential Looseness of the Bound:** The derivation in Section 3.1 uses the property that marginalizing variables cannot increase KL divergence (the data processing inequality). This step introduces a slack in the bound. While Appendix B shows the bound is tight on some toy examples, it's unclear how loose this bound might become for high-dimensional, complex posteriors, which are precisely the cases where diagnostics are most needed. Some discussion on the factors that might influence the tightness of the bound would be valuable.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivation is sound, and the claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments. The work is self-contained and complete. The authors are commendably careful in distinguishing between the rigorous guarantees on simulated data and the heuristic nature of the real-data protocol.

*   **Clarity:** The writing is excellent. A minor point of clarification could be made in Section 5.1.1 regarding the vertical shifting of the AIS estimate plots. The text says they are shifted "based on the largest forward AIS estimate." A more principled justification (e.g., aligning medians of the final-step estimates) might be more robust and convincing than relying on a potentially noisy maximum value.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the crucial insight to use the log-ML gap as a bound on sample divergence is novel. The connection is not obvious and represents a significant conceptual advance. The relationship to the concurrent work of [CTM16] is well-described and does not diminish the contribution; rather, it suggests the timeliness and importance of the idea.

*   **Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. It provides a much-needed tool for a critical problem in a rapidly growing field (probabilistic programming). Researchers developing new inference algorithms, practitioners applying PPLs to scientific problems, and developers building PPL systems could all benefit from BREAD. It has the potential to become a standard tool for validating and comparing AIS/SMC-based inference engines.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in MCMC diagnostics with a novel, theoretically-grounded approach.
*   The connection between the BDMC gap and Jeffreys divergence is a beautiful and important insight.
*   Strong empirical validation, including integration into major PPLs and compelling use cases for model comparison and debugging.
*   High potential for impact on both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and statistics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method's rigorous guarantees are confined to simulated data and a specific class of samplers (AIS/SMC).
*   The protocol for applying it to real data is heuristic, which somewhat undermines the central promise of a "rigorous" diagnostic.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The weaknesses are primarily limitations of scope rather than flaws in the work itself, and the authors are transparent about them. The strengths—originality, theoretical elegance, and practical utility—far outweigh these limitations. This work is likely to be highly influential and widely cited. It is a clear accept.
