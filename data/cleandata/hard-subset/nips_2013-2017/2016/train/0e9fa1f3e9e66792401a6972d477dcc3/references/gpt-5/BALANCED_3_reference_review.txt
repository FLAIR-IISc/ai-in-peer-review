Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) from marginal likelihood benchmarking to assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-style samplers viewed as importance sampling over extended state spaces. The key observation is that the gap between a forward AIS stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1 and a reverse-AIS-derived stochastic upper bound (enabled by an exact posterior sample on simulated data) upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal-state distribution and the true target. The method, termed BREAD, provides a quantitative, model- and algorithm-aware measure of approximate posterior quality.

Because exact posterior draws are available via ancestral sampling on simulated data, the paper proposes a protocol to use BREAD to guide inference quality on real data: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from the fitted hyperparameters, and compare AIS behavior (and bounds) between real and simulated data; initialize the reverse chain using a short MCMC run from the fitted point to approximate the needed posterior draw. The approach is integrated into WebPPL and Stan. Experiments include (i) tightness checks on toy distributions, (ii) five realistic models (linear/logistic regression, AR time series, matrix factorization, GMM) showing alignment between real and simulated data, (iii) robustness of reverse AIS to the number of MCMC steps used to initialize from the fitted hyperparameters, (iv) case studies comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization in both systems, and (v) a debugging example where BREAD revealed an implementation bug.

This work builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), nonequilibrium work identities (Jarzynski, 1997), BDMC and RAISE-style bounds (Burda et al., 2015), and is complementary to conventional MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman; Cowles–Carlin), which can fail to detect certain pathologies. It is also closely related to the concurrent “subjective divergence” estimator (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which reduces to BDMC when the reverse chain starts at the true posterior; the present analysis makes explicit the expected Jeffreys divergence bound and studies practical use in PPLs. Connections to Stein-based sample quality measures (Gorham & Mackey, 2015) are discussed.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a simple and compelling reuse of BDMC that yields an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples.
- Practical impact: integration into two widely used PPL stacks (WebPPL and Stan) with concrete case studies; demonstrates value for both model/representation choice and debugging.
- Thoughtful protocol for bridging simulated and real data, with empirical validation that forward-AIS behavior tracks between them on several models.
- Empirical robustness: reverse-AIS bounds appear insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used for approximate posterior initialization in the fitted-parameter protocol.
- Addresses a real need: quantitative, portable assessments of inference quality for AIS/SMC-like methods, complementing standard convergence diagnostics.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees require an exact posterior sample; for real data the proposed protocol is heuristic and lacks formal guarantees. The bound is only in expectation and may be loose in finite runs.
- Heavy-tailed AIS weights and reliance on single-run logs complicate uncertainty quantification; the paper avoids averaging logs but provides limited guidance on confidence intervals or estimator variance.
- Sensitivity to annealing schedule, choice of kernels, and number of intermediate distributions is not systematically explored; practical guidance is limited.
- Tightness of the bound is demonstrated only on small toy examples; there is no calibration against ground truth on higher-dimensional targets.
- The diagnostic applies to AIS/SMC-like procedures; it does not directly quantify long-run mixing of standard MCMC chains outside an annealing framework.
- Limited comparison to alternative sample-quality measures (e.g., Stein discrepancies) and to standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS) beyond qualitative comments.

Quality
The derivations are sound and clearly relate the bidirectional AIS gap to the expected Jeffreys divergence of the terminal distribution. The conditions and limitations are candidly stated. Empirical studies are sensible, though more systematic exploration of variance, schedule design, and sensitivity would strengthen the case. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is plausible but lacks theoretical control.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Section 3.1’s derivation is concise; some readers would benefit from an explicit algorithm box for BREAD and a brief recap of BDMC assumptions. The “simulated-to-real” protocol is clearly described and empirically validated. Figures emphasize medians/quartiles due to heavy tails; a brief discussion on presenting uncertainty would help.

Originality
Moderate. The core technique repurposes BDMC; the contribution is to (i) connect the BDMC gap to an expected Jeffreys divergence bound, (ii) develop a practical real-data protocol, and (iii) demonstrate utility within PPL toolchains. The concurrent work on subjective divergence is closely related; positioning and distinctions are appropriate.

Significance
Useful for practitioners building or using AIS/SMC within PPLs; can guide representation choices and uncover implementation errors. Broader impact is limited by the simulated-data requirement and expected-only nature of the bound, but within its scope the contribution is meaningful.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled estimator with reasonable empirical behavior.
- Integration into real systems; actionable insights (representation trade-offs, debugging).
- Addresses an important practical gap in evaluating inference quality.
- Careful discussion of related work and limitations.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees for real data and finite computation; bound is in expectation only.
- Sparse guidance on schedule design, variance control, and confidence intervals.
- Modest novelty relative to BDMC and concurrent subjective divergence.
- Limited calibration on challenging/high-dimensional targets and limited comparisons to other diagnostics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical guidance for annealing schedules and number of intermediate distributions; report sensitivity analyses.
- Quantify uncertainty: e.g., repeated runs with concentration bounds or bootstrap on log-weights; discuss when averaging can be justified.
- Calibrate bounds against ground truth on more complex synthetic targets; study correlation with R-hat/ESS and Stein discrepancies.
- Analyze the bias introduced by the fitted-hyperparameter protocol, or propose diagnostics to detect when the simulated-data proxy fails (e.g., posterior predictive checks, mismatch of forward AIS curves).
- Explore SMC variants (number of particles, resampling) and compare to AIS within BREAD.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce figures and facilitate adoption.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a practical, principled, and useful addition to the toolkit for assessing AIS/SMC-based inference in probabilistic programming, with solid empirical demonstrations, despite limited guarantees on real data and modest novelty.
