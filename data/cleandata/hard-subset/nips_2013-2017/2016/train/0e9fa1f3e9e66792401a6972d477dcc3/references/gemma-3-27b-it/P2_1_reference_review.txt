## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – originally proposed for marginal likelihood estimation – to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior.  The authors derive an estimator that *upper* bounds this divergence in expectation, validate its accuracy on toy examples, propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data (through hyperparameter estimation and simulated data analysis), and integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan.  A key contribution is “BREAD” (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) – a concrete implementation and application of this approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of BDMC:**  Extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to directly assess the quality of approximate posterior samples is a significant and clever idea.  This addresses a critical need in Bayesian inference – robust diagnostics beyond traditional convergence checks.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper provides a clear derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound and connects it to existing BDMC theory. The analysis is mathematically sound and well-explained.
* **Practical Protocol for Real-World Data:** Recognizing the limitation of requiring true posterior samples, the proposed protocol for hyperparameter estimation and simulation is a pragmatic and valuable contribution.  It attempts to bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical application.
* **Implementation and Validation:**  Integrating BREAD into two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) demonstrates the method's usability. The experiments, particularly the validation of consistency between real and simulated data, are convincing.
* **Debugging Application:** The discovery of a bug in WebPPL through the use of BREAD highlights the potential for this method as a powerful tool for verifying implementations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Applicability:** The method directly applies only to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue this has broader implications for MCMC, the direct reach is relatively narrow.  The connection to general MCMC is somewhat hand-wavy.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and thus BREAD, is computationally expensive. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains significantly increases the computational burden compared to standard MCMC diagnostics.  The paper doesn’t fully address this practical limitation and doesn’t quantify the scaling with problem dimensionality.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The protocol for real-world data relies on accurate hyperparameter estimation. Poor hyperparameter choices can lead to simulated data that doesn’t accurately reflect the real data, undermining the validity of the BREAD analysis. While the authors acknowledge this and attempt to address it with the MCMC refinement step, further investigation of its robustness is needed.
* **Quartile Visualization in Experiments:** While understandable given the presence of outliers, visualizing quartiles instead of means obscures information about central tendency.  A more robust visualization technique (e.g., trimmed means) could be considered.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations are correct, and the experimental results appear well-supported.  It’s a complete piece of work that clearly presents its method, validation, and potential applications.  The authors are careful about stating the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard, and the explanations are clear. However, some sections (particularly those detailing the AIS background) could be more concise.

**Originality:** The application of BDMC to measure posterior accuracy is novel.  While building upon existing work in BDMC and importance sampling, the authors’ contribution lies in framing it as a tool for assessing MCMC-based inference.  The related work section is comprehensive.

**Significance:** This work is potentially significant. Reliable diagnostics for Bayesian inference are crucial for building trust in the results produced by probabilistic programming systems and for advancing the field of MCMC. While the computational cost is a concern, the ability to rigorously bound the Jeffreys divergence offers a valuable addition to the toolbox of Bayesian practitioners.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The method is novel, theoretically sound, and practically demonstrated. While the computational cost and limited applicability are concerns, the potential benefits of having a rigorous diagnostic tool for MCMC outweigh these drawbacks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  Novel and theoretically sound method for assessing posterior accuracy.
*  Practical implementation and integration into popular probabilistic programming languages.
*  Addresses a critical need for robust diagnostics in Bayesian inference.
*  Provides a valuable protocol for applying the method to real-world data.
*  Demonstrates the method’s potential for debugging inference software.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*  Limited applicability to algorithms beyond AIS and SMC.
*  High computational cost.
*  Potential sensitivity to hyperparameter estimation.

**Recommendation:**  **Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper warrants acceptance to NIPS. The contribution is significant, the work is high quality, and the potential impact on the field is substantial.  I recommend that the authors address the comments regarding computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity in the camera-ready version.
