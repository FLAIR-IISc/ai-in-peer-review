## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the accuracy of MCMC-based inference, specifically targeting algorithms amenable to importance sampling re-weighting like AIS and SMC. The core idea of leveraging BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is sound, theoretically well-motivated, and practically demonstrated through integration with WebPPL and Stan. While the practical impact is currently limited by the need for simulated data (and the protocol for real data is somewhat heuristic), the work provides a rigorous tool for diagnosing inference quality and offers insights into model and algorithm design. I believe the paper is **above average** and recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors address a critical challenge in Bayesian inference: reliably validating the quality of MCMC samples. Existing diagnostics often lack quantitative rigor. They extend BDMC, originally used for log-marginal likelihood estimation, to estimate an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior obtained from AIS/SMC and the true posterior. This is achieved by viewing these algorithms through the lens of importance sampling and exploiting the strengths of BDMC in providing both upper and lower bounds. They also tackle the practical issue of applying this technique to real data via a hyperparameter estimation and simulation protocol. 

The related work section is well-structured and comprehensive, covering relevant areas like MCMC convergence diagnostics, sample quality measures (Stein's method), and alternative approaches like tempered transitions.  The discussion of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka's related work is particularly helpful.  It's good the authors acknowledge the connection to Jarzynski's work on non-equilibrium measurements as this provides historical context for the BDMC framework.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound is clearly presented and builds logically on the existing BDMC framework. The mathematical justification for the approach is a significant strength.
* **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into widely used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) significantly increases its accessibility and potential impact.
* **Diagnostic Utility:** BREAD provides a *quantitative* measure of inference reliability, going beyond qualitative diagnostics.  The case study demonstrating its use in guiding model representation choices in matrix factorization is compelling.
* **Addressing Real-World Application:** Recognizing the limitation of requiring simulated data, the authors propose a practical (though heuristic) protocol for applying BREAD to real-world problems.  Their validation of this protocol is a significant contribution.
* **Bug Detection:** The serendipitous discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a testing tool for probabilistic programming systems.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Simulated Data Dependence:** The primary limitation is the need for generating data from the *true* posterior, which necessitates knowing the hyperparameters.  The proposed protocol for real-world data relies on estimating hyperparameters and simulating data from those estimates, introducing potential bias.
* **Computational Cost:** Running BDMC can be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional models. The paper doesn't fully address the scalability of the approach, which could limit its applicability.
* **Heuristic Protocol for Real Data:** While the protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is a good starting point, it’s explicitly acknowledged as heuristic.  Further investigation into the robustness of this protocol is needed.
* **Limited Experiments:** The experimental validation focuses primarily on a small set of models.  A broader evaluation across diverse model classes would strengthen the claims.
* **Clarity of Figures:** Some figures (e.g., Fig. 2) could be improved in terms of clarity and labeling.



**4. Specific Comments & Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Scalability Discussion:**  Add a section discussing the computational complexity of BREAD and potential strategies for scaling it to more complex models (e.g., using approximations or parallelization).
* **Real Data Protocol Robustness:** Expand the validation of the real-data protocol. Consider exploring scenarios where the initial hyperparameter estimates are poor or where the true data-generating process deviates significantly from the model assumptions.  Investigate the impact of different numbers of MCMC steps (S) on the reverse AIS estimates.
* **Clarify Assumptions:** More explicitly state the assumptions underlying the real data protocol (e.g., that the estimated hyperparameters are reasonably close to the true ones).
* **Figure Improvements:** Improve the clarity of figures, ensuring proper labeling of axes and curves.
* **Discuss Alternatives:** Briefly discuss alternative methods for assessing MCMC convergence and their limitations compared to BREAD.



**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a rigorous, quantitative measure for assessing MCMC inference quality.
* Offers a practical tool for diagnosing inference problems and guiding model/algorithm design.
* Well-motivated theoretically and implemented in widely used probabilistic programming languages.
* Addresses a critical need in the Bayesian inference community.

**Con:**

* Reliance on simulated data limits immediate applicability to many real-world problems.
* The protocol for real-world data is heuristic and requires further validation.
* Computational cost may be a barrier for large-scale models.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The theoretical novelty, practical implementation, and potential diagnostic utility of BREAD make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program.  Addressing the suggested revisions will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
