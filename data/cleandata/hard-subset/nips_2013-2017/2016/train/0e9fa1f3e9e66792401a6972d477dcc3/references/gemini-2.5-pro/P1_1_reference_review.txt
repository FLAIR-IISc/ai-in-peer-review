### Paper Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of certain MCMC-based inference algorithms. The work builds upon Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15], which was originally proposed to provide stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood (log-ML). The authors' key theoretical insight is that the expected gap between these BDMC bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior.

This method is directly applicable to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling on an extended state space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). A critical requirement for BREAD is access to an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, which restricts its rigorous application to simulated data. To bridge this gap to real-world problems, the authors propose a practical protocol: 1) infer hyperparameters on the real dataset, 2) simulate a new dataset from these inferred hyperparameters, 3) apply BREAD to this simulated dataset to measure inference quality, and 4) validate that the sampler's behavior is consistent between the real and simulated datasets.

The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and conduct a series of experiments. They validate their real-data protocol by showing that inference behavior is similar on real and simulated data for several models. They then demonstrate the utility of BREAD through two compelling case studies: comparing the performance of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model, and uncovering a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL. This work is closely related to the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16].

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Contribution to a Fundamental Problem:** The paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of MCMC convergence diagnostics. Existing methods are often heuristic (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) and can only diagnose non-convergence, not confirm convergence. BREAD provides a rigorous, quantitative measure of sample quality (the Jeffreys divergence) in the simulated data setting, which is a major step forward.

2.  **Elegant Theoretical Result:** The core contribution—connecting the gap between forward and reverse AIS log-ML estimates to an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence—is both novel and elegant. The derivation in Section 3.1 is clear and builds logically on the existing BDMC framework.

3.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The paper does an excellent job of showing why this method is useful. The case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed matrix factorization models provides concrete, actionable guidance that would be difficult to obtain otherwise. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's value as a tool for validating the correctness of probabilistic programming systems.

4.  **Careful and Honest Evaluation:** The authors are transparent about the primary limitation of their method—the requirement of an exact posterior sample, restricting rigorous guarantees to simulated data. They directly address this by proposing and empirically validating a heuristic protocol for real-world data. The experiments in Section 5.1 convincingly show that the protocol is robust and that inference behavior on simulated data can be a good proxy for real data.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The background on AIS and BDMC is concise and clear, the theoretical derivation is well-explained, and the experimental setup and results are presented logically.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Heuristic Nature on Real Data:** While the authors do a good job validating their protocol, the fact remains that for real-world data, the method is a heuristic. The reverse chain is initialized from an *approximate* posterior sample, meaning the resulting "upper bound" on the log-ML is no longer a mathematically guaranteed bound. The quality of this heuristic depends on how well the initial MCMC run (on the real data) has explored the posterior to find representative hyperparameters. If the initial run gets stuck, the simulated data may not be representative, and the conclusions from BREAD could be misleading. This caveat could be stated more strongly.

2.  **Limited Direct Applicability:** The method is directly applicable to inference schemes like AIS and SMC. While the authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS (using, e.g., HMC kernels) can inform the design of standard MCMC algorithms, BREAD cannot be used to *directly* measure the quality of the stationary distribution of a standard HMC or Gibbs sampler. It only measures the quality of the final samples produced by a specific AIS/SMC run. This distinction is important and could be made clearer.

3.  **Practical Issue with Combining Runs:** Footnote 3 notes that the analysis does not justify averaging the weights of multiple AIS chains, and thus results from individual chains are reported. This is a significant practical limitation. A single AIS run can have very high variance, and practitioners almost always average results over many runs. It is unclear how the Jeffreys divergence bound behaves when combining multiple runs. For instance, should one average the log-ML estimates and then take the gap, or average the final bound estimates? The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this practical issue.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivation is correct, and the experimental results appear solid and well-supported. The work is complete, presenting a new theoretical idea, a practical protocol for its application, and strong empirical evidence of its utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear. The motivation is well-established, the method is explained step-by-step, and the connection to previous work is clearly articulated. The figures are informative and support the main claims.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the key insight of using the BDMC gap to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel. This reframes BDMC from a tool for marginal likelihood estimation to one for evaluating posterior approximation quality. The paper is also appropriately contextualized with respect to concurrent work [CTM16].

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. This work provides a much-needed tool for developers and sophisticated users of probabilistic programming languages and MCMC methods. The ability to quantitatively compare different model formulations or sampler settings is invaluable. The debugging application alone makes this a very useful contribution to the field. It is very likely that researchers and practitioners will build upon and use these ideas.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in probabilistic inference.
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical result connecting log-ML bounds to sample quality.
*   Demonstrates clear practical value through well-chosen case studies (model selection and debugging).
*   The proposed protocol for real-world data is thoughtfully designed and empirically validated.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to understand.
*   Implementation in popular PPLs (Stan, WebPPL) increases its potential impact.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The rigorous guarantees of the method are limited to simulated data; its application to real data is a heuristic.
*   The method is only directly applicable to AIS/SMC, not general MCMC algorithms, which limits its scope.
*   The paper lacks clear guidance on how to aggregate results from multiple runs, a crucial step in practice.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The strengths—a novel theoretical insight, high practical utility, and careful empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses. It provides a principled and quantitative method for assessing the quality of approximate inference, a problem of great importance to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
