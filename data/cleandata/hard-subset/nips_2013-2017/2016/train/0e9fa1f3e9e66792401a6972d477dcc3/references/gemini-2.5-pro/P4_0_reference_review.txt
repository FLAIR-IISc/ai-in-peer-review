This paper presents Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), a method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The central challenge addressed is the lack of rigorous tools for validating the output of approximate inference, a long-standing problem for both practitioners and algorithm developers. The proposed method extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed by Grosse et al. (2015) for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key insight is that algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), which can be framed as importance sampling on an extended state space, produce not just an estimate of the marginal likelihood but also a sample from an approximate posterior distribution.

The authors' main theoretical contribution is a refinement of the BDMC analysis to show that the expected gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provides a computable upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution of the samples and the true posterior. This provides a direct, quantitative measure of sample quality. A significant practical limitation is that this method requires an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, restricting its rigorous application to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset using these inferred hyperparameters; and third, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. The paper provides an implementation and empirical validation of BREAD within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility in comparing different model representations and as a powerful debugging tool. This work is closely related to concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also uses a BDMC-like procedure for evaluating inference.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly important problem in machine learning and statistics. The ability to trust the output of complex probabilistic models is crucial, and current MCMC convergence diagnostics are largely heuristic and often fail to detect subtle issues. BREAD offers a path toward rigorous, automated, and quantitative validation of inference quality, which could be transformative for the field of probabilistic programming. The successful application of BREAD to debug a subtle bug in WebPPL is a compelling demonstration of its practical impact.

2.  **Quality:** The technical contributions of the paper are sound. The derivation that connects the BDMC log-likelihood gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is elegant and clearly presented. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their method on five different models within the Stan framework, carefully validating the core assumptions of their proposed real-data protocol: (1) that inference behavior is consistent between real and appropriately simulated data, and (2) that their heuristic for initializing the reverse chain from fitted hyperparameters is robust. The scientific case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model provides a clear example of how BREAD can guide modeling and algorithmic decisions.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide a concise yet comprehensive background on AIS and BDMC, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is explained logically, and the experimental results are presented with clear figures and interpretations.

4.  **Originality:** While the work is a direct extension of BDMC, the application to measuring posterior sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and significant conceptual contribution. It repurposes a tool for model comparison into one for inference validation. The proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data is also a novel, practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Applicability of Rigorous Guarantees:** The primary weakness is that the rigorous theoretical guarantees of BREAD apply only to simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a sensible heuristic, but it is not guaranteed to be reliable, especially in cases of severe model misspecification where data simulated from inferred hyperparameters may not be representative of the real dataset. The authors acknowledge this and provide an example in the appendix, but this limitation remains a significant barrier to its use as a "black-box" diagnostic for arbitrary user-supplied models and data.

2.  **Scope of Samplers:** The method is directly applicable only to samplers that can be formulated as importance sampling over an extended path, such as AIS and SMC. It cannot be directly applied to evaluate standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling, which are workhorses of Bayesian inference. The authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS can inform the design of other MCMC methods, as they share many design choices (e.g., transition operators), but this connection is indirect.

3.  **Concurrent Work:** As the authors note, the paper has significant overlap with the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016). While this does not detract from the quality of the present paper, it does temper its claims to sole originality on the core idea of using BDMC for inference evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and unsolved problem in probabilistic inference.
*   Presents a novel, technically sound method with a clear theoretical justification.
*   The empirical validation is extensive and demonstrates significant practical value for comparing model representations and for debugging inference engines.
*   The paper is written with outstanding clarity.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method's rigorous guarantees are confined to simulated data, and the real-data protocol is a heuristic.
*   Applicability is limited to a specific class of samplers (AIS/SMC).
*   The existence of similar concurrent work slightly reduces the novelty.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the problem of evaluating approximate inference. The theoretical insight is clever, the experimental work is solid, and the potential impact on the development of reliable probabilistic programming systems is high. Despite the limitations regarding applicability to real data and general MCMC algorithms, the work represents a major step forward and is a clear fit for NIPS.
