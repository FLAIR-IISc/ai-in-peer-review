**Summary**

This paper proposes a method called Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD) to quantitatively measure the reliability of certain MCMC inference algorithms. The method extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained via forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS), provides an upper bound *in expectation* on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. Since this method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, it is strictly applicable only to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a heuristic protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters, then simulating data from the model using these parameters. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to compare different model representations for matrix factorization and to identify a bug in WebPPL.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality is of fundamental importance. Moving beyond heuristic diagnostics to quantitative, rigorous bounds is a significant goal. The paper's core idea of repurposing the BDMC framework to bound a formal divergence measure (Jeffreys divergence) is elegant and novel. This provides a much-needed tool for both developers of inference algorithms and users of probabilistic programming systems.

2.  **Practical Application:** The integration of BREAD into two popular PPLs (Stan and WebPPL) is a strong point. It demonstrates a clear path from theory to practice. The case studies—comparing model representations and debugging an implementation—are compelling examples of how such a tool can provide concrete, actionable insights that would be difficult to obtain otherwise.

3.  **Clarity of Derivation:** The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the expected gap in BDMC estimates to the Jeffreys divergence, is clear and follows logically from the properties of AIS and KL divergence.

**Weaknesses**

While the central idea is promising, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor and some not-so-minor flaws in its methodology, presentation, and experimental validation, which collectively undermine the contribution.

1.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript appears to be in a preliminary state.
    *   The header contains a formatting artifact: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain."
    *   The in-text citations are non-standard (e.g., "[GS; CGHL+ p]"). The "+ p" notation for Stan is particularly unconventional for a formal publication.
    *   The reference list is severely malformed, with extraneous characters and repeated author names (e.g., "Brooks and Gelman.,? \Q1998\E, Brooks and Gelman.,"). Such a lack of attention to detail is concerning.
    *   There is a clear typo in a central equation: `qfwd(pT )` should be `qfwd(xT )` in Equation (12).

2.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   **The Bound is on Expectation:** The main result is that `E[B_hat] >= J`. This means a single estimate `B_hat` is not a guaranteed upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence `J`. The paper does not discuss the variance of this estimator `B_hat`, which is critical for its practical use. A high-variance estimator would require many repetitions to yield a reliable estimate of its mean, potentially making the method computationally prohibitive.
    *   **Handling of AIS estimates:** Footnote 3 reveals a critical weakness. The authors state that their analysis "does not justify" averaging the AIS weights `wT` from multiple runs, so they report estimates from individual chains. Standard practice in AIS is to average the raw weights (not their logs) to produce a single, lower-variance estimate of the partition function ratio. By analyzing the log of individual, high-variance estimates, the resulting bound `B_hat` is likely to be extremely noisy. The decision to visualize quartiles in Figure 1 appears to be a way to obscure this variance, but it sidesteps the fundamental issue of the estimator's stability. This choice severely limits the practical utility of the proposed bound.
    *   **Heuristic Protocol for Real Data:** The paper's claim to rigor is immediately compromised by the protocol for real-world data in Section 3.2. This procedure is described as a "heuristic scheme" and relies on an *approximate* posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain. The validation in Section 5.1.2 is insufficient. Showing that the reverse AIS estimates are stable across different numbers of MCMC steps (`S`) for generating the initial state does not prove the validity of the procedure. It may simply indicate that the initialization MCMC chain is stuck in a local mode, and a longer run does not help it escape. The procedure abandons the mathematical guarantees that are the primary motivation for the work.

3.  **Scope and Evaluation:**
    *   **Overstated Generality:** The paper claims that "the ability to evaluate AIS-based inference should also yield insights which inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly." This is a strong claim that is not substantiated. It is not at all clear how tuning AIS—an algorithm with a specific structure—provides direct, transferable insights for fundamentally different samplers like Gibbs sampling or un-annealed HMC.
    *   **Tightness of the Bound:** The crucial question of how tight the bound `B = E[B_hat]` is to the true divergence `J` is relegated to Appendix B. For a paper whose central claim is this bound, this analysis should be a core part of the main text. Without it, the reader cannot judge whether the bound is tight enough to be useful or too loose to be informative.
    *   **Consistency Validation:** In Section 5.1.1, the forward AIS curves for real and simulated data are aligned by an arbitrary vertical shift. While the shapes appear similar, this visual comparison is qualitative. A more rigorous method for establishing the "consistency of the inference algorithm’s behavior" would be necessary to truly validate this part of the protocol.

**Recommendation: Reject**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of MCMC diagnostics.
*   The core theoretical insight connecting the BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence is novel and technically sound.
*   The successful application to debugging a PPL implementation is a strong proof of concept for the utility of this kind of tool.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper exhibits a lack of care in its presentation, with numerous formatting errors in the text and reference list.
*   A critical methodological choice—not averaging AIS estimates to reduce variance—makes the practical utility of the proposed estimator highly questionable due to noise.
*   The proposed protocol for real-world data is entirely heuristic, undermining the paper's central premise of providing rigorous, quantitative validation. The empirical validation of this protocol is weak.
*   Key results regarding the tightness of the bound are deferred to an appendix, and claims about the general applicability of the method are overstated.

While the theoretical idea is interesting, the paper as a whole does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. The combination of sloppy presentation, questionable methodological choices that impact practical utility, and a non-rigorous extension to the real-data setting that constitutes a major part of the contribution leads me to recommend rejection. The work would be much stronger if the authors addressed the variance of their estimator and provided a more robust validation of their real-data protocol.
