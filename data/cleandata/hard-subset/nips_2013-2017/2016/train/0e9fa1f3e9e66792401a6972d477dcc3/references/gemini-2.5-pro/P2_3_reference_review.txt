### Summary

This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms. The core idea extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and the true posterior. This provides a rigorous, quantitative measure of sampler quality. Since the method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse AIS chain, its guarantees only hold for simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters, and finally, run BREAD on this simulated dataset. The method is implemented and validated in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and is used to provide insights into model representation choices for a matrix factorization model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Assessing MCMC convergence is a critical and long-standing challenge in probabilistic modeling. Existing diagnostics are largely heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. This paper offers a path toward a rigorous, quantitative, and automated solution, which is of high significance to both researchers and practitioners.
2.  **Elegant Theoretical Contribution:** The key theoretical result—that the expected gap in BDMC upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence—is a novel and insightful extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). It connects the problem of marginal likelihood estimation to the problem of sample quality assessment in a non-obvious and powerful way. The choice of Jeffreys divergence is well-motivated, as it penalizes both missing modes and placing mass in low-probability regions.
3.  **Thoughtful Protocol for Real-World Data:** The authors directly confront the primary limitation of their method (the need for an exact posterior sample) and propose a sensible, practical protocol to apply it to real-world datasets. The experimental validation of this protocol, showing consistency between inference on real and simulated data and robustness to the quality of the initial reverse sample, is a major strength of the paper.
4.  **Strong and Convincing Experiments:** The paper provides a solid empirical validation of the proposed BREAD method.
    *   The validation across five different models in Stan demonstrates the general applicability of the real-data protocol.
    *   The use-case comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers for matrix factorization is a compelling demonstration of how BREAD can guide concrete modeling and algorithmic decisions.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the utility of the method as a rigorous debugging and validation tool for complex inference systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Applicability of Guarantees:** The rigorous mathematical guarantees apply only to inference methods that can be framed as importance sampling on an extended space, such as AIS and SMC. The method does not directly assess the quality of samples from a standard MCMC chain (e.g., NUTS or Metropolis-Hastings) after a burn-in period. While the authors argue that evaluating AIS provides insights for MCMC more broadly, this is an indirect connection, and the direct applicability is somewhat narrow.
2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data, while pragmatic, breaks the formal guarantees of the method. The reverse chain is initialized from an *approximate* posterior sample, meaning the resulting "upper bound" is no longer a mathematically guaranteed stochastic upper bound. While the experiments suggest this is a reasonable approximation, it's an important distinction that could be emphasized more clearly.
3.  **Computational Cost:** Running BREAD requires running both a forward and a reverse AIS procedure, which can be significantly more computationally expensive than the original inference task. The paper does not analyze this overhead, which is a key practical consideration for users. A discussion of the trade-off between the cost of the diagnostic and the confidence it provides would strengthen the paper.
4.  **Uncertainty of the Bound:** The bound holds *in expectation*. A single estimate of the gap `B` is a random variable and may be smaller than the true Jeffreys divergence `J`. Furthermore, as noted in Footnote 3, the analysis seems to apply to individual AIS chains rather than the average over multiple chains, which is standard practice for reducing the high variance of AIS estimates. This could mean that the practical utility of the bound from a small number of runs is limited by high variance.

### Detailed Comments on Review Criteria

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation in Section 3.1 that connects the BDMC gap to the Jeffreys divergence is sound and well-presented. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that are designed to validate the core assumptions of the proposed real-data protocol. The authors are honest about the limitations of the rigorous bounds (i.e., being restricted to simulated data) and make a credible effort to bridge the gap to practice. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The background section provides the necessary context on AIS and BDMC for a reader to understand the core contributions. The motivation is clear, the method is described precisely, and the experimental setup and results are easy to follow. The figures effectively visualize the key results. The connection to the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) is also clearly explained.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015), the main contribution—repurposing the BDMC bounds to measure sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence—is a novel and significant conceptual leap. The proposed protocol for real-world data and the application to debugging and comparing PPL implementations are also novel contributions that demonstrate the practical value of the core idea.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is very high. The lack of reliable, quantitative convergence diagnostics is a major bottleneck in the broader adoption of Bayesian methods, especially in the context of "black-box" probabilistic programming. BREAD provides a principled and automated tool that could become a standard for validating inference results, comparing algorithm performance, and debugging PPLs. It directly empowers both developers and users of these systems to have greater confidence in their results. This work is likely to be built upon and used by many in the NIPS community and beyond.

### Recommendation: **Accept**

This paper makes a strong and original contribution to a problem of fundamental importance. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and supported by convincing experiments. Despite some limitations regarding the scope of applicability and the heuristic nature of the real-data protocol, the core ideas are significant and provide a valuable new tool for the probabilistic inference community.
