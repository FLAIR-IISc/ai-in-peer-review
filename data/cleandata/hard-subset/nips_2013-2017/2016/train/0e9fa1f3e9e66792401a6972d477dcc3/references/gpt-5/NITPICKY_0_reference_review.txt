Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the quality of MCMC-based approximate posterior samples by using the gap between forward and reverse AIS log normalizer estimates to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal distribution and the true posterior. The authors (i) derive the expectation bound, (ii) show tightness on toy problems where the true Jeffreys divergence is computable, (iii) propose a pragmatic “fitted-hyperparameters” protocol to approximate the reverse-chain initialization on real data, and (iv) integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan, using it to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization and to catch an implementation bug.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] and reverse AIS/RAISE [Burda et al., 2015], and is related to Stein discrepancies [Gorham & Mackey, 2015]. The concurrent “subjective divergence” work [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016] is acknowledged and the equivalence when the true posterior is used is noted. The thermodynamic integration/tempered transitions lineage (Neal, 1996; 2001) and SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006] are appropriately cited.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical observation: the forward–reverse AIS log-gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; the derivation via joint KLs and marginalization is clean and pedagogically useful.
- Practical value: turning BDMC into a tool to stress-test PPL inference, including catching real bugs.
- Sensible real-data protocol: fitted-hyperparameter simulation to approximate the reverse initialization when exact posterior samples are not available.
- Breadth of experiments across models and two PPLs; the collapsed vs. uncollapsed case study is useful and actionable.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological and theoretical
- “Upper bound” only in expectation: The central guarantee is E[log R̂−log R̂rev] ≥ DJ(qfwd,pT). For practitioners, a single run is not a bound. There is no finite-sample concentration or coverage analysis for the gap. Markov’s inequality for individual log-estimates is mentioned, but not for the gap, nor are any empirical coverage diagnostics reported.
- Reverse-chain initialization on real data breaks guarantees: The fitted-hyperparameter protocol yields an approximate posterior state, hence the bound property no longer holds even in expectation. The empirical insensitivity to the number of MCMC refinement steps S may be model- and dataset-dependent; no stress tests (e.g., multimodality, heavy tails, hierarchical posteriors with weak priors) are provided.
- AIS design choices are underspecified: The annealing schedule, number of intermediate distributions, and per-temperature mixing are critical to the tightness and variance of the estimates. There is little analysis of sensitivity to schedule (e.g., geometric vs. moment averaging), number of steps per temperature, or kernel choices. The statement “standard ones provided by both platforms” is too vague.
- Use of Stan/NUTS within AIS: It is unclear whether adaptation (step size, mass matrix) is disabled during AIS proper. Using adaptive transitions at intermediate temperatures can violate invariance and break the AIS guarantee. NUTS’ dynamic trajectory length is fine, but warmup/dual averaging must not occur during AIS. The paper should clarify exactly how Stan was configured for each temperature and whether detailed balance/invariance holds.
- Scope beyond AIS: The introduction claims applicability to “algorithms which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS or SMC,” but only AIS is treated formally and implemented. No SMC derivation or experiments are provided.

Empirical
- Reporting and quantification: The main text does not report actual values of the Jeffreys-divergence upper bound (in nats) nor uncertainty. Plots of quartiles of forward/reverse log-ML estimates do not directly demonstrate bounding DJ. Show the log-gap distribution and its sample mean with confidence intervals; evaluate empirical coverage on simulated data (how often the gap exceeds the true DJ).
- Limited and indirect validation: The toy validation where J is exactly known is deferred to Appendix B, but the main text provides no numbers, and it is unclear how challenging these toys are. Real-data validation focuses on matching forward-AIS curves between real and simulated data after vertical shifts; this does not assess divergence bounds and may mask misspecification.
- Collapsed vs. uncollapsed study: Useful, but per-iteration comparisons can be misleading; wall-clock comparisons are briefly hinted at (Fig. 2(b)), yet exact runtimes, hardware, and Stan/WebPPL configuration details are missing. Sensitivity to K, N, D, and noise variances would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous formatting and citation issues: Many references are malformed (e.g., “[GS; CGHL+ p]”, “Grosse et al.,? Q2015E”), a stray “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)” line appears mid-text, equations contain typographical errors (Eq. (1) spacing, subscripts), footnote markers and punctuation are inconsistent. These reduce readability and suggest the draft has not been carefully prepared.
- Missing implementation details: Exact AIS schedules, number of temperatures, number of transitions per temperature, and initialization procedures (for both directions) should be fully specified for reproducibility. Code links are not provided in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide nonasymptotic guidance: Either derive simple concentration bounds for the gap (e.g., via Bennett/Chernoff on log-weights or by aggregating multiple runs as in Gogate et al., 2007) or provide empirical calibration on simulated data (coverage plots: fraction of runs where gap ≥ true J as a function of K,T).
- Clarify Stan configuration: Explicitly state that adaptation is completed before AIS and that fixed kernels are used at each temperature. Report acceptance rates, effective transitions, and diagnostics per temperature.
- Report actual DJ upper bounds with uncertainty, and compare to other diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin R̂, ESS, Stein discrepancy) to contextualize utility.
- Stress-test the fitted-hyperparameter protocol under misspecification and hierarchical settings; quantify failure modes (Appendix D shows one mismatch but lacks analysis).
- Expand on SMC applicability or refrain from claiming it; include at least a brief derivation or an experiment.
- Fix all references and typesetting issues; ensure the paper is reproducible (provide code/scripts, seeds, and exact settings).

Pros
- Useful conceptual bridge from BDMC to expected bounds on posterior-sample quality.
- Practical integration into real PPLs; demonstrated utility for debugging and model-representation choices.
- Broadly relevant problem; potential to influence practice.

Cons
- Core guarantee is only in expectation and is weakened or absent in the real-data protocol; no concentration or calibration.
- Ambiguity around AIS kernel correctness (Stan adaptation) and lack of sensitivity analyses.
- Limited quantitative validation; presentation and referencing need substantial polishing.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The ideas are promising and relevant, but the current submission has substantive methodological ambiguities, insufficient quantitative validation of the purported bounds, and many presentation issues. With clearer guarantees (or calibrated empirical coverage), precise implementation details, stronger experiments, and corrected formatting/citations, this work could be a strong contribution.
