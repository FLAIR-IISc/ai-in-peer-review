

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to rigorously measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that BDMC can be used to obtain upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, and they propose a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters. The paper integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validates it on various probabilistic models, and uses it to compare different model representations and identify bugs in probabilistic programming implementations.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
The theoretical foundation of the paper is strong and builds well on the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015). The derivation showing that BDMC can bound the Jeffreys divergence is carefully executed and mathematically sound. The authors provide a rigorous proof that the expectation of the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The experimental validation is thorough and well-executed. The authors test BREAD across multiple models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.) and demonstrate that inference behavior on simulated data is consistent with that on real data. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL using BREAD is a compelling practical demonstration of the method's utility.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's scope is limited to AIS and SMC, which are specific types of MCMC methods. The authors do not clearly address how general their approach is to other MCMC algorithms (e.g., standard Metropolis-Hastings, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo). This limitation should be more explicitly stated, as it affects the method's applicability to the broader MCMC community.

The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on the assumption that the behavior of inference on simulated data (with fitted hyperparameters) is consistent with that on real data. While the authors provide empirical validation for this assumption, a more rigorous theoretical justification would strengthen their claims. For instance, it's unclear under what conditions this assumption might fail, and how to diagnose such failures.

The computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains is not discussed, which is an important consideration for practical adoption of the method. The paper should include a comparison of the computational requirements of BREAD versus standard convergence diagnostics.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully and are easy to follow. The figures and experimental results are well-designed and effectively illustrate the key points.

However, the paper could benefit from more explicit discussion of the limitations and assumptions of the method. The claim that "the ability to evaluate AIS-based inference should also yield insights which inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly" is somewhat overstated given that the method is limited to specific algorithms. A more nuanced discussion of the scope would improve clarity.

The related work section mentions Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) but doesn't clearly differentiate their contribution from the current work. A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the paper's position in the literature.

## Originality

The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel. The authors present a new application of BDMC that goes beyond the original focus on marginal likelihood estimation. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper builds directly on the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015), so the core idea isn't entirely new. The authors could better emphasize how their specific contributions (bounding the Jeffreys divergence rather than marginal likelihood, the real-world data protocol) differentiate this work from previous BDMC applications.

The paper's use of BREAD for model representation comparison and bug detection is novel and demonstrates practical utility, but these applications stem from the core method rather than being novel ideas themselves.

## Significance

The ability to rigorously measure the reliability of MCMC inference is a significant contribution to the field. The paper addresses a real pain point for probabilistic programming users who lack reliable ways to assess the quality of posterior inference. The integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to a wide audience.

The practical application of BREAD to identify a bug in WebPPL is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates the method's utility for debugging probabilistic programming implementations. This has clear implications for the development and reliability of probabilistic programming tools.

However, the paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Geweke diagnostics). A more thorough comparison would help readers understand the relative advantages of BREAD and its potential impact on the field.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical applications are well-executed. The main weaknesses relate to the limited scope of the method (specifically to AIS and SMC) and the assumption about real-world data, but these are reasonable limitations given the nature of the problem.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More explicit discussion of the method's limitations to AIS/SMC
2. A clearer differentiation from related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016)
3. A discussion of computational costs
4. A more thorough comparison with existing convergence diagnostics

## Recommendation

**Accept**

## Pro-accept Arguments:
- Provides a rigorous method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference
- Solid theoretical foundation with careful mathematical analysis
- Practical integration with probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan)
- Demonstrated utility for model selection and debugging
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple models and frameworks
- Addresses a real pain point for probabilistic programming users

## Con-accept Arguments:
- Limited scope to AIS and SMC (not general MCMC)
- Relies on the assumption that simulated data behavior matches real data
- Doesn't address computational cost in detail
- Needs clearer differentiation from related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016)

## Final Comments

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The BREAD framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically useful. The integration with probabilistic programming languages and the demonstration of its utility for debugging are particularly compelling.

While the scope is limited to AIS and SMC, the authors provide a clear path to applying the method to related inference algorithms, and the insights gained from this specific context are valuable for the broader MCMC community. The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
