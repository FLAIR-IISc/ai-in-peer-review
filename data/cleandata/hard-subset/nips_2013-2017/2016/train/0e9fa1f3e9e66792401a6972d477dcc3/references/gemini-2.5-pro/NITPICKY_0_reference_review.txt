**Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"**

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for quantifying the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms. The method extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally developed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC provides an estimator that, in expectation, upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The method is applicable to algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). Because the reverse pass of BDMC requires an exact posterior sample, the rigorous guarantees only apply to simulated data. To address this limitation for real-world applications, the authors propose a protocol: infer hyperparameters on real data, simulate new data from these hyperparameters, and then apply BREAD on this simulated dataset. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference on several models and to compare different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) for a matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing problem in probabilistic inference: how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. Moving beyond heuristic convergence diagnostics to a quantitative bound on a formal divergence measure is a significant step forward. The connection between the BDMC log-likelihood gap and the Jeffreys divergence is an elegant and important insight.

2.  **Practical Utility:** The implementation and application within popular probabilistic programming systems like Stan and WebPPL demonstrate the method's practical potential. The experiments comparing model representations provide a compelling use case, showing how BREAD can guide modeling decisions that are often made based on intuition or expensive trial-and-error.

3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The use of BREAD to uncover a bug in the WebPPL library is a powerful testament to its utility as a rigorous validation and debugging tool for inference systems.

**Weaknesses:**

While the core idea of the paper is strong, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its technical presentation, methodological details, and formatting. These issues, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the work's carefulness and readiness for publication.

1.  **Technical and Notational Inaccuracies:** The paper contains several critical typographical errors in key mathematical statements, which suggests a lack of meticulous proofreading.
    *   In Section 2.2, the application of Jensen's inequality is written as "E[log ˆA]  logE[ ˆA]". This should be `E[log(Â)] ≤ log(E[Â])`.
    *   In Section 2.3, the relationship between forward and reverse log-ratios is stated as "logR = logRrev". This should be `log(R) = -log(R_rev)`. This is a significant sign error.
    *   In Section 3.1, the key equations for the expected log-likelihood estimates (Eqs. 8 and 11) appear to be missing minus signs before the DKL terms. For instance, Eq. (8) should likely read `log(Z_T) - log(Z_1) - DKL(...)`.
    *   The update rule in Eq. (1) is poorly formatted, mixing mathematical notation and procedural description on a single line, which hinders clarity.

2.  **Methodological Gaps and Overstatements:**
    *   The title and introduction claim to measure the reliability of "MCMC inference" more broadly, but the method is directly applicable only to a specific class of algorithms (AIS, SMC). The link to informing the design of "traditional MCMC methods" is tenuous and not directly supported; the method evaluates the specific algorithm it is applied to, not a general MCMC kernel in isolation.
    *   The proposed protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2) is a heuristic that fundamentally breaks the "rigorous bound" guarantee, which is the method's primary selling point. While the authors validate that the number of MCMC steps for the reverse chain's initialization has little effect, this only suggests insensitivity in the local posterior neighborhood of the estimated hyperparameters. It does not prove that this approximate sample is a suitable replacement for a true posterior sample, especially for complex, multimodal posteriors.
    *   A critical detail is relegated to Footnote 3: the theoretical bound applies to the divergence of the distribution of a single AIS sample, `q_fwd(x_T)`. However, in practice, AIS estimates are almost always improved by averaging many independent runs. The paper does not address how BREAD applies to the quality of an *ensemble* of samples, which is what practitioners actually use. This is a significant limitation of the current analysis.

3.  **Formatting and Professionalism:** The manuscript suffers from numerous formatting issues that are unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference.
    *   The citations are improperly rendered (e.g., "[CGHL+ p]", "[GS]").
    *   The footer on the first page contains a leftover template artifact: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain."
    *   The entire References section appears to be broken, with BibTeX compilation artifacts like "? \Q1998\E" and "et al\.,?". This makes it impossible to properly verify the cited literature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence for certain inference algorithms.
*   It addresses a highly significant problem in the field.
*   The empirical results, particularly the debugging of WebPPL and the comparison of model representations, are compelling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript is rife with technical typos in core equations, undermining its credibility.
*   The proposed solution for real-world data is a heuristic that compromises the method's main guarantee of rigor.
*   The analysis fails to address the practical use case of averaging multiple AIS chains.
*   The paper is extremely poorly formatted, with broken citations and leftover template text, suggesting a rushed and careless preparation.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea presented in this paper is promising and potentially of high impact, the execution in its current form is not up to the standards of NIPS. The sheer number of technical inaccuracies, methodological caveats that are not fully addressed, and severe formatting problems makes the paper difficult to trust and evaluate properly. The work is incomplete. I would strongly encourage the authors to perform a thorough revision to correct the mathematical notation, clarify the scope and limitations of their method (especially regarding real-world data and sample ensembles), and properly format the manuscript before resubmission. The core contribution is valuable, but it is obscured by a lack of diligence in the paper's preparation.
