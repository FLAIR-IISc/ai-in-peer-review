Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based approximate posterior sampling. The key idea (BREAD) is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (from prior to posterior) and reverse (initialized with an exact posterior sample available for simulated data), yielding stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log partition ratio log ZT/Z1. The authors refine BDMC’s analysis to show that the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS and the true target. They further propose a protocol to transfer these guarantees to real datasets: infer hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data from those hyperparameters, and validate that forward AIS behavior matches between simulated and real data; for reverse AIS on simulated data, they initialize from an approximate posterior over hyperparameters and verify robustness to the number of MCMC steps used. They integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan, validate on five models (regression, time series, mixture models, matrix factorization), compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations, and report a debugging use case that uncovered a bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001). The core theoretical step—relating expectations of log AIS weights to KL divergences—leverages standard importance sampling arguments and KL contractions. The method is related to CTM16 (“subjective divergence”) which also uses BDMC-style reverse chains; the authors clarify that their estimator reduces to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior and discuss differences when using a reference distribution. Connections to thermodynamic integration, Jarzynski equality, and convergence diagnostics are appropriately cited.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and useful guarantee: a simple, general procedure to upper bound in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between AIS’s approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior. This bridges partition function estimation and sample-quality assessment.
- Practical integration: implementation in two popular probabilistic programming platforms (WebPPL, Stan), with a clear protocol for use on real data via fitted hyperparameters and simulated datasets.
- Empirical validation: multiple models, consistent behavior between real and simulated data, and a robustness study varying the number of MCMC steps for approximate hyperparameter posterior initialization.
- Actionable insight: the collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization comparison yields platform-dependent recommendations; the method also helped detect an implementation bug, underscoring utility for testing and regression.
- Well-positioned contribution for practitioners seeking principled inference diagnostics beyond heuristic MCMC convergence checks.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Expectation-only bound: The central guarantee is E[log Rrev−1 − log R] ≥ DJ(q, p). A single run’s gap ˆB need not upper bound the Jeffreys divergence; there is no high-probability control on DJ. This limits direct decision-making from one execution.
- Applicability: Requires AIS/SMC-style samplers that can be framed as importance sampling over extended state spaces and, for tight upper bounds, an exact posterior sample (hence simulated data). The proposed real-data protocol is heuristic; there are no formal guarantees when initializing reverse AIS from an approximate hyperparameter posterior.
- Sensitivity and guidance: The quality of both bounds depends on annealing schedules, kernels, and T. The paper provides limited prescriptive guidance for choosing schedules, number of steps, and K to achieve tight and stable estimates. Reporting quartiles rather than means reflects heavy-tailed variability but leaves unclear how to estimate E[·] with error bars.
- Tightness evidence: The paper states the bound is “often a good match” on toy problems (Appendix B), but quantitative characterization of tightness across regimes (e.g., dimensionality, multimodality, mismatch of kernels) is limited.
- Generalization beyond AIS: Although SMC is discussed, experiments focus on AIS. It remains unclear how results extend empirically to SMC variants and more complex bridging schemes.

Quality
The theoretical derivation is sound and concise, relying on well-known AIS/IS identities and KL properties. Experimental methodology is careful in validating real/simulated consistency and the effect of approximate hyperparameter posteriors. However, the lack of high-probability bounds on DJ and limited quantitative analysis of tightness are notable. More rigorous statistical treatment of repeated runs (e.g., confidence intervals for E[log R], or concentration inequalities) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The exposition of forward/reverse AIS, the expectations and inequalities, and the practical protocol are understandable. Some references are incomplete in the excerpt (placeholders like [GS], [CGHL+ p]); please ensure full, consistent citations. Consider adding pseudocode for BREAD and explicit guidance for choosing schedules and number of intermediate distributions.

Originality
The step from BDMC’s log-ML sandwich to bounding sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence is a neat and useful twist. Integrating into PPLs and proposing a practical real-data protocol are valuable. Relative to BDMC, the theoretical novelty is incremental but consequential for practice.

Significance
Reliable, interpretable diagnostics for approximate inference are important. BREAD offers an implementable, model-agnostic tool for AIS/SMC-based inference and PPLs. The potential to inform modeling choices and catch bugs increases impact. Even with expectation-only guarantees, this is a meaningful addition to the toolkit.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide concentration results or empirical Bernstein-style intervals for E[log R] and E[log Rrev], and hence for E[ˆB], using multiple runs (cf. Gogate et al., 2007)? This would aid practical decision-making.
- Can you more systematically quantify the tightness of E[ˆB] vs DJ across synthetic families (varying dimension, condition number, multimodality)?
- Guidance on schedule design and T: can adaptive schedules (e.g., equal ESS temperature spacing) tighten bounds? Any heuristics validated across models?
- For real data, could you study alternative reference distributions (e.g., variational posteriors) and bound or empirically characterize the bias they introduce?
- Please report computational overheads (wall-clock) for BREAD vs. standard inference and K/T used to achieve the reported curves.

Pros
- Clear, sound theoretical link from BDMC to sample-quality assessment.
- Practical integration into PPLs with demonstrated usefulness (modeling choices, debugging).
- Empirical validation across multiple models and platforms.
- Addresses an important need in inference diagnostics.

Cons
- Guarantees are in expectation only; limited high-probability control on DJ.
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers and simulated data for rigorous bounds.
- Limited guidance on tuning and schedule selection; modest analysis of bound tightness.
