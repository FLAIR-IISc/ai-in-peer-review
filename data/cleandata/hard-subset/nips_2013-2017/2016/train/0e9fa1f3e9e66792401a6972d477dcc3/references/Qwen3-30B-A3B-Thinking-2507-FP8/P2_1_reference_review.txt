

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) that provides rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors build upon the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015) to create a method that upper bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence, which is more informative than simply measuring one direction of KL divergence. The paper makes four key contributions: theoretical derivation of the bound, validation on toy distributions, a protocol for applying the method to real-world data, and integration into WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming frameworks. The work addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference: how to rigorously evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior approximations.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is sound and builds logically upon the BDMC framework. The authors carefully derive that the gap between the forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation (Equation 12), which is a significant contribution to the field. The experimental validation is thorough and well-designed, with the authors not only testing on toy distributions where exact values can be computed (Appendix B) but also demonstrating the method's utility on real probabilistic programming models (Stan and WebPPL). The paper also includes a compelling case study where BREAD helped identify a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2), demonstrating practical utility beyond theoretical contribution.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from background to method to experiments. The authors effectively introduce necessary background concepts (Section 2) without overwhelming the reader. The connection between BDMC and the new BREAD method is well-explained, and the paper's structure makes it easy to follow the main contributions. The experimental section (Section 5) is particularly clear, with well-designed figures (Figures 1 and 2) that effectively communicate the main findings.

### Originality
This work makes a meaningful contribution to the literature by extending BDMC from bounding marginal likelihood to bounding the Jeffreys divergence, which provides a more comprehensive measure of inference quality. While the paper acknowledges related work (Gorham & Mackey, 2015; Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), it clearly distinguishes its contribution by providing a rigorous bound on a more informative divergence measure. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (Section 3.2) is also novel and addresses a key limitation of previous methods that only worked on simulated data.

### Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. As the authors note, "it is still difficult to rigorously validate the effectiveness of an MCMC inference algorithm," and this paper provides a practical solution to that problem. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to a wide range of probabilistic programming users. The method has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems by providing users with a quantitative measure of inference quality. The paper's demonstration that BREAD can guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) shows practical utility for model developers.

## Weaknesses

### Scope Limitation
The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), which is a subset of MCMC methods. While the authors argue that these algorithms "involve many of the same design choices as traditional MCMC methods," the scope limitation is significant. It would be helpful to discuss more concretely how the insights from BREAD might generalize to other MCMC methods beyond AIS/SMC.

### Implementation Details
While the paper describes integration into WebPPL and Stan, it lacks sufficient detail about the implementation specifics. For instance, how many AIS steps were used in practice? What were the MCMC transition kernels used in the reverse chains? The paper mentions "standard" transition kernels, but for reproducibility, more details would be valuable.

### Comparison with Alternatives
The paper acknowledges related work but doesn't thoroughly compare BREAD's performance or utility against other methods for evaluating inference quality. A more comprehensive comparison (e.g., against convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin or effective sample size) would strengthen the significance claim.

### Real-World Data Protocol
The protocol for using BREAD on real-world data (Section 3.2) relies on approximate posterior samples for the reverse chain initialization. While the paper validates that this approximation has little effect (Section 5.1.2), the authors could have provided more theoretical justification for why this approximation is valid in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference evaluation. The theoretical extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the integration into probabilistic programming frameworks demonstrates practical utility. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling that has received little attention in the literature.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria well:
- **Quality**: High - Theoretical and experimental validation is strong, and the authors are careful to acknowledge limitations.
- **Clarity**: High - Well-organized, well-written, and provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.
- **Originality**: High - The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel and meaningful.
- **Significance**: High - The method has immediate practical applications in probabilistic programming and could significantly improve the reliability of inference in real-world applications.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. It provides a rigorous, practical method for evaluating MCMC inference quality that addresses a long-standing challenge in the field. The integration into WebPPL and Stan, along with the demonstration of its utility in debugging (Section 5.2.2), makes it particularly compelling.

## Recommendation

Accept. This is a strong technical contribution with clear significance to the field of probabilistic modeling and inference. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in the field. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
