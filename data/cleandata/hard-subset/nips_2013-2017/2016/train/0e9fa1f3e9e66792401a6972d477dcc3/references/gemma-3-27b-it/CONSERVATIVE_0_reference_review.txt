This paper proposes BREAD, an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors. The core idea is sound – leveraging BDMC’s ability to bound marginal likelihoods to instead bound divergence measures – and builds upon recent work in BDMC and related areas like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors identify a practical hurdle – the need for exact posterior samples – and propose a protocol for applying the method to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulated data generation. They implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility in guiding modeling choices.

However, despite the clear presentation and the reasonable motivation, I have significant reservations about accepting this paper. My primary concern stems from the limited scope of applicability and the lack of compelling evidence that the proposed method delivers substantial practical benefit beyond its theoretical interest. The method *requires* applicability to algorithms viewable as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue this informs MCMC design more broadly, the connection feels tenuous. Most standard MCMC algorithms don’t easily fit into this framework. The core contribution – bounding the Jeffreys divergence – is valuable *when* the method can be applied, but the “when” is a significant restriction. 

The validation, while thorough, feels somewhat circular. The authors demonstrate the accuracy of the bound on toy examples where the true Jeffreys divergence *can* be computed, which is reassuring but doesn’t address the real-world utility. The experimental validation on real datasets, while aiming to bridge the gap, relies on a protocol (fitting hyperparameters, simulating data) that introduces its own potential biases. The consistency observed between real and simulated data is encouraging, but it doesn't guarantee that the bounds obtained are truly informative or actionable in practice. The observation that BREAD helped discover a bug in WebPPL is a positive side effect, but hardly a justification for acceptance on its own.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The connection to related work is thorough. However, the theoretical development, while correct, largely reworks existing results from BDMC. The novelty lies in the application to divergence measurement, but the limited applicability undermines its impact. The experimental section lacks a compelling demonstration that BREAD provides insights that cannot be obtained through more standard diagnostic tools (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size). The authors mention the potential for optimizing algorithmic hyperparameters, but the example provided (matrix factorization representation) seems fairly specific and doesn't demonstrate broad applicability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The method is theoretically sound and provides a rigorous bound on the Jeffreys divergence (for applicable algorithms).
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan is a valuable contribution, providing a tool for researchers and practitioners.
*   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of the method for code validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limited applicability of the method to a specific class of algorithms significantly restricts its impact.
*   The validation relies on a potentially biased protocol and lacks a compelling demonstration of practical benefit beyond theoretical guarantees.
*   The paper doesn’t clearly establish how BREAD improves upon existing MCMC diagnostic tools.
*   The theoretical contribution is largely a repurposing of existing BDMC results.



Overall, I believe this paper falls short of NIPS standards. While technically sound and well-presented, the limited scope, the reliance on potentially biased validation, and the lack of a strong demonstration of practical impact make it a weak submission. I recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the applicability concerns and providing a more compelling demonstration of practical utility would be needed for reconsideration. Specifically, I'd like to see a demonstration of BREAD applied to a wider range of MCMC algorithms, or a demonstration of how the insights gained from BREAD lead to significant improvements in inference performance that are not achievable through standard diagnostic tools.
