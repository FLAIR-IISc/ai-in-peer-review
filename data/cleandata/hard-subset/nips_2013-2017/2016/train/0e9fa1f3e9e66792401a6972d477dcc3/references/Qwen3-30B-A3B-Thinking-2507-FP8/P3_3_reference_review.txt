

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015) to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation for MCMC algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC). The paper also proposes a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, and integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan for practical use.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation is sound and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear derivation showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The validation experiments on toy distributions (Appendix B) demonstrate that the bound is reasonably tight. The protocol for real-world data is well-justified and addresses a critical limitation of BDMC (requiring exact posterior samples).

**Originality:** While BDMC was introduced by Grosse et al. (2015), the authors make a novel contribution by applying it to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors rather than just marginal likelihoods. This represents a meaningful extension with significant practical implications for evaluating MCMC inference quality.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference: how to rigorously assess the reliability of MCMC samples. The method could be valuable for probabilistic programming languages, allowing non-expert users to monitor inference quality. The empirical findings about model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) provide concrete insights for practitioners.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with thorough background on AIS, BDMC, and the necessary theoretical derivations. The experimental design is transparent, and the integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitation:** The method is only applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), excluding many common MCMC methods like Metropolis-Hastings with standard proposal distributions. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could more thoroughly discuss the practical implications for broader MCMC usage.

**Real-World Data Protocol:** While the protocol for real-world data is innovative, it relies on the assumption that hyperparameters fitted to real data yield simulated data that accurately reflects the real data distribution. The validation in Section 5.1.1 shows promising results for five models, but more extensive validation across diverse model classes would strengthen the case. The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where the fitted hyperparameters lead to simulated data that is substantially different from the real data.

**Computational Cost:** The method requires running both forward and reverse AIS chains, which doubles the computational cost compared to standard AIS. The paper doesn't discuss how this affects scalability to large models or datasets, which is a significant practical consideration for real-world applications.

**Related Work:** The comparison to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) is somewhat superficial. While the authors note that their method uses the true posterior to initialize the reverse chain (unlike subjective divergence), they don't fully explore the theoretical implications of this difference or how it affects the bounds.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The BREAD method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for evaluating the quality of posterior inference, addressing a critical gap in probabilistic programming. The integration with WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility, and the findings about model representation choices are valuable for practitioners.

The primary limitation is the restricted scope of applicability to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC). However, these algorithms are widely used and often serve as the basis for more complex MCMC methods, so the insights gained from BREAD can still inform broader MCMC design. The real-world data protocol is promising but needs more extensive validation.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem. It advances the state of the art in evaluating MCMC inference quality and provides a valuable tool for probabilistic programming practitioners.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Provides a rigorous method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors
- Well-motivated theoretical contribution extending BDMC
- Practical integration into two major probabilistic programming languages
- Demonstrates useful insights about model representation choices
- Clear, well-organized presentation with thorough validation

## Arguments for Rejection
- Limited to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC)
- Real-world data protocol requires validation across more diverse models
- Doesn't fully address computational cost implications

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with probabilistic models. The limitations are acknowledged and reasonable given the scope of the work, and the method's practical utility in probabilistic programming systems is compelling.
