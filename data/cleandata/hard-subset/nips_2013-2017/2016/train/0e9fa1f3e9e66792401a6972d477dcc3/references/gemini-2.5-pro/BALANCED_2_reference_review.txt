### Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core problem addressed is the long-standing difficulty of diagnosing MCMC convergence and assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples, a critical issue for users of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The proposed method builds upon Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was previously used to obtain stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood (log-ML). The key technical contribution is a new analysis showing that the gap between the BDMC bounds on the log-ML serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the distribution of samples produced by AIS and the true target posterior.

Because the method, in its rigorous form, requires an exact sample from the posterior, it is directly applicable only to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for evaluating inference on real-world datasets: first, fit hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these fitted hyperparameters; finally, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset, for which an exact posterior sample can be generated. The paper provides an implementation of BREAD in two prominent PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, and presents a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments validate the proposed protocol, demonstrate its utility in making modeling decisions (e.g., choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model), and even show its effectiveness in debugging PPL implementations.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental, important, and notoriously difficult problem in computational statistics and machine learning. A reliable, quantitative, and automated method for assessing the quality of posterior inference would be a major contribution to the field. This is especially valuable for the PPL community, as it could make powerful inference tools safer and more accessible to a broader audience of non-experts by providing a principled way to check the validity of their results.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The core idea of repurposing the BDMC framework from log-ML estimation to bound the Jeffreys divergence is both novel and elegant. The derivation in Section 3.1 is clear and appears technically sound. This provides a formal, quantitative measure of sample quality that is a significant step beyond existing heuristic-based convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat), which can only identify non-convergence. Unlike some other recent approaches (e.g., Gorham and Mackey, 2015), BREAD makes no restrictive assumptions like convexity on the posterior, making it broadly applicable.

3.  **Thoroughness and Practicality:** The work is very complete, covering theory, a well-motivated practical protocol for real data, and implementation. The integration into two very different PPLs (the M-H-based WebPPL and the HMC-based Stan) demonstrates the method's versatility. The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors not only validate their approach on multiple models but also provide two compelling use cases:
    *   **Scientific Insight:** The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations yields a non-trivial and useful result—that the optimal choice depends on the underlying inference engine (Stan vs. WebPPL). This showcases BREAD's ability to guide concrete modeling decisions.
    *   **Debugging:** The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful demonstration of the method's utility as a correctness-checking tool for PPL developers.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the technical background is explained concisely, and the contributions are laid out logically.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or expanded upon to strengthen it further.

1.  **Properties of the Bound:** The main theoretical result is that the *expectation* of the estimated gap, E[B̂], upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, J. It would be valuable to discuss the properties of a single estimate B̂ obtained from a finite number of AIS runs. What can be said about the variance of B̂? Is it possible to construct a high-probability bound on J itself, rather than a bound on its expectation? The original BDMC analysis provides strong tail bounds for the log-ML estimators; it is not immediately clear if a similar analysis can be applied here to bound J with high confidence.

2.  **Computational Overhead:** BREAD requires running both a forward and a reverse AIS procedure, which can be computationally intensive, likely much more so than the original inference run one wants to diagnose. A discussion of the computational cost would be helpful for practitioners. For example, providing guidance on how many annealing steps and how many parallel chains are typically needed would frame the method's practical trade-offs more clearly. Is this intended as a routine check or a "deep-dive" diagnostic tool?

3.  **Averaging AIS Estimates:** Footnote 3 states that the authors report estimates from individual AIS chains because "the analysis of Section 3.1 does not justify such averaging." This is a subtle but important point that deserves more prominent discussion. Standard AIS practice involves averaging the importance weights from multiple runs to get a lower-variance estimate. By linearity of expectation, it seems that averaging the gap estimates B̂ across multiple runs should still yield an unbiased estimator of E[B̂], which would still be an upper bound on J. Using an averaged B̂ would provide a more stable and reliable estimate of the bound. If averaging is indeed valid, the experiments should use it; if it is not, a clearer explanation of why the logic fails would be necessary.

4.  **Applicability to Standard MCMC:** The paper focuses on evaluating algorithms like AIS/SMC. It argues that insights should transfer to standard MCMC algorithms (like HMC/NUTS) because they often share the same transition kernels. This argument is plausible but could be strengthened. Could the authors elaborate on how a practitioner would use BREAD to, for instance, tune a standard HMC sampler in Stan? For example, can BREAD be used to find an optimal number of annealing steps, and does that choice provide guidance for setting parameters (e.g., integration time) for a long NUTS run? Clarifying this connection would broaden the perceived applicability of the method.

### Recommendation

This is a high-quality, innovative, and significant paper that addresses a critical need in the machine learning and statistics community. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and thoroughly validated. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to areas where clarification or further discussion could enhance the paper's impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem with a novel and principled approach.
*   Provides a quantitative measure of posterior quality, a significant improvement over existing heuristics.
*   The work is very complete: it includes theory, a practical protocol for real data, implementation in major PPLs, and strong empirical results.
*   High potential for impact on both researchers developing inference methods and practitioners using PPLs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None. The minor weaknesses identified can be addressed in a camera-ready version.
