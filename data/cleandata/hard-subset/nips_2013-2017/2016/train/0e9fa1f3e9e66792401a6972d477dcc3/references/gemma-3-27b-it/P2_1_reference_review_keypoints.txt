- Summary: Extends BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior, derives an expected upper-bound estimator, validates on toy examples, proposes a protocol for real data, and implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan.

- Strength — Novel application: Applies BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to directly assess posterior sample quality, addressing a key diagnostic gap.

- Strength — Theoretical foundation: Provides a clear, rigorous derivation connecting the Jeffreys divergence bound to BDMC theory.

- Strength — Practical protocol: Proposes a pragmatic hyperparameter-estimation and simulated-data workflow to apply the method to real datasets.

- Strength — Implementation & validation: Integrates into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates convincing experimental validation, including consistency checks between real and simulated data.

- Strength — Debugging utility: Successfully used to discover a bug in WebPPL, showing practical value for implementation verification.

- Weakness — Limited applicability: Directly applies only to algorithms expressible as importance sampling on extended state spaces (AIS, SMC); connection to general MCMC is not fully convincing.

- Weakness — Computational cost: BDMC/BREAD is computationally expensive (requires forward and reverse chains) and the paper lacks quantified scaling with problem dimensionality.

- Weakness — Hyperparameter sensitivity: The real-data protocol depends on accurate hyperparameter estimation; poor choices can invalidate the simulated-data analysis despite the proposed MCMC refinement.

- Weakness — Visualization choice: Using quartiles to summarize experiments obscures central tendency; more robust alternatives (e.g., trimmed means) could be better.

- Quality: Technically sound with correct derivations and well-supported experiments; limitations are acknowledged.

- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, though some AIS background sections could be more concise.

- Originality: Novel framing of BDMC as a tool for assessing MCMC-based inference; builds on prior work but contributes a new diagnostic perspective.

- Significance: Potentially significant for providing rigorous diagnostics for Bayesian inference, though practical impact is tempered by computational cost concerns.

- Overall assessment: Strong paper with valuable, well-executed contributions; computational cost and limited applicability are notable caveats.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel/theoretically sound method; practical implementations in popular PPLs; addresses a critical diagnostic need; provides a protocol for real data; useful for debugging inference software.

- Arguments against acceptance: Applicability limited beyond AIS/SMC; high computational cost; sensitivity to hyperparameter estimation.

- Recommendation: Strong Accept, with a request to address computational cost and hyperparameter-sensitivity concerns in the camera-ready version.
