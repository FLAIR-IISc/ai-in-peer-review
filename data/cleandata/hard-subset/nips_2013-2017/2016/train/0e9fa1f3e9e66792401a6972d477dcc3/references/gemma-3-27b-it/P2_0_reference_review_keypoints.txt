- Extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior.  
- Derives an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation and verifies accuracy on toy examples.  
- Proposes a protocol for real-data use by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validates this approach.  
- Implements the method (BREAD) in WebPPL and Stan and uses it to analyze model representations (e.g., matrix factorization).  
- Builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015) BDMC work and situates itself among MCMC diagnostics and sample-quality literature.  
- Unique contribution: directly bounding the Jeffreys divergence for assessing sample quality.  
- Novel and impactful application of BDMC to measure sample quality.  
- Strong theoretical foundation via a rigorous derivation and expectation bound.  
- Practical validation and useful implementations in popular probabilistic programming languages.  
- Protocol for real-world applicability is pragmatic and validated.  
- Discovery of a WebPPL bug demonstrates BREAD’s utility as a testing tool.  
- Paper is generally well-written with clear technical presentation.  
- Method is limited to algorithms representable as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), excluding many standard MCMC methods.  
- BDMC-based assessment can be computationally expensive, and scalability concerns are not fully addressed.  
- Real-world protocol depends on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters capture the true data-generating process, which may not always hold.  
- Quartile-based visualizations mitigate outliers but hinder direct comparisons; alternative visualizations could be more informative.  
- Overall evaluation: technically sound, clear, original, and potentially significant for probabilistic modeling.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to more thoroughly discuss computational costs and scalability limitations.
