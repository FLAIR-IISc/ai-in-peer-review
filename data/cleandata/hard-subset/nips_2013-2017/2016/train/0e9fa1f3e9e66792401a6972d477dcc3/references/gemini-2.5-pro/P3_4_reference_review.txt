This paper presents BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantifying the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods, to provide a quantitative measure of sampler quality. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution of samples and the true posterior. As BDMC's reverse pass requires an exact sample from the target posterior, the method's rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, fit hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters, and finally apply BREAD to assess inference on this simulated dataset. The method is implemented and validated in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and is used to provide practical insights into model representation and to debug an inference implementation.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing problem in probabilistic modeling: how to reliably assess the convergence and quality of MCMC samplers. Existing diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin R-hat) are heuristic and can fail to detect certain modes of non-convergence. BREAD provides a more principled approach by directly bounding a formal divergence measure. The key theoretical contribution—linking the BDMC log-likelihood gap to the Jeffreys divergence—is an elegant and novel extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). This is a significant step towards automated, rigorous validation of inference in probabilistic programming languages.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigorous Validation:** The derivation in Section 3.1 is sound and clearly presented. Crucially, the authors do not just present the theory but also conduct a thorough empirical validation.
    *   They validate the tightness of the bound on toy problems where the true divergence can be computed (Appendix B).
    *   They carefully validate the proposed protocol for real-world data (Section 5.1). The experiments showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data (when the model is well-specified) and that the reverse chain estimates are robust to the quality of the initial hyperparameter sample are convincing and essential for the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Compelling Use Cases and Practical Impact:** The paper demonstrates the utility of BREAD with two excellent examples.
    *   The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization (Section 5.2.1) is a perfect showcase. It provides a quantitative, principled answer to a common and tricky modeling choice, showing how the optimal strategy can differ across inference engines (Stan vs. WebPPL). This is a powerful demonstration of how BREAD can guide modeling decisions.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampler (Section 5.2.2) is a testament to the method's power as a debugging and verification tool. The fact that the forward and reverse bounds *must* sandwich the true value provides a strong consistency check that is missing from most other diagnostics. This is a highly valuable property for developers of PPLs.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background on AIS and BDMC is clear and concise, the derivations are easy to follow, and the experimental setup and results are presented logically. The connection to the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) is also clearly and generously explained.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Limited Applicability to General MCMC:** The method is directly applicable to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. The paper claims that insights from evaluating these algorithms can "inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly," but this link is somewhat tenuous. It would strengthen the paper to elaborate on how, for example, diagnosing an HMC kernel within an AIS schedule provides direct, actionable feedback for a standard HMC sampler that is not part of an annealing scheme. The current framing could lead readers to believe the method is more general than it is.

2.  **The Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data is a pragmatic and necessary step, but it is a heuristic that sacrifices the rigorous guarantees of the simulated-data setting. The validation experiments are good, but they rely on the assumption that the generative model is reasonably well-specified for the real data. If there is significant model misspecification, inference behavior on data simulated from `p(y|θ, η_hat)` might be very different from inference on `y_real`. This is a critical limitation that is mentioned in the appendix but deserves a more prominent discussion in the main text to properly manage reader expectations.

3.  **Computational Cost:** A full BREAD analysis requires running multiple forward and reverse AIS chains, which can be computationally intensive, especially if each chain requires many intermediate steps for the annealing to be effective. The paper does not discuss this computational overhead. A brief discussion of the cost and the trade-off between the computational budget for BREAD versus running longer production MCMC chains would be a valuable addition for practitioners considering its use.

4.  **Averaging vs. Individual Chains:** In a footnote (3), the authors mention that their theoretical analysis of the bound on Jeffreys divergence does not justify averaging the weights from multiple chains, and thus they report quartiles of estimates from individual chains. However, the theory is based on the *expectation* of the bound `E[B_hat]`. It is not entirely clear how using quartiles of single-chain estimates `B_hat_i` relates to this expectation. While this is a subtle point, a short clarification on whether this practical choice affects the interpretation of the results would be helpful.

### Summary

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. It introduces a novel, principled, and practically useful method for assessing the quality of a class of MCMC algorithms. The strengths—a clever theoretical insight, thorough validation, and compelling demonstrations of its utility for model selection and debugging—far outweigh the weaknesses. The primary limitations are the scope of applicability (AIS/SMC-like methods) and the heuristic nature of the real-data protocol, both of which are reasonably addressed. This work is likely to be highly influential, particularly for developers and advanced users of probabilistic programming languages.
