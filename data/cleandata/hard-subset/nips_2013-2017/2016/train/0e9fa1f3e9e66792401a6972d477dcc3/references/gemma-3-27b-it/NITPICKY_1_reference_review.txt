This paper proposes BREAD, an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior, applicable to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors also present a protocol for applying this to real-world data via hyperparameter inference and simulation, and implementations in WebPPL and Stan.

The core idea – leveraging BDMC to quantify MCMC reliability via a divergence bound – is conceptually sound and addresses a crucial challenge in probabilistic inference. The connection to existing BDMC work is clearly explained, and the mathematical derivation, while dense, appears correct.  However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet accumulating, issues that ultimately detract from its impact and raise concerns about meticulousness.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to divergence estimation for posterior checking is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a mathematically rigorous extension of prior work.
*   **Practical implementation:** Providing implementations in two widely-used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) is valuable.
*   **Addressing real-world applicability:** The proposed protocol for using BREAD on real data is a pragmatic attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees (requiring exact samples) and practical application.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing is often dense and convoluted.  For instance, the repeated switching between abstract notation and the Bayesian inference setting, while intended to highlight generality, makes following the logic difficult. Equations (4) and (5) are introduced without sufficient motivation. Several sentences are unnecessarily complex and could benefit from restructuring.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** Several small but irritating technical inaccuracies and inconsistencies exist.  The definition of reverse AIS introduces `p̃t` and `˜Tt` which are then immediately redefined as equivalent to the original distributions and operators, creating confusion. The justification for the approximation in Section 3.2 (using approximate hyperparameter samples) feels underdeveloped and relies heavily on hand-waving (“…will not be an exact posterior sample…However, the true hyperparameters…ought to be in a region of high posterior mass”).  The statement that the forward AIS chains are independent while reverse chains are not is potentially misleading; it's the *initialization* of the reverse chain that is shared, not a dependence across the chains themselves.
*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** While the authors present validation, the focus on just five models and the reliance on visual comparisons of quartiles (due to outlier sensitivity) weakens the conclusions. The example of a model misspecification being revealed is promising, but a more systematic exploration of the protocol’s robustness would be beneficial.  Specifically, the experiments in Section 5.1.1 should include quantitative comparisons of the distributions (e.g., Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests), not just visual inspection.
*   **Overstated Claims & Lack of Nuance:** The claim that BREAD provides a “rigorous, general, and automatic procedure” for monitoring posterior quality feels overstated given the limitations outlined above (the reliance on exact samples for guaranteed bounds, the approximation in the real-world protocol, and the sensitivity to hyperparameter inference). The authors downplay the computational cost of BDMC, which can be significant, especially for high-dimensional problems.
*   **Referencing inconsistencies:** The references are formatted inconsistently (e.g., some include question marks after author names, others don't).  The citation "[CGHL+ p]" is unclear and incomplete.
*   **Formatting:** The equation numbering is off in Section 2.3.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:** The sheer number of minor flaws, while individually not fatal, collectively suggest a lack of thoroughness and attention to detail.  This is particularly concerning given the precision demanded by the paper’s theoretical claims. The writing quality is not up to par for NIPS. The experimental validation is limited and lacks sufficient quantitative rigor.

**For:** The core idea is novel and potentially impactful. The theoretical analysis is correct (as far as I can tell). The implementation in two widely-used probabilistic programming languages adds practical value.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting approach, the multitude of minor flaws, coupled with the limited experimental validation and overstated claims, outweigh the strengths.  The lack of meticulousness undermines confidence in the results and detracts from the paper’s overall quality. The authors should address the clarity issues, correct the technical inaccuracies, provide more comprehensive experimental validation, and moderate their claims before resubmission.  A more thorough review and editing process would significantly improve the manuscript.  I believe the ideas have merit, but this version is not ready for NIPS.
