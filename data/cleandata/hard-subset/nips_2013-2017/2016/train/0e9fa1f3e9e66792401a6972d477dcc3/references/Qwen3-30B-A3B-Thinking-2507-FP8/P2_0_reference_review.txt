

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, which is applicable to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The paper presents both theoretical analysis and empirical validation, including integration into probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan. The authors also propose a protocol for applying their method to real-world data by simulating data from estimated hyperparameters and comparing inference behavior.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis. The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1) is rigorous, building logically on the BDMC framework introduced in Grosse et al. (2015). The authors carefully justify their bounds using KL divergence properties.
- The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors demonstrate the accuracy of their bound on toy distributions (Appendix B), and provide extensive experiments on real probabilistic models in Stan and WebPPL (Section 5).
- The work is complete, not just a position paper. The authors provide clear methodology, theoretical analysis, experimental validation, and practical integration into probabilistic programming systems.
- The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the requirement for exact posterior samples (which is only feasible for simulated data) and the need for an approximate posterior sample when applying to real data (Section 3.2).

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not thoroughly address the computational cost of the BREAD method. While the authors mention that the bounds converge as T→∞, they don't provide a detailed analysis of how the computational requirements scale with model complexity or how the method compares to other convergence diagnostics in terms of computational efficiency.
- The validation of the protocol for real-world data (Section 5.1) is limited to five models from the Stan manual. While these are good examples, a more comprehensive validation across a wider range of model types would strengthen the argument that the method generalizes well.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is clearly written with well-organized sections. The background (Section 2) provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC, and the main contributions are clearly stated in Section 1.
- The mathematical derivations are well-explained, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to follow the logic. The key theoretical result (Equation 12) is presented clearly with supporting derivations.
- The experimental section is well-structured, with clear descriptions of the models, datasets, and evaluation protocols.

**Weaknesses:**
- Some technical details about the implementation of BREAD in WebPPL and Stan could be more thoroughly explained. For instance, the paper mentions that they "extended both WebPPL and Stan" but doesn't provide details about the specific changes made to the inference engines.
- The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the BREAD bounds across different models and datasets. While Figure 1 shows the AIS estimates, it doesn't directly visualize the Jeffreys divergence bounds.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The paper makes a clear and significant extension to the BDMC framework. While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al., 2015), this paper is the first to apply it to measure the reliability of MCMC inference through bounding the Jeffreys divergence.
- The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches. They acknowledge and contrast with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), who also built on BDMC but used a different reference distribution for the reverse chain. The authors' approach of using the true posterior (or approximate samples from it) provides stronger theoretical guarantees.
- The paper presents a novel protocol for applying the method to real-world data (Section 3.2), which addresses a key limitation of previous approaches that only worked on simulated data.

**Weaknesses:**
- The core idea of using bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound divergence is not entirely novel; it builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015). However, the authors make a clear and valuable extension to a new application domain.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference: how to rigorously measure the reliability of MCMC inference. This is a significant issue for both practitioners (who need to trust their results) and researchers (who need to validate their algorithms).
- The proposed method has immediate practical value, as demonstrated by the integration into WebPPL and Stan. The paper shows how BREAD can be used to guide modeling decisions (e.g., model representation in matrix factorization), which has direct implications for users of probabilistic programming languages.
- The paper's debugging application (Section 5.2.2) demonstrates the practical utility of BREAD in identifying implementation errors, which could be valuable for developers of probabilistic programming systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison of BREAD with existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Geweke diagnostics). While the authors mention these in Section 4, they don't systematically compare the strengths and weaknesses of BREAD relative to these established methods.
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate how BREAD would be used in a typical workflow. For example, how would a user decide when to stop inference based on the BREAD bounds?

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical validation is thorough, and the practical integration into probabilistic programming languages demonstrates real-world applicability. The paper addresses an important problem in a novel way, with clear distinctions from related work.

The paper's main limitations are the lack of detailed computational cost analysis and the limited scope of the real-world validation. However, these are relatively minor concerns for a paper of this quality and significance.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications, and it is well-written and thoroughly validated. The method has the potential to become a standard diagnostic tool for probabilistic inference.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Provides a rigorous, general method for measuring MCMC reliability, addressing a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference.
- The theoretical analysis is sound and clearly presented.
- The method has been successfully integrated into major probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan).
- The paper demonstrates practical utility through model comparison and bug detection.

**Con Acceptance:**
- The computational cost of the method is not thoroughly analyzed.
- The validation on real-world data is limited to five models from the Stan manual.
- The paper doesn't provide a systematic comparison with existing convergence diagnostics.

Despite these minor limitations, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field that will likely be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS.
