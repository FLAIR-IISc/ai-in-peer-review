Summary
The paper proposes BREAD, a way to assess the quality of samples produced by AIS/SMC-style MCMC by leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The key theoretical point is that the gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS log-evidence estimators yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal sample distribution and the true target pT. The method is rigorous for simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available (via ancestral sampling). For real data, the authors propose fitting hyperparameters to real data, simulating from the fitted model, and using an approximate posterior over hyperparameters to initialize the reverse chain. They implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, validate consistency between real and simulated data on five models, compare collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization in both systems, and report finding a bug in WebPPL.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean connection between BDMC/AIS sandwiching and an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for approximate samples.
- Practical integration into two PPLs and demonstration across multiple models; the debugging example is compelling.
- The simulated-data protocol is reasonable and has practical value for PPL users.
- The analysis neatly recovers one-sided bounds when only one direction is available.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/claims:
- The main guarantee is only “in expectation.” No high-probability control of DJ, no confidence intervals for E[B], and no discussion of the heavy-tailed nature of log-weights and its impact on estimating E[B]. In several plots the paper reports quartiles because of outliers; quartiles do not estimate the expectation that underpins the bound. This undermines the practical use of the bound as stated.
- Estimation protocol is underspecified: How many forward/reverse runs are needed to get a reliable estimate of E[B]? How are CIs computed, especially given dependence in reverse runs (shared initial exact posterior sample)?
- Real-data protocol breaks rigor: when replacing exact posterior initialization with (η?, θ?) obtained by limited MCMC, the guarantee does not hold. The empirical check varies S ∈ {10, …, 10000} and claims “statistically indistinguishable,” but the paper does not specify the statistical test, error bars, or power. The conclusion seems too strong relative to the analysis.
- Scope mismatch: The title/positioning suggests measuring reliability of “MCMC” broadly, but the method applies to AIS/SMC-like samplers that can be cast as IS on an extended space and require an exact posterior sample (or an approximation) for the reverse pass. Conventional MCMC (e.g., single-chain MH/HMC without annealing/sequential structure) is out of scope.
- Relation to concurrent work [CTM16] appears incremental. The core inequality (gap’s expectation upper bounds Jeffreys) is essentially the same; differences (reference distribution choice, subjective divergence) are acknowledged but not analyzed deeply. The novelty over [GGA15, CTM16] is modest and should be more sharply delineated.
- Tightness evidence is limited to “toy distributions” in an appendix. No systematic study of tightness versus annealing schedule length, proposal quality, dimensionality, or multimodality.
- Empirical analysis uses small models/datasets and qualitative trends. The matrix factorization case study fixes hyperparameters and uses a single problem size; conclusions about collapsed vs uncollapsed are anecdotal and lack sensitivity analysis (N, D, K, noise level).
- No cost-benefit analysis: running reverse AIS can be expensive; the paper does not quantify overhead versus alternative diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS, PSIS diagnostics for importance sampling).
- The paper states the bounds “converge as T→∞” (true for log Z ratio under ergodicity) but does not translate that to guarantees on DJ estimation for finite T nor discuss bias of qfwd(xT) relative to pT under non-equilibrium annealing schedules.

Clarity/presentation:
- Many formatting and citation issues:
  - Numerous references are incomplete or incorrect (placeholders like [GS], “[CGHL+ p]”, “\Q2015\E”). NUTS is mis-cited as “Homan and Gelman.” It should be Hoffman and Gelman (2014).
  - Stray text: “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)…” appears mid-page.
  - Equation (1) is poorly typeset; indices and updates are ambiguous (“wt wt−1 ft(xt−1)/ft−1(xt−1)”).
  - Inconsistent notation for reverse distributions: p̃t, ˜Tt; occasionally unclear conditioning vs marginalization.
  - Footnote markers (e.g., “1”) not properly formatted; sentence fragments and odd line breaks.
- The experimental methodology needs more precise descriptions: annealing schedules, number of temperatures, transition kernels per temperature, adaptation details, number of repeats, wall-clock budgets, and seeding.
- The real vs simulated comparison “shifted vertically” is a heuristic; state clearly that this only visually aligns curves and does not constitute a bound/estimate.

Reproducibility:
- No explicit links to code, data, or scripts to reproduce figures; given the sensitivity to schedule and implementation, this is important.

Originality
- The extension from BDMC to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is neat but close to concurrent/independent work; the paper’s novelty is incremental. The fitted-hyperparameters real-data protocol is practical but heuristic.

Significance
- Useful for PPL practitioners who already use AIS/SMC; the debugging anecdote is valuable. However, the lack of high-probability guarantees, limited empirical rigor, and narrow scope limit broader impact.

Quality
- The core derivation appears correct, but the experimental evaluation does not align tightly with the theoretical guarantee (quartiles vs expectations, no CIs). Real-data results are heuristic. Numerous presentation errors detract from confidence.

Pros
- Clear theoretical link between BDMC gap and Jeffreys divergence (in expectation).
- Practical implementation in Stan and WebPPL; real debugging payoff.
- Sensible simulated-data protocol to mirror real datasets.

Cons
- Expectation-only guarantees; estimation practice deviates from what is needed.
- Real-data procedure breaks rigor; limited empirical validation.
- Incremental relative to [GGA15] and [CTM16]; scope overstated.
- Many formatting/citation issues and missing methodological details.

Questions for authors
- How do you recommend practitioners estimate E[B] and report uncertainty? Would median-of-means or robust estimators help with heavy tails?
- Can you provide high-probability bounds or at least concentration results for B under mild assumptions?
- How sensitive is B to temperature schedules and transition kernel quality? Any adaptive schedule guidelines?
- Can the method be extended to SMC with resampling and path degeneracy?
- Please clarify the statistical tests used in Section 5.1.2 and provide error bars.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the combination of incremental novelty, expectation-only guarantees without a sound estimation protocol, limited empirical rigor, scope-mismatched positioning, and numerous presentation/citation errors make the submission not yet ready. With tightened theory-to-practice alignment, stronger experiments (with uncertainty quantification), and careful editing, a future version could be impactful. Confidence: high.
