

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that BDMC can provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence when applied to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) a theoretical derivation showing that BDMC provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) an exact computation technique for both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on toy examples; (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters; and (4) integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, with validation on multiple probabilistic models.

The paper builds on Grosse et al. (2015), which introduced BDMC for estimating log marginal likelihood. The authors extend this work to evaluate inference quality rather than marginal likelihood, which represents a significant application shift. The paper also relates to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which similarly uses BDMC but with a different reference distribution for the reverse chain. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from this related work.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with detailed derivations showing that the expectation of the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1). The paper provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The authors carefully address limitations, such as the requirement for exact posterior samples (only feasible for simulated data), and develop a practical protocol for real-world applications (Section 3.2). The experimental validation (Section 5) thoroughly tests BREAD on five models across Stan and WebPPL, demonstrating its utility in model representation comparisons and debugging. The paper acknowledges the need for further work on the theoretical guarantees when using approximate posterior samples for initialization (Section 5.1.2), showing appropriate scholarly caution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The background section (Section 2) provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC. The mathematical derivations are carefully explained, with clear notation and logical flow. The figures (Fig. 1, Fig. 2) effectively illustrate key findings, and the experimental setup is described with sufficient detail to allow reproduction. The connection between theory and practice is well-maintained throughout, with the authors consistently linking their theoretical contributions to practical implementations in probabilistic programming systems.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding the Jeffreys divergence for evaluating posterior inference quality. While related to the work of Grosse et al. (2015) and Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), the specific application to bounding the Jeffreys divergence and the associated protocol for real-world data represent significant conceptual advances. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work in Section 4, particularly regarding the reference distribution used in the reverse chain. The application of BREAD to compare model representations in probabilistic programming languages (Section 5.2) represents a novel practical use case that wasn't previously explored.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference: the difficulty of reliably validating MCMC-based inference algorithms. By providing a rigorous method to bound the divergence between approximate and true posteriors, BREAD has the potential to significantly improve confidence in probabilistic programming systems. The demonstration of BREAD's utility in debugging (Section 5.2.2) and guiding model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) shows concrete practical impact. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to widely used probabilistic programming frameworks, which should be of significant interest to the broader machine learning community.

**Strengths:**
- Novel extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence for posterior evaluation
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear mathematical derivations
- Practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data
- Successful integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Demonstrated utility in model representation comparison and debugging
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple models and frameworks

**Weaknesses:**
- The method remains limited to simulated data for rigorous bounds, requiring the approximation protocol for real data
- The computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for large-scale problems
- The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the tradeoffs between computational cost and bound tightness
- The validation in Section 5.1.2 shows that the approximation of posterior samples has minimal impact, but the theoretical guarantees are still not fully established for this case

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in MCMC inference with a technically sound approach
- Makes a clear and novel contribution beyond previous BDMC work
- Provides both theoretical guarantees and practical implementation
- Demonstrates significant utility through multiple applications
- Well-written with thorough experimental validation

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The method's applicability is limited to cases where exact posterior samples are available (for simulated data)
- The computational cost may be prohibitive for large-scale applications
- The real-world data protocol relies on the assumption that simulated data from fitted hyperparameters is representative

Overall, this paper makes a strong technical contribution that advances the field of probabilistic inference evaluation. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and addresses an important problem with practical significance. The novelty is clear, the quality is high, and the significance is demonstrated through both theoretical analysis and practical applications. The weaknesses identified are limitations inherent to the problem space rather than flaws in the paper itself. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
