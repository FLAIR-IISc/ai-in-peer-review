## Peer Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the accuracy of MCMC-based inference, particularly within the context of probabilistic programming languages. The core idea – using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence – is sound, and the empirical validation, while limited, is encouraging. The work is well-motivated, tackles a critical problem in Bayesian inference (assessing reliability), and offers a practical tool for users of probabilistic programming systems. While the applicability is currently limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling, the insights gained are likely to be broadly applicable. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper addresses the long-standing challenge of validating MCMC inference. Existing diagnostics often provide only heuristic assessments of convergence, lacking rigorous quantitative measures. The authors leverage the recent BDMC framework, initially designed for estimating marginal likelihoods, to compute upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior obtained via MCMC (specifically, algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling like AIS and SMC) and the true posterior.  

The core contribution lies in refining the BDMC analysis to derive an estimator for this upper bound. They demonstrate its accuracy on toy distributions and develop a protocol to extend its use to real-world data through hyperparameter fitting and simulated data generation.  Importantly, the authors integrate their method ("BREAD") into WebPPL and Stan, providing practical implementations.

The work builds on Neal’s work on annealed importance sampling (AIS) and its connection to importance sampling over an extended state space. It is closely related to, and acknowledges, independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (CTM16) which also utilizes BDMC for divergence estimation, though with a slightly different approach to initialization. The paper also positions itself relative to existing MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998) and methods for estimating sample quality (Gorham & Mackey, 2015).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The application of BDMC to directly bound the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and significant contribution. It provides a more rigorous assessment of MCMC accuracy than traditional diagnostics. The integration with WebPPL and Stan dramatically increases its practical impact.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical derivation of the divergence bound appears solid, building on established results from the BDMC literature. The analysis connects well to information-theoretic concepts like KL divergence and Jeffreys divergence.
* **Practicality:**  The development of the BREAD protocol for real-world data analysis addresses a key limitation of the technique (requiring exact posterior samples). The implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages is a significant strength.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while focused on a limited number of models, demonstrate the feasibility and (initial) effectiveness of BREAD. The debugging case study highlights a real-world benefit.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the motivation for each step is readily apparent.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Applicability:** The method is currently limited to inference algorithms that can be expressed as importance sampling.  This excludes many standard MCMC algorithms directly.  While the authors acknowledge this and argue for broader insights, it remains a significant constraint.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC can be computationally expensive, particularly the reverse AIS chain. This cost may limit its application to complex models or large datasets.  A more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The success of the real-world data protocol relies on accurate hyperparameter estimation. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t fully explore the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices or the potential for misspecification.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The validation experiments, though illustrative, are limited in scope and depth.  More extensive testing on a wider range of models and datasets would strengthen the claims.  The observed consistency between real and simulated data is encouraging, but more systematic analysis is warranted.
* **Dependence on Exact Posterior Sample for Reverse Chain:** While the protocol addresses this, it introduces another source of approximation, and a more rigorous analysis of the impact of using an *approximate* posterior sample (obtained via MCMC) for initialization would be valuable.



**4. Detailed Evaluation (Addressing NIPS Criteria):**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and initial empirical results. It is a complete piece of work, although further empirical investigation would strengthen it. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of the method.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is well-written and well-organized. The notation is clear, and the ideas are presented in a logical manner.
* **Originality:** The application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel. While building on existing work in AIS and BDMC, the authors make a significant contribution by adapting the technique to assess MCMC accuracy.
* **Significance:** The results are important because they address a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference: ensuring the reliability of approximate posterior samples. The tool has the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners using probabilistic programming languages.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous and quantitative measure for assessing MCMC accuracy, addressing a significant gap in the field.
* Offers a practical tool for users of probabilistic programming languages.
* Well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly written.
* Demonstrates initial effectiveness through empirical validation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited applicability to algorithms beyond importance sampling.
* Potential computational cost concerns.
* Empirical validation could be more comprehensive.



**6. Recommendations and Questions for the Authors:**

* **Computational Cost:**  Discuss the computational cost of BREAD in more detail, particularly for complex models.  Can the cost be mitigated through approximations or parallelization?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the real-world data protocol to hyperparameter choices. Consider including results from simulations with different hyperparameter settings.
* **Alternative Initializations:** Explore alternative initialization strategies for the reverse chain, particularly in scenarios where obtaining an exact posterior sample is difficult.
* **Wider Empirical Evaluation:**  Expand the empirical evaluation to include a broader range of models, datasets, and MCMC algorithms (even if indirectly).
* **Further Theoretical Analysis:** Investigate the theoretical properties of the divergence bound when the reverse chain is initialized with an approximate posterior sample.

**Conclusion:**

Despite some limitations, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian inference. The novelty, soundness, and practicality of the proposed method outweigh its current constraints. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
