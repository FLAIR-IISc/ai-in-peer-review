- Summary: Extends BDMC to assess posterior-sample quality from AIS/SMC by showing the expected gap between forward/reverse AIS log-evidence estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence (BREAD), with derivation, toy tightness checks, a real-data protocol, and WebPPL/Stan integration.  
- Theory: The expectation bound E[log R̂ − log R̂rev] ≥ DJ(pT, qfwd) is sound and a novel explicit result derived from AIS-as-IS and KL properties.  
- Limitation of theory: The bound holds only in expectation and is not a per-run or finite-sample guarantee.  
- Scope/assumptions: Method requires an exact posterior sample for reverse AIS; the paper uses a fitted-hyperparameter plus short MCMC burn-in heuristic for real data and validates it empirically.  
- Empirics: Experiments are relevant and thoughtful, showing simulated-data behavior mirrors real data, reverse-AIS insensitivity to burn-in length, and actionable modeling guidance (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed MF).  
- Practical impact: Integration into WebPPL and Stan and a bug found in WebPPL demonstrate practical utility for PPL users.  
- Caveat — variance: Heavy-tailed AIS weights mean finite-sample uncertainty is unquantified; quartiles are reported but confidence intervals for E[gap] and variance discussion are needed.  
- Caveat — “rigorous bounds” phrasing: Claims of rigorous/high-probability sandwiching should be qualified; a single run does not guarantee a tight bracket or an upper bound on DJ.  
- Caveat — SMC: Paper emphasizes SMC applicability but provides only AIS results; at least one SMC experiment is needed to support generality.  
- Caveat — HMC/NUTS under tempering: More detail required on how Stan maintains invariance at each temperature (adaptation schedules, step-size/mass-matrix reuse, reversibility).  
- Clarity — expectation and practical estimation: Explicitly state BREAD gives an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence and provide a practical recipe to estimate this expectation with error bars (replicates/bootstrap).  
- Clarity — pseudocode: Provide forward/reverse AIS pseudocode used in Stan/WebPPL including kernels and annealing schedule.  
- Clarity — monotonicity: Clarify that no monotonicity guarantee exists for empirical gap vs intermediate distributions or MCMC updates and show representative trends.  
- Originality: Meaningful extension of BDMC to posterior-sample quality and useful integration into PPLs; relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work discussed but trade-offs should be delineated.  
- Significance: Offers a model-agnostic quantitative diagnostic for posterior quality that can guide modeling, algorithm choices, and debugging in PPLs.  
- Pros: Clear theoretical connection, practical protocol and tooling, compelling case studies, and actionable guidance for PPL users.  
- Cons: Expectation-only bound, lack of empirical SMC demonstration, some overstatements about guarantees, and limited exploration of very challenging cases (high-dim, multimodal, poor annealing).  
- Suggested improvements — variance: Report E[gap] with confidence intervals from independent runs, discuss variance/outlier robustness, and consider robust estimators (median-of-means).  
- Suggested improvements — SMC: Include at least one SMC experiment (e.g., data-tempering SMC) and compare AIS vs SMC under equal cost.  
- Suggested improvements — sensitivity analysis: Analyze sensitivity to annealing schedules and MCMC updates per temperature and give practitioner guidance.  
- Suggested improvements — diagnostic comparisons: Compare BREAD diagnostics to standard MCMC diagnostics (R̂, ESS) and Stein discrepancies to show complementarity.  
- Suggested improvements — HMC/NUTS details: Clarify and ensure kernels leave pt invariant during tempered runs (adaptation, invariance).  
- Overall recommendation: Solid and practically impactful contribution likely to be accepted pending clarifications and additional experiments (variance characterization and an SMC case).
