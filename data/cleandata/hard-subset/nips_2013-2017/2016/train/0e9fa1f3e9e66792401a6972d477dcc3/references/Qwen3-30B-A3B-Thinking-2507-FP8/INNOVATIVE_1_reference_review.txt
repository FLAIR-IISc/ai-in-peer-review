

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel technique for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to provide rigorous upper bounds on this divergence, which is a significant departure from the common practice of using heuristic convergence diagnostics. While BDMC was previously used for estimating log marginal likelihood, the authors cleverly repurpose it to measure inference quality - a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference that has lacked rigorous quantitative measures.

The core novelty lies in their derivation that the difference between forward and reverse AIS estimates provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence. This is a bold conceptual leap that transforms a method for estimating partition functions into a tool for evaluating inference quality. The authors further develop a protocol for applying this technique to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, which is a practical solution to the common challenge of lacking exact posterior samples on real data.

The paper's significance is amplified by its integration into two major probabilistic programming platforms (WebPPL and Stan), demonstrating immediate applicability to real-world inference systems. This bridges a critical gap between theoretical advances and practical implementation. The authors use BREAD to guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization), revealing insights that would be difficult to obtain through conventional diagnostics. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL through BREAD's theoretical guarantees further demonstrates the practical utility of their approach.

Compared to related work (including [CTM16] which uses a similar BDMC framework but with a different reference distribution), the authors' focus on rigorous bounds for inference quality represents a clear conceptual advance. While [GM15] also addresses sample quality estimation, BREAD's lack of distributional assumptions (unlike their strong convexity requirement) makes it more broadly applicable to challenging posteriors.

The paper's strengths are its bold conceptual contribution, practical integration with probabilistic programming systems, and the demonstration of how this technique can guide modeling decisions. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for simulated data) and provide a thoughtful protocol for real-world application. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited to specific models, the theoretical novelty and practical significance outweigh these methodological constraints for a paper that introduces a new framework for evaluating inference quality.

This work addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference that has persisted for decades. The authors' approach is not merely incremental - it creates a new lens through which to evaluate MCMC inference, with clear implications for both theory and practice. The ability to rigorously bound inference error represents a significant step toward making probabilistic programming more trustworthy for non-expert users, which aligns perfectly with the broader goals of the field.

I find the paper's conceptual contribution to be highly novel and significant. The authors successfully transform a technique for estimating partition functions into a tool for measuring inference quality, which is a bold and valuable shift in perspective. While the experimental validation could be expanded, the theoretical novelty and practical applicability are sufficient to warrant acceptance. This is the kind of paper that could establish a new standard for evaluating MCMC inference quality.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making a complex technical contribution accessible. The connection to probabilistic programming is particularly timely, as the field moves toward making sophisticated inference accessible to non-experts. The authors' demonstration of BREAD's utility in debugging WebPPL further underscores its practical value.

In summary, this paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a long-standing problem in probabilistic inference. It makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications, and I believe it will be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners alike. The authors have successfully created a new framework for evaluating MCMC reliability, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

Pro:
- Introduces a fundamentally new way to measure MCMC inference reliability
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for a problem with no prior solutions
- Integrates directly into major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Demonstrates practical utility for model selection and debugging
- Addresses a problem of broad interest to probabilistic inference practitioners

Con:
- Limited experimental validation on complex real-world models
- Some reliance on simulated data (though the authors provide a thoughtful protocol for real data)
- Could more deeply compare with [CTM16] (though this is minor)

This paper represents a significant step toward making probabilistic inference more trustworthy and measurable. The novelty of the approach and its potential impact on the field outweigh any minor methodological limitations. I recommend acceptance.
