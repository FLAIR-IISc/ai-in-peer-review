- Extends BDMC to upper-bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between AIS/SMC-produced approximate samples and the true posterior.  
- Core mechanism: forward AIS gives a stochastic lower bound on log Z, reverse AIS (from an exact posterior sample) gives a stochastic upper bound, and the expected forward–reverse gap upper-bounds DJ(p,q).  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validates on toy problems (exact values) and five realistic models.  
- Uses a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to generate simulated-data proxies for real-data behavior.  
- Demonstrates practical utility by comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and by uncovering a WebPPL implementation bug.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorously links the BDMC gap to expected Jeffreys divergence using extended-state importance sampling and the data-processing inequality.  
- Guarantee is in expectation over AIS randomness and requires an exact posterior sample to produce the stochastic upper bound.  
- Limitation: the bound is not a high-probability or finite-sample guarantee; estimating the expectation suffers Monte Carlo error and sensitivity to heavy-tailed AIS weights.  
- Empirical reporting relies on quartiles; reviewer recommends reporting sample means with Monte Carlo standard errors/confidence intervals to match the theoretical claim.  
- The fitted-hyperparameter (parametric-bootstrap-style) protocol is pragmatic and empirically consistent here but lacks general guarantees and can fail under model misspecification.  
- Experiments are solid system integrations and diverse models, but lack compute-time-normalized comparisons, sensitivity analyses (annealing schedules, kernels), and any empirical SMC evaluation.  
- Clarity is good overall; exposition of AIS/BDMC and the expected-bound derivation is clear.  
- Suggested clarity improvements: prominently state preconditions (reversibility, need for exact samples) and provide more details/sensitivity on temperature schedules and transition kernels.  
- Originality: a natural but nontrivial extension of BDMC to posterior-sample quality; the real-data protocol and PPL integrations add practical novelty despite overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” work.  
- Significance: addresses a real need for principled assessment of approximate posterior samples in PPLs and is likely to be adopted by developers and researchers, though scope is limited to AIS/SMC-style algorithms.  
- Strengths: correct theoretical link to Jeffreys divergence, practical PPL integrations, convincing empirical demonstrations, and demonstrable debugging utility.  
- Weaknesses: expectation-only bound, applicability restricted to reversible AIS/SMC with exact samples (or proxies), heuristic real-data protocol without guarantees, no SMC experiments, and limited sensitivity/runtime analyses.  
- Reviewer suggestions for authors: report means with CIs, provide compute-time-normalized plots, run at least one SMC experiment, discuss variance-reduction or tail-bound approaches for finite-sample reliability, and clarify how many reverse chains/steps are needed in practice.  
- Recommendation: lean accept — technically sound and practically useful, but with limitations in guarantee scope and breadth of empirical evaluation.
