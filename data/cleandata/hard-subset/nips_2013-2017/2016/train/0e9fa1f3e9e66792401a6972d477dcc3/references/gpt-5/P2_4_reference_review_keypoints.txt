- Extends BDMC/AIS to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between AIS terminal distribution and the true posterior via forward/reverse AIS gap.  
- Derives that E[log R_rev^{-1} − log R_fwd] ≥ Jeffreys divergence and shows how one-sided KLs can be bounded with extra log R bounds.  
- Verifies tightness on toy problems where true Jeffreys divergence is computable.  
- Proposes a practical real-data protocol: simulate data from fitted hyperparameters and initialize reverse AIS from approximate posterior samples.  
- Integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates utility across models, including finding a WebPPL bug.  
- Builds directly on BDMC/AIS and is closely related to contemporaneous CTM16; the paper acknowledges overlap but needs sharper delineation.  
- Theoretical derivations are sound, but the guarantee is an expectation bound, not a high-probability or finite-sample result.  
- Important practical implications follow from the expectation-only nature: variance, number of chains, and heavy-tailed weights affect estimation reliability.  
- Empirical results: toy problems support tightness; real-data protocol shows aligned AIS behavior between real and simulated data across five Stan models.  
- Reverse AIS stability with varied MCMC initialization steps is reassuring; matrix factorization case study and debugging example are compelling.  
- For real datasets, guarantees are lost and the method becomes a diagnostic reliant on similarity between real and simulated data.  
- Heavy-tailed AIS weights and lack of tail guarantees complicate practical estimation; reporting quartiles rather than means is noted but multiple chains are used.  
- Clarity is generally good: exposition and organization are solid with helpful appendices, but formatting/citation glitches and minor typos remain.  
- The paper should explicitly state assumptions for expectation bounds and summarize the computational protocol for estimating E[gap] with confidence intervals.  
- Originality is moderate: conceptual step is incremental relative to BDMC/CTM16, but practical protocol and system integrations are novel and useful.  
- Significance is high for probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics, enabling automated, model-agnostic sample-quality checks in Stan/WebPPL.  
- Strengths: clear theoretical connection, system-level integrations, thoughtful real-data protocol, informative case studies, and practical debugging impact.  
- Weaknesses: expectation-only bound, no high-probability control, reliance on simulated-data similarity, overlap with CTM16, limited experiment scale/diversity, and citation/format issues.  
- Suggestions: provide variance/confidence estimates for E[gap], recommend number of AIS chains, consider robust estimators (median-of-means), and explore concentration bounds.  
- Further suggestions: study sensitivity to temperature schedules and MCMC kernels, compare against Stein discrepancy metrics, and include an explicit empirical comparison to CTM16.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-motivated paper with correct derivation and strong practical contributions; leans toward acceptance after clarifying relation to CTM16 and strengthening empirical estimation of the expectation bound.
