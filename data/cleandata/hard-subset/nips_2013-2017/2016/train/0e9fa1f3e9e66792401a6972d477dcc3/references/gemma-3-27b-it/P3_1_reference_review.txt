## NIPS 2016 Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the challenging problem of diagnosing the reliability of MCMC-based inference. The extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence provides a rigorous, though computationally demanding, method for assessing approximate posterior quality, particularly in scenarios where exact posteriors are tractable (e.g., simulated data). The practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan, alongside the proposed protocol for real-world data assessment, significantly enhances the paper’s impact. While the computational cost remains a substantial limitation, the paper’s theoretical soundness, clear presentation, and compelling experimental validation warrant a **Strong Accept** recommendation.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper addresses a long-standing issue in Bayesian inference: confidently validating the accuracy of MCMC algorithms. Existing convergence diagnostics are often insufficient for guaranteeing reliable results. The authors leverage recent work on BDMC (originally developed for marginal likelihood estimation) to derive an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. This bound offers a quantitative measure of inference accuracy, potentially applicable to a broader range of MCMC methods via its applicability to algorithms expressible as importance sampling. 

The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of MCMC diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998), marginal likelihood estimation (Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015), and sequential Monte Carlo (del Moral et al., 2006). They also acknowledge related independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) which similarly utilizes BDMC for divergence estimation. The discussion of related approaches is thorough and places the current work in a well-defined context. The connection to parametric bootstrapping (Efron & Tibshirani, 1998) in the real-world data protocol is a clever and sensible approach.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence upper bound is mathematically sound and builds upon established results in BDMC. The analysis is clear and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method (BREAD).
* **Novel Application:** Extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to directly assess posterior accuracy is a significant and original contribution.
* **Practical Implementation:** The authors demonstrate a commitment to real-world applicability by integrating BREAD into two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. This lowers the barrier to adoption and facilitates wider use of the method.
* **Protocol for Real Data:**  The proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets—inferring hyperparameters, simulating data, and validating consistency—is a pragmatic solution to the inherent limitation of requiring exact posterior samples.
* **Compelling Experiments:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of BREAD in identifying differences in model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even in detecting a bug in WebPPL.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the explanations are intuitive.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The primary limitation of BREAD is its computational expense. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains, particularly for complex models, can be extremely time-consuming. This restricts its applicability to relatively small and tractable models. While acknowledged by the authors, this is a significant hurdle.
* **Dependence on Exact Posterior Samples (for Simulation):** The method fundamentally relies on the ability to generate exact posterior samples for simulated data.  While realistic for many models, this is not universally true, limiting the scope of its direct applicability. The proposed protocol attempts to mitigate this, but adds another layer of approximation.
* **Assumptions of AIS:** BREAD inherits the limitations of AIS. While applicable to algorithms *viewable* as importance sampling, it isn't directly applicable to all MCMC methods.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge the cost, a more detailed discussion of strategies to mitigate it (e.g., variance reduction techniques, parallelization) would be beneficial.



**4. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous and quantitative approach to assessing MCMC reliability.
* Successfully implemented in widely used probabilistic programming languages.
* Validated on realistic examples and used to uncover a bug in existing software.
* Addresses a long-standing and important problem in Bayesian inference.
* Clear, well-written, and technically sound.

**Against Acceptance:**

* High computational cost limits its applicability to complex, real-world problems.
* Reliance on exact posterior samples for simulations restricts its direct use.




**5. Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the computational limitations, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The theoretical novelty, practical implementation, and insightful experiments make this a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference.  The method provides a strong benchmark for evaluating and improving MCMC algorithms and offers a powerful tool for building confidence in probabilistic modeling results.  Therefore, I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016.
