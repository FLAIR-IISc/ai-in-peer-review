

**Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), extending the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to rigorously measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that BDMC can be used to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, and propose a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters. The paper integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validating it on multiple probabilistic models.

The work builds on Grosse et al. (2015), which introduced BDMC for estimating log marginal likelihoods, and extends it to provide bounds on the Jeffreys divergence rather than just the marginal likelihood. The theoretical derivation is clear and carefully connects to previous work, showing that the BDMC upper bound on the log marginal likelihood ratio can be used to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The authors validate their theoretical claims on toy distributions where both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound can be computed exactly, showing good agreement between the two.

A significant practical contribution is the protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data. The authors propose simulating data from fitted hyperparameters (obtained from inference on real data) and using these to evaluate inference quality. Their validation experiments (Section 5.1) demonstrate that inference behavior on simulated data closely matches that on real data for a range of models, providing strong empirical evidence for the protocol's validity. The paper also showcases BREAD's utility for model selection (comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization) and debugging (identifying a bug in WebPPL's Gaussian sampling implementation).

The paper excels in quality, with sound theoretical foundations and thorough experimental validation. The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound is rigorous, and the experimental section provides comprehensive validation of the method's key components. The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized sections that build logically from background to method to validation.

The originality is strong, as the authors extend BDMC to a novel application (bounding Jeffreys divergence) and develop a practical protocol for real-world use. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work (notably Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) by showing that their approach provides rigorous bounds when using the true posterior, whereas the subjective divergence in prior work lacks this guarantee unless the reference distribution is the true posterior.

The significance is high. Validating MCMC inference is a fundamental challenge in probabilistic modeling, and current methods (like convergence diagnostics) are heuristic and cannot confirm convergence. BREAD provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming systems where non-expert users need confidence in results. The method's ability to guide modeling decisions (e.g., choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed representations) and identify implementation bugs adds substantial practical value.

The main limitations include:
1. The method is restricted to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), which excludes many traditional MCMC methods. While the authors argue these algorithms involve similar design choices, this limitation is significant.
2. The real-world data protocol relies on the assumption that data simulated from fitted hyperparameters will be representative of the real data, which might not hold for all models or datasets. The authors provide validation, but a more comprehensive analysis of when this assumption fails would strengthen the paper.
3. The computational cost of BREAD is not discussed, which could be prohibitive for large-scale problems.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the background and theoretical derivations. The experimental section is thorough, with detailed validation of the method's key components. The integration with WebPPL and Stan and the demonstration of practical use cases (model comparison, debugging) are particularly strong.

To further improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a more detailed discussion of computational cost, including how it scales with problem size and number of AIS steps.
2. Providing a more thorough exploration of the real-world data protocol's limitations through experiments where the assumption of representative simulated data fails.
3. Including a comparison with other methods for measuring inference quality (e.g., Gorham & Mackey, 2015) to better contextualize BREAD's advantages.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference with a theoretically sound and practically useful method. While the scope of applicable algorithms is limited, this limitation is acknowledged and does not undermine the paper's main contribution. The method has clear potential to improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems and guide modeling decisions. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
