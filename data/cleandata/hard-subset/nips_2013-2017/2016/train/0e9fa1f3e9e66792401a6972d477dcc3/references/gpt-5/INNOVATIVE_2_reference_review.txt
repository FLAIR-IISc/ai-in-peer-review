Summary
The paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of sampling-based posterior inference. The core idea is to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior by “sandwiching” the log marginal likelihood via forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS). The forward chain yields a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1; with an exact posterior sample, a reverse AIS run provides a stochastic upper bound. The expected gap between these stochastic bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the target posterior. The authors further: (i) show on toy problems that the bound tracks the true Jeffreys divergence closely; (ii) propose a practical protocol for real data by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating from the fitted model, and checking that AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data; and (iii) integrate the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, using it to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and to uncover a bug in WebPPL.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Repurposing BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding posterior-sample divergence is a crisp, elegant idea. Framing the forward–reverse AIS log-gap as an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is new and insightful, and directly targets what users care about: whether approximate samples are close to the true posterior.
- Practical relevance: Integrations into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrations across multiple models, make this more than a position paper. The tool surfaces actionable insights (e.g., representation choices in matrix factorization) and even catches an implementation bug.
- Sensible protocol for real data: The fitted-hyperparameter simulation approach, and the empirical validation that real vs. simulated AIS curves align, provide a pragmatic path when exact posterior samples are unavailable on real datasets.
- Scope: Applicable to AIS/SMC-style samplers, which cover a large class of PPL backends and share design choices with more traditional MCMC.
- Relation to concurrent work: The connection to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka’s “subjective divergence” is acknowledged and contrasted.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees rely on an exact posterior sample: The rigorous bound holds only for simulated data or when an exact posterior sample can be produced. The fitted-hyperparameter heuristic forfeits guarantees. The empirical checks are convincing but limited; a more systematic analysis of failure modes (e.g., under model misspecification) would strengthen the case.
- Expectation vs. high-probability guarantees: The Jeffreys divergence is upper-bounded in expectation by the log-gap; in practice, users need finite-sample, high-probability statements. The paper notes heavy tails and avoids averaging due to the analysis, leaving open how to provide robust, actionable uncertainty quantification on the bound from a finite number of AIS runs.
- Limited validation of tightness: Exact comparisons of the bound to the true Jeffreys divergence are only on toy distributions. It would be helpful to show calibration in higher-dimensional or multimodal settings where AIS is stressed.
- Generality to non-AIS workflows: While the authors argue AIS/SMC share design choices with MCMC, direct evaluation of standard Stan NUTS sampling (without AIS tempering) is out of scope. The implications for everyday HMC/NUTS use are therefore indirect.
- Some presentational issues: A few references are incomplete; some derivations and implementation details are deferred to appendices.

Quality
The theoretical derivation is sound, clear, and builds appropriately on BDMC. The experimental methodology is careful, and the real/simulated consistency checks are persuasive within the chosen models. The reliance on expectation bounds and lack of finite-sample concentration for the gap is a notable limitation for a diagnostic tool.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The background on AIS/BDMC is helpful. It would help to more explicitly state what randomness the expectations are taken over and to offer practical guidance on the number of AIS chains/temperatures needed to get stable estimates. Cleaning up reference formatting would improve polish.

Originality
High. The shift from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding sample quality via a Jeffreys divergence interpretation is a meaningful conceptual advance. The proposed real-data protocol and PPL integrations add novelty in practice. The positioning relative to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriate.

Significance
Potentially high. A rigorous, automated way to assess inference quality is valuable for PPLs and Bayesian workflows. Even as a simulated-data diagnostic, BREAD can guide design choices and catch implementation issues. If further developed to yield robust finite-sample guarantees or extended reference distributions, it could become standard practice.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Can you provide finite-sample, high-probability bounds on the Jeffreys divergence using multiple AIS runs (e.g., via Chernoff/Markov-style results or median-of-means) and discuss tradeoffs?
- How sensitive are the bounds to temperature schedules and choice of transition kernels? Some ablations or rules-of-thumb would aid practitioners.
- Could you quantify tightness across a broader suite of synthetic targets (e.g., higher-dimensional Gaussians with strong correlations, mixture models with separated modes)?
- For the fitted-hyperparameter protocol, can you bound the bias introduced when the reverse chain is initialized from an approximate posterior (perhaps building on the “subjective divergence” framework)?
- Please add more details on the Stan integration (how HMC/NUTS is used within AIS transitions), and share code to facilitate adoption.

Decision
Accept. The paper presents a fresh, useful idea with clear theoretical grounding and practical impact. Despite limitations around exact posterior samples and finite-sample guarantees, the contribution opens a new direction for principled assessment of sampler reliability and is likely to influence both research and tooling in probabilistic programming.
