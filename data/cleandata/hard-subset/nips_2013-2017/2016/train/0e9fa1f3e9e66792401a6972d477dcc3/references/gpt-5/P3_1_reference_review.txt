Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of AIS/SMC-based posterior sampling. The key result is that the gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS log-partition estimators, ˆB = log(ˆRrev−1) − log(ˆR), upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of AIS’s final state qfwd(xT) and the true target pT. The authors implement this Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD) in WebPPL and Stan, propose a pragmatic protocol for real data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, validate behavior across five models, and use BREAD to compare model representations and even debug an inference engine.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Clear, elegant extension of BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to sample-quality assessment. The derivation connecting E[log ˆR] and E[log ˆRrev] to one-sided KLs and their sum to Jeffreys divergence is sound and useful.
- Practical relevance: Implementations in two PPLs, with demonstrations across regression, time series, clustering, and matrix factorization. The method exposes real design tradeoffs (collapsed vs uncollapsed representations) and even surfaced a bug in WebPPL.
- Protocol for real data: Sensible parametric-bootstrap–like approach to emulate real-data geometry by simulating at fitted hyperparameters, accompanied by sanity checks comparing forward-AIS behavior across real and simulated datasets.
- Clarity about scope: The paper is forthright about guarantees holding for simulated data with access to an exact posterior sample and that real-data analysis is heuristic.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Expectation-only bound: The primary guarantee is E[ˆB] ≥ J. In practice the paper frequently presents distributions of single-run estimates or quartiles, which do not constitute a bound on J. There is no high-probability or finite-sample confidence machinery around E[ˆB], and the variance of log-AIS estimators can be large. As a result, users may over-interpret single-run gaps as guarantees.
- Applicability restricted: BREAD applies to algorithms that admit an extended-space IS view with a tractable reverse procedure (AIS and some SMC variants) and requires an exact posterior sample for the reverse direction. It does not directly validate standard MCMC targeting p (e.g., vanilla NUTS on the posterior) without embedding it within AIS transitions across temperatures, so claims about informing “MCMC more broadly” are indirect.
- Real-data protocol breaks guarantees: Initializing the reverse chain from an approximate posterior obtained by brief MCMC at fitted hyperparameters removes the theoretical guarantee; the validation is empirical and could fail in multimodal, weakly identified, or misspecified settings (the appendix notes a misspecification example). More systematic stress tests would be valuable.
- Reverse SMC underspecified: The paper states applicability to SMC, but the reverse construction with resampling is nontrivial; a precise description and proof of the reverse estimator’s properties are missing here.
- Tightness analysis limited: Tightness of E[ˆB] as an estimate of J is shown on small toy problems; no conditions (e.g., smoothness of the annealing path, bounds on log-weight variance) are provided to predict when the bound tracks J well.
- Sensitivity and tuning: The gap depends on the annealing schedule, number of temperatures, and kernels. Practical guidance or adaptive schedule optimization to tighten bounds is not developed.

Quality
- Theory: The core inequalities (Eqs. 6–12) are correct and extend BDMC in a meaningful way. The use of marginalization to relate the bias of log ˆR and log ˆRrev to KLs is clean.
- Empirics: Experiments are careful and relevant, but the analysis would benefit from reporting Monte Carlo estimates of E[ˆB] with confidence intervals across independent runs and from discussing the number of runs needed to achieve a desired precision.
- Implementation: Integration with WebPPL and Stan and the debugging example convincingly support the practical utility of BREAD.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. It would help to present a concise algorithmic summary: how to compute ˆB in practice, how many independent runs are needed, and how to form an estimate and CI for E[ˆB]. Clarify explicitly that qfwd(xT) is the distribution of the final AIS state and that the guarantee is in expectation.
- The SMC applicability should be spelled out or deferred to an appendix.

Originality
- The work is a novel and useful reframing of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence of AIS/SMC samples. The connection to contemporaneous “subjective divergence” is appropriately acknowledged; the distinction (true posterior vs reference initialization) is clear.

Significance
- For PPL users and developers, a rigorous, automatable yardstick for sample quality on simulated data is valuable. Even with scope limitations, BREAD advances the state of the art in diagnosing and comparing inference strategies, and the debugging use case is compelling.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide high-confidence bounds on E[log ˆR] and E[log ˆRrev] by combining multiple runs, e.g., with concentration inequalities for stochastic lower/upper bounds (cf. Markov-based tail bounds and techniques like Gogate et al. 2007), or report bootstrap/t-based one-sided CIs.
- Offer guidance on choosing temperature schedules and number of steps to control the gap; explore schedule optimization to minimize E[ˆB].
- For real data, consider using stronger reference distributions (e.g., variational approximations) and quantify how initialization quality affects reverse estimates.
- Elaborate the reverse SMC construction and provide experiments with resampling-heavy SMC to demonstrate applicability.
- Include a challenging multimodal or high-dimensional example where standard diagnostics look acceptable but BREAD indicates non-negligible divergence.

Pros
- Solid theoretical contribution connecting BDMC to Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical implementations and diverse experiments.
- Clear utility for PPL development and model/algorithm comparison.
- Honest discussion of scope and limitations.

Cons
- Expectation-only guarantee without finite-sample CIs in practice.
- Limited applicability beyond AIS/SMC and simulated-data settings.
- Sparse theory on tightness and on SMC reverse construction.

Overall recommendation
A strong and practically impactful paper. Despite limitations around expectation-only guarantees and scope, the contribution is clear, useful, and well validated. I recommend acceptance, with the above suggestions to strengthen practical guidance and clarity.
