

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method to measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors build on Grosse et al. (2015) to derive an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence, which they validate on toy distributions where exact computation is possible. They also propose a practical protocol for applying their method to real-world data by inferring hyperparameters on real data, simulating data from those hyperparameters, and measuring inference quality on the simulated data. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility for model representation analysis and debugging.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical derivation (Section 3.1) showing how BDMC can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence. The mathematical arguments are rigorous and well-motivated, extending prior work on BDMC in a meaningful way.

2. **Practical Validation:** The authors carefully validate their method through multiple experiments (Section 5). The validation of the consistency between real and simulated data (5.1.1) and the robustness of the approximate posterior sampling protocol (5.1.2) are particularly convincing.

3. **Practical Utility:** The integration with WebPPL and Stan demonstrates tangible value. The method's ability to identify a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2) is a compelling practical demonstration of its utility for debugging inference implementations.

4. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper effectively articulates the challenge of rigorously validating MCMC inference, which is a significant gap in the probabilistic modeling literature, especially for non-expert users of probabilistic programming languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Applicability:** The method is restricted to inference algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC), which represents a subset of MCMC methods. The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the broader applicability of the approach.

2. **Assumptions About Real-World Data:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on the assumption that data simulated from fitted hyperparameters is representative of the real data. While the authors validate this assumption on five models, they don't explore scenarios where this might fail (e.g., model misspecification), which would be valuable for users of the method.

3. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of implementing BREAD, which could be prohibitive for large-scale applications. The need for both forward and reverse AIS chains would likely double the computational requirements compared to standard inference.

4. **Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper acknowledges Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), the comparison is limited. A more detailed discussion of how BREAD differs from their approach, particularly regarding the use of approximate posterior samples versus exact posterior samples, would strengthen the contribution.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. **Expand the Scope Discussion:** In the revised paper, it would be valuable to more thoroughly discuss the limitations of the method with respect to different MCMC algorithms. The authors could explore whether their approach could be adapted to other inference methods, or at least clearly delineate the boundaries of its applicability.

2. **Address Computational Cost:** The authors should add a discussion of the computational cost of BREAD, including how it scales with model size and complexity. This would help potential users assess whether the method is practical for their specific use cases.

3. **Include More Real-World Examples:** While the five models in Section 5.1 are good for validation, additional examples of how BREAD has been used to guide model development in real research settings would strengthen the significance argument.

4. **Clarify Relationship with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016):** A more detailed comparison with this related work would help readers understand the unique contributions of BREAD. Specifically, the paper should clarify how the use of approximate posterior samples (as in their protocol) affects the theoretical guarantees compared to using exact posterior samples.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference. The theoretical contribution is solid, and the practical validation is thorough. While the scope of the method is limited to specific inference algorithms, the paper demonstrates its utility in a compelling way through integration with popular probabilistic programming languages and real-world applications. The method provides a valuable tool for users of probabilistic programming languages to assess the reliability of their inference results.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a rigorous, practical method for evaluating the quality of approximate posterior inference. While the scope is somewhat limited, the approach is well-motivated and the validation is convincing. The paper advances the state of the art in assessing inference quality beyond heuristic convergence diagnostics, and it has clear potential to improve the reliability of automatic inference systems.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is of high quality, addresses an important problem, and provides a valuable tool for practitioners. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in a revised version, but they do not undermine the paper's core contribution.
