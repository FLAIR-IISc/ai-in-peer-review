This paper proposes BREAD, a method leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior, specifically for algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space like AIS and SMC. While the idea of extending BDMC for posterior evaluation is natural, and the theoretical grounding appears sound, I find the paper lacking in convincing empirical validation and overly reliant on simulated data, particularly given the stated goal of aiding users of probabilistic programming languages.

The core contribution – bounding Jeffreys divergence – is technically solid, building logically on prior BDMC work [GGA15]. The derivation of the estimator and its relationship to KL divergences is clearly presented. However, the "toy distribution" experiments, while demonstrating the accuracy of the bound *when the true divergence is known*, are insufficient to establish confidence in its practical utility. Real-world problems rarely afford such convenient analytical tractability. The demonstration that the bound is “a fairly good match” in these limited cases feels weak. 

The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data – fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating from those, and then evaluating on simulated data – is a reasonable attempt to address the limitation of needing true posterior samples. However, the validation of this protocol (Section 5.1) is also limited. The authors find “nearly identical” AIS curves for real and simulated data across five models. This is encouraging, but “nearly identical” is not a rigorous assessment. Quantitative comparisons (e.g., statistical tests on the AIS estimates) are missing. The conclusion that simulated data is a “useful proxy” feels premature.  The sensitivity analysis (varying the number of MCMC steps, S) is better, finding consistent results, but this primarily speaks to the robustness of the *reverse* AIS step within the protocol, not the protocol’s overall validity.

A significant weakness is the limited scope of the experiments. The matrix factorization example (Section 5.2) is interesting, revealing differences in performance between WebPPL and Stan based on variable collapsing. However, this is a single example, and the conclusions are qualified as being specific to the chosen problem size. More diverse models and datasets are needed to demonstrate the broad applicability of BREAD. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL, while a positive outcome, feels almost accidental and doesn’t constitute a strong argument for the general utility of the method.

The writing is generally clear, but occasionally dense. The notation is standard, but readers unfamiliar with BDMC and AIS will require some effort to follow the derivations. The related work section is comprehensive, but could benefit from a more critical assessment of how BREAD differs from existing diagnostics (e.g., convergence diagnostics mentioned briefly). 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is sound and extends a previously published method.
*   The idea of rigorously bounding divergence is valuable, particularly for assessing probabilistic programming systems.
*   The authors have implemented BREAD in two widely used probabilistic programming languages.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is weak and overly reliant on simulated data.
*   The real-world data validation protocol lacks rigorous quantitative assessment.
*   The experiments are limited in scope and do not demonstrate broad applicability.
*   The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed, impactful contribution.



Overall, I believe this paper falls short of NIPS standards. It presents a technically correct but not particularly groundbreaking extension of BDMC, and the empirical evidence provided is insufficient to justify its practical utility. The limited scope and reliance on simulated data are significant concerns. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to significantly expand the experimental validation and address the concerns raised above before resubmitting. A strong focus on real-world datasets and a more rigorous evaluation of the proposed protocol are essential.
