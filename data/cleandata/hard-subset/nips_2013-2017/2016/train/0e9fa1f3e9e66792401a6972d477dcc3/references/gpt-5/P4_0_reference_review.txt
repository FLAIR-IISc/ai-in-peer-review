Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by annealed importance sampling (AIS; Neal, 2001) or related methods and the true posterior. The key observation is that forward AIS yields, in expectation, a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1, while reverse AIS—initialized from an exact draw from the target—yields, in expectation, a stochastic upper bound. The expected gap between these two stochastic bounds equals or exceeds the Jeffreys divergence between the marginal distribution of AIS’s final state and the target posterior.

The authors (i) formalize this expected bound on Jeffreys divergence by refining the BDMC analysis, (ii) show on toy distributions that the bound is often numerically tight, (iii) propose a protocol to extrapolate from simulated to real data by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data at those hyperparameters, and validating that forward-AIS behaviors align between real and simulated datasets, and (iv) integrate the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, using it to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and to identify a bug in WebPPL.

This work builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006), and BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and is closely related to concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which shows that the BDMC-like gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence when initialized from the exact posterior. The paper also relates to sample-quality diagnostics based on Stein’s method (Gorham & Mackey, 2015) and to classical MCMC convergence diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Cowles & Carlin, 1996; Brooks & Gelman, 1998).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical connection: The derivation cleanly ties the expected forward–reverse AIS log-ratio gap to the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) and pT, giving a principled, quantitative diagnostic of sample quality for AIS-like samplers.
- Practical tooling: Integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates usability. The method surfaced an implementation bug, highlighting value for testing probabilistic programming systems.
- Sensible protocol for real data: The fitted-hyperparameter simulation strategy, and the empirical check that forward-AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data, provide a pragmatic workflow when exact posterior samples are unavailable.
- Empirical breadth across models: Experiments on linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and Gaussian mixtures give initial evidence of generality and utility, including an informative comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Guarantees limited to expectation and simulated data: The central bound is in expectation over AIS randomness, not a high-probability or per-run bound. For real data, the approach lacks formal guarantees because reverse AIS is initialized from approximate posterior samples via fitted hyperparameters. The empirical validations are encouraging but do not provide a theoretical guarantee of conservativeness.
- Estimation and variability: The paper acknowledges heavy-tailed distributions of AIS log-weights and reports quartiles rather than means, but the methodology for estimating E[gap] and quantifying its uncertainty is not fully specified. Guidance on the number of chains, temperatures, and schedules required to obtain reliable estimates would improve reproducibility.
- AIS/transition-kernel details: The analysis assumes invariant and reversible kernels at each temperature. The paper states that “standard” kernels are used, but does not fully specify whether, for Stan, HMC/NUTS parameters are fixed across temperatures and whether any adaptation might violate invariance within AIS transitions. More detail is important for technical soundness.
- Limited evaluation of other algorithms: Although the theory applies to SMC and other extended-space importance sampling schemes, experiments focus on AIS. Demonstrating applicability to SMC would strengthen the claim of broader relevance.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with helpful background. However, some references appear incomplete or with placeholders, and a few notational choices (e.g., mixing of tildes and reversed indices) could be streamlined. The paper would benefit from explicit algorithmic pseudocode for the forward and reverse AIS procedures as instantiated in WebPPL and Stan.

Originality
- The main theoretical insight—bounding the Jeffreys divergence via the expected BDMC gap—is a natural but nontrivial extension of BDMC’s log-partition bounds and aligns with concurrent “subjective divergence” work. Novelty lies in the specific derivation, the emphasis on Jeffreys divergence as a diagnostic of sample quality, and the end-to-end protocol and tooling in PPLs. Given the concurrent work, the conceptual novelty is moderate but still meaningful.

Significance
- Providing a quantitative, model-agnostic diagnostic with rigorous guarantees on simulated data is valuable for both researchers and practitioners, especially in probabilistic programming. The debugging use case and representation-choice study show practical impact. Broader adoption would benefit from more guidance on schedules, chain counts, and computation/accuracy trade-offs, and from demonstrations on larger-scale models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more explicit estimator for E[gap], with confidence intervals or concentration diagnostics, and practical guidance on the number of chains and temperatures. Consider variance reduction or tail-robust estimators (e.g., combining multiple runs as in Gogate et al., 2007).
- Clarify AIS transition kernels in Stan (e.g., HMC/NUTS parameter settings per temperature, reversibility, and invariance) and in WebPPL, to ensure assumptions hold.
- Include experiments with SMC-based samplers to support the claimed scope.
- Analyze tightness more broadly than toy examples, e.g., across the benchmark models, and discuss when the gap might systematically under- or overestimate Jeffreys divergence.
- Document and release code and scripts to reproduce all results, with details on annealing schedules, step counts, and runtime.
- Improve reference formatting and fix placeholder citations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a principled, quantitative diagnostic for approximate posterior sampling with clear theoretical grounding.
- Practical integration into major PPLs and evidence of utility for model representation choices and debugging.
- Addresses an important gap in assessing MCMC-based inference reliability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are expectation-based and limited to simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic without formal guarantees.
- Experimental evaluation focuses on AIS and relatively modest models; breadth and scale could be expanded.
- Some technical details (kernels, adaptation, schedules) are insufficiently specified, making reproducibility and soundness harder to assess.

Overall, this is a solid and useful contribution that advances the evaluation of MCMC-based inference, with clear potential impact in probabilistic programming and beyond.
