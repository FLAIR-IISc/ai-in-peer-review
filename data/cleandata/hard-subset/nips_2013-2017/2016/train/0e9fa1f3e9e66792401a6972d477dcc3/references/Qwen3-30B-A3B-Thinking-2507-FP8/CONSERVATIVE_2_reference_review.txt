

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors build on Grosse et al. (2015) to extend BDMC to evaluate MCMC-based inference, particularly for algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core contribution is a theoretical derivation showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, along with a protocol for applying this to real-world data through simulation from fitted hyperparameters.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough background on AIS and BDMC.
- The theoretical derivation of the upper bound is logically presented and appears sound.
- The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical applicability.
- The debugging example (identifying a bug in WebPPL's Gaussian sampling) shows the method's potential utility for implementation validation.
- The paper properly references related work, including the independent Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) work.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Scope and Practical Utility:** The method is only applicable to specific algorithms (AIS/SMC) and requires exact posterior samples, which are only available on simulated data. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently address how this restricts the method's practical value for the vast majority of MCMC applications. Most real-world problems don't have access to exact posterior samples, making the core contribution largely theoretical rather than practical.

2. **Unsubstantiated Real-World Protocol:** The proposed protocol for real-world data (simulating from fitted hyperparameters) is presented as a solution to the "exact posterior" limitation, but the validation is minimal. The paper only tests this protocol on five models from Stan's manual, with no analysis of how well it generalizes to more complex or diverse real-world problems. The claim that "data simulated using fitted hyperparameters can be a useful proxy for real data" is not rigorously justified, as the paper doesn't address the fundamental issue that fitted hyperparameters may not capture the true data-generating process.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper doesn't compare BREAD to existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size) in terms of practical utility, computational cost, or reliability. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether BREAD offers meaningful advantages over established, widely-used methods.

4. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims BREAD provides "a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference," but the method is neither general (only applicable to AIS/SMC) nor automatic (requires exact posterior samples, which are unavailable in most real-world scenarios).

5. **Limited Validation:** The "validation" on toy distributions (Appendix B) is insufficient to demonstrate the method's reliability. The paper shows the bound is "a good match" on some small examples, but doesn't provide evidence that the bound remains tight for more complex, high-dimensional problems where MCMC reliability is most critical.

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper makes a modest extension of existing BDMC theory to a specific application. While the theoretical derivation is correct, the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The core idea builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015), and the application to MCMC inference is a natural extension, not a novel conceptual advance. The related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) makes similar claims, suggesting the space is already being explored.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper that makes a minor theoretical extension to an existing method. However, the practical limitations of the approach (requiring exact posterior samples) severely restrict its applicability to real-world problems. The validation is insufficient to support the claims of "rigorous" and "general" evaluation. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that BREAD offers meaningful advantages over established convergence diagnostics, which are widely used and well-understood in practice.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper is well-written and technically correct, it presents a method with severe practical limitations that don't address the core problem of validating MCMC inference in the general case. The contribution is incremental and not sufficiently significant for NIPS. The authors would need to substantially extend the method to work without exact posterior samples or provide compelling evidence that their protocol for real-world data is reliable across diverse applications before the paper would be appropriate for publication.
