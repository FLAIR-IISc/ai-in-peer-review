

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors claim their method provides rigorous bounds on MCMC inference quality, with applications to probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan.

The paper's main technical contribution is an extension of the BDMC framework to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The authors derive this bound theoretically and validate it on small toy examples where the true Jeffreys divergence can be computed exactly. They then propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and using these simulations to evaluate inference quality.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is mathematically sound and builds logically on prior BDMC work [GGA15].
2. The integration with WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical applicability.
3. The debugging example (Section 5.2.2) shows BREAD can uncover implementation errors, which is valuable for probabilistic programming systems.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Validation:** The paper's validation is severely constrained. The only rigorous validation occurs on toy distributions where both the true Jeffreys divergence and the bound can be computed exactly. For practical applications, the paper shows results on only five models (Section 5.1), which is insufficient to demonstrate the method's reliability across diverse, realistic problems. The paper claims "rigorous bounds" but fails to show how well these bounds perform on models where the true divergence is unknown.

2. **Overstated Claims:** The paper overstates the practical utility of BREAD. The method requires exact posterior samples, which are only available for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2) assumes that data simulated from fitted hyperparameters will behave like real data, but this assumption is not rigorously validated beyond the five models studied. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that the protocol works generally.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper makes no meaningful comparison to established MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Geweke, or visual diagnostics). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether BREAD provides any advantage over existing, well-established methods for assessing MCMC reliability.

4. **Narrow Scope:** BREAD is only applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC), which excludes most common MCMC methods like standard Metropolis-Hastings or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo. The paper claims "this method is applicable to algorithms which can be viewed as importance sampling" but fails to demonstrate how this limited scope impacts its practical utility.

5. **Computational Cost:** The method requires running both forward and reverse AIS, which substantially increases computational cost compared to standard MCMC convergence diagnostics. The paper doesn't address whether this additional cost is justified by the benefits of the method.

**Significance and Contribution:**
While the theoretical extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence is mathematically interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate that this extension provides significant practical advantages over existing methods. The validation is insufficient to support the claim that BREAD provides a "rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference" as stated in the introduction. The paper's contribution appears to be a minor extension of an existing framework rather than a substantial advance in MCMC reliability assessment.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a theoretically sound extension of BDMC, it lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims about practical utility. The limited validation on toy examples and five models does not adequately demonstrate that BREAD provides meaningful advantages over established convergence diagnostics. The paper's claims about rigorous bounds and practical applicability are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented.

The field would benefit more from a paper that thoroughly compares BREAD to established diagnostics across a wide range of models and datasets, or that demonstrates clear practical advantages in real-world applications. As presented, this work does not advance the state of the art in MCMC reliability assessment sufficiently for publication at NIPS.
