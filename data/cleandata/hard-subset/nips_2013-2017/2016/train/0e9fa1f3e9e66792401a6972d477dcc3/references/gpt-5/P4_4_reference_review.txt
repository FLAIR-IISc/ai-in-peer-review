Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [Grosse et al., 2015] to assess the reliability of sampling-based posterior inference, specifically for methods that can be framed as importance sampling on extended state spaces, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) [Neal, 2001] and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) [del Moral et al., 2006]. The core idea is to run AIS in both directions along a temperature path: forward (from prior to posterior) and reverse (from posterior to prior). The forward AIS log-estimate is a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1, while the reverse log-estimate provides a stochastic upper bound when initialized from an exact posterior sample (as in BDMC). The paper refines the BDMC analysis to show that the expected gap between the reverse and forward log-estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS sample distribution qfwd(xT) and the target pT(xT). This yields a quantitative measure of sample quality.

Beyond simulated data, where an exact posterior sample is available via ancestral sampling (for models where joint sampling is tractable), the paper proposes a practical protocol for real datasets: fit hyperparameters on the real data; simulate data from those fitted hyperparameters; and use the simulated setting to probe sampler behavior. For the reverse chain initialization (needed to form an upper bound), the authors use a short MCMC run from the fitted hyperparameters to approximate a posterior draw. They implement these ideas in WebPPL and Stan, validate on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures), explore representation choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) across systems, and demonstrate how the method surfaced a bug in WebPPL.

The work connects to: BDMC for marginal likelihood sandwiching [Grosse et al., 2015], conservative likelihood estimates via reverse annealing [Burda et al., 2015], AIS foundations [Neal, 1996; 2001; Jarzynski, 1997], SMC [del Moral et al., 2006], and sample quality assessment via Stein discrepancies [Gorham and Mackey, 2015]. It is contemporaneous with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [2016], who define subjective divergence; when initialized at the true posterior, their estimator reduces to the same Jeffreys divergence upper bound in expectation.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Derivation: The paper gives a clear derivation that E[log R̂] and E[log R̂rev] differ from log ZT − log Z1 by KL terms between joint forward/reverse path distributions. Using marginalization (data processing) yields bounds by DKL(qfwd(xT) || pT(xT)) and DKL(pT(xT) || qfwd(xT)), and the expected gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. The steps are standard and appear correct under the AIS assumptions (reversible kernels leaving pt invariant; unbiasedness of AIS estimator).
- Scope: The guarantee is an expectation bound over AIS randomness, not a per-run bound, and requires an exact posterior sample for the reverse direction. The paper is careful to restrict rigorous claims to simulated-data settings and to characterize the real-data protocol as heuristic.
- Empirics: On toy problems (Appendix B), the expected gap reportedly tracks the true Jeffreys divergence closely; on several real(istic) models implemented in Stan/WebPPL, the method produces sensible trends and comparisons. The validation that simulated-data behavior mirrors real-data forward AIS behavior is informative, though indirect. The sensitivity study on the number of MCMC steps used to approximate reverse initialization suggests robustness in practice.
- Caveats: The variance of AIS log-weights can be high; the paper uses quartiles to display distributions and notes limitations of averaging for the log estimators used in the divergence bound. There is limited quantitative analysis of confidence intervals or computational budgets required to estimate the expected gap reliably.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized:
- Background on AIS, stochastic bounds, and BDMC is concise and accurate.
- The derivation of the expected Jeffreys divergence upper bound is clear.
- The real-data protocol is described plainly, including its limitations.
- The implementation and experiments are explained with enough detail to follow the setup; appendices reportedly contain model code and further results.
Suggested improvements:
- Provide more guidance on choosing annealing schedules and per-temperature transition counts, as these strongly influence bound tightness and variance.
- Include more details (or a summary) from Appendix B on the conditions under which the bound is tight vs loose.
- Clarify whether the analysis and implementation extend to SMC in this work (beyond stating applicability); if implemented, briefly outline the adaptations.

Originality
- The main theoretical contribution—connecting the BDMC sandwich to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence for AIS/extended-state samplers—is a novel and useful reframing. BDMC had been used for marginal likelihood assessment; using it to assess posterior sample quality is original.
- The real-data evaluation protocol is pragmatic and fills an important gap, though it is heuristic.
- The integration into two probabilistic programming systems and the demonstration on common models provide practical value.
- The relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016] is appropriately discussed.

Significance
- For AIS/SMC-like samplers and probabilistic programming contexts where those are used, the method provides a principled, quantitative way to assess sample quality and to compare modeling/inference choices (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed). The debugging use case is compelling.
- Limitations reduce generality: exact posterior initialization is required for formal guarantees (hence simulated data), and the bound is in expectation; the real-data heuristic is not guaranteed. The approach does not directly deliver quantitative guarantees for arbitrary MCMC samplers that cannot be cast within the extended-state IS framework.
- Nonetheless, the contribution is likely to influence practice in PPLs and research on benchmarking inference.

Pros
- Clear, correct derivation of an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence from BDMC/AIS.
- Practical protocol bridging simulated and real data; extensive experiments across systems and models.
- Actionable insights into model representation choices; demonstrated debugging utility.
- Well-related to prior work; timely with respect to PPL evaluation needs.

Cons
- Guarantees rely on simulated data and exact posterior samples; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Bound is in expectation; guidance on estimating the expectation with controlled uncertainty is limited.
- Sensitivity to annealing schedule and transition kernels is not deeply analyzed.
- SMC applicability is stated but not empirically demonstrated in this paper.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide empirical confidence intervals for the estimated expected gap and discuss the number of runs required for reliable estimation across models.
- Analyze sensitivity to temperature schedules and transition mixing (e.g., number of MCMC steps per temperature), and provide practical defaults.
- If feasible, include an SMC case study to substantiate the claimed generality beyond AIS.
- Discuss whether combining multiple reverse initializations can reduce variance or improve robustness.
- Consider leveraging stronger tail bounds (e.g., Gogate et al., 2007) when aggregating multiple AIS runs for log-bounds.

Overall assessment
This is a clear, technically sound, and practically useful extension of BDMC that provides an expected upper bound on the divergence between AIS-generated samples and the true posterior, with a pragmatic path to real-data assessment. Despite limitations (expectation-only, simulated-data requirement for guarantees), the contribution is significant for evaluating and improving inference in probabilistic programming and related MCMC-based workflows. Arguments favor acceptance outweigh the drawbacks.
