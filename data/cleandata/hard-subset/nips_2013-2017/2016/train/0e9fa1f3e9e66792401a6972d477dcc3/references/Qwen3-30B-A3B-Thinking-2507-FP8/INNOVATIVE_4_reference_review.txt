

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for rigorously measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework from Grosse et al. (2015) to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, which can be computed using standard MCMC methods like AIS or SMC. The key insight is that BDMC can be used to sandwich the log marginal likelihood, and this sandwiching technique can be repurposed to measure the accuracy of posterior samples.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) deriving a theoretical bound on the Jeffreys divergence using BDMC; (2) showing this bound is accurate on toy examples; (3) proposing a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data; and (4) integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility in analyzing model representations and debugging.

The paper's novelty is significant. While BDMC was previously used for estimating marginal likelihoods, this work repurposes it for a fundamentally different task: measuring the reliability of posterior samples. This represents a bold conceptual leap that addresses a critical gap in probabilistic inference - the lack of rigorous validation methods for MCMC results. The authors demonstrate that BREAD can be used to compare different model representations in probabilistic programming languages, revealing insights that would be difficult to obtain through other means. The debugging example in WebPPL (uncovering a subtle bug in multivariate Gaussian sampling) powerfully illustrates the practical utility of the method.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The derivation in Section 3.1 carefully extends the BDMC framework to provide the desired bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The validation experiments in Section 5 are thorough, showing that the upper bound closely matches the true Jeffreys divergence on toy examples, and that the protocol for real-world data produces consistent results between simulated and real data. The integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the approach immediately applicable to real probabilistic programming workflows.

While the method is limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC), this is a reasonable constraint given the nature of the BDMC framework. The authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a compelling argument for why this subset of algorithms is still broadly relevant for understanding MCMC design choices. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (using fitted hyperparameters to generate representative simulated data) is clever and addresses a major practical challenge.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The connections to related work (particularly the comparison with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016) are appropriately discussed. The experiments are convincing, and the scientific insights gained (e.g., the tradeoff between collapsed and uncollapsed representations in different probabilistic programming languages) demonstrate the method's practical value.

Strengths:
- Novel application of BDMC to measure MCMC reliability
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with practical implementation
- Demonstrated utility in model analysis and debugging
- Clear presentation of a significant problem and solution
- Integration with widely-used probabilistic programming systems

Weaknesses:
- Limited to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC)
- The real-world data protocol requires careful implementation
- Validation is primarily on toy examples and a few models

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in the field of probabilistic inference. While the technical execution is not perfect (e.g., the method's applicability is somewhat limited), the novelty and practical utility of the BREAD framework far outweigh these limitations. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling with a bold, novel approach that could become a standard tool for validating MCMC results. The authors have provided a rigorous method that can be immediately applied by practitioners to assess the quality of their posterior samples.

In contrast to many papers that make incremental improvements to existing methods, this work introduces a new conceptual framework for measuring inference reliability. The authors demonstrate not just the method's theoretical validity, but also its practical utility in real-world probabilistic programming contexts. The debugging example alone would be a valuable contribution to the field.

Given the reviewer's preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection, and the paper's significant contribution to a critical problem in probabilistic inference, I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, provides unique insights into MCMC reliability, and offers a practical tool that could benefit the entire probabilistic modeling community. The BREAD framework has the potential to become a standard technique for validating inference in probabilistic programming systems.
