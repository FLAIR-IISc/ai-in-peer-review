

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors claim to provide a rigorous method for evaluating MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly those that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space like AIS or SMC.

The paper builds on the BDMC framework introduced by Grosse et al. (2015), which provides stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood. The authors extend this to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors. The key technical contribution is the derivation showing that the gap between the forward and reverse BDMC bounds upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. They also propose a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

**Strengths:** The theoretical derivation is sound and well-motivated. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical applicability, and the debugging example (uncovering a bug in WebPPL) provides a compelling illustration of the method's utility. The paper's organization is clear, and the validation on toy distributions provides useful evidence that the upper bound is reasonably tight in simple cases.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's core contribution appears to be a refinement of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new method. BDMC itself is not new (Grosse et al. 2015), and the extension to bound Jeffreys divergence is a natural but incremental step. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from related work, particularly Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also builds on BDMC to evaluate posterior inference. The key difference claimed (using true posterior for reverse chain initialization vs. arbitrary reference distribution) is not convincingly justified as being substantially better in practice.

The most significant limitation is that the rigorous guarantees only apply to simulated data where exact posterior samples are available. The proposed protocol for real-world data (using fitted hyperparameters to simulate data) is heuristic and lacks rigorous justification. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't adequately address whether the simulated data can truly reflect the properties of real-world data. The validation in Section 5.1.1 shows consistency between real and simulated data for five simple models, but this is limited to models where the simulated data generation process matches the real data well. For complex real-world problems, the fitted hyperparameters may not produce representative simulated data, making the method's applicability questionable.

The paper also fails to thoroughly address computational cost. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains is significantly more expensive than standard MCMC diagnostics, and the paper doesn't compare this cost to the benefits of the method. For practical applications where MCMC is already computationally intensive, this could be a major drawback.

The paper's claim that BREAD provides "a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference" is overstated. The method is only applicable to certain algorithms (AIS, SMC) and requires exact posterior samples, which are typically unavailable in real-world applications. The extension to real data via the heuristic protocol lacks the rigor promised by the method.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution is not sufficiently novel. While the extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence is a useful technical refinement, it's a relatively small step from the existing BDMC framework. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this extension provides significant advantages over existing convergence diagnostics for MCMC.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem (evaluating MCMC reliability), but the proposed solution has limited applicability due to the requirement for exact posterior samples. The method is most useful for model developers working with simulated data rather than practitioners applying MCMC to real-world problems. The practical utility of the method is therefore questionable for the intended audience of probabilistic programming users.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound, and the validation on toy examples is appropriate. However, the paper lacks thorough empirical evaluation on more complex models where MCMC reliability is most challenging. The computational cost of the method is not adequately addressed, and the extension to real-world data lacks rigorous justification.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing methods, it fails to provide a sufficiently novel contribution to justify publication at NIPS. The method's applicability is limited to specific scenarios (simulated data), and the extension to real-world data is heuristic without rigorous validation. The paper would benefit from more substantial theoretical insights or extensive empirical validation on complex real-world problems before being considered for publication. The authors should also more thoroughly differentiate their work from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) and justify why their approach offers significant advantages over existing convergence diagnostics.

The paper is technically competent but does not advance the field substantially enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS, particularly given the field's emphasis on robust, generalizable methods that can be applied to real-world problems. The claims of providing a "rigorous, general, and automatic procedure" are not supported by the evidence presented.
