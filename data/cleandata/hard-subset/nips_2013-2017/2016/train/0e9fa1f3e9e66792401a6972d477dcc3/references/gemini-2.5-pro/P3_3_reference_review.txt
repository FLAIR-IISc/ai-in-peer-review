### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively assessing the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms. The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior. This method is directly applicable to algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC).

A key challenge is that the reverse part of BDMC requires an exact sample from the true posterior, which is only available for simulated data. To bridge this gap for real-world applications, the authors propose a protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset to measure inference quality. They validate this protocol and demonstrate the utility of BREAD by implementing it in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They use it to compare different model representations for matrix factorization and to uncover a bug in WebPPL's implementation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The central theoretical contribution—linking the BDMC log-likelihood gap to the Jeffreys divergence (Eqs. 8, 11, and 12)—is a clever and well-derived extension of the analysis in [GGA15]. This provides a much-needed, more rigorous alternative to heuristic convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin statistics. The experimental work is thorough. The authors validate their proposed protocol for real-world data on five different models, showing consistency between inference behavior on real and simulated data (Fig. 1). The case study comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization is a compelling demonstration of the method's practical utility for guiding modeling decisions. The fact that the method helped identify a bug in WebPPL is a strong testament to its power.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is clear and provides the necessary context. The derivation in Section 3.1 is easy to follow. The description of the protocol for real-world data is straightforward, and the experiments are clearly presented and interpreted. The figures are effective at communicating the key results. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the methodology and appreciate its implications.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the insight to use the gap between the bounds to measure sample quality, rather than just bracketing the marginal likelihood, is novel and significant. This reframes BDMC from a tool for model comparison into a tool for algorithm diagnosis. The proposed protocol for applying this rigorous tool to messy real-world problems is also a novel, pragmatic contribution. The paper appropriately situates itself relative to classical convergence diagnostics and the concurrent work of [CTM16], highlighting the key difference that BREAD aims to bound the true Jeffreys divergence on simulated data.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. The lack of reliable, quantitative measures for MCMC sample quality is a major bottleneck in probabilistic modeling, especially with the rise of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) that aim to automate inference for non-experts. BREAD provides a principled, general, and automatable procedure for assessing inference reliability. If widely adopted, it could become a standard tool for PPL developers to benchmark and debug their systems, and for practitioners to gain confidence in their modeling results. The ability to use it to make concrete modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed variables) is a powerful application that addresses a common dilemma for users.

### Detailed Comments

**Major Points (Weaknesses and Suggestions):**

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The primary limitation is that BREAD directly assesses AIS/SMC, not the MCMC algorithm used *within* the annealing steps. The paper argues that insights from tuning AIS will generalize to tuning standard MCMC. This is plausible but not fully substantiated. For example, in Stan, BREAD evaluates a sequence of short NUTS runs (one per annealing step), which may behave differently from a single, long NUTS run used in standard practice. The paper would be stronger if it discussed this distinction more explicitly and perhaps provided some empirical evidence suggesting that performance in the AIS context correlates with performance in a standard long-run MCMC context.

2.  **The Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol in Section 3.2 is a clever heuristic, but it's also the method's weakest link from a theoretical standpoint. It relies on an *approximate* posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, which voids the formal guarantee that BREAD provides an upper bound. The authors validate this empirically by showing that the reverse estimates are insensitive to the number of burn-in steps (S) used to generate the sample. However, this may not hold if the initial hyperparameter point estimate `ˆ⌘real` is in a region of low posterior mass or if the posterior is highly multi-modal. A discussion of the potential failure modes of this heuristic would be valuable. For instance, what happens if the initial MCMC run on the real data fails to find the main posterior modes? The entire BREAD analysis could then be performed on a simulated dataset that is unrepresentative of the true posterior landscape.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The method appears computationally expensive. To get a stable estimate of the Jeffreys divergence bound, one must run multiple (100 in the paper) forward and reverse AIS chains. Each chain involves `T` MCMC steps. This is a significant overhead. A discussion of the trade-off between the computational cost of BREAD and the confidence it provides would be beneficial. For complex models, is this procedure feasible? How does the cost compare to simply running a much longer standard MCMC chain?

4.  **Interpretation of the Bound:** The bound is on the *expectation* of the gap, `E[ˆB]`. However, in practice, one computes an empirical average over a finite number of chains. Given the high variance of AIS estimates (acknowledged by the authors in their use of quartiles for visualization), the empirical gap from a single run or a small number of runs could be a noisy estimate of the true expected gap. Some discussion on the variance of the bound estimator itself and how many chains are necessary to obtain a reliable estimate would be helpful for practitioners.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   Addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in computational statistics: how to reliably measure MCMC quality.
*   The core theoretical contribution is elegant, sound, and provides a principled, quantitative metric (an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence).
*   Provides a pragmatic protocol to apply the method to real-world data, with strong empirical validation.
*   Demonstrates clear utility through compelling case studies in two major PPLs, including model selection and debugging.
*   The work is highly significant and likely to influence how inference algorithms are developed, tested, and used in the PPL community.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The method is limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers, and the connection to the performance of standard, long-run MCMC is not fully established.
*   The protocol for real-world data is a heuristic that breaks the formal guarantees of the method, and its failure modes are not fully explored.
*   The computational cost is potentially very high, which might limit its practical adoption for large-scale problems.

**Conclusion:** This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The strengths—a novel, rigorous method for assessing inference quality, strong empirical validation, and demonstrated practical utility—far outweigh the weaknesses. The limitations are clearly acknowledged, and the proposed protocol for real-world data is a reasonable and well-justified practical compromise. This work has the potential to become a foundational technique for validating and debugging inference in probabilistic programming. I strongly recommend acceptance.
