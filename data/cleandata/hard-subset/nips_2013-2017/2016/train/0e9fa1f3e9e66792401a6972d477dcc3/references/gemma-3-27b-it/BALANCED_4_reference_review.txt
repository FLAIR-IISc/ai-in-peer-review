This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior distribution. The core idea leverages bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to sandwich the log marginal likelihood, and then relates this to an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The authors demonstrate the method's applicability to simulated data and propose a protocol for extending it to real-world datasets via hyperparameter estimation and data simulation. Finally, they implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and use it to analyze model representations in a matrix factorization example and uncover a bug in WebPPL.

This is a solid and well-motivated paper that tackles a very important problem – assessing the reliability of MCMC-based inference. The problem of diagnosing convergence and quantifying the accuracy of approximate inference is notoriously difficult, and the authors' approach of providing rigorous bounds (when possible) is highly valuable. The connection to BDMC is clever, and the extension to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation is a pragmatic and sensible approach. The implementations in both WebPPL and Stan are a significant contribution, and the demonstration of the method's utility in debugging WebPPL is particularly compelling. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the technical details.  The related work section is thorough, correctly identifying and discussing relevant literature, including recent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation connecting BDMC to the Jeffreys divergence is well-presented and appears correct. The analysis builds logically upon previous work.
*   **Significance:**  Providing a method for quantifying the reliability of inference, even under specific conditions, is significant.  It addresses a core challenge in probabilistic programming and Bayesian statistics.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex concepts accessible. The explanations are clear, and the organization is logical.
*   **Implementation:** Implementing BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) dramatically increases its accessibility and potential impact.
*   **Practical Application:** The use of BREAD to guide modeling decisions (matrix factorization) and uncover a bug in WebPPL demonstrates its practical value.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Applicability:** The method directly applies only to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC).  While the authors acknowledge this and argue that the insights generalize to MCMC, this remains a significant limitation. A more detailed discussion of *how* insights from AIS/SMC could inform the design of standard MCMC algorithms would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and therefore BREAD, can be computationally expensive, requiring both forward and reverse AIS chains. The paper doesn't thoroughly address the practical implications of this cost, particularly for high-dimensional models.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The protocol for real-world data relies on estimating hyperparameters, which introduces its own source of uncertainty. While the authors validate their approach, the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter estimation procedure isn’t fully explored.
*   **Assumptions about Hyperparameter Protocol:** The success of the hyperparameter protocol hinges on the assumption that the estimated hyperparameters are representative of the true underlying data generating process. This assumption might not hold in all cases, and a more nuanced discussion of potential failure modes is needed.  The extension to models where the *model itself* needs to be selected (model comparison) would be a substantial improvement.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference – assessing the reliability of approximate inference.
*   The proposed method (BREAD) is technically sound and provides rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence (in the simulated data setting).
*   The implementations in WebPPL and Stan are valuable contributions to the field.
*   The practical demonstration of the method's utility (debugging WebPPL, guiding model selection) is compelling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limited applicability of the method (to AIS/SMC) restricts its immediate impact.
*   The computational cost of BDMC could be prohibitive for large-scale models.
*   The reliance on hyperparameter estimation introduces additional uncertainty when applying the method to real-world data.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. While the limited applicability and computational cost are legitimate concerns, the technical soundness, significance, and practical value of the work outweigh these drawbacks. The paper offers a valuable tool for diagnosing and improving the reliability of probabilistic inference, and the implementations in WebPPL and Stan will undoubtedly be useful to a wide range of researchers.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Expand the discussion of how insights from AIS/SMC can inform the design of traditional MCMC algorithms.
*   Provide a more thorough analysis of the computational cost of BREAD and explore potential strategies for reducing it.
*   Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter estimation procedure.
*   Discuss potential failure modes of the hyperparameter protocol and suggest ways to mitigate them.
*   Consider how the method could be extended to model comparison scenarios.
