This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior distribution, particularly in the context of simulated data. The core idea leverages bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide both upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, which are then related to the Jeffreys divergence. A protocol is proposed for applying BREAD to real-world data by inferring hyperparameters, simulating data, and evaluating consistency. The authors demonstrate the utility of BREAD by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan, and use it to guide modeling choices in a matrix factorization example and even to identify a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampling.

The paper builds upon recent work on BDMC [GGA15] and extends it to a new application: assessing the quality of approximate inference. This is a valuable direction, as rigorously evaluating MCMC and related methods remains a challenge. The connection to Jeffreys divergence offers a theoretically sound metric for quantifying the discrepancy between approximate and true posteriors. The proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, while heuristic, is a pragmatic attempt to bridge the gap between the theoretical guarantees on simulated data and practical application. Implementing BREAD within popular probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan significantly enhances its accessibility and impact. The demonstration of its use in debugging WebPPL is a particularly compelling result.

The paper’s strengths lie in its technical soundness, its clear exposition of complex ideas, and its practical relevance. The derivations are well-presented, and the explanation of how BREAD relates to existing techniques (e.g., AIS, SMC, BDMC) is thorough. The experiments, while not exhaustive, are well-designed and provide convincing evidence that BREAD can be a useful tool for diagnosing and improving probabilistic inference. The identified bug in WebPPL is a strong testament to the method’s diagnostic capability. The writing is generally clear and concise, making the paper relatively easy to follow despite the technical intricacies.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The primary limitation is the reliance on simulated data for rigorous guarantees. While the proposed protocol for real-world data addresses this, it introduces a layer of approximation (hyperparameter inference and simulation) which could potentially invalidate the bounds. The experiments validating this protocol, while reassuring, could be more extensive, perhaps including datasets with more challenging characteristics or different model structures. The discussion of the limitations of the method, particularly regarding the conditions under which the Jeffreys divergence is a meaningful metric, could be expanded. The analysis of the matrix factorization model is somewhat limited; a more thorough exploration of the impact of different representations on inference speed and accuracy would strengthen the findings. While the connection to [CTM16] is acknowledged, a more detailed comparison, highlighting the specific advantages and disadvantages of each approach, would be beneficial. The assumption that reverse AIS chains can be initialized from approximate hyperposterior samples without significantly affecting bounds requires further justification beyond the empirical validation provided. Finally, the paper does not explicitly address computational costs associated with running BREAD, which could be a significant barrier to adoption for large-scale problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretically grounded method for evaluating approximate inference algorithms.
*   Extends existing work on BDMC to a novel and important application.
*   Demonstrates practical utility through implementation in WebPPL and Stan, and successful bug identification.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of complex concepts.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic modeling: quantifying the reliability of inference results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data.
*   The protocol for real-world data relies on approximations that may impact the validity of the bounds.
*   The experimental evaluation, while promising, could be more comprehensive.
*   Computational cost of BREAD is not discussed.
*   Limited in-depth comparison with related work [CTM16].

**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the limitations related to real-world data and computational cost are noteworthy, the theoretical soundness, clear presentation, and practical demonstrations make a strong case for acceptance. The potential of BREAD as a diagnostic tool for probabilistic programming systems is particularly exciting. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on expanding the discussion of limitations, providing a more detailed comparison to [CTM16], and potentially including a brief discussion of the computational cost of BREAD.
