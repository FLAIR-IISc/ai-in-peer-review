Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) from bounding log marginal likelihoods to assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key idea is to view algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and certain SMC samplers as importance sampling on an extended state space. Running AIS forward yields a stochastic lower bound on log(ZT/Z1), and running AIS “in reverse” from an exact posterior draw yields a stochastic upper bound. The expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the algorithm’s approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The authors call the method BREAD and argue it quantifies both over- and under-dispersion of the approximate sampler.

Because exact posterior samples are needed to run reverse AIS, the paper focuses on simulated data. To make conclusions relevant to real datasets, the authors propose a practical protocol: infer hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data from these fitted hyperparameters, and validate that AIS behavior on the simulated data mirrors that on the real data. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate: (i) the upper bound matches ground truth on small toy models; (ii) behavior on simulated data closely tracks behavior on several real datasets (linear and logistic regression, AR time series, matrix factorization, and MoG); (iii) BREAD can guide modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed MF) and even help debug implementations.

This work builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), reverse-annealing bounds for log partition functions (Burda et al., 2015), the original BDMC sandwich (Grosse et al., 2015), and relates to convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman), Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey), and independent concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016). The main novel contribution is to tie the forward/reverse AIS bounds directly to the expected Jeffreys divergence of the approximate sampler and to develop a practical, implemented protocol for using this to evaluate MCMC-based inference in PPLs.

Strengths
- Clear and useful theoretical insight: the expected BDMC sandwich gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions.
- Practicality: implemented in two popular PPLs, with a protocol to relate simulated to real data via fitted hyperparameters.
- Empirical validation on diverse models and a compelling case study (collapsed vs. uncollapsed MF) with actionable guidance differing across platforms.
- Diagnostic value: uncovering a real bug in WebPPL illustrates utility beyond performance assessment.
- Relation to prior art is conscientious; distinctions from CTM16 are discussed.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The guarantee is in expectation; the paper does not provide concentration results or confidence intervals for the estimated gap, nor guidance on how many AIS runs are needed for a reliable bound estimate in practice. Heavy-tailed AIS weights make this nontrivial.
- The real-data protocol relies on a heuristic approximation (fitted hyperparameters and a few MCMC steps to approximate a posterior draw), so the reverse bound is no longer rigorous. The empirical robustness study is helpful but could be strengthened.
- Experiments largely focus on AIS; claims of applicability to SMC-like methods would benefit from explicit SMC demonstrations.
- Tightness analysis is convincing on small toy cases but not quantified on realistic problems. Without some proxy for ground truth, it is hard to assess whether the bound is systematically loose/tight.
- Some notational/slight clarity issues (e.g., occasional inconsistencies in xT/qfwd notation), and the main theoretical result could be stated as a formal theorem with assumptions (ergodicity, reversibility, requirements on transitions).

Quality
- Generally technically sound. The derivations via extended-state AIS importance sampling and the inequality via marginalization are standard and correctly applied. The expectation-bound interpretation is carefully argued.
- Empirical validation is diverse but could be deeper: report variability and uncertainty of the bound estimates, computational overhead, and sensitivity to AIS schedule and kernels.
- The real-data protocol is sensible but heuristic; limitations are acknowledged.

Clarity
- Well written and organized. Background on AIS/BDMC is concise and accurate. Methods and experiments are readable.
- Suggestions: present the main bound as a theorem with assumptions; add pseudo-code for BREAD; consistently use notation for forward/reverse chains; clarify precisely which classes of SMC/AIS variants are covered.

Originality
- Extends BDMC in a meaningful way from log evidence bounds to expected Jeffreys divergence on approximate posterior sampling. The protocol for real data and integration into two PPLs are practically novel. There is overlap with contemporaneous “subjective divergence” work, but the framing and empirical emphasis differ.

Significance
- Assessing reliability of black-box inference is a central challenge in PPL and Bayesian computation. A tool that yields quantitative, interpretable diagnostics, can inform model/algorithm choices, and can even reveal implementation bugs is valuable. The main limitation is scope (algorithms amenable to AIS/SMC-style constructions and availability of simulated data), but within that scope, the impact is substantial.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution connecting BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical implementation in major PPLs with informative case studies.
- Clear potential to improve practice (model representation choices, debugging).
- Careful positioning w.r.t. related work.

Cons for acceptance
- Expectation-only guarantee; lack of statistical uncertainty quantification for estimates.
- Heuristic real-data protocol without formal guarantees; limited tightness assessment on complex models.
- No SMC experiments despite claims of applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide uncertainty quantification for the estimated gap (e.g., via repeated runs and bootstrap CIs) and discuss sample complexity under heavy tails; consider techniques like log-weight stabilization, control variates, or tempered schedules designed to reduce variance.
- Include at least one SMC-based experiment to substantiate generality.
- Explore multimodal/posterior pathologies to test robustness and show when bounds become loose.
- Quantify computational overhead and offer guidance on allocating compute between forward and reverse runs.
- Strengthen the real-data proxy analysis: sensitivity to prior misspecification, more systematic checks beyond curve overlap (e.g., comparing AIS ESS/variance, PSIS-LOO diagnostics, Stein discrepancies).
- Clarify assumptions and present the main bound formally; fix minor notation issues.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite limitations (expectation-only bounds and heuristic real-data protocol), the paper offers a clear theoretical insight, a practical and implemented methodology, and convincing demonstrations that will be useful to the NeurIPS community, especially in probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics. The suggestions above would further strengthen an already strong paper.
