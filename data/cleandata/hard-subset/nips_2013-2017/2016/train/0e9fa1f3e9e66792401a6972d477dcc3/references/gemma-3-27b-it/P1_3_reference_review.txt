## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Paper Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, to evaluate the accuracy of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) based inference. Specifically, it leverages BDMC to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution obtained from algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) and the true posterior. The authors refine the BDMC analysis to derive a divergence estimator, demonstrate its accuracy on toy distributions, and propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters. Finally, they integrate the method (dubbed BREAD - Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) into WebPPL and Stan, and use it to analyze model representations for a matrix factorization problem.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the landscape of MCMC diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998), methods for estimating marginal likelihoods (Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015), and recent advancements in probabilistic programming (Goodman et al., 2008). It highlights connections to reverse annealing and thermodynamic integration (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997) and acknowledges closely related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) which also leverages BDMC for inference evaluation. The authors effectively contextualize their contribution within existing approaches and clearly articulate how BREAD differs and builds upon previous work.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Approach:** The paper provides a theoretically grounded method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence.  The derivation connecting BDMC to divergence estimation is clear and well-motivated. 
* **Practical Application:**  The authors move beyond theoretical analysis by implementing BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, making it readily accessible to practitioners.
* **Addressing Real-World Challenges:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data through hyperparameter estimation and simulation is a crucial step towards making the method practical, as obtaining exact posterior samples is often impossible.
* **Demonstrated Utility:** The application to matrix factorization reveals meaningful insights into the trade-offs between different model representations in Stan and WebPPL.
* **Bug Detection:** The discovery of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates the utility of the method as a tool for validating implementations.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easier to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Applicability:** BREAD is currently restricted to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While these algorithms share features with general MCMC, the applicability is limited. The authors acknowledge this, but it's a significant constraint.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and therefore BREAD, can be computationally expensive, requiring both forward and reverse AIS runs.  The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the computational cost relative to other diagnostic methods.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The effectiveness of the hyperparameter fitting protocol (Section 3.2) and the number of MCMC steps (S) used to approximate the posterior over hyperparameters are not fully explored. The experiments suggest robustness, but a more detailed sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Jeffreys Divergence as a Metric:** While the Jeffreys divergence is a reasonable choice, the paper doesn't delve into why this divergence is preferred over alternatives like the Total Variation distance or Wasserstein distance, each with its own strengths and weaknesses.
* **Limited Validation:** While the validation in Section 5.1 demonstrates consistency between simulated and real data for the selected models, the scope is limited. Expanding the set of models and datasets would strengthen the argument for the generalizability of the protocol.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a novel and theoretically sound method for quantifying the reliability of approximate inference.
* Offers a practical tool for practitioners using probabilistic programming languages.
* Demonstrates the utility of the method through compelling examples.
* Addresses a critical need in the field of probabilistic inference: rigorous validation of results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited applicability of BREAD to a specific class of algorithms.
* The potential computational cost could limit its widespread adoption.
* The validation, while promising, could benefit from broader coverage.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference.  The authors present a novel and theoretically well-grounded method for evaluating the accuracy of MCMC-based inference.  The practical implementation in Stan and WebPPL, along with the protocol for handling real-world data, significantly enhances the value of the work. While the limited applicability and potential computational cost are weaknesses, they are acknowledged by the authors. The benefits of having a rigorous tool for evaluating inference reliability outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept** 

I believe this paper deserves to be accepted to NIPS 2016. It presents a valuable contribution that is likely to be influential in the field.  I recommend that the authors consider addressing the comments regarding computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity in a future version.
