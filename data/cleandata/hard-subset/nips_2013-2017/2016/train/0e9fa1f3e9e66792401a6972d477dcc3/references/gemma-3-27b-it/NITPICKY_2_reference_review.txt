This paper presents an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. While the core idea of leveraging BDMC for this purpose is potentially valuable, and the paper demonstrates implementation in WebPPL and Stan, a multitude of minor flaws and a somewhat overly cautious approach to presentation detract from its overall impact.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem of validating MCMC algorithms, but the writing is verbose and occasionally repetitive. The statement that “reliable quantitative measures are hard to find” feels overly broad and lacks nuance. While true for *rigorous* measures, many heuristics are routinely used and understood to have limitations. The connection drawn between AIS/SMC and traditional MCMC, while valid in principle, is stated vaguely and could benefit from more concrete examples. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, involving hyperparameter inference and simulated data, is reasonable, but its justification relies heavily on an analogy to the parametric bootstrap without substantial discussion of differences or potential pitfalls. The summary of contributions is accurate but somewhat dry.

Section 2 provides necessary background, but is also verbose. The explanation of AIS is standard, but could be more concise. The discussion of stochastic lower bounds is correct but feels like a reiteration of well-known results. The motivation for using reverse AIS is clear, and the connection to BDMC is well explained. The discussion of WebPPL and Stan is adequate but lacks depth.

Section 3, the core of the paper, is where the majority of the issues reside. The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound, while stated correctly, is presented without sufficient detail and relies heavily on referencing [GGA15]. A more self-contained explanation would improve readability. The notation is occasionally inconsistent and can be difficult to follow. The claim that BREAD is limited to cases with exact samples from pT, while technically true, is presented as a significant hurdle without sufficient discussion of potential approximations or alternative approaches. The heuristic for handling real-world data (Section 3.2) is reasonable, but the justification feels weak and relies on a somewhat hand-wavy argument about the plausibility of the fitted hyperparameters.

The related work section (Section 4) is reasonably thorough, but the discussion of [CTM16] feels somewhat dismissive. While the authors highlight differences in initialization, a more nuanced comparison of the subjective divergence and the Jeffreys divergence bound would be beneficial.

Section 5 presents experimental results. The validation experiments (Section 5.1) are well-designed and provide some evidence that BREAD can be used to evaluate inference algorithms. However, the presentation of the results is somewhat unclear. The use of quartiles is justified due to outliers, but the figures lack sufficient detail and are difficult to interpret. The discussion of the bug in WebPPL is a positive outcome, but feels somewhat tacked on. The comparison of model representations (Section 5.2.1) is interesting, but the conclusions are limited by the specific models and datasets used. The observation that collapsed models performed better in WebPPL than in Stan is a worthwhile finding, but requires further investigation.

**Strengths:**

*   The core idea of using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel and potentially valuable.
*   The implementation of BREAD in WebPPL and Stan provides a practical tool for evaluating MCMC inference.
*   The validation experiments provide some evidence that BREAD can be used to assess inference quality.
*   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates the potential usefulness of BREAD as a testing tool.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper is overly verbose and repetitive.
*   The presentation of the technical details is often lacking in rigor and clarity.
*   The justification for some of the key assumptions and heuristics is weak.
*   The experimental results are presented in a somewhat unclear and incomplete manner.
*   The paper lacks a strong, overarching narrative.
*   Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies throughout (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, poor equation formatting).

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Against:** The paper is marred by a large number of minor flaws that detract from its overall quality. The lack of a strong theoretical grounding and the limited scope of the experiments raise concerns about the generalizability of the results. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed research contribution.
*   **For:** The core idea is novel, the implementation is non-trivial, and the paper demonstrates a potential application of BDMC to a challenging problem. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a positive outcome.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially interesting idea, the numerous flaws in its presentation and execution prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper lacks the polish and rigor expected of a NIPS publication. A major revision addressing the issues outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. I recommend **rejection**.
