Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) from bounding marginal likelihoods to assessing the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key theoretical contribution is to show that, for AIS/SMC-style algorithms viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space, the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log-evidence estimators yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the algorithm’s approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior. This provides a quantitative measure of sample quality that is rigorous when one can initialize the reverse chain from an exact posterior draw—available for simulated datasets via ancestral sampling of the joint p(θ, z, y).

The authors further propose a practical protocol to transfer insights to real datasets: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data at those fitted values, and then use BDMC on the simulated data; for the reverse chain, approximately sample hyperparameters via a short MCMC run starting from the fitted values. They integrate the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, validate on toy distributions and five applied models, compare representations of a matrix factorization model, and even discover and help fix a bug in WebPPL.

Relative to prior work, this builds directly on BDMC and AIS (Neal, 2001), and relates to contemporaneous work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) on “subjective divergence.” The paper clarifies that when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior, the BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; when initialized from a reference distribution (as in [CTM16]), guarantees weaken. The work also complements diagnostics for convergence (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman, Cowles–Carlin), and recent sample-quality measures (Gorham–Mackey) that require structural assumptions.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations connecting the expected bias of forward/reverse AIS to one-sided KLs, and summing to the Jeffreys divergence, are sound and clearly presented. The “upper bound in expectation” caveat is appropriately emphasized.
- Scope/assumptions: Guarantees require exact posterior draws (hence simulated data) and algorithms that can be run “in reverse” (AIS/SMC-like). The real-data protocol is heuristic and loses guarantees but is empirically validated.
- Empirics: Implementations in two systems, validation on five models, and a representation case study are solid. The toy examples demonstrating tightness of the bound are reassuring, though limited. Reporting quartiles of single-run AIS logs is understandable given heavy tails, but to assess “upper bound in expectation,” Monte Carlo means with uncertainty would be more appropriate.
- Reproducibility: The description is detailed, with model code referenced; integration into widely used systems is a plus.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Background on AIS/BDMC is concise; the method and its limits are stated plainly. The real-data protocol and its validations are described carefully.

Originality
The main theoretical connection—using the BDMC forward/reverse gap to upper-bound Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples—is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial, even though it repurposes BDMC building blocks. The practical integration into probabilistic programming environments and the debugging demonstration are also valuable.

Significance
Providing a quantitative, interpretable, and in-principle rigorous way to measure the reliability of MCMC-based approximate sampling is important, especially for probabilistic programming. Even with the simulated-data caveat, the tool can guide design choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and catch implementation errors. The scope limitation to AIS/SMC-like algorithms tempers breadth, but many modern workflows already use tempering/SMC.

Strengths
- Clear, principled link between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples.
- Simple to use in systems that already support tempering; integrates with WebPPL and Stan.
- Empirical validation across multiple models and a useful case study; practical impact (bug detection).
- Thoughtful protocol to make simulated-data assessments relevant to real datasets.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Expectation bound vs. realized bound: The guarantee is in expectation; single-run bounds can be anti-conservative. Suggest reporting Monte Carlo means and confidence intervals for the gap, and studying concentration/tails of the estimator.
- Real-data protocol lacks guarantees: The approximate posterior initialization for the reverse chain voids rigor. The empirical study (varying the number of MCMC steps S) is helpful; adding checks such as sensitivity to different initializations, or diagnostics of closeness of the reference to the posterior, would strengthen confidence.
- Limited coverage of SMC: The method applies to SMC, but experiments focus on AIS/tempering. Including SMC results would broaden applicability.
- AIS/Stan details: Using NUTS/HMC transitions at intermediate temperatures requires care (parameter adaptation, invariance). Please clarify how transitions are tuned per temperature to ensure invariance and whether adaptation is disabled during runs.
- Tightness/scale: Beyond toy examples, it would be useful to quantify tightness on larger problems, e.g., by correlating BREAD with downstream error in posterior expectations, or contrasting with standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS), showing cases where BREAD detects issues they miss and vice versa.
- Tempering schedule: Results can be sensitive to temperature schedules. Providing guidance or auto-tuning strategies (e.g., moment averaging vs. geometric schedules) would aid practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- How many forward/reverse AIS runs are needed in practice to obtain a stable estimate of the expected gap? Do you provide SEs in the tool?
- How are HMC/NUTS parameters set across temperatures to guarantee invariance for each pt?
- Can your analysis be extended to give high-probability upper bounds (e.g., via concentration inequalities or combining multiple runs as in Gogate et al., 2007)?
- Did you observe cases where the heuristic reverse initialization yielded optimistic (invalid) bounds? Any diagnostics to flag this?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and practically useful criterion for MCMC reliability.
- Solid theoretical foundation with clear derivation.
- Integration into widely used tools; demonstrated practical utility (modeling choices, debugging).
- Timely contribution for probabilistic programming.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees limited to simulated data and AIS/SMC-like algorithms; heuristic for real data.
- Empirical evaluation could be deeper on estimator concentration, SMC, and larger-scale models.
- Some implementation details (temperatures, transition invariance) need clarification.

Recommendation: Accept
Despite scope limitations, the paper provides a rigorous, implementable, and informative methodology to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based inference, with theory, tooling, and meaningful empirical demonstrations. The contribution is likely to influence practice in probabilistic programming and sampler design. Constructive improvements as suggested would strengthen the final version.
