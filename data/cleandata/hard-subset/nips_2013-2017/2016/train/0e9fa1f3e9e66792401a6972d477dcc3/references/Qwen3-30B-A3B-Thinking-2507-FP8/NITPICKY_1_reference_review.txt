

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate their method on simulated data, show how to apply it to real-world data through a hyperparameter fitting protocol, and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. The paper builds on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), which provides stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a significant problem in probabilistic inference - reliably evaluating MCMC convergence. The method is well-motivated, the theoretical derivation is rigorous, and the experiments are well-designed. The integration into two popular probabilistic programming languages and the discovery of a WebPPL bug demonstrate practical value. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

**Weaknesses:** While the paper makes an important contribution, several minor but troubling issues undermine its technical precision and reproducibility:

1. **Inconsistent notation and terminology:** The paper uses "BREAD" as an acronym without clearly defining it in the main text (it's only defined in the abstract). Section 3.1 states "ˆB upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence" when the derivation shows only that E[ˆB] upper bounds J. This is a critical technical distinction that should be clarified.

2. **Formatting inconsistencies:** The references have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "GGA15" vs "Grosse et al., 2015"), with some entries using "Q" before years and "E" after (e.g., "Burda et al.,? \Q2015\E"). The citation "CGHL+ p" appears multiple times but is incomplete (should be "CGHL+14" or similar). The paper incorrectly states "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems" instead of "The Conference."

3. **Methodological ambiguity:** In Section 5.1.1, the paper claims "the distributions of AIS estimates included many outliers" but doesn't explain why this is problematic for their analysis (since they're reporting individual AIS chains rather than averaged weights, as noted in footnote 3). The justification for using quartiles rather than means is insufficient.

4. **Minor logical errors:** The paper states "BREAD is simply the combination of this stochastic upper bound with the stochastic lower bound of Section 2.2" when Section 2.2 discusses the stochastic lower bound on logR, not the lower bound from AIS. This misattribution is confusing.

5. **Reproducibility issues:** The paper mentions a bug in WebPPL but doesn't specify what the bug was (only referencing a GitHub issue). This makes it difficult to verify their debugging claim.

6. **Terminology confusion:** The paper refers to "exact posterior samples" in Section 2.3 but later in Section 5.1.2 uses "approximate posterior sample" for the hyperparameter-initialized reverse chain. The distinction between exact and approximate posterior samples should be clarified throughout.

7. **Minor mathematical oversight:** In Section 3.1, the paper states "the distribution of approximate samples qfwd(xT) is obtained by marginalizing out v" but doesn't explicitly state that xT is the final state of the AIS chain, which is critical to understanding the derivation.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents a valuable method that advances the field, the cumulative effect of these minor technical inconsistencies and formatting issues significantly undermines its rigor. As a perfectionist reviewer who prioritizes technical precision over novelty, I cannot recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper's contributions are important, but the lack of attention to these small details suggests a lack of thoroughness that is concerning for a venue like NIPS.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Clarifying the distinction between ˆB and E[ˆB] in Section 3.1
2. Defining BREAD explicitly in the main text
3. Correcting all reference formatting inconsistencies
4. Providing a more detailed explanation for using quartiles in Figure 1
5. Clarifying the terminology around "exact" vs. "approximate" posterior samples

Without these corrections, the paper's technical claims are not presented with sufficient precision for a NIPS publication. The minor issues collectively indicate a lack of meticulous attention to detail that is expected at this venue.
