

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15] to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, applicable to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The paper also proposes a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validating consistency between real and simulated data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality and Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a theoretically rigorous derivation showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The mathematical arguments are well-developed, with careful attention to the bias of the stochastic bounds. The authors validate their method on toy distributions where exact Jeffreys divergence can be computed, demonstrating that the upper bound is reasonably tight.

2. **Significance:** The problem of reliably assessing MCMC inference quality is highly relevant, especially for probabilistic programming systems like WebPPL and Stan that aim to make advanced inference accessible to non-experts. BREAD provides a rigorous, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, addressing a critical gap in current practice.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the background, methods, and experiments. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully, and the experimental validation is thorough. The connection to related work, particularly [CTM16], is appropriately discussed.

4. **Practical Impact:** The authors successfully integrate BREAD into two probabilistic programming languages, demonstrating its utility through concrete examples, including the discovery of a bug in WebPPL. The comparison of different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) provides valuable insights for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope:** While the authors acknowledge that BREAD applies only to algorithms like AIS and SMC (not general MCMC), they don't sufficiently explore the practical implications of this limitation. Many MCMC methods (e.g., standard Metropolis-Hastings) aren't covered by this approach, which may limit its applicability to a significant portion of MCMC usage.

2. **Validation on Real Data:** The validation protocol for real-world data (Section 3.2) relies on the assumption that data simulated from fitted hyperparameters will produce similar inference behavior to real data. While the authors validate this for five models, more extensive validation across diverse model classes and data types would strengthen their claims. The paper doesn't address cases where this assumption might fail.

3. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational burden of running both forward and reverse AIS chains, which could be substantial for large-scale applications. For many practitioners, the additional computational cost may outweigh the benefits of the reliability assessment.

4. **Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the paper mentions convergence diagnostics [CC96; GR92; BG98], it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of BREAD's performance against standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size). A more thorough comparison would better establish BREAD's practical utility.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the problem of assessing MCMC inference quality. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the practical implementation in probabilistic programming languages demonstrates clear utility. However, the limited scope of the method (applicable only to specific inference algorithms) and the lack of comprehensive validation on diverse real-world data present significant limitations.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Provides a theoretically rigorous method for bounding inference error
- Addresses an important practical problem for probabilistic programming
- Demonstrates real-world utility through integration with WebPPL and Stan
- Includes a novel protocol for applying the method to real data
- Validates the method through both toy examples and real models

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Limited applicability to only certain MCMC algorithms (AIS/SMC)
- Validation on real-world data is limited to only five models
- Doesn't address computational costs or scalability
- Lacks comparison with standard convergence diagnostics

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the validation to more diverse models and datasets to better demonstrate the generality of the real-world data protocol.

2. Conduct a more thorough comparison with existing convergence diagnostics to establish BREAD's relative advantages and disadvantages.

3. Discuss the computational cost of the method and potential tradeoffs between accuracy and efficiency.

4. Consider how the method might be extended to broader classes of MCMC algorithms, or clearly articulate the boundaries of its applicability.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, and with the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for publication. The work is relevant to NIPS' focus on probabilistic modeling and inference, and it addresses a significant practical challenge in the field.
