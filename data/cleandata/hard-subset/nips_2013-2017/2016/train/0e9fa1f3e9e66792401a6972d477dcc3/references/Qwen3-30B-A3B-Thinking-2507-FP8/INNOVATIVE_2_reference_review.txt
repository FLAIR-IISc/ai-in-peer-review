

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for rigorously evaluating the quality of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework from Grosse et al. (2015) to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, rather than using BDMC solely for marginal likelihood estimation. This represents a significant conceptual shift in how we think about validating MCMC inference.

The key novelty lies in recognizing that the BDMC framework can be repurposed to directly measure the accuracy of posterior samples, rather than just estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors derive a theoretical connection showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. They then propose a practical protocol for applying this to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters and using approximate posterior samples to initialize the reverse chain.

The paper makes four clear contributions: (1) theoretical derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, (2) exact computation of both the true divergence and upper bound on toy models, (3) protocol for real-world data evaluation, and (4) implementation in WebPPL and Stan with empirical validation. The validation experiments demonstrate BREAD's utility in guiding model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even discovering bugs in probabilistic programming implementations.

This work stands out from related approaches like Gorham and Mackey (2015) and Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) because it provides rigorous mathematical guarantees for the Jeffreys divergence bound without distributional assumptions. While other methods focus on estimating divergence or marginal likelihood, BREAD offers a direct way to measure posterior approximation quality—a critical but previously underserved need in probabilistic inference.

The significance of this work is substantial. Probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan aim to make advanced inference accessible to non-experts, but without reliable validation methods, users cannot trust the results. BREAD provides a "black-box" validation procedure that could be integrated into these systems to monitor inference quality automatically. The authors demonstrate this through concrete examples where BREAD reveals important tradeoffs between model representations across different probabilistic programming systems.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The theoretical derivations are sound, and the experimental validation covers both toy examples and realistic applications. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for exact posterior samples for simulation, the protocol for real-world data), and their validation of the protocol in Section 5.1.1-5.1.2 demonstrates careful consideration of practical implementation.

While the method is currently limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC), the authors compellingly argue that these algorithms inform broader MCMC design choices. This limitation is outweighed by the paper's conceptual novelty and practical utility.

Compared to previous NIPS work, this paper represents a significant conceptual advance beyond incremental improvements to existing convergence diagnostics. While there's a rich literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks and Gelman, 1998; Cowles and Carlin, 1996), these methods cannot rigorously demonstrate convergence, whereas BREAD can. The paper also distinguishes itself from marginal likelihood estimation work (Grosse et al., 2015) by shifting focus to posterior approximation quality.

The only minor limitation is that the protocol for real-world data relies on approximate posterior samples for the reverse chain initialization. However, the authors provide strong empirical evidence (Section 5.1.2) that even a small number of MCMC steps is sufficient for this purpose, making this a practical rather than fundamental limitation.

This paper introduces a genuinely new concept with broad applicability to probabilistic inference and programming. The authors successfully demonstrate its utility in guiding model representation choices and debugging inference systems. The work addresses a critical problem in the field—how to rigorously validate MCMC inference—and provides a principled, general solution that can be directly integrated into probabilistic programming tools.

The significance of this work cannot be overstated. For the first time, we have a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference. This has the potential to transform how probabilistic programming languages are used and trusted by non-experts. The paper's conceptual novelty, practical utility, and clear demonstration of its value in real-world applications make it a strong candidate for NIPS.

I recommend acceptance for its significant conceptual contribution and its potential to advance the field of probabilistic inference. The paper provides a novel framework for evaluating MCMC inference that goes beyond existing approaches and directly addresses a critical problem in the field.
