Summary
The paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) beyond marginal likelihood benchmarking to assess the quality of posterior samples from AIS/SMC-like algorithms. The main theoretical result shows that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log evidence estimates yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal distribution and the true posterior. The authors call the resulting procedure BREAD. They (i) derive the expectation bound, (ii) compute tightness on toy models where the true Jeffreys divergence is tractable, (iii) propose a protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters and approximate posterior initialization, and (iv) integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan. Experiments validate that simulated-data behavior mirrors real-data behavior for five models, demonstrate insensitivity of reverse-AIS to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the posterior hyperparameter sample, and show how BREAD can guide modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization). An implementation bug in WebPPL was detected using BREAD’s sandwich property.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation that E[log R̂] ≤ log(ZT/Z1) − DKL(qfwd(xT) || pT) and E[log R̂rev] ≤ log(Z1/ZT) − DKL(pT || qfwd) is sound and follows from Neal’s AIS-as-IS view and basic properties of KL under marginalization. The conclusion that E[log R̂−log R̂rev] ≥ DJ(pT, qfwd) is correct and novel in this explicit form. The limitation that the bound is only in expectation is clearly acknowledged.
- Scope/assumptions: The approach requires an exact posterior sample to run reverse AIS. The real-data protocol replaces this with a fitted-hyperparameter scheme and short MCMC burn-in; this is heuristic, but the paper provides empirical validation across several models.
- Empirics: The experiments are thoughtful and relevant to the PPL user community. The validation that real-vs-simulated forward AIS curves align, and that reverse AIS is insensitive to hyperparameter-initialization mixing length, supports practical use. The matrix factorization study is a nice demonstration of actionable insights.
- Caveats:
  - The expectation bound is not a per-run bound; finite-sample uncertainty is not quantified. Given heavy-tailed AIS weights, reporting quartiles rather than means is sensible, but the paper should provide confidence intervals for E[gap] and discuss variance.
  - Claims like “rigorous bounds” should be qualified; the stochastic bound property provides one-sided tail control but does not guarantee that a single sandwich tightly brackets log p(y) or that a single run upper-bounds DJ.
  - The paper emphasizes SMC applicability but presents results only for AIS. Including at least one SMC experiment (e.g., data tempering SMC) would strengthen the generality claim.
  - HMC/NUTS kernels for tempered distributions: more detail is needed on how Stan ensures invariance at each temperature (adaptation schedule, step size/mass matrix reuse across temperatures) and whether reversibility/volume preservation assumptions hold under adaptation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The background on AIS and stochastic bounds sets up the main result cleanly. Suggestions:
- Be explicit that BREAD provides an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence in expectation, and present a practical recipe for estimating this expectation with error bars (e.g., via replicated runs and a bootstrap).
- Provide pseudocode for forward/reverse AIS as used in Stan/WebPPL, including kernel specifics and the annealing schedule.
- Clarify monotonicity: do the empirical gap estimates typically decrease with the number of intermediate distributions or MCMC updates per temperature? State that no monotonicity guarantee exists, and show representative trends.

Originality
- Conceptually, the paper bridges BDMC to posterior sample-quality assessment via Jeffreys divergence and integrates it into real PPLs. This is a meaningful extension of BDMC’s remit.
- The relationship to concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) is fairly discussed; it would help to delineate trade-offs: BREAD’s need for an exact posterior sample vs subjective divergence’s use of a reference, unbiasedness of their estimator vs your expectation bound, and practical tightness/variance in both approaches.

Significance
- For practitioners, having a quantitative, model-agnostic way to assess posterior sample quality is important, particularly in probabilistic programming where black-box MCMC is common. The integration into WebPPL and Stan increases impact.
- The method can inform modeling and algorithmic choices (e.g., collapsing decisions), and acts as a regression/debugging test for PPL implementations.
- Limitations reduce scope: dependence on simulated data or fitted-hyperparameter surrogates, and absence of strong per-run guarantees. Still, the contribution is likely to be used and extended.

Pros
- Clear and neat theoretical connection: AIS BDMC gap → expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical protocol and toolchain integration; compelling case studies and debugging story.
- Useful guidance for modeling choices in PPLs.

Cons
- Bound only in expectation; finite-sample uncertainty and heavy-tailed variability not characterized.
- SMC applicability not empirically demonstrated.
- Some overstatements about “rigorous bounds” and “high probability” sandwiching; needs precise phrasing.
- Limited exploration of challenging cases (e.g., high-dimensional, strongly multimodal targets, poor annealing schedules).

Suggestions for improvement
- Report estimates of E[gap] with confidence intervals from multiple independent runs; discuss estimator variance and robustness to outliers, possibly using median-of-means.
- Include at least one SMC experiment and compare AIS vs SMC under identical computational budgets.
- Analyze sensitivity to annealing schedules and number of MCMC updates per temperature; provide guidance for practitioners.
- Compare BREAD-derived diagnostics with standard MCMC diagnostics (R̂, ESS) and with Stein discrepancies to show complementary behavior.
- Clarify HMC/NUTS details under tempering (adaptation, invariance) and ensure kernels strictly leave pt invariant during production runs.

Overall recommendation
A solid and practically impactful extension of BDMC with careful empirical validation and useful tooling. With clarifications and additional experiments (variance characterization and an SMC case), I would support acceptance.
