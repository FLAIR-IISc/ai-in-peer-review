This paper presents a novel and compelling extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC inference, specifically by bounding the Jeffreys divergence. The core idea – leveraging BDMC, traditionally used for marginal likelihood estimation, to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples – is a significant and potentially impactful contribution. The authors correctly identify a critical challenge in MCMC – the lack of robust quantitative validation metrics – and offer a promising avenue for addressing it. The connection to probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan is also a strong point, highlighting the practical relevance of the work.

The technical contributions are solid: demonstrating the Jeffreys divergence bound, validating it on toy distributions, proposing a protocol for real-world data analysis, and implementing BREAD in established probabilistic programming systems. The experimental validation, while not exhaustive, is convincing, especially the demonstration of consistency between simulated and real data, and the uncovering of a bug in WebPPL. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to divergence measurement is genuinely novel. It reframes a well-established technique for a new purpose, opening up new possibilities for MCMC diagnostics.
*   **Significance:** Reliable MCMC validation is a long-standing problem. A rigorous, quantitative measure like the one proposed here could significantly improve the trustworthiness of Bayesian inference, particularly for non-expert users of probabilistic programming languages.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation appears solid, and the experimental validation supports the theoretical claims, at least for the tested scenarios.
*   **Practicality:** The integration with WebPPL and Stan demonstrates a commitment to practical application and accessibility.
*   **Bold Approach:** The paper tackles a difficult problem with a sophisticated technique, rather than offering incremental improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Applicability:** The method currently applies directly only to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue this encompasses many MCMC designs, the direct applicability remains limited. The extension to more general MCMC methods isn’t fully explored.
*   **Computational Cost:** BDMC can be computationally expensive, potentially negating its benefits for very complex models. This trade-off isn’t thoroughly discussed.
*   **Real-World Data Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data relies on fitting hyperparameters and then simulating from those. The authors acknowledge potential issues with this, but the impact of inaccurate hyperparameter estimates isn’t fully quantified. While the validation experiments mitigate this concern, it remains a point to consider.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental section, while well-executed, is limited to a few models. Broader evaluation across a more diverse set of problems would strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a core problem in Bayesian inference with a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The potential impact on the field is significant, particularly for users of probabilistic programming languages.
*   The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature and make a substantial technical contribution.
*   The implementation and validation work demonstrate the practical feasibility of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The applicability of the method is currently limited to a specific class of algorithms.
*   The computational cost of BDMC could be prohibitive for complex models.
*   The protocol for real-world data relies on assumptions that require careful consideration.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor limitations, this is a highly promising and impactful paper. The novelty of the approach, combined with its potential significance for improving the reliability of Bayesian inference, outweighs the current limitations. While the applicability is restricted to certain algorithms, the insights gained from this work could inform the development of diagnostics for more general MCMC methods. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and recommend **acceptance**. The authors should be encouraged to explore extensions of the method to broader classes of MCMC algorithms in future work. The discovery of the bug in WebPPL further strengthens the case for this type of rigorous validation tool.
