This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC-based algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which provides stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the gap between these two bounds provides an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior. As the method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse AIS chain, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a heuristic protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters and then simulating a dataset. The method is implemented and tested in the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages, where it is used to compare different model representations and to identify a bug in WebPPL.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Assessing the convergence and quality of MCMC samplers is a long-standing, difficult, and highly important problem in Bayesian inference. Standard diagnostics like the Gelman-Rubin statistic are valuable heuristics but lack the quantitative guarantees this paper aims for. Proposing a method that bounds a formal statistical divergence is a laudable goal.

2.  **Sound Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation in Section 3.1, showing that the expected gap between the forward and reverse BDMC bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence, is a clever and elegant extension of the analysis in the original BDMC paper [GGA15]. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method in the setting where it is applicable.

3.  **Compelling Use Case for Debugging:** The discovery of a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampler is a strong and practical demonstration of the method's utility. When theoretical guarantees are violated in practice (i.e., the upper bound falls below the lower bound), it provides a clear signal of an implementation error. This is a valuable contribution for developers of probabilistic programming languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Fundamental Limitation for Practical Use:** The primary weakness of this work is that its core theoretical guarantee—a rigorous bound on divergence—is only applicable to simulated data where an exact posterior sample can be obtained. This is a very strong requirement that is almost never met in real-world applications, which are precisely where users need reliable diagnostics the most. The paper's main selling point is its rigor, but this rigor vanishes in the settings where it would be most useful.

2.  **The Real-Data Protocol is a Heuristic:** The authors propose a protocol to handle real-world data (Section 3.2) that involves fitting hyperparameters and then simulating data. This procedure, while plausible, is a heuristic that undermines the method's claims of rigor. The validation in Section 5.1.2 shows that the reverse bounds are stable with respect to the number of MCMC steps used to generate the approximate posterior sample, but it does not validate the core assumption: that inference quality on this "realistic" simulated dataset is a faithful proxy for inference quality on the actual real-world dataset. The method may provide confidence for a problem that is similar to, but materially different from, the one the user actually wants to solve.

3.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper makes no attempt to compare BREAD to any existing, widely-used MCMC convergence diagnostics. How does the BREAD divergence bound relate to metrics like Gelman-Rubin R-hat or effective sample size (ESS)? Does BREAD detect non-convergence in cases where these standard methods fail, or vice-versa? Without such a comparison, it is very difficult to assess the practical added value of this method over the tools that practitioners already use. An evaluation on canonical difficult posteriors (e.g., Neal's funnel) with comparisons to R-hat would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims of utility.

4.  **Limited Applicability:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. The authors claim that "insights which inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly" can be gained, but this is an indirect benefit and is not demonstrated. For many common MCMC algorithms, like standard Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling, it is not clear how BREAD could be applied.

### Detailed Comments

-   **Quality & Significance:** The theoretical quality of the derivation in Section 3.1 is high. However, the overall quality of the work as a complete and practical solution is diminished by the strong reliance on simulated data and the heuristic nature of the real-data protocol. The significance is therefore mixed. For PPL developers and researchers designing new samplers, it offers a valuable tool for testing and debugging in a controlled environment. For the average practitioner analyzing real data, its utility is much less clear, and the claims of providing a "rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference" seem overstated.

-   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, and the main derivation is straightforward.

-   **Originality:** The work is an incremental but clever extension of the existing BDMC framework [GGA15]. The main original contribution is the reinterpretation of the BDMC bounds in terms of Jeffreys divergence.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically neat idea for quantifying the error of certain MCMC algorithms. However, its practical utility is severely constrained by the requirement of an exact posterior sample. The proposed workaround for real data is a heuristic that sacrifices the method's core promise of rigor. While the debugging use case is compelling, the absence of comparisons to established diagnostic tools makes it difficult to endorse this method as a general-purpose replacement or supplement for existing practices.
