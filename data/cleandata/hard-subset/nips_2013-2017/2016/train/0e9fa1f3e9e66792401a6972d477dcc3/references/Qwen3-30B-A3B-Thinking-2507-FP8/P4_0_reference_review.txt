

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015), which previously provided sandwich bounds on log marginal likelihood, to establish a similar bounding technique for the Jeffreys divergence. The paper demonstrates that BREAD can be applied to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space. The authors also propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validating consistency between real and simulated data.

The paper builds on the NIPS 2015 work by Grosse et al. (Grosse et al., 2015) and relates to related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also uses BDMC to evaluate inference quality. The authors clearly differentiate their approach by showing that BREAD provides rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, while the subjective divergence in Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) only upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence when using the true posterior as reference.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is rigorous and clearly shows that the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The authors provide exact computations of both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small toy examples, demonstrating the practical accuracy of their bound.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes thorough experimental validation across five different models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, autoregressive time series, and mixture-of-Gaussians). The validation of consistency between real and simulated data (Section 5.1.1) and the validation of the approximate posterior sampling protocol (Section 5.1.2) are particularly compelling.

3. **Practical Utility:** The authors successfully integrate BREAD into both WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its practical utility in real probabilistic programming systems. The method helped uncover a bug in WebPPL, showcasing its potential as a debugging tool.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation and sufficient background for readers unfamiliar with AIS or BDMC. The figures effectively illustrate key results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Applicability:** The method is only directly applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), which excludes many standard MCMC methods. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't sufficiently explore how broadly the approach can be generalized.

2. **Real-World Data Protocol:** While the protocol for real-world data is innovative, the paper doesn't thoroughly address scenarios where the fitted hyperparameters may not generate representative simulated data. The validation in Section 5.1.1 shows consistency for five models, but doesn't prove the approach works universally.

3. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of BREAD, which could be significant for large-scale applications. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains with multiple MCMC steps may be prohibitively expensive for large models.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The paper compares BREAD to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) but doesn't thoroughly compare to other MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Geweke) or methods for measuring sample quality (e.g., Gorham and Mackey, 2015).

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a theoretically sound method for rigorously bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors.
- Demonstrates practical utility by integrating into two major probabilistic programming languages.
- Validates the method through careful experiments on both toy examples and real-world models.
- Offers a useful protocol for applying the method to real-world data, addressing a significant gap in the field.

*Con:*
- Limited applicability to specific classes of inference algorithms (AIS, SMC).
- The real-world data protocol relies on assumptions about data representativeness that aren't fully validated across diverse scenarios.
- Doesn't sufficiently address the computational cost of the method, which could limit its practical adoption.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The technical soundness is strong, with clear theoretical derivations and thorough experimental validation. While the method has limited applicability to certain inference algorithms and the computational cost isn't fully addressed, the paper demonstrates significant practical utility through its integration into WebPPL and Stan. The work is well-positioned to help make probabilistic programming more accessible to non-experts by providing a reliable way to assess inference quality.

The paper meets NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (quality), well-written (clarity), extends previous work in a meaningful way (originality), and addresses an important practical problem with potential for broad impact (significance). The limitations noted are reasonable but don't undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
