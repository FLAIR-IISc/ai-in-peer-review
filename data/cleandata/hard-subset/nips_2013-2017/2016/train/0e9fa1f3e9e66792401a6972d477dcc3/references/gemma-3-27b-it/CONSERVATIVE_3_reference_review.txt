This paper proposes BREAD, an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The core idea is sound – leveraging BDMC’s ability to bound marginal likelihood ratios to similarly bound divergence measures. However, while the theoretical contributions appear correct, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling empirical validation beyond toy examples, and the practical applicability, particularly on real-world data, feels limited given the current methodology.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the background concepts (AIS, BDMC, Jeffreys divergence). The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound is logically presented, and the connection to existing work (particularly [CTM16]) is acknowledged. The extensions to WebPPL and Stan are a positive step, demonstrating some practical implementation effort. However, the experimental section feels underwhelming. While the authors acknowledge the challenges of applying BREAD to real data and propose a protocol involving hyperparameter fitting and simulated data, the validation primarily focuses on demonstrating consistency *between* simulated and real data, rather than absolute accuracy of the divergence bounds themselves. This is a clever workaround for the lack of ground truth on real data, but it doesn’t inspire confidence that the bounds are truly informative. The fact that the reverse AIS curves matched those from the forward chain is a consistency check, but doesn’t tell us anything about how *tight* the bounds are. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a happy accident and a nice demonstration of the method’s potential for debugging, but not a primary justification for acceptance.

A major weakness is the limited scope of applicability. BREAD is currently restricted to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue that insights from these algorithms can inform MCMC design broadly, this connection feels tenuous. The vast majority of MCMC methods don’t neatly fit this framework. The theoretical guarantees rely on having access to an exact posterior sample, achievable only on simulated data, necessitating the potentially problematic hyperparameter-fitting protocol for real-world application. 

The toy examples, while helpful for validating the method, lack the complexity needed to assess its utility in realistic scenarios. The matrix factorization experiment, while interesting, is limited in scope. The observation that the choice of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations impacts performance isn't novel; it’s a common consideration in probabilistic programming. BREAD provides a way to *quantify* this trade-off, but the magnitude of the effect appears relatively modest in the presented experiment.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Sound theoretical contribution: The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound is correct and potentially useful.
*   Practical implementation: Extending BREAD to WebPPL and Stan is a positive step.
*   Potential for debugging: The identification of a bug in WebPPL highlights a valuable application.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope: The method is currently restricted to a specific class of algorithms.
*   Weak empirical validation: Validation primarily focuses on consistency between simulated and real data, not absolute accuracy.
*   Reliance on hyperparameter fitting: The protocol for real-world data introduces potential biases.
*   Incremental contribution: The matrix factorization experiment provides limited novel insights.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound but ultimately incremental contribution. The theoretical results are interesting, but the limited scope and weak empirical validation raise concerns about its practical impact. I believe the paper falls short of the NIPS acceptance bar. It would benefit from more extensive experiments on a wider range of models and datasets, and a more thorough analysis of the tightness of the divergence bounds. A discussion of the computational cost of BREAD compared to other diagnostics would also be valuable. I recommend **rejection**.
