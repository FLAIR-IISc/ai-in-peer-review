Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) beyond marginal likelihood estimation to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by AIS/SMC-style algorithms. The core theoretical result shows that the gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS log-normalizer estimates, ˆB = log(1/Ŕrev) − log Ŕ, upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence J between the distribution of AIS terminal states qfwd(xT) and the true target pT. The authors further (i) validate tightness on small toy models (exact J), (ii) propose a pragmatic protocol to diagnose inference on real data via fitted-hyperparameter simulation (parametric-bootstrap-like), (iii) integrate the method (“BREAD”) into WebPPL and Stan, and (iv) use it to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations and to uncover a subtle implementation bug.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001), connects with thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski, and relates to Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). It is contemporaneous with “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which can be seen as a closely related estimator where the reverse path need not start from an exact posterior. The paper appropriately positions its contribution and clarifies the differences, with the main novelty being the explicit Jeffreys-divergence upper bound in expectation and the practical tooling/protocol for probabilistic programming systems.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations in Eqs. (6)–(12) are correct and clearly motivated: forward and reverse AIS expectations yield one-sided KL terms, and the gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The reliance on expectation is important; unlike the BDMC sandwich for log Z, the divergence bound is not high-probability. This limitation is acknowledged but could be emphasized more prominently.
- Scope: The methodology applies to inference schemes representable as importance sampling on an extended state space (AIS, SMC). It does not directly validate standard long-run MCMC samplers, though AIS transitions can be built from MCMC kernels (e.g., NUTS in Stan), and design insights may transfer.
- Experiments: The empirical sections are useful and pragmatic, but somewhat qualitative. The real/simulated-data consistency checks rely on comparing quartiles of log-AIS estimates after vertical alignment; this supports the fitted-hyperparameter proxy but remains heuristic. The toy problems in the appendix provide exact J vs. bound comparisons; it would strengthen the paper to report quantitative estimates of E[ˆB] with confidence intervals on more realistic models, and to plot the estimated J upper bound as a function of T/K and annealing schedule. The demonstration of representation tradeoffs (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and the debugging anecdote are strong practical contributions.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. Suggestions:
- Make explicit how practitioners should estimate E[ˆB] (number of forward/reverse runs, averaging scheme, uncertainty quantification) and how to interpret a single realization of ˆB versus its expectation.
- Provide algorithmic pseudocode for BREAD as used in Stan/WebPPL, including temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and transition choices.
- More guidance on selecting schedules/kernels to obtain informative bounds (e.g., adaptive schedules, moment averaging).
- In Sec. 5, report the actual estimated divergence bounds (means/SEs) rather than only distributions of AIS estimates; show how bound tightness evolves with T and K.

Originality
The theoretical observation that the BDMC forward–reverse gap controls the Jeffreys divergence is simple but worthwhile and, to my knowledge, not explicitly articulated before BDMC was proposed, aside from very recent concurrent work (CTM 2016). The proposed real-data protocol and integration into two probabilistic programming frameworks are valuable and original in practice. The paper’s novelty is moderate: conceptually close to BDMC, but with a clear and useful twist plus tools.

Significance
Measuring sample quality for complex posteriors remains challenging. Providing a (nearly) automated, model-agnostic procedure to upper-bound a symmetric divergence—especially one that can expose implementation bugs and guide modeling choices—is impactful for probabilistic programming and for evaluating AIS/SMC schemes. The requirement for exact posterior samples limits rigor to simulated data; the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is a reasonable bridge but not a guarantee. Still, I expect this to be adopted by practitioners building inference tooling and by researchers comparing annealing/SMC designs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Only an expectation bound: No high-probability guarantee for J; a single run of ˆB can be misleading. Provide CIs or concentration results where possible.
- Dependence on schedule and kernels: The bound evaluates a specific AIS/SMC configuration, not “MCMC inference” in general; statements about general MCMC could be tempered.
- Real-data protocol is heuristic: Validation via quartile overlap is suggestive but not decisive; add stress tests (misspecification, heavy tails, multimodality) and show when the procedure fails.
- Reporting: The main text lacks quantitative divergence bound estimates on realistic models; include numeric results to complement curves.
- Comparisons: Limited empirical comparison with standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, trace pathologies) and with Stein discrepancies; showing cases where BREAD catches issues missed by others would strengthen the case.

Requests for clarification
- How many reverse AIS runs are used to estimate E[ˆB]? How is variance controlled for heavy-tailed weight distributions?
- Sensitivity to temperature schedules: any adaptive schemes explored?
- For Stan/NUTS transitions within AIS, what step-size/trajectory tuning was used at each temperature?

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution linking BDMC gaps to Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical integration in Stan/WebPPL; useful debugging tool.
- Sensible protocol for bridging to real data; validation across diverse models.
- Insightful application to representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed).

Cons
- Expectation-only guarantee; limited guidance on estimation accuracy.
- Real-data evaluation largely qualitative; missing numeric divergence bounds.
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-like algorithms.
- Some overstatement of “rigorous” monitoring beyond simulated-data settings.

Recommendation: Weak accept (7/10). The paper offers a useful and implementable advance with solid theory and practical implications for the community, despite limitations in empirical rigor and scope.

Confidence: High.
