### Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The central problem addressed is the long-standing difficulty of rigorously assessing whether an MCMC sampler has produced a good approximation to the true posterior distribution. Existing convergence diagnostics are largely heuristic and can only detect non-convergence rather than confirm convergence.

The authors' key insight is to repurpose the recently proposed bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. BDMC uses a forward and a reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chain to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. This paper's main theoretical contribution is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples generated by AIS and the true target distribution. This elegantly reframes a tool for estimating a point value (the marginal likelihood) into a powerful diagnostic for assessing the quality of an entire approximate distribution.

A critical requirement for the reverse AIS chain, and thus for the bound, is an exact sample from the true posterior. This is only feasible for simulated data. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, fit hyperparameters on the real dataset; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters (which provides an exact posterior sample for this simulated data); and third, run BREAD on the simulated data, after validating that the sampler's behavior is consistent between the real and simulated datasets. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to provide concrete guidance on model representation choices and to uncover a bug in WebPPL's implementation.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Originality & Significance:**

This work is exceptionally original and significant. The idea of using the BDMC log-likelihood gap to bound a statistical divergence is a brilliant and non-obvious conceptual leap. It moves beyond the traditional, limited paradigm of convergence diagnostics towards a quantitative measure of approximation error. This addresses one of the most critical and challenging open problems in probabilistic machine learning: how can we trust the output of black-box inference systems?

The potential impact is substantial. BREAD could become a standard, automated tool for developers of probabilistic programming languages to debug and benchmark their systems. The paper provides a compelling proof of concept by using BREAD to uncover a real bug in WebPPPL. For practitioners, it offers a principled way to make crucial modeling and algorithmic decisions, such as choosing between different model parameterizations (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed), as demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiment. This work does not offer an incremental improvement; it proposes a new and powerful paradigm for assessing inference quality.

**Quality:**

The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation connecting the BDMC gap to the Jeffreys divergence is clear, simple, and appears correct. The experimental work is thorough and well-designed. The authors do not simply propose the method but also carefully validate its crucial components. They show:
1.  The bound is reasonably tight on toy examples where the true divergence can be computed (Appendix B).
2.  The proposed protocol for real-world data is sound, demonstrating that sampler behavior is consistent between real data and data simulated from fitted hyperparameters (Section 5.1.1).
3.  The protocol is robust to the use of an approximate posterior sample for initializing the reverse chain (Section 5.1.2).

The main weakness is inherent to the problem: the rigorous theoretical guarantee applies only to simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a well-motivated and empirically validated heuristic, but it remains a heuristic. The authors are commendably transparent about this limitation. While this is a clear boundary on the method's guarantees, it does not diminish the novelty of the core idea or its utility in the simulated setting, which is invaluable for algorithm development and testing.

A minor point for improvement would be a more detailed discussion of the variance of the bound estimator `ˆB`. The theory provides a bound on `E[ˆB]`, but in practice, one obtains a finite set of samples of `ˆB`. Given that AIS weights can be heavy-tailed, guidance on how to reliably estimate this expectation would strengthen the practical recommendations.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on AIS and BDMC before clearly presenting their novel derivation. The structure is logical, and the experiments are explained clearly with informative figures. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty:** The connection between the BDMC gap and Jeffreys divergence is a fundamental and highly original contribution.
2.  **Major Significance:** The work addresses a grand challenge in the field—the validation of approximate inference—and has the potential to become a foundational tool for both researchers and practitioners.
3.  **Bold Vision:** This is exactly the kind of ambitious, paradigm-shifting research that NIPS should champion. It opens up numerous avenues for future work.
4.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The method is not just theoretical; it is implemented in major PPLs and thoroughly validated on a range of models, including a compelling demonstration of its utility in debugging and model comparison.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  **Heuristic for Real Data:** The primary limitation is that the rigorous guarantees are lost when applying the method to real-world data, where a heuristic protocol is required.

**Recommendation**

This paper is a clear accept. The central idea is bold, novel, and significant. It provides a new lens through which to view and evaluate the performance of inference algorithms. While the direct application to real-world data relies on a heuristic, this is a necessary compromise given the difficulty of the problem, and the authors' proposed protocol is sensible and well-validated. The weaknesses are minor compared to the profound strengths of the work. This paper represents a major step forward in making probabilistic inference more reliable and trustworthy, and I expect it will inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
