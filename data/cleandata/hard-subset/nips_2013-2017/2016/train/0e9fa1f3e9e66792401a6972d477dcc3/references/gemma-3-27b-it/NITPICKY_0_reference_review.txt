This paper proposes BREAD, an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior, applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC. The core idea is sound – leveraging the strengths of BDMC to provide quantifiable reliability measures for MCMC-based inference. The authors also propose a pragmatic protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulated data generation. While the technical contributions are incremental and the core idea builds heavily on existing work (BDMC, AIS), the application to assessing MCMC reliability *is* novel and potentially valuable. However, the execution and presentation suffer from a level of meticulousness that ultimately detracts from the overall impact and raises serious concerns about practical applicability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application:** Applying BDMC to quantify the accuracy of approximate posterior samples is a genuinely novel idea. The Jeffreys divergence is a well-motivated metric for assessing the quality of approximate inference.
*   **Rigorous Analysis (in principle):** The theoretical framework, while building on existing work, is presented with apparent rigor. The attempt to bound the Jeffreys divergence provides a concrete, quantifiable measure, unlike many heuristic convergence diagnostics.
*   **Implementation in Probabilistic Programming Languages:** Extending WebPPL and Stan demonstrates a commitment to making the method accessible to practitioners.
*   **Identification of a Bug:** Discovering a bug in WebPPL is a positive outcome, highlighting the potential of BREAD as a testing tool.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** The core technical contribution – showing BDMC yields an estimator upper-bounding Jeffreys divergence – feels more like a natural extension of existing work than a breakthrough. The analysis largely *re-derives* known results from BDMC in a slightly different context.
*   **Overly Detailed and Pedantic Presentation:** The paper is excessively detailed, bordering on pedantic. Every step is dissected, and the notation becomes cumbersome. This makes it difficult to grasp the core ideas quickly. For example, repeatedly stating “in expectation” feels unnecessarily verbose.
*   **Practical Limitations:** The requirement for exact posterior samples for the reverse chain, even with the proposed workaround, significantly limits applicability to real-world scenarios. The hyperparameter fitting protocol feels *ad hoc* and relies on assumptions (e.g., the fitted hyperparameters are representative) that are not thoroughly justified. The authors acknowledge these limitations, but don’t adequately address them.
*   **Experimental Validation is Insufficient:** While the authors validate the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data, the experiments are limited to a small number of models. The claim that the results generalize is not convincingly supported. The demonstration of a bug in WebPPL, while positive, isn't a sufficient justification for the entire work.
*   **Formatting Inconsistencies & Typos:** Numerous minor formatting issues and typos exist throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent equation numbering, references not always appearing in the bibliography, a floating period). While individually minor, their cumulative effect detracts from the paper’s professionalism and suggests a lack of careful review. Even the citation style is inconsistent (e.g., [GGA15] vs [CGHL+ p]).
*   **Unnecessary Redundancy:** The paper contains a significant amount of redundant material. For example, the background section repeats well-known concepts (AIS, stochastic lower bounds) with excessive detail, slowing down the reader.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of BREAD. Running multiple AIS chains, especially for complex models, is likely to be expensive, potentially making it impractical for routine use.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The application of BDMC to assess MCMC reliability is a novel and potentially impactful idea.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan could be beneficial to practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The technical contribution is incremental and doesn't represent a substantial advance in the field.
*   The practical limitations and computational cost raise serious concerns about the method's applicability.
*   The overly detailed and pedantic presentation obscures the core ideas.
*   The numerous formatting issues and typos indicate a lack of attention to detail.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the application, I recommend **rejection**. The paper presents a technically sound but ultimately incremental contribution. The limitations and practical challenges, combined with the excessively detailed presentation and numerous minor flaws, outweigh the potential benefits. While the authors’ meticulousness is evident, it ultimately hinders the paper’s clarity and impact. The paper would benefit significantly from a more concise presentation, a more thorough discussion of practical limitations, and a more compelling demonstration of its utility in realistic scenarios. The paper feels like an overly detailed proof-of-concept rather than a mature research contribution.
