- Paper extends BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and sampler output for AIS/SMC-like samplers via forward–reverse log-evidence gap (BREAD).
- Main theoretical result: expected forward-minus-reverse log-evidence gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence of the marginal distribution of the final AIS state.
- BREAD gives rigorous guarantees on simulated data (requires exact posterior sample) and a practical, heuristic protocol for real data using fitted hyperparameters and approximate reverse initialization.
- Authors implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validate it across five models, including a matrix factorization case study and a debugging example.
- Builds directly on BDMC and AIS; contrasts with Stein discrepancy and subjective divergence approaches and claims fewer distributional assumptions for simulated-data guarantees.
- Theoretical derivations are sound and correctly trace to BDMC; the bound is explicitly an expectation bound (not per-run high-probability).
- Key limitation: requirement of an exact posterior sample restricts rigorous guarantees to simulated-data scenarios.
- Real-data protocol is plausible but heuristic; approximate reverse initialization removes formal guarantees.
- Empirical work is valuable (PPL integration, multi-model validation, toy-calibration) but some detailed results are relegated to the appendix.
- Major evaluation weakness: the bound holds in expectation—paper does not quantify variability of the estimator ˆB or provide confidence intervals for E[ˆB].
- Tightness and sensitivity analyses are limited: not systematically studied across dimensionality, annealing schedules, number of temperatures, or MCMC kernel choices.
- Applicability is restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers; claims about broader MCMC design influence are largely heuristic.
- Empirical evidence suggests reverse curves are robust to number of hyperparameter MCMC steps, but lacks formal robustness analysis.
- Clarity is good overall; AIS/BDMC background and main derivation are clear and well organized.
- Suggested clarity improvements: add BREAD pseudo-code, emphasize that guarantees are on expectation and recommend reporting mean/SE over replicates, move key appendix figures into main text.
- Originality: neat, nontrivial extension of BDMC interpreting forward–reverse gap as a Jeffreys-divergence bound and useful PPL integration.
- Significance: practical tool for certifying inference on simulated data, informing model/algorithm selection, and debugging; likely to be adopted in PPL/MCMC communities.
- Pros for acceptance: correct theoretical contribution, practical impact via implementations and experiments, timely relevance, honest discussion of limitations.
- Cons for acceptance: expectation-only guarantees limited to simulated data, limited tightness/sensitivity exploration, and restricted applicability to AIS/SMC samplers.
- Actionable suggestions: report variability of ˆB and recommend replication strategies; analyze sensitivity to schedules, temperatures, and kernels; include a higher-dimensional example with cost analysis.
- Additional suggestions: discuss using existing logZ estimates (TI/VB) to bound one-sided KLs, and clarify whether averaging AIS runs/weights aligns with the theoretical bound.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed contribution meeting conference criteria; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
