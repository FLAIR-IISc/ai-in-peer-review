- Summary: Extends BDMC to bound the expected Jeffreys divergence between AIS/SMC terminal-state distribution and the true posterior by combining forward and reverse AIS estimates; proposes a pragmatic protocol to transfer guarantees from simulated to real data via fitted-hyperparameter simulation.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Neal’s AIS, thermodynamic integration, BDMC, Jarzynski equality, and relates to subjective divergence / Stein diagnostics, focusing on the reverse-from-true-posterior regime.

Strengths
- Theoretical link: Provides a clear, elegant derivation that the expected BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS output and target.
- Practical tooling: Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan with multiple canonical models and a detailed case study.
- Empirical utility: Demonstrates practical value (including finding a WebPPL bug) and a sensible protocol to approximate guarantees on real data.
- Scope clarity: Explicit about when guarantees hold (simulated data) versus when the procedure is heuristic (real-data fits).

Weaknesses / Suggestions
- Limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers: Conclusions do not directly extend to generic MCMC (e.g., HMC-only workflows); needs discussion of when AIS is a faithful proxy.
- Expectation vs instance guarantees: Bound is in expectation — provide guidance on estimating the expectation (number of runs, CIs, heavy-tail handling) and report mean gaps with robust uncertainty measures.
- Heuristic real-data protocol: Initializing reverse AIS from approximate hyperparameter posteriors breaks guarantees; add diagnostics comparing MLE vs posterior mean and quantify similarity between fitted and true posteriors.
- Tightness characterization lacking at scale: Report estimated DJ upper bounds with SEs and sensitivity to schedule length, temperatures, and kernel quality; give guidance on schedule design.
- SMC relation underexplored: Framework applies to SMC but experiments focus on AIS — include at least an implementation note or short experiment for SMC.
- Presentation / reproducibility: Add pseudocode, a how-to guide, and consolidated exact settings (temperatures, steps, tuning) for reproducibility.

Quality
- Theory is correct and clearly presented; experiments support claims but should improve reporting of expectations and uncertainties.

Clarity
- Paper is generally clear and organized; suggested additions include an algorithm box, more quantitative reporting, and a practitioner recipe.

Originality
- Novel extension of BDMC to bound posterior sample quality in expectation and a practical real-data protocol; relation to CTM16 acknowledged.

Significance
- Provides a rigorous, actionable diagnostic for AIS/SMC posterior inference with useful tooling likely to influence PPL and MCMC practice.

Questions for authors
- How many independent AIS runs are required in practice to estimate the expected gap with acceptable variance?
- Can you provide empirical calibration of the bound on larger models (e.g., via conservative surrogates or longer runs)?
- Any experience or recommendations for adaptive temperature schedules to tighten the bound?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with practical relevance and tooling across two PPLs.
- Diverse experiments and a compelling debugging example demonstrating real impact.
- Honest presentation of scope and limitations with constructive practitioner guidance.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees are expectation-only and mainly valid for simulated data; real-data protocol remains heuristic.
- Empirical focus is AIS-centric with limited SMC exploration.
- Reporting should be more quantitative regarding expectation estimates and uncertainty.

Recommendation
- Accept.
