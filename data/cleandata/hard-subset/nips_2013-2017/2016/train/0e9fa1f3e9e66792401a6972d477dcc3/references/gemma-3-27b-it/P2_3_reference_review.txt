## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – previously used for marginal likelihood estimation – to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior and the true posterior. The method is applicable to algorithms framed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS and SMC). The authors derive an estimator for this bound, demonstrate its accuracy on toy examples, propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data, and implement it within WebPPL and Stan. They also leverage the tool to analyze model representation choices in matrix factorization.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to directly assess posterior accuracy is a valuable contribution.  It provides a more rigorous approach to MCMC diagnostics than traditional methods relying on convergence heuristics.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence upper bound provides a strong theoretical basis for the method (BREAD). The connection to stochastic bounds and the use of reverse AIS are well-explained.
* **Practical Implementation & Validation:** Implementing BREAD in two widely-used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) increases its accessibility and impact. The validation experiments, particularly demonstrating consistency between real and simulated data, are crucial for establishing confidence in the method.
* **Useful Scientific Findings:** The application of BREAD to compare model representations in matrix factorization provides a concrete example of its utility in guiding modeling decisions.  The discovery of a bug in WebPPL further highlights its potential for code verification.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making the complex ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Applicability:**  The method's restriction to algorithms amenable to importance sampling framing (AIS, SMC) is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this and suggest broader implications, the direct application remains constrained.  A more thorough discussion of how the *insights* from BREAD could inform MCMC diagnostics *beyond* these specific algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:**  BDMC, even for marginal likelihood estimation, is computationally expensive.  Applying it for full posterior evaluation adds substantial overhead.  The paper doesn’t adequately address the practical scalability of BREAD for complex models and datasets.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (Real-World Protocol):** The protocol for real-world data relies on fitting hyperparameters and then simulating from those.  The performance of this process relies heavily on accurate hyperparameter estimation and the representativeness of the simulated data, as acknowledged by the authors, but could be a significant source of error in practice.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the validation experiments are commendable, the number of models tested (five) is relatively small. A broader range of models and datasets would further strengthen the claims.
* **Connection to Existing Diagnostics:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how BREAD complements (or surpasses) existing MCMC diagnostics like R-hat, effective sample size, and traceplots.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results. The connection to established concepts like Jeffreys divergence and stochastic bounds provides rigor. It’s a complete piece of work, presenting a new method, analysis, implementation, and application.  The authors are careful about the limitations, particularly regarding the applicability to algorithms beyond AIS/SMC.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is well-written and logically organized. The concepts are explained clearly, although some background knowledge of MCMC and importance sampling is assumed.
* **Originality:** The application of BDMC to assess posterior accuracy is novel. While the underlying techniques (BDMC, KL divergence, importance sampling) are established, their combination and the derived upper bound are original contributions. The related work section is thorough and accurately positions the work within the existing literature.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is significant. Providing a more reliable measure of MCMC accuracy could substantially improve trust in probabilistic inference, particularly for non-expert users of probabilistic programming languages.  The debugging application is also valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to MCMC diagnostics.
* Practical implementation in widely used tools (WebPPL, Stan).
* Demonstrates clear benefits and potential impact.
* Addresses a long-standing challenge in probabilistic inference: reliably validating MCMC results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited applicability to a specific class of algorithms.
* Potential computational cost may hinder widespread adoption.
* Real-world protocol relies on accurate hyperparameter estimation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the limited applicability and computational cost are valid concerns, the novelty of the approach, its theoretical rigor, and the practical implementation make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the scalability concerns and providing a more detailed comparison to existing MCMC diagnostics in future work.  A slight rewrite to more explicitly highlight the broader implications *beyond* AIS/SMC, even if they are currently speculative, would also be beneficial.
