### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of diagnosing the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. While numerous convergence diagnostics exist (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size), they are largely heuristic and cannot provide quantitative guarantees on the quality of the resulting approximate posterior. This work proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which provides a stochastic upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior.

The method is a clever and insightful extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15]. BDMC itself provides stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood by running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) [Nea01] in both forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions. The core theoretical contribution of this paper is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds from BDMC is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of samples generated by the forward AIS chain and the true target distribution. This connects the problem of estimating a normalizing constant (the focus of BDMC) to the problem of assessing sample quality.

The major caveat is that initializing the reverse AIS chain requires an exact sample from the true posterior, limiting the rigorous application of BREAD to simulated data. To bridge this gap for real-world problems, the authors propose a pragmatic protocol: fit model hyperparameters on the real data, simulate a new dataset from this fitted model, and then run BREAD on this synthetic dataset. The paper also discusses its relationship to concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which uses a similar mechanism.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The problem of evaluating MCMC reliability is fundamental to Bayesian statistics and machine learning. A tool that provides a quantitative, theoretically-grounded measure of divergence from the true posterior would be invaluable for practitioners choosing between models or inference strategies, and for developers of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) debugging their systems. This work is a significant step in that direction.

2.  **Elegant and Sound Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the expected gap in BDMC's log marginal likelihood bounds to the Jeffreys divergence, is the paper's key insight. It is simple, elegant, and appears to be technically sound. It cleverly repurposes the existing machinery of BDMC to answer a different, but equally important, question.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation.
    *   The protocol for applying the method to real-world data is well-motivated and validated by showing that the sampler's behavior is consistent between real and simulated datasets across several models (Sec 5.1.1).
    *   They pragmatically address the need for an exact posterior sample in their protocol by using an approximate one, and demonstrate that the results are largely insensitive to the quality of this approximation (Sec 5.1.2).
    *   The application of BREAD to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) in Stan and WebPPL (Sec 5.2.1) is a compelling use case that clearly demonstrates the method's utility for making practical modeling decisions.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL (Sec 5.2.2) is a powerful testament to the method's value as a rigorous diagnostic and debugging tool.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the background on AIS and BDMC is concise and sufficient, the core method is derived step-by-step, and the experiments are logically structured to validate the approach and then demonstrate its utility.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Limited Applicability of the Core Method:** The proposed divergence bound applies directly only to samplers that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended space, such as AIS and SMC. It does not directly apply to more standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling, which remain workhorses of the field. While the authors argue that insights from evaluating AIS can inform the design of MCMC more broadly, this is an indirect benefit. A more detailed discussion of the fundamental obstacles to extending this approach to general MCMC would be welcome.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data, while pragmatic, relies on two key approximations that break the rigorousness of the bound. First, it assumes that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters is a good proxy for behavior on real data. This may not hold if the model is misspecified. Second, it uses an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain. While the authors show empirically that this seems robust, it means the result is no longer a guaranteed bound in expectation. The paper is honest about this, but the transition from a "rigorous bound on simulated data" to a "validated heuristic on real data" could be emphasized more clearly.

3.  **Computational Cost:** Running BDMC is computationally intensive, requiring full AIS runs in both a forward and a reverse direction. The paper does not analyze or discuss this overhead. For a practitioner, the key question is whether the cost of running BREAD is justified compared to, for example, simply running the forward sampler for a much longer time. A discussion on the computational trade-offs would strengthen the paper's practical guidance.

4.  **Nature of the Bound:** The bound is on the *expectation* of the gap, i.e., `E[log(R_rev^-1) - log(R)] >= D_J`. This means that a single estimated gap is not a guaranteed upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. While the authors are careful with their language, the practical implications for interpreting a single BREAD run could be made more explicit. How many BDMC replicates are needed to get a reliable estimate of this expected gap?

### Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the important problem of MCMC diagnostics. The core idea of using the BDMC gap to bound the Jeffreys divergence is both clever and useful. The authors support their theoretical contribution with a strong set of experiments that demonstrate the practical utility of their method (BREAD) for guiding modeling decisions and debugging inference engines. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a reasonable and well-validated heuristic for overcoming the primary limitation of the core technique.

While the method's applicability is currently limited to AIS-style samplers and the computational cost may be high, these are acceptable limitations for an initial proposal of this kind. The strengths of the paper—its novelty, significance, technical soundness, and clear presentation—far outweigh its weaknesses. This work is likely to be influential and to inspire follow-up research on quantitative MCMC diagnostics.

**Recommendation: Accept**
