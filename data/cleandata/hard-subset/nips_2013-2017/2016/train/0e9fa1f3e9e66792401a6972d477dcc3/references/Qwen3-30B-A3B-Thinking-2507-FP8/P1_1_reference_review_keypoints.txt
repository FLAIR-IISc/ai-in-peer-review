- Introduces BREAD, an extension of BDMC that bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors for importance-sampling-over-extended-state-space algorithms (e.g., AIS, SMC).
- Main contribution 1: Shows BDMC yields an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Main contribution 2: Provides a technique to compute both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples.
- Main contribution 3: Proposes a protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.
- Main contribution 4: Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating practical applicability.
- Strength: Rigorous theoretical derivation extending BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1).
- Strength: Practical integration into popular probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan).
- Strength: Thoughtful validation protocol that shows simulated data can proxy for real data in experiments.
- Strength: Demonstrates scientific utility by guiding modeling/representation choices and debugging implementations.
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation combining theory, toy examples, and real-world models.
- Weakness: Applicability limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended state spaces; unclear extension to standard MCMC (e.g., MH, HMC).
- Weakness: Computational cost concerns not fully addressed—running forward and reverse AIS may be expensive for large-scale problems.
- Weakness: Real-data validation limited to five models; unclear performance on higher-dimensional or more complex datasets.
- Weakness: Lacks thorough empirical comparison to alternative MCMC reliability methods (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, ESS) in accuracy, cost, and usability.
- Weakness: Theoretical limitation that exact posterior samples are required (available only for simulated data), restricting direct real-world applicability without the proposed protocol.
- Intro: Well-motivated framing linking the need for rigorous MCMC reliability measures to probabilistic programming.
- Background: Clear, thorough explanation of reverse AIS and BDMC.
- Methods: Strong theoretical analysis showing the forward-reverse gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence; needs more discussion of reverse-chain computational cost.
- Experiments: Well-designed validation and useful WebPPL vs Stan comparison; would benefit from reporting computational resource comparisons to alternatives.
- Related work: Good coverage but needs deeper comparison to established convergence diagnostics and alternative reliability measures.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—novel, rigorous, practically useful method despite scope and cost limitations.
- Final scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.
