

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples generated by MCMC-based inference algorithms (specifically AIS and SMC) and the true posterior distribution. The authors build upon the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework introduced in [GGA15], which provides both stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between the forward and reverse AIS estimates (which BDMC provides) upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The paper then presents a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validating that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data.

This work builds on several important lines of research: the bidirectional Monte Carlo framework of [GGA15], the theoretical foundations of annealed importance sampling [Nea01], and the growing interest in convergence diagnostics for MCMC [Brooks and Gelman, 1998]. It also relates to recent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which similarly uses BDMC to evaluate posterior inference, though with a different initialization strategy for the reverse chain.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a rigorous derivation showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. This is a significant theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in MCMC validation.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors successfully extend both WebPPL and Stan to implement BREAD, making the method accessible to practitioners. Their application to matrix factorization models demonstrates the method's utility in guiding model representation choices.

3. **Validation of Protocol**: The authors carefully validate their protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by showing that inference behavior on simulated data (with fitted hyperparameters) closely matches that on real data across multiple model types. This is a critical step in making the method practically useful.

4. **Practical Utility**: The method uncovered a bug in WebPPL, demonstrating its potential as a debugging tool for probabilistic programming systems. This practical application adds significant value beyond theoretical contributions.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear background sections and detailed experimental results. The figures effectively communicate the key findings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the method provides rigorous bounds for simulated data, the protocol for real-world data relies on an unproven assumption that the behavior on simulated data matches that on real data. The paper does not provide a theoretical analysis of how the quality of the fitted hyperparameters affects the accuracy of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence.

2. **Limited Analysis of Approximation Error**: The paper shows that using approximate posterior samples (from fitted hyperparameters) for the reverse chain initialization does not significantly affect the results (Fig. 1), but doesn't provide a theoretical analysis of how the approximation error in the reverse chain initialization propagates to the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. This is a critical gap for real-world applications.

3. **Comparison to Alternative Validation Methods**: While the paper acknowledges related work, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison of BREAD with existing MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size). It would be valuable to understand how BREAD compares to these methods in terms of computational cost, reliability, and interpretability.

4. **Scalability Concerns**: The method requires running both forward and reverse AIS chains, which can be computationally expensive, especially for large-scale problems. The paper doesn't discuss how the computational cost scales with problem size or how this compares to other validation methods.

5. **Lack of Discussion on Multimodal Distributions**: While the paper mentions that BREAD makes no assumptions about the distribution (unlike [GM15]), it doesn't provide specific examples or analysis of how the method performs on multimodal posteriors, which are common in real-world applications.

## Technical Assessment

The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is sound and well-executed, providing a clear connection between BDMC and the Jeffreys divergence. The key insight that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is novel and valuable.

However, I have concerns about the practical implementation for real-world data. The protocol in Section 3.2 assumes that the fitted hyperparameters will generate data that is representative of the real data, but the paper doesn't provide a theoretical justification for this assumption. While the experiments in Section 5.1.1 show good consistency between real and simulated data for five models, this is limited to specific model classes and dataset sizes. The authors should discuss the conditions under which this protocol is likely to work and when it might fail.

The comparison to [CTM16] is somewhat superficial. The paper correctly notes that [CTM16] uses a reference distribution rather than the true posterior for the reverse chain initialization, but it doesn't explore the implications of this difference for the accuracy of the bounds. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper.

## Experimental Assessment

The experimental validation in Section 5.1 is thorough and well-executed. The authors convincingly demonstrate that inference behavior on simulated data is consistent with that on real data across multiple model types. The use of quartiles rather than means to visualize the AIS estimates is appropriate given the heavy-tailed nature of these estimates.

The application to matrix factorization models (Section 5.2.1) is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates how BREAD can provide actionable insights for model design. The finding that the optimal representation differs between WebPPL and Stan is a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic programming.

The debugging case (Section 5.2.2) is a strong practical demonstration of the method's utility, though it would be helpful to provide more details on the specific bug discovered and how it was resolved.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of validating MCMC inference. The theoretical foundation is solid, the method is well-motivated, and the practical implementation and validation are thorough. While the paper has some limitations in terms of theoretical guarantees for real-world data and scalability, these are common challenges in this field and don't detract from the overall quality of the work.

The paper addresses an important practical problem in probabilistic inference that has been largely neglected in the literature. The ability to provide rigorous bounds on the error of approximate posterior samples is valuable for both practitioners and researchers. The integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately useful to a wide audience.

## Recommendations

**Acceptance Recommendation**: Accept

**Reasoning**: This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference validation. The theoretical results are sound, the method is well-motivated, and the experimental validation is thorough. While there are some limitations in the scope of the theoretical guarantees, the practical utility of the method is demonstrated through multiple case studies. The paper is well-written, clear, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement**:

1. Add a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the real-world data protocol, including theoretical analysis of how the quality of the fitted hyperparameters affects the upper bound.

2. Provide a more comprehensive comparison to existing MCMC convergence diagnostics, including their strengths and weaknesses relative to BREAD.

3. Expand the discussion of how the method handles multimodal distributions, which are common in real-world applications.

4. Include a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of BREAD compared to other validation methods.

5. Clarify the relationship between BREAD and [CTM16] in the related work section, particularly regarding the implications of using a reference distribution versus the true posterior for the reverse chain initialization.

## Final Thoughts

This paper addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference: the lack of rigorous methods for validating the quality of MCMC-based posterior approximations. The authors provide a theoretically sound method with practical implementation that can be immediately useful to researchers and practitioners. While there are some limitations in the scope of the theoretical guarantees for real-world data, the experimental validation suggests the method is robust in practice. The paper is well-written, well-executed, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
