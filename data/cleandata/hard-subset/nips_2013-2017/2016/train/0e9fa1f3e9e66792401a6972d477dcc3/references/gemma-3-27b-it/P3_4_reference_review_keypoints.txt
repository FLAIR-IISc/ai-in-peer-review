- Extends BDMC to give a rigorous upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior.  
- Proposes a practical protocol (hyperparameter estimation + simulated-data validation) and implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating utility on matrix factorization and finding a WebPPL bug.  
- Builds on BDMC, AIS, and SMC and positions itself within MCMC convergence diagnostics, distinguishing itself from related work (e.g., Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka).  
- Strength: Provides a rigorous guarantee (upper bound) on Jeffreys divergence, improving confidence in MCMC results.  
- Strength: Practical protocol for applying the method to real data, with a connection to parametric bootstrap.  
- Strength: Implemented and validated in two probabilistic programming languages, showing practicality and reproducibility.  
- Strength: Demonstrated utility for model design decisions and as a debugging/testing tool.  
- Strength: Well-written and clearly organized with detailed derivations.  
- Weakness: Applicability is limited to inference algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space.  
- Weakness: Computationally expensive—requires exact posterior samples for simulated data and many AIS chains.  
- Weakness: Relies on accurate hyperparameter estimation for realistic simulations; needs more sensitivity analysis to initial conditions/prior choices.  
- Weakness: The divergence bound’s practical interpretation is limited—loose bounds may be uninformative about actual posterior error.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, rigorous, practically useful method addressing a key need, with solid implementation and presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited applicability and high computational cost could impede widespread adoption.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to discuss computational trade-offs and hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Reviewer confidence: 9/10.
