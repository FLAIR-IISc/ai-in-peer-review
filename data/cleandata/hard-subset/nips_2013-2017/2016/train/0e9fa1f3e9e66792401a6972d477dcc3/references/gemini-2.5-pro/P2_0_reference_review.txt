**Summary**

This paper addresses the long-standing and critical problem of diagnosing the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors propose a novel method, Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to use Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to generate both stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key technical contribution is showing that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

Because the method requires an exact sample from the posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, its rigorous guarantees apply only to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; and third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset, using an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate the real-world data protocol, compare different model representations, and even uncover a bug in WebPPL's implementation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of assessing MCMC convergence is fundamental to the field. Existing diagnostics like the Gelman-Rubin statistic are heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. This paper provides a method that, for simulated data, offers a rigorous, quantitative measure of sample quality (Jeffreys divergence) rather than just a binary "converged/not converged" diagnostic. This is a significant step forward and could become a standard tool for developing and validating inference algorithms and models.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper builds cleverly on the existing BDMC framework [GGA15]. The main theoretical contribution—linking the gap between the forward and reverse log-ML bounds to the Jeffreys divergence (Eqs. 8, 11, 12)—is simple, elegant, and appears technically sound. This reframing of a marginal likelihood estimation tool into an inference quality metric is a novel and valuable insight.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical validation is a major strength of this work.
    *   The authors don't just present the method; they carefully validate the proposed heuristic for real-world data across five different models. The results in Figure 1, showing consistency between real and simulated data behavior and robustness to the quality of the approximate posterior sample, provide strong evidence for the protocol's utility.
    *   The application of BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is an excellent use case. It demonstrates how BREAD can provide concrete, quantitative guidance for difficult modeling and implementation decisions, showing how the optimal choice can depend on the underlying inference engine (Stan vs. WebPPL).
    *   The anecdote about discovering a bug in WebPPL is a powerful demonstration of the method's practical value as a debugging and verification tool for probabilistic programming systems.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, the derivation of the main result is straightforward, and the experimental setup and conclusions are explained precisely.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Scope of Direct Applicability:** The primary limitation, which the authors are honest about, is that the rigorous bound only applies to simulated data. The real-world data protocol is a well-motivated and empirically supported heuristic, but it is a heuristic nonetheless. It relies on the assumption that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters is a good proxy for behavior on the actual data, which may not hold if the model is badly misspecified.

2.  **Applicability to Specific Algorithms:** BREAD is directly applicable to inference algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space, like AIS and SMC. It does not directly evaluate standard MCMC algorithms like HMC/NUTS or Metropolis-Hastings. The authors argue that insights from evaluating AIS (which uses these kernels internally) will generalize. This is a plausible claim, but it remains an indirect evaluation of the MCMC kernels themselves. It would be beneficial to discuss this distinction more explicitly.

3.  **Computational Cost:** A potential barrier to adoption is the computational overhead. BREAD requires running many AIS chains in both forward and reverse directions. This is likely to be significantly more expensive than running the original inference procedure one wishes to diagnose. A discussion of the computational trade-offs would be valuable. For instance, how many BREAD chains are needed to get a stable estimate of the divergence bound, and how does this cost scale relative to the original inference task?

4.  **Variance of the Bound Estimator:** Footnote 3 mentions that the analysis does not justify averaging weights across chains to tighten the bound on the divergence, unlike in standard AIS for log-ML estimation. This implies that the final bound could be sensitive to outlier chains. This is a subtle but important point that could be elaborated on in the main text. What is the variance of the gap estimator `ˆB`, and are there strategies to get a more robust estimate?

**Overall Evaluation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to an important and challenging area. The proposed method, BREAD, is novel, technically sound, and practically useful. The authors provide a clear explanation of the theory and support it with a comprehensive set of experiments that not only validate the approach but also demonstrate its utility in guiding modeling decisions and debugging inference engines. While the method has limitations in its direct applicability, the authors are upfront about them and provide a sensible protocol for real-world use. The work is of high quality and is likely to be influential, providing researchers and practitioners with a much-needed tool for rigorously evaluating probabilistic inference.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.
