Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between approximate samples produced by annealed importance sampling (AIS)/SMC-like algorithms and the true posterior. The key idea is to combine forward AIS (a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) with reverse AIS initialized from an exact posterior sample (a stochastic upper bound on the same quantity), and to show that the expected gap between the reverse and forward log estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal-state distribution q and the target p. The method (BREAD) is rigorously valid on simulated data (where an exact posterior sample is available via ancestral sampling of the joint), and the authors propose a pragmatic protocol to transfer insights to real datasets by (i) fitting hyperparameters on the real data, (ii) simulating data from these fitted hyperparameters, and (iii) validating that AIS behavior matches between simulated and real data.

Beyond the theoretical contribution, the paper implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, validates it on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures), demonstrates a matrix factorization case study comparing collapsed vs uncollapsed representations, and reports uncovering a subtle WebPPL bug via violations of the BDMC inequality. The work builds on Neal’s AIS, thermodynamic integration and Jarzynski’s non-equilibrium work, BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation, and relates to recent diagnostics such as Stein discrepancies and subjective divergence [CTM16]. The connection to [CTM16] is discussed: when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior, both analyses yield an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence; BREAD focuses on this regime and on a practical real-data protocol.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical link: a simple, elegant derivation shows that E[log R̂_rev^{-1} − log R̂_fwd] upper bounds DJ(p‖q) between the AIS terminal distribution and the target. This converts BDMC “sandwiching” into a quantitative sample-quality diagnostic.
- Practical validation and tooling: Integrations with Stan and WebPPL, multiple canonical models, and a detailed matrix factorization case study. The debugging story underscores the value of robust inequalities as implementation tests.
- Sensible real-data protocol: fitting hyperparameters to real data to simulate comparable datasets is a pragmatic way to bridge the requirement for exact posterior samples; empirical checks show close correspondence of AIS behavior between real and simulated data across models.
- Clarity about scope: the paper explains when guarantees hold (simulated data with exact posterior sample) and when they become heuristic (fitted-hyperparameter procedure).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers: The bound characterizes q induced by the AIS terminal state. While the authors argue that design choices are shared with other MCMC, the conclusions do not directly extend to generic MCMC outputs. It would help to more explicitly discuss when AIS is a faithful proxy for a user’s actual inference pipeline and when it is not (e.g., HMC-only workflows).
- Expectation vs instance-level guarantees: The bound holds in expectation over AIS randomness; single-run gaps are not guaranteed to upper bound DJ. In experiments, quartiles of single-chain estimates are reported. Please provide guidance on using multiple independent AIS runs to estimate the expectation and its uncertainty (e.g., number of runs, CIs, handling heavy tails), and report mean gap with robust uncertainty measures. If possible, add concentration results or empirical calibration plots (probability that the instantaneous gap exceeds DJ on toy problems).
- Real-data protocol is heuristic: Initializing reverse AIS from approximate posterior samples over hyperparameters breaks the guarantee. The empirical study varying the number of MCMC steps is reassuring, but additional diagnostics would strengthen the case: e.g., compare reverse estimates when hyperparameters are fixed to MLE vs posterior mean; quantify the similarity between the fitted-hyperparameter posterior and the true posterior on simulated data (e.g., via R̂, ESS, or additional BDMC runs).
- Tightness characterization: Toy examples show good match between the expected gap and DJ, but large-scale tasks (where this matters most) lack quantitative tightness assessments. It would be useful to report estimated DJ upper bounds (with SEs) and sensitivity to AIS schedule length, number/placement of temperatures, and transition kernel quality. Guidance on schedule design to reduce the gap would be valuable.
- Relation to SMC: The framework is applicable to SMC, but the paper focuses on AIS. A brief experiment or implementation note for SMC (e.g., tempering via data subsampling/resampling) would broaden applicability.
- Presentation details: Consider adding explicit pseudocode for BREAD, specifying inputs/outputs, and a short “how to use” guide. Include exact settings used (temperatures, number of intermediate distributions, MCMC steps per temperature, NUTS/HMC tuning) in the main text or a consolidated appendix table for reproducibility.

Quality
- The theory is correct and clearly presented. The main bound follows from standard AIS/BDMC analyses plus a KL contraction under marginalization. Experimental results support the claims, though reporting of expectations and uncertainties could be improved. The authors are honest about guarantees and limitations.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Suggested additions: concise algorithm box, more quantitative reporting (means/SEs, not only quartiles), and a brief practitioner recipe. Ensure all appendices (toy distributions, code, model specs) are accessible.

Originality
- Extends BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding posterior sample quality; to my knowledge, the expected Jeffreys bound from the BDMC gap in this context is new. The real-data protocol and dual implementation are practically novel. Relation to subjective divergence [CTM16] is adequately acknowledged; a short empirical comparison would strengthen the narrative.

Significance
- Offers a rigorous, actionable diagnostic for AIS/SMC-based posterior inference and useful tooling for probabilistic programming. Likely to influence practice in PPLs and MCMC evaluation. The debugging example and representation comparison illustrate real impact.

Questions for authors
- How many AIS runs are needed in practice to estimate the expected gap with acceptable variance? Any rules of thumb?
- Can you provide empirical calibration of the bound on larger models (e.g., via conservative surrogates for DJ or sandwiching with longer runs)?
- Any experience with adaptive temperature schedules to tighten the bound?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with practical relevance.
- Broadly useful tooling in two major PPLs, with diverse experiments.
- Clear communication of scope and limitations; constructive guidance to practitioners.
- Demonstrated debugging and modeling insights enabled by the method.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees are expectation-only and mainly for simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Limited exploration of SMC; AIS-centric empirical evaluation.
- Reporting could be more quantitative regarding expectation estimates and uncertainty.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical and practical contribution to evaluating MCMC-based inference, is well-executed, and will likely be useful to the NIPS community. The suggested clarifications would further strengthen the final version.
