Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper-bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC). The key idea is that the gap between a forward AIS stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1 and a reverse AIS stochastic upper bound (enabled by an exact posterior sample) has expected value that upper-bounds DKL(q||p) + DKL(p||q). The method, termed BREAD, is implemented in WebPPL and Stan. The authors also propose a practical protocol for real-data diagnosis via fitted hyperparameters: infer hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from those hyperparameters, and evaluate inference on simulated data; they empirically validate (i) that forward AIS behavior matches between real and simulated datasets across 5 models, and (ii) that approximate posterior initialization for the reverse chain (by short MCMC runs) yields reverse bounds indistinguishable across a wide range of steps. They demonstrate utility by comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization across WebPPL and Stan and report that BREAD helped uncover a bug in WebPPL.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation is clear and correct: using the extended-state AIS view, the expectations of log forward and reverse estimators lead to inequalities bounding DKL(q||p) and DKL(p||q), and their sum yields an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence. The argument relies on marginalization not increasing KL, and is a clean extension of BDMC’s log-ML sandwiching to posterior sample quality.
- Guarantees: Important caveats are explicit: the bound is in expectation, not per-run; rigorous bounds require an exact posterior sample (hence simulated data); and applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-like algorithms.
- Empirics: Validation is sensible but limited. Tightness of the bound vs. true Jeffreys divergence is shown only on toy distributions where both are exactly computable. The five real-data-inspired studies focus on consistency between real and simulated datasets and on the effect of approximate reverse-chain initialization; results support the proposed protocol. The matrix-factorization case study illustrates actionable insights. More quantitative reporting of bound magnitudes vs. computational budget, schedule choices, and variance across runs would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The background on AIS, stochastic bounds, and BDMC is concise and sufficient for reproduction by an expert. The notation for forward/reverse chains and the conditioning structures is clear. Limitations are acknowledged. Minor nits: some reference formatting issues in the provided text; occasional typos (e.g., “qfwd(pT)” looks like a typo for qfwd(xT)).

Originality
The main conceptual contribution—connecting the bidirectional log-ML gap to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples—is novel and useful. The practical protocol for real-data diagnosis and the integration into two PPLs are valuable. The overlap with concurrent work on “subjective divergence” is appropriately discussed; this paper’s specialization to true-posterior initialization yields interpretable guarantees, while the authors are careful about what is not guaranteed when using an approximate reference.

Significance
This work advances rigorous assessment of Monte Carlo inference in PPLs and Bayesian computation. Even with the simulated-data requirement, BREAD provides:
- A theoretically grounded, quantitative measure of sample quality for AIS/SMC-like samplers.
- A practical workflow to transfer insights to real datasets.
- A debugging tool for PPL implementations.
Its impact is likely among researchers and practitioners building/evaluating samplers and PPLs. The limitation to AIS/SMC and to expected bounds reduces generality relative to arbitrary MCMC, but many design choices overlap, and AIS/SMC are widely used.

Strengths
- Clear, correct theoretical link to Jeffreys divergence with explicit derivations.
- Thoughtful protocol to bridge simulated and real data, with empirical validation across diverse models.
- Implementations in WebPPL and Stan; concrete case study yielding nontrivial guidance (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and real debugging payoff.
- Careful positioning relative to BDMC and concurrent subjective divergence work.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Expected upper bound only: No per-run high-probability control of the divergence; discuss how to construct confidence intervals or use multiple runs to quantify uncertainty around E[ˆB].
- Dependence on exact posterior samples: For real data, guarantees do not hold; the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic. Provide stress tests where the prior is strongly misspecified or hyperparameters are poorly identified, to assess robustness.
- Sensitivity to AIS design: The bound likely depends on annealing schedule, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels. Include ablations and guidance for tuning to achieve informative bounds at a given compute budget.
- Scope: Applicability is limited to samplers that fit the AIS/SMC extended-state framework. Discuss prospects for extending ideas to diagnose standard MCMC chains directly (e.g., via coupling to reference AIS or using control variates/bridging).
- Reporting: Provide more quantitative metrics (e.g., wall-clock vs. bound tightness; variability across runs; numerical values of estimated Jeffreys bounds) and release code/recipes.

Questions for authors
- Can you propose a practical procedure to form empirical confidence intervals for the Jeffreys bound using multiple forward/reverse runs?
- How sensitive are your conclusions to the annealing schedule? Have you tried adaptive or moment-averaged annealing in the real-data-like studies?
- In Stan, what specific transition kernels were used at intermediate temperatures, and how was adaptivity handled to retain reversibility/invariance?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-motivated, and useful contribution that extends BDMC in a meaningful way and demonstrates practical value in PPL settings. Despite limitations (expected bounds, simulated-data requirement), the paper is technically sound and impactful enough for NeurIPS.

Recommendation: accept.
