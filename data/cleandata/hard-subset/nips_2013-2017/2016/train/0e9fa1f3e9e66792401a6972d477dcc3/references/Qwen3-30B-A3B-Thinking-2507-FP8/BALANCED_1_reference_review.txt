

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) that provides rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the BDMC framework from [GGA15] to bound the Jeffreys divergence rather than just the log marginal likelihood, and propose a practical protocol for applying this to real-world data. The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and validated on several probabilistic models.

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation of BREAD is sound, with a clear derivation showing that the BDMC estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation (Section 3.1). The authors provide empirical validation on toy distributions (Appendix B) where both the bound and true divergence can be computed, showing reasonable agreement. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (Section 3.2) is well-motivated, and the validation experiments (Section 5.1) demonstrate consistency between simulated and real data for multiple models. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the real-world data protocol. Specifically, it doesn't explore how model misspecification or poor hyperparameter estimation might affect the validity of the bound, which is a critical concern for real-world applications. The claim that BREAD "provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure" may be overstated given these assumptions.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong background section that properly contextualizes the problem and related work. The mathematical derivations are thorough and accessible to the NIPS audience. The experimental design is clear, though the explanation of the hyperparameter fitting protocol (Section 3.2) could be more precise. The figures (e.g., Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the key findings. One minor weakness is the lack of a concise summary of the algorithm's computational complexity and practical implementation considerations.

**Originality:** The core contribution of extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence rather than the log marginal likelihood is novel and valuable. While the authors acknowledge related work [CTM16], they clearly differentiate their approach (which uses exact posterior samples for the reverse chain) from the "subjective divergence" method. The application of BREAD to compare model representations in probabilistic programming languages is particularly insightful, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL through this method demonstrates practical utility beyond the theoretical contribution. This is a meaningful extension of the BDMC framework that addresses a gap in MCMC reliability measurement.

**Significance:** The problem of measuring MCMC reliability is significant, as current convergence diagnostics are heuristic and cannot confirm convergence. BREAD provides a rigorous, quantitative measure that can guide model design and algorithmic choices. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to practitioners. The insights gained from comparing model representations (Section 5.2.1) are valuable for probabilistic programming users. However, the paper could better articulate how BREAD changes practice compared to existing diagnostics. The connection to debugging probabilistic programming languages (Section 5.2.2) is a strong practical contribution that merits more emphasis.

**Strengths:**
1. Clear theoretical extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence
2. Practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data
3. Integration into two major probabilistic programming systems
4. Demonstrated utility in model selection and debugging
5. Strong validation on multiple models and datasets

**Weaknesses:**
1. Insufficient discussion of limitations in the real-world data protocol
2. Limited comparison with existing convergence diagnostics
3. Overstatement of the method's generality and automatic nature
4. Experiments on model representation are limited to one model type
5. Could better distinguish from concurrent work [CTM16]

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for measuring MCMC reliability. While the real-world application protocol has some limitations that need further exploration, the core theoretical contribution is sound and the practical applications are compelling. The method fills a genuine gap in the ability to quantify the accuracy of posterior approximations, which is crucial for practitioners of probabilistic programming.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand the discussion of limitations in Section 3.2, particularly regarding model misspecification and the sensitivity of the hyperparameter fitting protocol.
2. Include a more detailed comparison with standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Geweke) to better establish the value proposition.
3. Clarify how BREAD differs from [CTM16] in a dedicated subsection, highlighting the advantages of using exact posterior samples for the reverse chain.
4. Provide more general insights about when BREAD is particularly useful (beyond the matrix factorization example) to help readers determine if it's applicable to their work.
5. Consider adding a discussion of computational costs, as running both forward and reverse AIS chains will increase runtime.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, with a clear technical contribution that addresses a significant problem in probabilistic inference. The integration with existing probabilistic programming tools and the demonstration of practical utility through model comparison and debugging make this a valuable contribution to the field.
