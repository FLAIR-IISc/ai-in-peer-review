Summary
The paper builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based inference (specifically AIS/SMC-like importance sampling on extended spaces). The key result is that the gap between forward AIS (a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS (a stochastic upper bound when initialized from an exact posterior sample) upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the final-state distribution qfwd(xT) of AIS and the true target pT. The authors (i) formalize this expected upper bound, (ii) provide exact evaluations on toy distributions indicating the bound can be tight, (iii) propose a practical protocol for “real data” using fitted hyperparameters to simulate datasets and initialize reverse chains with an approximate posterior sample, and (iv) integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan, showing case studies across several models and using it to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization and to uncover a bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
This work extends BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and reverse AIS (Burda et al., 2015) from marginal likelihood bounding to sample quality assessment via KL/Jeffreys divergence. It relates to diagnostics (Gelman-Rubin; Cowles & Carlin), Stein-based sample quality (Gorham & Mackey), and to independent contemporaneous “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka). The core derivations leverage standard AIS extended-space analyses (Neal 2001) and KL inequalities.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations that E[log R̂rev−1 − log R̂] ≥ DJ(qfwd, pT) appear correct given the AIS extended-space formulation and KL contraction by marginalization. However, guarantees are in expectation; no concentration or high-probability control on the divergence bound is provided. The practical diagnostic value thus depends on variance, which can be large.
- Methodological soundness: The guarantees require an exact posterior sample to run reverse AIS. The proposed real-data protocol substitutes an approximate sample (obtained by limited MCMC steps from fitted hyperparameters) and thus forfeits rigorous bounds. The paper acknowledges this but leans on empirical similarity of AIS estimate distributions between real and simulated data. This is heuristic and potentially circular under model misspecification.
- Experimental support: The toy examples that compare true Jeffreys divergence and the bound are informative but limited. The real-data demonstrations (five Stan models, 100 AIS chains each) primarily compare distributions of AIS log-ML estimates rather than directly validating that the bound tracks posterior error. Sensitivity analyses (temperature schedules, number/type of transitions, dimension) are limited. The matrix factorization case study is useful but small-scale.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear background and derivations. The notation is standard for AIS. Some implementation details affecting validity (e.g., how Stan’s NUTS adaptation is handled across temperatures; whether kernels are strictly reversible and leave pt invariant; how p1 sampling is ensured when only a single sample is available) should be made explicit. The reliance on quartiles rather than means for reporting is understandable, but the theory is about expectations; the implications for practice should be discussed.

Originality
The main novelty is repurposing BDMC to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence of the AIS final-state distribution to the target, and proposing a protocol for pragmatic use on real data. Given independent closely related work (subjective divergence) and that BDMC already delivered bidirectional bounds on log-partition, the conceptual step, while useful, is incremental. The equivalence/relationship to subjective divergence when using true posterior initializations warrants a clearer, sharper positioning.

Significance
Reliable, quantitative diagnostics for approximate posterior sampling are important, particularly for probabilistic programming. Bounding DJ is appealing and could guide model/algorithm choices. However, the strongest guarantees apply only on simulated data with exact posterior samples; the proposed real-data protocol is heuristic. Without tighter variance control, broader empirical validation, or guidance that generalizes beyond AIS/SMC to mainstream MCMC, the immediate impact seems limited. The debugging example is a compelling byproduct.

Strengths
- Clear, principled derivation connecting BDMC gaps to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan; demonstrates utility in model representation choices and implementation debugging.
- Sensible protocol to emulate real-data conditions via fitted hyperparameters; empirical evidence of similar AIS behavior across real vs simulated datasets.
- Addresses a real need for quantitative diagnostics in probabilistic programming.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees only in expectation; no high-probability bounds or variance/concentration analysis of the divergence estimator.
- Heuristic real-data protocol breaks the rigorous guarantees; empirical validation is limited and could be circular under misspecification.
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers; claims of broader insights for MCMC are speculative.
- Limited experimental scale; lack of systematic sensitivity studies (temperature schedule, number of intermediate distributions, kernel choice, dimensionality).
- Unclear handling of reversible, invariant kernels in Stan (NUTS adaptation across temperatures may violate assumptions).
- Reporting focuses on quartiles of log-estimates; average-of-gaps vs gap-of-averages and the effect of multiple chains on the expectation bound are not clarified.

Questions for the authors
- How are HMC/NUTS kernels configured at intermediate temperatures to ensure reversibility and invariance to pt? Is adaptation disabled during AIS passes?
- Can you provide variance estimates and confidence intervals for the gap estimator, and any concentration results (even empirical) to support practical decision-making?
- How sensitive are the bounds to temperature schedules and number of intermediate distributions? Any guidance for practitioners?
- Can you compare BREAD’s bound to traditional diagnostics (R̂, ESS) and to ground-truth posterior error on problems where exact sampling is possible?
- For SMC, what specific proposal/resampling schemes were used, and do the same guarantees hold?
- When “having access to a sample from p1, but not a sampler,” what are the implications for unbiasedness and the stated bounds?

Pros
- Sound theoretical extension within BDMC/AIS framework.
- Practical integration and case studies; useful debugging demonstration.
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.

Cons
- Rigorous guarantees limited to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- No high-probability guarantees; limited exploration of estimator variance and sensitivity.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing BDMC and concurrent subjective divergence work.
- Limited scalability/dimensionality evidence.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the idea is principled and potentially useful, the paper’s strongest guarantees do not translate to real data, and the empirical validation is too limited to justify the practical claims. Strengthening the experimental section, clarifying kernel assumptions, providing variance/concentration analysis, and positioning more clearly against subjective divergence would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: High
