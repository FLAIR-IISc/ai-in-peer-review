Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference when the algorithm can be cast as importance sampling on an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC). The central theoretical result is that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log-evidence estimates provides, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the exact posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by the sampler. The method, termed BREAD, enables: (i) rigorous bounds on simulated data (where an exact posterior sample is available via ancestral sampling), and (ii) a practical protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters to simulate representative datasets, together with an approximate reverse initialization. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate behavior across five models, use it to compare model representations (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization), and show a debugging use case.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015) and AIS (Neal 2001), leveraging the reverse-AIS construction that yields stochastic upper bounds on log partition ratios when starting from an exact posterior sample. It clarifies how the expected forward–reverse log-evidence gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence of the marginal distribution of the final AIS state. It contrasts with Stein discrepancy approaches (Gorham & Mackey 2015), which provide sample-quality certificates under strong assumptions, and with subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016), which replaces the exact posterior start with a reference distribution. The paper positions itself as providing rigorous guarantees (on simulated data) without distributional assumptions and practical heuristics for real data.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations are sound and carefully trace back to BDMC. The key steps—expressing forward and reverse path distributions, using marginalization to relate KLs, and showing E[log R̂−log R̂rev] ≥ DJ(q, p)—are correct. It is clearly stated that this is an expectation bound, not a per-instance high-probability bound.
- Scope and assumptions: The requirement of an exact posterior sample restricts rigorous guarantees to simulated data; the proposed real-data protocol (fit hyperparameters, then simulate and initialize reverse via a short MCMC run) is plausible but heuristic. The paper is honest about this limitation and provides empirical validation.
- Empirics: Integration into two PPLs and experiments across five models are valuable. The calibration via toy distributions where J and the bound are computed exactly is appropriate (though details are in the appendix). The matrix factorization case study usefully demonstrates how BREAD informs modeling choices. The debugging example underscores practical utility.
- Weaknesses in evaluation:
  - The bound holds in expectation; in finite runs, ˆB can under- or overestimate J. The paper does not quantify variability of ˆB or provide confidence intervals for E[ˆB]. This matters for practitioners deciding when an inference configuration is “good enough.”
  - Tightness is examined on toy problems but not systematically across dimensionality, annealing schedules, or kernel choices. Since schedule and kernels affect q(xT), sensitivity analyses would strengthen the case.
  - The method applies to AIS/SMC-like samplers; claims about informing MCMC design more broadly are heuristic.
  - For real data, using approximate reverse initialization removes the guarantee. The empirical evidence is encouraging (reverse curves insensitive to number of hyperparameter MCMC steps), but a more formal robustness analysis would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The background on AIS/BDMC is clear; Section 3.1 concisely presents the main derivation. The real-data protocol is explained transparently. Suggestions:
- Provide a pseudo-code box for BREAD to make implementation steps explicit.
- Clarify in the main text that the practitioner typically observes a single ˆB and that guarantees are on its expectation; recommend practical repetition and reporting of mean/SE.
- Move some key appendix results (toy tightness study) into the main paper with at least one figure.

Originality
The idea to interpret the BDMC forward–reverse log-evidence gap as an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence of the sampler’s output distribution is a neat and nontrivial extension of BDMC’s original use for marginal likelihood benchmarking. Integrating the technique into PPLs and proposing a real-data protocol are useful contributions. The overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” work is fairly acknowledged; the distinctiveness here is the clean bound when using the true posterior start and the practical PPL integration and validation.

Significance
Reliable, quantitative assessment of MCMC-based inference is important, particularly for probabilistic programming. Even with its limitations, BREAD is a concrete, implementable tool that can:
- Certify inference accuracy on simulated data with rigorous guarantees,
- Provide informative diagnostics and model/algorithm selection guidance,
- Help uncover implementation bugs.
Given the widespread use of AIS/SMC bridges in practice, this is likely to be adopted and built upon.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution with correct, useful bound.
- Practical impact demonstrated via integration into Stan/WebPPL, multi-model validation, case study, and debugging.
- Timely and relevant to PPL and MCMC communities.
- Honest discussion of limitations and relation to concurrent work.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees are expectation-only and limited to simulated data; real-data protocol lacks theory.
- Limited exploration of tightness and sensitivity across higher dimensions, schedules, and kernels.
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Report variability of ˆB across runs and provide recommendations for computing stable estimates of E[ˆB] (e.g., number of replicates, robust averaging).
- Explore sensitivity of the bound and actual J to annealing schedules, number of temperatures, and MCMC kernel strength.
- Provide at least one higher-dimensional example illustrating bound behavior and computational cost.
- Discuss how to leverage existing bounds/estimates of logZ (e.g., from TI or VB) to bound one-sided KLs using one direction of AIS in practice.
- Clarify whether combining AIS runs by averaging weights can be reconciled with the bound (currently the analysis discourages averaging for the purpose of ˆB).

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed contribution that advances the state of practice in evaluating MCMC-based inference. Despite limitations, the method is useful and the paper meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I lean toward acceptance.
