This paper presents a novel and compelling extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based inference, specifically focusing on algorithms amenable to importance sampling interpretations like AIS and SMC. The core idea – leveraging BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence – is insightful and potentially impactful. While the immediate applicability is limited to a specific class of algorithms, the authors correctly argue that the insights gained are broadly relevant to MCMC design. The practical integration with WebPPL and Stan, along with the protocol for real-world data analysis, further strengthens the contribution.

The paper’s strengths lie in its originality and boldness. The connection between BDMC (previously used for marginal likelihood estimation) and quantifying posterior accuracy is non-trivial and demonstrates a creative application of existing techniques. The authors identify a critical gap in reliable quantitative measures for MCMC validation and propose a method that, for simulated data, provides rigorous bounds. The empirical validation, including the identification of a bug in WebPPL, showcases the practical value of their approach. The presentation is largely clear, although the mathematical details in Section 3 could benefit from further elaboration for a broader audience.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. The requirement for exact posterior samples limits immediate applicability. The proposed protocol for real-world data, while reasonable, introduces an approximation that, while seemingly validated in the experiments, weakens the theoretical guarantees. The experiments, though illustrative, could be more extensive and explore more diverse models and datasets. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more thorough discussion of scenarios where BREAD might *fail* to provide useful information would be beneficial. The connection between the Jeffreys divergence and practical utility (e.g., impact on downstream tasks) isn't explicitly addressed. Finally, the presentation of the experimental results relies heavily on quartiles due to outliers, which obscures some details of the performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to measure posterior accuracy is a significant and original contribution.
*   **Potential Impact:**  Provides a rigorous, albeit limited, method for evaluating MCMC algorithms and potentially guiding their design.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Integration with popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) and a protocol for real-world data increase usability.
*   **Bug Discovery:**  The unexpected discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the value of the proposed methodology as a testing tool.
*   **Bold Approach:** Addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference – quantifying the reliability of results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Applicability:** Dependence on importance sampling frameworks and exact posterior samples restricts immediate use.
*   **Approximation on Real Data:** The protocol for real-world data introduces an approximation that weakens theoretical guarantees.
*   **Experimental Scope:** Experiments, while validating, could be more comprehensive.
*   **Theoretical Depth:**  Some mathematical details could be clarified for wider accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty and boldness of the approach, coupled with the potential impact on the field, outweigh the current weaknesses. The paper introduces a genuinely new direction for MCMC validation and offers a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. While methodological perfection isn't present, the conceptual leap is significant enough to warrant inclusion in NIPS. I believe this work will stimulate further research in this important area. The authors have identified a critical problem and proposed a clever, if not fully general, solution. Further refinement and extension of this work are likely to yield even more impactful results.
