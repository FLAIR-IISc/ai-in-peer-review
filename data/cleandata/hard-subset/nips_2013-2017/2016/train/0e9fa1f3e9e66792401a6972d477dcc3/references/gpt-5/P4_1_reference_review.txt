Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence (sum of forward and reverse KL) between the target posterior p and the distribution q of approximate samples produced by algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC). Building on AIS theory [Neal 2001] and the BDMC framework for sandwiching log marginal likelihoods [Grosse et al. 2015], the authors show that the gap between reverse- and forward-direction AIS log-ratio estimates yields an estimator whose expectation upper-bounds DJ(q||p). They term the overall procedure BREAD.

The paper revisits the known property that log of a nonnegative unbiased estimator is a stochastic lower bound in expectation on the corresponding log quantity, and leverages reverse AIS (initialized with an exact posterior sample) to obtain a stochastic upper bound on log ZT/Z1. Combining the two gives a sandwich for the log marginal likelihood on simulated data and, critically here, an expectation upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal-state distribution and the true target.

Beyond this analysis, the paper proposes a protocol to assess inference quality on real data by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating data from the fitted hyperparameters, and applying BREAD on the simulated data. It integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and reports experimental validations on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures). The method is also used to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in WebPPL and Stan and to detect an implementation bug, illustrating utility for both model/algorithm design and software testing.

The work is closely related to concurrent “subjective divergence” estimators [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka 2016], which also build on BDMC; the present paper emphasizes expectation bounds on Jeffreys divergence and a diagnostics protocol within two probabilistic programming systems.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations for E[log R̂] and E[log R̂rev] are correct and standard in AIS/BDMC analysis. Using data processing inequality for KL to lower-bound biases and summing them to obtain an expectation upper bound on DJ(p||q) is sound. The guarantees are rigorous on simulated data when an exact posterior sample initializes the reverse chain. On real data, the fitted-hyperparameter protocol lacks formal guarantees; the reverse chain is initialized from an approximate posterior sample over hyperparameters, so the bound is no longer certified. The experiments partially validate that this approximation has minimal effect in the tested settings.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments demonstrate (i) that simulated-data diagnostics track real-data behavior across five models, (ii) apparent insensitivity of reverse bounds to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the hyperparameter posterior, and (iii) actionable guidance for representation choices in matrix factorization on two platforms. However, tightness of the Jeffreys-divergence upper bound is only studied on toy problems; no systematic assessment of variance, bias, or sample complexity is provided for higher-dimensional settings. Reporting quartiles rather than means for AIS log weights is reasonable, but confidence intervals or repeated-run statistics for the derived bounds would strengthen conclusions.
- Limitations: Applicability is limited to algorithms that fit the AIS/SMC structure and to settings where exact posterior samples are available (simulated data). The diagnostic for real data is heuristic. The bound is on the expectation of the estimator’s gap, not a high-probability bound on DJ; a single realization can understate DJ.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a concise recap of AIS/BDMC and a focused presentation of the new divergence bound. The notation is consistent, and the derivations are easy to follow. The experimental section is accessible, with sensible model choices. A few clarifications would improve readability:
- Provide explicit guidance on annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels used, as these choices affect both accuracy and variance.
- Quantify computational overhead (time per chain, number of chains) to contextualize practicality.
- State more explicitly that the guarantee is an expectation bound and discuss implications for using single-run diagnostics.

Originality
The main theoretical contribution is a modest but useful refinement of BDMC: connecting the forward–reverse AIS bias decomposition to an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between AIS’s terminal distribution and the target. While the building blocks are known, articulating this diagnostic perspective and implementing it across two systems is novel and practically valuable. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data is heuristic rather than theoretically new but addresses an important use case. The relation to [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka 2016] is acknowledged; clearer positioning of differences (assumptions, guarantees, and practical behavior when the reverse chain is not initialized from the exact posterior) would further delineate contributions.

Significance
Reliable, quantitative diagnostics for MCMC-based inference remain scarce. A method that (i) provides rigorous simulation-based guarantees, (ii) offers a practical proxy for real-data assessment, and (iii) integrates into widely used tools has potential impact for both researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated uses—model representation decisions and software debugging—underscore practical significance. The main caveat is scope: applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers and simulated-data settings with exact posterior sampling. Nonetheless, the approach can influence the design and evaluation of inference algorithms within probabilistic programming.

Pros
- Clear, correct derivation linking BDMC to an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC.
- Practical protocol to approximate real-data diagnostics and empirical validation across multiple models.
- Integration into WebPPL and Stan; actionable insights on collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations.
- Demonstrated utility for software validation (bug discovery).
- Careful discussion of related work and limitations.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees do not extend to the real-data protocol; reliance on approximate hyperparameter posterior samples invalidates the bound in principle.
- Limited analysis of bound tightness, variance, and sample complexity in high dimensions; no confidence intervals for the divergence estimates.
- Applicability restricted to importance-sampling-over-extended-state samplers; does not directly cover general MCMC without AIS/SMC scaffolding.
- The guarantee is in expectation; single-run diagnostics may be misleading without repeated runs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical calibration of bound tightness and variability on higher-dimensional, challenging posteriors (e.g., multimodal, ill-conditioned), with repeated runs and uncertainty quantification.
- Analyze theoretically or empirically the bias introduced by the fitted-hyperparameter initialization; specify conditions under which the heuristic remains accurate.
- Offer practical guidelines for choosing annealing schedules, number of temperatures, and number of chains to achieve reliable diagnostics at fixed compute.
- Clarify differences and trade-offs relative to the subjective divergence approach when the reverse initialization is approximate.

Overall, this is a solid and useful contribution that refines BDMC into a practical diagnostic for AIS/SMC-based posterior inference and demonstrates meaningful applications in probabilistic programming. The main weaknesses concern generality and formal guarantees on real data, which could be addressed in future work.
