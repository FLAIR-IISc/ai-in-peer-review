

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method that extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that the gap between forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. They further propose a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating datasets from hyperparameters estimated on real data. The approach is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and validated on multiple probabilistic models, including matrix factorization where it reveals differences in optimal model representations between the two frameworks.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a clear theoretical derivation (Section 3.1) showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. This extends the BDMC framework from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior quality assessment, addressing a critical need in probabilistic inference.

2. **Practical Validation**: The authors thoroughly validate their method on both toy examples (Appendix B) and realistic models (Section 5). Their protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (Section 3.2) is well-motivated and empirically validated across five different models, showing consistent behavior between real and simulated data.

3. **Real-World Utility**: The paper demonstrates the method's practical value through two concrete applications: (a) providing insights into model representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) for matrix factorization, and (b) uncovering a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampling implementation. This shows BREAD's utility as both an analytical tool and a debugging mechanism.

4. **Clear Connection to Probabilistic Programming**: The integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to widely used inference frameworks, addressing a key challenge in probabilistic programming: how to assess the reliability of black-box inference.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Limited Applicability**: The method is restricted to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC), excluding many common MCMC methods like standard Metropolis-Hastings. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently explore how generalizable their insights are to broader MCMC practices. The claim that "these algorithms involve many of the same design choices as traditional MCMC methods" feels somewhat tenuous.

2. **Approximation in Real-World Protocol**: The real-world protocol (Section 3.2) relies on using approximate posterior samples to initialize the reverse chain, which means the theoretical guarantees don't strictly hold for real data. While the authors validate that this approximation has little practical impact (Section 5.1.2), a more rigorous analysis of the error introduced by this approximation would strengthen the paper.

3. **Computational Cost**: Running both forward and reverse AIS chains is computationally expensive. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the scalability of the method for large models or datasets, which is a significant practical limitation for real-world applications.

4. **Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper references Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), it doesn't fully explore how BREAD relates to their "subjective divergence" method. The paper states that their approach is equivalent "when the true posterior is used," but doesn't clarify the practical differences when using approximate posteriors (as in the real-world protocol).

5. **Lack of Comparison to Other Quality Metrics**: The paper doesn't compare BREAD's performance to other posterior quality metrics (e.g., effective sample size, Geweke diagnostics) on the same models. This would help contextualize the method's utility relative to existing practices.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference by providing a rigorous method for assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical applications (including debugging) demonstrate clear utility.

While the method's applicability is limited to specific inference algorithms, this limitation is acknowledged and the authors provide a compelling argument for why their approach still yields valuable insights for broader MCMC practice. The real-world protocol, while relying on an approximation, is well-validated and represents a practical path forward for practitioners.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound, clearly written, makes a novel contribution to the field, and has significant potential impact for probabilistic programming systems. The limitations are acknowledged and addressed where possible.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances our ability to rigorously evaluate probabilistic inference methods, which is a critical need in the field, and provides a practical tool that can be immediately adopted by practitioners using probabilistic programming languages.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3.1, clarify the relationship between BREAD and Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) more explicitly, especially regarding the use of approximate versus exact posteriors.

2. Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational cost of BREAD relative to standard MCMC diagnostics, perhaps with a small scalability experiment.

3. In the conclusion, explicitly state the limitations of the method (applicability to specific inference algorithms, computational cost) to help readers understand when it's most appropriate to apply.

4. In Section 5.2.1, provide more context on why the tradeoff between collapsed and uncollapsed representations differs between WebPPL and Stan, which would deepen the scientific insights.

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
