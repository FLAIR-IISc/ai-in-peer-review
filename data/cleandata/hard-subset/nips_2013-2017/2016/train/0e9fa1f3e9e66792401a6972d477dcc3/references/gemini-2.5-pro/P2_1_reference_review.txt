### Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively assessing the quality of samples from certain MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution and the true posterior. Since BDMC's reverse pass requires an exact sample from the posterior, the method's formal guarantees apply only to simulated data. To address this, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world data: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these parameters; and finally, run BREAD on this simulated data, validating that inference behavior is consistent between the real and simulated datasets. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference on several models and to compare the performance of different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) for matrix factorization.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Assessing the convergence and sample quality of MCMC is a notoriously difficult and crucial problem in probabilistic modeling. Existing diagnostics are largely heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. This paper offers a step towards a more rigorous, quantitative measure of sample quality.
2.  **Elegant Theoretical Result:** The connection drawn between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence is novel and insightful. Deriving an expected upper bound on a formal divergence measure from the machinery of AIS is a significant theoretical contribution that cleanly extends the work of Grosse et al. (2015).
3.  **Strong Practical Utility and Demonstration:** The paper does an excellent job of demonstrating the utility of BREAD.
    *   The implementation in both Stan and WebPPL shows its applicability to real-world systems.
    *   The case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed matrix factorization provides concrete, actionable insights into model design choices that are often guided by intuition alone.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's usefulness as a rigorous diagnostic and debugging tool for developers of inference systems.
4.  **Thoughtful Experimental Design:** The authors carefully validate the key assumptions of their proposed real-data protocol. They explicitly test for consistency between inference on real and simulated data (Sec 5.1.1) and show that the results are robust to the number of MCMC steps used to generate the approximate posterior sample for the reverse chain (Sec 5.1.2). This diligence strengthens the credibility of the proposed heuristic.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Applicability:** The method is only directly applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). It cannot be used to directly diagnose the convergence of a standard MCMC chain (e.g., from Gibbs sampling or HMC/NUTS) without wrapping it inside an AIS/SMC framework. While the authors argue this still provides useful insights, it is a major limitation for the broader MCMC community.
2.  **Heuristic Nature on Real Data:** The proposed protocol for real-world data is pragmatic but breaks the formal guarantees. The reverse chain is initialized from an *approximate* posterior sample derived from an initial inference run. If this initial run fails to find the true posterior modes, the entire BREAD analysis could be misleading, providing a tight bound on the divergence to an incorrect posterior. While the experiments suggest this works well for the models tested, its reliability in more complex, multi-modal scenarios is an open question.
3.  **High Computational Cost:** Running BDMC is computationally expensive, requiring numerous forward and reverse AIS runs. This likely makes BREAD impractical for routine convergence checking and relegates it to a tool for in-depth model development, algorithm comparison, or system debugging, rather than a diagnostic for end-users.
4.  **Clarity on Averaging:** Footnote 3 mentions that the analysis does not justify averaging the weights from multiple AIS chains to get a single estimate of the bound gap, which is a common variance-reduction technique for the log-ML itself. This is a subtle but critical point. The bound holds for the *expectation* of the gap from individual chains. Without averaging, the estimate of the bound `B_hat` can be very high-variance, which may limit its practical tightness. This point could be elaborated on in the main text.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation in Section 3.1 that links the BDMC bounds to the Jeffreys divergence is correct and well-presented. The experimental work is thorough and directly addresses the most likely criticisms of the proposed real-data protocol. The work represents a complete contribution, moving from theory to practical implementation and demonstration.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is explained concisely and effectively. The core theoretical contribution is derived step-by-step. The real-data protocol is well-motivated and its steps are laid out unambiguously. The figures are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the key idea of re-purposing the bound gap to measure sample quality via Jeffreys divergence is novel. The authors are also careful to position their work relative to concurrent, related research [CTM16], clearly explaining the relationship between the methods. The application as a debugging and model-comparison tool in modern probabilistic programming languages is also a new and valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is high, particularly for researchers developing new inference algorithms and probabilistic programming systems. It provides a much-needed, principled tool for evaluating and debugging complex inference machinery. While the high computational cost and limited applicability to AIS/SMC may prevent its widespread adoption by all practitioners, it sets a new standard for rigorous evaluation in the field. The ability to quantitatively trade off computational cost per step against convergence speed (as shown in the matrix factorization experiment) is extremely valuable for making informed modeling decisions. This work is very likely to be built upon by others seeking to develop more general and efficient methods for MCMC validation.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a novel theoretical contribution and demonstrates its practical value with compelling experiments. The weaknesses, while notable, are clearly acknowledged or are inherent limitations of the underlying BDMC framework. The work represents a clear advance in the difficult area of MCMC diagnostics.
