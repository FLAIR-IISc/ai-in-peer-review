

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, which is a significant departure from the common practice of using heuristic convergence diagnostics. The key insight is that by running AIS in both forward and reverse directions, one can obtain stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood that sandwich the true value, and these bounds can be used to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) showing that BDMC yields an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) presenting a technique for exactly computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples; (3) proposing a protocol for evaluating inference accuracy on real-world datasets; and (4) implementing BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility in model representation decisions and debugging.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While previous work has focused on convergence diagnostics or marginal likelihood estimation, this paper establishes a rigorous framework for measuring the accuracy of approximate posterior samples themselves. The connection between BDMC and the Jeffreys divergence is both elegant and significant. The authors demonstrate that their method can be used to compare different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) in probabilistic programming languages, providing concrete insights that can guide modeling choices. Most impressively, they used BREAD to discover a bug in WebPPL's Gaussian sampling implementation, demonstrating the practical utility of their method.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with careful derivations showing how the reverse AIS chain provides a stochastic upper bound on the log marginal likelihood, which in turn upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. The empirical validation on toy distributions and real-world models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.) is convincing, showing that the upper bound is often a good match to the true divergence. The protocol for using BREAD with real-world data—fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data from those hyperparameters, and validating consistency between real and simulated data—addresses a critical practical challenge.

While the paper acknowledges that BREAD is limited to algorithms like AIS or SMC (rather than general MCMC), this limitation is not overly restrictive given that these algorithms underlie many modern inference systems. The authors also thoughtfully compare their work to related methods, including the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) that also builds on BDMC.

The paper's main limitations are its computational cost (requiring both forward and reverse AIS chains) and the fact that the real-world data protocol relies on simulated data from fitted hyperparameters, which may not perfectly match the true posterior. However, the authors provide convincing evidence that these limitations are manageable in practice.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in the evaluation of MCMC inference. The authors have taken a bold step beyond the usual heuristic diagnostics to provide a rigorous, quantitative measure of inference reliability. The practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan, along with the debugging application, demonstrates the method's real-world value. While the method is not universally applicable to all MCMC algorithms, it provides a principled framework for evaluating a broad class of inference methods that are widely used in practice.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for the importance of reliable inference evaluation. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach and provide strong evidence for its effectiveness. The integration of BREAD into probabilistic programming systems is particularly noteworthy, as it addresses a critical gap in making probabilistic programming accessible to non-experts.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel, rigorous method for measuring MCMC inference reliability. The conceptual novelty, theoretical grounding, and practical implementation make this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The work is not merely an incremental improvement but establishes a new direction for evaluating probabilistic inference algorithms, which aligns perfectly with NIPS's emphasis on bold, novel approaches.

**Recommendation: Accept**
