This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution, leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The core idea is insightful: extending the use of BDMC, previously applied to marginal likelihood estimation, to measure the quality of approximate inference algorithms like AIS and SMC. The authors correctly identify the difficulty in rigorously validating MCMC and offer a potentially valuable tool, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming languages where users may lack the expertise to assess inference reliability.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with clear motivation and a concise review of relevant background material (AIS, stochastic lower bounds, BDMC, and WebPPL/Stan). The technical contributions are clearly outlined: bounding Jeffreys divergence with BDMC, demonstrating accuracy on toy distributions, a protocol for real-world data application, and implementation within WebPPL and Stan. The experimental validation, including the bug detection in WebPPL, adds credibility to the approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC to divergence bounding is a clever and potentially impactful application. It provides a more informative metric than traditional convergence diagnostics, offering a bound on the *distance* between the approximate and true posteriors.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound and the analysis of its accuracy are mathematically sound and clearly presented.
* **Practical Implementation & Validation:** Implementing BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) demonstrates its practical utility. The validation on a variety of models and the demonstration of its ability to detect bugs are strong points.
* **Addressing Real-World Challenges:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation is a pragmatic attempt to overcome the requirement for exact posterior samples.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with sufficient detail to understand the key ideas and technical contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Applicability Limited to Specific Algorithms:** The method's direct applicability is limited to algorithms amenable to importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors acknowledge the broader implications for MCMC design, the direct impact on evaluating general MCMC algorithms is unclear. This is a significant limitation.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and therefore BREAD, is computationally expensive. It requires running both forward and reverse AIS chains, and potentially multiple simulations for hyperparameter estimation. The practical feasibility for complex models or high-dimensional data is questionable.
* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter Estimation:** The real-world data protocol relies on accurate hyperparameter estimation. Poor hyperparameter estimates could lead to simulated data that does not accurately reflect the real data, undermining the validity of the results. Although the authors attempt to address this, it remains a potential concern.
* **Limited Exploration of Alternative Reference Distributions:** The work largely focuses on using the true posterior as the reference distribution for the reverse chain. While acknowledging Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka’s related work using arbitrary reference distributions, there's no comparative analysis of the impact of different choices, potentially leaving room for improvement.
* **Quartile Visualizations:** While justified for handling outliers, the use of quartiles makes detailed comparison across experiments more difficult. Reporting additional summary statistics (e.g., medians) would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* BREAD offers a novel and theoretically sound method for quantifying the reliability of approximate inference algorithms.
* The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility and facilitates wider adoption.
* The bug detection in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a valuable debugging tool.
* The paper addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic programming: providing users with confidence in the accuracy of inference results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited applicability to a specific class of algorithms restricts the broader impact.
* The computational cost may be prohibitive for many real-world applications.
* The reliance on accurate hyperparameter estimation introduces potential for bias.
* The paper doesn’t sufficiently explore alternative approaches to the reference distribution choice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The BREAD method offers a rigorous approach to evaluating the accuracy of approximate inference, particularly within the context of algorithms like AIS and SMC. While the limited applicability and computational cost are concerns, the theoretical soundness, practical implementation, and demonstration of its utility in debugging a probabilistic programming language make it a worthwhile contribution. 

**Recommendation:**  Accept with minor revisions.

Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1. **Discuss computational cost more thoroughly:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of BREAD and suggest potential optimizations or approximations to reduce the cost.
2. **Elaborate on sensitivity to hyperparameter estimation:**  Explore the impact of inaccurate hyperparameter estimates on the BREAD results through simulations, or by providing guidance on best practices for hyperparameter selection.
3. **Briefly discuss alternative reference distributions:**  A brief discussion of the trade-offs involved in choosing different reference distributions for the reverse chain would strengthen the paper.
4. **Supplement quartile plots:** Add median values to the quartile plots to facilitate comparison.
