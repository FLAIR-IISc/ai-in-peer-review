## NIPS 2016 Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, initially proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods, to evaluate the accuracy of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based inference, specifically algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS and SMC). The authors demonstrate that BDMC can be used to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution obtained from these algorithms and the true posterior. They provide a technique to accurately compute both the Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on toy examples, demonstrating the tightness of the bound. Crucially, they address the challenge of applying this to real-world data by proposing a protocol involving hyperparameter inference, data simulation, and consistency checks. Finally, they implement this technique (dubbed BREAD) in WebPPL and Stan and use it to inform modeling and algorithmic decisions, highlighting its practical utility.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015) for log-marginal likelihood estimation. It acknowledges related work on MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996), automatic MCMC parameter tuning (Roberts & Rosenthal, 2001), and sample quality assessment (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). They correctly position their work relative to Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), another BDMC-based approach, clarifying the differences in the reference distribution used for reverse annealing. The paper also connects AIS to SMC, highlighting the broader applicability of their method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension of BDMC:** The application of BDMC to directly assess the quality of approximate posterior samples, rather than just marginal likelihoods, is a significant and novel contribution.
* **Rigorous Guarantee (for Simulated Data):** Providing an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence offers a rigorous, quantifiable measure of inference accuracy, unlike many heuristic diagnostics.
* **Practical Protocol for Real Data:** The proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is a clever and necessary addition, addressing the inherent limitation of requiring exact posterior samples.
* **Implementation and Validation:**  Implementing BREAD in two prominent probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) and demonstrating its use in practical scenarios lends credibility and practical value. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL through BREAD highlights its potential as a debugging tool.
* **Clear Explanation and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the underlying concepts and mathematical derivations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Applicability Limited to Specific Algorithms:** The method is currently limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors acknowledge the broader implications for MCMC design, the direct applicability is restricted.
* **Computational Cost:**  Running BDMC, especially for complex models, is computationally expensive, requiring significant sampling effort. This practical limitation isn't fully addressed.
* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter Fitting:**  The real-world data protocol relies on accurate hyperparameter estimation. While the authors attempt to validate this, a poor initial hyperparameter estimate could lead to misleading results. The robustness of the protocol to misspecification of the prior is not explored in detail.
* **Assumptions in Real Data Protocol:** The assumption that the fitted hyperparameters are representative of the true underlying data-generating process is a strong one. This is mitigated through simulation, but not fully addressed.
* **Jeffreys Divergence Focus:** While the Jeffreys divergence is a reasonable choice, the paper doesn't discuss the implications of focusing on this specific metric, or potential alternative divergence measures.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically grounded method for evaluating the quality of approximate inference.
* It addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic inference: reliably assessing the accuracy of MCMC algorithms.
* The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the practical feasibility and utility of the approach.
* The results suggest that BREAD can provide valuable insights into model representation choices and potentially identify bugs in inference software.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited applicability to certain algorithms restricts the broad impact of the method.
* The computational cost of BREAD might make it impractical for large-scale models.
* The real-world data protocol relies on assumptions that may not always hold.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the method has limitations in terms of applicability and computational cost, the novelty, rigor, and practical demonstration of BREAD make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. The authors clearly identify the limitations and provide a well-defined protocol for addressing the challenges of applying their method to real-world data.  I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Revision:**

*  Discuss the computational cost of BREAD more explicitly and potentially explore techniques for reducing it.
*  Elaborate on the sensitivity of the real-world data protocol to hyperparameter estimation and perhaps suggest alternative methods for obtaining more robust estimates.
*  Briefly discuss other potential divergence measures beyond the Jeffreys divergence and their suitability for different scenarios.
*  Add a brief discussion on the limitations of the method when dealing with highly complex or non-identifiable models.
