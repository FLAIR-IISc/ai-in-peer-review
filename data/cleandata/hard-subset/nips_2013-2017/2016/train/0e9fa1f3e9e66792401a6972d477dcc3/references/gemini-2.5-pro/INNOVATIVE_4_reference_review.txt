### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The work extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which was originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key insight is that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained from forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains, provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior distribution. While the method's theoretical guarantees require an exact posterior sample (and thus are limited to simulated data), the authors propose a practical protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters. The method is implemented and validated within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and is used to provide concrete guidance on model representation choices and even to uncover a bug in an existing PPL implementation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper's primary strength is its novelty. It tackles the long-standing and critical problem of MCMC convergence diagnostics with a fundamentally new approach. Moving beyond heuristic checks (like R-hat) to a quantitative, theoretically-grounded bound on a formal divergence measure is a significant conceptual leap. The idea of repurposing the BDMC framework to measure sample quality, rather than just the marginal likelihood, is both clever and insightful.

2.  **Elegant Theoretical Result:** The derivation showing that the expected gap between the forward and reverse AIS estimators upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is clean and elegant. It provides a clear theoretical link between the accuracy of the marginal likelihood estimate and the quality of the posterior samples produced by the algorithm.

3.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The authors provide compelling evidence that BREAD is not just a theoretical curiosity. By integrating it into Stan and WebPPL, they make it a tangible tool for researchers and developers. The experiments are well-designed and convincing:
    *   The analysis comparing collapsed versus uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization provides clear, actionable insights into algorithmic performance trade-offs that are often difficult to quantify.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's utility as a rigorous validation and debugging tool for complex inference systems. This is a significant contribution to the growing field of probabilistic programming.

4.  **Thoughtful Handling of Limitations:** The authors are upfront about the main limitation of their method: the requirement of an exact posterior sample, which restricts its formal guarantees to simulated data. They propose a pragmatic and reasonable protocol to bridge the gap to real-world data and provide empirical evidence suggesting this heuristic is effective in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Guarantees on Real-World Data:** The main weakness is that the formal guarantees of BREAD do not extend to the real-world data protocol proposed in Section 3.2. Initializing the reverse chain with an approximate posterior sample breaks the theoretical derivation, meaning the method is no longer guaranteed to provide a bound. While the empirical validation is encouraging, this remains a significant theoretical gap between the method's promise and its practical application.

2.  **Narrow Applicability of Algorithms:** The BREAD framework is directly applicable only to algorithms like AIS and SMC that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space. It cannot be directly used to evaluate standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling, which remain workhorses of the field. The authors argue that insights will generalize, but this is an indirect benefit and limits the method's scope as a general-purpose diagnostic tool.

3.  **Potential Looseness of the Bound:** While the paper shows the bound is tight on some toy examples (Appendix B), the tightness of the bound `E[B] >= J` depends on the KL divergence between the conditional distributions of the AIS path given the final state (i.e., `DKL(qfwd(v|xT) || qrev(v|xT))`). In complex, high-dimensional problems, this term could potentially be large, leading to a bound that is too loose to be useful. The paper could benefit from more discussion on the factors that influence the bound's tightness.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by derivations, and the experimental work is thorough and convincing. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding its application to real-world data.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the background sections on AIS and BDMC are concise and informative, and the core method is presented logically. The figures effectively illustrate the key results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC, its application to bound sample divergence is a novel and significant extension. It provides a new perspective on inference evaluation that is distinct from the long history of heuristic convergence diagnostics. The authors also do a good job of relating their work to contemporaneous, independent research [CTM16].

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high. It offers a powerful new tool for developers of probabilistic programming languages and MCMC algorithms, allowing for rigorous, automated testing and debugging. For sophisticated practitioners, it provides a quantitative way to make critical modeling and algorithmic decisions (e.g., choice of representation). This work opens a promising new direction in the crucial area of assessing inference reliability.

### Recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and conceptually bold approach to the fundamental problem of MCMC validation.
*   Provides a practical tool with demonstrated utility for comparing models/algorithms and debugging PPLs.
*   The core theoretical insight is elegant and likely to inspire follow-up research.
*   The work is well-executed, clearly written, and honestly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method's formal guarantees are limited to simulated data, which is a major constraint for practical applications.
*   The proposed real-world protocol is a heuristic that lacks theoretical guarantees.

**Final Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. It is a prime example of the kind of innovative, conceptually-driven research that NIPS should showcase. The core idea of using bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound the divergence of approximate samples is a significant contribution that opens up a new avenue for research into the reliability of probabilistic inference. While the reliance on simulated data for formal guarantees is a notable limitation, the authors address it thoughtfully and demonstrate immense practical value even with this constraint. The paper's potential impact on the development and validation of probabilistic programming languages and inference algorithms is substantial. The novelty and significance of the contribution easily outweigh its current limitations.
