- Extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound Jeffreys divergence between MCMC-based approximate posteriors (AIS/SMC) and the true posterior, and derives a divergence estimator.  
- Proposes a protocol (BREAD) for applying the method to real data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and applies it to compare model representations in a matrix factorization problem.  
- Situates the work within MCMC diagnostics, marginal likelihood estimation, reverse annealing/thermodynamic integration, and probabilistic programming literature.  
- Acknowledges closely related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) and clearly distinguishes the contribution.  
- Theoretical derivation is rigorous and clearly connects BDMC to divergence estimation.  
- Practical implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages increases accessibility to practitioners.  
- The hyperparameter-fitting protocol addresses real-world challenges where exact posterior samples are unavailable.  
- Demonstrated utility includes insights into representation trade-offs and discovery of a bug in WebPPL.  
- Paper is well-written and logically organized, aiding comprehension of technical details.  
- Applicability is limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling on extended state spaces (AIS, SMC), restricting general MCMC coverage.  
- Computational cost is potentially high due to required forward and reverse AIS runs and is not thoroughly compared to other diagnostics.  
- Sensitivity of the hyperparameter fitting protocol and choice of MCMC steps (S) is under-explored and needs more analysis.  
- The choice of Jeffreys divergence is not justified against alternatives (e.g., Total Variation, Wasserstein).  
- Validation scope is limited; broader model and dataset coverage would strengthen generalizability claims.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound method; practical tool with compelling examples; addresses need for rigorous inference validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited applicability, potential computational burden, and insufficiently broad validation.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution with useful implementations; weaknesses are acknowledged but do not outweigh benefits.  
- Recommendation: Strong Accept; suggest future work to address computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity.
