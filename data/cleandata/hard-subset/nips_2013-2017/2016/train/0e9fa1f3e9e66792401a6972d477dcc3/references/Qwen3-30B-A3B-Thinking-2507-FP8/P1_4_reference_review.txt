

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method of Grosse et al. (2015) to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, rather than just bounding the marginal likelihood. They validate their approach on toy distributions where exact calculations are possible, propose a protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, and integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages. The paper makes clear connections to related work, particularly the concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and significant theoretical contribution by extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, which is a more appropriate measure of posterior approximation quality than marginal likelihood alone. The derivation in Section 3.1 is rigorous and well-motivated, showing how the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors successfully integrate BREAD into two major probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan), demonstrating its practical utility. Their implementation reveals a bug in WebPPL, showcasing how the method can be used for debugging inference algorithms.

3. **Comprehensive Validation**: The validation experiments (Section 5.1) are well-designed to address potential concerns about the method's applicability to real-world data. The demonstration that inference behavior on simulated data matches that on real data for multiple models (linear regression, logistic regression, etc.) is particularly convincing.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of background concepts (AIS, BDMC) and a logical flow from theory to implementation to experiments. The figures effectively illustrate key results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Applicability**: The method is restricted to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC), which excludes many common MCMC approaches. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't adequately discuss how this constraint affects the method's general utility.

2. **Heuristic for Real-World Data**: The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (Section 3.2) relies on a heuristic (simulating from fitted hyperparameters) that may not always be valid. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on five models, but more thorough analysis of when this approach might fail would strengthen the paper.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the method scales to larger, more complex models. BREAD requires running both forward and reverse AIS chains, which could become computationally prohibitive for large-scale applications. The authors mention this as a limitation but don't provide any empirical evidence about scalability.

4. **Comparison with [CTM16]**: While the paper acknowledges related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), the comparison is somewhat superficial. The authors could have provided a more detailed analysis of the differences between "subjective divergence" (their term) and "Jeffreys divergence" when using approximate posterior samples.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of measuring MCMC reliability. The theoretical extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel and well-executed. The practical implementation and validation in real probabilistic programming systems demonstrate clear utility for the research community. While the method has limitations in scope, these are clearly acknowledged by the authors.

The paper addresses an important practical problem: how to determine if MCMC inference is reliable, especially for non-expert users of probabilistic programming systems. By providing a rigorous, quantitative measure of inference quality, BREAD has the potential to significantly improve trust in probabilistic programming outputs.

The paper meets all four NIPS evaluation criteria well:
- **Quality**: The theoretical derivation is sound, and the experimental validation is thorough.
- **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
- **Originality**: The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel.
- **Significance**: The problem addressed is important, and the method has clear practical utility.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is significant, the method is well-justified, and the implementation and validation demonstrate clear utility. The limitations are acknowledged and appropriately framed within the paper's scope. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, particularly for researchers and practitioners working with probabilistic programming and MCMC inference.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3.2, consider adding a brief discussion of when the heuristic for real-world data might fail (e.g., when the prior is very broad or the model is misspecified).

2. Consider adding a brief analysis of computational costs to help readers understand the practical limitations of the method.

3. Expand the comparison with [CTM16] to better clarify the relationship between their "subjective divergence" and the Jeffreys divergence measured by BREAD.

This paper represents a thoughtful and valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, with clear theoretical foundations and practical implementation. I believe it will be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS and beyond.
