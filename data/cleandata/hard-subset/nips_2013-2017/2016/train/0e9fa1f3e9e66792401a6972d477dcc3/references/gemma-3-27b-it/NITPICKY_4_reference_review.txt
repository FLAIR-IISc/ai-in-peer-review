This paper presents a refinement of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior, applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC. The core idea – leveraging reverse AIS to obtain an upper bound on the divergence – is a natural extension of the original BDMC work and, in principle, provides a rigorous method for assessing MCMC-based inference. The authors further propose a pragmatic protocol for applying this to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulated data validation, and demonstrate implementations within WebPPL and Stan.

While the underlying ideas are sound and build upon existing work in a logical manner, the paper suffers from a remarkable number of small, yet persistent, issues that cumulatively detract from its overall quality. These range from notational inconsistencies to a lack of thoroughness in experimental validation and presentation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC to directly bound the Jeffreys divergence is a valuable contribution, providing a more direct measure of sample quality than existing convergence diagnostics.
*   **Practical Protocol:** The proposed method for applying BREAD to real-world data, while heuristic, is a reasonable attempt to address the inherent difficulty of obtaining true posterior samples.
*   **Implementation in Probabilistic Programming Languages:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan increases its accessibility and potential impact.
*   **Bug Discovery:** The identification of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates a practical benefit of the proposed method – serving as a rigorous testing framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** Throughout the paper, notation varies subtly but significantly (e.g., using both `p(y)` and `p(✓, z, y)` seemingly interchangeably to represent the same quantity). This makes following the derivations and understanding the analysis unnecessarily challenging. Several equations are poorly formatted or lack sufficient explanation. For instance, Eq. 6-12 rely heavily on unexplained notation, assuming too much prior knowledge.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Experiments:** The experimental validation (Section 5.1) is not compelling. While the authors claim consistency between real and simulated data, the visualization (quartiles) feels defensive, masking potentially important discrepancies. The fact that curves are “shifted vertically” suggests the scales are not comparable, raising concerns about the validity of the comparison. Simply stating curves are “nearly identical” isn't a robust claim without quantitative metrics.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The choice of models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization etc.) is standard, but the experiments lack depth. The matrix factorization example, while highlighting a difference between WebPPL and Stan, feels superficial. A more systematic exploration of different model complexities and prior specifications would strengthen the findings.
*   **Overselling the Protocol:** The protocol for real-world data relies on fitting hyperparameters. The authors acknowledge limitations but don't adequately address the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter fitting process. The assumption that fitted hyperparameters accurately represent the "true" underlying distribution is a strong one.
*   **Minor Editorial Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The writing style, while generally clear, could be more concise and precise. Citations are sometimes inconsistent, and the full reference list isn't presented until the very end.
*   **Inadequate Discussion of Related Work:** While the authors mention Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], the discussion feels perfunctory. A more detailed comparison of the two approaches, highlighting the nuances of their respective assumptions and limitations, would be beneficial.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The core technical idea of extending BDMC is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes the method accessible to a wider audience.
*   The bug discovery demonstrates a practical benefit.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous technical imperfections (notational inconsistencies, formatting errors, lack of rigorous experimental validation) significantly detract from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The experimental results are not particularly compelling and fail to provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper lacks a sufficient level of polish and rigor expected of a NIPS publication.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting core idea, I recommend **rejection**. The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of attention to detail and a lack of thoroughness in its validation. While the authors demonstrate a reasonable understanding of the underlying concepts, the numerous flaws prevent this work from meeting the standards of NIPS. A major revision addressing the points above, with a significantly more robust experimental evaluation and careful attention to notation and presentation, might be reconsidered in the future. The potential of this approach is clear, but this manuscript does not fully realize it.
