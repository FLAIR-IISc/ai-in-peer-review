## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – originally proposed for marginal likelihood estimation – to provide a rigorous upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate the method's accuracy on toy distributions, propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulated data validation, and implement the BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) framework in WebPPL and Stan. They showcase its utility by analyzing the impact of model representation choices in a matrix factorization example, and even uncover a bug in WebPPL during the implementation process.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015) and leverages concepts from annealed importance sampling (Neal, 2001) and sequential Monte Carlo (del Moral et al., 2006).  It’s positioned within a broader landscape of MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Gelman & Rubin, 1992), offering a more *quantitative* and rigorous approach than traditional heuristics. The work is related to Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) which also leverages BDMC, but differs in its initialization of the reverse chain and resulting divergence bounds. The authors clearly acknowledge this related work. The connection to thermodynamic integration and Jarzynski’s equality (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997) provides valuable context.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Guarantee:** The core contribution – providing an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence – is a significant advance.  This offers a level of confidence in MCMC results that is typically lacking.
* **Practical Protocol for Real Data:** The proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets, including the hyperparameter estimation and data simulation step, is a crucial element for making the method usable in practice.  The connection to parametric bootstrap (Efron & Tibshirani, 1998) is insightful.
* **Implementation & Validation:**  The implementation in two widely-used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) and the thorough validation demonstrate the practicality and robustness of the approach.
* **Demonstrated Utility:** The matrix factorization example effectively illustrates how BREAD can inform model design choices.  The bug discovery is a compelling demonstration of the method's utility as a testing tool.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly explain the mathematical derivations and provide sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Applicability Limitations:** The method’s direct applicability is limited to algorithms viewable as importance sampling over an extended state space.  While the authors acknowledge this and argue that insights are transferable to broader MCMC methods, the direct benefit is confined to a subset of inference algorithms.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and consequently BREAD, is computationally demanding. The need for exact posterior samples (even for simulated data) and the execution of multiple AIS chains can be prohibitively expensive for complex models.  A more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The proposed protocol relies on accurately estimating hyperparameters from real data.  If the hyperparameter estimation is poor, the simulated data may not be representative, potentially leading to misleading results. While the experiments demonstrate robustness to the number of MCMC steps used for hyperparameter estimation, a more detailed sensitivity analysis to initial conditions and prior specification would be beneficial.
* **Interpretation of Divergence Bound:** While BREAD provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, it doesn’t necessarily tell us *how* far off the approximate posterior is from the true posterior.  A tight bound is reassuring, but a loose bound doesn't necessarily indicate a severely flawed inference process.  More discussion on the practical interpretation of the bound would be helpful.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  Offers a truly novel and rigorous method for evaluating MCMC-based inference.
*  Addresses a critical need in the field – reliable diagnostics for probabilistic inference.
*  Demonstrates practical utility through implementation and real-world applications.
*  The work is technically sound and well-presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Limited applicability to a subset of inference algorithms.
*  High computational cost may limit its widespread adoption.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference.  The proposed BREAD framework provides a valuable tool for assessing the reliability of MCMC-based methods. While the computational cost and limited applicability are concerns, the rigor and practicality of the approach outweigh these drawbacks.  The demonstration of its utility in bug detection is particularly compelling.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section (particularly a discussion of computational cost and a more detailed sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter estimation process).




**Confidence:** 9/10
