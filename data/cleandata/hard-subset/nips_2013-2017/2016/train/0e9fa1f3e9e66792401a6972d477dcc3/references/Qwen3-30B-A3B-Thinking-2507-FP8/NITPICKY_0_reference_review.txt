

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend BDMC to provide rigorous bounds on inference quality, which they integrate into WebPPL and Stan. The work builds on Grosse et al. (2015) and relates to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), but provides a more direct bound on the Jeffreys divergence.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an important problem in MCMC inference: how to rigorously measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples. The theoretical extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel and well-motivated. The practical integration into probabilistic programming languages is valuable for practitioners. The empirical validation across multiple models and languages demonstrates the method's utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and citation inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "GGA15" vs. "Grosse et al., 2015," "CGHL+ p" vs. "CGHL+14"), which undermines professionalism. In Section 1, "th Conference" is a clear typo. These issues suggest a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a NIPS submission.

2. **Theoretical justification gaps:** The paper claims in Section 3.1 that "marginalizing out variables cannot increase the KL divergence" when moving from equation (7) to (8). This is factually incorrect—marginalizing out variables can only decrease KL divergence, not increase it. The derivation of E[log R] from equation (7) to (8) lacks proper justification.

3. **Circularity in real-world data validation:** The protocol in Section 3.2 for applying BREAD to real data (using fitted hyperparameters to simulate data) creates a circular validation process. The method evaluates inference quality on data generated by the same inference algorithm, which may not reflect performance on the actual real-world data. The paper acknowledges this concern but fails to provide sufficient justification for why this approach is valid.

4. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper uses "Jeffreys divergence" interchangeably with "symmetrized KL divergence" without clarifying that these are equivalent. More troubling is the inconsistent use of "J" to denote the Jeffreys divergence in equation (12) but then using "J" later to denote something else in Section 5.2.1 ("J" is used to denote the Jeffreys divergence in the first paragraph of Section 5.2.1 but then appears as a variable in the figure caption).

5. **Experimental details:** The paper states in Section 5.1.1 that they used 100 AIS chains but doesn't specify the number of iterations per chain or MCMC steps. In Section 5.1.2, they vary S (number of MCMC steps) from 10 to 10000 without justifying these specific values or explaining why 10000 steps is sufficient for convergence.

6. **Clarity issues:** In Section 5.2.1, the authors state "the collapsed sampler resulted in slower updates but faster convergence (in terms of the number of steps)." This is logically contradictory—slower updates would typically require more steps to achieve the same convergence, not fewer. The paper should clarify whether they mean "faster convergence per unit time" or "faster convergence in terms of number of steps."

7. **Reference formatting:** The references contain inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Brooks and Gelman.,? \Q1998\E" should be "Brooks and Gelman (1998)") and inconsistent capitalization in paper titles (e.g., "Sandwiching the marginal likelihood with bidirectional Monte Carlo" vs. "Sandwiching the marginal likelihood with bidirectional Monte Carlo").

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting extension of BDMC for evaluating MCMC inference quality, the numerous minor but significant technical and formatting inconsistencies undermine the paper's credibility. The theoretical justification contains a factual error in the KL divergence argument, the validation protocol for real-world data creates a circularity problem that isn't properly addressed, and the inconsistent terminology and formatting issues suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

The paper's main contribution is potentially valuable, but these issues prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for technical rigor and presentation. The authors should address these concerns before reconsidering the paper for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject
