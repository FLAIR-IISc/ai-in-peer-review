

# Peer Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method that extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to rigorously measure the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors build on the BDMC framework proposed by Grosse et al. (2015), which produces stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) in both forward and reverse directions.

The key insight is that BDMC can be adapted to bound the Jeffreys divergence, which measures both the undercoverage and overcoverage of the approximate posterior relative to the true posterior. The authors provide theoretical derivations showing that the gap between the forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. They also propose a practical protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and using approximate posterior samples to initialize the reverse chain.

The paper demonstrates the utility of BREAD by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan, validating it on multiple probabilistic models, and using it to analyze tradeoffs between different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) in matrix factorization. As a side benefit, BREAD helped identify a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampling implementation.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions on convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks and Gelman, 1998; Cowles and Carlin, 1996), marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al., 2015), and methods for evaluating approximate inference (Gorham and Mackey, 2015; Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016). It builds on BDMC while extending its application to a new problem domain: measuring the reliability of MCMC inference.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical derivations showing that the BREAD method upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The authors carefully extend the analysis from Grosse et al. (2015) and provide a clear mathematical justification for their claims.

2. **Practical Utility**: The method addresses a significant pain point in probabilistic modeling - how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. This is especially valuable for probabilistic programming languages where users may not have expertise in MCMC diagnostics.

3. **Real-World Validation**: The authors go beyond theoretical claims by validating BREAD on real probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan) and demonstrating its utility through concrete experiments, including a debugging case that uncovered a bug in WebPPL.

4. **Clear Protocol for Real-World Data**: The paper's protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (using fitted hyperparameters to simulate data and approximate posterior samples) is well-motivated and rigorously validated through experiments on five different models.

5. **Novel Application of BDMC**: While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, this paper shows its value for a different purpose - measuring inference quality - which represents a meaningful extension of the framework.

6. **Comparative Analysis**: The paper provides a thoughtful comparison with related work, particularly [CTM16], and clarifies the differences in their approaches to using BDMC for inference quality assessment.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Applicability**: The method is only directly applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC), which excludes many common MCMC methods. While the authors argue that these algorithms capture many of the same design choices as traditional MCMC, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of which MCMC methods are excluded and why.

2. **Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational cost of the BREAD method. Running both forward and reverse AIS chains for each model evaluation could be significantly more expensive than standard MCMC diagnostics, which might limit its practical applicability for large-scale problems.

3. **Dependence on Simulated Data**: While the authors propose a protocol for real-world data, the method still fundamentally relies on simulated data to obtain exact posterior samples. This could be problematic for complex models where the simulation process itself is challenging.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the reverse AIS chain uses approximate posterior samples rather than exact ones, but doesn't thoroughly quantify how this approximation affects the quality of the upper bound. The experiments in Section 5.1.2 show that the bound is robust to this approximation, but a more detailed analysis would strengthen the paper.

5. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan but doesn't provide sufficient details about the implementation challenges or how it might be incorporated into other probabilistic programming frameworks.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical derivations are clear and correct, and the experimental validation is comprehensive. The authors carefully evaluate both the strengths and limitations of their method. The paper is a complete work, not merely a position paper, and provides a practical tool that can be used by practitioners. The authors are honest about the method's limitations, particularly the need for exact posterior samples (which requires simulated data) and the restricted applicability to certain inference algorithms.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly with appropriate notation. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings, particularly the consistency between real and simulated data in Figure 1 and the model representation comparisons in Figure 2. The paper provides sufficient detail about the experimental setup to allow for reproduction, though more implementation details about the BREAD integration in WebPPL and Stan would be helpful.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a clear and significant extension of the BDMC framework to a new problem domain (measuring inference reliability rather than marginal likelihood estimation). While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, this paper demonstrates its value for a different purpose, which is a novel contribution. The protocol for applying the method to real-world data is also novel. The paper adequately differentiates its approach from related work, particularly [CTM16], and provides a clear explanation of how their method differs.

## Significance Assessment

The work addresses an important practical problem in probabilistic modeling: how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. This is particularly relevant for probabilistic programming languages that aim to make advanced modeling accessible to non-experts. The paper's method has already proven useful in debugging a probabilistic programming language, demonstrating its practical value. The insights about model representation trade-offs (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) provide valuable guidance for practitioners. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a rigorous method for measuring inference reliability that can be integrated into existing probabilistic programming frameworks.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The work is theoretically sound, well-validated, and has clear practical utility for probabilistic programming. While there are some limitations in the scope of the method, these are well-acknowledged by the authors and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

## Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant practical problem in probabilistic modeling
- Provides a theoretically sound method with rigorous guarantees (for simulated data)
- Demonstrated practical utility through integration with WebPPL and Stan
- Validated on multiple models and with a debugging case
- Clear differentiation from related work
- Well-written and well-organized

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited applicability to only certain inference algorithms (AIS/SMC)
- Computational cost may be prohibitive for large-scale problems
- Still relies on simulated data (though a protocol for real-world data is proposed)
- Implementation details for probabilistic programming integration are somewhat sparse

Overall, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the work represents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference and MCMC diagnostics. The method has clear potential to improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems and guide model design choices.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3.2, provide more detail about the computational cost of the proposed protocol for real-world data, particularly how it scales with model size and complexity.

2. In Section 5.2.1, add a brief discussion of how the observed tradeoffs between collapsed and uncollapsed representations might generalize to other model sizes or types.

3. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method might be extended to handle cases where exact posterior samples are unavailable (even for simulated data), such as through approximate posterior sampling techniques.

4. In the conclusion, briefly mention potential directions for future work, such as extending the method to handle more general MCMC algorithms or reducing the computational cost of the BREAD protocol.

5. Provide more implementation details about how BREAD was integrated into WebPPL and Stan, particularly regarding how the reverse AIS chain was initialized with approximate posterior samples.

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, and I believe it would be valuable to the NIPS community.
