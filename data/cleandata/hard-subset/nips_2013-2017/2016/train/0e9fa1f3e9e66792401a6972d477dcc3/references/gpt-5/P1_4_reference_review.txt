Summary
The paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide quantitative diagnostics of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper-bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC and the true posterior. The key result is that the gap between forward and reverse AIS log marginal likelihood estimates, B = log R_rev^{-1} − log R_fwd, satisfies E[B] ≥ D_KL(q||p) + D_KL(p||q) where q is the AIS final-state distribution and p is the target. This uses BDMC’s forward/reverse chains and a refined analysis showing that each bound controls one direction of KL after marginalizing auxiliary variables. The method, called BREAD, gives rigorous bounds for simulated data (exact posterior sample available via ancestral sampling), and the authors propose a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters to the real dataset, simulate new data from those hyperparameters, and check that inference behavior matches between real and simulated data; use an approximately posterior-initialized reverse chain for the upper bound. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate tightness on toy cases, show consistency between real/simulated data across five models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, GMM), compare model representations (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) across platforms, and report a discovered bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
Builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), AIS (Neal, 2001), and reverse annealing. The main novelty is interpreting the BDMC sandwich as a bound on Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior samples and proposing a practical protocol for real-world use in PPLs. The work is contemporaneous with Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) on subjective divergence, which is equivalent when the reverse chain starts from the true posterior; the paper clearly explains the relationship. It also contrasts with convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman) and Stein discrepancies, emphasizing BREAD’s ability to give rigorous guarantees on simulated data without strong assumptions on the posterior.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical result: the forward/reverse AIS gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; the decomposition into one-sided KLs is elegant.
- Practical significance for PPL users: a general, automated, and rigorous (on simulated data) way to monitor inference quality; integrates with Stan and WebPPL.
- Thoughtful protocol to bridge simulated and real data via fitted hyperparameters, with empirical validation that inference behavior matches across the two.
- Useful scientific insights: comparison of collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization across platforms; uncovers an implementation bug—demonstrates utility as a testing tool.
- Careful discussion of scope (need for exact posterior sample) and related work.

Weaknesses / limitations
- Bound is only in expectation; a single run’s B may underestimate J. The paper does not provide high-probability bounds on J or practical guidance on aggregating runs to control this.
- Requires an exact posterior sample; for real data the “fitted hyperparameters” workaround loses rigor. The validation is empirical rather than guaranteed.
- Experiments emphasize AIS; despite stating applicability to SMC, there’s limited empirical evaluation with SMC or models where SMC is preferred.
- Tightness evaluation of the bound is limited to small toy cases; no systematic study of how tightness varies with annealing schedule, number of intermediate distributions, or multimodality.
- Some reporting choices (quartiles rather than averages for AIS weights, shared initial state in reverse AIS) complicate interpretation; uncertainty quantification of B across runs is not emphasized.
- Notation and citations occasionally rough (placeholder references), and some implementation details are sparse (annealing schedules, kernel choices, compute budgets).

Quality
Technically sound and builds correctly on BDMC/AIS theory. The derivations for E[log R] and E[log R_rev] leading to the KL bounds are standard but usefully repurposed. The experimental claims are supported: the real/simulated consistency checks across five models are convincing; the hyperparameter posterior initialization sensitivity analysis (varying S) is helpful; the matrix factorization case study is informative. However, more quantitative assessment of bound tightness and run-to-run variability would strengthen the empirical evidence.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The theoretical section is concise and clear. Suggestions:
- Provide algorithmic pseudocode for BREAD and the real-data protocol.
- Report the number of intermediate temperatures, schedules, and MCMC kernels used, with sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify guidance on how many forward/reverse AIS runs are needed and how to summarize B with uncertainty.
- Clean up references and notation typos.

Originality
Moderately original. The core estimator is a direct consequence of BDMC, but the insight to interpret the sandwich as an expected Jeffreys divergence bound for approximate posterior samples, plus the practical protocol and PPL integrations, are novel and valuable.

Significance
High practical impact for benchmarking and validating inference in probabilistic programming and MCMC-based pipelines. The ability to detect bugs and to inform design choices (e.g., collapsing) is compelling. The lack of rigorous guarantees on real data is a limitation, but the empirical protocol can still guide practice.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide concentration results or practical CI estimates for J via multiple runs (e.g., using control variates or combining Markov’s inequality across runs)?
- How sensitive is B to the temperature schedule and choice of kernels? Any guidance for practitioners?
- Can you include SMC experiments to substantiate generality?
- For multimodal targets, do you observe systematic conservatism or failures? Any heuristics to detect when the bound is unreliable?
- Could you exploit path-sampling estimators or bridge sampling as complementary bounds to reduce variance?
- Please release code and scripts to reproduce all figures.

Pros
- Sound theory; useful, actionable diagnostic.
- Integration into widely used PPLs.
- Empirical validation across multiple models; detects real bugs.
- Helps inform model representation choices.

Cons
- Expectation-only bound; no high-probability or finite-sample guarantees for J.
- Rigor restricted to simulated data; real-data protocol heuristic.
- Limited exploration of SMC and bound tightness across challenging targets.

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The paper makes a meaningful, practically useful contribution that will be of interest to NIPS audiences working on probabilistic inference and PPLs, despite some limitations in theoretical guarantees for real data and breadth of experiments.

Reviewer confidence
High in evaluation of theory and relevance; medium in empirical generality beyond reported models.
