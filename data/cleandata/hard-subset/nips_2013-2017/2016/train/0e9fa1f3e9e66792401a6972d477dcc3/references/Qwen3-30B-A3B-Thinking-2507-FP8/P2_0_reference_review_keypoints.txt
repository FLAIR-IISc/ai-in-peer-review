- Introduces BREAD: a method bounding Jeffreys divergence to measure MCMC reliability by extending BDMC to AIS and SMC.  
- Provides a rigorous theoretical derivation of an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (builds on BDMC).  
- Validates the bound empirically on toy distributions and multiple real probabilistic models.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and proposes a protocol for applying it to real data via simulated datasets.  
- Strength: technically sound, with careful and rigorous theoretical analysis.  
- Strength: claims are supported by both theory and empirical experiments.  
- Strength: complete work with methodology, analysis, experiments, and PPL integration.  
- Strength: authors acknowledge limitations (need for exact posterior samples for simulated data; approximate samples for real data).  
- Weakness: lacks a detailed analysis of computational cost and scaling with model complexity.  
- Weakness: limited real-world validation (only five Stan manual models).  
- Clarity strength: clearly written, well-organized background and derivations, and well-structured experiments.  
- Clarity weakness: implementation details of WebPPL/Stan extensions are under-specified and BREAD bounds need more visualizations.  
- Originality strength: meaningful extension of BDMC to bound inference divergence and a novel protocol for real data; clearly distinguishes from related work.  
- Originality weakness: concept builds directly on prior BDMC work and is not entirely novel.  
- Significance strength: addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference and has immediate practical value (modeling guidance and debugging).  
- Significance weakness: no systematic comparison with standard convergence diagnostics and unclear how to use BREAD in typical stopping/workflow decisions.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant contribution with solid theory and practical validation; recommended for acceptance.  
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS.  
- Pro arguments: rigorous general method, sound theory, PPL integration, practical utility for model comparison and bug detection.  
- Con arguments: insufficient computational cost analysis, limited real-data validation, lack of systematic comparison to existing diagnostics.
