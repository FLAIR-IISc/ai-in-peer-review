This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC and the true posterior distribution. The core idea builds upon bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide stochastic upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, leveraging the ability to obtain exact posterior samples from simulated data. A protocol is proposed for applying BREAD to real-world data by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data from those hyperparameters, and evaluating inference quality on the simulated data. The authors implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate its utility in model comparison and bug detection.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in MCMC: reliable validation of inference quality. The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a valuable contribution, providing a more principled approach than traditional convergence diagnostics. The protocol for handling real-world data, while heuristic, is a pragmatic solution to the limitation of requiring exact posterior samples. The implementation in two widely used probabilistic programming languages increases the practical impact of the work. The demonstration of BREAD’s ability to identify a bug in WebPPL highlights its potential as a valuable debugging tool.

Strengths:
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound appears rigorous, building upon established results from BDMC and importance sampling. The mathematical formulation is clear and well-presented.
* **Novelty:** Extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a novel application, providing a new way to assess the accuracy of approximate inference.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the technical details. Equations are clearly presented, and the notation is consistent.
* **Significance:** The ability to reliably quantify the accuracy of approximate inference is highly significant, particularly for users of probabilistic programming languages. The demonstration of bug detection is a compelling practical result.

Weaknesses:
* **Limited Applicability:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS/SMC). While the authors acknowledge the broader implications for MCMC design, the direct applicability remains restricted.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC can be computationally expensive, especially for complex models. The paper does not thoroughly address the scalability of the method.
* **Real-World Data Protocol Heuristic:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on the assumption that hyperparameters fitted to real data generate representative simulated data. While validated in the experiments, this remains a heuristic and could fail for datasets significantly different from those used in the evaluation.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental validation is primarily focused on a limited set of models and datasets. While the results are encouraging, more extensive evaluation on a wider range of problems would strengthen the claims.
* **Relationship to Existing Work:** While the paper discusses related work (e.g., [CTM16]), a more detailed comparison of the strengths and weaknesses of BREAD and other approaches would be beneficial. Specifically, the distinction between using the true posterior as the reference distribution (as in [CTM16]) versus an approximate posterior is not fully elaborated.



Arguments for Acceptance:
* The paper presents a technically sound and novel method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence, a key measure of posterior approximation quality.
* The implementation in WebPPL and Stan increases the practical impact and accessibility of the work.
* The demonstration of bug detection highlights the potential of BREAD as a valuable tool for probabilistic programming language development.
* The paper addresses an important challenge in MCMC and provides a more principled approach than traditional convergence diagnostics.

Arguments against Acceptance:
* The applicability of the method is limited to a specific class of algorithms.
* The computational cost of BDMC may be prohibitive for complex models.
* The real-world data protocol relies on a heuristic assumption that could fail in certain cases.
* More extensive empirical validation is needed to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the method.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the limitations are acknowledged and discussed, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding scalability, the heuristic nature of the real-world data protocol, and a more thorough comparison to related work.
