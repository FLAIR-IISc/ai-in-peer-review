**Summary**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The central problem addressed is the lack of rigorous, quantitative measures for MCMC convergence, which is a significant challenge for both practitioners and algorithm developers. The proposed method extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed by Grosse et al. (2015) for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The key theoretical contribution is a new analysis showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC serves as an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior.

Because BDMC's reverse pass requires an exact sample from the posterior, the method's theoretical guarantees are limited to simulated data. To bridge this gap for practical applications, the authors propose a protocol for real-world data: first, fit hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset to measure inference quality. The authors validate this protocol by showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data across several models. The BREAD method is implemented and demonstrated within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, showcasing its utility in comparing model representations and even debugging PPL implementations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a long-standing and critical problem in the MCMC literature: how to reliably assess the quality of posterior samples. Traditional diagnostics can only detect non-convergence, whereas this work provides a method that can offer positive evidence of convergence (on simulated data) by providing a quantitative bound on the divergence from the true posterior. The integration into popular PPLs like Stan and WebPPL demonstrates a clear path toward broad impact, potentially providing developers and users with a powerful, automated tool for validating inference.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the BDMC bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence, is a clever and elegant extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors validate the core method on toy examples (Appendix B), rigorously test the assumptions of their real-data protocol (Section 5.1), and provide compelling case studies showing how BREAD can yield practical scientific insights (e.g., comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations) and serve as a debugging tool.

3.  **Originality:** While the work builds directly on BDMC [GGA15] and AIS [Nea01], the idea of using the log-likelihood bound gap to bound a divergence on the *samples* is novel. This represents a significant conceptual shift from using BDMC for model comparison (via marginal likelihoods) to using it for inference validation. The proposed protocol for applying this to real-world data is also a pragmatic and original contribution. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contribution relative to concurrent work [CTM16].

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The background sections clearly explain the necessary concepts of AIS and BDMC. The derivation of the main result is presented with clarity, and the experimental setup and results are communicated effectively through both text and figures.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge and address thoughtfully, is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD apply only to simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a reasonable heuristic, but it relies on the assumption that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters will mirror behavior on the real dataset. While validated on several models, this assumption may not hold in cases of significant model misspecification.

2.  **Clarity on Estimation from Multiple Chains:** There appears to be a minor contradiction or lack of clarity regarding how to estimate the expected bound from multiple runs. The derivation in Section 3.1 shows that the *expectation* of the bound gap `E[B_hat]` upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. This suggests that one should average the `B_hat` values obtained from multiple independent runs to get an estimate of `E[B_hat]`. However, footnote 3 on page 7 states that the analysis "does not justify such averaging" and instead visualizes quartiles of individual chain estimates. This point is confusing and seems to contradict the standard practice of using sample means to estimate expectations. Further clarification on why averaging is not used, or a revised justification, would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Computational Cost:** Running BREAD involves executing both a forward and a reverse AIS chain, effectively doubling the cost of a standard AIS run for each sample of the bound. For complex models where a single AIS run is already computationally expensive, the overhead of BREAD could be prohibitive. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability challenges would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in probabilistic modeling and inference.
*   Provides a novel and technically sound method with rigorous guarantees (in the simulated data setting).
*   Demonstrates significant practical utility through implementation in major PPLs and compelling use cases.
*   The paper is very well-written and presents a complete piece of work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method's rigorous guarantees do not directly apply to real-world data, and the proposed protocol is a heuristic.
*   There is a minor point of confusion regarding the estimation of the expected bound from multiple runs.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a significant and original contribution to a fundamentally important area. The proposed method, BREAD, provides a much-needed tool for assessing the reliability of MCMC, and its demonstration in widely-used PPLs ensures its potential for high impact.
