Summary
This paper builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-style algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space. The key technical claim is that the gap between forward and reverse AIS log-evidence estimates provides, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the algorithm’s approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The paper also proposes a practical protocol for real data by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating new datasets from those estimates, and using an approximate posterior initialization for the reverse AIS run. The method (BREAD) is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, with experiments on several standard models and a case study comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization. The authors also report using BREAD to uncover a bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
The work directly extends BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and connects to reverse AIS (Neal, 2001). The “upper bound in expectation” on Jeffreys divergence largely follows from reinterpreting forward/reverse AIS bias terms; related contemporaneous work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) is acknowledged and, when the true posterior is used as a reference, yields similar guarantees. Diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman) and Stein-based sample quality measures (Gorham & Mackey, 2015) provide alternative assessments; the paper positions BREAD as providing rigorous guarantees on simulated data and a heuristic protocol for real data.

Strengths
- Clear and careful derivation showing that the AIS forward/reverse gap upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the sampler’s stationary output and the true posterior.
- Practical integration into two widely used probabilistic programming systems and demonstration that the tool can detect nontrivial implementation errors.
- Empirical exploration across several standard models and a representation comparison (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) that yields actionable guidance within each platform.
- Sensible discussion of why simulated-data guarantees do not directly carry over to real data and a pragmatic protocol to bridge that gap.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: The core identity that the forward–reverse AIS gap controls a symmetric KL in expectation is a modest extension/interpretation of BDMC. The overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” work is substantial; the distinct contribution beyond that appears incremental.
- Expectation-level guarantee: The bound is on E[gap], not on the realized gap. The paper reports quartiles of single-run gaps but does not provide unbiased or concentration-controlled estimates of the expectation, nor guidance on the number of runs needed to estimate E[gap] with confidence. As a reliability diagnostic, high-probability or at least variance/concentration analysis would be valuable.
- Scope limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers and simulated data. The “real data” protocol hinges on fitted hyperparameters and an approximate posterior initialization, which voids the guarantee. While the empirical consistency between real and simulated forward AIS curves is encouraging, it is not a rigorous validation, and may fail under misspecification (as the authors note).
- Experimental validation is relatively light given the generality of the claims. The tightness study is confined to toy cases; the five-model study does not compare BREAD’s diagnostic with established metrics (e.g., R̂, ESS) or with Stein discrepancies, nor does it quantify correlation with downstream expectation errors. No empirical SMC results are shown despite claims of applicability.
- Practicality and cost: Reverse AIS at each temperature in Stan/WebPPL is computationally heavy, especially if NUTS/HMC transitions are used. There is little discussion of computational budgets, schedule design, or sensitivity to kernels/temperatures. Without guidance, users may find the method too expensive or unstable (heavy-tailed weights).
- The link from Jeffreys divergence to accuracy of typical posterior summaries is not quantified. While KL is a reasonable target, the paper does not provide bounds translating DJ to errors in expectations of functions of interest, nor discuss when a given DJ magnitude is acceptable.
- Presentation issues: A number of references appear garbled (years/venues), and some details (e.g., exact toy setups where J is computable, schedules, transition choices, run times) are pushed to appendices; more in-paper detail would help.

Quality
Technically sound as an interpretation of BDMC/AIS bias with a correct expectation bound. However, empirical support is insufficient to establish robustness or practical utility beyond small-to-moderate problems. The real-data protocol is heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The derivations are clear. The paper would benefit from: (1) explicit statements about the gap being an expectation bound and how the expectation is estimated; (2) reporting means with confidence intervals instead of quartiles; (3) clearer cost accounting and sensitivity analyses.

Originality
Modest. The derivation is a natural extension of BDMC and overlaps conceptually with concurrent subjective divergence work. The engineering integration into PPLs is useful but not, by itself, a strong novelty claim.

Significance
Potentially useful as a tool within PPL ecosystems and for debugging/benchmarking AIS/SMC pipelines. As a general-purpose MCMC convergence diagnostic, the limitations (expectation-level bound, simulated-data requirement, computational cost) constrain immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide concentration results or empirical variance analyses for the gap; recommend how many runs are needed to estimate E[gap] within a desired tolerance.
- Compare against R̂, ESS, and Stein discrepancies across the same tasks; relate DJ estimates to errors in posterior expectations to calibrate practical significance.
- Include SMC experiments and sensitivity to schedules/kernels; report compute budgets and wall-clock tradeoffs.
- Strengthen the real-data protocol: assess robustness under misspecification; quantify how approximate reverse initialization biases the bound.
- Clarify guidance for practitioners on interpreting DJ magnitudes and choosing temperatures.

Pros
- Clear derivation; principled connection to symmetric KL.
- Practical integration and real debugging impact.
- Empirical case study providing actionable modeling guidance.

Cons
- Incremental novelty relative to BDMC/subjective divergence.
- Expectation-only bound; limited, small-scale validation.
- Heuristic real-data protocol without guarantees.
- No comparison to standard diagnostics or Stein methods; limited SMC evidence.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is sound and practically interesting within AIS/SMC/PPL contexts, but the novelty over existing BDMC-derived diagnostics is limited, and the empirical validation and practical guidance are not yet strong enough to justify acceptance. Strengthening the empirical and theoretical components as suggested could make this a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
