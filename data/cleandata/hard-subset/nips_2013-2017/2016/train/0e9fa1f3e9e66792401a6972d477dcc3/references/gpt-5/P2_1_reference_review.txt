Summary and relation to prior work
The paper builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of sampling-based posterior inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-style algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space. The key technical contribution is to show that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log marginal likelihood estimates is an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the algorithm’s approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. This yields a quantitative yardstick for sample quality on simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available (via ancestral sampling of the joint). The authors call the approach BREAD and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. They also propose a practical protocol for real datasets: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate synthetic data from those hyperparameters, and initialize the reverse chain from an approximate posterior draw, empirically validating that forward-AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data.

The work extends Grosse et al. (BDMC) beyond log-ML benchmarking to posterior sample quality, and connects to Gorham & Mackey’s Stein discrepancies (alternative diagnostics with different assumptions). It also overlaps with concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka), clarifying that when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior, their estimator upper bounds Jeffreys divergence—equivalent to the present analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean derivation shows E[log ˆR−log ˆR_rev] ≥ Jeffreys divergence between q_AIS and p, directly tying BDMC bounds to posterior sample quality.
- Practical integration: implementations in both WebPPL and Stan, with case studies across several standard models.
- Useful diagnostics: the method can detect implementation bugs (demonstrated with WebPPL) and inform modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization), with system-dependent insights.
- Real-data protocol: thoughtful heuristic (fit hyperparameters, simulate, validate consistency) with empirical evidence that forward-AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data for several models.
- Empirical robustness: reverse-AIS results appear insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to “approximate” a posterior initialization over hyperparameters.

Weaknesses
- Scope and assumptions: guarantees require an exact posterior sample and thus apply rigorously only on simulated data. The real-data protocol is heuristic with no formal guarantees.
- Expectation bound: the Jeffreys bound holds in expectation, not with high probability per run. This limits its use as a per-dataset certificate. The paper could clarify how to aggregate runs to estimate the expectation with confidence.
- Tightness and calibration: outside toy problems, the actual tightness of the bound B vs. true J is not quantified; experiments mostly show trends of AIS estimates rather than reporting B and uncertainty intervals.
- Limited algorithmic breadth: applicability is to AIS/SMC-like samplers; direct applicability to general MCMC (e.g., HMC without AIS tempering) is indirect.
- Experimental detail: more quantitative reporting (e.g., number of temperatures, transition kernel settings, computational cost, wall-clock analyses) would improve reproducibility and demonstrate practicality. Citation formatting is inconsistent.

Evaluation by review criteria
Quality: The theoretical derivation is sound and builds directly on established AIS/BDMC analyses; the inequality arises from KL contraction under marginalization. The empirical validation is reasonable and multi-faceted (toy tightness, five realistic models, robustness to approximate reverse initialization, and a modeling tradeoff study). However, the reliance on an expectation-bound and absence of high-probability certificates is a substantive limitation for users seeking rigorous per-instance guarantees. Also, the tightness beyond toys is not thoroughly quantified.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. The derivations are concise and easy to follow. The real-data protocol is explained plainly. That said, the paper would benefit from:
- Clearer discussion of how many AIS runs are required to estimate the expectation and how to report uncertainty on B.
- More explicit instructions/settings for SMC (beyond AIS) since the claims include SMC.
- Cleanup of references and minor editorial issues.

Originality: The step from BDMC’s log-ML sandwiching to an expectation bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS approximate samples is a neat and useful conceptual extension, though mathematically straightforward. Overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” is acknowledged; the paper’s contribution is distinct in emphasizing the Jeffreys bound, toolchain integration, and a practical real-data protocol.

Significance: Practical significance is solid for the probabilistic programming and Bayesian computation communities. Having an implementable, principled metric for sample quality—plus the demonstrated utility in debugging and in choosing representations—is valuable. The requirement of simulated data limits universal applicability, but many workflows already rely on simulation-based validation; the real-data proxy protocol further increases relevance. Impact is likely among users of AIS/SMC and PPLs; less so for practitioners exclusively using vanilla MCMC without tempering.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled connection between BDMC and posterior sample quality.
- Useful, implemented tool that can influence modeling and algorithmic choices and detect bugs.
- Empirical studies across multiple models and two PPLs; cross-system insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees restricted to simulated data; real-data protocol remains heuristic.
- Bound is only in expectation; guidance on estimating it with confidence is limited.
- Novelty is incremental relative to BDMC and concurrent work; tightness not deeply characterized on realistic problems.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report B with confidence intervals across runs; discuss sample sizes needed to estimate E[B].
- Provide quantitative analyses of bound tightness beyond toys (e.g., controlled settings where J is approximable).
- Detail temperature schedules, kernel parameters, and compute costs; include SMC experiments if claimed.
- Improve citation formatting and fix placeholders.
- Discuss prospects for high-probability or PAC-style bounds, or practical surrogates.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. While the theoretical step is modest, the paper offers a practical, principled diagnostic with real implementations and convincing demonstrations. The limitations are acknowledged and mitigated by a reasonable empirical protocol. The contribution is likely to be used and built upon in the PPL/Monte Carlo communities.
