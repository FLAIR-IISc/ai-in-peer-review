## Peer Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

**Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), originally proposed for log-marginal likelihood estimation, to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior distribution.  The core idea is to leverage BDMC's ability to provide upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood to establish an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence *in expectation*.  The authors present a technique to exactly compute both the Jeffreys divergence and the bound on toy examples, demonstrating good accuracy. Crucially, they also address the practical challenge of applying this to real-world data by proposing a protocol involving simulating data from hyperparameters inferred on the real data. Finally, they implement BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) within the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages, validating its utility through experiments on several models, and even uncovering a bug in WebPPL.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the work of Grosse et al. (2015) on BDMC. It acknowledges and differentiates itself from Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), highlighting differences in the initialization of the reverse chain.  It also situates its work within the broader context of MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996), emphasizing that BREAD offers rigorous bounds where traditional diagnostics provide only suggestive evidence. The authors also mention connections to tempered transitions (Neal, 1996), importance sampling, and related techniques. The references are comprehensive and appropriately highlight relevant work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Approach:** The paper provides a mathematically sound method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence, offering a more objective measure of MCMC performance than traditional heuristics.
*   **Practical Protocol for Real-World Data:** The proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real data addresses a significant hurdle – the need for exact posterior samples – and is a valuable contribution. The analogy to the parametric bootstrap is insightful.
*   **Implementation and Validation:**  Integrating BREAD into widely-used probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrating its utility on various models, significantly enhances its accessibility and impact. The bug discovery is a testament to the method’s utility.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the complex concepts relatively accessible. The use of equations is appropriate, and the explanations are generally clear.
*   **Insightful Experiments:** The matrix factorization experiments demonstrate how BREAD can inform modeling choices and identify tradeoffs between different model representations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While offering a powerful guarantee, the method is computationally expensive, requiring both forward and reverse AIS chains, and exact samples for simulated data. This cost may limit its applicability to complex models.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn't thoroughly explore potential optimizations or approximations.
*   **Applicability Limited to Specific Algorithms:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). The authors acknowledge this, and discuss broader implications, but its limited scope is a constraint.
*   **Assumption of Simulated Data Representativeness:** The protocol for real data relies on the assumption that the simulated data adequately reflects the real data, potentially compromised by hyperparameter uncertainty. The authors attempt to address this with MCMC to approximate the posterior over hyperparameters, but a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of results to the quality of this approximation would be beneficial.
*   **Quantile Visualization:** The use of quartiles in the visualizations (Fig. 1) to avoid being overwhelmed by outliers is a reasonable approach, but the loss of information might obscure important features of the distributions.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for evaluating the accuracy of MCMC inference.
*   The proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data is a significant practical contribution.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes the method readily accessible to a wide audience.
*   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the method's usefulness as a debugging tool.
*   The insights gained from the experiments on matrix factorization are valuable for practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the method may limit its applicability to complex models.
*   The limited scope of the method to specific algorithms restricts its broader impact.
*   The reliance on simulated data introduces a potential source of error.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The proposed method, BREAD, offers a rigorous and principled approach to evaluating the reliability of MCMC algorithms. The practical considerations addressed in the paper, such as the protocol for applying the method to real-world data and the implementation in widely-used probabilistic programming languages, significantly enhance its impact. While the computational cost and limited applicability are drawbacks, the benefits of the method outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016.  I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community and has the potential to influence the development of more reliable and trustworthy probabilistic inference methods.



**Minor Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational cost of BREAD, such as using approximations or parallelization.
*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the quality of the hyperparameter estimation in the real-world data protocol.
*   Consider providing alternative visualizations of the AIS estimates that preserve more information while still mitigating the impact of outliers.
*   Add a section in the appendix detailing runtime comparison between running BREAD and typical convergence diagnostics on the models tested.
