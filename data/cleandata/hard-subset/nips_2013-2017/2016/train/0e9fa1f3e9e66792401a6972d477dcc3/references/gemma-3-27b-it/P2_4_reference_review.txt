## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – originally proposed for log-marginal likelihood estimation – to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples (obtained via algorithms like AIS or SMC) and the true posterior. The core contribution is a theoretical result showing that BDMC provides an upper bound, in expectation, on this divergence. The authors validate this on toy examples, propose a protocol for applying BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing – their name for this extension) to real-world data, and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. They demonstrate its utility in analyzing model representation choices in a matrix factorization example and even uncover a bug in WebPPL.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension of BDMC:** Applying BDMC to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples is a clever and potentially impactful idea.  It moves beyond traditional convergence diagnostics, aiming for a more quantitative measure of accuracy.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method, at least in the simulated data setting.
* **Practical Implementation & Validation:**  Integrating BREAD into popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) is a significant practical contribution, making the method accessible to a wider audience. The validation on toy problems and demonstration of its use in guiding modeling choices are valuable.
* **Bug Detection:**  The discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a valuable testing tool for probabilistic programming systems.
* **Addresses a Critical Need:** Reliable assessment of MCMC/approximate inference quality is a long-standing challenge, and this work provides a novel approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Applicability:** BREAD, in its current form, is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors acknowledge the broader relevance to MCMC design, the immediate utility is constrained.
* **Simulated Data Reliance:** The strongest theoretical guarantees rely on access to exact posterior samples, which necessitates simulated data. The protocol for applying it to real-world data, while reasonable, introduces an additional layer of approximation that isn’t rigorously accounted for. The authors acknowledge this and perform some validation, but the limitations remain.
* **Computational Cost:**  Running BDMC, particularly the reverse chain, can be computationally expensive, potentially negating some of the benefits for complex models. This isn’t explicitly discussed in the paper.
* **Interpretation of Jeffreys Divergence:** While mathematically convenient, the Jeffreys divergence isn’t always the most intuitive measure of posterior accuracy for practitioners. Connecting it to more readily interpretable quantities (e.g., bias in parameter estimates) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the matrix factorization example is useful, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat limited.  More extensive testing on a wider range of models and datasets would strengthen the conclusions.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the experimental setup is reasonable. The theoretical contribution is significant, although the practical limitations should be carefully considered. It's a complete piece of work, though further exploration of the computational cost and broader applicability would enhance it.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the explanations are mostly understandable, though the background sections (especially 2.2 and 2.3) are dense and could benefit from some streamlining.
* **Originality:** The extension of BDMC to posterior divergence bounding is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.  The authors clearly position their work relative to existing literature on convergence diagnostics and approximate inference evaluation.
* **Significance:**  The potential impact of this work is considerable. A rigorous, automatic procedure for assessing posterior accuracy could greatly increase confidence in probabilistic programming results and guide algorithm development. The bug discovery in WebPPL underscores this potential.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to quantifying posterior accuracy.
* Practical implementation and integration into widely used probabilistic programming languages.
* Potential to significantly improve the reliability and trustworthiness of probabilistic inference.
* Demonstrated utility in model analysis and debugging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited applicability to a specific class of algorithms.
* Dependence on simulated data for strong theoretical guarantees.
* Potential computational cost concerns.
* Relatively limited empirical validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the limitations regarding applicability and computational cost are valid concerns, the theoretical guarantees, practical implementation, and demonstrated utility make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program.  I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – addressing clarity in background sections and discussing computational cost would be beneficial).
