- Main contribution: extends BDMC to show the forward–reverse AIS log-normalizer gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation between AIS terminal states and the true posterior.  
- Empirical work: validates tightness on small toy models, proposes a fitted-hyperparameter simulation protocol (BREAD) for real data, integrates tooling into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrates practical uses (representation comparison, bug finding).  
- Relation to prior work: builds on BDMC and AIS, relates to thermodynamic integration and Stein discrepancies, and is contemporaneous with "subjective divergence" but novel in the explicit Jeffreys bound and tooling.  
- Theory quality: derivations are correct and well-motivated; key limitation is that the bound holds only in expectation, not with high probability.  
- Scope limitation: method applies to AIS/SMC-style inference (importance sampling on extended state spaces) and does not directly validate standard long-run MCMC.  
- Experiments quality: empirical sections are pragmatic but qualitative; real/sim consistency checks are heuristic and toy problems are useful but realistic quantitative CIs for E[ˆB] are lacking.  
- Suggested empirical improvements: report E[ˆB] with confidence intervals on realistic models and plot bound tightness versus number of steps and annealing schedules.  
- Clarity suggestions: explicitly describe how to estimate E[ˆB] (runs, averaging, uncertainty), provide BREAD pseudocode for Stan/WebPPL, and give guidance on schedule/kernel selection.  
- Practical guidance missing: show how to interpret single realizations of ˆB versus its expectation and how to choose temperature schedules/kernels.  
- Originality: conceptually close to BDMC but provides a useful, previously-unarticulated Jeffreys-divergence bound and practical tooling; novelty is moderate.  
- Significance: useful, model-agnostic procedure that can expose bugs and guide modeling choices in PPLs, though rigor is limited to simulated-data settings.  
- Main weaknesses: expectation-only guarantee, dependence on specific schedules/kernels, heuristic real-data protocol, lack of numeric divergence bounds on realistic models, and limited comparisons to other diagnostics.  
- Requests for clarification: how many reverse AIS runs were used, variance control for heavy-tailed weights, sensitivity to adaptive temperature schedules, and NUTS tuning details per temperature.  
- Pros summary: clear theoretical link to Jeffreys divergence, practical Stan/WebPPL integrations, and valuable protocol for real-data bridging and model representation analysis.  
- Cons summary: only expectation-level guarantees, qualitative real-data evaluation, applicability limited to AIS/SMC, and some overstatement of rigor for non-simulated data.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (7/10) — useful and implementable advance with solid theory and practical impact despite empirical and scope limitations.
