### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15], which was originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods. BDMC runs AIS in a forward direction (from a simple prior to the posterior) and a reverse direction (from the posterior back to the prior) to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood.

The main theoretical contribution of this paper is showing that the gap between these stochastic bounds provides an upper bound, in expectation, on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples generated by the forward AIS chain and the true posterior distribution. This provides a quantitative measure of sample quality.

A key challenge is that running the reverse chain requires an exact sample from the true posterior, which is only feasible for simulated data. To address this for real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: 1) infer hyperparameters from the real data, 2) simulate a new dataset from the model using these hyperparameters, 3) run BREAD on this simulated dataset to measure inference quality, and 4) validate that the sampler's behavior is consistent between the real and simulated data.

The authors implement BREAD in two prominent probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They conduct experiments to validate the real-data protocol and then use BREAD to perform a case study comparing different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) for matrix factorization, demonstrating its utility in guiding modeling decisions. Finally, they report using BREAD to discover and debug an implementation error in WebPPL. The work is closely related to concurrent work by [CTM16], which is appropriately discussed.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and notoriously difficult problem in Bayesian statistics and machine learning: how to reliably assess the quality of approximate posterior samples from MCMC. Standard diagnostics can detect non-convergence but cannot certify convergence. By providing a quantitative upper bound on a divergence metric, BREAD offers a much more rigorous tool for practitioners and algorithm developers. Its integration into popular PPLs like Stan and WebPPL makes it immediately useful to a large community.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the gap in BDMC bounds to the Jeffreys divergence, is a simple but elegant and novel contribution. It builds cleverly upon the analysis in [GGA15] to extract new information about sample quality, not just the marginal likelihood. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a strong set of experiments.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is a major strength. The authors don't just present their method; they carefully validate its key assumptions.
    *   The validation of the real-data protocol (Sec 5.1.1) convincingly shows that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on real data for several models.
    *   The robustness check on the number of MCMC steps used to generate the approximate posterior sample for the reverse chain (Sec 5.1.2) provides confidence in their heuristic.
    *   The case study on matrix factorization is an excellent demonstration of how BREAD can be used to make concrete, non-obvious modeling and algorithmic choices.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's practical utility as a sanity check and debugging tool.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on AIS and BDMC before clearly presenting the main contribution. The structure is logical, moving from theory to the practical protocol and then to experimental validation and application.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Applicability:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended space, such as AIS and SMC. It cannot be directly applied to diagnose standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS after they have been run. The authors acknowledge this but suggest that insights will generalize. While plausible, this limits the method's direct use as a general-purpose diagnostic for the most common MCMC samplers.

2.  **Computational Cost:** BREAD is computationally intensive. It requires running many (in their experiments, 100) forward and reverse AIS chains, where each chain itself involves many intermediate steps. This cost is not explicitly discussed or benchmarked. A discussion of the trade-off between the computational cost of BREAD versus simply running a single, much longer MCMC chain would be valuable.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** While well-motivated and empirically validated, the protocol for real-world data breaks the formal guarantees of the method. The validity of the protocol rests on the assumption that the generative model is well-specified and that the fitted hyperparameters are representative. If the model is badly misspecified, the simulated data may have very different characteristics from the real data, potentially making the BREAD results misleading. This key limitation could be discussed more prominently.

### **Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the critical area of MCMC diagnostics. The central theoretical result is novel and elegant, and the proposed method, BREAD, is of great practical importance. The paper's value is substantially enhanced by its thorough empirical validation, its implementation in widely-used software, and its compelling demonstration as a tool for both scientific inquiry and debugging. While the method has limitations in its applicability and the real-data protocol is heuristic, these are clearly outweighed by the paper's strengths. This work is likely to be highly influential and widely used by both researchers developing new inference methods and practitioners applying probabilistic models.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**For:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, important, and unsolved problem in probabilistic inference.
*   Provides a novel theoretical result connecting the BDMC bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence.
*   The proposed method is practical, with a sensible protocol for real-world data and implementations in Stan and WebPPL.
*   Experiments are thorough, providing compelling validation and a clear demonstration of utility.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and a complete piece of work.

**Against:**
*   The method is only directly applicable to a subset of inference algorithms (AIS/SMC).
*   The computational cost can be high, which is not fully addressed.
*   The protocol for real-world data is a heuristic that relies on the model being well-specified.
