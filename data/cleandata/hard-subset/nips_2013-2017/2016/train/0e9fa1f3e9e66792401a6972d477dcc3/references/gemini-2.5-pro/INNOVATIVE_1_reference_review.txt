This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the quality of samples from certain MCMC inference algorithms. The method is a clever extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally developed to provide tight, sandwiching bounds on log marginal likelihoods. The key insight of this work is that the gap between the stochastic lower bound (from a forward Annealed Importance Sampling chain) and the stochastic upper bound (from a reverse AIS chain) on the log marginal likelihood itself provides a meaningful quantity: it is an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

This work builds directly upon the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015), but repurposes it from a tool for model selection (via marginal likelihoods) to one for inference diagnostics. This represents a significant conceptual shift and connects it to the long-standing literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman and Rubin, 1992). Unlike most traditional diagnostics which can only identify non-convergence, BREAD provides a quantitative measure of sample quality that is rigorously bounded, at least in the context of simulated data. The paper also acknowledges and clearly distinguishes itself from concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

The authors make several key contributions. First is the theoretical derivation linking the BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence. Second, recognizing that the method's formal guarantees require an exact posterior sample (available only for simulated data), they propose and validate a pragmatic protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets. Finally, they demonstrate the method’s practical utility by implementing it in two popular probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, and using it to generate valuable insights on model representation and even to uncover an implementation bug.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty:** The core idea of this paper is brilliant and highly original. Repurposing the gap between BDMC bounds to measure posterior quality is a non-obvious and powerful conceptual leap. It reframes the challenge of inference evaluation, moving away from heuristic checks towards a more principled, quantitative measure. This is precisely the kind of bold, creative thinking that pushes the field forward.

2.  **High Significance and Impact:** The problem of knowing when to trust the output of an MCMC sampler is fundamental and plagues both experts and novices. This is especially true with the rise of probabilistic programming languages that promise to automate inference. By providing a general, automated procedure for monitoring inference quality, BREAD has the potential to be a tremendously impactful tool for the community. The demonstrated ability to guide subtle but important decisions, like the choice between a collapsed and uncollapsed model representation, is a compelling proof of concept.

3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The paper does not stop at theory. The authors implement their method in two very different and widely used systems (Stan and WebPPL), showing its versatility. The experiments are well-designed, not only validating the method's assumptions (e.g., robustness of the real-data protocol) but also using it to produce genuine scientific findings. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a particularly powerful testament to the method's utility as a rigorous sanity check.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The primary limitation is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD apply only when an exact posterior sample is available, which in practice restricts it to simulated data. This is a significant constraint for practitioners whose main interest is inference on a specific, real-world dataset.

2.  **Heuristic Protocol for Real Data:** The protocol proposed to bridge the gap to real-world data is a well-motivated heuristic, but a heuristic nonetheless. It relies on an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, breaking the formal guarantees. While the authors provide good empirical evidence that this approximation is robust in their experiments, its reliability is not guaranteed to hold universally across all models and datasets.

3.  **Directly Evaluates AIS/SMC:** The method directly bounds the divergence for the distribution of samples produced by AIS or SMC, not necessarily the stationary distribution of the underlying MCMC kernel (e.g., NUTS in Stan) used for transitions. The argument that insights about the quality of AIS transitions generalize to the MCMC kernel itself is plausible but not formally established.

**Recommendation: Accept**

Arguments for acceptance:
*   The central idea is exceptionally novel and provides a new, principled direction for tackling the critical problem of evaluating MCMC inference.
*   The work has high potential for significant impact, providing a much-needed tool for users and developers of probabilistic programming systems.
*   The practical utility is convincingly demonstrated through strong experiments that yield concrete modeling insights and even aid in debugging.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and honest about its limitations.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   The method's formal guarantees are limited to the simulated data setting, which might limit its direct applicability.
*   The protocol for real-world data is a heuristic, which might be a concern for those seeking absolute rigor.

In my view, the strengths of this paper overwhelmingly outweigh its weaknesses. The conceptual novelty is outstanding, and the problem it addresses is of fundamental importance. While the reliance on simulated data is a notable limitation, the authors acknowledge this and propose a pragmatic, empirically-supported path forward. This is not a mere incremental improvement; it is a bold new approach that opens up exciting avenues for future work in making probabilistic inference more reliable and trustworthy. This paper is a significant contribution and is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
