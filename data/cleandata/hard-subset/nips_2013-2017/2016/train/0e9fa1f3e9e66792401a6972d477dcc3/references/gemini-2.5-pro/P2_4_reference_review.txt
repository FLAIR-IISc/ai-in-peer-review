### **Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"**

**Summary**
This paper proposes a method called BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) to quantitatively evaluate the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by certain MCMC algorithms. The method extends the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea of BDMC is to sandwich the true log marginal likelihood between a stochastic lower bound (from a standard forward Annealed Importance Sampling, or AIS, run) and a stochastic upper bound (from a reverse AIS run initialized from an exact posterior sample). The key contribution of this paper is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution of the samples and the true posterior.

Because the method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a protocol: 1) infer hyperparameters on the real data, 2) simulate a new dataset using these hyperparameters, 3) apply BREAD to this simulated dataset, and 4) validate that the inference behavior is consistent between the real and simulated data. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility for comparing model representations and for debugging inference implementations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and assessing sample quality is fundamental and notoriously difficult. Standard diagnostics like the Gelman-Rubin statistic can detect non-convergence but cannot provide a quantitative measure of accuracy. This paper offers a principled way to obtain a quantitative upper bound on a standard divergence metric. This is a significant step forward and is highly valuable for both developers of MCMC algorithms and users of probabilistic programming systems who need to trust the output of black-box inference. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the practical utility of this approach.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** While the work builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the key insight—to reinterpret the gap between the forward and reverse bounds as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence—is novel and clever. This reframes BDMC from a tool for marginal likelihood estimation into a tool for assessing sample quality, which is a distinct and often more critical goal for Bayesian practitioners. The connection is derived elegantly in Section 3.1.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical derivation is sound and clearly presented. The experimental work is thorough and thoughtfully designed to address the main potential criticisms of the method. The authors don't just present the method but also validate its key assumptions:
    *   They validate that the bound is reasonably tight on toy examples (Appendix B).
    *   They provide empirical evidence that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters is consistent with behavior on real data (Section 5.1.1).
    *   They show that the reverse-chain bounds are robust to the use of an approximate posterior sample for initialization in their real-data protocol (Section 5.1.2).
    This careful validation significantly strengthens the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the background on AIS and BDMC is concise and sufficient, and the core method is derived step-by-step. The experimental sections are easy to follow, and the figures effectively convey the results.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Applicability to Real-World Data:** The primary limitation, which the authors are upfront about, is that the rigorous guarantees only hold for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a sensible heuristic but lacks the same formal guarantees. The validation in Section 5.1.1 is encouraging, but it relies on the assumption that if the forward AIS curves match, then the Jeffreys divergence on the simulated data is a good proxy for the divergence on the real data. This is plausible but not proven. It would be beneficial to discuss the failure modes of this heuristic more explicitly. For example, what happens in cases of severe model misspecification, where the "true" posterior over the model's parameters for the real data is very different from any posterior generated by the model itself? The authors mention an example in the appendix, but this point is important enough to warrant a brief discussion in the main text.

2.  **Scope of Applicable Algorithms:** The paper claims the method is applicable to algorithms like AIS and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), but the entire derivation and all experiments are focused on AIS. While AIS and SMC are closely related, the connection is not elaborated upon. The paper would be more precise if it either focused its claims on AIS-like methods or briefly explained how the framework would apply to a broader class of SMC samplers.

3.  **Clarity on Averaging Samples:** In footnote 3, the authors state that their analysis does not justify averaging the importance weights from multiple chains (a common practice to reduce variance in AIS log-likelihood estimates) and thus they report estimates from individual chains. This is a crucial practical detail. A brief explanation of *why* the analysis doesn't carry over would be helpful. Is it because `E[log( (1/K) * sum(w_i) )]` does not have the same simple relationship to the KL divergences as `E[log(w)]`? Clarifying this would help readers understand the trade-offs and limitations when applying the method.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that addresses a critical and challenging problem in probabilistic inference. The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful. The authors provide strong empirical evidence for its effectiveness and are honest about its limitations. The work represents a significant contribution to the field by providing a much-needed tool for rigorously evaluating the quality of MCMC inference. The weaknesses are minor and mostly pertain to the scope of the claims and opportunities for further clarification. I strongly recommend acceptance.
