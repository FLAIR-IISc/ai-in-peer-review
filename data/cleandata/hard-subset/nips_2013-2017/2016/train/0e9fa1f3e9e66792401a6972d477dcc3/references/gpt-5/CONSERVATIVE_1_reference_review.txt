Summary
The paper builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based inference (notably AIS/SMC viewed as importance sampling on extended spaces). The main theoretical contribution is that the gap between forward AIS (a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS (a stochastic upper bound given an exact posterior sample) upper-bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS sample distribution and the true target. The authors call the resulting diagnostic BREAD. They also propose a heuristic protocol to apply BREAD to real data by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating data from those estimates, and using approximate posterior states to initialize the reverse chain. They integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan, validate on toy distributions and five standard models, and show a case where BREAD uncovered a bug in WebPPL. They further use BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations.

Strengths
- Timely problem: practical, quantitative assessment of sampler accuracy remains difficult; leveraging BDMC to target a divergence to the true posterior is appealing.
- Clear derivation connecting the BDMC gap to the Jeffreys divergence, with a simple and implementable recipe using standard AIS machinery.
- Integration into two widely used probabilistic programming platforms increases practical relevance; the debugging case is a nice demonstration of utility.
- The real-data protocol (fit hyperparameters, simulate, compare behavior) is sensible in spirit (akin to parametric bootstrap) and practically motivated.
- Empirical studies across multiple models, and a concrete example (matrix factorization) showing how the diagnostic can inform modeling choices.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Expectation bound vs. actionable diagnostic: The main guarantee is E[gap] ≥ Jeffreys divergence. In practice, users observe a single random gap (or a small number). No concentration results or variance control are provided, and the paper itself notes heavy-tailed behavior and resorts to reporting quartiles. Without a high-probability statement or a practical aggregation scheme with guarantees, it is unclear how reliable BREAD is for a given run.
- Dependence on exact posterior samples: The rigorous guarantee requires an exact posterior sample (available only for simulated data from the model). The proposed real-data protocol deliberately breaks this assumption (approximate reverse initialization), so guarantees no longer hold. The empirical evidence (insensitivity to the number of warmup transitions) is welcome but limited, and may not generalize to challenging or misspecified models.
- Scope limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers: The method does not assess generic stationary MCMC kernels directly (HMC/NUTS, Gibbs, MH, etc.) unless embedded within AIS/SMC constructions. This constrains applicability as a general MCMC reliability diagnostic.
- Novelty relative to BDMC: The theoretical step from BDMC’s sandwiching of log-marginal likelihood to an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is natural given the forward/reverse chain KL calculations. The paper’s main novelty is in framing, derivation details, and engineering/integration rather than a fundamentally new estimator or bound.
- Empirical validation is modest: Tightness is demonstrated on small toy problems; for real models, the paper does not quantify how close the bound is to the true Jeffreys divergence (which is admittedly unavailable), nor compare with established diagnostics (e.g., R-hat/ESS) or alternative discrepancy measures (e.g., Stein discrepancies) to show practical superiority or complementarity. Also, sensitivity to scheduling (temperature ladders), transition kernels, number of intermediate distributions, and computational budget is not systematically explored.
- Statistical handling of multiple AIS runs: The paper notes that averaging logs is not justified by the analysis. Yet the reported quartiles across runs do not translate into a principled bound on DJ. Guidance on how to aggregate multiple runs to obtain a meaningful diagnostic (with error bars) is missing.

Quality
- The derivations are plausible and well connected to prior BDMC/AIS analyses; however, formal theorem statements with explicit conditions (ergodicity, reversibility, regularity) would improve rigor.
- The real-data protocol is pragmatic but the lack of guarantees should be emphasized more strongly; current language might overstate reliability.
- Experimental methodology is generally competent, but stronger baselines and robustness analyses would improve confidence.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The exposition of AIS/BDMC/reverse AIS is clear. It would help to explicitly state the main theorem (E[gap] ≥ DJ) with assumptions, and to separate clearly the guaranteed (simulated-data) setting from the heuristic (real-data) setting.
- Reporting quartiles is understandable given heavy tails, but the implications for using BREAD in practice should be discussed more candidly.

Originality
- Incremental over BDMC; nonetheless, the focus on posterior sample quality and the integration into PPL systems are useful contributions.

Significance
- Potentially useful to practitioners within PPL ecosystems; the bug-finding story is compelling.
- Broader impact is limited by the expectation-only guarantee, requirement of exact posterior samples, and AIS/SMC scope.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors provide any concentration or variance bounds for the gap, or a practical aggregation scheme (e.g., using median-of-means or tail-robust estimators) with guarantees for DJ?
- How sensitive is the diagnostic to the annealing schedule and choice of transition kernels? Provide ablations and guidelines.
- Compare to Stein discrepancy-based diagnostics empirically on the same models.
- For the real-data protocol, can you quantify the bias induced by approximate reverse initialization (e.g., via controlled experiments where exact posterior samples are available)?
- Release code and scripts to reproduce all figures.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a method grounded in established AIS/BDMC theory.
- Practical integration into Stan/WebPPL; demonstrates real utility (debugging, model representation choice).
- Clear exposition and generally careful experiments.

Cons for acceptance
- Main guarantee is only in expectation and only with exact posterior samples; limited practical reliability without further statistical control.
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-like methods; novelty over BDMC is incremental.
- Empirical validation lacks comparisons to standard diagnostics and robustness analyses.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and well grounded in established techniques, but the current paper over-relies on expectation bounds and simulated-data assumptions, with limited evidence that the diagnostic is reliable and informative in typical real-data workflows. Strengthening theoretical guarantees (or providing robust aggregation procedures) and broader, comparative empirical validation would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
