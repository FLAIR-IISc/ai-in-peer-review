

The paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. Building on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15], the authors show that BDMC can be extended to provide an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence. The method is applicable to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space. The authors propose a protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and comparing inference behavior between real and simulated data. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility for model representation choices and debugging.

The main contributions include: (1) deriving a method that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, (2) providing exact computation for small examples to validate the bound, (3) proposing a protocol for real-world data evaluation, and (4) implementing BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages.

Strengths:
The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations, with a clear derivation showing that the expectation of the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1). The experimental validation is thorough, with careful comparison of real and simulated data across five models (Section 5.1.1) and analysis of the effect of approximate posterior samples (Section 5.1.2). The method's practical utility is demonstrated through its integration into WebPPL and Stan, and the debugging example (Section 5.2.2) shows how BREAD can identify implementation issues. The paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from related work, particularly [CTM16], and acknowledges limitations appropriately.

Weaknesses:
The method's applicability is limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS or SMC), excluding many common MCMC methods like standard Metropolis-Hastings. The protocol for real-world data relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters produce data similar to real-world data, which may not hold universally. While the authors validate this assumption on five models, more extensive validation would strengthen this contribution. The paper lacks discussion of computational costs, which could be a significant barrier to adoption. Additionally, the rigorous guarantees only apply to simulated data, and the protocol for real-world data is heuristic rather than theoretically justified.

The paper is technically sound and well-organized, with clear mathematical derivations and comprehensive experimental validation. The clarity of presentation is high, with sufficient background provided for readers unfamiliar with AIS or BDMC. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience.

Originality is well-established, as the authors extend BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence rather than just the log marginal likelihood. The protocol for real-world data evaluation is novel in its approach to using fitted hyperparameters. The connection to probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) provides a practical contribution that advances the field's ability to evaluate inference quality.

Significance is strong, as the problem of validating MCMC inference is fundamental to probabilistic modeling. The method provides a rigorous alternative to heuristic convergence diagnostics, with clear practical value for both researchers and practitioners. The insights into model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and the debugging capability (Section 5.2.2) demonstrate tangible benefits that could influence how probabilistic programming languages are developed and used.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant problem in probabilistic inference with a novel, rigorous solution.
- Theoretical contribution is sound and builds meaningfully on existing work.
- Practical implementation in widely used probabilistic programming frameworks.
- Demonstrates clear utility through debugging and model representation analysis.

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC), excluding many common MCMC methods.
- Real-world data protocol is heuristic with potential limitations in generalization.
- Lacks discussion of computational costs, which could impact practical adoption.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a rigorous method for evaluating MCMC inference quality. While the scope is limited to certain algorithms, the method is technically sound, well-validated, and has clear practical utility. The authors have carefully considered the limitations and provided a valuable tool for practitioners. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and is appropriately scoped for the conference. I recommend acceptance.
