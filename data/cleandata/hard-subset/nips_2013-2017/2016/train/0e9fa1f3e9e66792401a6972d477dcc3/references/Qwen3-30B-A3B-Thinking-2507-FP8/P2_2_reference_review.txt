

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) techniques. The authors extend the BDMC framework from [GGA15], which was originally designed to estimate marginal likelihoods, to measure the accuracy of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The key insight is that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence. The paper validates this approach on toy distributions, develops a protocol for applying it to real-world data, and integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan for practical use. The work builds on [GGA15] but makes a novel application to measuring inference quality rather than marginal likelihood estimation, distinguishing itself from related work [CTM16] that uses a different reference distribution.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical contribution (Section 3.1) is well-founded, with clear mathematical derivations showing that the expectation of the BDMC gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. The authors provide a careful analysis of the bias in the stochastic bounds, extending the work in [GGA15] to address the specific problem of bounding divergence rather than estimating marginal likelihood. 

The experimental validation is thorough and well-designed. The authors first validate the method on toy distributions where both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound can be computed exactly (Appendix B), showing that the bound is reasonably tight. They then address the critical practical challenge of applying the method to real-world data by proposing a protocol using fitted hyperparameters (Section 3.2), which they validate through careful experiments on five different models (Section 5.1). The experiments demonstrate that inference behavior on simulated data with fitted hyperparameters closely matches that on real data, validating the protocol.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that the method is limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC) and that the protocol for real-world data requires simulated data that is representative of the real data. The authors also demonstrate practical utility by using BREAD to guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and to debug a subtle bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2).

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem and the paper's contributions. The background section (Section 2) provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with AIS and BDMC, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The main technical contribution (Section 3) is presented with clear mathematical notation and logical flow.

Figures 1 and 2 effectively communicate the experimental results, and the paper includes helpful appendices with additional details (Appendix B on toy distributions, Appendix C on model details, Appendix D on experimental results). The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making it accessible to researchers in probabilistic modeling and machine learning. The connection to existing probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan) is well-explained, enhancing the paper's practical relevance.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to measuring the quality of posterior samples. While [GGA15] introduced BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods, this paper demonstrates how the same framework can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors. This is a novel application of the BDMC framework to a different problem domain.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly [CTM16], which also builds on BDMC for evaluating posterior inference but uses an arbitrary reference distribution rather than the true posterior. The authors show that their approach (using the true posterior) provides rigorous bounds, while [CTM16] makes different assumptions about the reference distribution. The paper also relates to convergence diagnostics work (e.g., Brooks-Gelman-Rubin), but argues that BREAD provides rigorous bounds rather than just diagnostic indicators.

The novelty is not merely a combination of existing techniques but represents a clear conceptual advance in how we can rigorously measure the quality of probabilistic inference algorithms.

## Significance

This paper addresses a critical problem in probabilistic modeling: the difficulty of validating MCMC inference. The authors correctly identify that while MCMC is widely used, it's challenging to rigorously validate the effectiveness of MCMC inference algorithms. This is particularly important for probabilistic programming systems where non-expert users rely on black-box inference.

The significance is demonstrated through multiple aspects:
1. The method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality
2. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes it immediately applicable to real-world probabilistic programming
3. The method yields practical insights for model representation choices (Section 5.2.1), showing that different representations can have different convergence properties across different probabilistic programming languages
4. The debugging application (Section 5.2.2) demonstrates how BREAD can uncover implementation bugs

The paper advances the state of the art in probabilistic inference validation by providing a method that is theoretically sound, practically applicable, and validated across multiple models and frameworks. This is a valuable contribution to the field that will likely be adopted by practitioners working with probabilistic models.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and inference. It addresses a fundamental problem with a theoretically sound and practically useful method. The paper is well-written, well-validated, and makes clear connections to related work. The method is novel, rigorous, and has clear practical significance.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Solves a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling: validating MCMC inference
- Theoretical foundation is strong with clear mathematical derivations
- Practical implementation in two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Demonstrated utility for guiding model representation choices and debugging
- Thorough experimental validation with careful attention to limitations
- Clear differentiation from related work, particularly [CTM16]

**Con:**
- Limited to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS/SMC), not general MCMC
- Requires exact posterior samples for simulation, limiting applicability to models where this is feasible
- The protocol for real-world data relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters produce representative data, which may not hold in all cases

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field with a well-founded, rigorously validated method that addresses an important problem. The paper is of high technical quality, well-written, and has clear practical significance. The limitations are acknowledged and appropriately addressed within the paper.

The paper advances the state of the art in probabilistic inference validation in a demonstrable way, providing a method that practitioners can immediately apply to their models. The connection to real-world probabilistic programming systems (WebPPL and Stan) enhances its practical impact.

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, providing researchers and practitioners with a new tool for evaluating the reliability of their MCMC inference algorithms.
