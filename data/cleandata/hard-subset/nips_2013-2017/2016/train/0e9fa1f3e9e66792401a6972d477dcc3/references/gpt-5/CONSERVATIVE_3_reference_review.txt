Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of certain MCMC-based inference algorithms by upper bounding (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between the algorithm’s approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The key observation is that the gap between forward AIS (a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1) and reverse AIS initialized from an exact posterior sample (a stochastic upper bound) has an expected value that upper bounds DJ(p||q). The approach is implemented in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrated on toy distributions (where exact J can be computed) and on several real models (linear/logistic regression, AR, MoG, matrix factorization). Because reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, the method yields rigorous guarantees only for simulated data; for real data, the authors propose a “fitted-hyperparameter” protocol to simulate realistic datasets and initialize reverse chains from an approximate posterior sample. They further show how BREAD can guide model representation choices and even uncover implementation bugs.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on AIS (Neal), BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015), and recent discourse on stochastic bounds for log normalizers (Burda et al. 2015). It is related to Stein discrepancy–based sample quality measures (Gorham & Mackey), traditional MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman), and recent concurrent work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka) that also leverages BDMC but with a “reference distribution” reverse chain. The novelty here is the explicit derivation linking the sandwich gap to the expected Jeffreys divergence of the final-state distribution of AIS/SMC-style samplers, and its integration into PPL tooling.

Strengths
- Theoretical contribution is clear: the expected sandwich gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) and pT. The derivation is straightforward and correct as far as I can tell.
- Practicality: integrating into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrating that BREAD can (a) expose implementation bugs and (b) inform model representation choices, is useful.
- Sensible protocol for simulated-data evaluation when only real datasets are of interest; empirical sanity checks showing similar AIS behavior between real and simulated data are appreciated.
- The toy examples where J and the bound can both be computed are helpful to calibrate tightness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited scope: The method applies when the inference algorithm can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space with access to forward and reverse AIS (or SMC) and, crucially, an exact posterior sample. Thus, the rigorous guarantees are confined to simulated data and to specific samplers (AIS/SMC-style). The paper’s claim that insights “inform the design of MCMC inference algorithms more broadly” is plausible but not demonstrated convincingly for generic MCMC (e.g., HMC on real datasets).
- Expectation bound only: The upper bound on Jeffreys divergence holds in expectation over runs, not per-run. In practice, one must average many forward/reverse runs to estimate this expectation, and the variance of the estimator can be large (authors note heavy-tailed behavior and use quartiles). There is no uncertainty quantification for the estimated DJ upper bound, making it difficult to act on the numbers with confidence.
- Real-data protocol lacks guarantees: The fitted-hyperparameter initialization for reverse AIS removes the rigorous bound and becomes heuristic. The empirical insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps S is encouraging but limited (five models, modest sizes). It is unclear how robust this is under model misspecification, multimodality, or weak identifiability—precisely the cases where diagnostics are needed most.
- Missing comparisons to established diagnostics and alternatives: There is no quantitative comparison to R-hat, effective sample size, or Stein discrepancy. Even though BREAD is different (and can, in principle, certify convergence on simulated data), practitioners will want to know when it offers strictly better guidance than mature diagnostics or simulation-based calibration-like procedures.
- Cost and scalability: The computational footprint (running both forward and reverse AIS with many temperatures and multiple replicates) is not analyzed. It is unclear how this scales to higher-dimensional models where AIS is itself challenging.
- Experimental evidence is moderate: Beyond toy validation, the empirical section is largely qualitative (overlapping quartiles, “curves nearly identical”). There is no ground-truth Jeffreys divergence for the real models, so the central claim—providing accurate bounds—cannot be verified outside toy problems. The matrix factorization case study is informative but small-scale.

Quality
Technically sound where claims are made (for simulated data with exact posterior samples). The extension from BDMC to expected Jeffreys divergence is correctly derived. However, the jump from expected bounds to actionable per-dataset diagnostics is underdeveloped, and the real-data protocol is heuristic without guarantees.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The AIS/BDMC background is helpful. Some aspects would benefit from more explicit caveats (e.g., expectation vs. per-run, variance of estimators) and a clearer description of how many runs are needed for a reliable estimate.

Originality
An incremental but meaningful extension of BDMC: reframing the sandwich gap as an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence and targeting sample quality. Integration into PPL toolchains is a practical contribution.

Significance
Potentially useful for benchmarking AIS/SMC implementations and for debugging PPLs. The requirement of exact posterior samples for guarantees and the restriction to AIS/SMC limit broader impact on general MCMC practice. With stronger empirical validation and clearer guidance on estimator reliability, impact could increase.

Pros
- Clean theoretical link between BDMC sandwich and expected Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical integration and evidence of utility (debugging, representation choice).
- Clear writing and adequate related work coverage.

Cons
- Guarantees limited to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Bound is only in expectation; no variance/CI or per-run guarantee.
- Limited comparison to established diagnostics and no scalability analysis.
- Empirical validation modest; no ground truth on realistic problems.

Questions and suggestions
- Please quantify the variance of the estimated gap/BREAD bound and provide confidence intervals or concentration results. How many runs are needed to get a stable estimate?
- Compare against Stein discrepancy and standard MCMC diagnostics (R-hat, ESS) on the same models. When do these disagree, and which correlates better with downstream error?
- Provide a scaling study (dimension, dataset size, number of temperatures) and runtime breakdown.
- Clarify to what extent BREAD can say anything about HMC/NUTS outputs directly, beyond using AIS as a proxy.
- For the fitted-hyperparameter protocol, examine challenging cases (misspecification, multimodality) and provide failure modes. Can you bound the bias introduced by approximate reverse initialization (e.g., via a reference-distribution analysis à la CTM16)?
- Release code and scripts to reproduce all experiments.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a neat and rigorous extension of BDMC with promising practical applications, but the guarantees are confined to simulated data and specific samplers, the estimator is only an expectation bound without reliability assessment, and the empirical validation is limited. Strengthening comparisons, uncertainty quantification, and scalability analysis would make this a stronger submission.
