This paper introduces a novel and compelling method, BREAD, for evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based inference, leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence. The core idea – extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to assess sample quality – is a significant contribution, and the theoretical grounding appears solid. While the practical validations are somewhat limited (focused primarily on Stan and WebPPL, and relying on simulated data with a clever, but potentially fragile, real-data workaround), the potential impact is substantial. This is a high-risk, high-reward submission that I believe merits acceptance, despite some areas for improvement.

**Summary:**

The authors tackle the notoriously difficult problem of validating MCMC inference. Existing diagnostics are often heuristic and lack quantitative guarantees. BREAD provides a principled approach to bounding the error in approximate posterior samples, specifically leveraging the Jeffreys divergence. The method's applicability is currently limited to algorithms amenable to the BDMC framework (AIS, SMC), but the authors correctly argue that insights gained from evaluating these algorithms can inform the design of more general MCMC methods.  The practical implementation within WebPPL and Stan, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL during testing, further demonstrate the method's utility.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of BDMC to divergence bounding is genuinely novel. It reframes BDMC as a more general tool for assessing inference quality, not just likelihood estimation.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound and its relationship to the forward and reverse AIS estimates are clearly presented and appear mathematically rigorous.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The ability to quantitatively assess MCMC reliability is crucial for both expert users and, more importantly, for democratizing probabilistic programming by providing users with confidence in results.
*   **Implementation and Bug Discovery:** Integrating BREAD into two widely used probabilistic programming languages and subsequently discovering a bug in one is a testament to the method's practicality and debugging potential.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, despite the complexity of the underlying concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The current method is directly applicable only to a subset of MCMC algorithms (those amenable to the BDMC framework). The authors acknowledge this, but expanding the applicability would be a significant future direction.
*   **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The core theoretical guarantees rely on having access to exact posterior samples, which is only feasible in simulated settings. The proposed workaround for real-world data (hyperparameter fitting and simulation) introduces potential bias and is not fully validated beyond the observation of consistency between real and simulated data. The method's behavior when this consistency breaks down isn't fully explored.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running BDMC, particularly the reverse chain, can be computationally expensive. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the computational tradeoffs would be beneficial.
*   **Empirical Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate the method's functionality, the evaluation is somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to a wider range of models and datasets would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new and promising approach to a long-standing problem in Bayesian inference.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and well-supported.
*   The practical implementation and bug discovery demonstrate the method’s utility and potential impact.
*   The limitations are clearly acknowledged and discussed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scope of applicability restricts the immediate impact of the method.
*   The reliance on simulated data and the potential for bias in the real-world data workaround raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the limitations. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential impact on the field make this a valuable contribution to NIPS. The paper introduces a genuinely new direction for evaluating MCMC methods and provides a concrete tool for assessing inference reliability. I recommend acceptance, with a strong encouragement to future work expanding the applicability of the method and rigorously validating its performance on a wider range of real-world datasets.  The authors should also consider providing a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with BREAD.
