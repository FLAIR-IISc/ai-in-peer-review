This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples obtained from algorithms like AIS or SMC and the true posterior distribution. The core idea leverages bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to sandwich the log marginal likelihood, ultimately providing an upper bound on the divergence. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on toy distributions, propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data, and implement it within WebPPL and Stan. The work also includes a practical application demonstrating how BREAD can inform modeling choices (matrix factorization representation) and even detect bugs in probabilistic programming language implementations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension of BDMC:** Extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to measure posterior accuracy is a significant contribution. The theoretical connection between bounding the log-ML and bounding the Jeffreys divergence is well-motivated and clearly explained.
* **Rigorous Approach:** BREAD provides a principled, quantitative approach to assessing MCMC-based inference, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. The use of upper bounds (in expectation) offers a conservative, yet informative measure of accuracy.
* **Practical Implementation & Validation:**  Integrating BREAD into widely used probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan significantly enhances its accessibility and impact. The validation experiments demonstrate its consistency between simulated and real data and reveal informative insights into model representation choices.
* **Bug Detection:** The serendipitous discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights the utility of BREAD as a correctness check for probabilistic programming systems.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the method, and the contributions are clearly summarized.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of applying BREAD directly to real-world data (requiring exact posterior samples) and propose a practical, if heuristic, protocol to overcome this.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Applicability Limited to Specific Algorithms:**  BREAD is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). While the authors argue the insights generalize to MCMC, the direct applicability is a limitation. The extent to which the insights gained from AIS/SMC-based analyses truly inform broader MCMC design is not fully demonstrated.
* **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and thus BREAD, can be computationally expensive, particularly for complex models. The paper does not thoroughly discuss the practical trade-offs between computational cost and the accuracy of the divergence bounds.
* **Heuristic Protocol for Real-World Data:** The protocol for using BREAD on real data relies on fitting hyperparameters and simulating data. This introduces potential biases and dependencies on the hyperparameter estimation procedure. While validated, the robustness of this approach to different datasets and model structures could be explored further.
* **Limited Scope of Validation:** The validation experiments, while informative, primarily focus on Stan. Expanding the validation to a wider range of probabilistic programming languages and inference algorithms would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Diagnostics:** The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison of BREAD to existing MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size). How does BREAD complement or improve upon these existing methods?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The work addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference – reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples.
* BREAD provides a novel and theoretically sound approach to bounding the Jeffreys divergence.
* The practical implementation and validation in widely used probabilistic programming languages significantly enhance the impact of the work.
* The serendipitous bug discovery demonstrates the method’s potential beyond accuracy assessment.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The direct applicability of BREAD is limited to specific inference algorithms (AIS, SMC).
* The computational cost could be prohibitive for complex models.
* The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data relies on heuristics that could introduce biases.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference.  The authors tackle a challenging problem with a rigorous and innovative approach. While the applicability is somewhat limited and the computational cost is a concern, the benefits of having a principled, quantitative measure of posterior accuracy, coupled with the practical implementation and validation, are compelling.  The serendipitous bug detection is a testament to the utility of the method. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. I suggest the authors:

1.  More thoroughly discuss the computational cost of BREAD and potential strategies for mitigating it.
2.  Provide a more detailed comparison of BREAD to existing MCMC convergence diagnostics.
3.  Acknowledge more explicitly the limitations of the heuristic protocol for real-world data and discuss potential avenues for improving its robustness.
4. Expand the validation experiments to include a wider range of probabilistic programming languages and inference algorithms, if feasible.
