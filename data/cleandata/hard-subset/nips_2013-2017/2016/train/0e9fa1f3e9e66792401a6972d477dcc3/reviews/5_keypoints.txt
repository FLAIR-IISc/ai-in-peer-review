- Paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) upper/lower stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and compare sampler efficiency.  
- Idea: choose MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC bounds for equal computational effort.  
- Key challenge: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors' workaround: run diagnostics on posteriors for synthetic data (where good approximate samples are easier) instead of the real-data posterior.  
- Method mainly applies to AIS and SMC samplers and produces diagnostics based only on marginal likelihood.  
- Strength: an attempt at a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tool, which is interesting.  
- Major drawback 1: assumes a known representative hyperparameter value η_real from prior MCMC runs, presuming those runs already converged and a single summary is sufficient.  
- Major drawback 2: assumes the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior, which is unjustified, especially under model misspecification.  
- Major drawback 3: assumes starting from η_real lets the MCMC quickly produce an almost exact sample, an optimistic and unverified assumption.  
- These assumptions directly relate to the original convergence problem, undermining the diagnostic’s validity if they fail.  
- Empirical evidence is limited: the simple examples in Section 5.1 are not convincing that the issues won’t matter in more complex settings.  
- Recommendation: authors should explain why their method handles multimodality/bad summary-statistic cases better and provide simulations comparing their diagnostic to standard methods.
