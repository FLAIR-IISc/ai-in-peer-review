This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. Using annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior), the authors obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. They show that the expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC. They integrate the approach into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on toy and realistic models, and demonstrate applications to model representation choices and debugging. They also propose a practical protocol—fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate from the fitted model, and evaluate the bounds on the simulated data—to connect simulated-data guarantees to real-data use.

Relation to prior work: BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) provides stochastic upper/lower bounds on log partition function ratios using reverse AIS given an exact posterior sample (available for simulated data). This paper refines that analysis to link the BDMC gap to a divergence between approximate and true posteriors. It complements Stein discrepancy–based measures (Gorham & Mackey, 2015) and classical MCMC diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Cowles & Carlin, 1996; Brooks & Gelman, 1998). The authors discuss concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: The expected BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. This offers a task- and algorithm-agnostic scalar measure of sample quality for AIS/SMC-style samplers.
- Practical impact: Integrations with WebPPL and Stan, empirical validation on several models, and examples showing guidance for model representation (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and debugging (surfacing a WebPPL bug).
- Thoughtful protocol to bridge simulated and real data via fitted hyperparameters, with empirical checks suggesting behavior on simulated data mirrors that on real datasets.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity: Section 3 is harder to follow than the rest. The transition into 3.1 introduces variables and distributions abruptly. I recommend:
  - Motivate the extended-state viewpoint before defining qfwd, qrev, and pT, and give a brief roadmap for the derivation.
  - State assumptions explicitly (ergodicity, exact posterior sample for reverse AIS on simulated data, independence of forward chains, etc.).
  - Emphasize clearly and early that the bound is on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; it is not a Jensen–Shannon divergence.
  - Provide a small pseudocode box for forward and reverse AIS as used here, with notation aligned to the text.
- Terminology/naming: Please adopt a single, explicit name for the method (e.g., BREAD) and define precisely what it encompasses (using BDMC with forward and reverse AIS to bound the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples). The current text intermittently labels components (BDMC, BREAD) but never crisply defines the overall procedure at first introduction.
- Scope and limitations: The guarantees require an exact posterior sample, typically only available on simulated data; the empirical protocol for real data relies on fitted hyperparameters and approximate reverse-chain initialization. It would help to:
  - Quantify the effect of reverse-chain initialization bias (beyond the qualitative invariance to the number of warmup steps S).
  - Report variability/uncertainty of the bounds (e.g., via multiple runs, confidence intervals for the gap) and computational cost vs. tightness (number of intermediate distributions, AIS steps, transition kernels).
  - Offer guidance on choosing annealing schedules and kernels; include sensitivity analyses.
- Comparisons: A more direct comparison with alternative sample-quality metrics (e.g., Stein discrepancy) on shared tasks would strengthen the empirical section.
- Notation and presentation:
  - The manuscript alternates between y and D for observations; please standardize.
  - Make explicit where log ˆR and log ˆR_rev are stochastic lower/upper bounds and where inequalities are in expectation vs. high probability.

Quality: The derivation appears sound and correctly ties the expected BDMC gap to the Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC-generated samples. Empirical studies are sensible and include both toy cases (for tightness) and realistic models. Assumptions and limitations should be stated more explicitly, and variance/tightness of the bounds should be better quantified.

Clarity: Generally readable and well-structured, but Section 3 needs more motivation, a clearer notation roadmap, and consistent terminology. The figures and supporting code help, but the exposition can be streamlined.

Originality: The link from BDMC to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples is, to my knowledge, new and valuable, though concurrent related work exists. Positioning relative to Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) is appropriate; emphasize what is novel here (the specific bound, implementations, and practical protocol).

Significance: High practical relevance for probabilistic programming and MCMC users. The method provides a principled diagnostic for AIS/SMC-style samplers, informs modeling choices, and aids debugging.

Specific comments and minor issues
- Please explicitly state that the main theoretical result is an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence and highlight it as a contribution.
- Line 149: verify whether p(θ, z | y) should instead be p(θ, z, y) in that context.
- Notational consistency: unify the symbol for data (y or D) throughout.
- Supplementary code:
  - For WebPPL, specify supported versions and provide installation/integration instructions; ideally release as an npm-installable package.
  - If feasible, provide analogous packaging/instructions for Stan.

Arguments for acceptance
- Rigorous, interpretable measure of sample quality with solid theoretical grounding.
- Practical implementations and convincing demonstrations across frameworks and models.
- Useful guidance for model representation and system debugging.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees require simulated data with exact posterior samples; the real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Bound is in expectation; finite-sample uncertainty and sensitivity are not fully characterized.
- Exposition in Section 3 needs refinement; terminology and notation should be standardized.
