The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—conceptually similar to those in the Tempered Transitions (TT) method [Neal, 1996]—and reinterprets the accumulated importance weights to form a lower and upper bound on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance metric for MCMC transition operators. The paper's technical contributions include implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

This paper has several strengths and weaknesses. The primary strength of the paper is its central idea: using BDMC to assess the convergence of MCMC operators is simple, elegant, and easy to implement. This simplicity gives the work high potential for impact. This is particularly relevant for probabilistic programming systems, the target application of this work, and I believe most such systems would benefit from implementing this method.

My main concerns relate to the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The authors build upon a previous arXiv submission on BDMC and wisely dedicate significant space to re-explaining the method. Unfortunately, this leaves the main technical contributions in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed, and it is in these sections that the writing quality declines. This results in several clarity issues, which I detail below.

Setting aside the clarity issues, the technical contributions of this paper are modest. Relative to the existing work on BDMC, the main novelty appears to be the observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds itself provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. This is a nice observation, although arguably not entirely novel.

This leads to a point about related work. The authors curiously omit a citation to Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which is highly relevant. This citation is also missing from the original BDMC manuscript, raising the question of whether the authors are aware of this connection. This is unfortunate, as it represents a missed opportunity to discuss the strong connections between the two methods. TT performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweeps as BDMC, but its goal is to create an effective "tempering" MCMC transition move to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep, and the resulting sample from the forward sweep is used as a proposal. This proposal is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of the importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. As far as I can tell, the acceptance ratio in TT is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds derived in BDMC. In fact, Section 3.3 of Neal's paper interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log Z_T - log Z_1 from above and below, and notes that a TT sampler accepts with probability 1 when these two estimates converge.

The analogy with TT also raises the question of why the authors do not pay more attention to the choice of the annealing schedule (the β_t values). In TT, it is well known that steps in Δβ = β_{t+1} - β_t that are too large lead to a poor acceptance ratio, while steps that are too small waste computation. The gap between the stochastic bounds in BDMC will similarly depend on Δβ, which provides clear intuition. If the bounds are not sufficiently close, BDMC should be re-run with smaller Δβ steps. Similarly, I suspect that comparisons between different MCMC transition operators are unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds approximately 1 nat. In this scenario, one should again reduce Δβ. It is unclear from the paper what the "auto" in AutoBDMC refers to. There appears to be an opportunity here to design an adaptive schedule that iteratively decreases Δβ until the bounds are sufficiently tight for reliable assessment. The experiments could be more thorough in this respect. A systematic study of how the bounds vary with the step size Δβ would be valuable.

Despite these criticisms, I believe this is a promising paper. As noted, the proposed method is potentially very useful. If the authors can convincingly address the issues of clarity and the omission of related work in their rebuttal and the camera-ready version, I would be inclined to argue for acceptance.

**Clarity**
*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is too imprecise. Presumably, this refers to $\{z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}\}$, but this is ambiguous because the algorithm contains two separate AIS sweeps. This ambiguity is compounded by the inconsistent use of labels ("rev" and "fwd" vs. "for" and "back"). It is unclear if this distinction is intentional.
*   In equations (5-7) and (9-11), the authors take an expectation over an estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ which is never defined. This makes it difficult to follow the derivation of these equations. Should this be $E[\log \hat{R}_{rev}]$ and $E[\log \hat{R}_{fwd}]$?
*   Equation (6) appears incorrect as written; the two distributions in the KL divergence are identical. Based on equations (4) and (10), I assume the second term should be something like $q_{back}(v | z, \theta, y)$?
*   The term "AutoBDMC" is never unambiguously defined. Does it refer to the protocol of (a) calculating the gap between the stochastic bounds to assess convergence, and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme from Section 3.2?

**Experiments**
*   It is not clear how the annealing schedule $\beta_t$ is chosen. From the code in the appendix (`var step = 1/options.steps`), it appears a simple linear schedule is used. Is AutoBDMC therefore parameterized by the number of steps, T, used to anneal from $\beta=0$ to $\beta=1$? This should be stated explicitly.
*   In Figures 2 and 3, does "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" on the x-axis refer to the number of steps used for the MCMC transition operator within each temperature level of the BDMC run?
*   The acronym RAIS (presumably Reverse AIS) is not defined in the captions. The variants (u)c(R)AIS are also undefined; I assume they refer to (un)collapsed versions?
*   The font size for the axes and tick labels in the figures should be increased for readability.

**Typos**
*   In the expression for $D_{JS}$, two closing parentheses are missing in the KL divergence terms.
*   In the NUTS citation, "Homan" should be "Hoffman".
