

The paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and compare schemes, advocating for the choice of scheme yielding the tightest bounds under fixed computational cost. The core challenge is BMC's requirement for an exact posterior sample to produce valid upper bounds. The authors address this by performing diagnostics on posteriors derived from synthetic data generated using a pre-specified hyperparameter value \(\eta_{\text{real}}\), arguing these synthetic posteriors are easier to sample and thus yield tighter BMC bounds. While the use of BMC's stochastic bounds for convergence diagnostics is theoretically interesting, the proposed methodology suffers from critical, unjustified assumptions that undermine its applicability and validity.

The first major issue concerns the assumption that \(\eta_{\text{real}}\)—a hyperparameter value representative of the *actual* posterior—can be reliably known *a priori*. This requires the prior MCMC run used to estimate \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) to have converged, which is precisely the convergence problem the authors aim to solve. Furthermore, summarizing the complex posterior with a single hyperparameter value is generally unjustifiable and likely invalid for non-trivial posteriors. The authors provide no justification for this strong simplification, effectively assuming the very convergence property they seek to diagnose.

Second, the methodology assumes the posterior induced by synthetic data (using \(\eta_{\text{real}}\)) exhibits similar MCMC convergence properties to the *true* posterior. This is a significant leap, especially under model misspecification, where synthetic and true posteriors may differ fundamentally. The approach thus shifts the diagnostic target from the original problem to a different posterior, raising questions about the relevance of the resulting bounds. The authors do not adequately justify this assumption, particularly in scenarios where model mismatch is plausible.

Third, the authors optimistically assume that starting MCMC from \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) rapidly produces an almost-exact sample from the *true* posterior. While this *might* hold in some cases, it is not guaranteed and depends on the same convergence properties the diagnostic aims to assess. Consequently, the proposed method relies on assumptions that are intrinsically tied to the very problem it purports to solve—effectively using the solution to validate itself.

The authors acknowledge these limitations (e.g., Section 5.1), but the limited examples provided fail to convincingly demonstrate robustness. The simple cases shown may avoid the pitfalls, yet they do not establish that the issues are negligible in more complex settings. The paper criticizes existing diagnostics (lines 203-205) for failing in multimodal or low-summary-statistic scenarios, but offers no evidence that their BMC-based approach overcomes these challenges. A clear explanation of *why* BMC diagnostics succeed where others fail, supported by relevant simulations, is missing.

More broadly, the methodology's value hinges on its ability to provide theoretically grounded diagnostics, yet its reliance on the three assumptions above significantly weakens this claim. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) provide rigorous justification for the synthetic data approach and the \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) assumption, perhaps through sensitivity analysis; (2) explicitly address how the method handles model misspecification; and (3) include comparative simulations against standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size) across diverse scenarios, including multimodal targets and cases with insufficient summary statistics. Without such validation, the proposed diagnostic remains a theoretical curiosity rather than a practical tool.

In summary, while the core idea of leveraging BMC bounds for convergence diagnostics is novel and theoretically appealing, the implementation's dependence on unverified assumptions—particularly the shift to synthetic posteriors and the reliance on \(\eta_{\text{real}}\)—undermines its credibility. The limited empirical validation fails to demonstrate robustness, and the absence of comparative evidence against established methods weakens the claim of practical utility. The paper would benefit significantly from addressing these critical gaps before being considered for publication.
