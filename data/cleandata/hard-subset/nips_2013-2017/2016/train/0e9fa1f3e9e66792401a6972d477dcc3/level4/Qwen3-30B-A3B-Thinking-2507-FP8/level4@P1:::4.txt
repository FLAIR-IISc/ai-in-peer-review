

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously assessing the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to evaluate MCMC algorithms through annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains run both forward and backward on simulated data. The key insight is that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate and true posterior distributions.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) derivation of a bound on the Jeffreys divergence using BDMC; (2) demonstration that this bound is accurate on toy distributions where exact computation is possible; (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from estimated hyperparameters; and (4) implementation of BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, with validation across multiple probabilistic models.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference: the difficulty of reliably assessing the quality of MCMC samples, particularly in black-box inference systems. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their method relates to prior work on convergence diagnostics and marginal likelihood estimation, particularly connecting to the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015). The paper's connection to the related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) is well-handled, with careful distinction made between their "subjective divergence" and the authors' approach.

The technical contributions are significant, particularly the derivation in Section 3.1 showing how the BDMC framework can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence. However, the presentation of this derivation could be improved. The transition into Section 3.1 is abrupt, as it immediately dives into variable definitions without sufficient motivation. The novelty of the divergence bound should be more explicitly stated, as this appears to be the core theoretical contribution that distinguishes the paper from prior work.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors validate their method on five realistic models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, time series modeling, and Gaussian mixtures) using both simulated and real-world data. The consistency check between real and simulated data (Section 5.1.1) is particularly convincing, showing that inference behavior on simulated data closely matches that on real data when using hyperparameters estimated from the real data. The validation of their approximate posterior sampling approach for hyperparameters (Section 5.1.2) further strengthens their methodology.

The paper also demonstrates practical applications of BREAD, including model selection (Section 5.2.1) and debugging (Section 5.2.2). The matrix factorization case study provides valuable insights into how different model representations affect inference performance across probabilistic programming frameworks, while the WebPPL bug discovery illustrates BREAD's utility for implementation validation.

The paper would benefit from several clarifications: The term "AutoBDMC" appears in the draft review but is never defined in the paper (the method is called BREAD), which is confusing. The paper uses both "y" and "D" to denote data, creating notational inconsistency. The WebPPL implementation details should include version compatibility and installation instructions, and releasing the code as a package would be helpful.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic inference. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical utility. While the presentation of the key theoretical contribution could be improved, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference with a method that is both rigorous and applicable to real-world problems. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, and the applications to model selection and debugging demonstrate the method's practical utility.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims (Quality); clearly written with good organization (Clarity); introduces a novel approach to bounding posterior divergence (Originality); and provides a valuable tool for practitioners to assess inference quality (Significance). I recommend acceptance.
