This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors integrate their method into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets.

The paper relates to previous work on diagnosing convergence of Markov chains and automatically configuring parameters of MCMC algorithms. However, the authors' approach is distinct in that it provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference.

The paper's main technical contributions include showing that bidirectional Monte Carlo yields an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples from the true posterior, presenting a technique for exactly computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, proposing a protocol for using bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate the accuracy of approximate inference on real-world datasets, and extending both WebPPL and Stan to implement the method.

The paper's strengths include its technical soundness, clarity of writing, and thorough evaluation of the method on various models and datasets. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the relationship between their method and previous work.

The paper's weaknesses include its limited scope, as the method is only directly applicable to certain algorithms such as annealed importance sampling or sequential Monte Carlo. Additionally, the authors' use of a heuristic scheme to generate simulated datasets that resemble real-world data may not be entirely convincing.

Arguments for acceptance include:

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the method on various models and datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clear in its explanation of the method and its relationship to previous work.

Arguments against acceptance include:

* The method's limited scope may reduce its impact and applicability.
* The authors' use of a heuristic scheme to generate simulated datasets may not be entirely convincing.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. While it has some limitations, its technical soundness, clarity of writing, and thorough evaluation make it a valuable contribution to the field.
