

The paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC framework [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based inference, specifically for algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). Key contributions include: (1) deriving an estimator that upper bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence; (2) validating this bound on toy distributions; (3) proposing a protocol to evaluate inference on real-world data via hyperparameter-fitted simulations; and (4) integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan for empirical validation across multiple models.

The work builds on prior NIPS contributions, particularly [GGA15] (BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation), and connects to related efforts like [CTM16] (subjective divergence) and MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92]. While [CTM16] shares conceptual overlap, BREAD’s theoretical refinement—explicitly bounding the Jeffreys divergence via BDMC and providing a practical real-data validation protocol—represents a distinct advancement. The paper also addresses a critical gap in probabilistic programming: enabling non-experts to assess inference reliability, a theme aligned with NIPS’ interest in practical inference tools [GS, CGHL+ p].

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical contribution (Section 3.1) is sound and well-motivated, extending BDMC to a practically useful divergence bound. The validation protocol (Section 3.2) is innovative and addresses a key limitation of prior methods (reliance on simulated data). The empirical implementation in WebPPL and Stan (Section 5) is thorough, demonstrating real-world utility through matrix factorization case studies and even uncovering a WebPPL bug. The comparison of model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) provides actionable insights for practitioners, highlighting language-specific trade-offs. The paper’s clarity is excellent, with precise definitions and well-structured experiments.

**Weaknesses:**  
The scope is limited to AIS/SMC-based inference, excluding broader MCMC methods (e.g., standard Metropolis-Hastings). While the authors note this limitation, the claim that insights generalize to "traditional MCMC" (Section 1) requires stronger justification—e.g., via additional experiments on non-AIS MCMC. The real-data validation (Section 5.1) is compelling but relies on fitted hyperparameters; the paper should more explicitly address whether this protocol generalizes to highly misspecified models. The comparison to [CTM16] is adequate, but the paper could better clarify how BREAD’s guarantees (exact posterior samples for simulated data) differ from the subjective divergence’s assumptions.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, technically rigorous paper that advances the state of inference diagnostics. It delivers a novel, implementable method (BREAD) with clear significance for probabilistic programming users, addressing a well-known pain point in black-box inference. The work aligns with NIPS’ focus on practical, scalable inference methods and demonstrates strong theoretical grounding combined with empirical validation. While the scope is narrower than ideal, the contributions are substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper does not merely implement known techniques—it refines theory, proposes a new validation protocol, and provides actionable insights for practitioners. The venue mismatch ("MCMC workshop") is unwarranted; NIPS has consistently published similar inference methodology papers (e.g., [Gorham and Mackey, NIPS 2015; Homan and Gelman, NIPS 2014]).

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to inference reliability assessment, with strong theoretical and empirical support. Its focus on practical diagnostics for probabilistic programming systems is highly relevant to NIPS’ community. The weaknesses are addressable in revision (e.g., clarifying scope limitations) but do not undermine the core contribution.
