The authors propose a method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique for bounding the marginal likelihood. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, which can be viewed as similar to Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal Stat & Comp 1996], and interprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This work proposes to utilize the gap between these bounds as a performance measure for MCMC operators. The main technical contributions are implementations of BDMC within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

This paper presents a compelling idea – leveraging BDMC for assessing MCMC convergence – that is conceptually simple and readily implementable, potentially making it a highly impactful contribution, especially within probabilistic programming. The ability to automatically assess MCMC performance within these systems is valuable, and adopting this method would likely benefit the community. 

However, the novelty and technical depth warrant careful consideration. While the authors appropriately cite related work on BDMC, the discussion of Tempered Transitions [Neal, 1996] is surprisingly absent. TT employs a similar "heating" and "annealing" process as BDMC, using the final sample from the reverse sweep as initialization for the forward sweep and utilizing the ratio of importance weights as an acceptance criterion.  The authors’ observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is insightful, though not entirely novel. The paper could be strengthened by explicitly connecting this work to TT and discussing the similarities and differences more thoroughly. 

The technical contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel somewhat rushed, and the writing clarity suffers in these sections. Specifically, the definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" lacks precision.  It is unclear whether this refers to $\{z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}\}$ and the inconsistent use of "rev"/"fwd" versus "for"/"back" adds to the confusion. Additionally, the expectation in equations (5-7) and (9-11) lacks a clear definition of the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$.  Equation (6) as written contains identical distributions within the KL divergence. The meaning of “AutoBDMC” remains ambiguous; is it the convergence assessment *and* the fixed hyperparameter scheme detailed in section 3.2? 

The experiments, while demonstrating the method's practical application, could benefit from more systematic investigation. Exploring how the lower and upper bounds vary with the step size (Δβ) would be valuable.  The authors note the importance of a sufficiently small Δβ, but a more detailed study of this parameter is needed. The experimental setup lacks clarity regarding the choice of βt and the meaning of "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" in figures 2 and 3. The acronym RAIS should be defined in figure captions. The figure axes and tick labels could also be enlarged for improved readability. There are also a few minor typos (e.g., missing parentheses in the Jeffreys divergence, typo in the NUTS citation).

Despite these criticisms, this is a worthwhile paper. The proposed method has the potential to be a useful tool for evaluating and debugging probabilistic programming systems and MCMC algorithms. If the authors can address the issues of clarity and incorporate a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly Tempered Transitions, a positive recommendation is likely.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The method is conceptually simple and potentially impactful.
*   Implementation in Stan and WebPPL provides practical value.
*   Demonstrates a novel use of BDMC for assessing MCMC quality.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The technical contributions are somewhat modest.
*   The writing clarity is lacking in key sections.
*   The discussion of related work, particularly Tempered Transitions, is incomplete.
*   The experiments could be more thorough.
