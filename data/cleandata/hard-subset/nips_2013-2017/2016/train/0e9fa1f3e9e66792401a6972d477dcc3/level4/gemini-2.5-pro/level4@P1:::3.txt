This paper proposes a general method for evaluating MCMC transition operators, building on the recently introduced Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to perform two Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) runs—one from the prior to the posterior (forward) and one in reverse—and to interpret the accumulated importance weights as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The authors propose using the gap between these two bounds as a quantitative measure of MCMC performance. The main contributions are the implementation of this method for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference on real-world data.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its elegant and simple core idea. Using BDMC to assess the convergence of MCMC operators is intuitive and easy to implement. This approach could have a significant impact, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming systems, which are the paper's intended use case. Indeed, this method seems valuable enough that all such systems should consider providing an implementation.

However, I have some concerns regarding the novelty and depth of the technical contributions, as well as the discussion of related work.

My main concern is that the novelty relative to existing work on BDMC seems modest. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to explaining the BDMC method itself, which leaves little space for the paper's own contributions in Sections 3.1 and 3.2. Consequently, these sections feel rushed, and the writing quality declines, leading to several clarity issues noted below. The primary new insight appears to be that the gap between the BDMC bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate and true posteriors. While this is a very nice observation, its novelty is debatable.

This brings me to a key point about related work. A significant omission is Radford Neal's 1996 paper on Tempered Transitions (TT), which is highly relevant. The TT algorithm performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweeps as BDMC, but does so to define a "tempering" MCMC transition that can improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep, and the resulting sample is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. This acceptance ratio appears to be precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds derived in BDMC. In fact, Section 3.3 of Neal's paper interprets this ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function ratio from above and below, noting that the sampler achieves a 100% acceptance rate when these two estimates converge.

This connection to TT also raises questions about the choice of the annealing schedule (the `βt` values), a critical aspect that receives little attention in the paper. It is well-known in the TT literature that large steps in `Δβ = β{t+1} - βt` lead to poor acceptance rates, whereas steps that are too small waste computation. Analogously, the gap between the BDMC bounds will depend critically on `Δβ`. This provides a clear intuition: if the bounds are not tight enough, one must re-run the procedure with smaller steps. I suspect that any comparison between MCMC transition operators will be unreliable if the gap between the log bounds is large (e.g., >1 nat). When this occurs, `Δβ` should be reduced. The paper claims its method (BREAD) is an "automatic procedure", but it is unclear what justifies the "automatic" description. There is a missed opportunity here to propose a schedule for iteratively decreasing `Δβ` until a sufficiently small gap is achieved.

The experiments could also be more thorough. A systematic study showing how the lower and upper bounds vary with the step size `Δβ` (or equivalently, the number of steps `T`) would strengthen the paper's claims.

Despite these criticisms, I believe this is a promising paper. The proposed method is potentially very useful, and if the authors can convincingly argue that the issues with clarity and the discussion of related work can be addressed in the final version, I would be inclined to argue for acceptance.

**Clarity Issues and Questions:**

*   The definition of `v` as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise. Does this refer to `{x1,...,xT-1}`? This is ambiguous because the algorithm involves two separate AIS sweeps.
*   The notation for the forward and reverse chains is inconsistent. The text uses "fwd" and "rev" in some places (e.g., `q_fwd`), while the reviewer's corrected version of Eq. 6 seems to imply "for" and "back". Is this distinction meaningful or an oversight?
*   It is not specified what estimator is being used in the expectations in equations (6-8) and (9-11), which makes the derivations difficult to follow. Are the authors referring to `E[log ˆR]` and `E[log ˆRrev]`?
*   Equation (7), as written, appears to be incorrect, as the KL divergence is between two identical distributions. Based on the surrounding context, I suspect the terms inside the KL divergence should be `qfwd(v,xT)` and `pT(xT)qrev(v|xT)`. Please clarify.

**Experimental Details:**

*   It is not clear how the annealing schedule `βt` is chosen. After inspecting the appendix, I found `var step = 1/options.steps`, which suggests a linear schedule. Is the method parameterized simply by the total number of steps `T`? This should be explicitly stated.
*   In Figures 1 and 2, what does the x-axis label "AIS steps" or "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" represent? Does this refer to the number of annealing steps `T`, or the number of MCMC transitions at each temperature?
*   The acronyms in Figure 2, such as RAIS and (u)c(R)AIS, are not defined in the caption. I assume these refer to (un)collapsed variants of reverse and forward AIS, but this should be made explicit.
*   The font size for the axes and tick labels in the figures is too small and should be increased for readability.
