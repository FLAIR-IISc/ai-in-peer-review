- Proposes BREAD: extends BDMC using forward/reverse AIS on simulated data to compute an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior.  
- Integrated BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating utility for model-representation comparisons and debugging inference implementations.  
- Notes the general idea of validating posterior simulators with simulated data is not new and should be related to prior work (e.g., Geweke 2004; Cook et al. 2012).  
- Strength: Using BDMC to quantitatively diagnose MCMC sampler performance is a significant contribution and more rigorous than common heuristic diagnostics.  
- Strength: The method is a valuable practical debugging tool for probabilistic programming systems, evidenced by finding a bug in WebPPL’s multivariate Gaussian sampler.  
- Strength: Effectively used to empirically study trade-offs between model representations (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization).  
- Weakness: Major clarity issues in Section 3.1—technical derivation is difficult to follow and hinders understanding of the estimator.  
- Weakness: Paper should better situate originality within existing literature on posterior predictive checks and simulation-based Bayesian software validation.  
- Weakness: Lacks a single, clear, self-contained description of the full BREAD protocol; the procedure is fragmented across sections.  
- Comment: Notation (e.g., q(.)) must be defined before use and Section 3.1 should be understandable without consulting the appendix.  
- Comment: Should explicitly cite and discuss Geweke (2004) and Cook et al. (2012) when framing validation via simulated data.  
- Minor stylistic comments: simplify unobservables notation (z vs. theta) for readability and fix small typos (e.g., missing parenthesis on line 165).
