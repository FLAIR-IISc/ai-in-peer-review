

This paper introduces a method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which leverages two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps to compute lower and upper bounds on marginal likelihood. The authors propose the gap between these bounds as a generic convergence metric, with implementations for Stan and WebPPL and a protocol for posterior inference quality assessment.  

**Strengths:** The core idea—using the BDMC bound gap as a simple, implementable performance measure—is elegant and potentially high-impact, especially for probabilistic programming systems (PPS). The approach is straightforward to integrate into PPS, making it broadly applicable.  

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1. **Lack of Novelty and Depth:** The primary technical contribution—using the bound gap to estimate Jensen-Shannon divergence—is not novel. Neal’s 1996 Tempered Transitions (TT) method [1] already establishes that the acceptance ratio (equivalent to the bound ratio in BDMC) bounds log Z_T - log Z_1, and TT accepts with probability 1 when bounds converge. The authors omit this critical connection, missing an opportunity to contextualize their work. BDMC’s core mechanics (reverse/forward sweeps, bound interpretation) are directly derived from TT, yet the paper fails to cite Neal’s foundational work or acknowledge the overlap. This undermines the perceived novelty.  
2. **Insufficient Technical Treatment:** Sections 3.1–3.2 are rushed, with unclear definitions and inconsistent notation (e.g., "rev"/"fwd" vs. "for"/"back"). The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled before the final stage" is ambiguous; it likely refers to $\{z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}\}$, but this is not specified. AutoBDMC is never unambiguously defined—does it imply (a) using the bound gap for convergence assessment or (b) the fixed hyperparameter scheme in Section 3.2? The "auto" aspect remains unclear, though an iterative Δβ scheduling strategy (e.g., decreasing Δβ until bounds converge) would strengthen the method.  
3. **Superficial Experiments:** The paper lacks a systematic analysis of how the bound gap depends on Δβ (β_{t+1} - β_t). The authors note that large Δβ causes unreliable comparisons (>1 nat gap), yet experiments do not explore this. Figure captions use undefined acronyms (e.g., RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS) and omit β_t scheduling details (e.g., "step = 1/options.steps" implies β_t = 1/T for T steps, but this should be stated explicitly).  

**Clarity and Presentation Issues:**  
- Equations 5–7 and 9–11 lack definition of $\hat{p}(y)$; the intended expressions likely involve expectations of log $\hat{R}_{\text{rev}}$ and log $\hat{R}_{\text{fwd}}$.  
- Equation 6 is incorrect (KL divergence between identical distributions); it should reference $p(z, \theta | y)$ and $q_{\text{back}}(\vec{v} | z, \theta, y)$ as in Equations 4 and 10.  
- Axes labels in Figures 2–3 are too small; tick labels require enlargement.  
- Typos: Missing closing parentheses in D_JS terms; "Homan" (NUTS citation) → "Hoffman".  

**Recommendation:** The method itself has merit, but the paper’s failure to engage with TT literature, superficial technical treatment, and unresolved clarity issues significantly weaken its contribution. A compelling response addressing the TT citation, clarifying AutoBDMC, and adding a Δβ sensitivity analysis could justify acceptance. Without these, the work appears derivative with modest technical depth. Given the high-impact potential of the core idea, I lean toward *weak acceptance* contingent on the authors’ ability to resolve these concerns in camera-ready form.  

**References**
