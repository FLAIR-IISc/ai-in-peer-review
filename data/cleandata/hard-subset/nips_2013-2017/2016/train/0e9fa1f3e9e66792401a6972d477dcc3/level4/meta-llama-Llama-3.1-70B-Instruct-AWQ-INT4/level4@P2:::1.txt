This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler and the target distribution of interest, with a particular eye towards debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The idea of using BDMC to diagnose failures of a sampler to converge seems potentially quite useful since existing MCMC diagnostics are indeed problematic.

The authors provide a clear summary of the main ideas and relate them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. They also discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, addressing the criteria described in the qualitative evaluation section. The paper is well-written, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the topic.

The main contribution of the paper is the extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors show that by running annealed importance sampling chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they can upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. They also integrate their method into two PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, it addresses a significant problem in the field of probabilistic inference, namely the difficulty of measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples. Secondly, the authors provide a rigorous and general method for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference, which can be used to guide the design of inference algorithms. Finally, the paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the technical details.

However, there are also some weaknesses. One major issue is the complexity of the derivation in Section 3.1, which may be difficult for some readers to follow. Additionally, the authors could provide more guidance on how to apply the method from start to finish, as the reader has to piece this together from different sections of the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on previous work on bidirectional Monte Carlo, but the authors provide a new application of this technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The paper also provides a new protocol for using this technique to analyze sampler accuracy on real-world data.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the accuracy and reliability of probabilistic inference methods. By providing a rigorous and general method for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference, the authors can help to increase the confidence of users in the results produced by PPLs.

In conclusion, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, and it has the potential to make a significant impact on the development of more accurate and reliable inference methods. However, the authors could improve the clarity of the derivation and provide more guidance on how to apply the method.
