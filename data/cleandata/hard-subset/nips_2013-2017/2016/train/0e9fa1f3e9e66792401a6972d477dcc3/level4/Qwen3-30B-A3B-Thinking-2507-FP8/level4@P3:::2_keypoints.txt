- Introduces BREAD, extending bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference quality.  
- Shows BDMC can upper bound the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between true posterior and approximate sample distribution.  
- Gives rigorous theoretical analysis proving the estimator upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Demonstrates exact computation of both true Jeffreys divergence and the bound on small examples to validate accuracy.  
- Proposes a protocol for real-world data by simulating datasets from fitted hyperparameters and checking consistency with real data.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, enabling practical use in popular probabilistic programming systems.  
- Empirical evaluation covers five realistic models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, time series, mixture-of-Gaussians) on real and simulated data.  
- Shows inference behavior on simulated data with fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on real data, supporting the protocol.  
- Uses careful visualization (e.g., AIS quartiles) and provides necessary mathematical details in main text and supplement.  
- Novelty: significant conceptual extension of BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior sample quality assessment.  
- Practical significance: provides a rigorous, automatic procedure for assessing black-box inference quality and aids model-representation choices.  
- Demonstrated utility: helped discover a bug in WebPPL, showing usefulness as a debugging tool.  
- Limitation: method requires access to exact posterior samples, typically restricting applicability to simulated data.  
- Limitation: protocol for real data depends on quality of fitted hyperparameters without formal analysis of approximation error.  
- Limitation: primary focus on AIS and SMC, with limited exploration of applicability to other MCMC methods (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings, Gibbs).  
- Weakness: lacks comprehensive empirical comparison with existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, effective sample size).  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound, practically implemented, and valuable contribution; recommend acceptance.
