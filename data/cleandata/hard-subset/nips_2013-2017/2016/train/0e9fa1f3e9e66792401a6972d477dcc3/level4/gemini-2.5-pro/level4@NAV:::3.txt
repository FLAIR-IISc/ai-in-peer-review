This paper introduces a general framework for assessing the performance of MCMC transition operators, leveraging the recently developed bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique. BDMC employs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—analogous to those in Tempered Transitions—and recasts the resulting importance weights to form a stochastic lower and upper bound on the marginal likelihood. The core proposal of this work is to utilize the gap between these BDMC bounds as a universal metric for the quality of an MCMC operator. The primary technical contributions consist of implementing BDMC within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming frameworks and establishing a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

The manuscript presents a mix of significant strengths and notable weaknesses. The principal strength lies in the elegant simplicity of using the BDMC gap to diagnose MCMC convergence. This idea is straightforward to implement, suggesting the work could have a substantial impact. Its utility is especially pronounced for probabilistic programming systems, the paper's target application, and it is a feature that all such systems would benefit from incorporating.

My main reservations concern the novelty and technical depth of the contributions. While the authors correctly cite the original BDMC preprint, a significant portion of the paper is dedicated to re-explaining this existing method. This leaves the new contributions in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling compressed, and the quality of the writing unfortunately diminishes in these sections, leading to clarity issues detailed below. Setting aside these clarity issues, the technical contributions are somewhat modest. Beyond the original BDMC work, the primary novel insight appears to be that the gap between the bounds itself constitutes an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. While this is a valuable observation, its complete novelty is debatable.

This leads to a point regarding related work. A significant omission is the lack of any reference to Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], a highly relevant method. This citation is also absent from the original BDMC manuscript, raising the question of whether the authors are aware of this prior art. If so, this oversight represents a missed opportunity to draw powerful connections between the two methods. TT utilizes the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweeps as BDMC, but its objective is to create a "tempering" MCMC move to improve mixing across modes. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep, and the resulting sample is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of the accumulated importance weights. Crucially, the acceptance probability of a TT move appears to be precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds derived in BDMC. Indeed, Section 3.3 of Neal's paper interprets this acceptance ratio via two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function difference, noting that the acceptance probability approaches 1 as the two estimates converge.

The parallel to TT also highlights the need for a more detailed discussion on the choice of the inverse temperature schedule, {βt}. It is well-established in the TT literature that the step size Δβ = β{t+1}-βt presents a critical trade-off: large steps lead to low acceptance rates, while small steps are computationally inefficient. The BDMC bounds are similarly sensitive to this schedule; if the gap is too wide, the logical solution is to rerun the algorithm with smaller steps. I hypothesize that comparisons between different MCMC operators become unreliable if the log-space gap between the bounds exceeds approximately 1 nat, necessitating a finer temperature schedule. The paper does not clearly define what the "auto" in AutoBDMC signifies. This presents an opportunity to develop an adaptive scheme that iteratively refines the temperature schedule until the bounds are sufficiently tight for reliable diagnosis. In this context, the experiments could be more comprehensive; a systematic study illustrating how the lower and upper bounds change as a function of the step size would be beneficial.

Despite these criticisms, this is a promising paper. As stated, the proposed method has the potential for significant utility. My recommendation leans toward acceptance, contingent on the authors convincingly demonstrating that the issues of clarity and the discussion of related work can be thoroughly addressed in the final version.

**Clarity**
*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous. It presumably refers to ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, but this should be clarified for both of the AIS sweeps in the algorithm. This ambiguity is compounded by the inconsistent use of "rev" and "fwd" versus "for" and "back"; it is unclear if this distinction is intentional.
*   The estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ is not defined in the context of the expectations in equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to understand their derivation. Should these be written as expectations of the log-ratios, i.e., E[log $\hat{R}_{rev}$] and E[log $\hat{R}_{fwd}$]?
*   As written, Equation (6) is incorrect, as the distributions within the KL divergence are identical. Based on Equations (4) and (10), the right-hand term should likely be something of the form p(z, θ | y) q_back(v | z, θ, y).
*   The paper never explicitly defines AutoBDMC. It seems to refer to (a) using the bound difference to assess convergence and (b) employing the fixed hyperparameter scheme from Section 3.2, but this should be stated clearly.

**Experiments**
*   The method for choosing βt is not explained. The appendix code suggests `var step = 1/options.steps`, implying a uniform schedule where AutoBDMC is parameterized by the total number of steps T. This should be explicitly stated in the main text.
*   In Figures 2 and 3, it is unclear what "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" refers to. Does this value correspond to the number of steps T used for the BDMC run?
*   The acronym RAIS is used in figure captions without prior definition (presumably Reverse AIS). Similarly, the variants (u)c(R)AIS are not explained (presumably (un)collapsed).
*   The legibility of plots would be improved by increasing the font size of the axes and tick labels.

**Typos**
*   In the expression for D_JS, two closing parentheses are missing from the DKL terms.
*   In the NUTS citation, "Homan" should be "Hoffman".
