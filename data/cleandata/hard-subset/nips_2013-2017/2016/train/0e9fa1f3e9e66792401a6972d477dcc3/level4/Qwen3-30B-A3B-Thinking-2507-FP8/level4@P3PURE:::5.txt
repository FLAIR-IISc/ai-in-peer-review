

The authors address the problem of assessing MCMC reliability and comparing scheme efficiency by leveraging upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood derived from the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. Specifically, they propose selecting MCMC schemes based on the tightness of BMC bounds achieved within equivalent computational effort. A central challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to yield a valid stochastic upper bound. To circumvent this, the authors introduce heuristics: for Bayesian posterior sampling, they perform diagnostics on the posterior induced by synthetic data (generated from an assumed hyperparameter value η_real) rather than the true data, exploiting the premise that exact sampling is easier for such synthetic posteriors. The methodology is evaluated on illustrative examples.  

While the idea of using BMC-derived stochastic bounds for theoretically grounded MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing, the proposed approach faces significant implementation drawbacks that undermine its appeal. These issues stem directly from the heuristics employed to address BMC’s requirement for an exact posterior sample. Three critical concerns arise:  

1.  The method assumes knowledge of η_real—a hyperparameter value representative of the *entire* posterior—obtained via a previously run MCMC algorithm. This assumes both (a) that the prior MCMC algorithm had converged (the very problem being diagnosed) and (b) that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single hyperparameter value, both of which are generally unjustified.  
2.  The approach assumes the synthetic posterior (from data generated under η_real) shares convergence properties with the original posterior. This is an unverified assumption, particularly critical in cases of model misspecification, where the synthetic posterior may exhibit qualitatively different behavior. The authors effectively "change the problem" by diagnosing a different posterior.  
3.  The method adopts an optimistic stance that starting from η_real enables the MCMC algorithm of interest to rapidly produce nearly exact samples from the posterior. While potentially true in some cases, this is not guaranteed and introduces further unverified assumptions.  

Crucially, all these assumptions are intrinsically linked to the very convergence properties the method aims to assess for the original MCMC algorithm. Although Section 5.1 demonstrates the method’s applicability in simple examples where these issues do not distort conclusions, such limited cases are insufficient to establish robustness across more complex scenarios. The authors critique existing diagnostics for failing in multimodal settings or when summary statistics are insufficiently expressive (lines 203–205). They should explicitly explain why their method overcomes these limitations and provide evidence, particularly in such challenging cases.  

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from comparative simulations against standard diagnostic tools (e.g., those in Section 4) to demonstrate concrete advantages in scenarios where existing methods fail. Without such evidence, the proposed diagnostic’s practical utility remains unsubstantiated.
