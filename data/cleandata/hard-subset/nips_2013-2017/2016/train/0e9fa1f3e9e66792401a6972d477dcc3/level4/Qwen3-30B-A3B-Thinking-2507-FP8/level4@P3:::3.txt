

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference by extending bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior distributions. The authors demonstrate that the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood (derived from forward and reverse annealed importance sampling) provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence. They implement BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, and propose a protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters.

The paper builds on Grosse et al.'s BDMC framework (2015), which provided stochastic bounds on marginal likelihood. The key insight is recognizing that the difference between these bounds upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. While related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) also leverages BDMC for inference evaluation, their approach uses a reference distribution rather than the true posterior, making the authors' analysis more theoretically grounded for simulated data. The connection to Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which uses similar annealing steps for improved MCMC mixing, is notably absent from the manuscript and related work discussion. This omission is significant as both methods share the same core annealing structure, though with different objectives.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-supported by theoretical derivations in Section 3.1, though some aspects of the mathematical presentation could be clearer (e.g., inconsistent labeling of forward/reverse chains, ambiguous definition of the intermediate variable $\vec{v}$). The experimental validation in Section 5 is thorough, with careful analysis of consistency between real and simulated data and the robustness of the hyperparameter fitting protocol. The method's implementation in two major probabilistic programming systems demonstrates practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper would benefit from improved exposition of key technical points. The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled before the final stage" is ambiguous given the dual AIS sweeps. The relationship between the gap between bounds and Jeffreys divergence could be introduced more clearly earlier in the paper. The acronyms "RAIS" and "(u)c(R)AIS" appear without definition in figures. The manuscript would be significantly improved with more precise mathematical notation and a clearer explanation of the "AutoBDMC" protocol (which appears to involve automatic adjustment of annealing steps).

**Originality:** The core technical contribution—recognizing the gap between BDMC bounds as an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence—is novel and valuable. However, the presentation of this contribution is somewhat buried within the paper's exposition of BDMC, and the novelty isn't sufficiently emphasized. The connection to Tempered Transitions (Neal, 1996), which uses identical annealing procedures for a different purpose, would better contextualize the novelty.

**Significance:** The method addresses a critical need in probabilistic programming: providing reliable, automated evaluation of inference quality for non-expert users. The authors successfully demonstrate how BREAD can guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and even reveal implementation bugs (Section 5.2.2). This potential impact on the usability of probabilistic programming systems makes the work highly significant for practitioners.

**Strengths:**
- Elegant, simple idea with clear practical utility
- Demonstrated implementation in two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Practical protocol for applying to real-world data
- Potential to improve reliability of inference in probabilistic programming
- Clear demonstration of the method's utility through model representation experiments

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient emphasis on novelty relative to BDMC and Tempered Transitions
- Some technical details presented too hastily (e.g., Section 3.1 derivations)
- Missing connection to Neal's Tempered Transitions work
- Ambiguous notation and terminology in key mathematical sections
- Limited exploration of automatic annealing schedule selection

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper presents a practical, implementable solution to a significant problem in probabilistic programming. The method is theoretically sound, well-validated, and has clear potential for broad adoption. The demonstration of the method's utility in debugging (Section 5.2.2) is particularly compelling.

*Con:* The novelty is somewhat overstated relative to existing BDMC work, and the connection to Tempered Transitions is a significant omission. The mathematical presentation could be substantially improved for clarity.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, addressing a real pain point for probabilistic programming users. While the novelty could be better articulated and the technical exposition improved, the core idea is strong and practically significant. With minor revisions to clarify the novelty, fix mathematical presentation issues, and properly contextualize the work relative to Tempered Transitions, this would be an excellent contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
