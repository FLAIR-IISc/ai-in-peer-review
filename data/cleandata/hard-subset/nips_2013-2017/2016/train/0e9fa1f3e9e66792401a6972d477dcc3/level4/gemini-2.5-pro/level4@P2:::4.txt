### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of assessing the quality of samples from Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms. The authors propose a method, which they term BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), to provide a quantitative measure of MCMC reliability. The approach extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which uses Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the expected gap between these upper and lower bounds is itself an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate sample distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior distribution.

Since the reverse AIS pass required for the upper bound necessitates an exact sample from the posterior, the method is technically limited to simulated data where the ground truth is known. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these inferred hyperparameters; and third, apply BREAD to measure inference quality on this synthetic data. The authors provide compelling experimental evidence that inference behavior on data generated this way is a good proxy for behavior on the original real-world data.

The method is implemented and validated within two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The authors demonstrate its utility not only for validating inference but also for guiding practical decisions, such as comparing the performance trade-offs of different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers) and for debugging the implementation of an inference engine.

**Strengths (Reasons for acceptance)**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly significant and challenging problem. Reliable, automated convergence diagnostics are a major bottleneck for the widespread and safe use of probabilistic programming systems, especially by non-experts. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative metric for sample quality, moving beyond common heuristics like the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic, which can fail to detect certain modes of non-convergence.

2.  **Originality:** The connection drawn between the BDMC log-likelihood gap and the Jeffreys divergence is an elegant and novel contribution. While BDMC was introduced for marginal likelihood estimation, repurposing it to bound a divergence metric between the approximate and true posteriors is a clever and impactful insight.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound is clear, and the proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data is well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors validate their method across five different models in Stan, demonstrate consistency between real and simulated data, and provide a compelling use-case in comparing model representations for matrix factorization in both Stan and WebPPL. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a strong testament to the method's practical utility.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for improvement)**

1.  **Clarity of Contributions:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound in Section 3.1 appears to be the central theoretical contribution of the paper. However, this is not explicitly stated. The paper would be strengthened if the authors clearly demarcated this as a novel result, as the paper's primary contribution hinges on it.

2.  **Clarity of Exposition in Section 3:** While the paper is generally well-written, Section 3 is more difficult to follow than the rest. The transition into Section 3.1 is abrupt, launching directly into equations without sufficient context or motivation. A short introductory paragraph explaining the goal of the section—to connect the BDMC bounds to a divergence metric—would significantly improve readability.

3.  **Clarity of Method Definition:** The name of the method, "BREAD", is introduced, but its precise definition could be made more explicit. It seems to encompass both the theoretical divergence bound and the practical protocol for real-world data. A clear, concise definition early on would be helpful for the reader.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Notational Consistency:** The paper alternates between using `y` and `D` to refer to observations/data. Standardizing this would improve clarity.
*   **Code and Reproducibility:** The authors have implemented their method in WebPPL and Stan, which is commendable. To maximize the impact of this work, I strongly encourage the authors to release their code in a user-friendly format. For example, the WebPPL extension could be released as an `npm` package, and similar packaging could be considered for the Stan implementation. At a minimum, the supplemental material should clearly state which versions of the languages the code is compatible with and provide clear instructions for integration.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, useful, and well-validated method for a significant problem in the machine learning community. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and can be addressed with minor revisions. I recommend acceptance.
