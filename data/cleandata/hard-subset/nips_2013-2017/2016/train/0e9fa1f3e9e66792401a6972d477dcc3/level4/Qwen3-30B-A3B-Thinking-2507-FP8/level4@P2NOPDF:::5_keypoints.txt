- Proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and choose schemes with tightest bounds under fixed cost.  
- Core challenge: BMC requires an exact posterior sample to produce valid upper bounds, which is generally unavailable.  
- Authors address this by diagnostics on posteriors from synthetic data generated with a pre-specified hyperparameter η_real.  
- Major flaw: assumes η_real (representative of the true posterior) can be known a priori, which requires a converged MCMC—precisely the problem being diagnosed.  
- Unjustified simplification: summarizing a complex posterior by a single hyperparameter η_real is generally invalid for non-trivial posteriors.  
- Problematic assumption: synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the true posterior, which may fail under model misspecification.  
- Circularity: assuming that initializing MCMC at η_real quickly yields an almost-exact sample from the true posterior depends on the very convergence properties the method aims to test.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations but provide only limited, simple examples that do not demonstrate robustness in complex settings.  
- No clear argument or evidence that BMC-based diagnostics succeed where standard diagnostics fail (e.g., multimodal targets or low-summary-statistic cases).  
- Recommendations: need rigorous justification/sensitivity analysis for η_real and synthetic-data approach, explicit treatment of model misspecification, and comparative simulations against standard diagnostics.  
- Conclusion: novel and theoretically appealing idea, but heavy reliance on unverified assumptions and limited empirical validation undermines practical credibility.
