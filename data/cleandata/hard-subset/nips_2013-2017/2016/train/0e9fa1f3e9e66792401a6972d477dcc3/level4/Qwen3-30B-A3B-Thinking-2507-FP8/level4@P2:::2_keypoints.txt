- Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to rigorously evaluate MCMC posterior inference by bounding divergence.  
- Builds on Grosse et al. (2015) to derive an estimator that upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence.  
- Contribution 1: Theoretical analysis showing BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence.  
- Contribution 2: Empirical validation of the bound on toy distributions where exact calculations are possible.  
- Contribution 3: Protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating datasets from fitted hyperparameters.  
- Contribution 4: Integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, with validation across multiple probabilistic models.  
- Relevance: Addresses a critical need in probabilistic programming for reliable assessment of approximate posterior samples.  
- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous derivations and careful experimental validation; limitations are acknowledged.  
- Clarity: Well-written and organized, with clear explanations and helpful figures (especially Figures 1–2).  
- Originality: Novel application of BDMC to evaluate MCMC inference quality and clear comparison to related work.  
- Significance: Provides a general automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality and can aid non-expert users; found a real implementation bug.  
- Strength: Provides a rigorous, empirically supported method for evaluating MCMC inference quality.  
- Strength: Practical integration into major probabilistic programming systems demonstrates real-world applicability.  
- Strength: Proposed real-data protocol addresses key limitations of prior methods.  
- Weakness: Limited to inference algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC).  
- Weakness: Real-data protocol depends on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters produce representative simulated data.  
- Weakness: Scalability to very large models or datasets is not explored.  
- Pro-accept: Solves an important unsolved problem with theoretical guarantees, empirical validation, practical integrations, and clear adoption potential.  
- Con-accept: Applicability restricted to specific algorithm classes and requires per-model validation of the real-data protocol.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept (paper is high-quality, significant, and practically useful).
