Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC). The method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), runs AIS forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data to obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on log marginal likelihood that sandwich the true value as computation increases, and shows that the gap between bounds upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior. The authors also propose a practical protocol for real datasets: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from those hyperparameters, and use reverse AIS initialized from an approximate posterior sample over hyperparameters to approximate the exact posterior sample required by BDMC. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate the approach on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, mixture of Gaussians), demonstrate that simulated-data behavior mirrors real data, and use BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed model representations and to uncover a bug in WebPPL.

This work builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), SMC samplers (Del Moral et al., 2006), stochastic bounds on partition functions (Burda et al., 2015), and connects to convergence diagnostics (Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Brooks and Gelman, 1998) and alternative sample quality measures (Gorham and Mackey, 2015). It is contemporaneous with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), who introduce “subjective divergence,” closely related to BDMC but with a reference distribution rather than the true posterior; the present work clarifies guarantees when the true posterior is used and gives a practical real-data protocol.

Quality:
- Theory: The derivations that the forward AIS lower bound and reverse AIS upper bound imply an expectation upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) and pT(xT) are sound and clearly linked to BDMC’s extended-state-space analysis. The use of marginalization to relate bound gaps to KL terms is correct. The guarantee is in expectation, not high probability, which is acknowledged.
- Practical protocol: The fitted-hyperparameter simulation protocol is well-motivated; experiments varying the number of MCMC steps used to obtain approximate posterior hyperparameters suggest robustness. The demonstration that forward AIS behavior on simulated data mirrors that on real data across five models increases confidence in practical utility.
- Experiments: The study spans multiple models and two probabilistic programming systems. The matrix factorization case study shows BREAD’s ability to inform representation choices. The debugging case is compelling.
- Caveats: The bound is only rigorous for simulated data and requires at least one exact posterior draw; the real-data protocol replaces this with an approximation, without a formal guarantee. The analysis focuses on AIS; SMC is discussed but not empirically evaluated. The estimator variance can be high; the paper notes outliers and reports quartiles rather than averages, but does not provide guidance on budget vs reliability or confidence intervals for the divergence bound. Sensitivity to annealing schedule, number of intermediate distributions, and choice of transition kernels is not systematically studied.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear background section and careful notation in the main derivation. A few suggestions:
- Provide a concise algorithmic summary (pseudo-code) of BREAD to aid reproducibility.
- State earlier and more prominently that the divergence bound is in expectation and only rigorous for simulated data with an exact posterior sample.
- Offer practical guidance on annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and diagnostics for when reverse initialization is inadequate.
- Report runtimes and wall-clock-normalized comparisons, not only “number of steps,” especially for the collapsed vs uncollapsed study.
- Minor typos are present; a careful proofreading would help.

Originality:
The core technical novelty is modest but meaningful: repurposing BDMC to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions, and packaging this as BREAD with an explicit real-data protocol. The integration into WebPPL and Stan and the diagnostic/debugging application are practical contributions. The relationship to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriately acknowledged and distinguished.

Significance:
Reliable, quantitative evaluation of MCMC-based approximate inference is an important and long-standing challenge. Providing an expectation upper bound on a symmetric divergence is valuable for both researchers and practitioners, especially within probabilistic programming. The ability to compare model representations and uncover implementation bugs is practically significant. Limitations include the need for simulatable data, the expectation-only nature of the bound, and a focus on AIS. Nonetheless, the contribution is likely to influence evaluation practices and tool development.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical link from BDMC to an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS-like samplers.
- Practical protocol bridging simulated and real data, with empirical validation across diverse models.
- Integration into two widely used probabilistic programming systems; useful case study and a real debugging success.
- Addresses an important practical gap in MCMC diagnostics with a method that can be automated.

Cons:
- Guarantees are limited to simulated data; real-data protocol lacks formal bounds.
- High-variance estimators; limited guidance on schedules, budgets, and confidence assessment.
- Empirical focus almost exclusively on AIS; no SMC results despite applicability claim.
- Results largely report steps rather than comparable compute budgets; limited sensitivity analyses.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Can you provide guidelines for choosing annealing schedules and number of intermediate distributions to stabilize the bound and reduce variance?
- How does BREAD perform on strongly multimodal posteriors where AIS often struggles? A stress test would be informative.
- Could you quantify uncertainty of the bound gap (e.g., via multiple reverse initializations or variance reduction techniques)?
- Do you foresee a principled way to extend guarantees to the fitted-hyperparameter protocol (e.g., bounding the error introduced by approximate reverse initialization)?
- Given the extended-state framework, can you include at least one SMC-based experiment?
- Please report wall-clock time and effective sample size per unit time where applicable, to complement “steps.”

Recommendation:
Accept. The paper addresses a timely and important problem with a principled method grounded in BDMC, provides a clear theoretical result, and demonstrates practical utility in widely used probabilistic programming systems. While the guarantees are limited to simulated data and the empirical scope could be broadened, the contribution is solid and will be useful to the community.
