Summary
This paper tackles the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. Building on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), the authors propose Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), which uses forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) to sandwich the log marginal likelihood and thereby upper-bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The method applies directly to AIS/SMC-style algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling on an extended state space. Recognizing that BDMC requires an exact posterior sample for the reverse bound, the authors propose a practical protocol for real datasets: infer hyperparameters on the real data, simulate synthetic data from these inferred hyperparameters, and run BDMC on the simulated data, initializing the reverse chain from an approximate posterior obtained by short additional MCMC. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and present experiments on several standard models, as well as a case study comparing collapsed versus uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization. They also show how BREAD can help detect implementation bugs.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing: uses theoretically grounded stochastic upper/lower bounds to assess inference quality.
- Clear technical contribution: the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence for simulated data.
- Practical integration: implementations in WebPPL and Stan, with illustrative examples and a compelling debugging anecdote.
- Potential utility: can inform modeling and algorithmic choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations).

Weaknesses and concerns
The main limitations stem from the need for an exact posterior sample to obtain a valid reverse bound. The proposed workaround—using fitted hyperparameters and synthetic data—introduces strong assumptions that are closely related to the very convergence properties the method seeks to assess.

1) Reliance on a representative point estimate for hyperparameters (η_real).
- The protocol assumes access to a hyperparameter setting η_real (e.g., from prior MCMC runs) that is representative of the posterior.
- This presupposes that the algorithm used to obtain η_real has converged—essentially the original problem of interest.
- It further assumes that the posterior over hyperparameters can be well summarized by a single value, which is generally unjustified.

2) Assumption that synthetic-data posteriors mimic the real-data posterior for convergence behavior.
- The method evaluates inference on synthetic data generated from η_real and assumes this is indicative of performance on the real dataset.
- This is not guaranteed, especially under model misspecification or weakly informative priors, and risks diagnosing a different posterior than the one of interest.

3) Optimistic initialization of the reverse chain.
- Starting the reverse chain from η_real and running a small number of MCMC transitions is assumed to yield an almost-exact posterior sample.
- This need not hold, particularly in complex, multimodal, or poorly mixing scenarios, and undermines the theoretical guarantee of the reverse bound.

While Section 5.1 provides examples suggesting these issues may not be problematic in some settings, the presented cases are limited. The evidence is not strong enough to claim that the heuristic protocol will be reliable in more challenging problems.

Relation to prior work and requested comparisons
The paper argues that standard convergence diagnostics can fail, e.g., in multimodal settings or when based on insufficient summaries. The authors should explain why BREAD fares better in these scenarios and provide empirical evidence. In particular:
- Include controlled experiments comparing BREAD to common diagnostics (e.g., R̂, effective sample size, autocorrelation-based diagnostics, and multichain convergence tests) on challenging targets (multimodal, heavy-tailed, ill-conditioned).
- Demonstrate cases where BREAD provides a more reliable or more conservative assessment than these baselines.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The derivations for the expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence are clear and sound for simulated data with exact posterior samples. However, the key practical contributions depend on heuristic approximations that weaken the formal guarantees. The experimental validation is helpful but limited in scope.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The links between BDMC, AIS/SMC, and the Jeffreys divergence are clearly presented. The limitations of the real-data protocol should be emphasized more prominently.
- Originality: Extending BDMC to upper-bound the Jeffreys divergence for AIS-generated samples, and integrating this into PPLs, is novel. The proposed real-data protocol is a pragmatic idea, though heuristic.
- Significance: Potentially useful for benchmarking AIS/SMC-based inference and for PPL development and debugging. Broader impact on general MCMC convergence assessment is limited by the reliance on synthetic data and approximations to exact posterior samples.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical or empirical conditions under which the synthetic-data protocol preserves the relative ranking of inference algorithms or representations.
- Conduct sensitivity analyses: vary η_real, the number of reverse-chain MCMC steps, and model misspecification, and quantify the impact on the bounds and conclusions.
- Compare directly against standard diagnostics across a suite of hard targets; highlight scenarios where those diagnostics are misleading and BREAD remains informative.
- Calibrate the bounds: assess tightness of the expected bound versus true Jeffreys divergence on additional toy problems, including multimodal cases.
- Clarify the scope: frame claims more narrowly as assessing AIS/SMC sample quality on simulated data and as a tool for PPL development and algorithm selection.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical foundation for simulated data; clear contribution connecting BDMC gaps to Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical implementations and demonstrations, including insightful case studies and a debugging success.
- Potentially valuable tool for PPL users/developers and for comparing model representations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical protocol for real data relies on strong, unvalidated assumptions (representative η_real, synthetic-data fidelity, near-exact reverse initialization).
- Limited empirical evidence, with few and relatively simple examples; no direct comparison to common diagnostics.
- Applicability constrained to AIS/SMC-style samplers and diagnostics based on marginal likelihood rather than general MCMC workflows.
