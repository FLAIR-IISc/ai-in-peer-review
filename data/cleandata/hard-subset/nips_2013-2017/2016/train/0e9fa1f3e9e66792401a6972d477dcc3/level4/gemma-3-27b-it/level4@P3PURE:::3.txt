The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps – essentially the same as those used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Stat & Comp 1996] – but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators, alongside implementations of BDMC for Stan and WebPPL, and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

This work has significant strengths. Using BDMC to assess MCMC operator convergence is a beautifully simple and easily implemented idea, potentially offering high impact, particularly within probabilistic programming systems. Implementing this method within such systems would be a valuable contribution. 

However, my main concerns relate to the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. While the authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC and wisely dedicate space to reiterating its description, sections 3.1 and 3.2, presenting the main contributions, feel somewhat rushed, and the writing quality declines in these sections, leading to clarity issues (detailed below). Beyond this, the technical contributions themselves are somewhat modest. The primary novelty appears to be the observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence – a nice observation, though arguably not entirely novel.

This leads to a question regarding related work. The authors curiously omit Radford Neal’s work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which is highly relevant. This omission also appears in the BDMC manuscript, raising the question of whether the authors are aware of it.  If not, this represents a missed opportunity to highlight connections between the two methods. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but aims to “temper” the MCMC transition move to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep, and the final forward sweep sample serves as a proposal accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. As far as I can tell, this acceptance ratio is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Section 3.3 of Neal’s 1996 paper interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of thermodynamic integration estimates bounding log ZT – log Z1, noting convergence implies an acceptance probability of 1.  

The analogy with TT also suggests a need for more attention to the choice of βt values. In TT, excessively large steps in Δβ = β{t+1}-βt degrade acceptance rates, while too-small steps waste computation. The BDMC bound difference will also depend on Δβ, providing clear intuitions.  Insufficiently close bounds necessitate rerunning BDMC with smaller Δβ.  Comparisons between MCMC operators are likely unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds approximately 1 nat, again suggesting a reduction in Δβ. It remains unclear what the “auto” component of AutoBDMC entails, but there is potential for a schedule that iteratively decreases Δβ until reliable inference results are achieved. The experiments could be strengthened by a systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with step size. 

Despite these criticisms, this is not a weak paper. The proposed method has the potential to be quite useful. Acceptance depends on the authors’ ability to convincingly address the clarity issues and demonstrate awareness of and engagement with related work during the camera-ready period.

**Clarity Issues:**

- The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. The authors likely mean ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, but this is ambiguous given the two AIS sweeps. This ambiguity is exacerbated by inconsistent labeling ("rev" and "fwd" vs. "for" and "back" – is this distinction significant?).
- The authors do not define the estimator \hat{p}(y) for the expectation in equations (5-7) and (9-11), hindering comprehension of their derivation. Do the authors intend E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and E[log \hat{R}{fwd}]?
- Equation (6), as written, is nonsensical due to identical distributions within the KL divergence. Based on equations (4) and (10), the right-hand terms should likely read something like p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y)?
- The meaning of AutoBDMC remains undefined. Is it (a) calculating the bound difference to assess convergence and (b) employing the fixed hyperparameter scheme in section 3.2?

**Experiments:**

- It’s unclear how βt is chosen. Examining the code in the appendix reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`.  Therefore, AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T? This should be stated explicitly.
- In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” – does this indicate the number of steps used *within* the BDMC procedure?
- The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) is undefined in the caption, as are its variants (u)c(R)AIS.
- Increased axes and tick label sizes would improve readability.

**Typos:**

- Missing closing parentheses in the DKL terms in the expression for D_JS.
- In the NUTS citation: “Homan” should be “Hoffman”.
