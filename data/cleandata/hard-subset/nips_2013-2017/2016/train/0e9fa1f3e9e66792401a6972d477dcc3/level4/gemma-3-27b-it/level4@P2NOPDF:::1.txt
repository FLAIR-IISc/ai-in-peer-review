This paper introduces a novel approach to estimating the Jensen-Shannon divergence between an MCMC sampler’s output and the target distribution, specifically targeting the debugging and evaluation of MCMC implementations within Probabilistic Programming Languages (PPLs). The core idea – leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to diagnose sampler convergence issues – is strong and potentially valuable. Current MCMC diagnostics often fall short, and this method offers a promising alternative. The work appropriately acknowledges related efforts, such as those described in [1, 2], which similarly assess sampler performance by evaluating their ability to recover samples from the prior distribution.

However, the presentation currently hinders a clear understanding of the method. Section 3.1, in particular, presents a challenging derivation. The notation introduces `q(.)` without prior definition, leaving the reader to infer, from context, that it represents the marginal distribution of the Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) sampler after T steps. This should be explicitly stated. While the supplementary material clarifies aspects of the derivation, the main text should be self-contained and readily understandable without requiring reference to external documents. 

More broadly, the paper would benefit from a more streamlined exposition of the complete methodology. Currently, the reader must synthesize the procedure from different parts of the text; a clear, step-by-step description of how to apply the method – from initialization to final divergence estimation – would significantly improve clarity.

A minor point regarding notation: the separation of unobserved variables into `z` and `θ` feels somewhat artificial given the general setting. As these variables are always considered jointly, eliminating either `z` or `θ` and consistently using a single variable representing the combined state could simplify the presentation. 

Finally, a minor typographical error exists on line 165 with a missing closing parenthesis.
