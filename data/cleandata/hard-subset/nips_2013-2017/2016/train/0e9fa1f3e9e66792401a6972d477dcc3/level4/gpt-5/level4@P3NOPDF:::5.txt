Summary
This paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) bounds on the log marginal likelihood as a convergence diagnostic and efficiency comparator for MCMC (with emphasis on AIS/SMC-based samplers). The idea is to select, under a fixed computational budget, the sampler that yields the tightest stochastic lower and upper bounds. Because BMC’s stochastic upper bound requires an exact draw from the posterior, the authors propose a heuristic workaround: generate synthetic datasets from a representative parameter value η_real, run the sampler on the corresponding synthetic posterior (for which near-exact samples are presumed easier to obtain), and compute BMC bounds there. The paper presents empirical demonstrations.

Strengths
- The overarching goal—providing theoretically grounded, quantitative diagnostics for MCMC—is important. Diagnostics that connect directly to a global quantity (the marginal likelihood) are attractive, and the bidirectional construction is intellectually appealing.
- The proposal offers a single, ostensibly comparable metric (bound width under a matched budget) that could aid in selecting among samplers and tuning choices.
- The paper is clearly motivated, with a practical angle that acknowledges obstacles to obtaining exact posterior draws.

Main concerns
1) Reliance on η_real and circularity. The approach assumes access to a “representative” parameter setting η_real, often obtained from previous MCMC or optimization. This introduces two issues:
   - It presumes that the method used to obtain η_real has effectively converged—precisely the property the diagnostic aims to assess.
   - It assumes the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point (or narrow region), which is not generally justified, especially for multimodal or heavy-tailed posteriors.

2) Synthetic-data posterior as a surrogate. The method evaluates convergence on the posterior induced by synthetic data simulated from η_real, assuming it reflects the convergence behavior of the real-data posterior. This is a strong and typically unverified assumption:
   - Model misspecification, data sparsity, or atypical observed datasets can make the synthetic posterior materially easier (or harder) than the real posterior.
   - Even when the model is correct, the geometry of the synthetic-data posterior can differ in ways that affect mixing (e.g., different curvature, tail behavior, or multimodality).

3) “Almost exact” posterior draws from η_real. The empirical strategy seems to count on rapid mixing when initialized near η_real, implying the sampler can quickly produce near-exact posterior draws on synthetic data. This is optimistic in many problems (e.g., funnels, strong correlations, non-identifiability). If this assumption fails, the stochastic “upper bound” may no longer be valid, undermining the interpretability of bound tightness.

Collectively, these assumptions are closely tied to the very convergence properties we wish to diagnose. Using them to circumvent BMC’s requirement of an exact posterior draw risks converting a principled bound into a heuristic whose accuracy is difficult to certify.

Scope and applicability
- The methodology appears most natural for AIS/SMC-based procedures and focuses on a single global functional (the marginal likelihood). While valuable, this limits applicability to common MCMC workflows (e.g., HMC/NUTS, Gibbs/Metropolis within Gibbs) unless those are embedded within AIS/SMC frameworks.
- A diagnostic based solely on the marginal likelihood may miss local pathologies (e.g., poor mixing in a small but important region) that affect posterior expectations but have minor impact on normalizing constants.

Empirical evaluation
- The demonstrations in Section 5.1 are helpful but limited in scope and difficulty. They do not convincingly show that the proposed heuristic remains reliable in more challenging or mis-specified settings, nor that it outperforms or complements standard diagnostics.
- The paper critiques conventional diagnostics for failing on multimodal targets or when low-dimensional summaries are uninformative. However, there is no direct evidence that the proposed method succeeds where those tools fail. This gap is central given the paper’s motivation.

Suggestions for strengthening the work
- Validate the surrogate strategy:
  - Provide theoretical or empirical conditions under which the synthetic-data posterior has comparable mixing properties to the real-data posterior.
  - Perform sensitivity analyses over the choice of η_real (e.g., multiple draws from approximate posterior fits, MAP vs. posterior mean) and quantify how bound tightness varies.
  - Include settings with known misspecification to test robustness.

- Calibrate the “upper bound” when exact posterior draws are not available:
  - Quantify bias introduced by using approximate posterior samples for the reverse direction (e.g., via coupling diagnostics, R-hat/ESS on the synthetic runs, or bounds on total variation distance).
  - Explore alternative avenues to obtain (near-)exact draws for small-to-moderate problems (perfect sampling in toy models, unbiased MCMC, conditional SMC with backward simulation) to establish ground truth and calibrate the heuristic.

- Comparative evaluation:
  - Benchmark against widely used diagnostics (split R-hat, univariate/multivariate ESS, Geweke, rank-based diagnostics for HMC, energy-BFMI) under a matched computational budget.
  - Design stress tests where standard diagnostics are known to struggle (multimodal mixtures, hierarchical funnels, heavy tails, sparse logistic regression, latent Gaussian models). Show that BMC-based diagnostics identify issues earlier or more reliably.

- Practical guidance and failure modes:
  - Provide decision rules linked to bound gaps (e.g., thresholds relative to posterior scale) and runtime.
  - Document detectable failure modes (e.g., when approximate “upper bounds” are over-optimistic) and propose safeguards such as repeated runs, cross-checks on real vs. synthetic data, or widening schedules.
  - Clarify applicability beyond AIS/SMC or provide recipes for integrating generic MCMC within the BMC framework.

- Clarity and presentation:
  - Clearly define η_real, how it is obtained, and its role; distinguish between hyperparameters and parameters if both appear.
  - State explicitly when a reported “upper bound” is only heuristic due to reliance on approximate posterior draws.
  - Report computational budgets and wall-clock fairness across methods, and include ablations showing how schedule choices and sample sizes affect bound width.

Overall assessment
The idea of leveraging bidirectional bounds to produce a principled, quantitative diagnostic is compelling, and the paper addresses a real gap in MCMC practice. However, the current workaround for the requirement of exact posterior draws rests on strong, problem-dependent assumptions. Without a more thorough justification, calibration, and comparative evaluation, it is difficult to conclude that the proposed approach yields reliable convergence assessments in general. Strengthening the theoretical underpinnings of the surrogate strategy, expanding experiments to challenging regimes, and demonstrating clear advantages over standard diagnostics would substantially improve the contribution.
