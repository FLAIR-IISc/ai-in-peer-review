The authors present a general approach for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC runs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—essentially the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996]—and reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance metric for MCMC transition operators. The main technical contributions are BDMC implementations for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for assessing the quality of posterior inference.

This work has notable strengths and some weaknesses. The key strength is that using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators is a beautifully simple and easy-to-implement idea that could have high impact, especially for probabilistic programming systems (the primary target setting here). I believe all such systems would benefit from at least implementing this method.

My main concerns relate to novelty and the depth of the technical contribution. The paper cites an arXiv submission on BDMC and (sensibly) devotes substantial space to re-explaining it; however, this leaves Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed, and the writing quality slips there, creating clarity issues (see below). Setting clarity aside, the new technical contributions are somewhat modest. Relative to prior BDMC work, the primary novelty seems to be the observation that the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds itself upper-bounds the Jensen–Shannon divergence. This is a very nice point, though arguably not entirely new.

Related work is also an issue. Curiously, the authors do not cite Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions paper [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353–366], which is highly relevant. This omission also appears in the BDMC manuscript and raises the question of whether the authors are aware of TT. If they are, it is unfortunate not to highlight the connections. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but uses them to construct a tempering transition to improve mixing in multimodal targets. In TT, the final state from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep; the final state of the forward sweep is then proposed and accepted or rejected according to the ratio of the importance weights accumulated across both sweeps. As far as I can tell, this TT acceptance ratio is precisely the ratio of the BDMC stochastic lower and upper bounds. Section 3.3 of Neal (1996) interprets this ratio via two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log Z_T − log Z_1 from above and below, noting that acceptance goes to 1 as the two estimates coincide.

The TT analogy also raises the question of how the authors choose the inverse-temperature schedule {β_t}. In TT it is well known that taking steps Δβ = β_{t+1} − β_t that are too large harms acceptance, while steps that are too small waste compute. The ratio between the BDMC lower and upper bounds will likewise depend on Δβ, yielding clear practical guidance: if the two bounds are too far apart, rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ. Similarly, comparisons between MCMC transition operators are likely unreliable when the log-gap between bounds exceeds about 1 nat; in such cases, Δβ should again be reduced. It also remains unclear what exactly is “auto” about AutoBDMC; there appears to be an opportunity to design an automatic schedule that iteratively decreases Δβ until the bounds are sufficiently tight to ensure reliable inference. In this respect, the experiments could be more thorough—e.g., a systematic study of how the bounds vary with step size would be valuable.

Despite these concerns, I think the paper is not a bad submission overall. As noted, the proposed use of BDMC is potentially quite useful. If the authors can convincingly address the clarity issues and improve the discussion of related work in the camera-ready version, I would be inclined to support acceptance.

Clarity
- The definition of v as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is too loose. I presume the authors mean {z_{1:T−1}, θ_{1:T−1}}, but this is ambiguous because the algorithm runs two AIS sweeps. The ambiguity is compounded by inconsistent labels (“rev”/“fwd” versus “for”/“back”): is this difference meaningful or accidental?
- The estimator p̂(y) whose expectation appears in Equations (5–7) and (9–11) is never defined, making the derivations hard to follow. Do the authors intend E[log R̂_rev] and E[log R̂_fwd]?
- As written, Equation (6) is nonsensical because the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical. Based on Equations (4) and (10), I believe the right-hand terms should look something like p(z, θ | y) times q_back(v | z, θ, y).
- I do not see an unambiguous definition of AutoBDMC. Presumably it means (a) computing the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence, and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme in Section 3.2?

Experiments
- It is not clear how β_t is chosen. From the appendix code, I see var step = 1/options.steps. I infer that AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T, with β_T = 1 and β_1 = 1/T. Please state this explicitly. In Figures 2 and 3, does “HMC/No-U-Turn steps” denote that BDMC is run with that many steps?
- The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) is undefined in the caption, as are the variants (u)c(R)AIS, which I assume mean (un)collapsed variants.
- Please enlarge axis and tick-label fonts.

Typos
- Two closing parentheses are missing in the D_KL terms within the expression for D_JS.
- In the NUTS citation: “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”
