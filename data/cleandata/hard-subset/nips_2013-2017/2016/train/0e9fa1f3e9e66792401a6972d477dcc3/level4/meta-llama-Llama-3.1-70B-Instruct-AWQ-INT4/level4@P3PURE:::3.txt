The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, similar to the Tempered Transitions (TT) method [Neal, 1996], but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as a lower and upper bound on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these two bounds as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The presented technical contributions are the implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

This paper has both strong and weak points. The main strong point is that using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators is a beautifully simple idea, easy to implement, and potentially high-impact, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming systems. The envisioned use case is well-suited for this method, and it would be beneficial for all such systems to implement this approach.

However, my main concerns lie with the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC as existing work but devote a significant amount of space to reiterating its description. Unfortunately, this means that the main technical contributions presented in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 are somewhat rushed, and the writing quality suffers as a result. Some issues with clarity arise, which are discussed below. Setting aside the clarity issues, the technical contributions in this paper are somewhat modest. Relative to the existing work on BDMC, the main novelty appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bound provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. This is a nice observation, although arguably not entirely novel.

Regarding related work, the authors curiously do not cite Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing, 1996], which is highly relevant in this context. This citation is also missing from the manuscript on BDMC, raising concerns about whether the authors are aware of it. If so, this is unfortunate, as there is a missed opportunity to point out connections between the two methods. TT performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweep as BDMC but with the interest of "tempering" MCMC transition moves to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep is used as the initial sample for the forward sweep, and the final sample from the forward sweep is then used as a proposal, which is accepted or rejected according to the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. The acceptance ratio in TT is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bound. Section 3.3 of Neal's paper presents an interpretation of this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log ZT - log Z1 from above and below, noting that a TT sampler will accept with probability 1 when the two converge.

The analogy with TT also raises questions about why the authors do not devote more attention to the choice of βt values. In TT, it is well-known that too large steps in Δβ = β{t+1} - βt will lead to a poor acceptance ratio, whereas too small steps Δβ waste computation. The ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bound in BDMC will also depend on Δβ, providing clear intuitions. If the two bounds are not close enough, one needs to rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ. Similarly, comparisons between MCMC transition operators are likely to be unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds is larger than 1 nat. When this is the case, one should again lower Δβ. It is unclear what the "auto" part in AutoBDMC refers to, but there is an opportunity to develop a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until sufficiently reliable inference results are obtained. The experiments could be more thorough, and it would be beneficial to see a systematic study of how the lower and upper bound vary with the step size.

Despite these criticisms, this is not a bad paper. The method proposed by the authors is potentially useful. Depending on whether the authors can convincingly address issues with clarity and discussion of related work by the camera-ready deadline, I could be inclined to argue for acceptance.

Clarity issues:

* The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is rather loose. The authors presumably mean ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}$, but this is not immediately unambiguous since there are two AIS sweeps in the algorithm.
* The authors do not define what estimator \hat{p}(y) they are taking the expectation of in equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to follow how these equations arise. Do the authors mean to write E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and [log \hat{R}{fwd}]?
* Equation (6), as written, makes no sense (the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical). Based on equations (4) and (10), I assume the right terms should read something like p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y)?
* The authors never unambiguously define what they mean by AutoBDMC. This presumably means (a) calculating the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bound to assess convergence and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme described in Section 3.2?

Experiments:

* It is unclear how βt is chosen. Looking at the code in the appendix, I see `var step = 1/options.steps`. I assume that AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T to go from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T? It would be helpful to state this somewhere.
* In figures 2 and 3, does "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" indicate that BDMC is performed with this number of steps?
* The acronym RAIS, which presumably stands for reverse AIS, is not defined in the caption, nor are the variants (u)c(R)AIS, which I am guessing refer to (un)collapsed variants?
* It would be helpful to increase the size of the axes and tick labels.
