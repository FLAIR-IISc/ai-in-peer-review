Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the long-standing problem of measuring the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. Building on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015), the authors show that for algorithms that can be viewed as importance samplers on an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC), the gap between reverse and forward AIS log marginal likelihood estimates yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS. They refer to the resulting procedure as BREAD. The analysis refines prior BDMC work by directly relating the bounding gap to posterior-sample quality rather than to marginal likelihood estimation. The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and evaluated on five models; they also demonstrate how BREAD can inform modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed factorization) and help debug inference implementations.

The work relates to a line of research on thermodynamic integration, AIS (Neal, 2001), non-equilibrium work identities (Jarzynski, 1997), and BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015). It connects to diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996), and to recent work on sample quality measures not tied to a specific sampler, such as Stein discrepancy (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). The concurrently developed “subjective divergence” estimator (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) is closely related; when initialized at the true posterior, their bound also recovers a Jeffreys divergence upper bound.

Strengths
- Conceptual: A clear theoretical link between BDMC bounds and posterior-sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence. This moves beyond heuristic MCMC diagnostics.
- Practical: Seamless integration into Stan and WebPPL; the tool can surface implementation issues (the authors identified and fixed a WebPPL Gaussian sampling bug).
- Empirical: On several models, simulated-data behavior mirrors real-data behavior when using fitted hyperparameters, suggesting the protocol can be informative in practice.
- Usability: The representation study (collapsed vs uncollapsed) shows how BREAD can guide modeling/inference choices in different systems.

Weaknesses
- Scope: The guarantees require an exact posterior draw to run reverse AIS, hence hold rigorously only for simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Dependence on fitted hyperparameters: Using a point estimate of hyperparameters to simulate data and to initialize a short MCMC warmup for reverse AIS risks bias, especially under model misspecification or multi-modality. The paper provides limited evidence beyond the five examples.
- Generality: The methodology applies to AIS/SMC-like algorithms; it does not directly validate generic MCMC (e.g., HMC/NUTS) unless wrapped in an AIS/SMC scheme, limiting immediate applicability.
- Empirical comparisons: No head-to-head evaluation against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, multichain behavior) on challenging cases. The claim that BREAD addresses multimodality better than existing diagnostics would benefit from direct evidence.
- Statistical efficiency: AIS estimates can be heavy-tailed; while the paper uses quantiles, guidance on stabilizing the bounds (e.g., via multiple runs or variance reduction) is limited. The tightness of the bound beyond toy settings is not quantified.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: The core derivations appear technically sound, and the expectation bound is well-motivated. However, the real-data protocol (fitted hyperparameters plus short MCMC to approximate an exact posterior draw) lacks theoretical guarantees. Experiments are informative but modest in scope, and do not stress-test the method on high-dimensional or strongly multimodal targets.
- Clarity: Generally well written and well organized. The relationship to CTM16 is explained; still, adding a concise statement of the precise algorithmic conditions under which the bound holds (what constitutes “importance sampling over an extended state space”) would help. More guidance on choosing annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and number of chains would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: Extending BDMC from log marginal likelihood estimation to bounding posterior-sample divergence is a novel and useful perspective. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is a pragmatic contribution, though heuristic. The connection to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriate and candid.
- Significance: If adopted, BREAD could become a valuable tool for PPL developers and users to validate inference and compare modeling choices. The current limitations (simulated-data requirement, AIS/SMC focus, heuristic real-data procedure) temper the immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide controlled comparisons against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, multichain discrepancy) on scenarios where they are known to be unreliable (e.g., multimodal mixtures) and show whether BREAD provides a clearer signal.
- Include explicit experiments under model misspecification to test the robustness of the fitted-hyperparameter protocol.
- Quantify sensitivity to the annealing schedule, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels; report computational overhead vs diagnostic benefit.
- Report the empirical tightness of the bound (gap vs true Jeffreys divergence) beyond toy problems, perhaps via cases where the posterior is tractable or via high-quality gold-standard runs.
- Clarify to what extent insights from AIS-based evaluation transfer to HMC/NUTS-only workflows, or provide a simple wrapper that enables BREAD for HMC via an AIS bridge.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution linking BDMC gaps to a posterior-sample divergence.
- Practical tool integrated into widely used PPLs; demonstrated usefulness (modeling choices, debugging).
- Addresses an important and challenging problem with principled methodology.

Arguments against acceptance
- Core guarantees apply only on simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic and may fail under misspecification or multimodality.
- Limited empirical evidence and no direct comparison with standard diagnostics on hard cases.
- Applicability is restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers a promising and principled direction with tangible practical value, but the current empirical validation and scope limitations leave open important questions about robustness and generality. Strengthening the experimental comparisons and stress tests would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
