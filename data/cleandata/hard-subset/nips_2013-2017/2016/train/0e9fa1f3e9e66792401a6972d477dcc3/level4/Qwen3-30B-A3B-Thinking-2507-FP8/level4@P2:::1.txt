

This paper proposes a method to evaluate the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms by extending bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors integrate their approach into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility for both debugging and guiding model representation choices in probabilistic programming. The work extends the BDMC framework introduced in Grosse et al. (2015), which was originally designed to estimate marginal likelihoods, to directly assess the quality of approximate posterior samples.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded method to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation using BDMC. Second, it introduces a practical protocol for applying this method to real-world datasets by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters. Third, it demonstrates how this technique can guide modeling decisions, such as whether to collapse latent variables in matrix factorization models. The authors validate their approach across multiple probabilistic models and show that the method can detect implementation bugs in probabilistic programming languages.

The paper is well-positioned in the context of NIPS literature. It builds on the BDMC framework from Grosse et al. (2015) and relates to recent work on probabilistic programming diagnostics (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016). The authors also discuss connections to traditional MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996), highlighting how their method provides rigorous bounds rather than merely identifying potential convergence issues.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The problem addressed is important: MCMC convergence diagnostics remain challenging, particularly for black-box inference methods where internal details are obscured. The authors' approach provides a theoretically sound, quantitative measure that can be directly integrated into probabilistic programming frameworks. The practical validation across multiple models and the demonstration of how the method can guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) showcase its utility. The debugging example that uncovered a WebPPL bug demonstrates the method's potential for improving implementation reliability.

However, several weaknesses prevent this paper from being a strong contribution. The most significant issue is the lack of clarity in the core methodological presentation. Section 3.1, which presents the theoretical foundation for bounding the Jeffreys divergence, is difficult to follow due to ambiguous notation and insufficient explanation. The paper uses q(·) without defining it explicitly, and the reader must infer from context that it represents the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after T steps. This is particularly problematic as the paper's core contribution depends on understanding this derivation. The supplemental material contains more clarity, but the main text should not require readers to consult supplementary materials to understand the primary argument.

The paper also lacks a clear, step-by-step guide for applying the method. The reader must piece together how to implement BREAD from various sections, rather than having a concise "how to use" description. For instance, it's unclear how many AIS chains should be run, how to determine appropriate intermediate distributions, or how to interpret the resulting bounds in practical settings.

Additionally, the paper uses redundant notation by distinguishing between unobserved variables z and θ when they serve the same purpose in the probabilistic model. This unnecessarily complicates the exposition. There's also a missing closing parenthesis on line 165 that should be corrected.

The paper addresses the significance criterion well by demonstrating practical applications in probabilistic programming and model debugging. The results on matrix factorization provide concrete evidence of how the method can inform modeling decisions. However, the theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental, building on the BDMC framework rather than introducing fundamentally new mathematical insights.

In terms of quality, the experimental validation is thorough and addresses important concerns about the method's applicability to real-world data. The authors carefully test the consistency of inference behavior between simulated and real data, and validate their protocol for using fitted hyperparameters. The results support the claim that BREAD provides reliable insights into inference quality.

The clarity of the paper is its weakest aspect. The theoretical presentation is convoluted, and the methodological exposition lacks the step-by-step guidance that would make it accessible to practitioners. The paper would benefit from a more structured presentation of the algorithm, including a clear description of inputs, outputs, and implementation steps.

The originality of the approach is moderate. While the application of BDMC to evaluate posterior samples is novel, the core technique builds directly on existing work. The authors effectively distinguish their contribution from related work, particularly Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), by showing how their method provides rigorous bounds under specific conditions.

In summary, this paper addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference with a methodologically sound approach that has clear practical value. The core theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental, but the practical implementation and validation demonstrate significant utility. The primary weakness is the lack of clarity in the methodological presentation, which would hinder adoption and understanding. With improved exposition and a more accessible presentation of the algorithm, this work would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The paper is recommended for acceptance with the following condition: the authors must significantly revise Section 3.1 to clarify the notation and provide a step-by-step explanation of how to apply the method. The authors should also eliminate redundant variable notation and fix the grammatical error mentioned above.
