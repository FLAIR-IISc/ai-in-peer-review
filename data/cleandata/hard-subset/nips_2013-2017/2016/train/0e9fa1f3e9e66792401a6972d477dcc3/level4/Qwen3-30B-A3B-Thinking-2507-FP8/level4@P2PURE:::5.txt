

The authors address the problem of assessing MCMC output reliability and comparing MCMC scheme efficiency by leveraging upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. Specifically, they propose selecting MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational budget. The core challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To address this, the authors introduce heuristics, specifically proposing to perform diagnostics on the posterior induced by synthetic data (generated using a pre-specified hyperparameter value η_real) rather than the actual posterior. This approach exploits the premise that approximate samples from the synthetic-data posterior are easier to obtain. The methodology is evaluated on several examples. While the idea of using BMC's stochastic lower bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting, the methodology appears restricted to AIS and SMC samplers and focuses solely on marginal likelihood-based diagnostics. Despite these limitations, it represents a valuable attempt to develop theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation suffers from significant drawbacks stemming from the heuristics used to circumvent BMC's requirement for an exact posterior sample. I highlight three critical issues:  
1. The method assumes prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value η_real (e.g., from a previously converged MCMC run). This assumes both that the algorithm used to determine η_real had converged (the very problem being diagnosed) and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value—both strong and unjustified assumptions.  
2. It further assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data (using η_real) shares similar MCMC convergence properties with the original posterior. This assumption lacks justification, particularly in cases of model misspecification, and effectively shifts the diagnostic target to a different posterior.  
3. The approach optimistically assumes that starting from η_real enables the target MCMC algorithm to rapidly produce an almost exact posterior sample (which may hold but is not guaranteed).  
Critically, these assumptions are directly tied to the convergence properties the method aims to assess, creating a circular dependency. While Section 5.1 demonstrates that these issues do not invalidate the method in simple examples, these limited cases are insufficient to claim robustness across more complex scenarios, as the authors themselves acknowledge. The authors critique prior diagnostics for failing in multimodal settings or with insufficiently expressive summary statistics (lines 203–205). They should clarify why their method overcomes these limitations and provide empirical evidence. More generally, comparative simulations against standard diagnostics (e.g., those in Section 4) would strengthen the case by demonstrating scenarios where their method yields more reliable diagnostics.
