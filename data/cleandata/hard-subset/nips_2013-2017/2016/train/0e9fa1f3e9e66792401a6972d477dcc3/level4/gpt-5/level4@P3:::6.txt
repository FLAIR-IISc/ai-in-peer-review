Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] from estimating marginal likelihoods to assessing the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) both forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior). The gap between the stochastic lower bound from forward AIS and the stochastic upper bound from reverse AIS is shown to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS. The authors refer to this procedure as BREAD.

Beyond the theory, the paper integrates BREAD into two probabilistic programming systems, WebPPL and Stan, and evaluates it on several models. Because reverse AIS requires exact posterior samples, the authors propose a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from the fitted hyperparameters, and use approximate posterior samples (obtained by short MCMC runs) to initialize the reverse chain. They validate that the behavior on simulated and real data is consistent and that reverse AIS estimates are insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the posterior initialization. They further use BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization in WebPPL and Stan, and report discovering and fixing a bug in WebPPL revealed by violated bounds.

Relation to prior work
BDMC was previously used to sandwich the log marginal likelihood on simulated data [GGA15]. This paper connects the BDMC sandwich gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors. The work relates to convergence diagnostics [GR92, CC96, BG98], Stein discrepancies for sample quality [GM15], thermodynamic integration and tempered transitions [Nea96], AIS [Nea01], and the Jarzynski equality [Jar97]. Independent concurrent work [CTM16] introduces subjective divergence; when initialized from the true posterior, their estimator coincides with BDMC and also upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, overlapping with this paper’s main theoretical insight.

Strengths
- Clear and useful connection between BDMC sandwiching and an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC-style samplers.
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan, with a protocol that helps transfer insights from simulated to real data.
- Empirical validation across several models; analysis of representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) provides actionable guidance.
- Demonstrated utility for debugging probabilistic programming systems.
- Careful discussion of limitations and relation to concurrent work.

Weaknesses
- The main theoretical result is an expectation bound and can be loose; the paper provides limited quantitative characterization of tightness beyond toy cases.
- Applicability is restricted to algorithms expressible as importance sampling on an extended state space and requires exact posterior samples for rigorous guarantees (thus largely confined to simulated data).
- For real-data use, the fitted-hyperparameter initialization for reverse AIS is heuristic; no formal guarantees are provided on the bias introduced.
- Overlap with [CTM16] reduces novelty; differences and relative advantages could be clarified more sharply.
- Limited reporting on computational cost and tradeoffs (e.g., schedules, number of intermediate distributions, runtime vs. bound tightness).

Quality
The derivations are sound and extend prior BDMC analysis to bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The empirical section is thorough for the scope: five models, consistency checks between simulated and real data, and sensitivity analyses for reverse initialization. However, the paper could better quantify when the bound is tight or informative (e.g., relation to effective sample size, diagnostics, or mixing properties), and provide more systematic runtime/accuracy tradeoffs.

Clarity
Generally clearly written and well organized. Suggestions:
- Emphasize early that the upper bound holds in expectation; use consistent terminology around “stochastic bounds.”
- Provide pseudocode for BREAD to improve reproducibility.
- Clarify notation for forward/reverse chains and conditionals; a brief diagram could help.
- Summarize guidance on choosing annealing schedules and number of intermediate distributions.

Originality
Moderate. The link from BDMC’s sandwich gap to an expected Jeffreys divergence bound is a neat and practically relevant observation, but closely related to the independent “subjective divergence” framework [CTM16]. The implementation in two PPLs and the empirical protocol add practical value but are not conceptually novel.

Significance
For practitioners using probabilistic programming, having a principled, nearly black-box way to diagnose sample quality and compare modeling choices is valuable. The debugging application is compelling. However, the dependence on simulated data (or heuristic approximations for real data) and on AIS/SMC limits generality.

Pro-accept
- Provides a principled, implementable tool for assessing posterior sample quality within widely used PPLs.
- Empirical studies illustrate actionable insights and real debugging value.
- Bridges theory (BDMC) and practice in a way likely to influence PPL workflows.

Pro-reject
- Incremental theoretical contribution with nontrivial overlap with concurrent work [CTM16].
- Guarantees are limited (expectation bound; simulated data requirement), with heuristic steps for real data and no formal error control.
- Limited analysis of bound tightness and computational tradeoffs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify bound tightness empirically across more settings; relate the bound to effective sample size, R-hat, or Stein discrepancies.
- Provide guidance on annealing schedules and number of temperatures; study variance reduction from averaging multiple AIS runs and its effect on the bound.
- Theoretically analyze the impact of approximate reverse-initialization on the bound (e.g., bias as a function of total variation distance from the true posterior).
- Expand comparisons with [CTM16], including when each approach is preferable, and any empirical head-to-head evaluation.
- Report wall-clock costs for forward/reverse AIS across models to inform practical adoption.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well executed and practically useful, but the novelty over BDMC and concurrent work is limited, and the strongest guarantees require simulated data. With additional analysis of tightness, clearer positioning relative to [CTM16], and more comprehensive cost–accuracy tradeoffs, this would be a stronger fit.
