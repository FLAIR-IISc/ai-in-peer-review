

The paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. This builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015), who used BDMC to estimate marginal likelihoods via stochastic bounds. The authors adapt this framework to MCMC diagnostics by leveraging the fact that for *simulated data*, exact posterior samples can be generated via ancestral sampling (via Equation 3), enabling rigorous bounds on inference quality. They integrate their method, named BREAD, into WebPPL and Stan, and propose a protocol for real-world data: fitting hyperparameters to real data, simulating data from these hyperparameters, and evaluating inference on the synthetic data. The approach is validated on five models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.), with additional insights into model representation tradeoffs and a WebPPL bug discovery.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Novel application of BDMC:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by repurposing BDMC for MCMC diagnostics—a gap not addressed in prior work (e.g., Gorham & Mackey, 2015 focuses on sample quality without MCMC context).  
2. **Practical utility:** BREAD is implemented in two major probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan), demonstrating real-world applicability. The debugging use case (Section 5.2.2) highlights its value for identifying implementation flaws.  
3. **Rigorous validation:** The protocol’s consistency between real/synthetic data (Section 5.1.1) and insensitivity to approximate reverse initialization (Section 5.1.2) provide strong empirical support for their approach.  
4. **Clear technical contributions:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound (Section 3.1) is mathematically sound and directly addresses a core need in MCMC validation.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Circularity in the protocol’s assumptions:** The method relies on *fitted hyperparameters* (e.g., \(\hat{\eta}_{\text{real}}\)) to generate synthetic data. This assumes the hyperparameter-fitting MCMC has already converged—a problem the diagnostic aims to solve (Section 3.2). While Section 5.1.1 shows consistency in simple models, the approach’s validity hinges on this assumption, which is untested in complex or misspecified cases (e.g., multimodal posteriors). The paper does not address how this affects diagnostics in such scenarios.  
2. **Limited scope:** BREAD applies *only* to inference algorithms amenable to AIS/SMC (e.g., those using extended state spaces), excluding common MCMC methods like Metropolis-Hastings. This narrows its relevance, especially since the paper claims broader applicability to "MCMC inference" (Section 1).  
3. **Insufficient comparison with existing diagnostics:** The critique of prior methods (e.g., "convergence diagnostics cannot confirm convergence" in Section 4) lacks evidence that BREAD outperforms them in *multimodal or challenging cases*—exactly where prior diagnostics fail. A comparative experiment (e.g., with Gelman-Rubin or Geweke diagnostics) on a multimodal model would strengthen the case.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro acceptance:*  
- First theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic for probabilistic programming.  
- Validated across diverse models and integrated into widely used tools.  
- Provides actionable insights (e.g., model representation tradeoffs in Section 5.2.1).  

*Con acceptance:*  
- Protocol assumptions risk circularity, undermining diagnostic reliability.  
- Narrow applicability to AIS/SMC limits utility for general MCMC.  
- No evidence of superiority over existing diagnostics in challenging scenarios.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically sound and practically useful method for MCMC diagnostics, with strong empirical validation. However, the protocol’s reliance on assumptions that are themselves the focus of the diagnostic creates a critical gap. While the validation on simple models (Section 5.1) is convincing, the lack of evidence for complex cases—particularly multimodal posteriors where prior diagnostics fail—prevents a strong claim of *general* utility. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should:  
1. Explicitly discuss the limitations of their protocol in misspecified or multimodal settings,  
2. Extend comparisons to standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) on challenging models,  
3. Clarify the scope (e.g., "BREAD applies to AIS/SMC-based inference, not general MCMC").  

As currently written, the work is a valuable step toward rigorous MCMC diagnostics but falls short of demonstrating broad applicability. I recommend *weak acceptance* (score 3) for its novelty and implementation, with the understanding that it requires significant revision to address the scope and assumption limitations before reaching its full potential.
