The authors propose a generic method for evaluating the performance of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) transition operators based on the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method. BDMC, which was recently introduced, performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps and interprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper suggests using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The technical contributions presented include implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

This paper has both strong and weak points. One of the main strengths is that using BDMC to assess the convergence of MCMC operators is a simple yet elegant idea, easy to implement, and potentially high-impact, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming systems. The envisioned use case for this method is indeed in such systems, and it would be beneficial for all such systems to implement this method.

However, my main concerns lie with the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC as existing work and devote a significant amount of space to reiterating its description. While this is a wise decision, it also means that the main technical contributions presented in sections 3.1 and 3.2 are somewhat rushed, leading to issues with writing quality and clarity. As a result, some points require further clarification.

Relative to the existing work on BDMC, the main novelty appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. This is a nice observation, although arguably not entirely novel. This brings me to a point about related work. The authors curiously do not cite Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions (TT) [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which is highly relevant in this context. This citation is also missing from the manuscript on BDMC, which makes me wonder whether the authors are aware of it. If so, this is unfortunate, as there is a missed opportunity to point out connections between the two methods.

TT performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweep as BDMC but with the interest of "tempering" MCMC transition moves to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep is used as the initial sample for the forward sweep, and the final sample from the forward sweep is then used as a proposal, accepted or rejected according to the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. The acceptance ratio in TT is, as far as I can tell, precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bound. Section 3.3 of Neal's paper presents an interpretation of this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log ZT - log Z1 from above and below, noting that a TT sampler will accept with probability 1 when the two converge.

The analogy with TT also leads me to wonder why the authors do not spend more attention on the choice of βt values. In TT, it is well-known that too large steps in Δβ = β{t+1}-βt will lead to a poor acceptance ratio, whereas too small steps Δβ waste computation. The ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bound in BDMC will also depend on Δβ, providing clear intuitions. If the two bounds are not close enough, one needs to rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ. Similarly, I suspect that comparisons between MCMC transition operators are likely to be unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds is larger than 1 nat. When this is the case, one should again lower Δβ.

It is not entirely clear to me from reading the paper what the "auto" part in AutoBDMC is, but I think there is an opportunity to come up with a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until sufficiently reliable inference results are obtained. In this respect, the experiments could be more thorough. It would be good to see a systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with the step size.

These criticisms aside, I think this is not a bad paper. As noted above, I think the method proposed by the authors is potentially quite useful. Depending on whether the authors' response can convincingly argue that issues with clarity and discussion of related work can be addressed by camera-ready, I could be inclined to argue for acceptance.

Regarding clarity, some points require further clarification:

- The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is rather loose. The authors presumably mean ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}$, but this is not immediately unambiguous since there are two AIS sweeps in the algorithm. This is not helped by the use of inconsistent labels "rev" and "fwd" on the one hand and "for" and "back" on the other (is this difference significant, or a mere accident?).

- The authors do not define what estimator \hat{p}(y) they are taking the expectation of in equations (5-7) and (9-11), which makes it difficult to follow how these equations arise. Do the authors mean to write E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and [log \hat{R}{fwd}]?

- Equation (6), as written, makes no sense (the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical). Based on equations (4) and (10), I take it the right terms should read something like p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y)?

- As far as I can tell, the authors never unambiguously define what they mean by AutoBDMC. This presumably means (a) calculating the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bound in order to assess convergence and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme described in section 3.2?

Regarding experiments, some points require further clarification:

- It is not clear how βt is chosen. Looking at the code in the appendix, I see `var step = 1/options.steps`. I assume that AutoBDMC is therefore parameterized by the number of steps T to go from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T? It would be helpful to state this somewhere.

- In figures 2 and 3, does "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" indicate that BDMC is performed with this number of steps?

- The acronym RAIS, which presumably stands for reverse AIS, is not defined in the caption, nor are the variants (u)c(R)AIS, which I am guessing refer to (un)collapsed variants?

- It would be helpful to increase the size of the axes and tick labels.
