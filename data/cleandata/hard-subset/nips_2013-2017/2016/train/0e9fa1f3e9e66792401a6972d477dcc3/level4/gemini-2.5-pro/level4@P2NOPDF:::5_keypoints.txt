- Addresses MCMC reliability and efficiency comparison by using BMC stochastic upper/lower bounds to select the algorithm that gives the tightest bounds for a given budget—an interesting, theoretically motivated diagnostic beyond heuristics.
- BMC requires an exact sample to compute a valid stochastic upper bound; authors propose a heuristic workaround using a synthetic posterior.
- Synthetic posterior is generated from data simulated at a pre-selected parameter eta_real assumed representative, enabling near-exact sampling.
- The approach presumes eta_real is known (often from a prior MCMC run), creating a circular dependency for diagnosing convergence.
- Method implicitly assumes a posterior can be summarized by a single representative point, which fails for complex, multimodal, or high-dimensional posteriors.
- Assumes MCMC convergence properties on the synthetic posterior match those of the true posterior—an unjustified assumption that may not hold, especially under misspecification or data-sensitive likelihoods.
- Assumes initializing the sampler at eta_real will quickly yield near-exact samples—another strong convergence assumption that undermines the diagnostic’s independence.
- Collectively, the method’s validity is conditional on the original algorithm already performing well, reducing its practical utility as a diagnostic.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to simple examples and does not test challenging scenarios (multimodality, strong correlations) where diagnostics matter most.
- Paper criticizes existing diagnostics but fails to demonstrate why the BMC-based method would outperform them in hard cases; needs comparative experiments and stronger theoretical or empirical justification.
