This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for diagnosing MCMC convergence using Bidirectional Monte Carlo. The approach employs annealed importance sampling (AIS) and its reverse variant to produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The gap between these bounds provides a quantitative upper bound on the Jensen–Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, yielding a single scalar diagnostic for convergence.

Because the method requires synthetic data generated from the joint model, the authors evaluate whether diagnostics computed on synthetic data predict behavior on real datasets. The presented experiments support this claim, and the paper also demonstrates applications to model selection and debugging. Overall, the paper is readable and well-motivated, the method appears practically useful, and the empirical evaluation is reasonably convincing.

That said, Section 3 is substantially harder to follow than the rest. The transition into Section 3.1 is abrupt: it introduces variables and equations without sufficient motivation or intuition. If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 is a novel theoretical result (as it appears to be), this should be stated explicitly and highlighted as a central contribution. More broadly, the paper needs a clear and early definition of “AutoBDMC.” As written, it is not obvious whether AutoBDMC refers to (i) the use of AIS and reverse AIS on synthetic data to bound the log marginal likelihood, (ii) the induced bound on the Jensen–Shannon divergence as a diagnostic, or (iii) the full pipeline that automates these steps for MCMC convergence assessment. A concise definition or algorithmic summary near the start would greatly improve clarity. By the time Section 3.2 states that “this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC,” the term should be unambiguous.
