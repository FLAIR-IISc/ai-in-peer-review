The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique for bounding the marginal likelihood. The method leverages two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, analogous to tempered transitions [Neal Stat & Comp 1996], and interprets the difference between the resulting lower and upper bounds as a performance measure for MCMC operators. The presented contributions include implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality. This paper has both strengths and weaknesses. A key strength is that using BDMC to assess MCMC convergence is a conceptually simple and readily implementable idea, potentially offering high impact, particularly within probabilistic programming systems – where implementation would be broadly beneficial. However, the novelty and technical depth are somewhat limited. While the authors reiterate the description of BDMC and cite related work, the technical contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel somewhat rushed, impacting clarity. This leads to some issues that need to be addressed. Despite these concerns, the proposed method is potentially useful, and acceptance hinges on whether the authors can address clarity issues and adequately discuss related work in a camera-ready version.

**Clarity:** The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. The authors likely mean ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, but this is not immediately clear, particularly given the differing labels "rev" and "fwd" versus "for" and "back" – the significance of this distinction is unclear. The estimator \hat{p}(y) used in equations (5-7) and (9-11) is not defined, hindering comprehension of the derivations. It is unclear whether the authors intend to write E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and E[log \hat{R}{fwd}]. Equation (6), as written, is nonsensical; the distributions within the KL divergence are identical. Based on equations (4) and (10), a correction might be p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y). The term "AutoBDMC" remains undefined. Is it (a) calculating the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence and (b) employing the fixed hyperparameter scheme described in section 3.2?

**Experiments:** The choice of βt is not explicitly stated. Examination of the code reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`, suggesting AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps *T* to move from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T; this should be clarified in the manuscript. In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” does not clearly indicate the number of steps used in the BDMC procedure. The acronym RAIS is undefined in the figure captions, as are the (u)c(R)AIS variants. Axis labels and tick marks should be enlarged for improved readability.

**Typos:** The expressions for D_JS are missing closing parentheses in the DKL terms. The NUTS citation incorrectly lists Homan as Hoffman.

**Originality:** The core idea of using the gap between BDMC bounds to assess MCMC performance is insightful but not entirely novel. The authors should more explicitly acknowledge the relationship to Radford Neal’s work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which performs similar reverse and forward “annealing” sweeps. TT utilizes this for tempering MCMC transitions to enhance mixing in multimodal distributions. The acceptance ratio in TT appears to be directly related to the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. The authors could explore the connection and potential interplay between the step size (Δβ) in BDMC and the acceptance ratio, drawing parallels to the known sensitivity of TT to step size. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how to automatically adjust Δβ to ensure reliable bounds.

**Significance:** Despite the limited novelty, the method has practical significance. Providing a rigorous, automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference within probabilistic programming languages is valuable, potentially increasing user confidence in results. The demonstration of the method’s utility in debugging a WebPPL implementation further underscores its practical relevance.

**Quality:** The technical analysis appears sound, but the rushed presentation of sections 3.1 and 3.2 affects the clarity of the derivations. The experimental validation is adequate, but a more systematic study of the influence of step size on the bounds would strengthen the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Potentially high impact due to the simplicity and practical utility of the proposed method.
*   Successful implementation in two popular probabilistic programming systems (Stan and WebPPL).
*   Demonstration of the method’s ability to identify bugs in existing software.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty of the core idea.
*   Lack of thorough discussion of related work, particularly Tempered Transitions.
*   Clarity issues in the technical presentation.
*   Insufficient exploration of the sensitivity of the bounds to parameter settings.
