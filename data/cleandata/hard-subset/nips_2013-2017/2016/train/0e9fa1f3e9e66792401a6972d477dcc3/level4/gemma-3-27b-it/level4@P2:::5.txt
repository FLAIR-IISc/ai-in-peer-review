This paper addresses the challenging problem of reliably assessing the quality of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors propose leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique for bounding the log marginal likelihood, to upper bound the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence – a measure of difference between the approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The core idea is to utilize the tightest BDMC bounds achievable with a given computational budget as a proxy for MCMC efficiency and reliability. A key difficulty with BDMC is its requirement for an exact sample from the target distribution to provide a valid upper bound; the authors propose heuristics to apply the method to real-world data by performing diagnostics on data simulated from hyperparameters estimated from the real data. The approach is implemented within the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages and validated on several models.

The motivation for developing more rigorous MCMC diagnostics is strong, and the application of BDMC to this problem is a novel and potentially valuable contribution. While the method’s direct applicability is limited to algorithms amenable to importance sampling (like AIS and SMC), the authors reasonably argue that insights gained from evaluating these algorithms can inform the design and assessment of more general MCMC methods. The integration of the method into popular probabilistic programming languages is also a significant step towards making it accessible to a wider audience.

However, the proposed methodology relies on several assumptions that introduce potential limitations. The authors acknowledge that the initial hyperparameter estimates used to generate synthetic data must be representative of the posterior, and that the behavior of inference on simulated data should mirror that on real data. These assumptions, while practical, are not universally valid, especially in scenarios involving model misspecification or complex, multimodal posteriors. The effectiveness of the method hinges on the quality of these initial hyperparameter estimates, creating a circularity: assessing MCMC convergence relies on an already-converged (or presumed converged) MCMC run.

The validation presented in Section 5.1, while demonstrating consistency between real and simulated data in the tested cases, feels somewhat limited in scope. The authors recognize that the observed consistency doesn't guarantee generalizability and acknowledge that more complex scenarios could reveal critical issues.  The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparative analysis against established MCMC diagnostic techniques (e.g., those cited in lines 203-205) – including scenarios where existing methods fail, and demonstrating how the proposed approach performs in those cases.  A more detailed discussion of *why* the BDMC-based approach might offer advantages in challenging cases (e.g., multimodality) is also needed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of BDMC for MCMC diagnostics.
*   Potential for providing more theoretically grounded convergence assessments.
*   Implementation in widely-used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Identified a bug in WebPPL, demonstrating diagnostic utility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on potentially strong and unverifiable assumptions regarding hyperparameter estimation and the representativeness of simulated data.
*   Limited validation scope; insufficient comparative analysis with existing diagnostic methods.
*   Method’s applicability is somewhat restricted to AIS/SMC-like algorithms.
*   The inherent circularity of relying on an initial MCMC run to assess convergence.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea with the potential to advance the field of MCMC diagnostics. However, the reliance on simplifying assumptions and the limited validation raise concerns about its practical applicability and robustness. While the theoretical foundation is sound, the practical benefits need to be more convincingly demonstrated.  I would recommend **weak reject** at this time, with encouragement to address the aforementioned concerns through more extensive validation and a more thorough comparative analysis.  The authors should focus on demonstrating scenarios where their method provides a clear advantage over existing approaches and rigorously justifying the assumptions underpinning their methodology.
