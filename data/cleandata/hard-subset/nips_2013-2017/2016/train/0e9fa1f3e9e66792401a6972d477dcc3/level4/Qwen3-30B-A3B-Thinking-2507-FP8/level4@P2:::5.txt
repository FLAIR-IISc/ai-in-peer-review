

The paper addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic inference: reliably assessing the quality of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) outputs. The authors propose BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. This is particularly valuable for black-box inference methods where traditional convergence diagnostics often fail.

The main technical contribution is a refinement of the BDMC framework to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that their method can be applied to MCMC-based inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), which are widely used in probabilistic programming systems. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) deriving an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, (2) providing exact computation methods for small examples, (3) proposing a protocol for applying the method to real-world data, and (4) integrating the approach into WebPPL and Stan for practical validation.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a genuine need in probabilistic inference. The authors clearly relate their work to previous NIPS contributions, particularly the BDMC framework introduced in Grosse et al. (2015) and the related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016). The paper's significance is strong: it provides a theoretically grounded approach to evaluating inference quality, which is crucial for probabilistic programming systems aiming to make sophisticated inference accessible to non-experts.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound method with solid theoretical underpinnings. The derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is rigorous and properly contextualized within existing literature. The experimental validation on multiple models and probabilistic programming languages demonstrates practical utility. However, the method's reliance on exact posterior samples for simulated data creates a significant practical limitation. While the authors propose a heuristic solution using fitted hyperparameters for real-world data, the assumptions behind this approach warrant deeper justification. Specifically, the protocol assumes that (1) the hyperparameters estimated from real data adequately represent the posterior distribution, (2) the posterior induced by synthetic data shares convergence properties with the real posterior, and (3) a small number of MCMC steps from the fitted hyperparameters yields a sufficiently accurate posterior sample. These assumptions are strong and potentially circular, as they rely on the very convergence properties the authors aim to assess.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized exposition of the technical contributions. The methodology is clearly described, and the integration with WebPPL and Stan is well-documented. However, the paper could better explain how BREAD differs from related work, particularly the subjective divergence approach in Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which shares similar theoretical foundations but uses a different reference distribution for the reverse chain.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the specific application of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence is novel, the core technique builds directly on the BDMC framework. The most significant novelty lies in the practical application to MCMC inference and the protocol for extending the method to real-world data.

The significance of the paper is substantial. It provides a theoretically grounded approach to a persistent problem in probabilistic inference. The ability to rigorously assess inference quality could substantially improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems. However, the paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with existing diagnostic methods, particularly in challenging scenarios like multimodal posteriors where traditional diagnostics often fail. The authors briefly acknowledge this limitation but do not provide concrete evidence of BREAD's superior performance in such cases.

Strengths:
- Provides a theoretically rigorous method for evaluating MCMC inference quality
- Integrates with two major probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan)
- Offers practical guidance for applying the method to real-world problems
- Demonstrates the method's utility through debugging a WebPPL bug
- Addresses an important practical need for probabilistic inference

Weaknesses:
- The heuristic approach for real-world data relies on strong, potentially circular assumptions
- Insufficient comparison with existing convergence diagnostics in challenging scenarios
- Limited discussion of the method's computational cost relative to existing approaches
- The protocol for real-world data lacks rigorous validation beyond simple examples

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but its practical applicability is somewhat limited by the assumptions required for real-world application. While the method works well on simulated data and in the simple examples presented, the authors should provide stronger evidence that the heuristic for real-world data generalizes to more complex scenarios. I recommend strengthening the discussion of the method's limitations and providing more comprehensive comparisons with existing diagnostic tools.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with significant potential impact, but its practical utility depends on the validity of the assumptions made for real-world application. With more thorough validation and comparisons, this work could become an important tool for probabilistic inference practitioners.
