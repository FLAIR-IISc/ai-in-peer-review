This paper presents a method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), for assessing MCMC convergence. The method uses the recently-developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds for the log marginal likelihood, relying on annealed importance sampling (AIS). The authors show that the gap between the upper and lower bounds is itself a bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, thus providing a single scalar quantity to assess MCMC convergence behavior.

The paper is well-motivated, and the presented method seems clearly useful. The experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling, and the authors provide evidence that their method can be used for model selection and debugging purposes.

However, I found Section 3 harder to follow than the rest of the paper. The transition into Section 3.1 is especially jarring, as it launches into variable and equation definitions without motivating them. If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 is a new result (which I believe it is), then this should be made explicit, as the novelty/contribution of the paper seems to hinge on this.

The authors could improve the clarity of their method by explicitly defining BREAD and its components. For instance, it would be helpful to state that BREAD is the approach of using AIS and reverse AIS to analyze MCMC convergence. Additionally, the authors switch between using y and D to denote data/observations, which can be confusing at times.

In terms of the quality of the paper, I would say that it is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their method. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough background information for the reader to understand the context and relevance of their work.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to provide a rigorous and general method for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in probabilistic programming languages. The authors demonstrate the usefulness of their method in various experiments, including model selection and debugging.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it relies on simulated data to analyze convergence, which may not always be representative of real-world data. However, the authors provide experimental evidence that their method can be used to analyze convergence on real-world data by simulating data from the joint distribution.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to improve clarity and consistency. Specifically, the authors should:

* Explicitly define BREAD and its components
* Motivate the variable and equation definitions in Section 3.1
* Consistently use notation throughout the paper
* Provide more information about the WebPPL and Stan code, including version numbers and integration instructions
