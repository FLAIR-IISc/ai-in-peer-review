

This paper proposes a method for evaluating MCMC inference quality using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), extending the technique originally introduced in Grosse et al. (2015) to measure the reliability of approximate posterior samples. The authors demonstrate that the gap between the lower and upper bounds produced by BDMC provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. They implement this technique, which they call BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), in WebPPL and Stan, and develop a protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data using simulated data from fitted hyperparameters. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) demonstrating that BDMC bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, (2) showing the upper bound is accurate on toy examples, (3) proposing a protocol for real-world data evaluation, and (4) implementing BREAD in two probabilistic programming systems.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The core idea is elegant and simple to implement, making it highly practical for probabilistic programming systems where users often lack expertise to assess inference quality. The method provides rigorous bounds on inference quality for simulated data, addressing a critical need in black-box inference systems. The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates clear practical utility, and the authors' validation on multiple models and datasets provides convincing empirical support. The connection to existing work on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) is well-motivated, and the proposed protocol for real-world data evaluation is thoughtful and well-justified.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses that require attention. First, the novelty claim is underdeveloped. The key insight—that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence—is presented as novel, but it is closely related to work by Neal (1996) on Tempered Transitions (TT), which also uses forward and reverse annealing sweeps with similar theoretical underpinnings. The authors should explicitly discuss this connection, as the TT method also bounds log ZT - log Z1 from above and below, with the acceptance ratio in TT corresponding to the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds. This omission is particularly surprising given that the BDMC manuscript itself fails to cite Neal's work, raising questions about whether the authors are aware of this relevant connection.

Second, the technical presentation in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 lacks sufficient depth. The derivation of how the gap between bounds upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence is presented somewhat hastily, and key mathematical details are unclear. For instance, the definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous—does this include both forward and reverse AIS chains, or just one? The inconsistent use of "rev/fwd" versus "for/back" labels further confuses the presentation. Additionally, the paper never clearly defines "AutoBDMC," though it appears to refer to the implementation of the method with the fixed hyperparameter scheme described in Section 3.2. The paper also fails to specify how the $\beta_t$ values are chosen (the manuscript simply states "var step = 1/options.steps" in the appendix), which is critical for reproducibility.

Third, the experimental section would benefit from more thorough analysis. The authors claim that the reverse AIS estimates are statistically indistinguishable across different numbers of MCMC steps used to generate approximate posterior samples (S ∈ {10, 100, 1000, 10000}), but they don't provide evidence of statistical significance testing. A systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with step size $\Delta\beta$ would strengthen the paper, as the gap between bounds should narrow with smaller $\Delta\beta$, providing a clear criterion for when results are reliable (e.g., when the gap is less than 1 nat).

Fourth, the paper contains several technical errors that need correction. Equation (6) as written contains identical distributions inside the KL divergence, which is nonsensical. The authors should clarify whether they intend to write $E[\log \hat{R}] = \ldots$ or $E[\log \hat{R}_{rev}] = \ldots$, as the notation is inconsistent. Additionally, the acronym "RAIS" is used without definition in Figure 2 captions, and the variants "(u)c(R)AIS" are not explained.

Despite these concerns, the paper's core idea is valuable and potentially impactful for probabilistic programming systems. The method is straightforward to implement and provides a much-needed rigorous evaluation metric for inference quality. The authors' use of BREAD to uncover a bug in WebPPL demonstrates its practical utility for debugging inference implementations.

To summarize, the paper's main strengths are its elegant simplicity, clear practical motivation, and convincing validation across multiple models. The main weaknesses are the insufficient novelty discussion (particularly regarding Neal's TT work), technical ambiguities in the derivation, and insufficient experimental analysis. The writing quality is generally good but contains some awkward phrasing and unclear technical points.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic inference evaluation. With careful revisions to address the related work discussion, clarify the technical details, and strengthen the experimental analysis, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend revision to address these points before final acceptance.
