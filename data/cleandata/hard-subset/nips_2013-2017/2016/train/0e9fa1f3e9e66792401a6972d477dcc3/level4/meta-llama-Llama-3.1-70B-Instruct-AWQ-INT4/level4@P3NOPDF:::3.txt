This paper proposes a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method. The BDMC method, which is based on two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as a lower and upper bound on the marginal likelihood. The authors suggest using the gap between these two bounds as a performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The technical contributions of this paper include the implementation of BDMC in the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

The paper has both strong and weak points. One of the strong points is that using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators is a simple and elegant idea, easy to implement, and potentially high-impact, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming systems. However, the novelty and depth of the technical contribution are concerns. The authors cite an arxiv submission on BDMC as existing work and devote a significant amount of space to reiterating its description. This results in the main technical contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 being somewhat rushed, leading to issues of clarity.

The technical contributions in this paper are modest, and the main novelty appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bound provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. While this is a nice observation, it is not entirely novel. The authors fail to cite Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions, which is highly relevant in this context. The Tempered Transitions method performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweep as BDMC but with the goal of "tempering" MCMC transition moves to improve mixing in multimodal distributions.

The analogy with Tempered Transitions leads to questions about the choice of βt values. In Tempered Transitions, it is well-known that too large steps in Δβ will lead to a poor acceptance ratio, whereas too small steps waste computation. The ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bound in BDMC will also depend on Δβ, providing clear intuitions. If the two bounds are not close enough, BDMC should be rerun with smaller Δβ. Comparisons between MCMC transition operators are likely to be unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds is larger than 1 nat, and Δβ should be lowered in such cases.

The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the "auto" part in AutoBDMC, which is not clearly explained. There is an opportunity to develop a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until sufficiently reliable inference results are obtained. The experiments could be improved by including a systematic study of how the lower and upper bound vary with the step size.

Despite these criticisms, the paper has potential. The method proposed by the authors is potentially useful, and depending on the authors' response to the concerns raised, the paper could be considered for acceptance.

There are several issues with clarity in the paper. The definition of $\vec{v}$ is loose and could be clarified. The authors should define the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ they are taking the expectation of in equations (5-7) and (9-11). Equation (6) appears to be incorrect and should be revised. The authors should also clearly define what they mean by AutoBDMC.

In the experiments section, it is not clear how βt is chosen, and the authors should state this explicitly. The caption of figures 2 and 3 could be improved by defining the acronym RAIS and its variants. The size of the axes and tick labels could be increased for better readability.

Finally, there are a few typos in the paper, including missing closing parentheses in the DKL terms and a misspelling in the NUTS citation.
