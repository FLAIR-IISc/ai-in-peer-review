

The authors introduce a generic approach for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed method. BDMC conducts two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—structurally identical to those in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing 1996]—but interprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The paper proposes the gap between these bounds as a universal performance metric for MCMC transition operators. Key contributions include BDMC implementations for Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, a protocol for posterior inference quality assessment, and a discussion of theoretical properties.  

The paper’s strongest aspect is the elegant simplicity of using BDMC to evaluate MCMC convergence, which is highly implementable and holds significant potential impact, particularly for probabilistic programming systems (the stated use case). All such systems would benefit from adopting this method. However, concerns regarding novelty and technical depth persist. While the authors appropriately dedicate substantial space to re-explaining BDMC (citing an arXiv preprint), sections 3.1 and 3.2 present rushed technical contributions with diminished clarity. The core novelty—recognizing that the bound gap upper-bounds the Jensen-Shannon divergence—is insightful but not entirely novel. Critically, the authors omit citing Neal’s seminal TT work [Statistics and Computing 1996], which is highly relevant: TT employs identical reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweeps to improve multimodal mixing, using the reverse sweep’s final sample as the forward sweep’s initial state. Crucially, TT’s acceptance ratio corresponds precisely to the ratio of BDMC’s stochastic bounds, and Neal’s Section 3.3 explicitly frames this as bounding log Z_T - log Z_1, noting acceptance probability approaches 1 as bounds converge. This omission misses a key connection.  

The analysis of β_t scheduling (e.g., Δβ step sizes) is underdeveloped. In TT, suboptimal Δβ causes poor acceptance or wasted computation; similarly, the BDMC bound gap depends on Δβ, suggesting an iterative Δβ reduction (e.g., via "AutoBDMC") could ensure reliable inference when the gap exceeds 1 nat. The paper’s "AutoBDMC" definition remains ambiguous: it appears to combine bound-gap convergence assessment with the fixed hyperparameter scheme in Section 3.2, but this is unclear. The experiments lack systematic analysis of bound variation with Δβ, and β_t choice (e.g., "step = 1/options.steps" in code) should be explicitly defined.  

**Clarity issues**:  
- Definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous (likely ${z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}}$), compounded by inconsistent labels ("rev"/"fwd" vs. "for"/"back").  
- Equations (5–7) and (9–11) omit the estimator for $\hat{p}(y)$, and equations (5–7) likely require $E[\log \hat{R}_{\text{rev}}]$/$E[\log \hat{R}_{\text{fwd}}]$.  
- Equation (6) is nonsensical (identical distributions in KL); it should reference $p(z, \theta | y)$ and $q_{\text{back}}(v | z, \theta, y)$.  
- "AutoBDMC" is never unambiguously defined.  

**Experiments**:  
- β_t scheduling (e.g., "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" in figures) lacks clear explanation.  
- Acronyms RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS are undefined in captions.  
- Axes/tick labels in figures are too small.  

**Typos**:  
- Missing closing parentheses in D_KL terms for D_JS.  
- "Homan" → "Hoffman" in NUTS citation.  

Despite these issues, the method is potentially valuable. If the authors address clarity and related work in the camera-ready version, I would recommend acceptance.
