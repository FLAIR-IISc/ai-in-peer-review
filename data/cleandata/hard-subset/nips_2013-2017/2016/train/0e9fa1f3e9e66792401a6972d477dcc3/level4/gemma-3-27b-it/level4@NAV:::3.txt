The authors present a method for evaluating MCMC transition operators leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique that, similar to Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal Stat & Comp 1996], performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps. BDMC reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood, and this work proposes utilizing the gap between these bounds as a generic performance metric for MCMC operators. The key technical contributions are implementations of BDMC within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, alongside a protocol for assessing posterior inference quality. 

The core strength of this work lies in the simplicity and potential impact of using BDMC to assess MCMC convergence – it is easily implemented and particularly well-suited to probabilistic programming systems, where its adoption could be highly beneficial. However, the novelty and depth of the technical contributions raise some concerns. While the authors appropriately dedicate space to describing BDMC, citing a recent arXiv submission, this emphasis leads to a somewhat rushed presentation of their core contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2, impacting writing clarity. 

The primary novelty appears to be the observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence, a useful insight, though not entirely novel.  Notably, the paper omits a citation to Radford Neal’s foundational work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], a significant oversight given the close relationship between the two methods. TT also employs reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps, using the final sample from each to construct an acceptance ratio – which, as far as I can tell, corresponds precisely to the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Neal’s paper already interprets this ratio within a thermodynamic integration framework, highlighting convergence when the bounds align. This connection suggests a need for greater attention to the selection of βt values, mirroring the considerations in TT where step size (Δβ) critically impacts acceptance rates.  The gap between bounds in BDMC similarly depends on Δβ; insufficient closeness necessitates smaller steps, and comparisons between operators are potentially unreliable with gaps exceeding approximately 1 nat. The “auto” functionality of AutoBDMC is unclear, but there’s an opportunity to develop an iterative schedule for decreasing Δβ to achieve reliable inference.  The experiments could be more comprehensive, systematically studying the variation of the bounds with step size.

Despite these criticisms, this is a worthwhile contribution. The proposed method holds significant promise. Acceptance depends on the authors’ ability to address concerns regarding clarity and the discussion of related work in the camera-ready version.

**Clarity Issues:**

*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise. The intended meaning, ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, is not immediately clear given the two AIS sweeps. Inconsistent labeling (“rev”/“fwd” vs. “for”/“back”) further complicates understanding.
*   The estimator \hat{p}(y) used in equations (5-7) and (9-11) is not defined, hindering comprehension of the equations. Clarification regarding E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and E[log \hat{R}{fwd}] is needed.
*   Equation (6), as written, contains an error; the distributions within the KL divergence are identical. Based on equations (4) and (10), the right term should likely be p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y).
*   AutoBDMC remains ambiguously defined. Is it (a) calculating the bound difference for convergence assessment and (b) using the hyperparameter scheme from section 3.2?

**Experiments:**

*   The method for choosing βt is unclear in the text. The code reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`, suggesting AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps (T) to go from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T. This should be explicitly stated.
*   In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” – does this indicate the number of BDMC steps?
*   The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) and its variants (u)c(R)AIS are undefined in the captions.
*   Axes and tick labels should be enlarged for readability.

**Typos:**

*   The expression for D_JS is missing two closing parentheses in the DKL terms.
*   The NUTS citation incorrectly states “Homan” instead of “Hoffman”.
