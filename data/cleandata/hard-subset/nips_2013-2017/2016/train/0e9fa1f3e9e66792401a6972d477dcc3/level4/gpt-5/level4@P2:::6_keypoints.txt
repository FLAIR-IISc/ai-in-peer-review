- Paper applies bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess MCMC posterior inference quality for AIS/SMC-style samplers by estimating a gap that upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation (BREAD).  
- Integrates the procedure into WebPPL and Stan, validates on toy problems and standard models, and proposes a heuristic protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters.  
- Builds directly on prior BDMC and AIS work; adapts forward/reverse sandwiching to bound symmetrized KL for approximate posterior samples.  
- Acknowledges related diagnostics (Stein discrepancies, subjective divergence) but lacks deep empirical comparisons.  
- Strength: Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan provides a systematic, implementable diagnostic for practitioners.  
- Strength: Clear focus on an important problem—quantifying MCMC reliability in black-box settings.  
- Strength: Demonstrations show the method can guide modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and detect implementation bugs.  
- Strength: Protocol on simulated data is straightforward and reproducible; implementation in two platforms suggests generality.  
- Weakness: Limited originality—core conceptual/theoretical components largely known and close to prior BDMC/subjective divergence analyses.  
- Weakness: Guarantees require exact posterior samples, limiting rigorous use to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic without formal guarantees.  
- Weakness: Applicability confined to AIS/SMC-like samplers over extended state spaces, not general-purpose MCMC workflows.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is modest in scale and lacks comparisons to standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, BFMI) or Stein-based measures.  
- Weakness: Limited evidence on bound tightness; guidance on when the bound is informative versus vacuous is insufficient.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly clarify novelty versus BDMC and subjective divergence, including formal and empirical comparisons.  
- Suggestion: Strengthen theory for real-data usage or provide bounds on bias from approximate reverse initialization.  
- Suggestion: Expand empirical evaluation to larger, multimodal, and high-dimensional posteriors, and include ablations (schedules, intermediates, kernels).  
- Suggestion: Quantify computational overhead (wall-clock cost) and provide guidance on when the diagnostic is most useful.  
- Suggestion: Offer practical interpretation criteria for bound magnitudes, failure diagnostics, and tuning advice for AIS schedules.  
- For acceptance: Provides a concrete, implementable diagnostic useful for PPL reliability, modeling trade-offs, and debugging.  
- Against acceptance: Methodological novelty is incremental; main contributions are implementation and application rather than new theory.  
- Against acceptance: Theoretical guarantees are limited to simulated-data settings; real-data protocol lacks formal backing.  
- Against acceptance: Empirical work is not comprehensive and does not benchmark against standard diagnostics, weakening practical claims.  
- Recommendation: Useful for PPL community but currently more suitable for a workshop; needs stronger theory, broader experiments, and clearer positioning to merit main-conference acceptance.
