- Introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to bound the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between MCMC samples and the true posterior.  
- Extends the BDMC framework by running AIS chains forward and backward on simulated data to evaluate MCMC algorithms.  
- Key insight: the gap between stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood gives an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence.  
- Main contributions: (1) derivation of a Jeffreys divergence bound using BDMC.  
- Main contributions: (2) empirical validation of the bound on toy distributions where exact computation is possible.  
- Main contributions: (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real data via simulation from estimated hyperparameters.  
- Main contributions: (4) implementations in WebPPL and Stan, validated across multiple probabilistic models.  
- Well-motivated solution to the important problem of assessing MCMC sample quality, especially for black-box inference systems.  
- Clear connection to prior work, notably BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), and careful distinction from Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016).  
- Significant technical contribution in Section 3.1, though the derivation’s presentation is abrupt and needs more motivation.  
- The paper should more explicitly state the novelty of the divergence bound as its core theoretical contribution.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation on five realistic models using both simulated and real data.  
- Convincing consistency checks showing inference on simulated data matches real data when using estimated hyperparameters.  
- Demonstrated practical uses: model selection, debugging (including a WebPPL bug discovery), and insights from a matrix factorization case study.  
- Requested clarifications: undefined term "AutoBDMC", inconsistent data notation (y vs D), and more WebPPL implementation/version/install details plus packaged code release.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound, practically useful, meets NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance), and recommended for acceptance.
