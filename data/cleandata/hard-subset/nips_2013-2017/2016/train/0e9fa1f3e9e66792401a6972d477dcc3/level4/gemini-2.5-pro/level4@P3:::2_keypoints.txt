- Problem: evaluating quality of approximate posterior samples from MCMC/black-box inference systems.  
- Proposal: BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of BDMC.  
- Core method: use forward AIS (prior→posterior) for a stochastic lower bound and reverse AIS (exact posterior→prior) for a stochastic upper bound on log marginal likelihood.  
- Theoretical result: the expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the AIS sample distribution.  
- Limitation: requires an exact posterior sample, so rigorous guarantees hold only for simulated data.  
- Practical protocol: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from them, then apply BREAD to evaluate inference on the simulated data.  
- Implementation: implemented in WebPPL and Stan.  
- Empirical validation: experiments across five models show inference behavior on simulated data matches real data.  
- Application: used BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization representations in Stan and WebPPL.  
- Debugging utility: discovered a bug in WebPPL, demonstrating practical usefulness for developer testing.  
- Strength — significance: provides a principled, quantitative diagnostic for sampler performance, addressing a critical need.  
- Strength — novelty: novel theoretical link from bidirectional likelihood estimates to bounding sample divergence.  
- Strength — empirical quality and clarity: thorough experiments and clear exposition.  
- Weakness — sampler scope: directly evaluates AIS/SMC sample distributions; connection to standard long-run MCMC convergence is indirect.  
- Weakness — computational cost: bidirectional AIS is computationally intensive and may not be practical as a routine end-user diagnostic.  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, original, and significant work; strongly recommended for acceptance.
