This paper addresses the critical problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC samplers and comparing their efficiency. The proposed method, named BREAD, leverages the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to derive stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The core idea is that the gap between these bounds, which in expectation upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence, provides a quantitative measure of sampler quality. This allows one to select, for instance, the MCMC scheme that produces the tightest bounds for a given computational budget. A key challenge is that BDMC's stochastic upper bound requires an exact sample from the target posterior, which is unavailable for real-world data. To circumvent this, the authors propose a heuristic: performing the diagnostic on a posterior derived from synthetic data, for which a high-quality approximate sample is more easily obtained. The paper validates this approach on several models within the WebPPL and Stan frameworks.

The central idea of using the gap between BDMC bounds as a diagnostic for MCMC convergence is novel and interesting. It represents a commendable attempt to develop more theoretically grounded diagnostic tools, moving beyond common heuristics. While the method is primarily applicable to samplers like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), it is nonetheless a valuable contribution in this direction.

However, the proposed methodology has several significant drawbacks, primarily stemming from the heuristics used to apply BDMC to real-world data. These heuristics rely on a cascade of strong assumptions that are closely related to the very problem of assessing convergence, potentially undermining the method's reliability. The main concerns are as follows:

1.  **Circular Reasoning in Hyperparameter Estimation:** The procedure for real-world data requires first estimating a set of hyperparameters, `\hat{\eta}_{real}`. This implicitly assumes that the MCMC run used to obtain this estimate has already converged, which is precisely the problem the proposed diagnostic aims to solve. This introduces a circular dependency. Furthermore, using a single point estimate `\hat{\eta}_{real}` to generate synthetic data assumes that this value can adequately represent the entire posterior, which is a strong assumption that may not hold for complex or multimodal posteriors.

2.  **Diagnostics on a Surrogate Problem:** The method rests on the assumption that the posterior conditioned on synthetic data exhibits convergence properties similar to the true posterior conditioned on real data. This is a crucial and largely unjustified assumption. It is not clear that performance on this surrogate problem translates to the original problem of interest, especially in cases of model misspecification. In essence, the proposed heuristic seems to address the challenge of BDMC by "changing the problem"—performing diagnostics on a different, and potentially simpler, posterior distribution.

3.  **Reliance on High-Quality Approximate Samples:** The initialization of the reverse chain relies on the optimistic assumption that a short MCMC run starting from the estimated parameters can quickly produce a sample that is a close proxy for an exact posterior draw. While this may be true in some cases, it is not guaranteed in general, and the quality of this approximate sample directly impacts the validity of the resulting upper bound.

While the experiments in Section 5.1 show cases where these heuristics appear to work, the examples are not sufficient to provide confidence that these fundamental issues will not be critical in more complex and challenging inference scenarios. The authors rightly criticize existing diagnostic tools for their potential failures with, for example, multimodal posteriors. However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why the proposed method would be more reliable in such difficult settings.

To strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial for the authors to provide experiments that directly compare their method against standard diagnostics (e.g., the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic, effective sample size). It would be particularly valuable to see scenarios where traditional methods fail but BREAD provides a more reliable assessment of convergence, thereby demonstrating a clear practical advantage.
