The authors consider the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose to address this issue by utilizing the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The idea is, for instance, to choose among available MCMC schemes the one that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds given the same computational effort. The primary challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To overcome this issue, the authors propose some heuristics.

Their solution, in the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involves performing the diagnostic on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data, leveraging the fact that for such posterior, it is easier to produce good approximate samples. Finally, the proposed methodology is tested on some examples. The idea of using stochastic lower bounds given by Bidirectional Monte Carlo to perform MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. Although the methodology appears to be specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and produces diagnostics based only on the marginal likelihood, it could still be valuable as it is an attempt to develop theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools.

However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, make the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing. These drawbacks arise from the heuristic methodology attempting to overcome the fact that Bidirectional Monte Carlo needs an exact sample from the posterior to be valid. I mention three of them. Firstly, the authors assume that they know in advance (e.g., from previously run MCMC) a value of the hyperparameters, η_real, which is representative of the whole posterior. This assumption is strong and unjustified in general, as it presumes both that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged (which is basically the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value.

Secondly, they assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) similar to the original posterior. This assumption is also unjustified and should be carefully justified, especially in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It appears that the authors try to overcome the difficulty posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo at the cost of "changing the problem," i.e., performing diagnostics on a different posterior.

Thirdly, they adopt an optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior, which may be the case but is not guaranteed. Therefore, the proposed methodology seems to rely on various assumptions that are closely related to the actual thing we wanted to assess in the first instance (the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm).

I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents some simple examples where these issues appear not to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology. Nevertheless, the few simple examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues that the authors themselves recognize will not be critical in other, possibly more complicated, examples.

The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools because they can fail in multimodal cases or in cases where few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive (lines 203-205). I think the authors should explain why their method can perform better in such scenarios and provide some evidence. More generally, I believe it would be helpful to have simulations that compare their method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., the ones described in sections 4) to demonstrate scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.
