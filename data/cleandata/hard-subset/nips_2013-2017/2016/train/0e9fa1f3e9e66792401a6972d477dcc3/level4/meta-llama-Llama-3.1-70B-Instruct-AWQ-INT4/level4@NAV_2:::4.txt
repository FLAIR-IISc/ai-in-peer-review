This paper proposes a novel method, termed BREAD, for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples obtained from MCMC-based inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The authors relate their work to previous research in MCMC convergence diagnostics and automatic configuration of MCMC algorithms. They also discuss the limitations of existing methods, such as the inability to confirm convergence and the reliance on method-specific analyses. In contrast, BREAD provides a rigorous and general procedure for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, making it easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the bidirectional Monte Carlo technique and its application to MCMC inference. The experimental evaluation is also well-executed, demonstrating the effectiveness of BREAD in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference on both simulated and real-world data.

The authors also explore the use of BREAD for model selection and debugging purposes, highlighting its potential as a valuable tool for practitioners. However, some minor issues with notation and terminology are noted, such as the inconsistent use of y and D to denote data/observations.

The paper's main contribution is the development of BREAD, which provides a novel and effective method for evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference. The authors demonstrate the usefulness of BREAD in various settings, including model selection and debugging. Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of MCMC inference.
