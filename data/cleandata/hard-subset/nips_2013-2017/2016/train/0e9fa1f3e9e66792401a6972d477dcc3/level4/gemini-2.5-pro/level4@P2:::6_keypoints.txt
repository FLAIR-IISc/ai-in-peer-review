- Proposal: BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) quantifies reliability of MCMC by running AIS forward and reverse and using their log-evidence gap.
- Theoretical result: The BDMC bound gap provides an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior.
- Limitation: Reverse chain requires an exact posterior sample, so formal guarantees hold only for simulated-data settings.
- Practical protocol: For real data, fit hyperparameters, simulate a dataset from them, and evaluate inference on the simulated data with known posterior samples.
- Implementation: BREAD is implemented in WebPPL and Stan and used to validate inference across multiple models.
- Experimental validation: Tests on five Stan manual models show thorough empirical support, including consistency between real and simulated-data inference (Section 5.1.1).
- Robustness: Reverse bounds are empirically robust to the quality of the approximate posterior used for initialization (Section 5.1.2).
- Utility: Method can detect subtle implementation bugs (demonstrated in WebPPL) and compares model representations (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed samplers).
- Originality: Main novelty is reframing BDMC bounds as divergence bounds and proposing a pragmatic real-data protocol.
- Clarity and quality: Paper is well-written, derivations are clear, and experiments support claims.
- Applicability constraint: Guarantees only apply to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended state spaces (e.g., AIS, SMC).
- Model-misspecification risk: Protocol assumes the generative model fits the data; misspecification can make simulated-data evaluation misleading.
- Incrementality: The approach is an incremental extension of Grosse et al. (2015) — same core algorithm with new interpretation and application.
- Suggested improvements: Explain why AIS weight averaging is not used and whether analysis could be extended to allow it; move the model-misspecification discussion/examples from the appendix into the main text.
