

The authors address the critical challenge of reliably assessing MCMC inference quality by extending Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. Their method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), leverages BDMC's stochastic upper/lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood to provide theoretically grounded convergence diagnostics. The core innovation lies in adapting BDMC—which requires exact posterior samples—to practical settings by evaluating inference on *simulated data* generated from fitted hyperparameters, rather than real data. This avoids the impracticality of exact posterior sampling for real-world inference while enabling rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate it across multiple models (including matrix factorization), and demonstrate its utility in comparing model representations and debugging implementations (e.g., uncovering a WebPPL bug).

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a compelling case for BREAD as a theoretically sound diagnostic tool, addressing a significant gap in MCMC evaluation. The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence (rather than just the marginal likelihood) is novel and well-motivated. The validation experiments (Section 5.1) convincingly show that inference behavior on simulated data matches real data across five models, and the approximate posterior initialization (Section 3.2) proves robust even with minimal MCMC steps (Fig. 1). The debugging application (Section 5.2.2) further demonstrates BREAD’s practical value for probabilistic programming systems.

**Weaknesses:**  
While promising, the methodology hinges on three assumptions that undermine its validity as a *general* diagnostic tool:  
1. **Circularity in hyperparameter estimation:** The approach requires first estimating hyperparameters (e.g., \(\hat{\eta}_{\text{real}}\)) via MCMC on real data—precisely the convergence problem being diagnosed. The authors assume this initial inference has converged (Section 3.2), which is unjustified without independent validation.  
2. **Unverified similarity between posteriors:** The method assumes that the posterior induced by data simulated from \(\hat{\eta}_{\text{real}}\) mirrors the real-data posterior’s convergence properties. This is untested under model misspecification (e.g., as hinted in Appendix D), where discrepancies could invalidate results.  
3. **Overstated efficiency of approximate initialization:** The protocol assumes that MCMC initialized from \(\hat{\eta}_{\text{real}}\) rapidly produces near-exact posterior samples (Section 3.2). While Section 5.1.2 shows negligible impact with \(S = 100\) steps, this may not hold for complex multimodal posteriors where convergence is slow.  

These assumptions risk conflating *diagnostic validity* with *algorithmic performance*. The authors acknowledge these limitations but rely on limited validation (Section 5.1.1) to dismiss them. Their experiments use simple models (e.g., linear regression), yet the paper claims broad applicability. Crucially, they do not compare BREAD’s diagnostics to standard tools (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, autocorrelation) in challenging scenarios (e.g., multimodal posteriors), despite criticizing such methods for failing there (lines 203–205). Without evidence that BREAD outperforms existing diagnostics in these cases, its advantage remains unproven.

**Recommendation:**  
The technical contribution is sound and the implementation is thorough, but the method’s reliance on unverified assumptions weakens its claim as a *general* diagnostic. The paper would be significantly stronger with:  
- A comparison of BREAD against established convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size) on complex, multimodal examples.  
- A clearer discussion of when the hyperparameter-fitting heuristic *fails* (e.g., under severe model misspecification).  
- Explicit acknowledgment that BREAD evaluates *simulated-data inference* rather than *real-data inference*, with caveats about the fidelity of the simulation protocol.  

As-is, the work is valuable for specific use cases (e.g., debugging probabilistic programming systems) but overreaches in claiming generality. The validation on simple models is insufficient to support broad adoption. I recommend *weak acceptance* due to the method’s niche utility and lack of comparative evidence, but it could be strengthened substantially with the suggested revisions.
