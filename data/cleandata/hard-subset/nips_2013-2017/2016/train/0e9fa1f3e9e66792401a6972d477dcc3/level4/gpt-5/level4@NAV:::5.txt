The authors tackle the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. They propose doing so by leveraging the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The idea is, for example, to select among available MCMC schemes the one that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds under the same computational budget. The main hurdle is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors introduce heuristics to address this. In Bayesian posterior sampling, their solution is to apply the diagnostic to the posterior induced by synthetic data rather than the observed data, exploiting that such posteriors are easier to approximately sample from. They then evaluate the methodology on several examples.

The use of stochastic lower bounds from BMC for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. Although the approach appears tailored to AIS and SMC samplers and provides diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it could still be valuable as a step toward theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostics. However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my view, limit the appeal of the proposed diagnostic. These stem from the heuristic strategy meant to bypass the need for an exact posterior sample in BMC. I highlight three concerns.

- First, the authors assume prior knowledge (e.g., from previously run MCMC) of a hyperparameter value, eta_real, that is representative of the entire posterior. This assumes both that the algorithm used to obtain eta_real has already converged (which is essentially the original issue) and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both assumptions are strong and generally unjustified.

- Second, they assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from eta_real has similar properties (with respect to MCMC convergence) to the original posterior. This is also unjustified and, for example, would require careful treatment in the presence of model misspecification. It seems the method sidesteps the difficulty posed by bidirectional Monte Carlo by effectively “changing the problem,” i.e., running diagnostics on a different posterior.

- Third, they adopt an optimistic view that initializing at eta_real enables the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly produce an almost exact posterior sample (which may occur but is not guaranteed). As a result, the methodology relies on assumptions that are tightly coupled to the very property we ultimately want to assess: the convergence behavior of the original MCMC algorithm.

I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not appear to affect the conclusions drawn. Nevertheless, a few simple cases are not sufficient to argue that the acknowledged issues will not be critical in other, potentially more complex, settings. The authors criticize previously proposed diagnostic tools for failing in multimodal situations or when a small set of summary statistics is insufficiently informative (lines 203–205). They should explain why their method would fare better in such scenarios and provide supporting evidence. More generally, it would be helpful to include simulations comparing their method to commonly used diagnostics (e.g., those in Section 4) to demonstrate cases where it yields more reliable assessments.
