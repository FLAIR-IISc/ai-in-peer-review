This paper addresses the important problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. The authors propose using the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, as computed by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure, as a diagnostic tool. The core idea is to select, from a set of available MCMC algorithms, the one that produces the tightest BMC bounds for a given computational budget. A key challenge in applying BMC is its requirement for an exact sample from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To circumvent this, the authors propose a heuristic for Bayesian posterior sampling problems. Their solution involves performing the diagnostic on a posterior distribution generated from synthetic data rather than the actual observed data, capitalizing on the fact that generating high-quality approximate samples is often easier for such synthetic posteriors. The proposed methodology is then demonstrated on several examples.

The central idea of using stochastic bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo as a basis for MCMC diagnostics is interesting. While the proposed method appears specific to samplers like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) and focuses solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools.

However, the practical implementation of this idea has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, significantly undermine its utility. These concerns stem directly from the heuristic methodology designed to overcome the need for an exact posterior sample. I will highlight three major issues.

First, the methodology assumes that a single hyperparameter value, η_real, which is "representative" of the true posterior, is known in advance (e.g., from a previously completed MCMC run). This assumption is problematic for two reasons: it presumes that the initial MCMC run used to find η_real has already converged (which is the very problem we are trying to solve), and it assumes that a complex, potentially high-dimensional posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point estimate. Both are strong assumptions that are unlikely to hold in general.

Second, the authors assume that the posterior distribution induced by synthetic data generated from η_real exhibits MCMC convergence properties similar to those of the original posterior based on the real data. This is another strong and unsubstantiated assumption. In scenarios involving model misspecification, for instance, the characteristics of the synthetic and real posteriors could be vastly different. In essence, to bypass the primary difficulty of the BMC method, the authors "change the problem" by performing diagnostics on a different, likely simpler, posterior distribution. The relevance of these diagnostics to the original problem is therefore questionable.

Third, the approach relies on the optimistic view that initializing an MCMC algorithm at η_real will allow it to quickly produce a nearly exact sample from the synthetic posterior. While this may be true in some simple cases, it is by no means guaranteed for more complex models.

Consequently, the proposed methodology rests on a series of assumptions that are critically intertwined with the very properties of the MCMC algorithm that it aims to assess. This introduces a circularity that weakens the credibility of the diagnostic. Although the authors present simple examples in Section 5.1 where these issues do not seem to invalidate the conclusions, these examples are not sufficiently complex or diverse to be convincing. They fail to allay concerns that these foundational issues will become critical in more challenging, realistic applications.

Finally, the authors criticize existing convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failures in multimodal settings or when simple summary statistics are insufficient. I believe the paper would be substantially strengthened if the authors demonstrated why their method is better equipped to handle such scenarios. The authors should provide empirical evidence comparing their method against commonly used diagnostics (such as those described in Section 4) on problems where traditional methods are known to fail. Such a comparison would provide a much clearer and more compelling case for the practical value of their proposed approach.
