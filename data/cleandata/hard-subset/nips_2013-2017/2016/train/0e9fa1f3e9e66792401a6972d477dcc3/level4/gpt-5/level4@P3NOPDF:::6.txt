Summary
This submission surveys previously published results on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) and reports an implementation and empirical evaluation in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The focus is on making BDMC accessible to practitioners by demonstrating how to apply known techniques within these frameworks. The paper positions itself as a practical guide and implementation report rather than an introduction of new algorithms or theory.

Strengths
- Practical value: Implementations in WebPPL and Stan can lower the barrier to applying BDMC in common probabilistic programming workflows. This may benefit practitioners who are less familiar with the methodological details.
- Consolidation of prior work: The manuscript appears to collect and clarify the steps required to deploy BDMC in two distinct environments, which could help standardize usage and encourage reproducibility.
- Cross-language perspective: Showing how the same methodology maps onto multiple PPLs is useful for comparing tooling, defaults, diagnostics, and potential pitfalls arising from language-specific design choices.
- Reproducibility: If code and scripts are released as stated, this contributes to the ecosystem by providing reference implementations.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The paper does not appear to introduce new algorithms, new theoretical results, or substantively new empirical insights beyond what is already known about BDMC. The primary contribution is implementation, which is unlikely to meet the novelty bar expected for NeurIPS.
- Insufficient empirical depth: The experiments (as described) seem to demonstrate feasibility rather than deliver new conclusions about when and why BDMC is preferable to competing approaches. Comparative baselines, ablations, and stress tests (e.g., varying model complexity, dimensionality, or posterior geometry) are either limited or absent.
- Unclear guidance: The paper would benefit from explicit, evidence-based guidelines on when BDMC is practical or advantageous in WebPPL or Stan, including computational overhead, convergence behavior, and robustness to model misspecification.
- Missing positioning versus alternatives: It is not clear how BDMC, in these PPLs, compares against standard marginal likelihood estimators or diagnostics already available (e.g., annealed importance sampling, bridge sampling, thermodynamic integration, SMC-based estimators), both in accuracy and computational cost.
- Scope for a different venue: The contribution is primarily a software/implementation note. A workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, or a venue emphasizing tools and reproducibility, would likely be a better fit.

Clarity and presentation
- The writing is generally clear and the high-level description of BDMC is accessible. However, more detail on engineering choices would help readers re-use the implementations (e.g., initialization, path design, temperature schedules, choice of proposal distributions, diagnostics used to assess estimator reliability).
- It would help to standardize terminology and clearly separate what is reproduced from prior literature versus what is newly engineered or adapted for each PPL.

Suggestions for improvement
- Articulate a concrete, novel contribution. Examples:
  - A new diagnostic or adaptive scheme specific to BDMC in PPL settings.
  - A theoretically motivated modification that improves variance, bias, or computational efficiency.
  - Language-specific optimizations that yield measurable gains and are generalizable.
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Include head-to-head comparisons against widely used alternatives across a diverse set of models (conjugate, nonconjugate, hierarchical, high-dimensional).
  - Report estimator variance, bias (where ground truth is available or via tight bounds), and compute cost (wall-clock, gradient evaluations) under matched budgets.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses (e.g., number of intermediate distributions, schedule design) and ablations of implementation choices.
- Provide actionable guidance:
  - When should users prefer BDMC in WebPPL or Stan? What diagnostics indicate failure modes? What default settings are robust?
  - Discuss scalability and limitations (e.g., models with discrete latent variables, multimodality, strong correlations).
- Improve reproducibility:
  - Release well-documented code, with scripts to reproduce all figures and tables.
  - Include a minimal API and examples to integrate BDMC into typical workflows in both languages.
- Positioning and context:
  - Clarify the relationship to prior BDMC papers and to competing estimators. Summarize known theoretical guarantees and practical trade-offs, emphasizing what is new in this submission.

Recommendation
While the implementations may be useful to the community, the paper in its current form does not meet the novelty and significance bar expected for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection for the main conference and encourage submission to a specialized workshop (e.g., on MCMC or probabilistic programming) or to a venue focused on tools and reproducibility. Strengthening the empirical study and adding a distinct methodological or diagnostic contribution would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
