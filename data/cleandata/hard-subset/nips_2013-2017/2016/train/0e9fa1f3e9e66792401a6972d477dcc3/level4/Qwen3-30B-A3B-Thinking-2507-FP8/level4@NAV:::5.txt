

The authors address the challenge of evaluating MCMC output reliability and comparing MCMC scheme efficiency by proposing the use of upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood derived from the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The core idea involves selecting MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for equivalent computational effort. A primary obstacle is BMC's requirement for an exact sample from the target measure to generate a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors introduce heuristics to circumvent this, specifically leveraging synthetic data in Bayesian posterior sampling to produce a posterior where approximate samples are more feasible. The methodology is evaluated through empirical examples. While the concept of employing BMC-derived stochastic bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing, the approach appears limited to AIS and SMC samplers and focuses solely on marginal likelihood diagnostics. Despite its theoretical motivation, the implementation suffers from significant drawbacks that undermine its appeal. These stem from the heuristic solutions to BMC's exact sample requirement, specifically:  
- The assumption of prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value, η_real (e.g., from a pre-run MCMC), which itself necessitates convergence of the very algorithm being diagnosed and implies the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point—both strong and unjustified assumptions.  
- The assumption that the posterior induced by synthetic data (generated from η_real) shares convergence properties with the original posterior, an unverified claim particularly vulnerable to model misspecification. This effectively shifts the diagnostic target to a different posterior.  
- The optimistic assertion that starting from η_real enables rapid production of near-exact posterior samples, which may not hold generally.  
Critically, these assumptions directly relate to the convergence properties the methodology aims to assess, creating a circular dependency. Although Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not compromise results, such limited cases are insufficient to generalize beyond the tested scenarios. The authors critique existing diagnostics for failures in multimodal settings or with insufficient summary statistics (lines 203–205), yet fail to demonstrate how their method overcomes these limitations or provide evidence for its superiority. Comprehensive simulations comparing the proposed method against standard diagnostics (e.g., those in Section 4) across diverse challenging cases are essential to substantiate its reliability.
