This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for assessing the quality of MCMC inference. The approach extends the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique, which uses forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors' key theoretical contribution is to show that the gap between these bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. This gap thus serves as a useful scalar metric for convergence.

Because BREAD is rigorously applicable only to simulated data (where an exact posterior sample is available), the authors propose and validate a protocol for applying it to real-world problems: they first fit hyperparameters on the real data, then generate synthetic data from those hyperparameters to perform the analysis. The authors provide compelling experimental evidence that this protocol is effective, showing that inference behavior on the synthetic data is predictive of behavior on the real data. Finally, they demonstrate the practical utility of their method for comparing model representations and for debugging inference implementations in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and valuable paper. The proposed method appears useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the notoriously difficult problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence. BREAD provides a principled and quantitative measure of sample quality, which is a significant contribution. The ability to summarize convergence behavior with a single scalar metric is particularly appealing.
*   **Utility:** The applications for comparing model representations and debugging probabilistic programming frameworks are convincing and highlight the method's practical utility for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thoughtfully designed. The validation of the protocol for extending the method to real-world data is crucial for the method's applicability and is well-executed.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Section 3:** While the paper is mostly clear, Section 3 was significantly harder to follow than the rest of the manuscript. The transition into Section 3.1, in particular, is abrupt, launching directly into technical details and equations without first motivating the subsequent derivation. A clearer introduction to this section, outlining the steps of the derivation, would greatly improve readability.
*   **Highlighting Novelty:** The paper's main theoretical novelty appears to be the derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound in Section 3.1. While this is an important result, its significance could be emphasized more clearly. The authors should explicitly frame this as their primary theoretical contribution to distinguish it more sharply from the original BDMC paper on which it builds.
*   **Method Definition:** The name of the method, BREAD, is introduced at the end of the introduction but could be defined more formally. A clear, standalone definition of what constitutes the BREAD method and protocol would be helpful before the term is used throughout the paper.

**Minor Comments:**
*   **Notation:** On line 149, please double-check if `p(theta, z | y)` should be `p(theta, z, y)`.
*   **Consistency:** The paper appears to switch between using `y` and `D` to denote observations. Using consistent notation would improve clarity.
*   **Supplementary Code:** The usefulness of the supplementary code for WebPPL and Stan could be greatly enhanced by including: (a) information on compatible software versions, and (b) clear instructions for integration and usage. Ideally, the code would be released as a standalone package (e.g., via npm for WebPPL) to facilitate wider adoption by the community.
