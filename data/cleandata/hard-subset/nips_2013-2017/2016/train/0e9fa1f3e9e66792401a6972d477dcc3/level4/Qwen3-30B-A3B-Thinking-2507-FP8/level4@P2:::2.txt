

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method that extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to rigorously evaluate the quality of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) posterior inference. The authors build upon Grosse et al.'s (2015) BDMC framework, which provides stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihoods, to derive an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. This represents a significant advancement over existing convergence diagnostics, which typically cannot confirm convergence but only identify particular forms of non-convergence.

The paper makes four key contributions: (1) a theoretical analysis showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence; (2) a validation of this bound on toy distributions where exact calculations are possible; (3) a protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating datasets from fitted hyperparameters; and (4) integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, with validation across multiple probabilistic models.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic programming: the lack of reliable methods to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by black-box inference algorithms. The authors demonstrate how BREAD can inform the design of inference algorithms, such as by comparing the effectiveness of different model representations in WebPPL and Stan. This is particularly valuable given the growing adoption of probabilistic programming languages by non-experts who lack the expertise to evaluate inference quality.

Regarding the review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by empirical validation. The authors provide clear derivations showing how BDMC can bound the Jeffreys divergence, and they validate the bound on toy examples where exact calculations are possible. The experimental design is strong, with careful validation of the protocol for real-world data. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the method is limited to certain inference algorithms), demonstrating appropriate honesty about the scope of their contribution.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical contributions and methodological details. The connection between BDMC and the new application to posterior inference is well-motivated. The experimental section effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the method. The presentation of results in Figures 1-2 is particularly helpful for understanding the comparative performance of different model representations.

Originality: While building on Grosse et al. (2015), the application to evaluate MCMC inference quality is novel. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from related approaches, including the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), and provide a thorough comparison. The paper addresses a gap in the literature by providing a method that can rigorously bound the quality of approximate samples, rather than relying on heuristic convergence diagnostics.

Significance: The problem addressed is highly significant for the probabilistic modeling community, as the reliability of inference is critical for practical applications. The method provides a general, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality in probabilistic programming systems, potentially enabling non-expert users to have confidence in their results. The paper demonstrates this utility through concrete examples, including uncovering a bug in WebPPL's implementation of multivariate Gaussian sampling. The method has clear potential for broader adoption in probabilistic programming frameworks.

Strengths:
- Provides a rigorous method for evaluating MCMC inference quality, addressing a critical gap in the literature
- Theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by empirical validation
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates real-world applicability
- The proposed protocol for real-world data addresses a key limitation of previous methods
- The debugging example demonstrates the method's utility for implementation validation

Weaknesses:
- The method is limited to inference algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC), though the authors make a reasonable case that this informs broader MCMC design
- The protocol for real-world data relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters produce representative simulated data, which may not hold for all models
- The paper does not explore how the method scales to very large models or datasets

The authors have made a strong contribution to the field by developing a method that provides rigorous bounds on inference quality, which is a significant advance over existing heuristic approaches. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility, and the debugging example shows the method's potential to improve implementation reliability.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in probabilistic inference (reliability of approximate posterior samples)
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees supported by empirical validation
- Demonstrates practical utility through integration into two major probabilistic programming languages
- Offers a general, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality
- Has clear potential for adoption by the probabilistic programming community

Con-acceptance arguments:
- The method is limited to specific inference algorithms (though this is a reasonable limitation)
- The real-world data protocol requires careful validation for each model

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with clear practical implications. The method has the potential to become a standard tool for evaluating inference quality in probabilistic programming systems. I recommend acceptance.
