### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method, named BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for quantitatively evaluating the quality of samples from MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. BDMC uses Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) run in both a forward direction (from prior to posterior) and a reverse direction (from posterior to prior) to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key theoretical contribution of this work is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution generated by the forward AIS chain and the true posterior.

Since the reverse procedure requires an exact sample from the true posterior, the method's rigorous guarantees apply only to simulated data. To bridge this gap for practical applications, the authors propose a protocol for real-world datasets: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from a model conditioned on these hyperparameters; and finally, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset to assess inference quality.

The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They conduct a series of experiments to validate the approach, showing that inference behavior on simulated data (using their protocol) closely mirrors behavior on real data. As a demonstration of its utility, they use BREAD to compare the performance trade-offs of collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations for matrix factorization in both languages, providing concrete guidance for modelers. They also report using the method to uncover a bug in WebPPL's sampler, highlighting its usefulness for software validation.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of how to reliably assess the quality of MCMC posterior samples. The central idea of using the BDMC log-likelihood gap to bound the Jeffreys divergence is elegant, novel, and provides a much-needed, principled alternative to heuristic convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat). This is a significant conceptual advance.

2.  **Practical Utility and Implementation:** The authors go well beyond a theoretical proposal. By integrating BREAD into Stan and WebPPL, they demonstrate its practicality for users of modern probabilistic programming languages. The case study comparing model representations for matrix factorization is an excellent example of how this tool can provide actionable insights into modeling choices that are often guided by intuition alone. Furthermore, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's utility for debugging and verifying the correctness of complex inference machinery.

3.  **Rigorous Experimental Validation:** The experimental design is thoughtful and addresses the most critical potential weaknesses of the approach. The validation in Section 5.1.1, which shows that the behavior of forward AIS estimates is consistent between real and carefully simulated data, provides strong evidence for the utility of the proposed protocol. Similarly, Section 5.1.2 demonstrates that the results are robust to the use of an approximate (rather than exact) posterior sample for initializing the reverse chain's hyperparameters, which is a crucial check for the real-world data protocol.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, and the derivation of the main result in Section 3.1 is straightforward. The connection between the theoretical result and its practical application is explained well, making the work accessible and its contributions clear.

### **Weaknesses (Constructive Feedback)**

1.  **Limited Scope of Direct Applicability:** The method's direct application is to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space. The paper argues that insights gained from evaluating these samplers (e.g., choice of transition kernels, model parameterization) will transfer to standard MCMC algorithms. While plausible, this is an indirect connection. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how BREAD might be used to diagnose a standard, long-run MCMC chain (e.g., HMC in Stan), or the limitations of transferring insights from an AIS-based analysis to a different sampler.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Real-Data Protocol:** The authors are transparent that the rigorous theoretical guarantees are lost when applying the method to real-world data, as the protocol relies on an approximate posterior sample to generate simulated data and initialize the reverse chain. While the validation experiments are encouraging, it would be beneficial to discuss the potential failure modes of this heuristic. For instance, in cases of severe model misspecification, the inferred hyperparameters might be very far from any "true" data-generating parameters, and the behavior of the sampler on simulated data could diverge significantly from its behavior on the real dataset.

3.  **Computational Overhead:** The proposed method is computationally intensive, as it requires running many forward and reverse AIS chains to get stable estimates of the bounds. A brief discussion of the computational cost of BREAD relative to the cost of the original inference task would be helpful. This would help practitioners understand when the additional expense is justified (e.g., for validating a new model, developing a PPL, or for high-stakes applications where sample quality is paramount).

### **Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically useful method for tackling a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference. The connection drawn between the BDMC marginal likelihood bounds and the Jeffreys divergence is a significant insight. The authors' efforts to implement and validate the method in widely-used probabilistic programming systems, and to demonstrate its utility in concrete scientific and engineering tasks (model selection and debugging), make this a complete and compelling piece of work.

The weaknesses, primarily concerning the scope of applicability and the heuristic nature of the real-data protocol, are well-acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the core contributions. The strengths of the paper—its novelty, significance, and practical impact—are substantial. This work provides a valuable new tool for researchers and practitioners in machine learning and statistics and is likely to inspire further research in the area of inference validation.

**I strongly recommend acceptance.**
