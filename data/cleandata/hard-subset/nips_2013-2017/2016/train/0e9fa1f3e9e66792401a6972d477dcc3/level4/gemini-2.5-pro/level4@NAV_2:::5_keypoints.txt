- Extends BDMC to assess MCMC sample quality by using the AIS forward/reverse log-marginal-likelihood gap as an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence.  
- Main practical challenge: the reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, which is not available for real datasets.  
- BREAD protocol: estimate hyperparameters from real data, simulate a dataset from these hyperparameters, and run the diagnostic on the simulated data using an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain.  
- Implemented the method in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrated it on several models.  
- Strength: addresses an important practical problem of quantitative MCMC diagnostics for probabilistic programming and algorithm design.  
- Strength: novel, theoretically grounded use of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, moving beyond heuristic diagnostics.  
- Strength: insights are relevant for samplers that can be framed as importance sampling (AIS/SMC) and may inform broader MCMC tuning.  
- Weakness: requires several heuristics to apply to real problems, which undermine the method’s theoretical guarantees.  
- Weakness: circular dependency—assumes a reliable preliminary hyperparameter estimate to assess convergence, which may not hold.  
- Weakness: diagnostic is run on synthetic data, assuming sampler behavior transfers to the real posterior; this is unjustified and fragile under model misspecification.  
- Weakness: reverse chain initialization with an approximate posterior sample weakens the rigorous bounding properties of BDMC.  
- Overall concern: the method relies on a cascade of assumptions that are closely tied to the very convergence issues it aims to measure.  
- Suggestion: include experiments on pathologically difficult cases (e.g., multimodality) to demonstrate robustness.  
- Suggestion: provide direct comparisons with standard diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, ESS) to show practical advantage or complementary value.
