The authors present a method for evaluating MCMC transition operators leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique recently introduced that performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps – functionally equivalent to Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, *Stat & Comp*, 1996] – but interprets the resulting probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This work proposes utilizing the gap between these bounds as a generic performance metric for MCMC operators, with implementations provided for Stan and WebPPL, alongside a protocol for posterior inference quality evaluation.

The core strength of this paper lies in the simplicity and potential impact of using BDMC to assess MCMC convergence, particularly within probabilistic programming systems where its implementation would be broadly beneficial. However, the novelty and technical depth of the contribution warrant closer examination. While the authors appropriately dedicate space to describing BDMC, the presentation of their own contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feels somewhat rushed, impacting clarity. 

A key observation is the demonstration that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. While insightful, this result isn’t entirely novel. A significant omission is the lack of citation to Radford Neal’s work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing, 1996], a highly relevant method that shares a strong mechanistic connection with BDMC.  Both TT and BDMC employ reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps. TT uses the final sample from the reverse sweep to initialize the forward sweep, accepting or rejecting proposals based on the ratio of importance weights – a ratio that appears to directly correspond to the BDMC lower/upper bound ratio.  Neal’s work even interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of thermodynamic integration estimates bounding log ZT - log Z1, accepting when these converge. 

This connection to TT also raises questions about the attention given to the choice of βt values. TT demonstrates the importance of controlling the step size (Δβ) to maintain an acceptable acceptance ratio; a similar consideration should apply to BDMC, where the gap between bounds will depend on Δβ.  A clear guideline – reducing Δβ if the gap exceeds a threshold (e.g., 1 nat) – would be valuable.  The functionality of “AutoBDMC” remains unclear, but presents an opportunity for an iterative schedule to decrease Δβ until reliable inference is achieved.  The experiments could be strengthened by a systematic study of bound variation with step size.

Despite these criticisms, the proposed method holds promise. Acceptance hinges on the authors’ ability to address concerns regarding clarity and the discussion of related work in the camera-ready version.

**Clarity Issues:**

*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. The authors likely intend ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, but the two AIS sweeps introduce ambiguity. Inconsistent labeling (“rev”/“fwd” vs. “for”/“back”) further complicates this.
*   The estimator \hat{p}(y) used in equations (5-7) and (9-11) is undefined, hindering comprehension of the equation derivations. Is the intention to write E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and E[log \hat{R}{fwd}]?
*   Equation (6) is currently nonsensical due to identical distributions within the KL divergence. Based on equations (4) and (10), the right-hand side should likely be p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y).
*   The meaning of “AutoBDMC” is not explicitly defined. Does it encompass both convergence assessment *and* the hyperparameter scheme in section 3.2?

**Experiments:**

*   The method for choosing βt is unclear. Code in the appendix reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`, suggesting AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps *T* to go from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T. This should be stated explicitly.
*   In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” – does this indicate the number of BDMC steps used?
*   The acronym RAIS is undefined in the caption, as are the variants (u)c(R)AIS.
*   Axes and tick labels should be enlarged for improved readability.

**Typos:**

*   The expression for D_JS is missing two closing parentheses in the DKL terms.
*   In the NUTS citation, “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”
