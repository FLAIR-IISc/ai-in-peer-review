- Main contribution: Extends BDMC to bound the expected Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate MCMC/AIS samples via a forward–reverse AIS sandwich.

- Method summary: Run AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data to get stochastic lower/upper bounds on log marginal likelihood; their gap upper-bounds expected Jeffreys divergence.

- Implementation: Integrated approach into WebPPL and Stan.

- Empirical validation: Tested on toy and realistic models; bound tracks true divergence in toys and shows consistent forward-AIS behavior on real vs. simulated data.

- Practical utility: Can guide choices of model representation (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and detect implementation bugs.

- Bridge-to-real-data protocol: Fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate from those fits, and assess agreement between real and simulated AIS behavior.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on BDMC and AIS; relates to SMC, standard MCMC diagnostics, Stein-method sample-quality measures, and concurrent "subjective divergence" work.

- Theoretical strength: Provides a principled, expectation-level upper bound targeting sample quality directly (unlike many convergence diagnostics).

- Scope limitation: Rigorous guarantees hold only on simulated data with exact posterior samples; the fitted-hyperparameter approach weakens formal guarantees.

- Risk cases: Need clearer discussion of when the simulated-data proxy can mislead (e.g., strong prior–likelihood conflict).

- Exposition issue: Section 3.1 is hard to follow; q(·) and distributions qfwd(xT), qfwd(v|xT), qrev(v|xT) are used before being defined.

- Missing recipe: Paper lacks a compact end-to-end procedure or pseudocode (temperature schedule, AIS steps/chains, uncertainty reporting recommendations).

- Variability and reporting: The gap is an expectation and can be noisy; authors should report variability (e.g., across chains) and recommend how many chains stabilize bounds.

- Notation clutter: Splitting latents into θ and z is unnecessary; recommend collapsing to a single latent vector and fixing minor typos (e.g., missing parenthesis).

- Additional technical discussion needed: Address reliance on AIS and discuss nonreversible transitions and alternative intermediate distributions.

- Presentation: Generally well written but would benefit from clearer notation, an algorithmic checklist, and moving key method details from supplement to main text.

- Originality: Novel use of BDMC sandwich for bounding sample quality, despite building on known components; concurrent related work is acknowledged.

- Significance: Valuable diagnostic for PPL users/developers with demonstrated practical impact.

- Arguments for acceptance: Theoretically grounded, implementable diagnostic with cross-PPL demonstrations and a pragmatic real-data protocol.

- Arguments against acceptance: Accessibility issues (especially Section 3.1), heuristic real-data guarantees, and limited empirical scope (model sizes, sampler diversity).

- Suggested fixes: Define qfwd/qrev explicitly and state xT is the AIS terminal state; add concise algorithmic procedure and parameter recommendations; report confidence intervals; clarify failure modes of the proxy; streamline notation; discuss computational cost vs. bound tightness and annealing sensitivity.

- Additional references to include: Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006) for simulation-based sampler validation.
