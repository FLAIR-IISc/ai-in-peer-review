This paper proposes a method for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC technique to measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples obtained from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), uses annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution. The technical contributions of the paper are the implementation of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference. The authors also provide experimental results to validate the method and demonstrate its usefulness in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in realistic settings.

The paper has some strong points, including the simplicity and elegance of the proposed method, which makes it easy to implement and potentially high-impact. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work, including the connection to Tempered Transitions (TT) and the differences between BDMC and TT.

However, there are some issues with clarity and novelty. The definition of some variables, such as $\vec{v}$, is not clear, and the authors use inconsistent labels for the AIS sweeps. The authors also do not define the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ used in some equations, which makes it difficult to follow the derivation. Additionally, the connection to TT is not fully explored, and the authors do not discuss the choice of $\beta_t$ values, which is important for the performance of the method.

In terms of originality, the main novelty of the paper appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bound provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. While this is a nice observation, it is not entirely new, and the authors could have explored this connection more thoroughly.

Overall, I think this is a good paper that proposes a useful method for evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference. However, the authors need to address the issues with clarity and novelty to make the paper more convincing.
