The paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms, specifically by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. This builds upon recent work in accurately estimating marginal likelihoods and extends its application to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples obtained from algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS; [Nea01]) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC; [MDJ06]). The authors integrate their method, termed BREAD, into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p], and validate it on several models and datasets. They also demonstrate its use in guiding design choices regarding model representations within these frameworks.

The core strength of this work lies in its practical application of a theoretically grounded technique (BDMC) to a challenging problem in Bayesian inference – quantifying the reliability of MCMC samples. The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a significant engineering effort, potentially providing users with a valuable tool for diagnosing inference quality, particularly in “black box” settings. The validation on multiple models and the demonstration of its utility in comparing model representations are commendable. The identification of a bug in WebPPL showcases the potential of BREAD for verifying implementations. 

However, the paper’s originality is limited. The core theoretical contributions rely heavily on existing work [GGA15], and the primary contribution is the implementation and application of these techniques within specific probabilistic programming languages. While the experimental validation is thorough, the presented findings do not represent a substantial advancement in the underlying theory of MCMC or approximate inference. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, while pragmatic, relies on a heuristic that could be further justified with more rigorous analysis. Moreover, the paper assumes familiarity with AIS and SMC, and the explanation of these techniques is brief.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a practical tool (BREAD) for assessing the quality of MCMC samples.
*   Demonstrates successful integration into widely used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan).
*   Highlights a potential application for guiding model design choices.
*   Uncovers a bug in WebPPL, showcasing the method’s utility for implementation verification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical novelty; builds directly upon existing work [GGA15].
*   The application is primarily an engineering effort rather than a breakthrough in inference methodology.
*   The heuristic for real-world data analysis could benefit from further theoretical justification.
*   May be more appropriate for a workshop focused on MCMC implementations rather than a core NIPS conference.

Overall, while the paper presents a sound and well-executed implementation of existing techniques with practical benefits, its limited novelty raises concerns regarding its suitability for NIPS. A venue more focused on methodological advancements in MCMC and probabilistic programming might be a better fit.
