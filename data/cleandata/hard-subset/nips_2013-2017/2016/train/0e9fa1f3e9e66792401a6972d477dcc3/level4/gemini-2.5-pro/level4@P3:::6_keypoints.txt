- Proposes BREAD, a method to quantify MCMC sample quality by bounding divergences using reverse annealing.  
- Extends BDMC/AIS and shows the gap between forward and reverse AIS log marginal bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Rigorous guarantee requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, so formal guarantees hold only for simulated data.  
- For real data, authors propose a protocol: infer hyperparameters, simulate data from the model, then run BREAD on the simulated dataset.  
- Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and used it to validate the method and compare model representations.  
- Strength: Addresses a fundamental MCMC diagnostic problem and could provide a positive certificate of posterior approximation quality.  
- Strength: Novel and elegant theoretical contribution extending BDMC to measure posterior accuracy (derivation appears sound).  
- Strength: Thorough empirical validation — protocol checks, robustness to approximate reverse initialization, a practical case study, and a real bug discovery.  
- Strength: Clear, well-organized paper with good exposition of AIS/BDMC and the KL connection.  
- Weakness: Guarantees limited to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic and depends on model correctness.  
- Weakness: Bound may be loose due to marginalization/data-processing inequality; unclear tightness in high-dimensional/complex models.  
- Weakness: Computational cost of running AIS forward and reverse is potentially high; comparison to simply running longer MCMC is not fully explored.  
- For acceptance: tackles important problem, presents novel theory, strong experiments, and practical implementations.  
- Against acceptance: guarantees confined to simulated data, tightness in complex settings not fully explored, and practical computational expense concerns.  
- Overall: Reviewer finds strengths outweigh weaknesses and recommends acceptance.
