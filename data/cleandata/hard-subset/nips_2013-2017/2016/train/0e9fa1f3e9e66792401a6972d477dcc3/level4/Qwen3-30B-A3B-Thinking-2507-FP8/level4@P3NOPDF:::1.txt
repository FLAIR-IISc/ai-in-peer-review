

This paper introduces bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) as a method for estimating the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD) between an MCMC sampler and its target distribution, with a specific focus on debugging and validating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The core contribution—leveraging BDMC to diagnose sampler failures—addresses a critical gap, as existing MCMC diagnostics often lack rigor or scalability. The approach is particularly relevant given prior work by Geweke (2004) and Cook et al. (2012), who also assess samplers by testing convergence on data drawn from the prior. This paper builds on that foundation by proposing a concrete, computationally feasible diagnostic tool, making it a valuable addition to the PPL validation toolkit.  

However, the technical exposition requires significant refinement to ensure clarity and accessibility. The primary concern lies in Section 3.1, where the derivation of the JSD estimator is presented. The notation is confusing: the marginal distribution \(q(\cdot)\) of the AIS sampler after \(T\) steps is used without explicit definition, forcing readers to infer its meaning from context. While the supplement clarifies this, the main text should stand alone; readers should not need supplementary material to grasp foundational concepts. The derivation’s logical flow is also opaque—key steps (e.g., the role of the bidirectional coupling, the justification for the estimator’s bias) are omitted, making it difficult to verify the correctness of the approach. A concise, self-contained step-by-step explanation of how to compute the JSD estimate from raw MCMC samples (including pseudocode or a clear algorithmic summary) would greatly strengthen the paper. Without this, the method’s practical application remains ambiguous, despite its promising theoretical motivation.  

Beyond the derivation, several minor issues undermine readability. The paper unnecessarily partitions unobserved variables into \(z\) and \(\theta\), implying a distinction between them (e.g., latent variables vs. parameters). In a general probabilistic model, these labels are arbitrary and conflated in practice; eliminating one variable (e.g., treating all unobserved variables as \(\theta\)) would simplify notation without loss of generality. Additionally, Line 165 contains a missing closing parenthesis, a trivial but distracting error that should be corrected.  

The paper’s connection to prior work could also be sharpened. While [1,2] are cited as relevant, the manuscript does not explicitly articulate how its diagnostic method advances beyond these approaches. For instance: Does BDMC provide a more scalable or statistically rigorous test than Geweke’s joint distribution test or Cook et al.’s posterior quantile validation? Clarifying this distinction would better position the contribution.  

The experimental section demonstrates the method’s utility on synthetic and real-world models, but the evaluation lacks depth. For example, the paper claims BDMC detects "failure modes" (e.g., multimodal targets), yet the results show only qualitative JSD trends without quantitative thresholds for "failure." How does the JSD value correlate with actual sampler performance (e.g., effective sample size, bias)? Including such metrics would solidify the diagnostic’s practical value. The ablation study on AIS parameters is useful but could better contextualize how BDMC’s sensitivity to these parameters informs debugging workflows.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The core idea—using BDMC for MCMC diagnostics in PPLs—is compelling and addresses a genuine need. The method’s potential to streamline sampler validation in complex probabilistic models is significant, especially given the growing adoption of PPLs in applied work. However, the current presentation obscures this contribution through unclear notation, an incomplete derivation, and insufficient experimental justification. These issues prevent the reader from confidently assessing the method’s validity or applicability.  

**Recommendation**  
**Major Revision**. The authors should:  
1. **Clarify Section 3.1**: Define all notation upfront, provide a step-by-step derivation of the JSD estimator, and include a concise "Algorithm" box summarizing the end-to-end procedure.  
2. **Simplify notation**: Eliminate the artificial distinction between \(z\) and \(\theta\) to reduce confusion.  
3. **Strengthen experimental analysis**: Add quantitative benchmarks linking JSD to sampler performance (e.g., "JSD > 0.1 correlates with >20% bias in posterior means") and explicitly contrast the method with [1,2] in the discussion.  
4. **Correct minor errors**: Fix the missing parenthesis and refine ambiguous phrasing.  

With these revisions, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the PPL community. As it stands, the technical gaps limit its impact. The reviewers must be convinced the method is both theoretically sound and practically actionable—this requires addressing the clarity issues outlined above. The potential is there; the execution needs refinement.
