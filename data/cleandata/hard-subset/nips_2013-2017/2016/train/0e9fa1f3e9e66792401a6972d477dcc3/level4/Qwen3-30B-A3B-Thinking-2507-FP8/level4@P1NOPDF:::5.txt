

The authors address the problem of assessing MCMC reliability and comparing scheme efficiency using stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood derived from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). Their core idea is to select MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC bounds for equivalent computational effort. However, BMC requires an exact sample from the target posterior to produce valid stochastic upper bounds—a significant challenge the authors address via heuristics. Specifically, for Bayesian posterior sampling, they perform diagnostics on posteriors generated from synthetic data (using a pre-specified hyperparameter η_real) rather than real data, arguing that such posteriors are easier to sample approximately. The methodology is tested on example cases. While the use of BMC for theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostics is an interesting contribution, the implementation suffers from critical limitations that undermine its practical appeal. These stem from the authors' heuristic approach to BMC's exact-sampling requirement, which introduces three key assumptions.  

First, the method assumes η_real is known *a priori* (e.g., from a previously run MCMC), which itself requires convergence of that initial MCMC—a problem the authors aim to solve. Moreover, summarizing the entire posterior with a single hyperparameter value is generally unjustified. Second, the approach assumes the posterior induced by synthetic data (with η_real) shares convergence properties with the true posterior. This is particularly questionable under model misspecification, where no justification is provided. Effectively, the authors shift the diagnostic target to a different posterior, altering the original problem. Third, they optimistically assume that starting from η_real enables rapid convergence to an "almost exact" posterior sample for the diagnostic MCMC. While this may hold in some cases, it is not guaranteed. Crucially, all three assumptions directly depend on the very convergence properties the diagnostic aims to assess, creating a circular dependency.  

The authors acknowledge these issues in Section 5.1, where simple examples show minimal impact on conclusions. However, these limited cases are insufficient to dismiss potential failures in more complex scenarios. The paper criticizes existing diagnostics for failing in multimodal settings or with insufficient summary statistics (lines 203–205), yet fails to clarify how their method overcomes these limitations or provide evidence for its robustness in such cases. More generally, the review would benefit from comparative simulations against established diagnostics (e.g., those in Section 4), demonstrating specific contexts where the proposed method yields more reliable results. Without such evidence, the claimed advantages remain speculative.  

In summary, while the BMC-based diagnostic concept is theoretically promising, the reliance on unverified assumptions—particularly the synthetic-data approach—renders the methodology less compelling. The authors’ heuristic solutions effectively sidestep BMC’s exact-sampling requirement by changing the problem, rather than solving it. For this diagnostic to be broadly useful, the authors must rigorously justify the three assumptions or propose a more principled solution. The current implementation, without robust empirical validation across diverse challenging cases, does not sufficiently advance the state of MCMC convergence diagnostics.
