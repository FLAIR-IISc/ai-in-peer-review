This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is to use annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains, run both from prior to posterior and vice versa, to upper bound the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors integrate this method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+p], and validate it on several models and datasets. They also demonstrate its use in guiding model design choices, specifically comparing different model representations in WebPPL and Stan.

This work builds upon the foundations laid by Neal [Nea01] regarding AIS for estimating marginal likelihoods, and extends the BDMC framework introduced by Grosse et al. [GGA15]. The authors appropriately situate their contributions within the broader context of MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and methods for automatically configuring MCMC parameters [RR01]. Recent related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which also leverages BDMC for posterior evaluation, is also acknowledged and discussed.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Approach:** The paper provides a theoretically grounded method for evaluating MCMC inference quality, offering an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. This contrasts with often heuristic-based convergence diagnostics.
* **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into widely used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) is a significant practical contribution, making it accessible to a broad user base.
* **Demonstrated Utility:** The application to compare model representations in WebPPL and Stan showcases the potential of BREAD to inform modeling and algorithmic choices. The identification of a bug in WebPPL is a compelling example of the method's debugging capabilities.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the technical concepts are explained reasonably clearly, with references to key prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The primary contribution lies in the implementation and application of existing techniques rather than the development of fundamentally new algorithms.  The core theoretical result – extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence – feels like a natural extension of prior work [GGA15].
* **Simulated Data Dependence:** The method's theoretical guarantees rely on having access to exact posterior samples, achievable only with simulated data. While the authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data using hyperparameter fitting, the validity of this protocol requires careful consideration and further validation.  The authors acknowledge this limitation, but its impact on the practical applicability of the method is significant.
* **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains (both forward and reverse) can be computationally expensive, potentially limiting the scalability of BREAD to complex models and large datasets. This cost is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Quartile Plots:** The use of quartile plots in the experimental validation, while addressing outliers, obscures the distribution's shape and makes it difficult to perform a precise comparison of distributions. Reporting summary statistics or using alternative visualization methods might be more informative.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method provides a rigorous, albeit computationally intensive, way to assess MCMC convergence and sample quality.
* Implementation in popular probabilistic programming languages significantly enhances its accessibility.
* The demonstration of its utility in guiding model selection and uncovering bugs is a strong positive. 

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of substantial novelty might make it a better fit for a workshop rather than a main NIPS conference.
* The reliance on simulated data and the assumptions underlying the real-data protocol raise concerns about the method's generalizability.
* The computational cost could limit its practical application to relatively simple models.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid engineering contribution – a careful implementation and evaluation of a promising technique for assessing MCMC inference. However, its limited novelty and reliance on potentially restrictive assumptions raise concerns about its suitability for a top-tier conference like NIPS.  I lean towards **rejecting** the paper, but would be open to reconsidering if the authors could address the concerns regarding computational cost and the validity of their real-data protocol, perhaps with more extensive empirical validation.  A strong rebuttal addressing these points could shift my assessment.
