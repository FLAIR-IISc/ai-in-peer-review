- Paper proposes selecting the most efficient MCMC by choosing the sampler that yields the tightest Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) bounds within a fixed compute budget.
- Novel idea: using BMC's stochastic lower bounds as an MCMC convergence diagnostic, particularly for AIS and SMC samplers, is interesting and theoretically motivated.
- Core practical limitation: BMC's validity requires an exact sample from the target, so the authors introduce heuristics to avoid this strict requirement.
- Heuristic 1 / concern: method assumes knowledge of a representative hyperparameter η_real (e.g., from a prior MCMC run), creating circularity since that run’s convergence is what the diagnostic should assess.
- Heuristic 2 / concern: assumes the synthetic posterior (from data simulated at η_real) has convergence behavior similar to the true posterior, an unsubstantiated and potentially fragile assumption under model misspecification.
- Heuristic 3 / concern: assumes initializing from η_real lets the sampler rapidly produce near-exact samples, which may hold sometimes but is not guaranteed in general.
- These assumptions are intertwined with the very convergence property the method aims to evaluate, undermining the diagnostic’s reliability in challenging or high-dimensional cases.
- Experiments are limited to simple examples (Section 5.1) and do not demonstrate robustness in complex, multimodal, or high-dimensional scenarios.
- The authors should justify why their method is more robust than existing diagnostics in multimodal/low-summary-statistic failures and provide empirical comparisons with standard diagnostics (as noted in Section 4).
