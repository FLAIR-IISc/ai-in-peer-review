This paper extends the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors develop a method to upper bound, in expectation, the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. This is achieved by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data. The method, termed Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), is integrated into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p], and validated on several models and datasets. As a demonstration of its utility, the authors apply BREAD to study the effectiveness of different model representations in WebPPL and Stan.

The core idea of this paper builds upon the BDMC framework [GGA15] to provide a quantitative measure for assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples, a notoriously difficult problem in probabilistic inference. This is particularly relevant in the context of “black box” inference methods where internal details are obscured. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing convergence diagnostics and propose a method grounded in information-theoretic principles. The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a significant theoretical contribution, and the practical implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan increases the potential impact of this work. The demonstration of BREAD’s application to guide model representation choices provides a compelling example of its utility.

The strengths of this paper lie in its sound theoretical foundation, its practical relevance to the probabilistic programming community, and its clear presentation. The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, and the connection to existing work on BDMC and AIS is well-established. The implementation in WebPPL and Stan makes the method accessible to a broad audience. The validation experiments, including the demonstration of consistency between real and simulated data, and the application to model representation choices, are well-designed and convincing. The authors also address potential limitations, such as the requirement for simulated data, and propose a pragmatic protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets. The identification of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the value of this work as a tool for verifying software implementations.

However, there are some minor areas for improvement. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of applying BREAD to real-world data, the proposed protocol for simulating data from fitted hyperparameters relies on an approximate posterior sample. A more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the results to the quality of this approximate sample would be beneficial. Additionally, the discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in convergence diagnostics and posterior evaluation techniques. The presentation of the experimental results could also be improved with more detailed statistical comparisons and error bars.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference: reliably evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples.
*   The theoretical contribution of extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is significant.
*   The practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan increases the accessibility and impact of the work.
*   The validation experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The application of BREAD to real-world data relies on an approximate posterior sample, which could potentially affect the accuracy of the results.
*   The related work section could be more comprehensive.
*   The presentation of the experimental results could be improved with more detailed statistical analysis.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The strengths of the paper outweigh the minor weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
