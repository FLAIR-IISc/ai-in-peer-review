Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. BDMC runs annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior), using simulated data to obtain an exact posterior sample for the reverse run. The gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood provides, in expectation, an upper bound on the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The authors integrate this into WebPPL and Stan, propose a protocol to transfer insights from simulated to real data (via fitted hyperparameters), and present experiments validating the approach and using it to compare model representations. The method is particularly relevant for probabilistic programming, where black-box inference makes diagnostics challenging.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable: repurposing BDMC as a posterior-sample quality measure is elegant and easy to implement. This could have high impact, especially for probabilistic programming systems.
- Practical integration: implementations in Stan and WebPPL are valuable and likely to be adopted.
- Sensible evaluation protocol: the fitted-hyperparameter simulation strategy is pragmatic and the consistency checks between real and simulated data are reassuring.
- Useful findings: the matrix factorization experiments provide actionable insights about collapsed vs uncollapsed representations across platforms; the debugging example shows real utility.

Concerns about novelty and depth
Relative to prior BDMC work, the main novelty is recognizing that the forward–reverse gap upper-bounds (in expectation) the symmetrized KL divergence between the AIS sample distribution and the true posterior. This is a nice observation, though arguably incremental. Sections 3.1–3.2 contain the core contributions but feel rushed and, at times, unclear (see clarity comments). A more careful and self-contained exposition would strengthen the contribution.

Relation to Tempered Transitions (TT)
The paper (and the BDMC preprint) does not discuss Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions (Statistics and Computing, 1996), which is closely related. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps to propose transitions that improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the proposal is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of the accumulated AIS-like weights from the two sweeps. That acceptance ratio appears to be the ratio of BDMC’s stochastic upper and lower bound estimates; Neal’s Section 3.3 interprets it via thermodynamic integration bounds on log ZT − log Z1 and notes acceptance tends to 1 as the bounds converge. Drawing out these connections would clarify the landscape and likely benefit readers.

Choice of temperature schedule βt and “AutoBDMC”
The gap depends strongly on the annealing schedule. As in TT, large Δβ can yield poor behavior, while overly small Δβ wastes computation. If the forward–reverse bounds are far apart, one should decrease Δβ and/or strengthen transition kernels. Also, comparisons between transition operators may be unreliable if the bound gap is large (e.g., >1 nat); tightening the schedule would help. It would be useful to:
- Explain how βt is chosen in all experiments.
- Clarify what “AutoBDMC” automates (if present): does it adapt T or βt, or only compute the gap?
- Provide a systematic study of how the bounds vary with Δβ and T, and how this impacts operator comparisons.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The bound interpretation and the fitted-hyperparameter protocol are reasonable, and the experiments are well chosen. The reverse-chain initialization with approximate posterior samples for hyperparameters seems to work well empirically (Section 5.1.2), though the caveat that it breaks the strict bound is important.

Clarity
Several issues impede readability:
- Definition of v: “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is too loose. Please define v explicitly (e.g., all intermediate states x1:T−1 for the forward run) and state clearly that xT is the approximate sample. Avoid overloading notation across the two sweeps.
- Consistent labels: unify “fwd/rev” vs “for/back.” If “rev/fwd” denote the BDMC directions and “for/back” denote conditional densities, say so; otherwise, use a single convention.
- Estimator notation in Eqs. (5–7) and (9–11): explicitly write E[log R̂fwd] and E[log R̂rev] (or equivalent), and define R̂ for each sweep.
- Equation (6): as written, the KL arguments appear identical. Based on Eqs. (4) and (10), the intended terms likely involve pT(xT) qrev(v|xT) and qfwd(xT) qfwd(v|xT). Please correct.
- Define AutoBDMC: what is automated? Bound computation only, or adaptation of schedule/steps as well?
- Acronyms in figures: define RAIS (reverse AIS) and (u)c(R)AIS (uncollapsed/collapsed (reverse) AIS). Increase axis/tick label sizes for legibility.
- Experimental details: state explicitly how βt is set (e.g., linear schedule βt = t/T; in the appendix code step = 1/options.steps). In Figs. 2–3, clarify whether “HMC/NUTS steps” denotes the number of transition steps per AIS temperature, and how many temperatures are used.

Originality
Moderate. The key use of the BDMC gap as an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL is neat but incremental; the broader framework and AIS reverse run are due to prior work. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is pragmatic but not fundamentally new.

Significance
High practical significance within probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics. Even with limited novelty, a robust, easy-to-use reliability measure is valuable, and the demonstrated ability to catch implementation bugs is compelling.

Pros
- Simple, general, and useful diagnostic; easy to implement.
- Integrations in Stan and WebPPL; practical experiments and insights.
- Clear protocol to relate simulated and real data; empirical validation.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond BDMC; underdeveloped connection to Tempered Transitions.
- Rushed and occasionally confusing exposition in Sections 3.1–3.2.
- Insufficient discussion of annealing schedules and adaptation (“AutoBDMC”).

Typos/minor
- Missing closing parentheses in DKL terms in the expression for D_JS.
- NUTS citation: “Homan” → “Hoffman.”

Overall evaluation
A useful and practically important contribution with modest theoretical novelty. With clearer exposition, an explicit discussion of TT and β schedules, and minor fixes, I would lean toward acceptance.
