

This paper introduces a method for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms through an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors present a technique they term BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) that uses AIS chains run in both directions to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, and integrate this into WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages. Their work builds upon the BDMC framework introduced in Grosse et al. (2015), which established stochastic upper and lower bounds on marginal likelihood estimates.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) deriving an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, (2) demonstrating this bound's accuracy on toy distributions, (3) proposing a protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data through hyperparameter fitting, and (4) implementing BREAD in two probabilistic programming systems. This work relates to previous NIPS contributions on convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks and Gelman, 1998; Cowles and Carlin, 1996) and recent probabilistic programming research (e.g., Goodman et al., 2008; Carpenter et al., 2017), while addressing a significant gap in the ability to rigorously assess MCMC reliability.

Strengths include the elegant simplicity of the approach, its direct applicability to probabilistic programming systems, and the practical implementation that enables users to monitor inference quality automatically. The method's potential impact is substantial, as it provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for assessing posterior inference reliability—a critical need in probabilistic programming where users often operate with "black box" inference. The validation experiments across multiple models and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL through BREAD's application demonstrate its practical utility.

However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited, as the key insight—that the gap between the BDMC bounds upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence—is a natural extension of existing work. The authors do not adequately acknowledge the connection to Neal's Tempered Transitions (TT) method (1996), which performs similar forward and reverse sweeps but for a different purpose (improving MCMC mixing). The paper's technical development in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 appears rushed, with insufficient detail on the theoretical derivation of the bounds. Several clarity issues persist: the definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous, the notation for forward/reverse chains is inconsistent, and key equations contain errors (e.g., Equation 6's KL divergence terms are identical). The manuscript also lacks explanation of the "auto" aspect of AutoBDMC and how $\beta_t$ values are chosen.

While the paper's core idea has significant potential, its contribution to the field appears incremental. The method is simple to implement but requires additional work to address the clarity issues and missing connections to related work. The experiments could be strengthened by systematically varying $\Delta\beta$ to demonstrate how the bounds change with step size, which would better inform users about when the method can be trusted.

Arguments for acceptance: The method is simple, potentially high-impact for probabilistic programming systems, and provides a valuable tool for assessing inference reliability. The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility.

Arguments against acceptance: The novelty is limited, the technical contributions are not sufficiently developed, and the paper fails to acknowledge a key related work (Neal's TT method). The clarity issues and theoretical presentation require substantial revision.

Overall, this paper presents a useful tool for probabilistic programming users but falls short in its contribution to the broader MCMC literature. With significant revisions addressing the clarity issues and related work, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, the paper's potential impact is diminished by its lack of novelty and incomplete technical presentation.
