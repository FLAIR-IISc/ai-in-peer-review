This paper addresses the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC output and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. The authors propose a method based on the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The central idea is to select the MCMC scheme that produces the tightest BMC bounds for a given computational budget.

The main challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To overcome this issue, the authors propose a heuristic approach. In the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, their solution involves performing the diagnostic on a posterior conditioned on synthetic data, rather than the actual data. This is motivated by the fact that it is often easier to generate high-quality approximate samples from such a synthetic posterior. The proposed methodology is then demonstrated on several examples.

The core idea of using BMC's stochastic bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. While the methodology appears tailored to AIS and SMC samplers and provides diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools.

However, the proposed implementation suffers from several drawbacks that, in my view, limit its practical appeal. These drawbacks stem from the heuristics used to circumvent the requirement that BMC needs an exact sample from the posterior to produce a valid upper bound. I will highlight three major concerns:

1.  First, the authors assume that a representative hyperparameter value, η{real}, is known in advance (e.g., from a previous MCMC run). This introduces two strong and generally unjustified assumptions: (a) that the MCMC algorithm used to find η{real} has already converged (which begs the question, as this is the very problem the diagnostic is meant to solve), and (b) that the entire posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point estimate.

2.  Second, the method assumes that the posterior conditioned on synthetic data (generated from η{real}) exhibits MCMC convergence properties similar to those of the true posterior (conditioned on the real data). This is another strong assumption that lacks justification, particularly in settings involving potential model misspecification. Essentially, the authors appear to be circumventing the core difficulty of BMC by "changing the problem"—that is, performing diagnostics on a different, more convenient posterior distribution.

3.  Third, the authors seem to optimistically assume that initializing an MCMC chain at η{real} will allow it to quickly generate a nearly exact sample from the synthetic posterior. While this may sometimes be true, it is by no means guaranteed.

Therefore, the proposed methodology rests on a series of assumptions that are, crucially, entangled with the very convergence properties it aims to assess. I acknowledge that the authors present simple examples in Section 5.1 where these issues do not appear to affect the results. Nevertheless, these few examples are insufficient to conclude that the issues—some of which the authors themselves acknowledge—will not be critical in more complex, realistic scenarios.

The authors motivate their work by criticizing existing diagnostic tools for failing in multimodal settings or when simple summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive (lines 203-205). Given this criticism, the authors should explicitly explain and demonstrate why their method is expected to perform better in these challenging scenarios. More broadly, the paper would be significantly strengthened by direct empirical comparisons against commonly used diagnostic methods (such as those mentioned in Section 4). Such a comparison should aim to highlight specific scenarios where the proposed method provides more reliable diagnostics than existing approaches.
