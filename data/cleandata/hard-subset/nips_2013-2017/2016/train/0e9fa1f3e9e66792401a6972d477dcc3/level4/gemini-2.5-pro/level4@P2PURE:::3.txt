This paper proposes a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The BDMC method, introduced in a recent preprint, employs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—analogous to those in Tempered Transitions (TT)—and interprets the resulting importance weights as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The authors propose using the gap between these bounds as a performance metric for MCMC operators. The primary technical contributions presented are implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

The paper has significant strengths but also notable weaknesses. Its main strength is the core idea itself: using the BDMC bound gap to assess MCMC operator performance is simple, elegant, and easy to implement. This simplicity gives the work high potential for impact, particularly within probabilistic programming systems, which are the target application. Indeed, it would be beneficial for such systems to incorporate this evaluation method.

However, my primary concerns relate to the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The paper devotes considerable space to describing the BDMC method, which the authors acknowledge as existing work. While this background is helpful, it leaves the novel contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed and less developed, and the quality of the writing suffers in these sections. Beyond implementation, the main novel contribution appears to be the observation that the difference between the BDMC bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the AIS-generated posterior approximation. While this is a nice insight, it is arguably not an entirely new one.

A more significant issue is the omission of highly relevant related work, specifically Radford Neal's paper on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366]. The connection is striking: TT performs the same forward "annealing" and reverse "heating" sweeps as BDMC to propose a powerful MCMC move. In TT, the sample from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep, and the resulting sample is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of the accumulated importance weights from both sweeps. This acceptance ratio is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds derived in BDMC. Furthermore, Section 3.3 of Neal's paper interprets this ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function difference, noting that the acceptance probability becomes 1 as the two estimates converge. The failure to cite and discuss this work is a missed opportunity to contextualize the contributions and draw important connections.

The analogy with TT also highlights that the paper gives insufficient attention to the choice of the annealing schedule (the sequence of inverse temperatures, βt). It is well-known in the TT literature that the step size, Δβ = β{t+1}-βt, is critical: large steps lead to poor acceptance ratios (and thus a large BDMC gap), while overly small steps are computationally wasteful. The BDMC gap is similarly sensitive to Δβ, and this intuition should be discussed. For instance, comparisons between MCMC operators are likely unreliable if the gap between the log-bounds is large (e.g., >1 nat), in which case Δβ should be reduced. The paper also does not clearly define what "Auto" in "AutoBDMC" refers to. This seems like a perfect opportunity to introduce an adaptive scheme that iteratively tightens the annealing schedule (i.e., reduces Δβ) until the bound gap is acceptably small.

The experiments could be strengthened by incorporating these ideas. A systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary as a function of the annealing step size would be highly informative.

Despite these criticisms, the paper presents a valuable and potentially useful idea. If the authors can convincingly address the issues of clarity and the omission of related work in a revised version, I would be inclined to argue for acceptance.

**Specific Points for Clarification:**

**Clarity**
*   The definition of `v` as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous. It should be clarified if this refers to `{z_{1:T-1}, θ_{1:T-1}}` from one or both of the AIS sweeps.
*   The use of inconsistent labels for the AIS sweeps ("rev" and "fwd" vs. "for" and "back") is confusing. Please use consistent terminology.
*   The estimator `p̂(y)` is not defined in the context of equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to understand the derivations. Are these expectations of `log R̂_rev` and `log R̂_fwd`?
*   Equation (6) appears to be incorrect, as it computes the KL divergence between two identical distributions. Based on the surrounding context, the right-hand distribution in the KL term should likely be `q_back(v | z, θ, y)`. Please verify and correct this.
*   The term "AutoBDMC" is never explicitly defined. Does it refer simply to the protocol of computing the bound gap, or does it also imply the fixed hyperparameter scheme from section 3.2?

**Experiments**
*   The method for choosing the annealing schedule `βt` is not described in the main text. The appendix code suggests a uniform schedule (`var step = 1/options.steps`), meaning `βt` is determined solely by the total number of steps `T`. This parameterization should be explicitly stated.
*   In Figures 2 and 3, it is unclear what "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" refers to. Is this the number of steps `T` used for the BDMC annealing schedule?
*   The acronym RAIS is used in figure captions without being defined. Similarly, the variants (u)c(R)AIS are not explained.
*   The font size for axes and tick labels in the figures should be increased for readability.

**Typos**
*   The expression for D_JS is missing two closing parentheses for the D_KL terms.
*   In the NUTS citation, "Homan" should be "Hoffman".
