- Extends BDMC to bound the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between true posterior and MCMC approximate samples; method named BREAD.  
- Targets algorithms like AIS and SMC by treating them as importance sampling on an extended state space.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validates on multiple models and datasets.  
- Theoretically grounded approach that provides a rigorous bound rather than just heuristic diagnostics.  
- Builds on and properly connects to prior work (AIS, SMC, BDMC, stochastic bounds, related PPL methods).  
- Demonstrates practical utility, including using BREAD to find a bug in WebPPL (debugging application).  
- Limited novelty: primarily an application/extension of existing techniques rather than a new theoretical contribution.  
- Applicability is restricted to inference algorithms that can be expressed as importance sampling over an extended state space.  
- Rigorous bounds require simulated data and a parametric-bootstrap-like protocol to extend to real datasets.  
- Potentially high computational cost (BDMC/AIS scalability concerns for complex models).  
- Presentation could be clearer: theoretical contributions, implementation, and experiments are not cleanly separated and the narrative is dense.  
- Arguments for acceptance: provides a rigorous MCMC evaluation tool, practical PPL implementations, and insightful experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, limited applicability, and computational expense.  
- Overall: solid engineering and validation but incremental; better suited to a specialized workshop than a main NIPS venue.  
- Recommendation: Soft reject for NIPS; encourage resubmission to a probabilistic programming or MCMC-focused workshop.
