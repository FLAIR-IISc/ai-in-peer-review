- Proposes BREAD: extends BDMC by running AIS forward and reverse to produce stochastic lower/upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood and uses their expected gap as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior.  
- Requires exact posterior samples so is limited to simulated data; proposes a protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from the model, then run BREAD.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates practical uses including comparing model representations and debugging inference (successfully found a WebPPL bug).  
- Addresses the important problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and reliability in black-box probabilistic programming systems.  
- BREAD is a clever, practical extension of BDMC that gives a quantitative upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, moving beyond heuristic diagnostics.  
- Paper is generally well-motivated, clearly written, and supported by compelling experiments validating the protocol on real-world data.  
- Methods section needs clearer motivation and smoother transition into technical derivations (Section 3 → 3.1 feels abrupt).  
- The novel connection between the BDMC gap and Jeffreys divergence should be emphasized more and stated as a formal claim/theorem.  
- The term “BREAD” should be defined explicitly and early in the paper to better anchor readers.  
- Notational inconsistencies (e.g., observed data denoted as both y and D) should be standardized.  
- Verify notation in Section 3.2 (possible confusion over p(η,θ | D) vs p(η,θ,D)).  
- Supplementary code needs better documentation (compatible WebPPL/Stan versions, usage instructions) and consider releasing implementations as packages (e.g., npm for WebPPL).
