

This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [Grosse et al., 2015] to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference quality by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior distributions. The authors introduce BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which combines forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) to provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence for simulated data. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on multiple models, and propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

The paper makes several technical contributions. First, it refines the analysis of BDMC to derive an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. Second, it demonstrates that this upper bound closely matches the true Jeffreys divergence on toy examples. Third, it proposes a practical protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data. Finally, it implements BREAD in two probabilistic programming frameworks and uses it to analyze model representation choices.

In terms of quality, the paper presents technically sound methods with clear theoretical derivations. The authors provide rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence and validate their approach through extensive experiments. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility, and the debugging example (identifying a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampling) shows real-world value. However, the approach is limited to settings where exact posterior samples can be obtained (simulated data), which restricts its direct applicability to most real-world inference problems.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence is well-explained, and the experimental section thoroughly validates the method on both toy examples and realistic models. The writing is professional and accessible to the NIPS audience.

Regarding originality, the core technique builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015), and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish between novel contributions and extensions of existing work. The protocol for real-world data evaluation is a useful application, but the fundamental approach isn't new. The related work section properly cites relevant literature, including the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), though the relationship to this prior work could be more clearly articulated.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by its restricted applicability. While the method provides a valuable tool for evaluating inference quality in probabilistic programming systems, it requires simulated data with exact posterior samples, which may not be feasible for many real-world applications. The ability to guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed models in matrix factorization) is a meaningful contribution, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this would impact broader research or practice beyond specific probabilistic programming contexts.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical derivation of bounds on Jeffreys divergence
- Practical implementation in two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Useful protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data
- Demonstrated utility in debugging (WebPPL bug discovery)
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple models

Weaknesses:
- Limited applicability to real-world inference without simulation
- Core technique builds directly on prior work without sufficient novelty
- Insufficient differentiation between novel contributions and extensions
- The significance of the method for broader inference problems isn't fully established
- The protocol for real-world data evaluation relies on strong assumptions about hyperparameter fitting

The paper makes a useful contribution to the evaluation of probabilistic inference, particularly for probabilistic programming users. However, its technical novelty is modest, and its applicability is constrained by the need for simulated data with exact posterior samples. While the method is valuable for debugging and guiding model design in probabilistic programming, it doesn't represent a major advancement in MCMC inference methodology that would be transformative for the broader NIPS community.

Given the focus on probabilistic programming and inference evaluation rather than fundamental advances in MCMC theory or algorithms, this work might be better suited for a venue focused on probabilistic programming or MCMC (such as the Bayesian Methods for Machine Learning workshop or a dedicated MCMC workshop). The paper lacks the significant theoretical or methodological innovation expected for NIPS, and its primary contribution appears to be a practical implementation of existing techniques.

In summary, while the paper is technically sound and provides a useful tool for probabilistic programming users, its novelty and significance fall below the threshold for acceptance at NIPS. The work is better positioned for a venue more focused on applied probabilistic inference or probabilistic programming systems.
