The authors study how to assess the reliability of MCMC outputs and compare the efficiency of different MCMC schemes by using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). The idea is to select, among available MCMC schemes, the one that achieves the tightest BMC stochastic bounds under equal computational budgets. The main obstacle is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to yield a valid stochastic upper bound. To address this, the authors propose heuristics: in Bayesian posterior sampling, they run the diagnostic on the posterior induced by synthetic data rather than the observed data, exploiting the belief that such posteriors are easier to sample from approximately. They then test the method on some examples. The idea of using the BMC stochastic lower bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. Although the approach appears tailored to AIS and SMC schemes and to diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it remains a worthwhile attempt to develop theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostics. However, several drawbacks in the implementation make the proposed diagnostic less compelling. These issues stem from heuristics introduced to circumvent BMC’s need for an exact posterior draw. I note three specific concerns:

- First, the method assumes a hyperparameter value, eta_real, known in advance (e.g., from a prior MCMC run) that is representative of the entire posterior. This presumes both that the algorithm used to obtain eta_real had already converged—which is essentially the original problem of interest—and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both assumptions are strong and generally unjustified.

- Second, it assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from eta_real has MCMC convergence properties similar to those of the original posterior. This is also unjustified and should be carefully argued, especially in settings with model misspecification. In effect, the method sidesteps the core difficulty of BMC by changing the problem—performing diagnostics on a different posterior.

- Third, it adopts an optimistic view that initializing at eta_real enables the MCMC algorithm to quickly produce an almost exact posterior sample. This may occur, but it is by no means guaranteed.

As a result, the methodology relies on assumptions that are, crucially, closely tied to the very property we aim to evaluate in the first place: the convergence behavior of the original MCMC algorithm. I appreciate that Section 5.1 provides simple examples where these issues do not appear to affect the conclusions, but a few such examples are not sufficient to demonstrate that the acknowledged issues will not be critical in other, potentially more complex, settings.

The authors criticize existing convergence diagnostics for failing in multimodal scenarios or when a few summary statistics are insufficiently expressive (lines 203–205). They should explain why their method performs better in such cases and provide supporting evidence. More generally, comparative simulations against commonly used diagnostics (e.g., those in Section 4) would help illustrate scenarios where the proposed method delivers more reliable diagnostics.
