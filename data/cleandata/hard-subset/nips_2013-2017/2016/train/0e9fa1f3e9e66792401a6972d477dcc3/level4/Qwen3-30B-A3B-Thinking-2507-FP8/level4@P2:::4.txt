

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously evaluate the quality of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15] to provide stochastic upper and lower bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, rather than just the marginal likelihood. They demonstrate that the gap between these bounds serves as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, providing a single scalar metric for assessing MCMC convergence. The method is validated on simulated data, and the authors propose a protocol to extend its use to real-world data by fitting hyperparameters on the real data and generating synthetic data from these estimates. The approach is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, with applications to model selection (comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization) and debugging (identifying a subtle bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampling).

The paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of evaluating MCMC convergence, which has been a persistent challenge in probabilistic inference. While previous work has focused on convergence diagnostics that cannot confirm convergence (e.g., [CC96, GR92]), BREAD provides rigorous bounds under specific conditions. The method builds on the BDMC framework [GGA15], but extends it to directly evaluate posterior approximation quality rather than just marginal likelihood estimation. This is a substantial conceptual advance from the related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which used a similar framework but with a different reference distribution.

Regarding quality, the theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by experiments on toy distributions where exact Jeffreys divergence can be computed. The protocol for extending to real-world data is well-motivated and validated across five different models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, autoregressive time series, and mixture-of-Gaussians clustering). The experimental evaluation demonstrates the method's utility in both model selection and debugging, providing concrete evidence of its practical value.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though Section 3 (particularly 3.1) is challenging to follow due to abrupt transitions into technical notation without sufficient motivation. The key theoretical contribution—showing that the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence—should be explicitly stated as the paper's main novelty, as this is the foundation of their method. The paper's title and abstract refer to "BREAD" but the draft review mistakenly calls it "AutoBDMC," which should be corrected throughout the paper.

For clarity, the paper would benefit from:
1) A precise definition of BREAD at the beginning of Section 3 (rather than leaving it implicit until Section 3.2)
2) Consistent notation for data (the paper switches between y and D)
3) More explicit discussion of how BREAD differs from the related [CTM16] work
4) A clearer statement of the limitations (e.g., applicability only to algorithms like AIS/SMC, which are closely related to MCMC)

The significance of this work is high. Probabilistic programming systems like WebPPL and Stan are increasingly used by non-experts, but lack reliable tools for assessing inference quality. BREAD provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring posterior inference quality, addressing a critical need in the field. The method's application to debugging (uncovering a bug in WebPPL) demonstrates its practical utility beyond theoretical contributions.

The paper's main strengths are its clear problem statement, the significant theoretical contribution of extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, and the compelling experimental validation across multiple models and probabilistic programming frameworks. The method directly addresses a fundamental limitation in MCMC inference—lack of reliable quality assessment—making it highly relevant to both practitioners and researchers.

The primary weakness is the lack of explicit statement about the novelty of the Jeffreys divergence bound in Section 3.1, which is the paper's most important technical contribution. Additionally, the paper's title and abstract do not clearly distinguish BREAD from the underlying BDMC framework, potentially causing confusion.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The paper addresses a significant practical problem with a technically sound method that has been well-validated. The theoretical extension to the Jeffreys divergence is novel and impactful, and the practical applications demonstrate clear value for both researchers and practitioners. The minor issues with clarity and exposition can be addressed with minor revisions, making this paper an excellent fit for NIPS.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and has demonstrated practical utility. The theoretical novelty is substantial, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
