- Summary: Extends BDMC to assess MCMC posterior quality by running AIS forward and reverse (BREAD) and shows the AIS sandwich gap upper-bounds expected Jeffreys divergence between true and approximate posteriors.  
- Practical protocol: Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, using simulated data and heuristic reverse-initialization for real data; validates consistency and insensitivity to short MCMC initialization.  
- Empirical results: Evaluated across five models, compared collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations, and used BREAD to find and fix a WebPPL bug.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on BDMC and connects to convergence diagnostics, Stein discrepancies, thermodynamic integration, AIS, Jarzynski equality, and overlaps with concurrent subjective divergence work [CTM16].  
- Main strength 1: Clear theoretical link between BDMC sandwich gap and an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC samplers.  
- Main strength 2: Practical implementation in two popular PPLs and a protocol to transfer simulated-data insights to real data.  
- Main strength 3: Empirical validation and actionable guidance on representation choices; demonstrated debugging utility.  
- Main strength 4: Careful discussion of limitations and relation to concurrent work.  
- Weakness 1: Theoretical result is an expectation bound that can be loose; limited quantitative analysis of tightness beyond toy cases.  
- Weakness 2: Applicability limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling on extended state spaces and requires exact posterior samples for rigorous guarantees.  
- Weakness 3: Real-data protocol (fitted-hyperparameter initialization) is heuristic with no formal bias guarantees.  
- Weakness 4: Significant overlap with concurrent work [CTM16], reducing novelty and needing clearer differentiation.  
- Weakness 5: Limited reporting on computational costs and tradeoffs (schedules, number of distributions, runtime vs. bound tightness).  
- Quality: Sound derivations and thorough empirical section for scope, but needs better quantification of when the bound is tight and runtime/accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Clarity suggestions: Emphasize expectation nature of the upper bound, provide pseudocode, clarify forward/reverse notation, and summarize guidance on annealing schedules.  
- Originality: Moderate — neat connection but closely related to [CTM16]; practical implementations add value but limited conceptual novelty.  
- Significance: Valuable for PPL practitioners as a near black-box diagnostic and for debugging, but limited generality due to simulated-data dependence and AIS/SMC restriction.  
- Pro-accept reasons: Principled, implementable tool for posterior quality assessment; empirical studies provide actionable insights and debugging value; bridges BDMC theory and PPL practice.  
- Pro-reject reasons: Incremental theoretical contribution with overlap to [CTM16], limited guarantees, heuristic real-data steps, and insufficient analysis of tightness and costs.  
- Suggestions for improvement: Empirically quantify bound tightness and relate to ESS/R-hat/Stein, provide annealing schedule guidance and variance-reduction analysis, theoretically study approximate reverse-initialization bias, compare more directly with [CTM16], and report wall-clock costs.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline reject — well executed and useful but limited novelty and strongest guarantees confined to simulated data; needs tighter analysis and clearer positioning to be stronger.
