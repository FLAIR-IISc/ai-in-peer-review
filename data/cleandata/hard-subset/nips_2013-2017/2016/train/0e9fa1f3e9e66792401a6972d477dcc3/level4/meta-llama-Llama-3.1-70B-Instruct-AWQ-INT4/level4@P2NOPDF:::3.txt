The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, similar to the Tempered Transitions (TT) method, but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The technical contributions include implementing BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference.

This paper has both strong and weak points. The main strength is that using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators is a simple and easy-to-implement idea, potentially high-impact, especially in probabilistic programming systems. However, the novelty and depth of the technical contribution are concerns. The authors cite an existing work on BDMC but devote significant space to reiterating its description, making the main technical contributions seem rushed and affecting the writing quality.

The technical contributions, while modest, include the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. However, this observation, although nice, is not entirely novel. A notable omission is the citation of Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions, which is highly relevant and shares similarities with BDMC. The authors could have explored connections between the two methods, particularly in the context of tempering MCMC transition moves.

The analogy with TT raises questions about the choice of βt values, which is crucial in TT for achieving a good acceptance ratio. The authors could have provided more insight into the choice of βt values and their impact on the gap between the log upper and lower bounds. A systematic study of how the bounds vary with the step size would be beneficial. Additionally, the "auto" part in AutoBDMC is unclear, and an opportunity exists to develop a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until reliable inference results are obtained.

Regarding clarity, several issues arise. The definition of $\vec{v}$ is ambiguous, and the use of inconsistent labels ("rev" and "fwd" vs. "for" and "back") is confusing. The estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ is not defined, making it difficult to follow the equations. Equation (6) appears to be incorrect, and the definition of AutoBDMC is unclear.

In the experiments, the choice of βt is not clearly explained, and the code snippet in the appendix suggests that AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T. The acronym RAIS and its variants are not defined, and the figures could benefit from larger axes and tick labels. Finally, there are a few typos, including missing parentheses in the DKL terms and an incorrect citation.

Despite these criticisms, the paper has potential, and the proposed method could be useful. The authors' response to these concerns, particularly regarding clarity and related work, will be crucial in determining the paper's acceptability.
