- Proposes BREAD: relates the gap between reverse and forward AIS log marginal likelihood estimates to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and AIS-produced samples.  
- Builds on BDMC and refines prior work by linking bounds directly to posterior-sample quality rather than only marginal likelihood estimation.  
- Applicable to algorithms viewable as importance samplers on an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC).  
- Integrated implementations in WebPPL and Stan; used to detect and fix a WebPPL Gaussian sampling bug.  
- Empirical evaluation on five models shows simulated-data behavior often mirrors real-data behavior when using fitted hyperparameters.  
- Demonstrated utility for guiding modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed factorization) and debugging inference.  
- Theoretical contribution: clear link between BDMC bounds and Jeffreys divergence, moving beyond heuristic MCMC diagnostics.  
- Practical contribution: tool integration into popular PPLs and demonstration of practical usefulness.  
- Novelty: extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding posterior-sample divergence is original and useful.  
- Main limitation: guarantees require an exact posterior draw for reverse AIS, so rigorous guarantees hold only for simulated data.  
- Real-data protocol (using fitted hyperparameters and short MCMC warmup) is heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees.  
- Dependence on fitted hyperparameters risks bias under model misspecification or multimodality; limited evidence beyond five examples.  
- Method restricted to AIS/SMC-like algorithms and does not directly validate generic MCMC (e.g., HMC/NUTS) unless wrapped in AIS/SMC.  
- No direct head-to-head empirical comparisons with standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, multichain behavior), especially on challenging multimodal cases.  
- AIS estimates can be heavy-tailed; paper offers limited guidance on stabilizing bounds or variance-reduction strategies.  
- Experiments are informative but modest in scope and do not stress-test high-dimensional or strongly multimodal targets.  
- Clarity is good overall, but paper should state precise algorithmic conditions for the bound and give guidance on annealing schedules, number of distributions, and chains.  
- Suggested experiments: controlled comparisons with standard diagnostics, robustness under model misspecification, sensitivity analyses for annealing/transition choices, and empirical tightness assessments against true Jeffreys divergence.  
- Suggested extension: clarify transferability to HMC/NUTS workflows or provide an AIS wrapper for HMC to enable BREAD.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear theoretical link, practical integrations, and potential value for PPL developers and users.  
- Arguments against acceptance: core guarantees only for simulated data, limited empirical validation and comparisons, and restricted applicability to AIS/SMC-style samplers.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject—promising direction but needs stronger empirical validation and scope expansion to improve robustness and generality.
