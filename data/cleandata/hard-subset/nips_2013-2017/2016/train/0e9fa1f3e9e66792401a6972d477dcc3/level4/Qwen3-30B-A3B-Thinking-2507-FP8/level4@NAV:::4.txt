

This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for evaluating MCMC convergence that leverages the Bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds for the log marginal likelihood (utilizing annealed importance sampling). The authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds serves as a bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution, yielding a single scalar metric for assessing convergence behavior. Given that the method requires synthetic data generated from the joint distribution, the authors present experimental evidence showing its synthetic analyses predict real-world performance. They further illustrate applications for model selection and debugging. Overall, the paper is well-motivated, readable, and presents a clearly useful method with a reasonably compelling evaluation. However, Section 3 is notably harder to follow than other sections; the transition into 3.1 is particularly abrupt, launching into variable and equation definitions without motivation. If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 is a novel contribution (which appears to be the case), this novelty should be explicitly stated, as the paper's core contribution hinges on it. Crucially, the paper never explicitly defines AutoBDMC. Based on my understanding, it appears to refer to the approach of using AIS and rAIS for MCMC convergence analysis, and this definition should be provided early, especially before Section 3.2 references "this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC." Minor points: Line 149 likely requires correction from p(theta, z | y) to p(theta, z, y). The paper inconsistently uses y and D to denote data/observations, causing occasional confusion. The WebPPL code in the supplement should specify compatible WebPPL versions and provide installation instructions (ideally as a npm-installed package); a similar Stan code release would be highly beneficial.
