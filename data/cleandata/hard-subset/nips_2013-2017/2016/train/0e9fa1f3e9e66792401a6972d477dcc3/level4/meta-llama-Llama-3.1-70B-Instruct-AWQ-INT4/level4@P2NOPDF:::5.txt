The authors address the problem of evaluating the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure to achieve this. The idea is to select the MCMC scheme that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. However, a significant challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To overcome this, the authors suggest some heuristics. 

Their solution, in the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involves performing diagnostics on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data. This approach leverages the fact that it is easier to produce good approximate samples for such posteriors. The proposed methodology is then tested on several examples. 

The concept of using stochastic lower bounds given by Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing. Although the methodology appears to be specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and only provides diagnostics based on the marginal likelihood, it still holds value as an attempt to develop theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation has several drawbacks that make the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing. These drawbacks stem from the heuristic methodology attempting to overcome the need for an exact sample from the posterior for Bidirectional Monte Carlo to be valid.

Three primary concerns arise from this approach. Firstly, the authors assume prior knowledge of a value of the hyperparameters, η_real, which is representative of the entire posterior. This assumption is based on two strong and unjustified claims: that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged (which is the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value. 

Secondly, the authors assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has similar properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) to the original posterior. This assumption is also unjustified and may require careful justification, particularly in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It appears that the authors are attempting to overcome the challenges posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo by "changing the problem," i.e., performing diagnostics on a different posterior.

Thirdly, the authors adopt an optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. However, this assumption may not always hold. As a result, the proposed methodology relies on several assumptions closely related to the actual thing being assessed (the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm).

Although Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not seem to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology, these examples are not convincing enough to claim that the issues will not be critical in more complicated examples. The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failures in multimodal cases or when few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive. However, they should explain why their method can perform better in such scenarios and provide evidence to support this claim.

Furthermore, it would be beneficial to include simulations comparing the proposed method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in sections 4) to demonstrate scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.
