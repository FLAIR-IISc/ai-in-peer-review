This paper presents a method – termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) – for assessing MCMC convergence. The method leverages the recently developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood (relying on annealed importance sampling). The authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, offering a scalar measure for evaluating MCMC convergence. Recognizing that rigorous bounds are directly applicable only to simulated data, the authors provide empirical evidence demonstrating the method's predictive power when applied to real-world data. They further showcase applications in model selection and debugging. Overall, I found this paper well-written and motivated. The presented method appears genuinely useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably convincing. I found Section 3 more technically dense than the rest of the paper. The transition into Section 3.1 is particularly abrupt, introducing variables and equations without sufficient contextualization. If the derivation of the divergence bound in Section 3.1 represents a novel contribution – which I believe it does – this should be explicitly highlighted, as the paper's novelty appears to hinge on this result. What, precisely, *is* BREAD? This remains undefined throughout the paper. My understanding is that BREAD is the approach of utilizing AIS and reverse AIS (rAIS) to analyze MCMC convergence, and I would expect a clear statement to that effect. By the time Section 3.2 refers to "…this poses a challenge for BREAD," the method should be formally defined. Several minor points: Line 149: Should p(✓, z | y) be p(✓, z, y)? Notational inconsistencies exist, with the paper alternating between using *y* and *D* to denote data/observations, which occasionally caused confusion. Regarding the WebPPL code in the supplemental material: it should be accompanied by (a) information regarding the compatible WebPPL version(s), and (b) instructions for integrating it into a WebPPL distribution for practical use. Ideally, releasing this as a publicly installable WebPPL package via npm would greatly enhance usability. A similar release for the Stan implementation would also be highly valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence using BDMC appears to be a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in sound theoretical principles (BDMC, AIS, KL divergence).
*   **Practical Utility:** The method provides a quantifiable metric for assessing MCMC convergence, addressing a long-standing challenge in probabilistic inference.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the method's applicability and predictive power.
*   **Debugging Application:** Demonstrating the method's utility in identifying bugs in probabilistic programming languages adds significant value.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** Section 3 is challenging to follow and lacks sufficient motivation for key equations.
*   **Definition of BREAD:** The method (BREAD) is not explicitly defined, leading to some ambiguity.
*   **Minor Notational Issues:** Inconsistencies in notation (y vs. D) could cause confusion for some readers.
*   **Reproducibility:** Lack of a readily installable package for WebPPL and Stan hinders immediate reproducibility and adoption.
