The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—essentially the same forward and reverse sweeps used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996]—and reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a general performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The technical contributions are implementations of BDMC in the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, plus a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

The paper has clear strengths and some weaknesses. The main strength is that using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators is a simple, elegant idea that is easy to implement and potentially high-impact—especially for probabilistic programming systems, which are the target use case here. I believe all such systems would benefit from implementing this method.

My main concerns are with novelty and the depth of the technical contribution. The authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC and (wisely) devote substantial space to explaining it. However, this makes Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel rushed, and the writing quality dips there, leading to clarity issues (see below). Setting clarity aside, the contributions relative to prior BDMC work seem modest. The primary novelty appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bounds itself provides an upper bound on the Jensen–Shannon divergence. This is a nice observation, though arguably not entirely new.

Related work is also incomplete. The paper does not cite Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353–366], which is highly relevant, and the BDMC manuscript also omits it. This is a missed opportunity to highlight important connections. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but as a tempering move to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final state from the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep; the final state from the forward sweep is proposed and accepted or rejected using the ratio of the accumulated importance weights from both sweeps. As far as I can tell, this acceptance ratio is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds. Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper interprets this acceptance probability via two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log Z_T − log Z_1 from above and below, noting that acceptance tends to 1 when the estimates converge.

This analogy raises the question of how the β_t schedule is chosen. In TT it is well known that large steps Δβ = β_{t+1} − β_t harm acceptance, while very small steps waste computation. The BDMC bound gap will also depend on Δβ, which offers clear guidance: if the bounds are not tight, rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ. Similarly, comparisons between MCMC transition operators are likely unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds about 1 nat; in that case, decrease Δβ. It is unclear what the “auto” in AutoBDMC entails, but there seems to be an opportunity to devise an adaptive schedule that iteratively reduces Δβ until sufficiently reliable results are obtained. The experiments would benefit from a systematic study of how the bounds vary with step size.

Despite these criticisms, this is not a bad paper. The proposed method is potentially very useful. If the authors can address the clarity issues and strengthen the related work discussion in the camera-ready, I would be inclined to argue for acceptance.

Clarity
- The definition of v as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is too loose. Presumably this means {z_{1:T−1}, θ_{1:T−1}}, but this is ambiguous given there are two AIS sweeps. The inconsistency between labels “rev”/“fwd” and “for”/“back” adds to the confusion—are these differences meaningful or accidental?
- The estimator p̂(y) is not defined in equations (5–7) and (9–11), making it hard to follow the derivations. Do the authors intend E[log R̂_rev] and E[log R̂_fwd]?
- Equation (6) as written is incorrect (the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical). Based on (4) and (10), the right-hand terms should be something like p(z, θ | y) q_back(v | z, θ, y).
- AutoBDMC is never unambiguously defined. Does it mean (a) computing the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence, and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme in Section 3.2?

Experiments
- It is not clear how β_t is chosen. The appendix code shows var step = 1/options.steps. I assume AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T, going from β_T = 1 to β_1 = 1/T? Please state this explicitly. In Figures 2 and 3, does “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” indicate BDMC is run with this number of steps?
- The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) is undefined in the captions, as are the variants (u)c(R)AIS (likely (un)collapsed variants).
- Please increase the size of axes and tick labels.

Typos
- Two closing parentheses are missing in the D_KL terms in the D_JS expression.
- In the NUTS citation: Homan → Hoffman.
