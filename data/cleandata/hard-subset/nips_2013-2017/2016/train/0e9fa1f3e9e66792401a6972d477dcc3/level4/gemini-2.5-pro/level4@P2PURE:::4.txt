This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for diagnosing MCMC convergence. The approach leverages Bidirectional Monte Carlo, which utilizes annealed importance sampling (AIS), to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds also provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence between the true posterior and the approximate posterior generated by the MCMC sampler. This gap serves as a single scalar metric for convergence. The method is designed for synthetic data generated from the model's joint distribution; to address this, the authors provide compelling experimental evidence suggesting that performance on synthetic data is predictive of performance on real-world data. They further illustrate the utility of AutoBDMC for model selection and debugging.

Overall, the paper is well-motivated and clearly written. The proposed method is demonstrably useful, and the experimental evaluation is compelling.

However, the clarity of the paper diminishes in Section 3, which is significantly harder to follow than other sections. The transition into Section 3.1 is particularly abrupt, introducing variables and equations without sufficient motivation. Furthermore, if the divergence bound presented in this section is a novel contribution—as it appears to be—this should be stated explicitly. This result seems to be a cornerstone of the paper's contribution, and its novelty should be highlighted.

A significant point of confusion is the lack of a precise definition for "AutoBDMC." The paper never explicitly states what the term encompasses. Based on my reading, AutoBDMC appears to be the overall framework of using AIS and reverse AIS to analyze MCMC convergence via the derived bounds. The paper would benefit greatly from an explicit definition. For instance, when Section 3.2 refers to a "challenge for AutoBDMC," the reader should already have a clear understanding of what AutoBDMC is.

A few minor points for improvement:
*   **Line 149:** There may be a typo. Should `p(theta, z | y)` be the joint `p(theta, z, y)`?
*   **Notation:** The paper inconsistently uses both `y` and `D` to denote observed data, which can be confusing.
*   **Supplemental Code:** The provided WebPPL code would be more useful if it included information about compatible WebPPL versions and instructions for integration. A better approach would be to release it as a formal WebPPL package installable via npm. A similar packaged release for the Stan implementation would also be valuable.
