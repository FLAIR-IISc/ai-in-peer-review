- Proposes evaluating MCMC transition operators using BDMC by measuring the gap between AIS-derived lower and upper bounds, implemented in Stan and WebPPL with a protocol for posterior inference evaluation.  
- Potentially high-impact: simple, implementable diagnostic for MCMC convergence, especially useful in probabilistic programming systems.  
- Implementation in widely used systems (Stan, WebPPL) is a strong practical contribution.  
- Core technical sections (3.1, 3.2) feel rushed and lack clarity, weakening the presentation.  
- Key insight: the BDMC lower–upper bound gap provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, but this may not be entirely novel.  
- Fails to cite and discuss Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996), which is highly relevant to heating/annealing sweeps and acceptance-ratio connections.  
- Should discuss implications of Tempered Transitions for choosing βt and consider an iterative “AutoBDMC” schedule that decreases step sizes until inference is reliable.  
- Notation and terminology issues: imprecise definition of 'v' (should specify {z1:T-1, θ1:T-1}), inconsistent labels (“rev”/“fwd”/“for”/“back”).  
- Equation problems: Equations (6) and (9–11) lack clarity about estimators used in expectations, and Equation (6) appears to have an error in KL terms.  
- “AutoBDMC” is mentioned but never explicitly defined.  
- Experimental details are insufficient: missing βt schedule description, unclear relation between HMC/NUTS steps and BDMC steps, and undefined acronyms (RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS) in figures.  
- Presentation issues: small axis labels/ticks reduce readability and minor typos/citation misspellings persist.  
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant, impactful method; practical implementations; insightful use of BDMC bound gap as divergence measure.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited technical novelty, incomplete related-work discussion, clarity problems, and insufficient experimental sensitivity analysis.  
- Overall quality: technically sound but needs equation clarifications and expanded empirical validation.  
- Clarity assessment: currently weak; improved notation, definitions, and explanations required.  
- Originality: moderate — novel application of BDMC but builds on established techniques.  
- Significance: high potential if clarity, related work, and experimental concerns are addressed.
