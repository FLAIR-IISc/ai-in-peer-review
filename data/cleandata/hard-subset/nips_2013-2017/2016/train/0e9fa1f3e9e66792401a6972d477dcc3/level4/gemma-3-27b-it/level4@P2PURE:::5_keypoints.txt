- Proposes selecting MCMC schemes by choosing the one with the tightest Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) upper/lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood for a fixed compute budget.  
- Uses heuristics to avoid BMC’s requirement for exact target samples by running diagnostics on posteriors from synthetic data, assuming approximate samples are easier to obtain there.  
- Interesting concept leveraging stochastic lower bounds from BMC as an MCMC convergence diagnostic, especially for AIS/SMC samplers and marginal-likelihood-based assessment.  
- Major drawback: the heuristic workaround undermines BMC’s theoretical guarantee and relies on questionable assumptions about obtaining near-exact samples.  
- Key concern 1: assumes prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value (η_real), typically from preliminary MCMC, which reintroduces the original convergence problem and oversimplifies the posterior via a single value.  
- Key concern 2: assumes the synthetic-data posterior from η_real has similar MCMC convergence properties to the real posterior, an unjustified claim that fails under model misspecification.  
- Key concern 3: assumes starting from η_real allows rapid generation of nearly exact posterior samples, which is not guaranteed and ties the diagnostic to the very convergence it aims to assess.  
- Empirical validation (Section 5.1) uses simple cases that do not demonstrate robustness to complex scenarios like multimodality or poor summary-statistics, leaving claims unconvincing.  
- Recommends clearer explanation of how the method addresses multimodality/summary-statistics failures and empirical comparisons against established diagnostics to show practical advantages.
