Summary
This paper tackles the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference and comparing alternative inference schemes. Building on Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), the authors use forward and reverse AIS chains to sandwich the log marginal likelihood and, crucially, show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds upper-bounds (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC. Because reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, the method provides rigorous guarantees only on simulated data. To assess performance on real data, the authors propose a heuristic protocol: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from those fitted hyperparameters, run BDMC on the simulated problem, and verify that AIS behavior on real and simulated data is similar. They integrate the approach into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on several models, use it to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations, and demonstrate that it can detect implementation bugs.

Relation to prior work
The paper relates to classical MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman), to AIS and thermodynamic/nonequilibrium identities (Neal; Jarzynski), to BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al.), and to sample-quality measures such as Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey). It is closely connected to contemporaneous “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka), which also uses BDMC-style reverse chains; the present paper focuses on expected bounds on Jeffreys divergence when reverse chains are initialized from the true posterior (available for simulated data).

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing use of BDMC to obtain theoretically grounded, quantitative diagnostics for sample quality via an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Clear connection to AIS/SMC as importance sampling on extended state spaces; the bound converges with increasing annealing steps.
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan; the method can inform modeling choices (e.g., collapsing) and exposed a real bug in WebPPL.
- Empirical protocol to approximate real-data diagnosis by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, with some evidence that forward-AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data for several models.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on an exact posterior sample confines the rigorous guarantee to simulated data. The proposed real-data protocol relies on strong, largely unverified assumptions:
  1) The availability of a representative hyperparameter estimate η_real (often obtained by MCMC or optimization). This risks circularity (assuming convergence to assess convergence) and reduces a potentially complex posterior to a point estimate.
  2) The posterior induced by data simulated from η_real behaves like the real-data posterior with respect to MCMC convergence. This may fail under model misspecification or when the real-data posterior has structure not captured by the fitted hyperparameters.
  3) Initializing the reverse chain near η_real and running a short MCMC produces an “almost exact” posterior sample. This optimism is not guaranteed, especially in high-dimensional or multimodal problems.
- The methodology is tailored to AIS/SMC (and diagnostics derived from marginal likelihood sandwiching). Its applicability to general MCMC samplers without an AIS/SMC wrapper is limited.
- Experimental support is modest: the examples are encouraging but do not convincingly establish robustness of the heuristics across challenging regimes (e.g., strong multimodality, heavy tails, pronounced misspecification).
- The paper critiques classical diagnostics in multimodal settings but does not demonstrate scenarios where the proposed method is clearly superior or complementary.

Quality
The core theoretical result—that the AIS forward/reverse gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation when initialized from an exact posterior sample—is sound and clearly motivated. However, the transition to real-data evaluation is heuristic and lacks formal guarantees. The experimental validation is careful within its scope but does not fully probe failure modes or sensitivity to the key assumptions.

Clarity
The presentation is generally clear and well organized. The assumptions underlying the real-data protocol and the conditions under which simulated data should mirror real-data inference behavior would benefit from more explicit discussion and guidance for practitioners.

Originality
Extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples and embedding this in probabilistic programming workflows is a novel and useful conceptual step. The idea of using BDMC-derived bounds as an MCMC diagnostic is original relative to standard convergence diagnostics.

Significance
If the practical limitations can be addressed, this could become a valuable tool for debugging inference implementations, comparing modeling choices, and stress-testing AIS/SMC pipelines. As it stands, the need for exact posterior samples (or strong heuristics) limits impact for routine real-data analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide head-to-head comparisons with standard diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS, split-R-hat, and Stein discrepancies), especially on multimodal or misspecified models, to demonstrate clear advantages or complementarity.
- Offer sensitivity analyses quantifying how the bound degrades when the reverse-chain initializer is approximate (varying S, dimensionality, and posterior complexity), and include challenging synthetic benchmarks where the assumptions are stressed.
- Report computational cost vs. diagnostic tightness (wall-clock trade-offs) and practical guidance on interpreting bound magnitudes.
- Discuss alternative reference distributions (e.g., tempered/variational posteriors) and relate to subjective divergence guarantees when the true posterior is unavailable.
- Clarify scope: when is BREAD advisable (AIS/SMC pipelines, PPL debugging) and when not (general-purpose MCMC without AIS scaffolding).

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical foundation for an expected upper bound on sample quality.
- Practical integration into major PPLs; demonstrated utility in model representation choices and debugging.
- Timely contribution addressing a recognized gap in rigorous MCMC diagnostics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Real-data applicability relies on heuristic assumptions closely tied to the very convergence issues being assessed.
- Limited empirical evidence on difficult regimes; no direct comparison to established diagnostics.
- Method primarily benefits AIS/SMC workflows, restricting generality.

Overall evaluation
An interesting and promising direction with clear theoretical underpinnings for simulated data and practical utility in PPL settings. However, the current reliance on heuristics for real data and the limited empirical comparisons reduce confidence in its broader applicability. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying the scope and limits would significantly improve the paper.
