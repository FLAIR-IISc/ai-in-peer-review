This paper addresses the challenge of evaluating the reliability and comparing the performance of different MCMC algorithms. The proposed approach leverages the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood derived from the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) framework. The core concept is to select the most efficient MCMC scheme by identifying which one yields the tightest BMC bounds within a fixed computational budget. A primary difficulty with this approach is that BMC's validity, particularly for the stochastic upper bound, hinges on the availability of an exact sample from the target distribution. To circumvent this requirement, the authors introduce several heuristics. Specifically for Bayesian posterior sampling, their solution involves conducting the diagnostic not on the true posterior, but on a surrogate posterior derived from synthetic data. This is motivated by the assumption that generating high-quality approximate samples is more feasible for this synthetic target. The paper concludes by demonstrating the methodology on a set of examples.

The central premise of employing BMC's stochastic lower bounds as an MCMC convergence diagnostic is novel and interesting. While the technique appears tailored to AIS and SMC samplers and provides a diagnostic based solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a commendable effort to develop more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the practical implementation of this method suffers from several significant drawbacks that, in my view, diminish its overall appeal. These shortcomings stem directly from the heuristics designed to bypass the need for an exact posterior sample, a strict requirement for valid BMC. I will highlight three primary concerns.

- First, the method presupposes prior knowledge of a hyperparameter value, η{real}, which is assumed to be representative of the entire posterior distribution (e.g., obtained from a prior MCMC run). This introduces a circular dependency, as it requires assuming the convergence of the preliminary MCMC run (the very problem the diagnostic aims to solve) and also presumes that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point estimate. Both of these are strong assumptions that may not hold in general.
- Second, the authors assume that the synthetic posterior, generated using data simulated from η{real}, exhibits convergence properties analogous to the true posterior. This is another unsubstantiated assumption that would require careful justification, particularly in settings with potential model misspecification. Effectively, to circumvent the main difficulty of BMC, the authors seem to be "changing the problem" by performing diagnostics on a different, potentially simpler, posterior distribution.
- Third, the approach is predicated on the optimistic assumption that initializing from η{real} enables the MCMC sampler to rapidly generate near-exact samples from the posterior. While plausible in some cases, this is not guaranteed.

Consequently, the proposed methodology rests on a series of assumptions that are, critically, intertwined with the very property being evaluated—the convergence of the original MCMC algorithm on the true posterior. I acknowledge that the experiments in Section 5.1 present simple cases where these limitations do not seem to impact the method's conclusions. However, this limited set of straightforward examples is insufficient to demonstrate that these acknowledged issues would not become critical in more complex, high-dimensional, or otherwise challenging scenarios.

In the paper, the authors critique existing diagnostic tools for their potential failures in multimodal settings or when low-dimensional summary statistics are inadequate (lines 203-205). It is therefore incumbent upon the authors to articulate why their proposed method is more robust in these specific scenarios and to provide empirical evidence to support such claims. More broadly, the paper would be significantly strengthened by direct comparisons with commonly used diagnostic methods (such as those mentioned in Section 4). Such simulations should be designed to highlight specific scenarios where the proposed technique offers more reliable or insightful diagnostics.
