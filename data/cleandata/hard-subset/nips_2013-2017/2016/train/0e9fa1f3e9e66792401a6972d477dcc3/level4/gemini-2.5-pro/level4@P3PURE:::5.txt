This paper addresses the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. The proposed method uses the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The core idea is to select, from a set of available MCMC schemes, the one that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational budget.

A key challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors' main contribution is a set of heuristics to overcome this requirement. In the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, their solution involves performing the diagnostic on a posterior generated from synthetic data rather than the actual data. This approach leverages the fact that it is often easier to generate high-quality approximate samples from such a synthetic posterior. The methodology is then demonstrated on several examples.

The central idea of using stochastic bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is novel and promising. Although the methodology appears specific to AIS and SMC samplers and provides a diagnostic based solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools.

However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, significantly reduce the appeal of the proposed methodology. These drawbacks arise from the heuristics used to circumvent the need for an exact posterior sample. I will highlight three major concerns:

1.  The method requires a pre-existing hyperparameter value, η{real}, which is assumed to be representative of the true posterior. This introduces two strong and generally unjustified assumptions: first, that the algorithm used to find η{real} had already converged (which is the very problem the diagnostic aims to solve), and second, that a single point estimate can adequately summarize the entire posterior distribution.

2.  The method assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η{real} has convergence properties similar to those of the original posterior. This is a strong claim that requires careful justification, particularly in contexts involving model misspecification. In effect, the proposed diagnostic avoids the primary difficulty of BMC by "changing the problem"—it assesses convergence on a different, and potentially simpler, posterior distribution.

3.  The approach relies on the optimistic assumption that initializing an MCMC algorithm at η{real} will allow it to quickly draw a nearly exact sample from the synthetic posterior. While this may happen in some cases, it is not a guaranteed property.

Therefore, the proposed methodology rests on several assumptions that are critically intertwined with the very property it aims to assess: the convergence of the original MCMC algorithm.

While I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not appear to invalidate the method's conclusions, these examples are not sufficiently convincing. They fail to provide strong evidence that the acknowledged limitations will not be critical in more complex and realistic settings.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest the following:

*   The authors critique existing diagnostic tools for their potential failures with multimodal distributions or when low-dimensional summary statistics are insufficient (lines 203-205). The paper should explicitly explain why their marginal-likelihood-based approach is better suited for these challenging scenarios and provide empirical evidence to support these claims.

*   The experimental section would be significantly improved by direct comparisons with commonly used diagnostic methods (such as those described in Section 4). Including simulations that highlight specific scenarios where the proposed method provides more reliable diagnostics than established techniques would make a much stronger case for its adoption.
