- Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC, to bound Jeffreys divergence between AIS approximate posterior and true posterior via the gap between forward and reverse AIS marginal likelihood bounds.  
- Implements BREAD in Stan and WebPPL and proposes a protocol for diagnosing inference quality on real data.  

Strengths:
- Elegant and intuitive core idea: using BDMC bound gap as a principled, information-theoretic measure of MCMC sample quality.  
- High potential significance for probabilistic programming: provides automated validation and debugging (e.g., found a WebPPL bug).  
- Convincing empirical application: demonstrates practical guidance (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and quantitative comparisons.  

Weaknesses:
- Originality concerns: omits discussion of Neal (1996) Tempered Transitions, which is closely related to bidirectional annealing and may reduce novelty claims.  
- Missing analysis of key hyperparameters: no systematic study of how annealing schedule (β_t) and number of steps affect the bound gap and variance of importance weights.  
- Technical clarity: Section 3.1 derivation is condensed, has ambiguous notation and typos, making the core theoretical claim hard to verify.  

Detailed comments / questions:
- Strongly recommend adding discussion comparing BREAD/BDMC to Neal’s Tempered Transitions and clarifying their relationship.  
- Clarify definition of v in AIS (does it mean the sequence {x_1,…,x_{T-1}}?) and other ambiguous notation.  
- Provide a step-by-step derivation for Equations (6–11), explicitly define the estimator (e.g., log R_hat) whose expectation is computed, and show how the KL terms arise.  
- Fix typos in the Jeffreys divergence expression (missing closing parentheses).  
- Experimental details: specify how annealing schedules (β_t) were chosen (linear or otherwise) and whether “Number of AIS steps” refers to T.  
- Define undefined acronyms in captions (e.g., (u)c(R)AIS) and improve figure font sizes for readability.  

Conclusion:
- Core contribution is valuable and practical; acceptance contingent on addressing prior-work alignment and improving clarity of derivations and experimental details.
