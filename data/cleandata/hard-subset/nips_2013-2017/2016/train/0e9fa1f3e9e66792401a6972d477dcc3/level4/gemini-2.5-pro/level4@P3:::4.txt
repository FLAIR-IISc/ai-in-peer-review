This paper presents a method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), for quantitatively assessing the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The approach extends the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) on simulated data, both in a "forward" direction (from prior to posterior) and a "reverse" direction (from posterior to prior). This process yields stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood, respectively.

The key theoretical contribution of this work is to show that the gap between these two bounds serves as an upper bound in expectation for the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the distribution of the approximate samples and the true posterior. This provides a single, principled scalar value for evaluating the quality of the MCMC sampler's output. Since the reverse pass requires an exact posterior sample, the method is rigorously applicable only to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose and validate a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world problems: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate new data from these fitted hyperparameters to evaluate the inference algorithm. The authors implement their method within two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility for comparing model representations and for debugging inference implementations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and assessing sample quality is of fundamental importance, yet it remains a notoriously difficult challenge. This paper offers a novel and practical solution. The proposed method, BREAD, provides a quantitative and interpretable measure of sample quality that goes beyond existing heuristics. This is particularly valuable for users of "black-box" inference systems like probabilistic programming languages, who need to trust the results they obtain.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical result connecting the BDMC bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence is elegant and well-founded. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors wisely validate their core assumption—that results on simulated data are predictive of performance on real data—across five different models. The experiments showing the utility of BREAD for comparing model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and for debugging (finding a bug in WebPPL) are compelling and clearly demonstrate the method's practical value.
*   **Clarity & Originality:** The paper is, for the most part, well-written, well-motivated, and easy to follow. The extension of BDMC from a tool for estimating marginal likelihoods to a diagnostic for posterior approximation quality is a novel and clever contribution.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, but its clarity could be further improved in a few areas.

1.  **Clarity of the Main Contribution:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound in Section 3.1 is the paper's central theoretical contribution. This could be highlighted more explicitly. The section currently begins rather abruptly with a series of equations. It would benefit from a clearer introduction that states the goal of the section (i.e., to derive an upper bound on the divergence) before diving into the mathematical details. Explicitly stating that Equations (8) and (11) lead to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence would strengthen the narrative.

2.  **Minor Notational Issues:**
    *   The paper appears to use `y` and `D` interchangeably to refer to the observed data. Standardizing on one symbol would improve consistency.
    *   In Equation (3), the relationship `p(y) p(θ, z|y) = p(θ, z, y)` is fundamental, but presenting it this way might briefly cause confusion. A minor rephrasing or an additional clarifying sentence could prevent any potential misreading.

3.  **Reproducibility and Usability of Code:** The authors provide code for WebPPL and Stan, which is commendable. The utility of this contribution could be significantly increased by providing more detailed instructions. For instance, for the WebPPL code, specifying which version(s) it is compatible with and how to integrate it would be very helpful. Even better would be to release the code as an easily installable package (e.g., via npm for WebPPL), which would lower the barrier for other researchers and practitioners to adopt and build upon this excellent work.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and useful contribution to the field. It addresses an important, difficult problem with a novel approach, supports its claims with both theory and strong empirical evidence, and demonstrates clear practical applications. The weaknesses are minor and related to presentation and code usability, which can be easily addressed. I recommend acceptance.
