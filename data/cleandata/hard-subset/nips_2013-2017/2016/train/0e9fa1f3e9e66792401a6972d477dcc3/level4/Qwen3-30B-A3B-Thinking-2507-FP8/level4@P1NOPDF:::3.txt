

The authors propose a simple yet powerful method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which leverages two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps to produce lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The core contribution is using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance metric for MCMC operators. The paper demonstrates implementations for Stan and WebPPL and proposes a protocol for posterior inference quality assessment.  

This work has significant potential impact, particularly for probabilistic programming systems—its intended use case. The simplicity of the approach makes it highly implementable, and I believe all such systems would benefit from adopting it. However, the technical contributions require careful evaluation. While the authors correctly cite BDMC as prior work, they dedicate substantial space to re-explaining it, leaving sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed and reducing the perceived novelty. Crucially, the paper omits a key connection to Neal’s Tempered Transitions (TT) method (Stat Comput 1996), which performs identical forward/reverse AIS sweeps but uses them to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. TT’s acceptance ratio (based on the ratio of accumulated importance weights) is precisely the ratio of BDMC’s stochastic bounds, and Neal’s Section 3.3 explicitly shows this ratio bounds log Z_T - log Z_1 and implies perfect acceptance when bounds converge. This omission is problematic, as it misses an opportunity to contextualize BDMC’s relationship to TT.  

The observation that the bound gap upper-bounds the Jensen-Shannon divergence is insightful but not entirely novel. More critically, the paper lacks a systematic analysis of how the bound gap depends on the temperature schedule parameter Δβ. In TT, Δβ critically affects acceptance rates (large steps reduce acceptance, small steps waste computation), and the same intuition applies to BDMC: a large gap suggests insufficiently fine temperature steps. The authors should clarify that comparisons between MCMC operators are unreliable when the log-bound gap exceeds 1 nat, necessitating smaller Δβ. The "auto" aspect of AutoBDMC remains unclear—does it imply adaptive Δβ scheduling? If so, this could be a valuable extension to iteratively refine Δβ until reliable bounds are obtained, but the paper does not elaborate.  

Several clarity issues undermine the presentation:  
- **Definition of $\vec{v}$**: The description "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous. The intended meaning ($z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}$) should be explicitly stated, especially given dual AIS sweeps.  
- **Inconsistent labeling**: The use of "rev/fwd" versus "for/back" creates confusion—these terms should be standardized.  
- **Undefined estimators**: Equations (5–7) and (9–11) reference $\hat{p}(y)$ without defining the estimator, making the derivations hard to follow. It seems the authors intended $E[\log \hat{R}_{\text{rev}}]$ and $E[\log \hat{R}_{\text{fwd}}]$.  
- **Equation (6)**: As written, the KL divergence has identical distributions, rendering it meaningless. Based on equations (4) and (10), the correct form should involve $p(z, \theta | y)$ and $q_{\text{back}}(v | z, \theta, y)$.  
- **AutoBDMC definition**: The method is never clearly defined. It appears to combine (a) using the bound gap for convergence assessment and (b) the fixed hyperparameter scheme from Section 3.2. This should be explicitly stated.  

The experiments also need refinement:  
- The choice of $\beta_t$ is unclear. The appendix code uses `step = 1/options.steps`, implying $T$ steps from $\beta_T = 1$ to $\beta_1 = 1/T$. This should be specified in the main text.  
- Figure captions omit definitions for acronyms like RAIS (reverse AIS) and variants (u/c)(R)AIS.  
- Axes and tick labels in Figures 2–3 should be larger for readability.  

Minor typos require correction:  
- Missing closing parentheses in D_JS expressions.  
- "Homan" in the NUTS citation should be "Hoffman."  

Despite these issues, the core idea is compelling. If the authors address the clarity gaps, explicitly connect BDMC to TT, and clarify AutoBDMC’s adaptive scheduling potential, this work could be highly valuable. The proposed metric is simple, implementable, and directly applicable to probabilistic programming. With revisions, I would support acceptance. For now, the current version’s lack of contextual depth and technical precision limits its impact. The paper’s strength lies in its practicality, but its technical contribution appears modest relative to the existing TT framework. Addressing these points would transform a promising idea into a significant contribution.
