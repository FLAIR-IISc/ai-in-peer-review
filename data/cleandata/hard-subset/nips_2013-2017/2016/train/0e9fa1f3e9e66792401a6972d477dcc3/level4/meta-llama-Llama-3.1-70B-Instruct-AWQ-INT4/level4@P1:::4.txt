This paper presents a method for assessing MCMC convergence using the recently-developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique. The authors show that BDMC can be used to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. The method, which the authors refer to as Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), is applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC).

The paper begins by introducing the problem of measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples in MCMC inference. The authors note that traditional heuristics for diagnosing convergence are not reliable and that there is a need for quantitative measures of convergence. They then introduce BDMC, which produces stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the gap between these bounds is itself a bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples.

The authors provide experimental evidence that their method can be used to analyze convergence on synthetic data generated from the joint distribution, and that the results are predictive of behavior on real-world data. They also demonstrate the use of their method for model selection and debugging purposes.

Overall, I found this paper to be well-motivated and clearly written. The presented method seems useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling. However, I had some difficulty following Section 3, particularly the transition into 3.1, which launches into variable and equation definitions without motivating them. It would be helpful to clarify the novelty and contribution of the paper, particularly with respect to the divergence bound in Section 3.1.

Additionally, there are some minor issues with notation and terminology. For example, the paper switches between using y and D to denote data/observations, which can be confusing at times. It would be helpful to standardize the notation throughout the paper.
