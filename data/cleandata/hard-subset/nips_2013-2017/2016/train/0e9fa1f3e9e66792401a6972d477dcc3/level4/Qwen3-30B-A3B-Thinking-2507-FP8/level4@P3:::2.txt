

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to evaluate the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors show that BDMC can be used to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by methods like annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). This builds on Grosse et al. (2015), who introduced BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods, but represents a significant conceptual leap by applying it to posterior inference quality assessment.

The paper makes four key contributions. First, it provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that BDMC yields an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. Second, it presents a technique for exactly computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, demonstrating the bound's accuracy. Third, it proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and validating consistency between real and simulated data. Fourth, it integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and applies it to analyze model representations in probabilistic programming.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality, with careful derivation of the upper bound and validation on toy distributions where exact computation is possible. The experimental section is thorough, with five realistic models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, time series modeling, and mixture-of-Gaussians) applied to both real and simulated data. The authors convincingly show that inference behavior on simulated data with fitted hyperparameters closely matches that on real data, validating their protocol. The integration with WebPPL and Stan is practical and well-executed, and the method's application to analyzing model representations (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) demonstrates clear utility.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with the necessary mathematical details provided in the main text and supplementary material. The experimental results are presented with appropriate visualizations, including quartiles of AIS estimates to handle outliers, which is particularly thoughtful given the nature of the data.

In terms of originality, this work represents a significant extension of BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation. While previous work has focused on estimating the marginal likelihood (Grosse et al., 2015), this paper uniquely applies the framework to evaluate the accuracy of posterior samples. The connection to probabilistic programming languages is particularly novel, as it addresses a critical gap in the field: the lack of reliable methods to assess the quality of black-box inference. The related work section effectively contextualizes the paper within the broader literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996) and recent methods for estimating sample quality (Gorham & Mackey, 2015; Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

The significance of this work is substantial. As probabilistic programming languages become more widely adopted, the need for reliable inference quality assessment grows. BREAD provides a general, rigorous, and automatic procedure that can be integrated into these systems, giving users confidence in their results. The paper's insights into model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed models) have practical value for practitioners, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL through BREAD application demonstrates its utility as a debugging tool.

While the paper is strong overall, there are some limitations to consider. The method is inherently limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained, which typically requires simulated data. The authors propose a protocol for real-world data using fitted hyperparameters, but the effectiveness of this approach depends on the quality of the hyperparameter estimates, and there's no formal analysis of how this approximation affects the bounds. Additionally, while the authors argue that their method informs broader MCMC design choices, the paper primarily focuses on AIS and SMC, with less exploration of applicability to other MCMC methods like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampling.

The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing convergence diagnostics. While the authors note that BREAD "can rigorously demonstrate convergence" while other diagnostics "cannot confirm convergence," a detailed comparison with methods like Gelman-Rubin statistics or effective sample size would strengthen the claims about the method's advantages.

Despite these limitations, this is an important contribution that addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference. The paper provides a theoretically sound, practically applicable method for evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference, with clear demonstration of its utility in probabilistic programming systems. The integration with WebPPL and Stan makes it immediately relevant to a broad audience of researchers and practitioners.

Strengths:
- Theoretical novelty in extending BDMC to posterior inference evaluation
- Rigorous validation on toy distributions and realistic models
- Practical implementation in two major probabilistic programming languages
- Demonstrated utility for model representation analysis
- Clear, well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
- Limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained
- Protocol for real-world data relies on fitted hyperparameters without formal analysis of approximation effects
- Limited exploration of applicability to MCMC methods beyond AIS/SMC
- Insufficient comparison with existing convergence diagnostics

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It addresses a fundamental problem with clear theoretical foundations and practical implementation. The method is well-motivated, rigorously analyzed, and demonstrated to be useful in real-world contexts. While the limitations are worth noting, they do not undermine the paper's substantial contribution. I recommend acceptance.
