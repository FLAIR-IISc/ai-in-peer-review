

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework proposed by Grosse et al. (NIPS 2015) to directly bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples obtained via MCMC. The key insight is that the gap between BDMC's stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood directly upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. This provides a single, interpretable metric for evaluating inference quality, addressing a critical challenge in probabilistic programming where users need to assess the reliability of black-box inference without expert knowledge.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a theoretically sound connection between BDMC and posterior approximation quality, extending Grosse et al.'s work beyond marginal likelihood estimation. Second, it presents a practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating datasets from estimated hyperparameters and validating consistency between simulated and real data. Third, it integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility for model selection (comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization) and debugging (identifying a subtle bug in WebPPL's Gaussian sampling).

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a genuinely important problem in probabilistic modeling. The theoretical development in Section 3 is the paper's strongest aspect, though the transition into Section 3.1 could be smoother. The authors should explicitly state that the Jeffreys divergence bound is a novel contribution, as it's central to the paper's significance. The experimental validation is comprehensive, with five models across Stan, and the protocol for real-world data validation (Section 5.1) provides strong evidence that simulated data analysis reliably predicts real-world performance.

The paper's clarity is generally good, though some notational inconsistencies could be improved (e.g., switching between y and D to denote data). The integration into WebPPL and Stan is particularly valuable, though the paper would benefit from more specific guidance on implementation (e.g., version compatibility and installation instructions for the code). The comparison to related work, particularly Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), is helpful but could more explicitly clarify how BREAD's theoretical guarantees differ from their "subjective divergence" approach.

The method's significance is strong for probabilistic programming communities. By providing a rigorous, automated way to assess inference quality, BREAD addresses a major barrier to wider adoption of probabilistic programming languages by non-experts. The debugging application (Section 5.2.2) demonstrates practical value beyond theoretical contribution. The paper's focus on both theoretical guarantees and practical implementation makes it particularly relevant to NIPS readers.

Regarding the review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical justification and thorough experimental validation. The protocol for real-world data analysis effectively addresses a key limitation of BDMC.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, though some sections (particularly Section 3) would benefit from more explicit motivation for the mathematical developments.
- Originality: While building on BDMC, the paper makes a novel contribution by extending it to bound the Jeffreys divergence directly, which is more relevant for assessing posterior approximation quality than marginal likelihood.
- Significance: The method provides a practical tool for probabilistic programming users to assess inference reliability, addressing a critical gap in current practice.

I have a few minor concerns. The paper states that BREAD "is applicable to algorithms which can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space" (Section 1), but doesn't clearly specify the exact class of MCMC algorithms to which it applies. A more precise characterization would strengthen the paper. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates the method's utility for model selection, it would be helpful to see more discussion of how the BREAD metric could guide practitioners in choosing between different MCMC algorithms for specific problems.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference. The method is theoretically sound, practically implemented, and validated across multiple models. The paper's integration into mainstream probabilistic programming languages makes it immediately useful to a broad audience. The minor weaknesses in presentation and notation do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity in the theoretical presentation and to provide more specific implementation guidance for the code. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, and the BREAD method has clear potential to improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems.
