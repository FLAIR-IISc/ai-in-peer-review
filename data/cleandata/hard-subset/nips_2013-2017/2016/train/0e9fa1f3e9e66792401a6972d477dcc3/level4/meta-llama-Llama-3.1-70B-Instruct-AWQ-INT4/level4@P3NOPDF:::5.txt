The authors address the problem of evaluating the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose utilizing the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure to achieve this goal. The idea is to select the MCMC scheme that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds given the same computational effort. However, the main challenge lies in the fact that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.

The authors propose some heuristics to overcome this issue. In the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, their solution involves performing the diagnostic on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data. This approach leverages the fact that it is easier to produce good approximate samples for such posteriors. Finally, the proposed methodology is tested on some examples.

The concept of utilizing stochastic lower bounds given by Bidirectional Monte Carlo to perform MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing. Although the methodology appears to be specific to Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) sampler schemes and produces diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it still holds value as an attempt to develop theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, make the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing.

These drawbacks arise from the heuristic methodology attempting to overcome the requirement of Bidirectional Monte Carlo needing an exact sample from the posterior to be valid. I would like to highlight three of these drawbacks. Firstly, the authors assume prior knowledge of a value of the hyperparameters, η_real, which is representative of the whole posterior. This assumption is based on two strong and unjustified premises: that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged (which is essentially the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value.

Secondly, the authors assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has similar properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) to the original posterior. This assumption lacks justification, particularly in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It appears that the authors are attempting to overcome the difficulties posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo by "changing the problem," i.e., performing diagnostics on a different posterior.

Lastly, the authors adopt an optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. However, this assumption may not always hold. Therefore, the proposed methodology relies on various assumptions closely related to the actual thing being assessed – the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm.

I appreciate the fact that Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not seem to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology. Nevertheless, these few examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues recognized by the authors will not be critical in more complicated examples.

The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failure in multimodal cases or when few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive. I believe the authors should provide explanations for why their method can perform better in such scenarios and offer evidence to support their claims. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to include simulations comparing their method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in sections 4) to demonstrate scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.

Overall, while the idea of utilizing Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting, the proposed methodology requires further development to address the drawbacks and assumptions mentioned above.
