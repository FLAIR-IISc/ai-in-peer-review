The authors propose a general method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on a recently proposed method known as Bidimensional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The BDMC method sweeps two annealed significance samples (AIS), essentially the same sweep used in the tempered transition (TT) method [Neil State & Comp 1996], but reinterpreting the accumulated probabilities as the lower and upper limits on the marginal probability. This paper proposes using the difference between these two limits as a general performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The presented technical contribution is an implementation of BDMC for the Stein and WebbPPPL probabilistic programming system and a protocol for evaluating the quality of post-estimation. This paper has some strong points and some not so strong. The main strong point is that using BDMC to assess the convergence of MCMC operators is a very simple idea, and it is easy to implement, which in my opinion means that this work is potentially highly impactful. This is especially true in the context of probabilistic programming systems, which is indeed the use case envisioned here, and I think all such systems should implement this method at least. My main concern is with the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The authors cite an arxiv submission on BDMC as existing work, but (I think wisely) choose to dedicate a relatively large amount of space to reiterate its details. Unfortunately, this does not mean that the main technical contributions presented in sections 3.1 and 3.2 are somewhat hasty, and unfortunately it also means that the quality of writing has been slightly reduced. This results in some issues of clarity (see below). Setting aside the clarity issues, the technical contribution in this paper is somewhat modest. Relative to existing work on BDMC, the main novelty appears in the realization that the difference between the lower and upper limits provides an upper limit on the Jensen-Shannon deviation itself. This is indeed a very good observation, although arguably not entirely new. This brings me to a point about related work. The authors, somewhat curiously, do not cite Redford Neil's work on tempered transition [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which is highly relevant in this context. This citation is also missing from the manuscript on BDMC, which makes me wonder if the authors actually know about it. If so, it is unfortunate in the sense that the opportunity to point out the relationships between the two methods has been missed. TT sweeps the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" as BDMC, but in the interest of "tampering" MCMC transition moves to improve mixing in multimodal distribution. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep is used as the initial sample for the forward sweep, and the final sample from the forward sweep is then used as a proposal that is accepted or rejected according to the proportion of accumulated significance weights in both sweeps. The acceptance ratio in TT, as far as I can tell, is the exact ratio of the stochastic lower and upper limits. Section 3.3 of Neil's paper actually presents an explanation of this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bind the log ZT - log Z1 from above and below, given that a TT sampler will accept with probability 1 when both converge. The analogy with TT also leads me to think about why the authors pay more attention to the choice of βt values. It is well known in TT that very large steps in Δβ = β{t+1}-βt will lead to a poor acceptance ratio, while very small steps will ruin the Δβ calculation. The ratio of stochastic lower and upper limits in BDMC will also depend on Δβ and this provides some very clear intuition. If your two limits are not close enough, you need to rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ. Similarly, I suspect that the comparison between MCMC transition operators is likely to be unreliable when the difference between the log upper and lower limits is large >1 nat. When this happens, one should rerun Δβ. It was not entirely clear from reading the paper what the "auto" part of AutoBDMC is, but I think there is an opportunity to come up with some schedule here so that Δβ can be constantly reduced until sufficiently reliable estimation results are obtained. Experiments in this regard could be a little more intensive. It would be good to conduct a systematic study of how the lower and upper limit varies with the step size. Setting aside these criticisms, I think this is not a bad paper. As mentioned above, I think the method proposed by the authors is potentially quite useful.
