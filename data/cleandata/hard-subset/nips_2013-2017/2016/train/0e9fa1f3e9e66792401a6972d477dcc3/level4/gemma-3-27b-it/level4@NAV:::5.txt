The authors address the important problem of MCMC reliability assessment and scheme comparison, proposing a method based on stochastic bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) on the log marginal likelihood. The core idea—selecting the most efficient MCMC scheme by minimizing BMC stochastic bound widths for a given computational cost—is promising. A key challenge lies in BMC’s requirement for exact target samples to guarantee valid upper bounds, which the authors attempt to circumvent through heuristics. Their approach involves performing diagnostics on posteriors generated using synthetic data informed by initial MCMC estimates, capitalizing on the relative ease of obtaining good approximate samples in these scenarios. The methodology is then demonstrated with several examples.

The use of BMC-derived stochastic lower bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is an interesting concept. While potentially limited in applicability to AIS and SMC samplers, and focused solely on marginal likelihood-based diagnostics, it represents a valuable effort towards theoretically grounded MCMC assessment tools. However, the current implementation suffers from several drawbacks stemming from the heuristic adjustments made to address BMC’s exact sample requirement, significantly reducing the appeal of the proposed diagnostic. 

Specifically, the methodology relies on three questionable assumptions. First, it presupposes prior knowledge of representative hyperparameter values (\eta{real}) obtained from potentially unconverged preliminary MCMC runs, and assumes a single value can adequately summarize the posterior – both strong and often unwarranted assumptions. Second, it assumes the posterior generated from synthetic data based on \eta{real} faithfully reflects the properties of the original posterior, an assumption particularly suspect in cases of model mis-specification and lacking justification. This appears to address the BMC difficulty by effectively altering the diagnostic problem. Third, it optimistically posits that initializing MCMC from \eta{real} readily yields near-exact samples, a condition that isn’t guaranteed.  Crucially, these assumptions all depend on the very MCMC convergence properties the method aims to evaluate.

While Section 5.1 demonstrates scenarios where these issues do not demonstrably impact conclusions, the limited scope of these examples is insufficient to convincingly establish robustness across more complex or challenging cases. The authors rightly critique existing diagnostic tools for their limitations in multimodal or poorly summarized scenarios (lines 203-205), yet fail to articulate how their method overcomes these weaknesses or provide supporting evidence.  Furthermore, comparative simulations against commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those outlined in Section 4) would be highly beneficial in highlighting situations where the proposed approach provides more reliable diagnostics.
