This paper introduces Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), a method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide a quantitative bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by an algorithm (like Annealed Importance Sampling) and the true posterior. This diagnostic is primarily applicable to simulated data, where an exact posterior sample can be generated to initialize a "reverse" AIS chain. By combining the standard "forward" AIS estimate of the marginal likelihood with the reverse one, the authors derive an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The paper also proposes a practical protocol for applying this technique to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters and then simulating new data from the model. The authors demonstrate their method by integrating it into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to compare different model representations and, impressively, to uncover a bug in WebPPL.

**Strengths:**
The problem of rigorously diagnosing the convergence and sample quality of MCMC algorithms is a long-standing and critical challenge in computational statistics. Existing heuristics are often unreliable and can fail to detect serious issues. This paper offers a principled and quantitative approach to this problem. The main strengths are:

1.  **A Principled Metric:** Instead of relying on heuristics, BREAD provides a quantitative bound on the Jeffreys divergence, a well-defined measure of discrepancy between two distributions. This allows for a more objective evaluation and comparison of sampler performance.
2.  **Novelty and Practical Utility:** The extension of BDMC to bound a divergence metric is a clever and useful idea. It provides a powerful tool for developers of inference algorithms and users of probabilistic programming languages to validate and debug their models and implementations. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a compelling demonstration of its practical value.
3.  **Application to Probabilistic Programming Languages (PPLs):** Applying this method within popular PPLs like Stan and WebPPL is a valuable contribution, as it makes these advanced diagnostics more accessible to a broad audience of practitioners who may not be inference experts.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While the core idea is strong, the paper could be improved in terms of clarity and by better situating itself within existing literature.

1.  **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of prior work on validating posterior simulators using simulated data. The general approach of generating data from the model's prior predictive distribution and then checking if the inference algorithm can recover the "true" parameters is a known technique in the Bayesian workflow. In particular, the authors should consider and discuss the relationship of their work to methods proposed in [1] and [2]. These papers provide a relevant context for the problem of validating Bayesian software and inference.
2.  **Clarity of the Derivation (Section 3.1):** I found the derivation in Section 3.1 difficult to follow. The notation `q(.)` is used to denote several different distributions without being clearly defined upfront. While its meaning can be inferred from the context and the appendix (e.g., as the marginal distribution of the samples from the forward AIS chain), the paper should explicitly define all distributions before they are used. The key theoretical results should be presented in a way that is self-contained within the main text, without requiring the reader to consult the appendix for essential details.
3.  **Overall Algorithmic Description:** The paper lacks a clear, high-level description of how to apply the BREAD method from start to finish. A reader interested in implementing or using the method must piece together the details from different sections. I would strongly recommend adding a concise, step-by-step algorithmic description or a pseudo-code box that outlines the entire procedure, from data simulation to the computation of the divergence bound.
4.  **Minor Points:**
    *   In the notation, the unobserved variables are broken down into parameters `theta` and latent variables `z`. However, throughout the paper, they always appear together as `(theta, z)` and are treated identically. For simplicity and clarity, it might be better to use a single symbol to represent all unobserved quantities.
    *   There appears to be a missing closing parenthesis on line 165.
