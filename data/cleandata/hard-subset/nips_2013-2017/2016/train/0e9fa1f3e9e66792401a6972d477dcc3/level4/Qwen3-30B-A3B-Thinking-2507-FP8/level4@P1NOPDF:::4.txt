

This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for assessing MCMC convergence using the recently developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo approach to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood (via annealed importance sampling). The authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds serves as a bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution, providing a single scalar metric for convergence assessment. While the method inherently requires synthetic data generated from the joint distribution, the authors provide empirical evidence suggesting its synthetic-data analyses predict real-world behavior. They further showcase applications for model selection and debugging. Overall, the paper is well-written, well-motivated, and presents a clearly useful method with reasonably compelling experimental results.  

However, several issues require clarification. Section 3 is notably harder to follow than the rest of the paper. The transition into Section 3.1 is particularly disruptive, launching directly into variable definitions and equations without justification. If the divergence bound derived in Section 3.1 constitutes a novel contribution (which appears to be the case), the paper must explicitly state this, as the method’s core novelty hinges on it. Crucially, the paper never explicitly defines AutoBDMC. Based on the description, it seems AutoBDMC refers to the approach of using AIS and rAIS to analyze MCMC convergence. This definition should be stated upfront—by Section 3.2, where the method is referenced ("this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC"), its meaning must be unambiguous.  

Additional minor points:  
- Line 149: The expression \( p(\theta, z | y) \) likely should be \( p(\theta, z, y) \), as the joint distribution typically includes data.  
- Notational inconsistencies: The paper alternates between \( y \) and \( D \) to denote data/observations, which is occasionally confusing.  
- Supplemental WebPPL code: This should include (a) compatible WebPPL version(s) and (b) instructions for integration into the WebPPL distribution. Ideally, it would be released as a standalone WebPPL package installable via npm. A similar release for Stan code would also be highly valuable.
