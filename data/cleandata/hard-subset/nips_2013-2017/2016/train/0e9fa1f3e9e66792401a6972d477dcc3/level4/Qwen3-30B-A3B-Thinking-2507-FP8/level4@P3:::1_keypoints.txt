- Proposes BREAD, a method to bound the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between MCMC approximate samples and the true posterior using BDMC with reverse annealing.  
- Extends BDMC to evaluate MCMC algorithms that act as importance sampling on an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC).  
- Key theoretical insight: BDMC produces an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence when applied to simulated data with access to exact posterior samples.  
- Develops a practical protocol for real-data application by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters to approximate the required exact-posterior setting.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates utility for guiding model representation choices and debugging inference implementations.  
- Contributions: rigorous method to bound Jeffreys divergence, protocol for real-world data, and concrete applications in probabilistic programming.  
- Strength: technically sound with rigorous justification and empirical validation across multiple models and frameworks.  
- Strength: addresses a real need—providing reliable quantitative measures for MCMC convergence beyond heuristic diagnostics.  
- Demonstrated practical value by uncovering a bug in WebPPL and informing model representation choices.  
- Significance: improves accessibility and reliability of probabilistic programming by enabling automatic inference-quality monitoring.  
- Clarity weakness: Section 3.1 derivation is hard to follow, notation q(·) used before definition, and forward/reverse AIS relationship not clearly explained.  
- Notational issue: unnecessary split of unobserved variables into z and θ complicates exposition; minor typographical error noted (missing parenthesis).  
- Presentation weakness: main text lacks a clear step-by-step usage guide for BREAD; supplement fills gaps but core paper should be self-contained.  
- Related work: connection to CTM16 and differences between subjective divergence and Jeffreys divergence need more thorough discussion.  
- Overall assessment: recommendation to accept — strong, novel, and useful contribution with fixable clarity/exposition issues.
