The authors address the important problem of MCMC reliability assessment and scheme comparison, proposing a method based on stochastic upper and lower bounds of the log marginal likelihood derived from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). The core idea is to select MCMC schemes based on the tightness of their BMC bounds for a given computational cost. A key challenge lies in BMC’s requirement for exact samples from the target distribution to ensure a valid upper bound. The authors introduce heuristics to circumvent this, focusing on diagnostics performed on posteriors generated from synthetic data – leveraging the relative ease of obtaining good approximate samples in this context. The methodology is then demonstrated through several examples.

The concept of utilizing BMC-derived stochastic bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing. While the approach appears tailored to AIS and SMC samplers and focuses solely on marginal likelihood-based diagnostics, it represents a valuable attempt to develop theoretically grounded MCMC assessment tools. However, the implementation suffers from several limitations that diminish its practical appeal. These stem from the heuristic strategies employed to address BMC’s need for exact posterior samples. 

Specifically, the method relies on three potentially problematic assumptions. First, it presupposes prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value, η<sub>real</sub>, obtained from preliminary MCMC runs. This assumes both the convergence of the initial algorithm – effectively re-stating the original problem – and the ability to adequately summarize the posterior with a single value, both of which are strong and generally unsupported assumptions. Second, the approach assumes similarity in MCMC convergence properties between the posterior induced by synthetic data (generated using η<sub>real</sub>) and the original posterior. This assumption is particularly questionable in the presence of model misspecification and represents a shift in the diagnostic focus. Third, the method optimistically assumes that starting from η<sub>real</sub> allows the MCMC algorithm to rapidly generate near-exact samples from the posterior, a condition that is not guaranteed. Consequently, the proposed methodology appears to depend on assumptions intimately linked to the very convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm it aims to evaluate.

While Section 5.1 presents examples where these issues do not demonstrably impact the conclusions, these simple cases are insufficient to establish the robustness of the method in more complex scenarios. The authors rightly critique existing convergence diagnostics for their potential failures in multimodal landscapes or when using insufficient summary statistics (lines 203-205). However, they should articulate how their method overcomes these limitations and provide supporting evidence. More broadly, comparative simulations against established diagnostic methods (e.g., those detailed in Section 4) would be highly beneficial to demonstrate scenarios where the proposed method offers more reliable diagnostics.
