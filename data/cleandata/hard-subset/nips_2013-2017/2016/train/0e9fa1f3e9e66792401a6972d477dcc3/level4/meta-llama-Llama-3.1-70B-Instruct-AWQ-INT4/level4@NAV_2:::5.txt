The authors propose a method to assess the reliability of MCMC outputs and compare the efficiency of MCMC schemes by using upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood given by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The idea is to choose, among available MCMC schemes, the one that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds given the same computational effort. The main challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose some heuristics to overcome this issue, specifically in the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involving performing the diagnostic on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data, leveraging the fact that for such posterior it is easier to produce good approximate samples.

The authors extend the recently introduced Bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. They integrate their method into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets. The authors also present a protocol for using this technique to analyze sampler accuracy on real-world data.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method. The authors provide a detailed description of the Bidirectional Monte Carlo technique and its application to MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in realistic settings.

However, there are some concerns regarding the assumptions made by the authors. Firstly, the authors assume that the hyperparameters used to generate synthetic data are representative of the whole posterior. This assumption may not always hold, especially in cases where the posterior is multimodal or has a complex structure. Secondly, the authors assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from the hyperparameters has properties similar to the original posterior. This assumption may also not hold in cases where the model is misspecified.

The authors also adopt an optimistic view that starting from the hyperparameters allows the MCMC algorithm to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. This assumption may not always hold, especially in cases where the posterior is complex or has a large number of modes.

Despite these concerns, the authors provide some evidence that their method can work well in simple examples. However, more simulations are needed to compare their method with commonly used diagnostic methods to show scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.

In terms of the quality of the paper, it is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method. The authors provide a detailed description of the Bidirectional Monte Carlo technique and its application to MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in realistic settings.

However, the paper could benefit from more simulations to compare their method with commonly used diagnostic methods. Additionally, the authors could provide more evidence that their method can work well in more complex scenarios, such as multimodal or misspecified models.

Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field of MCMC diagnostics, but it requires more work to demonstrate its effectiveness in more complex scenarios.
