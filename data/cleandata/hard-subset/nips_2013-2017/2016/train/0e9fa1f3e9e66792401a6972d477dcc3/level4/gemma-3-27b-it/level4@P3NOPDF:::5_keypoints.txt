- Tackles assessing MCMC output reliability and comparing MCMC scheme efficiency using BMC stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood.  
- Core idea: choose the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC upper/lower bounds for a given compute budget.  
- Major hurdle: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to guarantee a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors’ workaround: heuristics that apply the diagnostic to posteriors from synthetic data where good approximate samples are easier to obtain.  
- Demonstrated the methodology on several simple examples.  
- Novelty: interesting, theoretically grounded attempt to use BMC lower bounds as an MCMC convergence diagnostic, especially for AIS/SMC.  
- Method relies solely on marginal likelihood, which may limit sensitivity to some convergence failures.  
- Key criticism: heuristics introduce assumptions tied to the very convergence problem being diagnosed, weakening validity.  
- Assumption 1: prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter η_real from a supposedly converged MCMC, which is generally unjustified.  
- Assumption 2: posterior from synthetic data using η_real has similar convergence behavior to the real-data posterior, problematic under model misspecification.  
- Assumption 3: initializing MCMC near η_real will quickly produce an almost exact sample—this is not guaranteed.  
- Consequently, the method’s validity depends on the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm, possibly shifting rather than solving the diagnostic problem.  
- Simple examples in Section 5.1 are insufficient to demonstrate robustness in complex, real-world scenarios.  
- Recommendation: authors should explain how their method overcomes failures of existing diagnostics (e.g., multimodality, poor summary stats) and provide empirical comparisons with standard tools.
