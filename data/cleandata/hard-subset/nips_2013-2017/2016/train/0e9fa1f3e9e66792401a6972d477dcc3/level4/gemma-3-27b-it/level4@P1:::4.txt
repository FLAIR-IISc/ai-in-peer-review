This paper presents a method—termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing)—for assessing MCMC inference quality. The method leverages the recently developed bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, relying on annealed importance sampling (AIS). The authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples, offering a scalar quantity to evaluate MCMC performance. Because rigorous bounds require access to exact posterior samples, obtainable only with simulated data, the authors present a protocol and experimental evidence demonstrating that analyses performed on simulated data are indicative of behavior on real-world datasets. They also showcase applications of BREAD for guiding model design and debugging implementations. Overall, I found this paper to be well-written and motivated. The presented method appears genuinely useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling. 

Section 3 was the most challenging part of the paper to follow. The transition into 3.1 is particularly abrupt, as it introduces variables and equations without sufficient contextualization. If the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1 is a novel result—which I believe it is—this should be explicitly highlighted, as the paper’s novelty and contribution appear to hinge on it. What, precisely, *is* BREAD? This is never explicitly stated. My understanding is that BREAD is the approach of utilizing AIS and reverse AIS (rAIS) to analyze MCMC convergence. A clear statement to that effect would be beneficial. By the time Section 3.2 mentions that “…this poses a challenge for BREAD,” the method should already be clearly defined. 

Some minor points: Line 149: Should p(✓, z | y) be p(✓, z, y)? Notational inconsistencies: the paper occasionally switches between using *y* and *D* to denote data/observations, which caused some confusion. The WebPPL code in the supplemental materials should be accompanied by (a) information about which version(s) of WebPPL it is known to work with, and (b) instructions on how to integrate it into a WebPPL distribution for ease of use. Even better would be to release this as a publicly installable WebPPL package via npm. If feasible, a similar release for the Stan code would also be highly appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior is a significant contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
*   **Practicality:** The method provides a concrete, quantifiable metric for assessing MCMC convergence, which is a long-standing challenge in Bayesian inference.
*   **Demonstrated Utility:** The authors demonstrate the practical utility of BREAD through applications in model selection and debugging, and show its applicability to both WebPPL and Stan.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite some localized difficulties, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity in Section 3:** Section 3 could benefit from improved exposition, particularly in motivating the initial derivations and explicitly defining BREAD.
*   **Limited Scope:** The method currently requires access to simulated data, limiting its direct applicability to real-world problems. The protocol for using it with real data relies on assumptions that may not always hold.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method addresses a fundamental challenge—assessing MCMC convergence—with a novel and theoretically sound approach. While the clarity of Section 3 could be improved, and the reliance on simulated data introduces some limitations, the potential benefits of BREAD outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance.
