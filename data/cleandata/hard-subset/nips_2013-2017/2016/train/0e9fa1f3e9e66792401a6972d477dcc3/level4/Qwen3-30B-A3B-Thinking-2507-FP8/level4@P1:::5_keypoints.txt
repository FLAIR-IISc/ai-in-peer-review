- Core contribution: Extends BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior (BREAD).  
- Methodology: Uses BDMC’s stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood and evaluates inference on simulated data from fitted hyperparameters to avoid needing exact posterior samples.  
- Implementation & validation: Integrated into WebPPL and Stan, validated across multiple models (including matrix factorization), and used to find a WebPPL bug.  

- Strength — theoretical grounding: Provides a theoretically sound diagnostic by adapting BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence.  
- Strength — empirical match: Experiments show inference behavior on simulated data matches real data across five models; approximate posterior initialization appears robust even with few MCMC steps.  
- Strength — practical utility: Demonstrates usefulness for comparing representations and debugging probabilistic programming systems.  

- Weakness — hyperparameter circularity: Requires hyperparameters estimated via MCMC on real data and assumes that initial inference has converged, creating circularity.  
- Weakness — unverified posterior similarity: Assumes simulated-data posteriors mirror real-data posteriors, which is untested and may fail under model misspecification.  
- Weakness — optimistic initialization claim: Assumes rapid convergence from approximate initialization; this may break for complex or multimodal posteriors.  
- Weakness — limited validation scope: Experiments primarily use simple models, so broad applicability claims are unsupported.  
- Weakness — missing benchmarks: Does not compare BREAD to standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, ESS), especially on challenging multimodal cases.  

- Suggested improvements: Add comparisons to established diagnostics on multimodal examples, clarify failure cases for the hyperparameter heuristic, and explicitly acknowledge BREAD evaluates simulated-data rather than real-data inference.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — technically solid and useful in niche/debugging contexts but overreaches in generality without comparative evidence.
