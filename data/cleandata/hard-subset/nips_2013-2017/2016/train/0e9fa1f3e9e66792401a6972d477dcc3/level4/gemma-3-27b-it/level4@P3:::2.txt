This paper extends the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is to leverage annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains run both from prior to posterior and vice versa to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples produced by MCMC. The authors integrate their method, dubbed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p], and validate it on several models and datasets. They also demonstrate its utility in guiding modeling choices, specifically by analyzing the effectiveness of different model representations in WebPPL and Stan.

This work builds on a growing body of research aimed at reliably evaluating the quality of approximate inference, a particularly challenging problem in black-box inference systems. BDMC, originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods, is cleverly repurposed here to provide a quantitative measure of posterior accuracy. This approach is particularly valuable as traditional convergence diagnostics can be insufficient to guarantee the correctness of MCMC results. Related work includes convergence diagnostics like those by Brooks and Gelman [BG98], methods for automatically configuring MCMC parameters [RR01], and alternative sample quality measures like that of Gorham and Mackey [GM15]. Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16] also utilize BDMC for evaluating probabilistic programming inference, employing a similar technique but with a different initialization for the reverse chain, as discussed in the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application of BDMC:** The application of BDMC to bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors is a significant contribution. It provides a more rigorous approach to evaluating MCMC inference than traditional diagnostics.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan makes it accessible to a broad range of users and facilitates real-world application.
*   **Validation on Multiple Models:** The experiments demonstrate the method’s effectiveness across different probabilistic models and datasets, enhancing its credibility.
*   **Guidance for Model Design:**  The application to matrix factorization showcases the potential of BREAD to inform modeling choices and improve inference performance.
*   **Bug Discovery:** The identification of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates BREAD’s utility as a testing tool for probabilistic programming systems.
*   **Clear and Thorough Analysis:** The paper includes a detailed theoretical analysis and a comprehensive empirical evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Simulated Data Requirement:** The method currently requires access to exact posterior samples, necessitating simulated data for rigorous validation. While the authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation, the validity of this protocol relies on the accuracy of the hyperparameter inference.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains, particularly in reverse, can be computationally expensive. This may limit the scalability of BREAD to very complex models.
*   **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The performance of BREAD may be sensitive to the initialization of the reverse AIS chain, as acknowledged by the authors.
*   **Complexity of Derivation:** While mathematically sound, the derivation relating BDMC to the Jeffreys divergence might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the underlying theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for evaluating MCMC inference.
*   The practical implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages is a significant contribution.
*   The experimental results validate the method’s effectiveness and demonstrate its potential impact.
*   The work addresses an important and challenging problem in Bayesian inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on simulated data limits the immediate applicability of the method to real-world problems.
*   The computational cost may hinder its scalability to complex models.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method provides a more rigorous and quantitative way to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. While the reliance on simulated data and computational cost are limitations, the authors address these concerns and demonstrate the practical utility of their approach. The clear writing, thorough analysis, and compelling experimental results make this a worthy addition to NIPS.  

**Recommendation:** Accept.
