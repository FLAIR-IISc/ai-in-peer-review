- Proposal: use Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) upper/lower stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood to evaluate MCMC reliability and compare sampler efficiency.
- Core idea: choose the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational budget.
- Fundamental obstacle: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.
- Authors’ workaround: heuristics using diagnostics on posteriors generated from synthetic data where good approximate samples are easier to obtain.
- Method appears specific to AIS and SMC samplers and assesses only the marginal likelihood rather than full posterior behavior.
- Potential value: provides a theoretically motivated MCMC diagnostic, but practical utility is limited by the heuristics.
- Major criticism 1: requires prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value (η_real), which presumes the original convergence problem is already solved.
- Major criticism 2: assumes the synthetic-data-induced posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the real posterior, which is unjustified, especially under model misspecification.
- Major criticism 3: optimistic assumption that starting at η_real yields an almost exact sample quickly may not hold in general.
- The methodology effectively “changes the problem” by performing diagnostics on a different posterior, undermining its relevance to the original target.
- Empirical evidence is weak: provided examples are simple and not convincing that issues won’t arise in more complex settings.
- Recommendation: authors should explain why their method would handle multimodality or insufficiency of summary statistics better, and include direct simulations comparing to standard diagnostics.
