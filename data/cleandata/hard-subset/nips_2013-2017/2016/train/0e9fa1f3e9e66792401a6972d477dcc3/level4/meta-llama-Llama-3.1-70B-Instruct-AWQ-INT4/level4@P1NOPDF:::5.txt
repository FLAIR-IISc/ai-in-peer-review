The authors address the problem of evaluating the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure to achieve this. The idea is to choose the MCMC scheme that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. However, a significant challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose heuristics to overcome this issue. Their solution, in the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involves performing diagnostics on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data, leveraging the fact that it is easier to produce good approximate samples for such posteriors. The proposed methodology is then tested on several examples.

The idea of using stochastic lower bounds given by Bidirectional Monte Carlo to perform MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. Although the methodology appears to be specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and produces diagnostics based only on the marginal likelihood, it could still be valuable as it attempts to provide theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, make the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing. These drawbacks arise from the heuristic methodology attempting to overcome the fact that Bidirectional Monte Carlo requires an exact sample from the posterior to be valid. I will mention three of them.

First, the authors assume prior knowledge of a value of the hyperparameters, \eta{real}, which is representative of the whole posterior. This assumption is strong and unjustified in general, as it presupposes that the algorithm used to find \eta{real} had converged (which is essentially the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value.

Second, the authors assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from \eta{real} has similar properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) to the original posterior. This assumption is also unjustified and should be carefully justified, especially in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It appears that the authors try to overcome the difficulty posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo at the cost of "changing the problem," i.e., performing diagnostics on a different posterior.

Third, the authors adopt an optimistic view that starting from \eta{real} allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. However, this assumption may not always hold. Therefore, the proposed methodology seems to rely on various assumptions closely related to the actual thing being assessed (the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm).

I appreciate that Section 5.1 provides simple examples where these issues do not seem to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology. Nevertheless, these few simple examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues recognized by the authors will not be critical in other, possibly more complicated, examples.

The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools for failing in multimodal cases or when few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive (lines 203-205). I believe the authors should explain why their method can perform better in such scenarios and provide evidence to support this claim. Furthermore, it would be helpful to have simulations comparing their method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in sections 4) to demonstrate scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.
