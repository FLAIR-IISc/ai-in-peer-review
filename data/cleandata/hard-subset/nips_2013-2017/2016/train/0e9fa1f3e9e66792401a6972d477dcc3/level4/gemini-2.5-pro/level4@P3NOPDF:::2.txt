This paper presents a novel and practical method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by applying bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). The core contribution is a technique to compute tight, computationally efficient lower bounds on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the true posterior and the approximate distribution generated by an MCMC sampler. The authors successfully demonstrate the utility of their approach by integrating it into two widely-used probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the crucial and long-standing problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and assessing the quality of posterior approximations. Traditional diagnostics can be unreliable, and this work provides a more rigorous, quantitative alternative. The application of the recently developed bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to this problem is a novel and insightful idea.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the method appears solid. Using BMC to estimate lower bounds on the KL divergence provides a principled way to measure the discrepancy between the target and the approximation, which is a significant step beyond qualitative or heuristic diagnostics.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** A major strength of this work is its direct applicability. The integration into popular frameworks like Stan and WebPPL is a commendable effort that makes the method immediately accessible to a broad community of practitioners and researchers. This moves the contribution beyond a purely theoretical exercise and provides a tangible tool for the field.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Clarification:**

While the paper is strong, its impact could be further enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Computational Overhead:** A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method would be beneficial. How does the cost of computing the KL lower bound scale with the dimensionality of the parameter space or the size of the dataset? Understanding these limitations is important for practical adoption.

2.  **Comparison to Standard Diagnostics:** The paper would be strengthened by a more direct empirical comparison with traditional MCMC diagnostic tools (e.g., the Gelman-Rubin statistic, effective sample size). Demonstrating a scenario where the proposed KL-based diagnostic reveals a problem that standard diagnostics fail to detect would make a very compelling case for its superiority.

3.  **Guidance for Practitioners:** It would be helpful if the authors could provide some practical guidance on interpreting the magnitude of the KL divergence lower bound. For a user of Stan or WebPPL, what numerical value of the bound might be considered "good" or indicative of a converged, reliable posterior approximation?

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. It introduces a powerful, theoretically grounded, and practical technique for tackling a fundamental problem. The combination of novelty, technical quality, and demonstrated utility makes this a strong submission for the conference.

I recommend acceptance.
