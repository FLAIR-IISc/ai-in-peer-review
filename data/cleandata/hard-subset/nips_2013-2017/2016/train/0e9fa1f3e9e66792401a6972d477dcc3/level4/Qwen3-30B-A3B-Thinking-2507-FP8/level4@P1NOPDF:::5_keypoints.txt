- Core contribution: use Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds to assess MCMC reliability and choose schemes with the tightest bounds for equal computational cost.
- Fundamental obstacle: BMC requires an exact sample from the posterior, which the authors address using heuristics rather than providing exact sampling.
- Key assumption 1: η_real (a single hyperparameter summarizing the posterior) is known a priori, which itself would require a converged MCMC—circular and generally unjustified.
- Key assumption 1 cont.: Representing the entire posterior by one hyperparameter is problematic and oversimplifies posterior complexity.
- Key assumption 2: The synthetic-data posterior generated from η_real has the same convergence properties as the true posterior—unsupported, especially under model misspecification.
- Key assumption 3: Initializing MCMC at η_real produces rapid convergence to an “almost exact” sample—optimistic and not generally guaranteed.
- Circular dependency: all three assumptions depend on the convergence behavior the diagnostic is supposed to evaluate, undermining validity.
- Empirical validation is weak: limited toy examples in Section 5.1 are insufficient to demonstrate robustness in complex or multimodal scenarios.
- Lack of comparative evaluation: no thorough simulations against established diagnostics to show specific contexts where the proposed method outperforms alternatives.
- Overall verdict: theoretically interesting but practically unconvincing—the method sidesteps BMC’s exact-sampling requirement via heuristics, and needs rigorous justification of assumptions or a more principled approach plus broader empirical validation.
