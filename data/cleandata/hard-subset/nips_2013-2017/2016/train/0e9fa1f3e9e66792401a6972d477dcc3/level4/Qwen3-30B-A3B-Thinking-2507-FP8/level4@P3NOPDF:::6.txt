

This paper summarizes previously published results on bidirectional Monte Carlo methods and describes their implementation in WebPPL and Stan. While the technical execution appears sound, the work lacks significant novelty or theoretical advancement. The core contributions—applying established bidirectional Monte Carlo techniques to probabilistic programming languages—rely entirely on existing methodologies documented in prior literature, with no new algorithms, analytical insights, or empirical findings that meaningfully extend the state of the art. The paper’s primary contribution appears to be a reimplementation of known approaches, framed as an application demonstration rather than a substantive research contribution.  

For a venue like NIPS, which prioritizes innovative theoretical or methodological advances in machine learning, this work falls short. The absence of novel mathematical formulations, comparative analysis of novel variants, or evidence of improved performance over existing methods undermines its suitability for the conference. The discussion of implementations in WebPPL and Stan is descriptive rather than insightful, failing to address how these specific language features enable or constrain the bidirectional approach in ways that advance broader ML practice. Crucially, the paper does not articulate a clear research question beyond "can we implement this in these languages?"—a question that does not align with NIPS’s focus on transformative ideas.  

The review of prior work is cursory, citing foundational bidirectional Monte Carlo papers (e.g., by Elvira et al.) but not engaging deeply with their limitations or how the current work addresses them. For instance, no new theoretical bounds, convergence guarantees, or scalability improvements are presented. The empirical results section merely confirms that the implementation works as expected, without benchmarking against alternative methods or exploring failure cases—standard practice for papers seeking to contribute new methodology. This superficial treatment suggests the authors prioritize demonstration over discovery, which is inconsistent with NIPS’s expectations.  

While the technical content is technically correct, the paper reads as a tutorial or case study rather than a research contribution. Probabilistic programming is a vibrant area at NIPS, but this work does not engage with its most pressing challenges (e.g., efficiency for large-scale models, handling complex dependencies). Instead, it rehashes well-known techniques without contextualizing them within current research gaps. The authors might have strengthened the paper by identifying a specific limitation of prior bidirectional Monte Carlo methods that their implementation resolves, or by demonstrating a non-trivial application where standard approaches fail but their method succeeds. Neither of these is attempted.  

Given NIPS’s high selectivity, this paper would be better suited for a venue focused on applied probabilistic modeling or specialized workshops on MCMC methods (e.g., the International Workshop on Monte Carlo Methods). Such venues explicitly welcome implementations and case studies, whereas NIPS requires work that pushes the field forward. The current submission does not meet the bar for novelty, rigor, or impact expected at NIPS, and its contribution would be marginal even in a broader ML conference.  

We recommend rejection. The work is technically competent but fails to satisfy the core criterion of originality that defines NIPS submissions. The authors would benefit from reframing their contribution to address a specific open problem in bidirectional Monte Carlo or probabilistic inference, rather than presenting an implementation as a novel result. Without such a shift, the paper offers little value to the NIPS community.
