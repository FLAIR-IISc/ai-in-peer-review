Summary
This paper leverages bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference. The authors derive computable lower bounds on the KL divergence between the true posterior and an approximate distribution and integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan. The problem—reliably assessing the accuracy of MCMC—is important and timely, and the proposed approach is principled and practical. I recommend acceptance.

Strengths
- Relevance and significance: Evaluating posterior accuracy for MCMC without ground truth is a longstanding challenge. Using BMC to obtain quantitative guarantees addresses a real need for both researchers and practitioners.
- Conceptual clarity: Framing evaluation in terms of KL divergence to the true posterior provides a clear, interpretable target that connects to statistical decision-making.
- Theoretical contribution: The derivation and analysis of lower bounds on KL divergence are valuable. Under appropriate conditions, such bounds can offer conservative, model-aware diagnostics rather than purely heuristic metrics.
- Practical impact: Integrating the method into WebPPL and Stan makes it accessible and likely to see adoption. Tooling in established ecosystems is an important step toward broader use.
- Generality: The approach appears broadly applicable across models and MCMC variants implemented in these systems.

Weaknesses and questions
- Tightness and calibration of bounds: How tight are the KL lower bounds in practice, and under what conditions do they become informative versus vacuous? It would help to include guidance (or empirical evidence) on interpreting bound magnitudes and how they vary with computation budget.
- Computational overhead: BMC typically requires running both forward and reverse chains (e.g., via AIS or related constructs), which can be expensive. Please quantify the additional cost relative to the base MCMC run and discuss trade-offs between tighter bounds and runtime.
- Sensitivity to design choices: The performance of BMC often depends on annealing schedules, choice of intermediate distributions, and proposal kernels. How robust are the conclusions to these choices, and do you provide default settings that work reliably across common model classes?
- Scalability: What happens in high-dimensional, strongly correlated, or multimodal posteriors? Is there empirical or theoretical evidence indicating when the bounds degrade, and can diagnostics signal a lack of reliability?
- Applicability beyond standard settings: How does the method handle adaptive MCMC, non-reversible dynamics, or algorithms with heavy tuning (e.g., NUTS with adaptation)? Are any corrections required to maintain validity of the bounds?
- Coverage of approximate methods: The paper focuses on MCMC-based posterior inference. Can the same framework evaluate SMC, VI, or other samplers in a comparable way, or are additional assumptions needed?
- Validation on tractable cases: To calibrate intuition, results on models with known posteriors or normalizing constants would help demonstrate that the bounds reflect true error in controlled settings.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper reads well overall. To further improve clarity, consider including:
  - A concise algorithmic summary or pseudocode for the BMC-based evaluation procedure.
  - A clear statement of assumptions required for the bounds to hold (e.g., ergodicity, absolute continuity).
  - Practical guidance: default settings for schedules, number of intermediate steps, diagnostics for convergence of the bound, and recommended compute budgets.
  - A discussion of failure modes and how users can detect and mitigate them (e.g., variance diagnostics, repeated runs).
- Integration details: Briefly describe what changes, if any, users must make to Stan and WebPPL models to enable evaluation, and whether the implementation reuses compiled code and gradients (for Stan) to minimize overhead.

Empirical evaluation (if present)
- Ensure comparisons include a range of MCMC configurations (e.g., step sizes, trajectory lengths), report runtime-normalized metrics, and present variability across runs.
- Provide ablations for schedule length and intermediate distributions to show sensitivity.
- Include examples where the bound meaningfully distinguishes between well- and poorly-tuned samplers.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a principled and practically meaningful approach to evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo, with a concrete theoretical contribution (KL lower bounds) and integration into widely used probabilistic programming systems. While additional discussion on tightness, computational cost, and sensitivity would strengthen the work, the contribution is novel, timely, and useful. I recommend acceptance.
