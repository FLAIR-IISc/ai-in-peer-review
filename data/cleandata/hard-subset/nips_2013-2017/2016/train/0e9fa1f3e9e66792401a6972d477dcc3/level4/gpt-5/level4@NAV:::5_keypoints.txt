- Paper proposes using BMC-derived stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and compare sampler efficiency.
- Idea: choose the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC bounds given a fixed computational budget.
- Main obstacle: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound; authors introduce heuristics to bypass this.
- Practical workaround: apply diagnostics to posteriors induced by synthetic data (from a chosen hyperparameter eta_real), which are easier to approximately sample from.
- Positive: using BMC stochastic lower bounds for MCMC diagnostics is an interesting, potentially theoretically grounded direction, especially for AIS/SMC samplers.
- Major drawback: the heuristics rely on prior knowledge of eta_real, which itself presumes access to a converged MCMC result and that a single parameter summarizes the posterior—both strong, unjustified assumptions.
- Concern: assuming the synthetic-data posterior resembles the original posterior in terms of MCMC convergence is unjustified and may fail under model misspecification—method effectively “changes the problem.”
- Concern: assuming initialization at eta_real leads quickly to an almost exact posterior sample is optimistic; the method depends on the very convergence behavior it aims to evaluate.
- Evidence provided (Section 5.1) is limited to simple examples where issues didn’t manifest; this is insufficient to establish robustness in complex settings.
- Authors should explain why their method would handle multimodality or insufficient summary statistics better than existing diagnostics and provide simulation comparisons to standard diagnostics to demonstrate advantages.
