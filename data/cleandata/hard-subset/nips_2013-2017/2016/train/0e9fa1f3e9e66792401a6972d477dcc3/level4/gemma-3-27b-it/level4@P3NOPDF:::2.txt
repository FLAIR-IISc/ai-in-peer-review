This paper presents a compelling analysis of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference methods using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). The authors leverage BMC to establish lower bounds on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the true posterior distribution and the approximate distribution obtained via MCMC. A significant strength of this work is its practical integration with popular probabilistic programming systems, WebPPL and Stan, enabling empirical validation of the theoretical findings. 

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The problem addressed – accurately diagnosing and understanding the limitations of MCMC sampling – is both important and actively researched within the machine learning community. The application of BMC to this problem is a novel and interesting approach. The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the performance of MCMC algorithms and offers a principled way to assess their accuracy.

The key contribution lies in providing quantifiable lower bounds on the KL divergence. Existing methods often struggle to definitively determine whether observed discrepancies between the approximate and true posterior are due to limitations of the MCMC algorithm or inherent intractability of the model. This work addresses this gap by offering a statistically rigorous framework for diagnosing MCMC convergence and approximation quality. 

The integration with WebPPL and Stan is particularly commendable. It demonstrates the practicality of the proposed method and provides a pathway for broader adoption within the probabilistic programming community. The authors clearly explain how BMC can be incorporated into existing MCMC workflows, making it accessible to practitioners. While the paper doesn’t present entirely new sampling algorithms, it offers a powerful diagnostic tool *for* existing algorithms.

There are a few minor areas where clarification could improve the paper:

* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of BMC, particularly as it scales with problem dimensionality, would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge the computational expense, quantifying this overhead and comparing it to alternative diagnostic methods (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size) would strengthen the work.
* **Practical Guidelines:** While the paper demonstrates the use of BMC, providing more concrete guidelines for interpreting the lower bounds on KL divergence would be helpful. For example, what level of KL divergence is considered practically significant in different application contexts?
* **Assumptions:** A clearer articulation of the assumptions underlying the BMC analysis, and a discussion of how violations of these assumptions might affect the validity of the results, would be valuable.

Despite these minor points, this paper represents a significant advance in the analysis of MCMC methods. The theoretical rigor, practical integration, and potential impact on the field are all highly commendable. I recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS 2016.
