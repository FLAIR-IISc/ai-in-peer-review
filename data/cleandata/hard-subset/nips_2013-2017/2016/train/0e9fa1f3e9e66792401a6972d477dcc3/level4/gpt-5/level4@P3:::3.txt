Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) and use the gap between the two log marginal likelihood estimates to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by the inference procedure. The method, dubbed BREAD, is integrated into WebPPL and Stan. The authors validate the bound on toy problems where exact comparisons are possible, propose a practical protocol for real data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, and use BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations and to identify an implementation bug in WebPPL.

The work builds directly on BDMC and reverse AIS analyses (e.g., Grosse et al. 2015; Burda et al. 2015), AIS (Neal 2001), and is related to thermodynamic integration and to tempered transitions (Neal 1996), which also use heating and cooling paths. It is also complementary to recent “subjective divergence” estimators (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) and to sample-quality diagnostics (e.g., Stein-based measures; Gorham & Mackey, 2015).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with rigorous guarantees in the simulated-data setting: the forward/reverse AIS gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, offering a single, interpretable quantity to monitor inference quality.
- Practical integration into two widely used probabilistic programming systems, with helpful case studies and a concrete debugging win.
- Sensible protocol for bridging to real data via fitted hyperparameters, with empirical evidence that the reverse-AIS results are stable to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate posterior hyperparameter draws.
- The collapsed vs. uncollapsed comparison illustrates how BREAD can guide representation and sampler choices in practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality and technical soundness
- The bound is in expectation and depends on having an exact posterior sample; the real-data protocol replaces this with an approximate scheme. The paper does a good job empirically probing this approximation, but the limitations (e.g., when posteriors are highly concentrated away from the fitted-hyperparameter region) should be discussed more explicitly, along with failure modes.
- The method’s sensitivity to the annealing schedule (sequence of inverse temperatures) is not systematically analyzed. Since the forward/reverse gap depends strongly on the step sizes in beta, it would be valuable to include a controlled study of how the gap and variance scale with the number of intermediate distributions and with schedule design (linear vs. nonlinear schedules).
- Consider reporting both per-step and wall-clock convergence, and normalizing by computational cost (e.g., effective sample size per second or divergence gap per second), especially when comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and across platforms.

Clarity
There are several places where notation and definitions can be tightened:
- Please define precisely the AIS state variables v (e.g., the sequence of intermediate states x_1,...,x_{T-1} for the forward chain) and make clear that the reverse chain uses p_T samples. The phrase “all variables sampled before the final stage” is ambiguous given two directions.
- Use consistent notation for forward and reverse chains (avoid mixing fwd/back/for/rev). Explicitly name q_fwd(x_T), q_fwd(v|x_T), p_T(x_T), and q_rev(v|x_T).
- In the derivations around Eqs. (6)–(11), please state the estimators whose expectations are being taken (e.g., E[log R_hat] for forward AIS and E[log R_rev_hat] for reverse AIS). As written, Eq. (6) appears to place identical distributions inside a KL; check the terms and ensure they match Eqs. (4) and (10) and the surrounding text.
- Define “AutoBDMC” (if used): what is automated (e.g., computing the forward/reverse gap, the fitted-hyperparameter simulation, schedule selection)?
- Experimental details: state explicitly how beta_t are chosen. From the code, it appears you use a linear schedule with step size 1/T. If figures report “HMC/NUTS steps,” clarify whether this is the number of intermediate distributions, the number of MCMC transitions per intermediate distribution, or both. Define RAIS and any abbreviations such as (u)c(R)AIS. Improve figure readability (axis labels, tick marks).

Originality
- The main theoretical novelty is the observation and proof that the forward/reverse AIS gap upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. This is a solid and useful refinement to BDMC’s original focus on marginal likelihood estimation.
- Please discuss connections to tempered transitions (Neal, 1996). TT also performs reverse and forward sweeps and relates acceptance probabilities to thermodynamic integration bounds. Clarifying similarities and differences (assumptions, goals, and estimators) would strengthen the paper and help readers leverage TT intuitions (e.g., the role of beta spacing).

Significance
- The ability to produce a quantitative, interpretable, and often tight upper bound on posterior-sample quality is valuable for probabilistic programming and beyond. The integration into WebPPL and Stan and the demonstration of actionable decisions (representation choice) underscore potential impact.
- Broader adoption would be facilitated by guidance on scheduling (possibly adaptive), computational budgets (number of AIS chains), and when the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is trustworthy.

Specific questions for the authors
- Can you provide guidance or an adaptive heuristic for choosing the beta schedule (e.g., refine until the gap is below a target threshold or acceptance in a TT-like diagnostic stabilizes)?
- How many AIS chains are recommended in practice to control the variance of the gap estimator? Do you aggregate chains in any way beyond reporting quantiles?
- In high-dimensional models with heavy tails or strong multimodality, how robust is the fitted-hyperparameter protocol? Any empirical failures?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method with rigorous guarantees on simulated data and practical utility in real workflows.
- Clear, useful integrations and case studies; potential for broad adoption in PPLs.
- Theoretical contribution (Jeffreys-divergence bound) is sound and relevant.

Cons for acceptance
- Some derivations and notation need clarification/correction.
- Limited analysis of schedule sensitivity and computational trade-offs.
- Real-data protocol relies on heuristic approximations without formal guarantees.

Minor issues and typos
- Correct “Jensen-Shannon” to “Jeffreys” divergence throughout (if applicable).
- Fix missing closing parentheses in the D_JS expression; use consistent notation for the divergence you bound (Jeffreys).
- NUTS citation: Hoffman, not Homan.
- Add Neal (1996) Tempered Transitions and discuss connections.
- Define RAIS and collapsed/uncollapsed abbreviations in figure captions.

Overall recommendation
I find the idea practical and impactful, with a meaningful theoretical refinement and convincing integrations. If the authors address the clarity issues and expand the discussion on beta scheduling and related work (especially TT), I would lean toward acceptance.
