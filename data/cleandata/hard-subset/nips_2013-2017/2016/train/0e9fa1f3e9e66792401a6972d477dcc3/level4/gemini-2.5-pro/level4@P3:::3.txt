### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method, "Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing" (BREAD), for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The approach extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which uses forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) runs to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the model's marginal likelihood. The key contribution of this work is to show that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution and the true posterior. The authors implement BREAD within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and propose a protocol for applying the method to real-world datasets by first fitting hyperparameters and then generating simulated data. They demonstrate its utility by comparing different model representations for matrix factorization and by using it to uncover a bug in an inference engine.

### **Strengths**

*   The central idea of using the BDMC gap to assess the quality of MCMC inference is simple, elegant, and intuitive.
*   The method is broadly applicable and relatively easy to implement, making it a potentially high-impact contribution. It could serve as a valuable diagnostic tool for developers and users of probabilistic programming systems.
*   The application of BREAD to compare different model parameterizations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) effectively demonstrates the method's practical utility for guiding modeling decisions.
*   The paper reports successfully using the method to identify a bug in WebPPL, highlighting its usefulness as a tool for validating the correctness of inference implementations.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Novelty and Technical Depth:** The paper builds heavily on the existing BDMC framework [GGA15]. While the connection between the BDMC gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a valuable insight, it is the primary new theoretical contribution, and its analysis feels somewhat brief. A significant portion of the paper is devoted to reviewing AIS and BDMC, which leaves the novel contributions in Section 3 feeling rushed.
*   **Omission of Key Related Work:** The paper fails to cite or discuss Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions (TT) [Statistics and Computing, 1996]. This is a significant omission, as the TT algorithm is conceptually very similar to the forward-and-reverse AIS procedure used here. In TT, a "heating" (reverse) pass is followed by a "cooling" (forward) pass to generate a proposal for a Metropolis-Hastings update. The acceptance probability for this update is directly related to the ratio of the importance weights accumulated in both sweeps, which corresponds precisely to the ratio of the upper and lower bound estimates in BREAD. Section 3.3 of Neal's paper provides an interpretation of this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function difference from above and below. Acknowledging and discussing this connection would have strengthened the paper's foundations and provided important context.
*   **Analysis of Annealing Schedule:** The performance of AIS, and therefore BREAD, is critically dependent on the choice of the annealing schedule (the intermediate temperatures, `τ_t`). A schedule with too few steps (i.e., large gaps between temperatures) will result in a loose gap between the upper and lower bounds, rendering the divergence estimate uninformative. Conversely, too many steps are computationally wasteful. The paper does not explore this crucial trade-off. A more thorough analysis, perhaps including a method for adaptively choosing the schedule to achieve a desired tightness of the bounds, would have significantly increased the paper's contribution.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** While the experiments are illustrative, they could be more systematic. For instance, a study showing how the bounds tighten as the number of annealing steps increases would provide valuable characterization of the method's behavior and computational cost.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Clarity in Section 3.1:** The technical derivation in Section 3.1 could be improved for clarity.
    *   The definition of `v` as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous. Does this refer to the sequence of states `{x_1, ..., x_{T-1}}`? Please define this term precisely.
    *   In Equations (6) through (11), it is not explicitly stated which estimators are being used inside the expectation. For example, is the expectation in Equation (6) taken with respect to `log \hat{R}`? Clarifying this would make the derivation easier to follow.
    *   The paper derives an inequality in Equation (8) from Equation (7) by noting that marginalizing variables cannot increase KL divergence. It would be helpful to explicitly state the property being used for the reader's benefit.
*   **Experimental Details:**
    *   How was the annealing schedule (the sequence of `τ_t` values) chosen for the experiments? The appendix seems to suggest a linear schedule based on the number of steps, but this should be stated clearly in the main text.
    *   In Figures 1 and 2, what does the x-axis label "AIS steps" or "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" represent? Does this refer to the number of intermediate distributions `T` in the annealing schedule, or the number of MCMC transitions performed at each temperature? This is a critical detail for interpreting the results.
    *   The captions for the figures should be self-contained. Please define acronyms like RAIS and the conventions used for collapsed/uncollapsed models (e.g., `(u)c(R)AIS`).
    *   The font size for axis labels and ticks in the figures is too small, making them difficult to read.
*   **Typographical Errors:**
    *   The citation for the No-U-Turn Sampler should be Hoffman and Gelman, 2014, not Homan and Gelman.

### **Conclusion**

This paper introduces a valuable and practical idea for assessing the quality of MCMC inference, which is particularly relevant in the context of automated probabilistic programming systems. The core concept is strong, and the experimental demonstrations are promising. However, the paper's contributions are currently undermined by a lack of technical depth, the omission of highly relevant prior work, and several points of unclarity in the technical sections and experimental reporting.

I would be willing to argue for acceptance if the authors can convincingly address these issues, particularly by incorporating a discussion of Tempered Transitions, clarifying the technical derivations, and providing more detail and rigor in the experimental setup and presentation. The proposed method has the potential to be a widely adopted tool, and strengthening the paper in these areas would ensure it realizes that potential.
