The authors consider the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose to do so by using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood given by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The idea is to choose, among available MCMC schemes, the one that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds given the same computational effort.

The main challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose some heuristics to overcome this issue. Their solution, in the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involves performing the diagnostic on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data, leveraging the fact that for such posterior it is easier to produce good approximate samples.

The proposed methodology is interesting, as it is an attempt to have theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation has several drawbacks that make the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing. One major concern is that the authors assume they know in advance (e.g., from previously run MCMC) a value of the hyperparameters, η_real, which is representative of the whole posterior. This assumes both that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged (which is basically the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value. Both assumptions are strong and unjustified in general.

Another issue is that the authors assume the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) similar to the original posterior. This again is an unjustified assumption that, for example, should be carefully justified in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It seems that the authors try to overcome the difficulty posed by bidirectional Monte Carlo at the price of "changing the problem", i.e., performing diagnostic on a different posterior.

Furthermore, the authors adopt the optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to be quickly able to draw an almost exact sample from the posterior (which may be the case but again does not need to be). Therefore, the proposed methodology seems to rely on various assumptions which, crucially, are closely related to the actual thing we wanted to assess in the first instance (the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm).

I appreciate the fact that Section 5.1 shows some simple examples where these issues appear not to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology. Nevertheless, the few simple examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues that the authors themselves recognize will not be critical in other, maybe more complicated, examples.

The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools because they can fail in multimodal cases or in cases where few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive. I think the authors should explain why their method can do better in such scenarios and provide some evidence. More generally, I think it would help to have simulations to compare their method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., the ones described in sections 4) to show scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential pitfalls of their approach. Additionally, the authors could provide more evidence to support their claims, particularly in terms of comparing their method to existing diagnostic tools.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology. However, some of the technical details may be difficult to follow for non-experts. The authors could consider providing more background information or explanations to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new methodology for assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. While the idea of using bidirectional Monte Carlo is not new, the authors' application of this method to MCMC diagnostics is novel.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of Bayesian statistics, namely the assessment of MCMC convergence. The authors' methodology has the potential to provide more reliable diagnostics than existing methods, particularly in cases where the posterior distribution is multimodal or has a complex structure. However, more evidence is needed to support the authors' claims, and the limitations of their approach need to be more thoroughly discussed.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel methodology for assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes.
* The authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology and discuss its potential advantages over existing diagnostic tools.
* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of Bayesian statistics.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper relies on several strong assumptions that may not be justified in general.
* The authors do not provide sufficient evidence to support their claims, particularly in terms of comparing their method to existing diagnostic tools.
* The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential pitfalls of the authors' approach.
