This paper addresses the challenging problem of assessing the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, particularly within “black box” probabilistic programming systems. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based algorithms by bounding the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate posterior and the true posterior. They demonstrate this approach, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan and validating it on several models and datasets. The core idea—leveraging BDMC’s bounds on the log marginal likelihood to assess sample quality—is intriguing and represents a valuable attempt to provide a theoretically grounded diagnostic for MCMC.

The work builds upon the recent BDMC framework [GGA15] and addresses a critical need for robust MCMC diagnostics. Existing heuristics often lack quantitative guarantees, making it difficult for users to confidently interpret results from automatic inference systems. This paper directly tackles this issue by providing an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, offering a quantifiable measure of the discrepancy between the approximate and true posteriors. The extensions to WebPPL and Stan are a significant practical contribution, making the method accessible to a wider audience. The application to analyze model representations in both frameworks further highlights the method’s potential for guiding algorithmic and modeling choices. 

However, the paper’s reliance on simulated data and the heuristics employed to extend the method to real-world scenarios raise substantial concerns. The fundamental limitation of BDMC—requiring an exact posterior sample for a valid upper bound—is addressed by a protocol involving hyperparameter estimation on real data, simulation from those hyperparameters, and inference on the simulated data. This approach, while pragmatic, introduces several potentially problematic assumptions. The assumption that hyperparameters estimated from real data accurately represent the true posterior (and thus generate representative simulated data) is strong and could be violated in cases of model misspecification or complex posterior landscapes. The authors acknowledge this, but the validation presented (Section 5.1) with only five models is not sufficient to establish the robustness of this approach across diverse scenarios. Furthermore, the assumption that a relatively small number of MCMC steps from the estimated hyperparameters yields a sufficient approximation of an exact posterior sample also lacks strong justification and could undermine the theoretical guarantees.

The paper’s comparison to existing diagnostics is limited. While the authors critique existing methods (lines 203-205), they do not present a comprehensive comparison of BREAD’s performance against established diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin or trace plots, particularly in challenging scenarios such as multimodal posteriors. Simulations comparing BREAD with these methods would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution and demonstrate its advantages (or disadvantages) more clearly.  The potential for BREAD to outperform traditional methods in complex scenarios needs to be demonstrated, not simply asserted. Additionally, the relationship between BREAD and recent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which also builds on BDMC, is adequately discussed, but a more detailed comparison of the two methods’ strengths and weaknesses would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application of BDMC:** Extending BDMC to evaluate posterior accuracy is a creative and potentially impactful idea.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The method provides a theoretically grounded upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method readily available to practitioners.
*   **Demonstrated Usefulness:** The application to model representation comparison showcases the method’s potential for guiding modeling decisions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The need for exact posterior samples necessitates the use of simulated data, which introduces assumptions about hyperparameter accuracy and posterior representativeness.
*   **Limited Validation:** The validation with only five models is insufficient to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
*   **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to established MCMC diagnostics.
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The heuristics employed to address the need for exact posterior samples rely on strong assumptions that may not hold in all cases.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important open problem in Bayesian inference: reliable MCMC diagnostics.
*   The proposed method is theoretically sound and provides a quantifiable measure of posterior accuracy.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on simulated data and the associated assumptions significantly limit the applicability and robustness of the method.
*   The lack of comparative analysis makes it difficult to assess the method’s advantages over existing diagnostics.
*   The limited validation raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. While the core idea is promising and the technical execution is solid, the substantial limitations related to the reliance on simulated data and the lack of rigorous validation prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. The authors have identified the key challenges associated with their approach, but the proposed solutions remain largely heuristic and unproven. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly through more comprehensive simulations and a thorough comparison with existing diagnostics, would significantly strengthen the paper.
