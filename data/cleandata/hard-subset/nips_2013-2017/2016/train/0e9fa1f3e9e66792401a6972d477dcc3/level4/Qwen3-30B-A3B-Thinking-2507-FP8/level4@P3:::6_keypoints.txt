- Introduces BREAD, an extension of BDMC to measure reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference.  
- Extends BDMC to provide an upper bound on the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between approximate and true posteriors.  
- Provides theoretical derivations showing the BREAD estimator upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Proposes a validation protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by generating simulated data from fitted hyperparameters.  
- Integrates BREAD into two probabilistic programming systems: WebPPL and Stan.  
- Empirically demonstrates BREAD guiding model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed factorization).  
- Validates method on toy distributions where exact divergence is computable, supporting theoretical claims.  
- Strength: addresses a practical need in probabilistic programming for automatic, reliable inference-quality assessment.  
- Strength: well-written, clear background and derivations making the contribution accessible.  
- Strength: practical implementation and experiments, including finding a bug in WebPPL, show real utility.  
- Limitation: applicability restricted to inference algorithms like AIS/SMC that align with BDMC assumptions.  
- Limitation: real-data protocol relies on fitted-hyperparameter simulation heuristic which may not always represent true data.  
- Limitation: paper could discuss computational cost of running forward and reverse AIS chains more thoroughly.  
- Originality: meaningful extension of BDMC to a new application (posterior divergence measurement), though BDMC itself is prior work.  
- Scope concern: may be seen as more targeted to probabilistic programming systems, but still fits NIPS due to relevance to probabilistic inference.  
- Recommendation: strong paper with solid theory, implementation, and empirical validation; should be accepted.
