This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantifying the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The approach extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015). By running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both a forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) direction on simulated data, the method produces stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors' main theoretical contribution is to show that the expected gap between these bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. This provides a quantitative measure of sample quality. Since the method requires an exact posterior sample and is thus limited to simulated data, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world datasets: first, fit hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate new data from the model using these hyperparameters to perform the analysis. The authors integrate BREAD into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to demonstrate practical applications, such as comparing the effectiveness of different model representations and for debugging inference code.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of diagnosing the convergence and reliability of MCMC inference, which is particularly critical for users of black-box probabilistic programming systems.
*   The proposed method, BREAD, is a clever and practical application of the BDMC framework. Providing a quantitative upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a significant step beyond existing heuristic convergence diagnostics.
*   The paper is generally well-motivated and clearly written. The experimental evaluation is compelling, providing solid validation for the proposed protocol on real-world data and demonstrating the method's utility for making concrete modeling and algorithmic choices.
*   The application of BREAD to debug a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL is a powerful demonstration of the method's value as a correctness-checking tool.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The clarity of the methods section could be improved. Section 3, and particularly the transition into Section 3.1, is abrupt. It launches directly into technical definitions and equations without sufficient motivation or high-level intuition for the reader. A brief paragraph motivating the subsequent derivation would make this core section of the paper much more accessible.
*   The novelty of the divergence bound should be highlighted more explicitly. The connection between the BDMC gap and the Jeffreys divergence is, to my knowledge, a new result and forms the paper's central technical contribution. While mentioned in the introduction, this result deserves to be stated more prominently as a formal claim or theorem to underscore its importance.
*   While the paper names the method BREAD, its definition could be stated more explicitly early in the paper to help anchor the reader.

**Minor Points:**
*   There are some notational inconsistencies. For instance, the observed data is variously referred to as `y` and `D`. Standardizing the notation would improve readability.
*   In Section 3.2, the protocol for real-world data is described. The authors may want to double-check their notation throughout this section for correctness. For example, the reviewer is prompted to check if `p(⌘,✓ |D)` should be `p(⌘,✓, D)` in one instance, though this could be a misreading. A careful pass would be beneficial.
*   The provided code in the supplementary material would be much more useful to the community with additional documentation. Specifically, it should include which versions of WebPPL and Stan the code is compatible with and clear instructions on how to integrate and use it. For wider adoption, the authors should consider releasing the implementations as packages (e.g., via npm for WebPPL), which would be a valuable contribution.
