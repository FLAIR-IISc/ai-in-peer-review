The authors address the problem of evaluating MCMC reliability and comparing sampling efficiency, proposing a method based on upper and lower stochastic bounds of the log marginal likelihood derived from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). Their core idea is to select the MCMC scheme yielding the tightest BMC bounds for a given computational budget. A key challenge is BMC’s requirement for exact samples from the target distribution to ensure a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose heuristics to circumvent this, focusing on Bayesian posterior sampling by performing diagnostics on posteriors generated from synthetic data. This leverages the relative ease of obtaining good approximate samples for such posteriors. The methodology is then demonstrated through several examples. The concept of utilizing stochastic lower bounds from BMC for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. While the method appears tailored to AIS and SMC samplers, and focuses solely on marginal likelihood-based diagnostics, it represents a valuable effort toward theoretically grounded MCMC assessment tools. However, significant implementation drawbacks diminish the appeal of the proposed diagnostic, stemming from the heuristic approach used to address BMC’s need for exact posterior samples. 

I identify three key concerns. First, the authors assume prior knowledge of a hyperparameter value, \eta{real}, representative of the entire posterior distribution, likely obtained from preliminary MCMC runs. This presupposes both the convergence of that initial MCMC algorithm – effectively re-introducing the original problem – and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value, both of which are strong and generally unjustified assumptions. Second, they assume the posterior induced by synthetic data generated using \eta{real} exhibits similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior. This assumption lacks justification, particularly in scenarios involving model misspecification. The approach appears to address BMC's difficulty by, in effect, altering the problem and performing diagnostics on a different posterior. Third, the authors optimistically posit that starting from \eta{real} enables rapid generation of nearly exact posterior samples by the MCMC algorithm of interest, which is not guaranteed. Thus, the methodology relies on assumptions critically linked to the very convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm that it aims to assess. 

While Section 5.1 demonstrates examples where these issues do not appear to impact conclusions, these simple cases are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate robustness across more complex scenarios. The authors critique existing convergence diagnostics for failing in multimodal cases or when using insufficiently expressive summary statistics (lines 203-205), and should therefore clarify how their method overcomes these limitations and provide supporting evidence. More broadly, simulations comparing their method to established diagnostics (e.g., those described in Section 4) would be beneficial to illustrate scenarios where it offers more reliable assessments.
