- Paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic lower/upper bounds on log marginal likelihood as an MCMC diagnostic and for comparing sampler efficiency under equal computational budgets.  
- Method is theoretically motivated and most applicable to AIS/SMC-type samplers, focusing on marginal likelihood rather than arbitrary functionals.  
- Core practical obstacle: BMC requires an exact draw from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors' heuristic: run diagnostics on posteriors induced by synthetic data from a point estimate η_real, assuming easier approximate sampling there.  
- Heuristic assumes η_real is representative of the true posterior, which presumes prior MCMC convergence and reduces the posterior to a single-point summary—strong, often unjustified assumptions.  
- It also assumes the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the real posterior, which can fail under model misspecification or differing likelihood structures.  
- The approach optimistically assumes starting from η_real yields an almost exact posterior sample, which may not hold in high-dimensional or multimodal settings.  
- These assumptions are tightly coupled to the convergence behavior being diagnosed, risking circularity and changing the problem being evaluated.  
- Experiments are limited and do not convincingly demonstrate robustness in complex or adversarial scenarios.  
- Paper should explain and empirically show why the method improves over existing diagnostics in multimodal cases or when summary statistics are weak.  
- Recommend comparative experiments against standard diagnostics to show improved detection speed, reliability, or consistency.  
- Suggest clarifications: precisely define “same computational effort,” analyze sensitivity to approximate-sample quality, and provide a principled, non-circular strategy for choosing/validating η_real.  
- Ask for characterization of failure modes when synthetic and real posteriors differ, diagnostics to detect such discrepancies, and explicit statement of scope if mainly for AIS/SMC.  
- Request practical guidance on using the BMC gap as a diagnostic threshold, including variance estimates and stopping/acceptance rules.  
- Summary judgment: promising idea but current heuristic workaround for the exact-sample requirement raises substantive concerns; needs stronger theoretical justification, systematic empirical comparisons, and clearer practical guidance before being recommended as a general diagnostic.
