The authors tackle the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC output and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose using the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). The idea is to select, among candidate MCMC schemes, the one that yields the tightest BMC bounds for a fixed computational budget. The main challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To address this, the authors introduce heuristics. In the Bayesian posterior setting, they suggest running the diagnostic on a posterior induced by synthetic data rather than the observed data, arguing that it is easier to obtain good approximate samples in this setup. They then evaluate the approach on several examples.

The idea of using BMC-based stochastic lower bounds as an MCMC convergence diagnostic is interesting. While the methodology appears tailored to AIS and SMC samplers and yields diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it remains a worthwhile attempt at a theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic.

However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my view, limit its appeal. These stem from heuristics introduced to circumvent the need for an exact posterior sample in BMC. In particular:
- The method assumes one already knows a value of the hyperparameters, eta_real (e.g., from a prior MCMC run), that is representative of the entire posterior. This presumes both that the algorithm used to find eta_real has converged—which is essentially the original problem of interest—and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both assumptions are strong and generally unjustified.
- It further assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from eta_real has MCMC convergence properties similar to those of the original posterior. This is again unjustified, and should be carefully argued, especially in the presence of model misspecification. In effect, the procedure addresses the difficulty posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo by “changing the problem,” i.e., performing the diagnostic on a different posterior.
- The approach adopts an optimistic view that initializing at eta_real enables the MCMC algorithm to very quickly produce an almost exact posterior sample. This may hold in some cases, but it is by no means guaranteed.

Consequently, the methodology relies on assumptions that are closely tied to the very object we want to assess in the first place—the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm.

I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents simple examples in which these issues do not appear to distort the conclusions. Nevertheless, these few, simple cases are not sufficient to claim that the acknowledged issues will not be critical in other, potentially more challenging, settings.

The authors criticize existing convergence diagnostics for failing in multimodal settings or when a few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive (lines 203–205). They should explain why their method is expected to perform better in such scenarios and provide evidence. More generally, simulations comparing their approach with commonly used diagnostics (e.g., those described in Section 4) would be valuable, highlighting scenarios where it yields more reliable assessments.
