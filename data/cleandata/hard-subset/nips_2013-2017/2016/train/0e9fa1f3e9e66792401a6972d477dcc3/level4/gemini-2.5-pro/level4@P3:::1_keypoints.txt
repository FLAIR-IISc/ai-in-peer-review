- Problem: Diagnoses reliability of MCMC inference in black-box PPLs by quantifying sampler quality rather than using heuristic diagnostics.  
- Method: Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC by using AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) passes and using the log-marginal-likelihood gap.  
- Theory claim: Shows the expected gap between stochastic upper and lower log-marginal bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS approximate and true posterior.  
- Applicability limitation: Reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, so BREAD is primarily applicable to simulated data with known ground-truth parameters.  
- Real-data protocol: Proposes fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating datasets from those hyperparameters, then applying BREAD.  
- Implementation: Implemented in WebPPL and Stan and used to compare model representations and debug PPLs (found a WebPPL bug).  
- Strength — significance/novelty: Tackles an important, under-addressed problem and provides a theoretically grounded, novel use of BDMC gap to estimate divergence.  
- Strength — practical utility: Demonstrates practical application in PPLs and usefulness for modeling decisions and debugging.  
- Strength — soundness: Theoretical derivation appears correct and experiments support consistency and robustness claims.  
- Weakness — clarity: Section 3.1 derivation is hard to follow with dense notation and undeclared terms (e.g., qfwd(xT)), reducing accessibility.  
- Weakness — related work: Fails to cite and discuss foundational simulation-based MCMC validation papers (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006).  
- Weakness — organization/reproducibility: Procedure is scattered; paper needs a consolidated, algorithmic description of the full BREAD protocol (including real-data steps).  
- Suggestion — notation/clarity: Explicitly define all q distributions before use, consider merging θ and z into a single latent variable for cleaner derivations.  
- Suggestion — edit: Rewrite Section 3.1 step-by-step, fix minor typos (e.g., missing parenthesis).  
- Recommendation: Paper is promising and suitable for acceptance if authors improve Section 3.1 clarity and properly situate the work in related literature (Geweke 2004; Cook et al. 2006).
