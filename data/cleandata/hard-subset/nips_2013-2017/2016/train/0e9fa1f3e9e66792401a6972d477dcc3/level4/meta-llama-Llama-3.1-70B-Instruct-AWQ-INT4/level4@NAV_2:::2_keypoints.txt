- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized (Jeffreys) KL divergence between true posterior and approximate samples.  
- Integrates the method into two probabilistic programming languages: WebPPL and Stan.  
- Relates to prior work on MCMC convergence diagnostics and automatic configuration but is distinct by providing a rigorous, general, automatic monitoring procedure.  
- Main technical result: bidirectional Monte Carlo yields an estimator that upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples.  
- Shows how to exactly compute both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples.  
- Proposes a practical protocol for using bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate inference accuracy on real-world datasets.  
- Implements and validates the method with thorough experiments across several models and datasets.  
- Strengths: technically sound, clearly written, and thoroughly evaluated; explains relation to previous work well.  
- Weakness: limited scope—directly applicable mainly to algorithms like annealed importance sampling or sequential Monte Carlo.  
- Weakness: simulated dataset generation relies on a heuristic scheme that may not fully convince.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid method with comprehensive evaluation and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited applicability and potential concerns about the realism of simulated datasets.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, despite limitations, because of technical soundness, clarity, and thorough evaluation.
