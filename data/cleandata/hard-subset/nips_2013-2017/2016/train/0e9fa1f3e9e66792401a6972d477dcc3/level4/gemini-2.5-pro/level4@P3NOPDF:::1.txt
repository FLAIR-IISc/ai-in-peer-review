This paper proposes a method for debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations by using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jensen-Shannon (J-S) divergence between the distribution produced by a sampler and its intended target distribution. The primary application is for validating samplers within Probabilistic Programming Languages (PPLs).

The core idea of using BDMC to diagnose the failure of a sampler to converge is compelling and potentially very useful. Existing MCMC diagnostics are known to be problematic, and the development of more principled, quantitative tools for sampler validation is a significant challenge. This work offers a promising approach to address this important gap.

However, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, primarily related to clarity and its positioning with respect to prior work.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarity of Presentation and Derivation:** The paper's main weakness is its lack of clarity, which makes the proposed method difficult to fully understand and evaluate. The derivation in Section 3.1, in particular, is hard to follow. For example, the notation `q(.)` is used before it is formally defined. While context suggests it represents the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after T steps, this should be made explicit for the reader. While the supplementary material helps clarify some points, the main text should be self-contained and understandable on its own.

2.  **Need for a Clear Algorithmic Description:** Related to the point above, the paper would be significantly improved by a clear, step-by-step statement of how to apply the method from start to finish. Currently, the reader must piece together the procedure from various sections. Including an algorithm box or a concise procedural summary would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility and utility for practitioners wishing to implement this diagnostic tool.

3.  **Omission of Relevant Literature:** The paper overlooks highly relevant prior work on MCMC validation. The technique of testing samplers by running them on data generated from the model's prior has been explored in [1] and [2]. The authors should cite and discuss this related work, clarifying how their proposed J-S divergence-based method compares to, or improves upon, these existing validation techniques.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The notational distinction between unobserved variables `z` and `theta` seems unnecessary and adds clutter, as they always appear together in the derivations. The authors could simplify the presentation by using a single symbol for all unobserved variables.
*   Line 165: There is a missing closing parenthesis.

Overall, this paper addresses an important problem with a novel idea. However, its potential impact is currently limited by significant issues with clarity and a lack of engagement with key related literature. I would be willing to reconsider my evaluation if the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address these concerns.
