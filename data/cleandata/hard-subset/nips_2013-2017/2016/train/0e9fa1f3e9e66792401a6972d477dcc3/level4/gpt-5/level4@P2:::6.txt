Summary: The paper applies bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-style samplers. By running AIS forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data, the authors estimate a gap that, in expectation, upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior (BREAD). They integrate this procedure into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on toy distributions and several standard models, propose a heuristic protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters, and show how the method can guide modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and uncover implementation bugs.

Relation to prior work: The core methodology builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001), with connections to SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006). The idea of using forward and reverse chains to sandwich log normalizers is established; the present work adapts that framework to estimate a bound on the symmetrized KL for approximate posterior samples. Related diagnostics such as Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015) and concurrent “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) are acknowledged conceptually but not deeply compared empirically.

Strengths:
- Practicality: Integrating BDMC-inspired diagnostics into WebPPL and Stan is useful for practitioners, offering a systematic way to gauge sample quality and detect implementation errors.
- Clarity of goal: The paper targets a real pain point—quantifying MCMC reliability, especially in black-box settings.
- Demonstrations: Examples show the method can inform model representation choices and even catch subtle bugs, underscoring value for PPL development and testing.
- Reproducibility potential: The protocol on simulated data is straightforward, and implementation in two platforms suggests generality.

Weaknesses:
- Limited originality: Most conceptual and theoretical components (forward/reverse AIS, stochastic bounds on log normalizers) are known. The derivation that the bidirectional gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is close to prior BDMC analyses and overlaps with concurrent work on subjective divergence.
- Scope constraints: The guarantees require exact posterior samples, restricting rigorous use to simulated data; the real-data protocol via fitted hyperparameters is heuristic, with no formal guarantees.
- Applicability: The approach applies primarily to AIS/SMC-like samplers over extended state spaces, not to general-purpose MCMC workflows without such structure.
- Empirical breadth: Experiments, while illustrative, are modest in scale and do not thoroughly compare against widely used convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS, BFMI), or against alternative sample-quality measures (e.g., Stein discrepancy).
- Tightness and informativeness: Evidence that the bound is tight is limited to small toy problems; practical guidance on when the bound is informative (versus vacuous) is limited. The “in expectation” nature of the bound can be a drawback for per-run assessment.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and position novelty: Precisely delineate what is new versus BDMC and concurrent subjective divergence, ideally including a formal comparison and empirical head-to-head results.
- Strengthen theory for real-data usage: Provide analysis or guarantees for the fitted-hyperparameter protocol, or bounds on bias introduced by approximate reverse initialization.
- Expand empirical evaluation: Include larger, more challenging posteriors (e.g., multimodal, high-dimensional), thorough comparisons to standard MCMC diagnostics and Stein-based measures, and ablations (temperature schedules, number of intermediates, transition kernels).
- Report computational overhead: Quantify wall-clock cost versus diagnostic benefit, and discuss guidance for settings where the diagnostic is most useful.
- Practical guidance: Offer criteria for interpreting bound magnitudes, diagnosing failures (e.g., reverse > forward), and tuning AIS schedules to improve bound tightness.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a concrete, implementable diagnostic that can improve reliability and debugging in probabilistic programming systems.
- Helps illuminate modeling and representation trade-offs with quantitative evidence.
- Clear exposition and useful integration into two widely used tools.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Conceptual and theoretical contributions are incremental relative to BDMC; much of the novelty is in implementation and application rather than new methodology.
- Guarantees hinge on simulated data with exact posterior samples; the real-data protocol lacks theoretical backing.
- Empirical evaluation is not sufficiently comprehensive and does not benchmark against standard diagnostics or competing measures of sample quality.

Recommendation: While the paper offers practical value for the PPL community and demonstrates useful diagnostics, its methodological novelty is limited and the guarantees are restricted in scope. As written, it seems better suited to a specialized MCMC or probabilistic programming workshop. Strengthening theoretical contributions, expanding empirical comparisons, and clarifying the relationship to concurrent work could make it more compelling for the main conference.
