- Paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood to evaluate MCMC reliability and compare MCMC schemes.
- Idea: select the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC upper/lower bounds for the same computational effort.
- Core challenge: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.
- Authors propose heuristics that sidestep this by running diagnostics on posteriors induced by synthetic data, where good approximate samples are easier to obtain.
- Method is evaluated on several example problems.
- The concept is interesting and offers a theoretically motivated diagnostic for AIS/SMC-type schemes, though it targets only marginal likelihood-based diagnostics.
- Major concern: the heuristic workarounds have significant drawbacks that reduce the practical appeal of the proposed diagnostic.
- First concern: method assumes prior knowledge of a single hyperparameter value (η_real) representative of the whole posterior, which presumes convergence of an algorithm and that a single value summarizes the posterior—both unjustified.
- Second concern: assumes the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior, an unjustified assumption that can fail under model misspecification.
- Third concern: assumes starting from η_real lets the MCMC rapidly draw an almost exact sample, an optimistic premise tied to the very convergence properties under assessment.
- The provided simple examples (Section 5.1) do not convincingly address these issues for more complex or realistic scenarios.
- The authors should explain and provide evidence for why their method would perform better in multimodal cases or when low-dimensional summaries are insufficient.
- Recommendation: include simulations comparing this method to standard diagnostics to demonstrate cases where it yields more reliable assessments.
