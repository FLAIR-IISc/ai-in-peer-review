This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of assessing the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors propose a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to evaluate MCMC-based algorithms. The core idea is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in two directions: a "forward" chain from the prior to the posterior and a "reverse" chain from the posterior to the prior. This process produces stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The paper's main theoretical contribution is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds serves as an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior.

A key challenge is that the reverse chain requires an exact sample from the target posterior, which is typically unavailable for real-world data. To address this, the authors propose a protocol for practical use: first, run approximate inference on the real dataset to estimate hyperparameters; second, generate a synthetic dataset using these hyperparameters; and third, perform the BREAD analysis on this synthetic data, for which an exact posterior sample can be generated. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference on several models and to compare the efficiency of different model representations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Quantitative Diagnostic:** The primary strength of this work is its attempt to create a more theoretically grounded and quantitative tool for MCMC diagnostics. Unlike many common heuristics (e.g., trace plots, Gelman-Rubin statistic) that can fail to detect non-convergence, BREAD provides a bound on a formal statistical distance (Jeffreys divergence). This is a valuable contribution toward making MCMC diagnostics more rigorous.

2.  **Novel Application of BDMC:** The paper cleverly adapts the BDMC framework, originally proposed for marginal likelihood estimation, to the problem of sample quality assessment. The derivation showing the connection between the log-likelihood gap and the Jeffreys divergence is elegant and insightful.

3.  **Practical Implementation and Use Case:** The integration of BREAD into popular probabilistic programming languages like Stan and WebPPL demonstrates its potential for practical utility. The experiment comparing collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is a compelling example of how BREAD can guide modeling decisions by providing a clear, quantitative criterion for convergence speed versus computational cost. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL further underscores its value as a tool for validating inference implementations.

**Weaknesses**

The main weaknesses of the paper arise from the heuristic protocol designed to overcome the need for an exact posterior sample when dealing with real-world data. This protocol relies on several strong assumptions that are not fully justified and, crucially, are closely related to the very problem the method aims to solve.

1.  **Circular Reliance on a Converged Sampler:** The protocol begins by performing approximate inference on the real data to obtain representative hyperparameters (ˆ⌘real). This implicitly assumes that this initial MCMC run has already converged and that a single point estimate can adequately represent the hyperparameter posterior. However, verifying the convergence of this initial run is precisely the kind of problem BREAD is supposed to solve. This introduces a degree of circular reasoning that undermines the method's rigor when applied to real data.

2.  **Assumption of Similar Inference Dynamics:** The method's validity hinges on the assumption that the MCMC sampler's convergence properties on a synthetic dataset (generated from ˆ⌘real) are similar to its properties on the original, real-world dataset. This is a strong and often untestable assumption. In cases of model misspecification, for example, the posterior geometry for the real data could be substantially different and more challenging to explore than that of the well-specified synthetic data. In effect, the method risks "changing the problem" from diagnosing inference on the true posterior to diagnosing it on a potentially much simpler one.

3.  **Optimistic Initialization of the Reverse Chain:** The reverse chain for the synthetic dataset is initialized using an approximate posterior sample obtained by running a few MCMC steps starting from the parameters used to generate the data. The assumption is that this "warm start" quickly yields a sample that is a good proxy for an exact posterior draw. This may be true in simple cases, but it is not guaranteed, especially for complex or multimodal posteriors where a short run may not be sufficient to equilibrate.

While the validation experiments in Section 5.1 are appreciated, the selected models are relatively simple. These examples are not sufficient to convincingly argue that the aforementioned issues will not be critical limitations in more complex, real-world scenarios where such diagnostic tools are most needed.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Direct Comparison with Existing Diagnostics:** The authors criticize existing diagnostic tools for being heuristic. To make a stronger case for BREAD's superiority, the paper would benefit immensely from a direct empirical comparison. It would be compelling to see a scenario (e.g., a model with multimodality or strong posterior correlations) where standard diagnostics like the Gelman-Rubin R-hat or effective sample size (ESS) falsely indicate convergence, while BREAD correctly identifies poor sample quality.

2.  **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper would be more balanced and scientifically valuable if it explored the limitations of its own protocol more thoroughly. An experiment demonstrating a case where the diagnostic on synthetic data fails to reflect the true convergence behavior on real data (e.g., due to model misspecification) would provide readers with a clearer understanding of the method's boundaries.

3.  **Clarification on Multimodality:** The authors mention that BREAD should be applicable to multimodal posteriors. Given that this is a common failure mode for MCMC and a weakness of many existing diagnostics, demonstrating this capability on a well-known multimodal example would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
