This paper addresses the important problem of assessing the reliability and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. The central proposal is to leverage the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) method. The core idea is to select the MCMC algorithm that produces the tightest bounds for a given computational budget. This approach is interesting because it represents an attempt to develop a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tool, moving beyond heuristics.

The primary challenge in implementing this idea is that the BMC procedure requires an exact sample from the target distribution to compute a valid stochastic upper bound. To circumvent this requirement, the authors propose a heuristic solution. In the context of Bayesian inference, instead of diagnosing convergence on the true posterior, they perform the diagnostic on a synthetic posterior. This synthetic posterior is generated using data simulated from a pre-selected parameter value, `eta_real`, which is assumed to be representative of the true posterior. The rationale is that it is easier to generate high-quality, near-exact samples from this synthetic target. The proposed methodology is then demonstrated on several examples.

While the conceptual foundation is appealing, the proposed heuristic introduces several significant drawbacks that undermine its practical utility. These issues stem from a series of strong and generally unjustified assumptions made to bypass the exact sampling requirement of BMC.

First, the method presupposes that a representative parameter value, `eta_real`, is known beforehand, perhaps from a previous MCMC run. This introduces a circular dependency: to diagnose the convergence of an MCMC algorithm, one must first run an algorithm and assume it has already converged to a representative region of the posterior. Furthermore, this approach implicitly assumes that the posterior distribution can be adequately summarized by a single point estimate, which is often not the case for complex, multimodal, or high-dimensional posteriors.

Second, the methodology rests on the critical assumption that the MCMC convergence properties of the synthetic posterior are similar to those of the true posterior. This assumption lacks justification and may not hold in many practical settings, particularly in cases of model misspecification or where the likelihood function is highly sensitive to the data. In effect, the proposed solution sidesteps the original challenge by "changing the problem"—it performs diagnostics on a different, and likely simpler, posterior distribution. The connection between performance on this proxy problem and the original problem of interest remains tenuous.

Third, the approach optimistically assumes that by initializing an MCMC algorithm at `eta_real`, the sampler can quickly produce a nearly exact sample from the synthetic posterior. While plausible in some cases, this is not guaranteed and itself constitutes a convergence assumption that is central to the problem the authors aim to solve.

Collectively, these assumptions mean the diagnostic tool relies on conditions that are deeply intertwined with the very convergence properties it is meant to assess. The validity of the diagnostic becomes conditional on the original MCMC algorithm having already performed well.

The empirical evaluation in Section 5.1, while appreciated, is not sufficient to allay these concerns. The simple examples demonstrate cases where the method appears to work, but they do not convincingly show that the identified issues will be benign in more challenging scenarios. The true test of a diagnostic tool is in complex situations (e.g., multimodality, strong correlations) where convergence is difficult to ascertain, but the experiments do not venture into such territory.

Furthermore, the authors criticize existing diagnostic tools for their potential to fail in multimodal cases or when simple summary statistics are insufficient. This is a valid point; however, the paper does not sufficiently explain or demonstrate why the proposed BMC-based method would perform better in precisely these scenarios. To be compelling, the paper should include experiments that directly compare the proposed method with commonly used diagnostics (such as those mentioned in Section 4). These experiments should feature challenging posterior landscapes designed to highlight the failures of existing methods and showcase the relative strengths of the proposed approach.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting idea for MCMC diagnostics. However, its practical implementation relies on a heuristic framework built on a cascade of strong, unverified assumptions that compromise its reliability as a diagnostic tool. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should provide theoretical or more rigorous empirical justification for these assumptions and conduct a comparative study against standard diagnostics on challenging, well-motivated problems.
