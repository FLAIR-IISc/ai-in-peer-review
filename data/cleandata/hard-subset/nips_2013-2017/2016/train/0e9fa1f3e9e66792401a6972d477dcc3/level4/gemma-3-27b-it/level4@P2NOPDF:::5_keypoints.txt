- Paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) bounds on log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and compare MCMC schemes.
- Main obstacle: BMC requires an exact sample for a valid stochastic upper bound, which is rarely available in practice.
- Authors propose heuristics: perform diagnostics on posteriors from synthetic data where high-quality approximate samples are easier to obtain.
- Method is evaluated empirically on several example problems.
- The core idea is appealing and theoretically grounded, particularly for AIS and SMC, but is specialized to marginal likelihood–based estimators.
- Heavy reliance on heuristics substantially reduces the method’s practical appeal and reliability.
- Problematic assumption 1: requires prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter η_real from preliminary MCMC, implying the initial MCMC has already converged and that the posterior can be summarized by a single value.
- Problematic assumption 2: assumes the synthetic-data posterior has similar convergence behavior to the true posterior, which is unjustified especially under model misspecification.
- Problematic assumption 3: assumes starting at η_real yields a near-exact sample quickly, which is not guaranteed.
- Consequently, the methodology’s validity is circularly dependent on the original MCMC’s convergence.
- The simple examples shown (Section 5.1) do not demonstrate robustness in more complex or challenging scenarios.
- Authors should explicitly explain how their method overcomes failures of existing diagnostics in multimodal or low-summary-statistic settings and provide supporting evidence.
- Recommend comparative simulation studies against common diagnostics (Section 4 methods) to demonstrate practical advantages and failure modes.
