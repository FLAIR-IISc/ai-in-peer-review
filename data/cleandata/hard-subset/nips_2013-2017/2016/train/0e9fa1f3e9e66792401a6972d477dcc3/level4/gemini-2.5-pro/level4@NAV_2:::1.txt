This paper presents BREAD, a method for evaluating MCMC-based inference algorithms by extending the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The key idea is to use forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains on simulated data to compute an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors integrate this technique into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility for comparing model representations and for debugging inference implementations. The idea of validating posterior simulators using data generated from the model has been previously explored (e.g., Geweke, 2004; Cook et al., 2012), and relating this work to that context would strengthen the paper.

**Strengths:**
*   The core proposal to use BDMC to quantitatively diagnose MCMC sampler performance is a significant contribution. It offers a more rigorous alternative to the often heuristic and unreliable convergence diagnostics currently in common use.
*   The application of this method as a tool for debugging probabilistic programming systems is particularly valuable and compelling. The reported discovery of a bug in WebPPL's multivariate Gaussian sampler is a strong testament to the practical utility of the proposed technique.
*   The use of BREAD to empirically study the trade-offs between different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) provides a clear and useful example of how the method can guide modeling and algorithmic decisions in a principled way.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The clarity of the main technical derivation in Section 3.1 is a significant issue. The presentation is difficult to follow, making it hard for the reader to grasp the core mechanics of the proposed estimator.
*   The paper's discussion of originality could be improved by situating the work within the existing literature on posterior predictive checks and simulation-based validation of Bayesian software.
*   The paper lacks a single, clear, and self-contained description of the BREAD protocol from start to finish. The reader currently has to piece together the full procedure from different sections, which may hinder adoption.

**Detailed Comments for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Section 3.1:** This section is very difficult to understand in its current form. For example, the notation `q(.)` is used before it is formally defined. While it can be inferred from the context that it represents the marginal distribution of the final state of the AIS sampler, this should be made explicit for the reader. The main text should be self-contained, and a reader should not need to consult the appendix to understand this central derivation. A rewrite of this section with a focus on clarity would substantially strengthen the paper.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper would benefit from citing and discussing the following highly relevant works on validating posterior samplers using simulated data:
    *   [1] Geweke, J. (2004). Getting it right: Joint distribution tests of posterior simulators. *Journal of the American Statistical Association*, 99(467), 799–804.
    *   [2] Cook, S. R., Gelman, A., & Rubin, D. B. (2012). Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles. *Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics*, 15(3), 675–692.
*   **Minor Stylistic Notes:**
    *   The notational distinction between unobserved variables `z` and `theta` seems somewhat arbitrary, as they consistently appear together. Simplifying the notation by using a single variable for all unobservables could improve readability.
    *   There appears to be a missing closing parenthesis on line 165.
