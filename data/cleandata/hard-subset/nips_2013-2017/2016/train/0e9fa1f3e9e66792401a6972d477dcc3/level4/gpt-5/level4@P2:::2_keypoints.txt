- Paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate MCMC samples and the true posterior using forward and reverse AIS; calls the method BREAD.
- Core idea: run AIS prior→posterior and posterior→prior on simulated data; the reverse-minus-forward log-AIS gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validates on linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and Gaussian mixtures.
- Proposes a pragmatic real-data protocol: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate from those fits, compare AIS behavior on real vs simulated data, and approximate reverse exact draws with short MCMC.
- Uses BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed representations and discovered a WebPPL multivariate Gaussian sampling bug.
- Builds on AIS, SMC, and BDMC literature and complements classical convergence diagnostics and Stein-based sample-quality metrics.
- Clarifies relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work and when their estimator upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence.
- Theoretical strength: derives a clear expectation-based upper bound on Jeffreys divergence with a clean BDMC connection.
- Practical strength: integration into major PPLs and diverse experiments that yield actionable modeling and debugging insights.
- Sensible real-data bridge: validates the simulation protocol and empirically shows reverse AIS robustness to MCMC burn-in for hyperparameters.
- Empirical validation: small toy problems show tight bounds; realistic case studies demonstrate usability.
- Broad scope: applicable to AIS/SMC-style algorithms and MCMC design choices, useful for probabilistic programming.
- Limitation: guarantees hold only in expectation and require exact posterior draws on simulated data; real-data protocol remains heuristic.
- Tightness concern: bound tightness mainly demonstrated on small examples; larger models lack quantitative estimates of looseness and confidence intervals.
- Computational cost: AIS schedules, numbers of temperatures, and wall-clock budgets substantially affect bounds and practicality; more guidance needed.
- Comparison gap: limited direct comparison to standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS) and Stein discrepancies on the same tasks.
- Clarity issues: some uneven notation and references; recommend consolidating main bounds earlier and giving a concise practitioner recipe (chains, temperatures, checks).
- Evaluation: theoretical development is careful; experiments reasonable but need more quantitative tightness analysis for large models.
- Originality and significance: novel BDMC application to posterior sample quality with practical tooling; high practical significance for probabilistic programming.
- Pros for acceptance: theoretically grounded metric for sample quality, cross-platform utility, actionable insights, and debugging value.
- Cons for acceptance: guarantees limited to simulated settings, limited analysis of tightness and computational trade-offs in high dimensions, and sparse comparisons to alternative diagnostics.
- Recommendation: Accept.
