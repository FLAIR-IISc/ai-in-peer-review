This paper addresses the challenging problem of assessing the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) outputs and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. The authors propose leveraging the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to achieve this goal – for instance, selecting the MCMC scheme yielding the tightest bounds for a given computational budget. A key obstacle is that obtaining a valid stochastic upper bound via BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution, a condition rarely met in practice. The authors introduce heuristics to circumvent this requirement, focusing on performing diagnostics on posteriors generated from synthetic data rather than the true data. This approach exploits the relative ease of obtaining high-quality approximate samples for these synthetic posteriors. The method is then evaluated on several examples.

The core idea of using BMC’s stochastic lower bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is appealing. While the methodology appears specifically tailored to Approximate Inference Samplers (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) schemes and relies solely on marginal likelihood estimation, it represents a valuable attempt to ground MCMC diagnostics in theoretical foundations. However, the implementation’s dependence on several heuristics to address the need for exact samples significantly diminishes its practical appeal. 

Specifically, the proposed approach relies on three potentially problematic assumptions. First, the authors assume prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value, denoted as η<sub>real</sub>, obtained from preliminary MCMC runs. This presupposes that the initial MCMC algorithm *has already converged* – essentially re-introducing the very problem the diagnostic intends to solve – and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value, a strong and often unwarranted assumption. Second, the method assumes the posterior distribution induced by synthetic data generated using η<sub>real</sub> exhibits similar convergence properties to the original, true posterior. This assumption requires substantial justification, particularly in scenarios where model misspecification is a concern. It appears the authors attempt to address BMC’s limitations by, in effect, altering the problem itself, performing diagnostics on a different posterior distribution. Finally, the methodology optimistically posits that starting from η<sub>real</sub> will enable the MCMC algorithm to quickly obtain a nearly exact sample from the posterior, a condition that is not guaranteed. 

Consequently, the proposed methodology’s validity appears intrinsically linked to the very convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm it seeks to assess. While Section 5.1 presents examples where these issues do not demonstrably impact the conclusions, these simple cases are insufficient to establish the robustness of the method in more complex or challenging scenarios.

The authors critique existing convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failures in multimodal settings or when using insufficiently expressive summary statistics (lines 203-205). They should explicitly explain how their method overcomes these limitations and provide supporting evidence. Furthermore, a comparative analysis with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in Section 4) through simulations would strengthen the paper. Such comparisons should highlight scenarios where the proposed method provides more reliable diagnostics than existing alternatives. This would demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach and help establish its value within the broader landscape of MCMC diagnostics.
