This paper presents a method, termed AutoBDMC, for assessing MCMC convergence. The method leverages the recently developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds for the log marginal likelihood, relying on annealed importance sampling. The authors demonstrate that the gap between the upper and lower bounds serves as a bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, thereby providing a single scalar quantity to evaluate MCMC convergence behavior. 

Since the method is limited to analyzing convergence on synthetic data generated from the joint distribution, the authors provide experimental evidence suggesting that its analyses on such synthetic data are predictive of behavior on real-world data. They also showcase the method's utility for model selection and debugging purposes. Overall, I found the paper to be quite readable and well-motivated. The presented method appears to be clearly useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling.

However, I found Section 3 to be more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. The transition into Section 3.1 is particularly jarring, as it abruptly introduces variable and equation definitions without providing sufficient motivation. If the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1 is a novel result (which I believe it is), this should be explicitly stated, as the paper's novelty and contribution seem to hinge on this finding.

Furthermore, the definition of AutoBDMC is not explicitly stated anywhere in the paper. Based on my understanding, AutoBDMC appears to be the approach of using AIS and rAIS to analyze MCMC convergence. I would expect to see a clear statement to this effect. By the time Section 3.2 mentions that "…this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC," the definition of AutoBDMC should be clearly established.

Additionally, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. On Line 149, it seems that p(theta, z | y) should be p(theta, z, y). Moreover, the paper exhibits notational inconsistencies, switching between using y and D to denote data/observations, which I found somewhat confusing at times.

Regarding the supplemental WebPPL code, it would be beneficial to include information on (a) which version(s) of WebPPL this code is known to work with and (b) how to integrate it into the WebPPL distribution for use. Alternatively, releasing this code as a WebPPL package that can be installed via npm would be even more convenient. If possible, a similar release for the Stan version of the code would also be greatly appreciated.
