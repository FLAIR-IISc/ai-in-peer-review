This paper proposes a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The core idea is to leverage the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood, which are produced by BDMC's forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, as a direct performance measure for MCMC operators. The primary contributions are the protocol for this evaluation and its implementation within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its core idea, which is both elegant and practical. Using the BDMC bound gap to assess the quality of posterior inference is a beautifully simple concept that is relatively easy to implement. This makes the work potentially high-impact, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming systems, where automated and reliable convergence diagnostics are critically needed. I believe most developers of such systems would find this method a valuable addition to their toolkits.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper has significant weaknesses concerning novelty, discussion of related work, and the clarity and depth of its technical contributions.

1.  **Novelty and Omission of Key Related Work:** The paper builds upon a recent arXiv submission on BDMC, and a substantial portion of the text is dedicated to re-explaining this existing method. The main novel insight presented here is that the gap between the BDMC bounds itself provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate posterior defined by the reverse AIS chain. While this is a nice observation, its novelty is debatable.

    More concerning is the complete omission of Radford Neal's seminal work on Tempered Transitions (TT) [Statistics and Computing, 1996]. This work is highly relevant and its absence is a major oversight. The TT algorithm performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweeps as BDMC, using them to construct a Metropolis-Hastings move that can traverse modes in a multi-modal distribution. In TT, the sample from the end of the reverse sweep is used to initialize the forward sweep, and the final sample of the forward sweep is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights from both sweeps.

    This acceptance ratio in TT is, as far as I can tell, precisely the ratio of the stochastic upper and lower bounds computed in BDMC. Neal's paper (Section 3.3) even interprets this ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function difference, noting that the acceptance probability approaches 1 as the two estimates converge. Acknowledging this connection would not only properly credit prior work but also provide valuable intuition. For instance, the choice of the annealing schedule (the `β_t` values) is critical in TT. Too large a step `Δβ` leads to a poor acceptance ratio, while steps that are too small are computationally wasteful. This directly translates to the BDMC gap: if the gap is large, it suggests the annealing schedule is too coarse, and the comparison between MCMC operators may be unreliable. This connection seems to have been missed.

2.  **Clarity and Technical Depth:** The focus on re-explaining BDMC leaves the new technical contributions in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed, which compromises their clarity.
    *   **Undefined Terms:** The definition of `v` as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous. It presumably refers to `{z_{1:T-1}, θ_{1:T-1}}`, but it is unclear from which sweep (forward or reverse). The inconsistent use of "rev" and "fwd" versus "for" and "back" further adds to the confusion.
    *   **Unclear Equations:** The estimator `p̂(y)` in Equations (5-7) and (9-11) is not defined, making it very difficult to follow the derivations. It seems likely the authors mean `E[log R̂_rev]` and `E[log R̂_fwd]`, but this should be stated explicitly.
    *   **Incorrect Equation:** Equation (6) appears to be incorrect as written, since the two distributions inside the KL divergence are identical. Based on the context from Equations (4) and (10), the second term should likely be `p(z, θ | y) q_back(v | z, θ, y)`.
    *   **"AutoBDMC":** The paper uses the term "AutoBDMC" but never defines what the "Auto" part signifies. Does it refer simply to the protocol of using the bound gap as a diagnostic, or does it imply an automated procedure for setting hyperparameters, such as the annealing schedule? There is a missed opportunity here to propose a method that iteratively refines the schedule (i.e., decreases `Δβ`) until the bound gap is smaller than a desired threshold.

3.  **Experiments:** The experimental setup lacks important details.
    *   **Annealing Schedule:** It is not clear how the `β_t` values are chosen. After inspecting the code in the appendix, it appears a linear schedule is used, parameterized by the total number of steps `T`. This crucial detail should be in the main paper.
    *   **Systematic Study:** The experiments would be much stronger with a systematic study showing how the lower and upper bounds (and their gap) vary as a function of the annealing step size. This would provide practical guidance on how to use the method and lend credibility to the comparisons between different MCMC operators. I suspect that comparisons are unreliable when the log-bound gap is large (>1 nat), and a more aggressive annealing schedule should be used in such cases.

**Minor Issues:**
*   **Typos:** The expression for `D_JS` is missing two closing parentheses. The citation for NUTS should be to Hoffman and Gelman, not "Homan".
*   **Figure Clarity:** The axis and tick labels in Figures 2 and 3 are too small and difficult to read.
*   **Acronyms:** The acronym RAIS (presumably Reverse AIS) and its variants `(u)c(R)AIS` are used in a figure caption without prior definition.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a valuable and useful idea for MCMC diagnostics. However, its execution is marred by a significant omission of related work, a lack of clarity in its technical descriptions, and insufficient experimental detail. While the core concept is strong, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards for publication. My recommendation for acceptance is conditional on the authors convincingly addressing the issues of clarity, the omission of key related work (specifically Neal, 1996), and the lack of experimental detail in their response and a revised manuscript.
