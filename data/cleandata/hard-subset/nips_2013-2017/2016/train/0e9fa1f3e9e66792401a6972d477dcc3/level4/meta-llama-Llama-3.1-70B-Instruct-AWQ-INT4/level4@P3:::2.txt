This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference methods is indeed a challenging and important one. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and their proposed solution is well-explained and easy to follow. The use of bidirectional Monte Carlo is a clever idea, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the method's strengths and weaknesses.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a technique for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the approximate distribution. The authors show that this upper bound is quite accurate on some toy distributions and provide a protocol for using this technique to analyze sampler accuracy on real-world data. They also integrate their method into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method. The writing is good, and the paper is easy to follow. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work and clearly explain how their method differs from previous approaches.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a rigorous and general method for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference. The authors' use of bidirectional Monte Carlo is a clever idea, and their analysis of the method's strengths and weaknesses is thorough. The paper's results are also impressive, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their findings.

In terms of weaknesses, one potential issue is that the method may not be applicable to all types of MCMC-based posterior inference methods. The authors note that their method is only directly applicable to algorithms such as AIS or sequential Monte Carlo, and it is unclear how well the method would work for other types of algorithms. Additionally, the method requires an exact sample from the target distribution, which may not always be available.
