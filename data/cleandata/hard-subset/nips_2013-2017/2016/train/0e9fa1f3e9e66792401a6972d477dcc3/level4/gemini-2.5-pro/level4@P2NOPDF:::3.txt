This paper proposes a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators by using the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the marginal likelihood, as estimated by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The technical contributions include implementations of this diagnostic for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for assessing the quality of posterior inference.

The primary strength of this work lies in its core proposal: using the BDMC bound gap to assess MCMC operator quality is an elegant, simple, and easily implementable idea. This simplicity suggests the potential for high impact, particularly within probabilistic programming systems, which are the paper's main target. The inclusion of this diagnostic would be a valuable addition to any such system.

However, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, primarily concerning novelty, the discussion of related work, and clarity.

**Novelty and Discussion of Related Work**

My main concern relates to the novelty of the technical contribution and the omission of key related work. The paper relies heavily on a prior manuscript on BDMC, and a significant portion of this paper is dedicated to re-explaining that method. As a result, the novel contributions feel somewhat rushed. The main new insight appears to be that the gap between the BDMC bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate posterior defined by a reverse AIS pass. While this is a nice observation, its novelty is debatable.

More importantly, the paper fails to cite or discuss Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions (TT) [Statistics and Computing, 1996], which is highly relevant. The BDMC procedure—performing a reverse "heating" sweep followed by a forward "annealing" sweep—is functionally identical to the proposal mechanism in TT. In Neal's method, the final sample from the forward sweep is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. This acceptance ratio is, as far as I can tell, precisely the ratio of the stochastic upper and lower bounds calculated in this paper.

Section 3.3 of Neal's paper even interprets this ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function ratio, noting that the acceptance probability approaches 1 as the two estimates converge. Acknowledging this connection would significantly strengthen the paper by placing the work in its proper context. The current omission is a missed opportunity to draw insightful parallels and build upon a well-established foundation.

**Clarity of Technical Details**

The rushed presentation of the technical contributions leads to several clarity issues:

1.  The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise. It is presumably meant to be $\{z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}\}$, but this is ambiguous given the two separate AIS sweeps. This confusion is compounded by the inconsistent use of labels ("rev" and "fwd" vs. "for" and "back").

2.  The estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ is not defined in the context of equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to understand the derivation of these expectations. The authors should clarify if they mean, for example, $E[\log \hat{R}_{\text{rev}}]$ and $E[\log \hat{R}_{\text{fwd}}]$.

3.  Equation (6) appears to be incorrect as written, since the KL divergence is between two identical distributions. Based on the surrounding context (e.g., equations 4 and 10), it seems the second term should be something like $p(z, \theta | y) q_{\text{back}}(v | z, \theta, y)$. This needs correction.

4.  The meaning of "AutoBDMC" is never explicitly defined. It seems to refer to the protocol of using the bound gap as a diagnostic with the fixed hyperparameter scheme from Section 3.2, but this should be stated clearly.

**Experiments and the Annealing Schedule**

The connection to Tempered Transitions highlights a missed opportunity in the discussion of the annealing schedule (the choice of $\beta_t$). In TT, it is well-known that the step sizes, $\Delta\beta_t = \beta_{t+1} - \beta_t$, are critical: steps that are too large lead to poor acceptance ratios (i.e., a large bound gap), while steps that are too small are computationally wasteful.

A similar intuition applies directly here, and the paper would be stronger if it included a discussion of this dependence. If the bounds are not close, the user should re-run BDMC with a finer annealing schedule (smaller $\Delta\beta_t$). The "Auto" in AutoBDMC presents an opportunity to develop an adaptive scheme that iteratively refines the schedule until the bound gap is below a desired threshold.

The experimental details could also be clearer:
- The method for choosing $\beta_t$ is not stated. The appendix code suggests a uniform spacing, but this should be specified in the main text. For Figures 2 and 3, it is unclear if "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" refers to the number of steps *within* each temperature of the BDMC run or something else.
- The acronym RAIS is used in figure captions without being defined. Similarly, the variants (u)c(R)AIS are not explained.
- The font size for axes and tick labels in the figures should be increased for readability.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and practical idea for diagnosing MCMC performance. However, the manuscript in its current form suffers from significant weaknesses, particularly the omission of a crucial piece of related work (Tempered Transitions) and a lack of clarity in key technical details and experimental descriptions. If the authors can convincingly address the relationship with TT and rectify the clarity issues in their rebuttal and final version, I would be inclined to recommend acceptance. The core idea is strong enough to warrant publication, provided these shortcomings are addressed.
