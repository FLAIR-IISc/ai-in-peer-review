This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to extend the recently proposed bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. By running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains in both the forward direction (from prior to posterior) and the reverse direction (from posterior to prior), the method establishes stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors' key theoretical contribution is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

A significant challenge for this approach is that the reverse chain requires an exact sample from the posterior, which is typically unavailable for real-world data. The authors address this by proposing a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters from the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters; and finally, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. The paper provides a strong empirical validation of this protocol. The method is implemented and tested within two popular probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, demonstrating its practical utility.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the critical and notoriously difficult problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC inference. While many convergence diagnostics exist (e.g., Gelman-Rubin), they typically only detect specific modes of non-convergence and do not provide a quantitative measure of the quality of the resulting posterior approximation. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative bound on the Jeffreys divergence, offering a much more direct assessment of sample quality. This is a significant advance over existing heuristics.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the BDMC bounds to the Jeffreys divergence, is elegant and sound. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the primary limitation of requiring an exact posterior sample. The proposed protocol for handling real-world data is pragmatic and is convincingly validated in Section 5.1, where the authors show that inference behavior on simulated data closely mirrors that on real data for several models.

3.  **Practical Impact and Utility:** The integration of BREAD into Stan and WebPPL makes this a readily usable tool for a broad audience of practitioners and researchers. This is a crucial step in moving the technique from a theoretical concept to a practical diagnostic. The experiments demonstrate this utility compellingly:
    *   BREAD is used to provide concrete guidance on modeling choices, comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model in both Stan and WebPPL and revealing interesting performance trade-offs.
    *   The authors report using BREAD to uncover a subtle bug in WebPPL's implementation, highlighting its value as a powerful tool for validating and debugging inference systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The primary limitation is that the rigorous guarantees of the method only hold for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world applications is a well-reasoned heuristic, but it is not a formal guarantee. The effectiveness of the protocol relies on the initial inference run on the real data to obtain good hyperparameter estimates. If this initial run produces poor estimates, the subsequently simulated data may not be representative, potentially leading to misleading conclusions about inference quality.

2.  **Applicability to Specific Samplers:** The method is directly applicable to samplers that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). It does not directly apply to more standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS in their typical usage. The authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS-based inference can inform the design of general MCMC algorithms, which is a reasonable claim since AIS often uses MCMC kernels. However, this transfer of insight is indirect.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field. It presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful method for tackling the important problem of MCMC quality assessment. The authors are clear about the limitations of their approach and provide a well-validated practical protocol for applying it to real-world data. The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the value of the method for both guiding modeling decisions and debugging inference software. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its limitations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
