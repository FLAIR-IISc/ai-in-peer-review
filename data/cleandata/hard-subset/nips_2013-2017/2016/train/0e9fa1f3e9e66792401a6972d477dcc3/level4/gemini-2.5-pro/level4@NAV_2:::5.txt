This paper addresses the important problem of assessing the quality of samples from Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms. The authors extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally proposed for estimating marginal likelihoods, to this task. The core idea is to use the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood—obtained by running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions—as an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence between the approximate and true posterior distributions. A key challenge is that the reverse procedure requires an exact sample from the posterior, which is unavailable for real-world data. The authors propose a protocol to circumvent this: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, generate simulated data from these hyperparameters; and finally, perform the diagnostic on this simulated dataset, where an approximate posterior sample can be generated to initialize the reverse chain. The method, termed BREAD, is implemented in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrated on several models.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles the critical and difficult challenge of creating reliable, quantitative diagnostics for MCMC, a problem of great practical importance for users of probabilistic programming languages and for algorithm designers.
*   The proposal to use the BDMC framework to bound the Jeffreys divergence is novel and theoretically grounded. It represents a principled attempt to move beyond heuristics and develop more rigorous diagnostic tools.
*   Even though the method is primarily applicable to samplers that can be framed as importance sampling (like AIS or SMC), the insights gained could be valuable for designing and tuning MCMC algorithms more broadly.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weaknesses of the paper stem from the series of heuristics required to apply the method to real-world problems, which undermine its core theoretical guarantees.

*   The proposed protocol relies on first obtaining a representative hyperparameter value, `ˆ⌘real`, from the real dataset. This introduces a circular dependency: to assess the convergence of an MCMC algorithm, one must first assume that a preliminary inference run has already produced a reliable result. This approach also implicitly assumes that the posterior over hyperparameters is well-characterized by a single point estimate, which is a strong assumption that may not hold in general.
*   The core diagnostic is performed not on the original problem, but on a synthetic dataset generated from `ˆ⌘real`. The validity of this approach rests on the crucial assumption that the sampler's convergence behavior on the synthetic posterior is representative of its behavior on the true posterior. This assumption is not justified, and it seems particularly fragile in the common case of model misspecification, where the posterior geometry for simulated data could differ significantly from that of the real data. In essence, the method seems to address the difficulty of requiring an exact posterior sample by changing the problem being analyzed.
*   The reverse chain is initialized using an *approximate* posterior sample. While the experiments in Section 5.1.2 suggest this is robust for the models tested, it remains a heuristic that weakens the rigorous bounding properties of the underlying BDMC framework.

Collectively, the proposed methodology appears to rely on a cascade of assumptions that are intimately linked to the very convergence properties it is designed to measure.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The empirical validation in Section 5.1 is helpful, but the examples are not sufficient to build confidence that the identified issues will not be critical in more complex, realistic scenarios. The authors criticize existing diagnostics for failing in challenging cases (e.g., multimodality), but they do not provide evidence that their method performs better in such settings. Including experiments on problems with known pathologies would strengthen the paper's claims.
*   To better demonstrate the significance of this work, the paper would benefit from a direct comparison with widely used diagnostic methods (e.g., the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic, effective sample size). Showing concrete examples where BREAD provides more reliable or insightful diagnostics than these established tools would make a much stronger case for its practical utility.
