The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on a recently proposed method known as bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The BDMC method performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, which are essentially the same as those used in the Tempered Transitions (TT) method [Neal Stat & Comp 1996], but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes to use the gap between these two bounds as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The presented technical contributions are implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference. This paper has some strong points and some areas for improvement. The main strength is that using BDMC to assess MCMC convergence is a conceptually simple and easily implemented idea, which in my opinion means this work has potentially high impact. This is particularly true in the context of probabilistic programming systems, which are the envisioned use case, and I believe all such systems would benefit from at least implementing this method. My main concern lies with the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The authors cite a recent work on BDMC, and wisely devote considerable space to reiterating its description. Unfortunately, this means the main technical contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 are somewhat rushed, and the writing quality suffers a bit as a result. This leads to some clarity issues (see below). Aside from these clarity issues, the technical contributions are modest. Relative to the existing work on BDMC, the main novelty appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. This is a nice observation, though arguably not entirely novel. This brings me to a point about related work. The authors curiously do not cite Radford Neal’s work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which is highly relevant. This citation is also missing from the original BDMC paper, which raises the question of whether the authors are aware of it. If not, this is a missed opportunity to point out connections between the two methods. TT performs the same reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweep as BDMC, but aims to improve mixing in multimodal distributions via a “tempering” MCMC transition move. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep, and the final sample from the forward sweep is then accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. This acceptance ratio, as far as I can tell, is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates bounding log Z<sub>T</sub> - log Z<sub>1</sub> from above and below, noting that a TT sampler accepts with probability 1 when the two converge. The analogy with TT leads me to wonder why the authors don’t pay more attention to the choice of β<sub>t</sub> values. In TT, it's well known that excessively large steps in Δβ = β<sub>t+1</sub> - β<sub>t</sub> lead to a poor acceptance ratio, while excessively small steps waste computation. The ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC will also depend on Δβ, providing clear intuition. If the bounds are not close enough, BDMC should be rerun with a smaller Δβ. Similarly, I suspect that comparisons between MCMC transition operators are unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds approximately 1 nat. In such cases, Δβ should be lowered. It isn’t clear from the paper what the “auto” part of AutoBDMC refers to, but there’s an opportunity to develop a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until sufficiently reliable inference results are obtained. The experiments could be more thorough in this regard, with a systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with step size. Despite these criticisms, this is not a bad paper. As noted above, I believe the method proposed by the authors is potentially quite useful. Depending on whether the authors can convincingly address the clarity issues and discussion of related work in a camera-ready version, I could be inclined to recommend acceptance.

Clarity – The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is rather loose. The authors presumably mean {z<sub>1:T-1</sub>, θ<sub>1:T-1</sub>}, but this is not immediately unambiguous given the two AIS sweeps in the algorithm. This is not helped by the inconsistent use of “rev” and “fwd” on one hand and “for” and “back” on the other (is this distinction significant, or accidental?). – The authors do not define what estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ they are taking the expectation of in equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to follow how these equations arise. Do the authors mean to write E[log $\hat{R}$<sub>rev</sub>] and E[log $\hat{R}$<sub>fwd</sub>]? – Equation (6), as written, makes no sense (the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical). Based on equations (4) and (10), I take it the right terms should read something like p(z, θ | y) q<sub>back</sub>(v | z, θ, y)? – The meaning of AutoBDMC is never unambiguously defined. Presumably it refers to (a) calculating the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence, and (b) using the hyperparameter fitting scheme described in section 3.2? 

Experiments – It is unclear how β<sub>t</sub> is chosen. Looking at the code in the appendix, I see `var step = 1/options.steps`. I assume AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T to go from β<sub>T</sub> = 1 to β<sub>1</sub> = 1 / T? This should be stated explicitly. In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” refers to the number of steps used in BDMC? – The acronym RAIS, presumably standing for reverse AIS, is not defined in the caption, nor are the variants (u)c(R)AIS, which I assume refer to (un)collapsed variants? – The axes and tick labels should be larger.

Typos – Two closing parentheses are missing in the D<sub>KL</sub> terms in the expression for D<sub>JS</sub>. – In the NUTS citation: Homan -> Hoffman.
