- Proposal: use BMC-derived stochastic upper/lower bounds to choose MCMC schemes by bound tightness for equal computational effort.  
- Core challenge: BMC needs an exact sample from the target to yield a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors’ workaround: run BMC on a synthetic posterior from data generated under an assumed hyperparameter η_real.  
- Heuristic 1 critique: assumes η_real is known from a prior MCMC run, which presumes the very convergence the method should diagnose and that the posterior is well summarized by one hyperparameter.  
- Heuristic 2 critique: assumes the synthetic posterior’s convergence behavior matches the original posterior, which can fail under model misspecification (i.e., changes the problem being diagnosed).  
- Heuristic 3 critique: assumes initializing at η_real lets the MCMC quickly produce nearly exact samples—an optimistic, unverified claim.  
- Circularity concern: the heuristics’ validity is intrinsically tied to the original algorithm’s convergence properties, undermining the diagnostic’s reliability.  
- Empirical limitation: demonstrations use simple examples where these issues don’t manifest, insufficient to establish robustness on complex/multimodal targets.  
- Claim gap: authors assert advantages over existing diagnostics for multimodality/insufficient summaries but do not explain or prove why their method overcomes these limitations.  
- Missing comparisons: no comparative simulations against standard diagnostics are provided to demonstrate concrete practical benefits.  
- Overall assessment: conceptually interesting but practical utility is undermined by unverified heuristics, implementation drawbacks, and lack of rigorous comparative evidence.
