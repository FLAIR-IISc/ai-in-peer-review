This paper extends the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is to upper bound the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence—also known as the Jeffreys divergence—between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples obtained from MCMC. This is achieved by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa, leveraging the framework developed in [GGA15] for estimating the log marginal likelihood. The authors integrate their method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p], and validate it on several models and datasets. They further demonstrate the utility of BREAD by applying it to compare different model representations in WebPPL and Stan, revealing performance tradeoffs.

The paper addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic inference: reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC, especially within “black box” inference systems where internal details are obscured. While convergence diagnostics exist, quantitative measures of accuracy are scarce. This work offers a promising approach to provide such a measure.  The connection to bidirectional Monte Carlo and the extension to divergence bounding is a natural and valuable contribution, building on a recent and relevant line of work.  The implementation in both WebPPL and Stan enhances the practical impact of the research. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, extending the BDMC framework in a principled manner. The authors provide a clear derivation of the Jeffreys divergence upper bound.
*   **Originality:** Applying BDMC to assess the quality of MCMC samples, rather than solely for marginal likelihood estimation, is a novel and insightful contribution.
*   **Significance:** The ability to quantitatively assess the accuracy of posterior inference is highly significant, particularly for practitioners using probabilistic programming languages. Providing users with a way to evaluate the reliability of results from these systems is a substantial advancement. The identification of performance differences between model representations in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the practical utility of the method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The authors make a conscientious effort to connect the theoretical analysis to the practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan.
*   **Implementation:** Integrating the method into two popular probabilistic programming languages is commendable and increases its accessibility.
*   **Validation:** The validation experiments, including the consistency checks between real and simulated data, are important and strengthen the credibility of the findings.



**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** AIS, and therefore BREAD, can be computationally expensive, especially for complex models. The paper acknowledges this implicitly but does not fully discuss the practical limitations regarding scalability.
*   **Simulated Data Dependence:** While the authors address the issue of applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulation, the validity of this approach relies on the quality of the hyperparameter estimates and the representativeness of the simulated data.
*   **Limited Scope:** The method is directly applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC, which may limit its applicability to other MCMC methods. 
*   **Potential for Misinterpretation:** The use of approximate posterior samples in the hyperparameter fitting step (Section 3.2) introduces a potential source of error that isn't fully quantified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in probabilistic inference.
*   The proposed method is theoretically sound and builds upon a recent and relevant body of work.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the practical applicability of the technique.
*   The validation experiments support the effectiveness of the method.
*   The insights gained from applying BREAD to compare model representations highlight its utility for guiding modeling decisions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of BREAD could limit its widespread adoption.
*   The dependence on simulated data requires careful consideration to ensure the validity of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The proposed method provides a principled and quantitative approach to assessing the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference, which is crucial for building trust in results obtained from probabilistic programming systems. Despite some limitations regarding computational cost and applicability, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
