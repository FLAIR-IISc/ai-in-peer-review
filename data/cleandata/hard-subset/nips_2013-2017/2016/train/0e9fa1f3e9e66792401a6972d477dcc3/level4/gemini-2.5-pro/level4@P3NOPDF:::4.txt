This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a novel method for assessing MCMC convergence in probabilistic models. The approach leverages the recently developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method, which uses Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and its reverse counterpart to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors make the key contribution of showing that the gap between these bounds is itself an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of the approximate samples. This provides a single, interpretable scalar quantity to diagnose MCMC convergence.

Recognizing that this method requires generating synthetic data from the model's joint distribution, the authors provide a compelling experimental evaluation showing that convergence analyses on synthetic data are predictive of MCMC performance on real-world datasets. They further demonstrate the utility of AutoBDMC for practical tasks such as model selection and debugging MCMC samplers.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and addresses the important and challenging problem of MCMC convergence diagnostics.
*   The proposed method is of significant practical utility. It provides a principled, quantitative metric for convergence that is grounded in information theory (the JS divergence), moving beyond heuristic checks.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and persuasive. The demonstration that diagnostics on synthetic data correlate with performance on real data is a crucial and convincing result that validates the method's applicability. The case studies on model selection and debugging effectively showcase the practical benefits of the framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary areas for improvement relate to the clarity of the paper's core contributions and theoretical exposition.

1.  **Clarity of Exposition in Section 3:** While the paper is generally readable, the exposition in Section 3 is significantly harder to follow than the rest of the manuscript. The transition into Section 3.1 is particularly abrupt, introducing variables and equations without sufficient context or motivation. A brief introductory paragraph explaining the goal of the section—to establish the theoretical link between the BDMC bound gap and a formal divergence measure—would greatly enhance readability and guide the reader through the derivation.

2.  **Highlighting the Novelty of the Divergence Bound:** The paper's primary theoretical contribution appears to be the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1, which connects the BDMC gap to the JS divergence. If this is indeed a novel result, it should be explicitly stated and highlighted as such. Clarifying the novelty and significance of this contribution is essential for properly contextualizing the paper's impact, as the overall method hinges on this key insight.

3.  **Explicit Definition of AutoBDMC:** The term "AutoBDMC" is used throughout the paper but is never formally defined. While a reader can infer that it refers to the complete framework of applying BDMC with AIS/rAIS for automated convergence analysis, the paper would benefit from a concise, explicit definition. This definition should be provided early on, and certainly before its first use in a technical context (e.g., the statement in Section 3.2, "...this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC").

**Minor Issues:**

*   **Potential Typo (Line 149):** The term `p(theta, z | y)` appears to be a typo and should likely be the joint probability `p(theta, z, y)`. Please verify this.
*   **Notational Consistency:** The paper alternates between `y` and `D` to denote observed data. Using a single, consistent notation throughout would reduce minor confusion for the reader.
*   **Supplementary Code:** The inclusion of code is a valuable addition. To maximize its utility and facilitate reproducibility, I suggest the authors enhance the release with the following:
    *   For the WebPPL code, please specify which version(s) it is compatible with and provide clear instructions for integration. Ideally, releasing it as a package that can be installed via npm would be the most user-friendly approach.
    *   A similar user-friendly release format or clear usage instructions for the Stan implementation would also be greatly appreciated by the community.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a useful and well-validated method for a fundamental problem in Bayesian inference. The connection between the marginal likelihood gap and the JS divergence is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation. My recommendations are focused on improving the clarity of the presentation, particularly around the core theoretical contributions, to ensure the paper's impact is fully appreciated. With these revisions, I believe this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
