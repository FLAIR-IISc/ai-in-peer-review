

This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors develop Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which leverages reverse annealed importance sampling to provide rigorous upper bounds on this divergence. The method is applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which underlie many MCMC approaches, and the authors integrate BREAD into both WebPPL and Stan for practical validation. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) deriving a theoretical bound on the Jeffreys divergence using BDMC, (2) demonstrating the accuracy of this bound on toy distributions, (3) proposing a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data through hyperparameter fitting and simulation, and (4) validating the method across multiple probabilistic models in both probabilistic programming systems.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through its careful theoretical derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, supported by empirical validation on toy distributions where exact calculations are possible. The experimental protocol for real-world data application is well-justified and addresses a critical challenge in applying theoretical bounds to practical inference problems. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical utility and provides concrete insights into model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization), with the paper even uncovering a bug in WebPPL through its application. The connection to related work [CTM16] is appropriately addressed, and the paper clearly differentiates itself by providing rigorous bounds that do not require strong assumptions about the posterior distribution.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized exposition and sufficient technical detail to reproduce the results. The relationship between BREAD and previous work on BDMC [GGA15] is clearly articulated, and the paper effectively contextualizes its contributions within the broader literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98]. The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic programming: providing a reliable, automatic method to assess the quality of black-box inference, which is essential for making these tools accessible to non-expert users.

Strengths: (1) Novel application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC inference, (2) Rigorous theoretical foundation with practical validation, (3) Demonstrated utility through integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks, (4) Insightful analysis of model representation tradeoffs, (5) Practical debugging capability demonstrated through WebPPL bug discovery.

Weaknesses: (1) The method is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained (simulated data), though the paper provides a reasonable protocol for extending to real data, (2) The computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains may be prohibitive for very large models, (3) The paper could provide more detailed comparison with alternative convergence diagnostics.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS as it addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference with significant practical implications. It makes a clear scientific contribution that advances the state of the art in evaluating MCMC inference, providing a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality. The method's ability to guide model representation choices and identify implementation bugs demonstrates its practical value beyond theoretical novelty. I strongly recommend acceptance.
