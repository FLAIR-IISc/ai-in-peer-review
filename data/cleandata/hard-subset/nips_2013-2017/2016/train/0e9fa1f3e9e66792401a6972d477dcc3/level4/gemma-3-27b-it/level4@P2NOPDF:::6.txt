This paper presents an application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to inference problems within the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages. The work primarily summarizes previously published BMC methodology and details its implementation within these two frameworks. While the implementations may be valuable as practical demonstrations, the paper lacks novel theoretical contributions. It essentially documents the application of established techniques to new software packages. 

The core contribution appears to be a demonstration of BMC’s functionality within WebPPL and Stan, showcasing how existing algorithms can be leveraged within these probabilistic programming ecosystems. However, the paper doesn't offer any significant insights into the behavior of BMC *within* these languages, nor does it propose any modifications or improvements to the algorithm itself. The empirical evaluation, while present, does not appear to highlight any compelling advantages of using BMC in these specific environments compared to existing inference methods already available in WebPPL and Stan (e.g., standard MCMC).

My primary concern is the limited novelty of this work. BMC is a well-established technique, and the presented contribution centers around implementation rather than innovation. The paper reads largely as an engineering report detailing the translation of an algorithm into two specific programming languages. While solid engineering is valuable, it generally falls outside the scope of a NeurIPS publication. A workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming implementation—such as the MCMC workshop—would likely be a more suitable venue for disseminating this work to a relevant audience. 

Specifically, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing inference routines within WebPPL and Stan. For example, a direct comparison of runtime, convergence rates, and accuracy between BMC and standard MCMC samplers (like NUTS in Stan) would strengthen the paper’s justification for its contribution. Currently, the lack of such a comparative analysis leaves the reader unconvinced of BMC’s practical advantage in these settings.

Furthermore, the paper could be improved by discussing any challenges encountered during implementation and how these challenges were addressed. This would provide useful insights for others attempting similar integrations. Detailing specific performance bottlenecks or implementation quirks would elevate the paper beyond a simple demonstration.

In summary, while the work demonstrates a functional implementation of BMC in WebPPL and Stan, the lack of novel ideas or a compelling comparative analysis makes it a less suitable submission for NeurIPS. The work is technically sound but lacks the significant contribution typically expected at this conference.
