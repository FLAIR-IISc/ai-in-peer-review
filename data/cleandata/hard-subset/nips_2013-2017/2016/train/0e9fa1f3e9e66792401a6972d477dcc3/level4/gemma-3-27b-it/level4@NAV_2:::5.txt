The authors address the challenging problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC inference, and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. They propose leveraging the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure as a diagnostic tool. The core idea is to select, among available MCMC schemes, the one that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. A key challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose heuristics to overcome this limitation, focusing on applying the diagnostic to posterior distributions generated from synthetic data, rather than real data, where obtaining good approximate samples is easier. The methodology is then validated on several examples.

The notion of utilizing stochastic lower bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing. While seemingly specific to AIS and SMC samplers and relying on diagnostics based on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation faces several drawbacks that, in my assessment, limit the appeal of the proposed diagnostic methodology. These drawbacks stem from the heuristic approaches used to circumvent the requirement of an exact posterior sample for Bidirectional Monte Carlo. 

Specifically, the authors assume prior knowledge of hyperparameters, denoted as ηreal, that are representative of the entire posterior distribution. This assumes both that the algorithm used to estimate ηreal has converged (effectively reiterating the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value – both strong and generally unjustified assumptions. Furthermore, they assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from ηreal exhibits similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior. This assumption requires careful justification, particularly in scenarios involving model misspecification. The approach appears to shift the problem rather than solve it, by performing diagnostics on a different posterior distribution. Finally, the method optimistically assumes that starting from ηreal allows the MCMC algorithm to quickly obtain a nearly exact posterior sample, an outcome that is not guaranteed. Therefore, the proposed methodology relies on assumptions deeply intertwined with the very aspects of MCMC convergence we aim to assess.

I appreciate the authors acknowledging these limitations and presenting initial results in Section 5.1 suggesting these issues may not critically affect conclusions in simple examples. However, these limited examples are not sufficiently compelling to claim robustness across more complex scenarios. The authors critique existing convergence diagnostics for potential failure in multimodal cases or when summary statistics are insufficient (lines 203-205). Clarifying how their method overcomes these limitations, and providing supporting evidence, would strengthen the contribution. More broadly, a comparative analysis of their method against established diagnostic tools (e.g., those described in Section 4), through simulations, would demonstrate scenarios where it provides more reliable diagnostics. 

The paper demonstrates quality in its technical soundness and clear presentation of the method. However, the originality is somewhat limited as it builds directly on existing work (BDMC) and applies it in a specific context. While the significance is potentially high – a reliable diagnostic for MCMC is valuable – the practical applicability is constrained by the strong assumptions. The clarity of the paper is excellent, and it is well-organized and informative.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Proposes a novel application of Bidirectional Monte Carlo for assessing MCMC reliability.
*   Identifies a critical challenge in MCMC diagnostics – quantifying the accuracy of approximate samples.
*   Provides a practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on strong and potentially unrealistic assumptions to overcome the need for exact posterior samples.
*   Limited validation on simple examples, lacking a comprehensive comparison to existing diagnostics.
*   The scope of applicability is restricted to algorithms amenable to AIS or SMC-based analysis.
