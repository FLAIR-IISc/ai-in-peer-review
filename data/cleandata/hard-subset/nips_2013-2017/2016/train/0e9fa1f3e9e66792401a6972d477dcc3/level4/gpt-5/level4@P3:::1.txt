Summary and relation to prior work
This paper adapts bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key idea is to run AIS both forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data. The gap between the forward stochastic lower bound and the reverse stochastic upper bound on log p(y) is shown to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS and the true posterior. The paper integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan, validates it on several models, proposes a practical protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters, and demonstrates how the tool can guide model-representation choices and even help uncover implementation bugs.

This work builds directly on BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015], AIS [Neal, 2001], and SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006], and relates to convergence diagnostics [Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Brooks and Gelman, 1998], sample quality measures [Gorham and Mackey, 2015], and thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski-type arguments [Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997]. It is also closely related to contemporaneous work on subjective divergence in probabilistic programming [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016]. Relative to software validation approaches that use simulated data—e.g., Geweke’s joint distribution test [Geweke, 2004] and posterior-quantile checks [Cook, Gelman, and Rubin, 2006]—this paper provides a quantitative, information-theoretic criterion that specifically targets the discrepancy between approximate samples and the true posterior.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clear, principled way to turn BDMC into an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC samplers.
- Practical impact: integration with WebPPL and Stan, with experiments on several canonical models; the method surfaces actionable insights (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and helped find a real bug.
- Relevance: addresses the important problem of evaluating black-box MCMC in probabilistic programming.
- Empirical protocol: proposes a pragmatic fitted-hyperparameter scheme to transfer insights from simulated to real data and validates that forward-AIS behavior matches closely between the two.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
Clarity
- Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. The distributions qfwd(·) and qrev(·) are used before being defined, and the conditioning structure (qfwd(v, xT) = qfwd(v) qfwd(xT|v), qrev(v, xT) = pT(xT) qrev(v|xT)) should be stated upfront. Please define:
  - qfwd(xT): the marginal distribution over final AIS states (the approximate sample distribution).
  - qfwd(v|xT), qrev(v|xT): the conditional path distributions for forward and reverse chains.
- Provide a concise “how to apply BREAD” recipe in the main text (a boxed algorithm would help): inputs, assumptions (reversibility/ergodicity of transitions, exact sample availability on simulated data), forward/reverse AIS steps, and how to compute/report the bound (including combining multiple runs).
- Clarify precisely which expectations the bound relies on (AIS randomness; for simulated-data results, also over the data-generation process).
- Minor presentation: splitting latent variables into θ and z adds clutter in this general setting; since they appear only jointly, consider using a single x throughout. Line 165 appears to be missing a closing parenthesis.

Quality
- The theoretical result is sound for AIS/SMC with reversible kernels and exact posterior initialization of the reverse chain. However, the bound holds in expectation; per-instance guarantees are not claimed. Please emphasize this distinction clearly.
- Tightness is demonstrated on toy problems; more quantitative results (e.g., reporting the estimated bound B with uncertainty across runs) on the larger models would strengthen the empirical case.
- For real data, the fitted-hyperparameter initialization for the reverse chain breaks the rigorous guarantee. The empirical checks are reassuring (reverse curves invariant to the number of MCMC steps), but a more explicit discussion of failure modes (e.g., model misspecification, weakly informative priors concentrating away from fitted values) would be valuable.

Originality
- Adapting BDMC to bound expected Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples is a novel and useful perspective. The relation to subjective divergence is well noted; contrasting the assumptions and practical consequences (true posterior vs. reference distribution) could be expanded.

Significance
- The method offers a rare, quantitative lens on posterior sample quality in black-box settings, with immediate utility for PPL users. The demonstrations—representation trade-offs in WebPPL vs. Stan and real-world debugging—underscore its potential.

Experimental methodology and reporting
- Temperature schedules and kernel choices strongly affect AIS; provide guidance on schedule design, kernel tuning, and computational budget. Reporting wall-clock cost for achieving a target bound would aid practical adoption.
- The distributions of AIS log-weights are heavy-tailed; since the theoretical analysis uses single-run log-estimates rather than averaged weights, please justify your aggregation (e.g., medians/quantiles) and report variability of the bound across independent runs.
- Where feasible, include comparisons to alternative checks (e.g., Geweke’s test; posterior-quantile test) to contextualize what BREAD detects that those may miss.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a principled, implementable technique.
- Integrated into widely used tools; produces actionable insights and detects real bugs.
- Solid theoretical framing with supportive empirical validation.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity gaps in the core derivation and missing step-by-step guidance for practitioners.
- Guarantees apply only in expectation and only rigorously on simulated data; practical bound for real data is heuristic.
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers; less directly informative for arbitrary MCMC used without annealing.

Recommendations to the authors
- Revise Section 3.1 to define qfwd, qrev, and all conditionals before use; add a concise algorithmic recipe and explicitly state assumptions and expectations.
- Report numeric estimates of the expected Jeffreys upper bound with uncertainty for each model and compare schedules/kernels.
- Expand discussion of the real-data protocol’s assumptions and potential failure modes; add sensitivity analyses beyond the number of reverse-chain burn-in steps.
- Simplify notation (use a single latent variable symbol) and fix minor typos (e.g., missing parenthesis).
- Where space permits, briefly compare to Geweke (2004) and Cook–Gelman–Rubin (2006) to position BREAD among software validation tools.

Overall assessment
A strong and relevant contribution that advances our ability to quantify posterior sample quality in probabilistic programming. With improved exposition and slightly stronger empirical reporting, this would be a compelling addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance. References cited in this review: Geweke (2004); Cook, Gelman, and Rubin (2006).
