This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a novel method for assessing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) convergence. The approach leverages Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) – specifically, annealed importance sampling (AIS) and reverse AIS (rAIS) – to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds provides a quantifiable upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution represented by the MCMC samples. This yields a single scalar value indicative of MCMC convergence quality.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear motivation and readability. The proposed method appears genuinely useful for diagnosing MCMC behavior, and the experimental results provide reasonably strong evidence supporting its efficacy. The authors validate AutoBDMC initially on synthetic data, demonstrating its ability to predict convergence behavior on more complex, real-world datasets. They further illustrate its practical applications in model selection and debugging.

However, I found Section 3 to be less accessible than the remainder of the paper. The introduction to Section 3.1 is particularly abrupt; the immediate presentation of variables and equations lacks sufficient context or motivation. If the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1 represents a novel theoretical contribution – which I believe it does – this should be explicitly stated, as the paper’s core novelty appears to rest on this result. 

A significant clarity issue is the lack of a concise definition of “AutoBDMC” itself. While I infer that AutoBDMC refers to the methodology of applying AIS and rAIS to analyze MCMC convergence, the paper never explicitly states this. Consequently, the reference to “AutoBDMC” in Section 3.2 feels premature; a clear definition should precede its use.

Several minor points also warrant attention. On line 149, the notation *p(θ, z | y)* seems likely to be a typo and should be *p(θ, z, y)*. Furthermore, there’s notational inconsistency regarding the representation of data/observations, with the paper switching between *y* and *D*. This caused occasional confusion.

Regarding the supplemental code, the provided WebPPL implementation should be accompanied by information specifying the compatible WebPPL version(s) and clear instructions on how to integrate the code with existing WebPPL distributions. Ideally, packaging the code as a distributable WebPPL package via npm would significantly enhance usability. A similar release for the Stan implementation would also be greatly appreciated and would broaden the impact of this work.

Overall, this is a promising and well-written paper that addresses an important problem in Bayesian inference. Addressing the clarity issues in Section 3 and formally defining AutoBDMC would substantially strengthen the presentation. Providing more user-friendly code releases would further increase the paper’s practical value.
