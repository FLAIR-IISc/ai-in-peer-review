This paper presents BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by MCMC algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS). The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which was originally proposed for estimating marginal likelihoods. By running AIS chains in both the forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions on simulated data, the authors derive an estimator that, in expectation, provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The method is particularly valuable in the context of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs), where inference mechanisms can be opaque to the user. The authors demonstrate its utility by integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validating it on several models, and using it to guide model design choices and even uncover a bug in WebPPL's implementation.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality. Standard diagnostics are often heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. BREAD offers a more rigorous, quantitative measure by bounding a formal statistical divergence. Applying the BDMC framework to bound sampler divergence, rather than just the marginal likelihood, is a clever and valuable contribution.

2.  **Practical Utility:** The method has clear practical applications, especially for developers and sophisticated users of PPLs. The case study where BREAD was used to uncover a bug in a WebPPL sampler is a powerful demonstration of its utility as a correctness-checking tool. Furthermore, the analysis comparing collapsed versus uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model shows how BREAD can provide quantitative guidance for making important modeling decisions that impact sampler performance.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors implement their method in two distinct and widely-used PPLs (Stan and WebPPL), which rely on very different MCMC kernels (HMC/NUTS and M-H, respectively). They validate the proposed protocol for real-world data across five different models, showing that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches the behavior on the real data. This careful validation strengthens the claim that BREAD is a generally applicable and useful tool.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of the Core Derivation:** The derivation in Section 3.1, which is central to the paper's contribution, is difficult to follow. The notation for the distribution of approximate samples, `qfwd(xT)`, is used in equations (e.g., Eq. 7) before it is clearly defined as the marginal distribution of the final state of the AIS chain. While this can be inferred from the context and the appendix, the main text should be self-contained. The clarity of this section would be greatly improved by:
    *   Explicitly defining all distributions, particularly `qfwd(xT)`, before they are used in the derivation.
    *   Including a concise, step-by-step algorithmic description of the BREAD procedure somewhere in the main text. As it stands, the reader must piece the full procedure together from different sections.

2.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper positions itself as a new way to rigorously validate MCMC, but it overlooks highly relevant prior work on MCMC validation that also leverages simulated data. The authors should discuss and differentiate their work from established "joint distribution tests" for posterior simulators. In particular, the following works are directly relevant and should be cited:
    *   Geweke, J. (2004). *Getting it right: Joint distribution tests of posterior simulators.* Journal of the American Statistical Association.
    *   Cook, S. R., Gelman, A., & Rubin, D. B. (2006). *Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles.* Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics.
    These methods also test a sampler's ability to recover true parameters from data simulated from the model, and a discussion of the relative merits of BREAD compared to these approaches would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Minor Notational Issues:** The distinction between parameters `θ` and latent variables `z` feels unnecessary in the general formulation, as they are always treated as a single block `(θ, z)`. Combining them into a single variable representing all unobserved quantities in the theoretical sections could reduce notational clutter and simplify the presentation. Additionally, there is a missing closing parenthesis on line 165.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. The idea of using the BDMC "sandwich" to bound the Jeffreys divergence of an MCMC sampler is novel and addresses a significant need in the community. The experimental results, particularly the successful debugging of a PPL and the quantitative comparison of model representations, provide compelling evidence of the method's utility. The primary weaknesses are the lack of clarity in the main theoretical derivation and the omission of key related work on sampler validation. These are addressable issues, and if the authors can revise the paper to improve clarity and properly situate their contribution within the existing literature, it will represent an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
