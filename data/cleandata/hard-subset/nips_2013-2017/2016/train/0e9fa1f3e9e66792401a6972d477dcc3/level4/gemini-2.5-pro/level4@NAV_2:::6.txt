This paper proposes a method, which the authors call BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The approach extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, previously introduced in [GGA15] for estimating log marginal likelihoods. The core theoretical contribution is to show that the gap between the forward and reverse annealed importance sampling (AIS) estimates, used in BDMC, provides an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. Since rigorous bounds are only available for simulated data where an exact posterior sample is known, the authors also propose a practical protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data. This involves fitting hyperparameters on the real data, simulating a new dataset from these parameters, and then applying BREAD. The authors implement their method in two prominent probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility on several models. They showcase its value by using it to compare the performance of different model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and to uncover a bug in the WebPPL implementation.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and persistent problem in Bayesian inference: how to reliably assess the quality of posterior samples. This is particularly important in the context of "black-box" probabilistic programming, where users may lack the expertise to diagnose convergence failures. Providing a quantitative measure of divergence from the true posterior is a significant step forward from heuristic diagnostics.
*   **Originality and Quality:** While the work builds heavily on AIS [Nea01] and BDMC [GGA15], the contribution is more than a simple application. The formal analysis connecting the BDMC gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and valuable theoretical insight. The proposed protocol for applying the technique to real-world data is a thoughtful attempt to bridge the gap between theory and practice.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is thorough. The authors validate their method across five different models in Stan, demonstrating that inference behavior on simulated data (using their protocol) closely matches that on real data. The experiments comparing model representations provide a compelling use case, yielding practical insights into algorithmic and modeling choices. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL further underscores the method's utility as a rigorous validation tool.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limitations of the Method:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is that the rigorous theoretical guarantees apply only to simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a reasonable heuristic, but it is not guaranteed to be foolproof, for instance in cases of severe model misspecification. Furthermore, the method is directly applicable only to algorithms like AIS or SMC, not to the most common MCMC methods like standard Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS, though insights may transfer.
*   **Novelty in Context:** The core machinery of running AIS in forward and reverse directions comes directly from [GGA15]. The novelty is thus in the new analysis (bounding divergence rather than bracketing marginal likelihood) and the application to evaluating inference quality. While this is a solid contribution, the conceptual leap from the prior work is incremental.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles an important, unsolved problem in a principled way.
2.  It presents a novel theoretical result by connecting the BDMC framework to the Jeffreys divergence.
3.  The method is implemented and validated in widely used probabilistic programming systems.
4.  The experiments provide concrete, useful examples of how the method can guide model design and debug inference engines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The work is a direct extension of existing methods (BDMC), limiting its fundamental originality.
2.  The rigorous guarantees of the method are restricted to the synthetic data setting, which may limit its perceived impact for practitioners working with real data.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significance in addressing a major challenge in applied MCMC and the quality of the technical contribution and experiments, outweigh its weaknesses. The work provides a valuable new tool and perspective for both developers and users of probabilistic inference systems.
