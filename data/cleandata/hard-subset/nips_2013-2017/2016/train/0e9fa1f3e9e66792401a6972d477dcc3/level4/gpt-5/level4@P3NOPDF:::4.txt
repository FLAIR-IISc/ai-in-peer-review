This paper proposes AutoBDMC, a method for assessing MCMC convergence using Bidirectional Monte Carlo with annealed importance sampling (AIS) and reverse AIS to obtain stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jensen–Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, yielding a single scalar diagnostic of convergence. Because this requires synthetic data drawn from the model’s joint distribution, the authors test whether diagnostics on synthetic data predict behavior on real datasets and demonstrate applications to model selection and debugging. The paper is well motivated, the approach appears useful, and the experimental results are reasonably convincing.

Major comments and requests for revision:
- Define AutoBDMC clearly and early. The paper never explicitly states what is meant by “AutoBDMC.” Please provide a concise definition in the introduction. For example, clarify that AutoBDMC refers to the automated application of BDMC using AIS/rAIS to produce stochastic bounds on log evidence and, via their gap, a convergence diagnostic for MCMC on synthetic datasets from the model. Also delineate what is automated versus what the user must specify (e.g., annealing schedule, base distribution, number of intermediate distributions, MCMC kernels, number of particles).
- Clarify Section 3, especially the transition into 3.1. The current text launches into variables and equations without motivation or intuition. A brief roadmap, followed by intuitive explanations before formal definitions, would greatly improve readability.
- Make the status of the divergence bound explicit. If the bound relating the AIS/rAIS gap to the Jensen–Shannon divergence is novel, state this prominently (ideally as a theorem with clear assumptions and a proof sketch), as the paper’s main contribution appears to hinge on this result. If it follows directly from existing inequalities, please cite the precise source and explain the connection.
- Scope and limitations. Emphasize that AutoBDMC evaluates convergence on synthetic datasets generated from the model, not necessarily on the observed dataset. Discuss when this proxy is predictive and when it might fail (e.g., model misspecification, heavy tails, severe multimodality, or when real data lie in atypical regions of the model’s prior predictive). Any additional empirical evidence or guidance on when synthetic-data diagnostics transfer to real-data performance would strengthen the claims.
- Computational cost and sensitivity. Please quantify the computational overhead of AIS/rAIS relative to baseline MCMC, and analyze sensitivity to the annealing schedule, number of temperatures, choice of transition kernels, and number of AIS particles. Are the bounds tight in higher dimensions or for complex posteriors? A small ablation study would be helpful.
- Relation to existing diagnostics. Provide a clearer comparison with standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat/PSRF, effective sample size, Geweke diagnostics, and simulation-based calibration). Discuss complementary strengths and weaknesses and, where feasible, include empirical comparisons showing when AutoBDMC provides additional or more reliable signals.

Minor comments:
- Line 149: Should p(theta, z | y) be p(theta, z, y)? Please verify and correct if needed.
- Notation: The paper alternates between y and D for observed data. Choose one and use it consistently.
- Acronyms and terminology: Define AIS and rAIS on first use, and maintain consistent capitalization and hyphenation (e.g., Bidirectional Monte Carlo).
- Code and reproducibility:
  - WebPPL: Include the exact WebPPL version(s) supported and clear installation/integration instructions. Ideally, release the code as an npm-installable WebPPL package.
  - Stan: Provide version information and straightforward instructions for use; a packaged release would be very helpful.
  - Document default hyperparameters (e.g., temperatures, number of particles, kernel settings) and provide scripts to reproduce all figures and tables.
- Presentation: Adding brief intuition and toy examples illustrating how the bound behaves as MCMC improves (e.g., the gap shrinking with increased mixing) would help readers build intuition.

Assessment:
- Significance: A theoretically grounded scalar diagnostic with actionable bounds is valuable, particularly if the Jensen–Shannon divergence result is new.
- Originality: Dependent on the novelty and framing of the divergence bound and the automation around BDMC; please make these contributions explicit in the introduction.
- Quality: The methodology appears sound and the experiments are informative, but Section 3 requires clearer exposition, and the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of assumptions, limitations, and computational trade-offs.
- Clarity: Generally readable, with the noted exceptions in Section 3.
- Reproducibility: Good overall; packaging and clearer instructions would make it stronger.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on clarifying and foregrounding the theoretical contribution in Section 3, explicitly defining AutoBDMC, tightening notation and exposition, and improving reproducibility details. The approach is promising and likely to be useful for practitioners engaged in MCMC-based inference, model selection, and debugging.
