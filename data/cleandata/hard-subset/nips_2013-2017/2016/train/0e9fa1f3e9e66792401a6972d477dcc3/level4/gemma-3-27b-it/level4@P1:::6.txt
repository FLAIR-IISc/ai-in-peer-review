The paper extends the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, the authors upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. They integrate this method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p], and validate it on several models and datasets. As a demonstration, they apply BREAD to analyze the effectiveness of different model representations in both probabilistic programming frameworks.

The paper builds upon existing work in Monte Carlo methods, particularly AIS [Nea01] and SMC [MDJ06], framing MCMC algorithms as importance sampling over an extended state space. It leverages the BDMC framework [GGA15] originally proposed for estimating log marginal likelihoods, adapting it to quantify the accuracy of approximate posterior samples. The work also relates to research on convergence diagnostics [CC96; GR92; BG98] and automatic MCMC parameter tuning [RR01]. Related concurrent work [CTM16] also explores BDMC-based evaluation of probabilistic programming inference, and the authors appropriately discuss the connections and differences between their approach and that of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Approach:** The paper presents a theoretically sound method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence, offering a quantitative measure of MCMC accuracy, unlike many existing heuristics.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan makes it readily accessible to practitioners of probabilistic programming.
*   **Validation on Multiple Systems:** Demonstrating BREAD’s applicability across two different probabilistic programming languages strengthens the credibility of the results.
*   **Insightful Application:** Using BREAD to compare different model representations and uncover a bug in WebPPL illustrates the method’s practical utility for both model development and debugging.
*   **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully situates its contributions within the broader landscape of Monte Carlo inference and probabilistic programming.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of extending BDMC for posterior evaluation relies heavily on previously published work. While the implementation and validation are valuable, the technical innovation is incremental.
*   **Simulated Data Reliance:** The method fundamentally requires access to exact posterior samples, limiting its direct applicability to real-world data. The proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (fitting hyperparameters and simulating data) introduces potential sources of error and may not always accurately reflect performance on real datasets.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains in both directions can be computationally expensive, potentially limiting the scalability of the method to complex models.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter Choices:** The performance of the method may be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters in the AIS chains.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper offers a valuable tool for rigorously evaluating the accuracy of MCMC inference, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field.
*   The practical implementation and validation in WebPPL and Stan make the method accessible to a wide audience of practitioners.
*   The demonstration of BREAD’s utility for model comparison and debugging highlights its potential impact on the development of probabilistic programming systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical novelty of the paper is limited.
*   The reliance on simulated data restricts the direct applicability of the method to real-world problems.
*   The computational cost may limit its scalability to complex models.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-executed and technically sound extension of existing work. While the core idea is not groundbreaking, the practical implementation and validation in two popular probabilistic programming languages are valuable contributions. The rigorous approach to quantifying MCMC accuracy and the demonstration of its utility for model comparison and debugging are significant strengths. However, the reliance on simulated data and the computational cost are limitations. 

I believe this paper is a solid contribution and would be a good fit for NIPS, although perhaps not at the very top tier of accepted papers.
