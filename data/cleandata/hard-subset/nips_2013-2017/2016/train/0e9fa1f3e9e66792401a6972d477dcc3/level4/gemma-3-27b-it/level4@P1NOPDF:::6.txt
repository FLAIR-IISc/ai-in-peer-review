The paper summarizes previously published results on bidirectional Monte Carlo and reports on its application using two probabilistic programming languages. There do not appear to be any novel ideas presented; the work primarily describes the implementation of established techniques within the WebPPL and Stan languages. An MCMC workshop might be a more suitable venue for this contribution.

The core contribution of this paper is demonstrating the practical application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) within probabilistic programming frameworks. While BMC is a valuable technique, its theoretical foundations and advantages have been well-established in prior work. This paper doesn’t expand upon those foundations. The authors successfully implement BMC in WebPPL and Stan, and provide illustrative examples. However, the paper largely serves as a case study of applying a known method to specific tools rather than introducing new methodological insights.

My primary concern is the lack of novelty. The paper doesn't present any new theoretical developments or algorithmic improvements to BMC. The focus is solely on the implementation details and application to WebPPL and Stan. While a clear and accessible demonstration of a technique's application can be valuable, the paper doesn't offer sufficient justification for publication in a venue like NeurIPS, which typically prioritizes novel research. 

Regarding the implementations, the paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison to standard MCMC methods (e.g., Hamiltonian Monte Carlo) within WebPPL and Stan. A quantitative comparison – demonstrating BMC’s performance advantages (or disadvantages) in terms of convergence speed, mixing, or accuracy – would strengthen the paper's contribution. The current evaluation relies primarily on qualitative observations and a single example.  Further, the choice of examples seems limited; exploring a broader range of models would provide a more comprehensive assessment of BMC’s effectiveness in these languages.

I also found the presentation of background material somewhat cursory. Readers unfamiliar with BMC might struggle to fully appreciate the nuances of the implementation. A more detailed explanation of the core concepts and theoretical underpinnings of BMC would be beneficial. Specifically, elaborating on the weighting scheme and its impact on convergence would enhance understanding.

The writing quality is generally clear, but could be improved with more careful editing. There are instances of awkward phrasing that detract from readability. (e.g., in section X, the sentence “…” could be rephrased as “…”)

**Specific questions for the authors:**

*   What specific advantages does BMC offer when implemented within WebPPL and Stan, compared to existing MCMC samplers supported by these languages?
*   Have the authors considered any optimizations to the BMC implementation to improve its performance in these probabilistic programming environments?
*   Are there any limitations of applying BMC within WebPPL and Stan that are not addressed in the paper?

**Overall:**

This paper presents a technically sound implementation of a known technique. However, the lack of novelty and limited empirical evaluation make it a less compelling fit for NeurIPS. While the work might be valuable to practitioners using WebPPL and Stan, it would likely be more appropriately presented in a more specialized workshop focusing on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a more suitable venue.
