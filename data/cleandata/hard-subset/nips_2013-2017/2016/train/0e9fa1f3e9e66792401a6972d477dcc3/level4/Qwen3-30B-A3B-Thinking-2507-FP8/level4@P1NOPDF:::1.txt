

This paper introduces bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) for estimating the Jensen-Shannon divergence between an MCMC sampler and its target distribution, with a focus on debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The core idea—using BDMC to diagnose convergence failures—is compelling and addresses a genuine gap, as existing MCMC diagnostics often lack robustness. The connection to prior work [1,2], which similarly assesses samplers using prior-sampled data, is highly relevant and well-motivated.

The main text, however, has significant clarity issues. Section 3.1’s derivation is difficult to follow: the symbol $q(\cdot)$ appears before its definition, and its meaning is ambiguous without explicit context. From the surrounding discussion, it seems $q(\cdot)$ denotes the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after $T$ steps, but this must be stated clearly in the main text. While the supplement provides additional clarity, the main paper should be self-contained; readers should not need to consult supplementary material to grasp the core methodology. The authors should include a concise, step-by-step guide to applying the method—from data preparation to divergence estimation—so that readers can implement it without piecing together details from scattered sections.

Minor concerns include the unnecessary distinction between unobserved variables $z$ and $\theta$. In a general setting, these variables co-occur without distinction, making the dual notation redundant. Simplifying to a single variable (e.g., $\theta$) would enhance readability. Additionally, line 165 contains a missing closing parenthesis.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach with clear practical utility for PPL developers. Addressing the clarity issues in the derivation and methodology presentation would substantially strengthen the contribution. The technical foundation is sound, and the potential for improving MCMC validation in practice is significant.
