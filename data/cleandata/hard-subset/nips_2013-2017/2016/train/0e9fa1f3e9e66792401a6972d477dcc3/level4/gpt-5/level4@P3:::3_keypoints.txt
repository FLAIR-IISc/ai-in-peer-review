- Paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) / reverse AIS to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate MCMC samples by the forward/reverse AIS log-marginal gap (BREAD), implemented in WebPPL and Stan.
- Validated on toy problems with exact comparisons; proposes a practical real-data protocol using simulations from fitted hyperparameters and shows empirical stability of reverse-AIS to MCMC steps.
- Demonstrated practical utility: compared collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and found a WebPPL implementation bug using BREAD.
- Builds on prior work: BDMC, reverse AIS, AIS, thermodynamic integration, tempered transitions, and complements subjective divergence and sample-quality diagnostics.
- Strength: conceptually simple with a rigorous expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, providing an interpretable diagnostic of inference quality.
- Strength: practical integration into widely used PPLs with useful case studies and a concrete debugging success.
- Strength: sensible fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data with empirical evidence of reverse-AIS stability.
- Strength: shows BREAD can guide representation and sampler choices in practice.
- Weakness: bound holds in expectation and requires exact posterior samples; the real-data approximate protocol’s limitations and failure modes need clearer discussion.
- Weakness: sensitivity to annealing schedule (beta sequence) is not systematically analyzed; gap likely depends strongly on beta step sizes.
- Weakness: should report per-step and wall-clock convergence and normalize by computational cost when comparing methods and platforms.
- Clarity issue: need precise definitions for AIS state variables (v) and clear statement that reverse chain uses p_T samples.
- Clarity issue: use consistent notation for forward/reverse chains and explicitly name distributions like q_fwd(x_T), q_fwd(v|x_T), p_T(x_T), q_rev(v|x_T).
- Clarity issue: in derivations (Eqs. 6–11) explicitly state which estimators’ expectations are taken and check consistency with earlier equations.
- Clarity issue: define “AutoBDMC” and what is automated (gap computation, simulation, schedule selection).
- Experimental detail request: specify how beta_t are chosen, clarify what reported “HMC/NUTS steps” mean, and define RAIS and other abbreviations; improve figure readability.
- Originality: main novelty is proving the forward/reverse AIS gap upper bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation—useful refinement of BDMC.
- Suggestion: discuss connections and differences to Tempered Transitions (Neal 1996) to clarify assumptions, goals, and intuition about beta spacing.
- Significance: provides a quantitative, interpretable bound on posterior-sample quality with practical PPL integrations and actionable guidance potential.
- Recommendation for broader adoption: provide guidance on scheduling (possibly adaptive), computational budgets (number of AIS chains), and trustworthiness of the fitted-hyperparameter protocol.
- Specific question: request heuristics or adaptive methods for choosing beta schedules (e.g., refine until gap below threshold).
- Specific question: how many AIS chains are recommended and how are chains aggregated beyond reporting quantiles?
- Specific question: robustness of fitted-hyperparameter protocol in high-dimensional, heavy-tailed, or multimodal settings and any empirical failures.
- Pros for acceptance: simple, general method with theoretical guarantees on simulated data, practical utility, clear integrations, and sound theoretical contribution.
- Cons for acceptance: needs clarity/corrections in derivations and notation, limited schedule sensitivity and computational trade-off analysis, and reliance on heuristic real-data protocol.
- Minor issues: correct “Jensen-Shannon” to “Jeffreys” divergence, fix notation/parentheses, correct NUTS citation (Hoffman), add Neal (1996) citation, and define RAIS and abbreviations in captions.
- Overall: reviewer leans toward acceptance if clarity issues and discussion of beta scheduling and related work (especially TT) are addressed.
