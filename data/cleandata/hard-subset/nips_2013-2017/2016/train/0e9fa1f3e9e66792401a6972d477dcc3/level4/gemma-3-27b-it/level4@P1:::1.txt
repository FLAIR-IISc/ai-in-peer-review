This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler and the target distribution, with a particular focus on debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). I like the idea of using BDMC to diagnose sampler failures – this seems potentially quite useful, as existing MCMC diagnostics are often problematic. Relevant related work includes [1, 2], which also assess MCMC sampler performance using data sampled from the prior.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from the prior to the posterior and vice versa, they upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the approximate samples. They integrate this method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan and validate it on several models and datasets.  As an example, they use BREAD to study the effectiveness of different model representations in WebPPL and Stan.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Application of BDMC:** Applying BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence for evaluating MCMC samplers is a clever idea and a novel contribution.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into popular PPLs like WebPPL and Stan makes the method readily accessible to practitioners.
*   **Rigorous Bounds:** The theoretical guarantees provided by BDMC (for simulated data) are a significant advantage over heuristic MCMC diagnostics.
*   **Demonstrated Usefulness:** The application to comparing model representations in WebPPL and Stan showcases the practical value of the method for guiding modeling decisions.
*   **Bug Detection:** Identifying a bug in WebPPL through the use of BREAD demonstrates its potential for verifying the correctness of probabilistic programming implementations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Derivation Clarity:** The derivation in Section 3.1 could be more clearly presented. Definitions and notation are introduced without sufficient explanation.
*   **Application Overview:** A more explicit and concise guide to applying the method from start to finish would improve usability.
*   **Real-World Data Protocol:** The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (relying on fitting hyperparameters and simulating from them) is a heuristic and may not always be reliable. More investigation into the robustness of this protocol is needed.
*   **Limited Scalability:** While the method is valuable for diagnostics, its computational cost might limit its applicability to very large and complex models.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in Bayesian inference – evaluating the quality of MCMC samples.
*   The proposed method is theoretically sound and offers rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan and the validation experiments demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.
*   The method has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The derivation in Section 3.1 lacks clarity and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The protocol for applying the method to real-world data relies on a heuristic and requires further validation.
*   The computational cost of BDMC might limit its scalability to larger models.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. While the clarity of the derivation and the robustness of the real-world data protocol could be improved, the theoretical soundness, practical implementation, and demonstrated usefulness of the method outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and provide more discussion on the limitations of the approach.
