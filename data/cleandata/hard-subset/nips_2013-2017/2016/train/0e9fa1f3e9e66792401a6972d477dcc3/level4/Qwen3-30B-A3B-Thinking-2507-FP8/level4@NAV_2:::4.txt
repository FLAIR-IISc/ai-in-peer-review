

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors demonstrate that BDMC can be refined to provide an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence, which they validate on toy distributions where exact computation is possible. They further develop a protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from hyperparameters estimated on the real data, and integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan. The work extends previous NIPS contributions on MCMC diagnostics [Brooks and Gelman, 1998; Cowles and Carlin, 1996] and related techniques for marginal likelihood estimation [Grosse et al., 2015; Burda et al., 2015], while distinguishing itself from concurrent work [CTM16] by using exact posterior samples for reverse AIS initialization.

Strengths: The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in probabilistic inference—assessing the reliability of MCMC samples—by providing a theoretically grounded, general-purpose method. The technical contribution of bounding the Jeffreys divergence via BDMC is novel and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The integration into two major probabilistic programming languages demonstrates practical utility, and the application to model representation comparison and bug detection showcases the method's real-world value. The experimental validation showing consistency between simulated and real-world data provides compelling evidence for the method's reliability. The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated, with a strong connection to practical inference challenges faced by users of probabilistic programming systems.

Weaknesses: Section 3 is particularly challenging to follow, especially the transition into Section 3.1, which launches into variable and equation definitions without sufficient motivation. The paper refers to "AutoBDMC" without clear definition (it appears to be a misnomer for the BREAD method), creating confusion about the core contribution. The notation inconsistently switches between y and D for data/observations, which is distracting. The implementation details for WebPPL and Stan would be significantly improved with packaged code (e.g., as WebPPL npm packages) rather than supplemental code that requires manual integration.

Quality: The method is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical foundation, well-supported by experimental results on toy distributions and real-world models. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is carefully validated, though the use of approximate posterior samples for reverse chain initialization in Section 3.2 requires careful interpretation.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and readable, but Section 3 would benefit from clearer motivation and a more gradual introduction to the technical details. The term "AutoBDMC" should be replaced with "BREAD" throughout to maintain consistency with the paper's main contribution.

Originality: The extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence is a novel technical contribution that clearly distinguishes this work from previous NIPS papers on MCMC diagnostics [Brooks and Gelman, 1998; Cowles and Carlin, 1996] and related work [CTM16].

Significance: The method addresses a critical gap in probabilistic inference practice by providing a rigorous, automated way to assess MCMC reliability. The ability to guide model representation choices and detect implementation bugs makes this work highly relevant to practitioners of probabilistic programming, with potential to improve the reliability of inference in real-world applications.

Pro: Novel theoretical contribution, practical implementation in two major probabilistic programming systems, compelling experimental validation, useful for model selection and debugging.
Con: Section 3 is difficult to follow, unclear terminology ("AutoBDMC"), notational inconsistencies, lack of packaged code for WebPPL and Stan.
