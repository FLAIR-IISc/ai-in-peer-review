- Paper proposes using forward/reverse AIS (BDMC) to sandwich the log marginal likelihood and bounds the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and AIS/SMC approximate samples in expectation.  
- Reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, so theoretical guarantees hold only for simulated data.  
- For real data, authors propose a heuristic: fit hyperparameters, simulate data, run BDMC, and compare AIS behavior between real and simulated datasets.  
- Implemented the method in WebPPL and Stan and validated on several models, detecting a real implementation bug.  
- Connects to prior work on MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin), AIS/thermodynamic identities, BDMC, Stein discrepancies, and contemporaneous subjective divergence work.  
- Main strength: conceptually appealing, theoretically grounded expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.  
- Strength: clear link to AIS/SMC as importance sampling on extended state spaces, with bounds improving with more annealing steps.  
- Strength: practical integration into PPLs and utility for modeling choices (e.g., collapsing).  
- Weakness: reliance on exact posterior samples confines rigor to simulated settings.  
- Weakness: real-data protocol depends on strong, largely unverified assumptions (representative hyperparameter estimate, simulated posterior matching real posterior, near-exact reverse-chain initialization).  
- Weakness: method is tailored to AIS/SMC and has limited applicability to general MCMC samplers without AIS wrappers.  
- Weakness: experimental support is modest and doesn’t convincingly probe challenging regimes like multimodality or misspecification.  
- Weakness: paper criticizes classical diagnostics but lacks demonstrations showing clear superiority or complementarity.  
- Theoretical core result is sound, but transition to real-data evaluation is heuristic and lacks guarantees.  
- Presentation is clear, but assumptions behind the real-data protocol need more explicit discussion and practitioner guidance.  
- Originality: novel extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence and embedding in PPL workflows.  
- Significance: could be valuable for debugging and comparing AIS/SMC pipelines if practical limitations are addressed.  
- Suggestion: provide head-to-head comparisons with standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, Stein discrepancies), especially on difficult models.  
- Suggestion: include sensitivity analyses showing how bounds degrade with approximate reverse initializers and tougher synthetic benchmarks.  
- Suggestion: report computational cost vs. diagnostic tightness and give practical interpretation guidance for bound magnitudes.  
- Suggestion: discuss alternative reference distributions and relate to subjective divergence when true posterior is unavailable.  
- Recommendation: clarify scope—when BREAD is advisable (AIS/SMC, PPL debugging) and when not (general MCMC).  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theoretical foundation, PPL integration, demonstrated practical utility and debugging capability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: real-data applicability rests on circular heuristics, limited empirical evidence on hard regimes, and restricted generality to AIS/SMC workflows.  
- Overall: promising and theoretically grounded for simulated data, but reliance on heuristics for real data and limited empirical comparisons reduce confidence in broad applicability.
