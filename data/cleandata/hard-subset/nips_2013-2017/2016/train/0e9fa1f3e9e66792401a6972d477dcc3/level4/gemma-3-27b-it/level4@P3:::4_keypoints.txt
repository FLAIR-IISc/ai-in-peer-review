- Proposes BREAD, using BDMC and AIS to compute stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood for assessing MCMC reliability.  
- Shows the gap between these bounds upper-bounds the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence, yielding a scalar MCMC convergence metric.  
- Notes direct, rigorous use requires exact posterior samples (i.e., simulated data); authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real datasets via hyperparameter inference and data simulation.  
- Implements and validates BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating utility for model selection and debugging.  
- Paper is well-motivated, mostly clearly written, and tackles an important challenge in Bayesian inference.  
- Section 3’s mathematical derivations are dense and abrupt; Section 3.1 lacks sufficient contextualization and motivation.  
- If the divergence bound derivation in Section 3.1 is novel, it should be explicitly stated.  
- The acronym “AutoBDMC” appears without prior definition and the relation between AutoBDMC and BREAD should be clarified.  
- Minor errors: typographical error on line 149 (p(θ, z | y) vs p(theta, z | y)) and inconsistent notation using ‘y’ vs ‘D’ for data.  
- Supplementary WebPPL code should specify compatible versions and clearer integration instructions (ideally an npm package); a packaged Stan release would also help.  
- Strength: Novel application of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence for MCMC diagnostics.  
- Strength: Theoretically grounded, providing quantifiable upper bounds (for simulated data).  
- Strength: Practicality through integration with widely used probabilistic programming languages.  
- Strength: Experimental validation shows effectiveness and actionable insights.  
- Weakness: Clarity issues in core mathematical section may limit accessibility and impact.  
- Weakness: Rigorous validation depends on simulated data; the real-data protocol relies on assumptions needing further study.  
- Weakness: Notational/terminology inconsistencies (e.g., AutoBDMC, y vs D) reduce clarity.  
- Weakness: Lack of packaged code hinders reproducibility and adoption.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, sound method with practical integrations and compelling experiments addressing a fundamental challenge.  
- Arguments for rejection: Dense math presentation, reliance on simulated data for rigor, and missing packaged code.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to improve Section 3’s clarity and to release packaged code.
