- Introduces BREAD: extends BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between an approximate posterior (e.g., from AIS) and the true posterior using reverse annealing, mainly applicable when simulated data allow exact posterior initialization.

- Combines forward and reverse AIS marginal likelihood estimates to produce an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.

- Proposes a practical protocol for real data by fitting hyperparameters, simulating new data from the model, and applying the BREAD diagnostic.

- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, uses it to compare model representations, and uncovers a real bug in WebPPL.

- Strength: provides a principled, quantitative metric (Jeffreys divergence) for evaluating MCMC sample quality rather than relying on unreliable heuristics.

- Strength: novel and practically useful extension of BDMC that aids developers and PPL users in validating and debugging inference algorithms.

- Strength: integration into popular PPLs makes advanced diagnostics accessible to practitioners who are not inference experts.

- Weakness: paper should better position itself relative to prior work on validating posterior simulators and prior predictive checks (e.g., Geweke 2004; Cook et al. 2006).

- Weakness: derivation in Section 3.1 is hard to follow and uses ambiguous notation (e.g., q(.) used for multiple distributions); distributions should be explicitly defined and main results made self-contained.

- Weakness: lacks a clear, high-level algorithmic description or pseudocode outlining the end-to-end BREAD procedure for implementers.

- Minor issues: simplify notation for unobserved quantities (use a single symbol instead of theta and z separately) and fix small typos (e.g., missing parenthesis on line 165).
