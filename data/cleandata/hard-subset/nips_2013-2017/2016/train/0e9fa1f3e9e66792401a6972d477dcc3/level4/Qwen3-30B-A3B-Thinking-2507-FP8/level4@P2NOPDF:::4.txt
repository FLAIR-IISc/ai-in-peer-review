

This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for assessing MCMC convergence by leveraging the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood via annealed importance sampling (AIS). Crucially, the authors establish that the gap between these bounds serves as a provable upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate samples generated by MCMC. This yields a single, interpretable scalar metric for diagnosing convergence behavior. To address the limitation that the method inherently analyzes synthetic data (generated from the joint distribution), the authors empirically demonstrate that AutoBDMC's convergence assessments on synthetic data reliably predict performance on real-world datasets. They further illustrate practical applications for model selection and MCMC debugging. The paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a method with evident utility; the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling.

However, several significant issues require attention before publication. The most critical concern is the absence of a precise definition for "AutoBDMC" itself. The term appears throughout the paper without explicit explanation, creating confusion about the method's core identity. Based on the content, AutoBDMC appears to denote the *application* of BDMC (specifically AIS and rAIS) to MCMC convergence assessment. This foundational definition must be stated clearly, ideally in the introduction or method section, as the novelty and contribution of the paper hinge on this specific application. The phrase "this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC" in Section 3.2 is meaningless without a prior definition.

Furthermore, Section 3 presents substantial clarity issues. The transition into Section 3.1 is disjointed, launching directly into variable definitions and equations without sufficient motivation. If the divergence bound derived in Section 3.1 constitutes a novel theoretical contribution (which appears to be the case), this novelty must be explicitly stated and emphasized. The paper's contribution is significantly undermined if this key result is presented as a straightforward consequence rather than a new insight.

Minor but noteworthy concerns include:
1.  **Notational inconsistency:** The paper uses both `y` and `D` interchangeably to denote observed data/likelihood. This inconsistency (e.g., in Equation 3 vs. Line 149) is distracting and should be resolved. Line 149 likely requires `p(theta, z, y)` instead of `p(theta, z | y)`.
2.  **Supplemental code:** The WebPPL implementation should specify compatible WebPPL versions and provide clear instructions for integration into the standard distribution. Releasing it as a dedicated WebPPL package (installable via npm) would significantly enhance reproducibility and usability. A similar package for Stan would be highly beneficial.

Overall, the core idea—using BDMC bounds to provide a convergence diagnostic via a Jensen-Shannon divergence bound—is promising and well-motivated. The experimental validation strategy (synthetic-to-real prediction) is reasonable. However, the lack of a clear definition for AutoBDMC and the poorly motivated theoretical development in Section 3 are major barriers to understanding the paper's contribution. Addressing these issues is essential for the paper to be accepted. The current draft risks confusing readers about the method's novelty and core mechanism.

The paper has strong potential but requires substantial revisions to clarify its central contribution and improve exposition. If the authors explicitly define AutoBDMC as the *method of applying BDMC/AIS to assess MCMC convergence* early on, and clearly articulate the novelty and motivation for the key bound in Section 3.1, the paper would be significantly stronger. The minor notational and code issues are readily fixable. As written, the clarity problems in Sections 3 and the missing definition prevent a clear assessment of the technical contribution. The reviewer recommends **major revision** to resolve these fundamental clarity issues before acceptance.
