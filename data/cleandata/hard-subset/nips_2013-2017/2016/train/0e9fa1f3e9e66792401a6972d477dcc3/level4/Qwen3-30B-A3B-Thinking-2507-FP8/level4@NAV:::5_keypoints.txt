- Paper proposes using BMC-derived stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to evaluate MCMC reliability and compare sampler efficiency.  
- Core selection rule: choose MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC bounds for the same computational cost.  
- Major obstacle: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to obtain valid stochastic upper bounds.  
- Authors introduce heuristics using synthetic data and a representative hyperparameter η_real to sidestep the exact-sample requirement.  
- Heuristic 1 problem: assuming prior knowledge of η_real (e.g., from a pre-run) presupposes convergence of the very algorithm under test and assumes the posterior is well summarized by a single point.  
- Heuristic 2 problem: assuming the synthetic-data posterior shares convergence properties with the original posterior is unverified and vulnerable to model misspecification, effectively diagnosing a different posterior.  
- Heuristic 3 problem: assuming η_real yields rapid near-exact posterior samples is optimistic and may not hold generally.  
- These assumptions create a circular dependency: the method relies on properties (convergence, representative η) that it is supposed to diagnose.  
- Empirical validation is limited to simple examples; these do not justify generalization to harder multimodal or poorly summarized cases.  
- Method is limited in scope: mainly applies to AIS/SMC and only targets marginal likelihood diagnostics.  
- Paper fails to demonstrate superiority over existing diagnostics or show how it overcomes known failures (e.g., multimodality, insufficient summaries); comprehensive comparative simulations are needed.
