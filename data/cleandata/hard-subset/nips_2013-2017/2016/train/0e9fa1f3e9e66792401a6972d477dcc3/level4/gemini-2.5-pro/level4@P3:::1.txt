This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of diagnosing the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, particularly within the context of black-box probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The authors propose a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to use Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to estimate the log marginal likelihood in two directions: a "forward" pass from the prior to the posterior and a "reverse" pass from the posterior to the prior. The authors show that the gap between the resulting stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

Because the reverse pass requires an exact sample from the posterior, the method is primarily applicable to simulated data where the ground truth parameters are known. The authors propose a protocol to extend its utility to real-world datasets by first fitting hyperparameters, then simulating data from the model using these hyperparameters. The method is implemented and validated within two popular PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, and used to provide practical insights, such as comparing the efficiency of different model representations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a problem of high significance. Standard MCMC convergence diagnostics are often heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. Providing a quantitative, theoretically-grounded measure of sampler quality is a valuable contribution. The idea of using the gap between BDMC bounds to estimate a divergence metric is clever and appears to be a novel application of this technique.

2.  **Practical Utility:** The integration of BREAD into Stan and WebPPL demonstrates a clear path to practical application. The experiments effectively showcase its utility not only for validating inference but also for guiding modeling decisions (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization) and for debugging PPL implementations, as evidenced by the discovery of a bug in WebPPL.

3.  **Soundness:** The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the expected gap in log-likelihood estimates to the Jeffreys divergence, appears sound. The validation experiments, particularly those confirming the consistency between real and simulated data behavior (Fig. 1) and the robustness of the hyperparameter estimation protocol, lend credibility to the proposed methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The derivation in Section 3.1 is quite difficult to follow. The notation is dense, and key terms are used before being formally defined. For instance, `qfwd(xT)` is used in equations without a clear statement that it represents the marginal distribution of the final state produced by the forward AIS chain. The reader is forced to infer this from context, which makes a critical part of the paper unnecessarily opaque. The paper would be significantly improved by a clearer, more step-by-step derivation in this section.

2.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks highly relevant prior work on MCMC validation using simulated data. The core idea of testing a posterior simulator by running it on data generated from the model's prior predictive distribution is not new. Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman, & Rubin (2006) both proposed influential methods based on this principle. While BREAD is technically distinct—providing a bound on Jeffreys divergence rather than testing posterior quantiles—these papers share the same foundational philosophy and should be cited and discussed to properly contextualize the paper's contribution.

3.  **Organization and Reproducibility:** The overall procedure is somewhat scattered throughout the text. The paper would benefit from a more consolidated, algorithmic description of how to apply BREAD from start to finish. This would clarify the steps involved, especially for the proposed protocol for real-world data, and improve the paper's utility for readers wishing to implement or apply the method.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Clarity of Section 3.1:** I strongly recommend rewriting this section for clarity. Define all `q` distributions explicitly before they are used in the equations. For example, state that `qfwd(xT) = ∫ qfwd(v, xT) dv` is the distribution of approximate samples that the method aims to evaluate.
*   **Notation:** The distinction between parameters `θ` and latent variables `z` feels unnecessary in the general theoretical sections, as they are always treated as a single block `(θ, z)`. Simplifying the notation by using a single variable for all unobserved quantities could make the derivations cleaner.
*   **Minor typo:** There is a missing closing parenthesis in the first equation of Section 3.1 (line 165 in the original submission).

In summary, this paper presents a novel and potentially very useful method for evaluating the quality of MCMC samplers. The core idea is clever, and the practical demonstrations are compelling. The primary weaknesses are the lack of discussion of key related work and the poor clarity of the main theoretical derivation. I believe the paper is a good candidate for acceptance if the authors can address these points, particularly by improving the clarity of Section 3.1 and situating their work with respect to the existing literature on simulation-based model checking.

**References to include:**
