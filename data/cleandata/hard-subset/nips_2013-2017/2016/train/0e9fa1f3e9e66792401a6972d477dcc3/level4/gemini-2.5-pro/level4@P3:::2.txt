This paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, particularly in the context of "black-box" inference systems like probabilistic programming languages. The authors propose a novel method, which they name BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), that extends the recently introduced Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework.

The core idea is to leverage Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to obtain rigorous, quantitative measures of inference quality on simulated data. A standard "forward" AIS chain, transitioning from the prior to the posterior, provides a stochastic lower bound on the log marginal likelihood. The key insight of BDMC is that by starting with an exact sample from the posterior and running a "reverse" AIS chain back to the prior, one can obtain a stochastic upper bound. The authors' main theoretical contribution is to show that the expected gap between these stochastic upper and lower bounds provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution generated by AIS.

Since this method requires an exact posterior sample, it is rigorously applicable only to simulated data. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset, then simulate a new dataset from these inferred hyperparameters, and finally, apply BREAD to evaluate inference on this simulated data. The authors implement their method in two prominent probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and conduct a series of experiments to validate the approach and demonstrate its utility. These experiments show that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data and that the method can be used to guide practical modeling decisions, such as choosing between different model representations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. As probabilistic programming languages become more widespread, the need for automatic, reliable, and quantitative diagnostics for inference quality is paramount. Existing heuristics can often fail to detect non-convergence, which can lead to silently incorrect scientific conclusions. This work provides a principled approach to rigorously assess sampler performance, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The primary technical contribution—linking the expected gap in bidirectional log-likelihood estimates to an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence—is both clever and elegant. This extends the original BDMC work from a tool for estimating marginal likelihoods to a more direct measure of sample quality. The theoretical derivation is clear and appears sound.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments that go well beyond a simple proof-of-concept.
    *   They validate the proposed protocol for real-world data across five different models, showing that inference behavior on simulated data (using fitted hyperparameters) closely matches that on real data.
    *   They demonstrate the practical utility of BREAD by using it to analyze the performance trade-offs between collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model in both Stan and WebPPL, providing valuable, quantitative insights.
    *   The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's usefulness as a tool for developers to debug and verify the correctness of inference implementations.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on AIS and BDMC before clearly laying out its own contributions. The experimental sections are well-structured, and the results are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limitation to Simulated Data:** The primary limitation, which the authors acknowledge and address, is that the rigorous guarantees of BREAD only hold for simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a well-reasoned heuristic, but it relies on the assumption that the model is well-specified and that inference behaves similarly on real and simulated data. While validated in the paper's experiments, this assumption may not hold universally.

2.  **Applicability to Specific Samplers:** The method directly evaluates the sample distribution produced by AIS or SMC, not a standard, long-run MCMC chain. The authors argue that since AIS uses the same transition kernels, insights about kernel performance will generalize. This is a reasonable argument, but the connection is indirect. The method evaluates the quality of an AIS sampler, which is related to but distinct from the convergence of a standard MCMC sampler.

3.  **Computational Cost:** Running bidirectional AIS chains is computationally intensive, likely requiring significantly more computation than the original inference task itself. This may limit its use as a routine diagnostic for end-users, positioning it more as a powerful tool for researchers developing new models or for developers testing inference algorithms.

**Critique based on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by clear derivations, and the experimental work is thorough, carefully designed, and convincing. It represents a complete and polished piece of research.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is very clearly written and well-organized. It successfully communicates a complex set of ideas to the reader.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. It builds upon recent work on BDMC but provides a novel and important extension by re-purposing the framework to bound the divergence of posterior samples, a different and arguably more practical goal than estimating the marginal likelihood.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. It offers a much-needed, principled tool for assessing the reliability of MCMC inference. This has the potential to become a standard technique for validating inference algorithms, comparing modeling choices, and debugging probabilistic programming systems.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, well-executed, and important contribution to the problem of evaluating MCMC inference. While the rigorous bounds are limited to the simulated data setting, the proposed framework and the practical protocol provide a powerful tool for researchers and developers. The strengths of this work far outweigh its limitations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
