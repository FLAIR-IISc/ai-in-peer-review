- Proposes using BDMC/AIS stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC sampler accuracy and compare samplers by tighter bounds per fixed compute budget.  
- BDMC gives a valid stochastic upper bound only if an exact posterior sample is available; authors mitigate this by running diagnostics on posteriors induced by simulated data to obtain exact/near-exact samples.  
- Method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrated empirically; it is theoretically grounded and most applicable to AIS/SMC-style samplers and marginal likelihood-focused diagnostics.  
- Key concern: the approach assumes knowledge of a single representative hyperparameter η_real (e.g., from prior MCMC), presuming that earlier runs have converged and that a single point summarizes the posterior.  
- Assumes the synthetic-data posterior at η_real exhibits MCMC behavior comparable to the real posterior, which is unvalidated and may be fragile under model misspecification.  
- Assumes that initializing MCMC from η_real will rapidly yield an almost exact posterior sample to seed the reverse chain—an optimistic, potentially unjustified assumption.  
- Consequently, the diagnostic depends on assumptions tightly coupled to the very convergence property it aims to evaluate, limiting practical reliability.  
- Small-scale demonstrations in Section 5.1 do not establish robustness in more challenging or misspecified settings.  
- Paper would be strengthened by explaining why BDMC should outperform standard diagnostics in multimodal or low-dimensional-summary cases and by including comparative simulations against common diagnostics.
