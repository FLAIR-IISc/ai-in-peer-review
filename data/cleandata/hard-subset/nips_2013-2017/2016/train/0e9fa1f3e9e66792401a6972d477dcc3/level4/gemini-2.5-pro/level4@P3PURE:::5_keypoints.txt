- Paper proposes assessing MCMC reliability and comparing schemes using stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC).  
- Core idea: pick the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational budget.  
- Key challenge: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Main contribution: heuristics to avoid needing an exact posterior sample by running diagnostics on a posterior generated from synthetic data.  
- Rationale: synthetic-data posteriors are often easier to sample from, and the method is demonstrated on several examples.  
- Novelty: using BMC stochastic bounds as an MCMC convergence diagnostic is a new and promising idea, especially for AIS/SMC samplers.  
- Limitation: method is specific to AIS/SMC and provides diagnostics based solely on marginal likelihood.  
- Major concern 1: method requires a pre-existing hyperparameter η_real, assuming that it was found by a converged algorithm and that a single point summarizes the posterior—both unjustified.  
- Major concern 2: assumes the synthetic-data posterior has similar convergence properties to the original posterior, which can fail under model misspecification and effectively changes the problem.  
- Major concern 3: assumes initializing MCMC at η_real will quickly yield a nearly exact sample from the synthetic posterior, which is not guaranteed.  
- The method’s assumptions are interdependent with the very convergence property it aims to assess, undermining its validity.  
- Examples in Section 5.1 are simple and insufficient to show the method’s reliability in complex, realistic settings.  
- Suggested improvement 1: explicitly justify and empirically demonstrate why marginal-likelihood-based diagnostics handle multimodality and low-dimensional-summary failures better.  
- Suggested improvement 2: provide direct comparisons with common diagnostics and simulations that highlight scenarios where the proposed method outperforms established techniques.
