The paper tackles the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC output and comparing MCMC efficiency using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The core idea is to prefer algorithms that yield tighter BMC bounds under the same computational budget. The main obstacle is that BMC requires an exact draw from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To address this, the authors propose heuristics: in Bayesian settings, they run the diagnostic on the posterior induced by synthetic data rather than the observed data, arguing that it is easier to obtain good approximate samples there. They then evaluate the approach on several examples.

The use of BMC-based stochastic bounds as an MCMC diagnostic is interesting and theoretically motivated. Although the method appears most directly applicable to AIS/SMC-type samplers and focuses on the marginal likelihood rather than more general functionals, it could still be useful as a principled diagnostic tool.

However, the practical implementation relies on heuristics that introduce significant drawbacks, all stemming from the need to circumvent BMC’s requirement for an exact posterior sample. In particular:

- The approach assumes a prior estimate of hyperparameters, η_real (e.g., obtained from earlier MCMC), that is representative of the posterior. This presumes (i) that the algorithm used to obtain η_real already converged—essentially the original concern—and (ii) that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point. Both assumptions are strong and often unjustified.

- It further assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has similar MCMC convergence properties to the true posterior. This is questionable, especially under model misspecification or when the likelihood structure differs meaningfully between synthetic and real data. As a result, the diagnostic may be applied to a different target distribution, effectively “changing the problem.”

- The method adopts an optimistic view that starting from η_real enables the MCMC algorithm to quickly generate an almost exact posterior sample. This may hold in simple or well-behaved cases but is not guaranteed, particularly in high-dimensional or multimodal settings.

Consequently, the methodology depends on assumptions that are tightly coupled to the very convergence properties we seek to evaluate. While Section 5.1 shows simple examples where these issues do not appear to affect conclusions, the limited scope of experiments does not convincingly demonstrate that the method will be robust in more complex or adversarial scenarios.

The paper also critiques prior diagnostics for failing in multimodal settings or when summary statistics lack expressiveness (lines 203–205). The authors should explain why their method would fare better in such cases and provide empirical evidence. More broadly, comparative experiments against commonly used diagnostics (e.g., those reviewed in Section 4) would strengthen the paper: show scenarios where the proposed method detects nonconvergence more reliably, more quickly, or more consistently than alternatives.

Additional suggestions and clarifications that would improve the work:
- Define “same computational effort” precisely and ensure fair comparisons across algorithms (e.g., wall-clock time, likelihood evaluations, effective sample size).
- Analyze sensitivity to the quality of the approximate sample used for the BMC upper bound, including how deviations from exactness affect coverage and bias.
- Provide a principled strategy for choosing or validating η_real without circularity, and discuss robustness when η_real is poorly estimated.
- Characterize failure modes when synthetic and real posteriors differ (e.g., due to misspecification, multimodality, or heavy tails), and propose diagnostics to detect such discrepancies.
- Clarify the scope: if the method is primarily for AIS/SMC, state this explicitly; if broader applicability is claimed, demonstrate it.
- Offer practical guidance on using the BMC gap as a diagnostic threshold, including variance estimates and recommended stopping or acceptance rules.

In summary, the paper presents a promising idea—leveraging BMC bounds for MCMC diagnostics and algorithm comparison—but the current heuristic workaround for obtaining an exact sample raises substantive concerns. Stronger theoretical justification of the assumptions, systematic empirical comparisons with standard diagnostics, and clearer guidance on how to verify or relax these assumptions are needed before the method can be recommended as a reliable, general-purpose diagnostic.
