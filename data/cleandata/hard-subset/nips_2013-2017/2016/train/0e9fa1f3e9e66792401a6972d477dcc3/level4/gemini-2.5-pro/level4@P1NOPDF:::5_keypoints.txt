- Paper addresses assessing MCMC reliability and comparing MCMC schemes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood.  
- Central idea: select the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC bounds for a fixed computational budget.  
- Major obstacle: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors' heuristic: run diagnostics on a posterior conditioned on synthetic data (generated from a representative hyperparameter) to obtain high-quality approximate samples.  
- Method is primarily tailored to AIS and SMC samplers and evaluates diagnostics solely via marginal likelihood bounds.  
- Strength: conceptually interesting and a step toward more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostics.  
- Key drawback: relies on heuristics that undermine BMC’s requirement for exact posterior samples, limiting practical appeal.  
- Concern 1: assumes a known representative hyperparameter η_real from a converged MCMC, which is circular and reduces the posterior to a single point summary.  
- Concern 2: assumes the synthetic-data posterior has convergence properties similar to the true posterior, an unjustified leap, especially under model misspecification.  
- Concern 3: assumes initializing chains at η_real rapidly yields near-exact samples from the synthetic posterior, which is not guaranteed.  
- Conclusion: the method’s assumptions are entangled with the convergence properties it seeks to diagnose, and the presented simple examples do not suffice to allay these concerns.  
- Recommendation: paper should explicitly justify and demonstrate superior performance in multimodal/challenging settings and include empirical comparisons with standard diagnostics.
