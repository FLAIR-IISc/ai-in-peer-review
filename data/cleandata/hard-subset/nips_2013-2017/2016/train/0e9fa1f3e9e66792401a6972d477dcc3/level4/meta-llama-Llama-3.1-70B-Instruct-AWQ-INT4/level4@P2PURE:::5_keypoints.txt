- Paper tackles evaluating MCMC reliability and comparing MCMC schemes using BMC upper/lower stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood.  
- Proposal: select MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for fixed computational effort.  
- Core challenge: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors’ workaround: heuristics that run diagnostics on posteriors from synthetic data, since those are easier to sample from.  
- Method is tested on several examples and is specific to AIS and SMC samplers, providing diagnostics based only on marginal likelihood.  
- Positive: using BMC stochastic bounds for MCMC diagnostics is an intriguing, theoretically motivated idea.  
- Main critique: heuristics to avoid needing an exact posterior sample introduce several strong, unjustified assumptions.  
- Drawback 1: assumes prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter η_real (from previous MCMC), which presumes that earlier MCMC had converged.  
- Drawback 2: assumes the synthetic-data posterior (from η_real) has convergence properties similar to the real posterior, which is unjustified especially under model misspecification.  
- Drawback 3: assumes starting from η_real lets the algorithm quickly produce an almost exact sample, an optimistic and unproven claim.  
- The methodology’s assumptions closely mirror the very convergence properties it aims to assess, weakening its validity.  
- Demonstrations in Section 5.1 use simple examples that do not convincingly show robustness in more complex or multimodal settings.  
- Authors should explain why their method would outperform existing diagnostics in multimodal or hard cases and provide empirical comparisons to common diagnostics.  
- Overall: theoretically interesting but implementation heuristics and strong assumptions make the diagnostic approach less appealing without further justification and comparative evidence.
