This paper presents a novel method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), for assessing the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. Building on the recently developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique, the authors leverage annealed importance sampling (AIS) to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. Crucially, they demonstrate that the gap between these bounds provides a quantifiable upper bound on the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence – also known as the Jeffreys divergence – between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples generated by MCMC. This provides a scalar metric for evaluating MCMC convergence. Recognizing the limitation that direct application requires access to exact posterior samples (feasible only with simulated data), the authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets via hyperparameter inference and data simulation. They validate their approach and demonstrate its utility for model selection and debugging by integrating it into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written for the most part, and addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian inference: rigorously assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples. The method appears promising, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling. The authors correctly identify the difficulty of validating MCMC convergence and provide a novel approach that moves beyond traditional heuristics. Integrating BREAD into widely used probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan is a significant contribution, potentially empowering users to gain greater confidence in their inference results.

Section 3, detailing the mathematical foundations of BREAD, proved the most challenging to follow. The transition into Section 3.1 feels abrupt, launching into variable definitions and equations without sufficient contextualization. If the derivation of the divergence bound in Section 3.1 represents a novel theoretical result—which seems likely given its centrality to the paper’s contribution—this should be explicitly stated. The acronym "AutoBDMC" is used without prior definition, leading to initial confusion. Clarifying that BREAD *is* the approach of utilizing AIS and reverse AIS (rAIS) to analyze MCMC convergence would improve clarity. The phrase "AutoBDMC" appears later in Section 3.2, reinforcing the need for an early definition. 

Minor points include a typographical error on line 149 [should be p(θ, z | y) instead of p(theta, z | y)], and a notational inconsistency in the use of ‘y’ and ‘D’ to represent data/observations. The supplemental WebPPL code should specify compatible WebPPL versions and provide clear integration instructions, ideally through a publishable npm package. A similar release for the Stan implementation would also be highly valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence offers a new approach to assessing MCMC convergence.
*   **Rigor:** The method provides quantifiable, theoretically grounded upper bounds on the divergence between the approximate and true posteriors (for simulated data).
*   **Practicality:** Integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method accessible to a wide range of users.
*   **Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the method’s effectiveness and its ability to provide actionable insights (e.g., model selection, debugging).
*   **Clarity (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and explains complex concepts in a digestible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Section 3:** The mathematical derivations in Section 3 are dense and lack sufficient motivation and explanation.
*   **Undefined Terminology:** The acronym “AutoBDMC” is used without initial definition.
*   **Limited Scope:** The method currently requires simulated data for rigorous validation. The protocol for real-world data is promising but relies on assumptions that need further investigation.
*   **Minor Notational Inconsistencies:** The switching between ‘y’ and ‘D’ for data can be confusing.
*   **Reproducibility:** Lack of packaged code for WebPPL and Stan hinders immediate reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for assessing MCMC convergence.
*   The integration with WebPPL and Stan is a significant practical contribution.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the method's utility.
*   The work addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian inference and has the potential to improve the reliability of probabilistic programming.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The mathematical details in Section 3 are challenging to follow, potentially limiting the paper’s impact.
*   The reliance on simulated data for rigorous validation remains a limitation.
*   The lack of packaged code hinders reproducibility and widespread adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and MCMC diagnostics. While the clarity of Section 3 could be improved, the novelty, rigor, and practical implications of the work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and encouraging the release of packaged code.
