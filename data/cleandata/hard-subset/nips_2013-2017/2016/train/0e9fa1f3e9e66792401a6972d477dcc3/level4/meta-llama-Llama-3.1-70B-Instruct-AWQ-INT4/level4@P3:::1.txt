This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler and the target distribution of interest, with a particular eye towards debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The idea of using bidirectional Monte Carlo to diagnose failures of a sampler to converge seems potentially quite useful since existing MCMC diagnostics are indeed problematic.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges in measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples obtained through Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The proposed method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), is a technique for using bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound the Jeffreys divergence from the true posterior on simulated data. The authors also provide a protocol for using this technique to analyze sampler accuracy on real-world data.

The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the method. However, I found Section 3.1, which presents the derivation of the method, to be somewhat difficult to follow. The notation could be clarified, and it would be helpful to have a clear statement of how to apply the method from start to finish.

The experiments presented in the paper are thorough and well-designed. The authors validate the method on several models and datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference. The results are also compared to those obtained using other methods, which helps to establish the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.

One minor issue is the notation used in the paper. The authors break the unobserved variables into z and θ, which can be confusing. It would be clearer to use a single notation for the unobserved variables.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough discussion of the existing methods for diagnosing convergence of MCMC samplers. However, they could also mention the work of Geweke (2004) and Cook et al. (2012), which also test the ability of MCMC samplers by running them on data sampled from the prior.

Overall, I think the paper is well-written and presents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic programming. With some minor revisions to clarify the notation and provide a clear statement of the method, I believe the paper would be even stronger.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel method for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in PPLs.
* The technical contributions are sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the method.
* The experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the results are compared to those obtained using other methods.

Weaknesses:

* The notation in Section 3.1 could be clarified.
* A clear statement of how to apply the method from start to finish would be helpful.
* The authors could mention the work of Geweke (2004) and Cook et al. (2012) in the related work section.

Acceptance:

* I would recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above.
