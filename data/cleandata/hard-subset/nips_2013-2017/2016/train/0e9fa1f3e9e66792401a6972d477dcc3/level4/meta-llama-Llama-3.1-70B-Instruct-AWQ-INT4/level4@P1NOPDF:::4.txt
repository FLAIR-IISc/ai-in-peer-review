This paper presents a method, termed AutoBDMC, for assessing MCMC convergence. The method leverages the recently-developed Bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds for the log marginal likelihood, relying on annealed importance sampling. The authors demonstrate that the gap between the upper and lower bounds serves as a bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, providing a single scalar quantity to evaluate MCMC convergence behavior.

Since the method is limited to analyzing convergence on synthetic data generated from the joint distribution, the authors provide experimental evidence suggesting that its analyses on such synthetic data are predictive of behavior on real-world data. They also illustrate the method's utility for model selection and debugging purposes. Overall, I found this paper to be quite readable and well-motivated. The presented method appears to be clearly useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling.

However, I found Section 3 to be more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. The transition into Section 3.1 is particularly jarring, as it abruptly introduces variable and equation definitions without providing sufficient motivation. If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 is a novel result (which I believe it is), this should be explicitly stated, as the paper's contribution seems to hinge on this finding. Furthermore, the definition of AutoBDMC is not explicitly stated anywhere in the paper. In my understanding, AutoBDMC seems to refer to the approach of using AIS and rAIS to analyze MCMC convergence. I would expect a clear statement to this effect.

By the time Section 3.2 mentions that "…this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC," the definition of AutoBDMC should be clearly established. Some minor issues include: Line 149, where p(theta, z | y) should likely be p(theta, z, y). Additionally, there are notational inconsistencies, as the paper switches between using y and D to denote data/observations, which I found slightly confusing at times.

Regarding the supplemental WebPPL code, it would be beneficial to provide (a) the version(s) of WebPPL with which this code is known to work and (b) instructions on how to integrate it into the WebPPL distribution for use. Ideally, releasing this as a WebPPL package that can be installed via npm would be even more convenient. A similar release for the Stan version of the code would also be greatly appreciated.
