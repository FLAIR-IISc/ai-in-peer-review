This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jensen–Shannon divergence between an MCMC sampler and the target distribution, with an eye to debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). I find the idea compelling: using BDMC to diagnose failures of a sampler to converge seems potentially very useful, especially given the well-known limitations of existing MCMC diagnostics.

Two closely related and relevant references are [1,2], which also assess MCMC samplers by running them on data generated from the prior. It would help to position the contribution more explicitly relative to these works—what advantages BDMC offers, what settings it improves upon, and any trade-offs.

Section 3.1: I found this derivation difficult to follow. In particular, q(·) is used before it is defined. From context, it appears to denote the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after T steps, but this should be stated explicitly when q(·) first appears. While reading the supplement makes certain points clearer, the main text should be self-contained. More generally, the paper would benefit from a concise, end-to-end description of how to apply the method in practice. As it stands, the reader must piece the procedure together from different sections. A brief algorithmic summary—covering the setup, the construction of the forward and reverse chains, what is estimated, and how to interpret the BDMC outputs—would greatly improve clarity.

Minor notes:
- It is unnecessarily cluttered to split unobserved variables into z and θ in a general presentation, since they always appear together. If there is no substantive distinction in this context, consider consolidating them into a single block of latent variables.
- Line 165: missing closing parenthesis.
