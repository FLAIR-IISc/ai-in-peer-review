

The authors propose using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and compare scheme efficiency. The core idea is to select MCMC methods producing the tightest BMC bounds for equivalent computational effort. However, BMC requires an exact sample from the target posterior to yield valid stochastic upper bounds—a critical hurdle. The authors address this by performing diagnostics on posteriors derived from synthetic data generated using a pre-specified hyperparameter \(\eta_{\text{real}}\), under the assumption that this synthetic posterior is easier to sample and its convergence properties mirror those of the true posterior. While the conceptual link between BMC bounds and convergence diagnostics is intriguing, the proposed methodology suffers from three fundamental flaws that undermine its validity and practical utility.

First, the approach assumes \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) is known *a priori* and representative of the true posterior. This implicitly requires a prior MCMC run that has *already* converged—a circular assumption that defeats the purpose of the diagnostic. Moreover, summarizing the entire posterior with a single hyperparameter \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) is unjustified for complex or multimodal posteriors, where a single point estimate cannot capture structural properties. The paper offers no justification for this simplification, rendering the method inapplicable to general settings.

Second, the authors posit that the synthetic posterior (induced by \(\eta_{\text{real}}\)) shares key convergence properties with the true posterior. This assumption is particularly problematic under model misspecification, where synthetic and true posteriors may diverge significantly. For instance, in cases where the model is incorrect or data is noisy, the synthetic posterior might converge rapidly while the true posterior remains poorly sampled. The authors provide no theoretical or empirical evidence to support this equivalence, making the diagnostic unreliable for real-world applications where model misspecification is common.

Third, the method relies on the optimistic premise that MCMC initialized at \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) quickly produces near-exact samples from the synthetic posterior. While plausible in simple settings, this is not guaranteed—especially for challenging posteriors where convergence is slow. The diagnostic’s validity thus hinges on an unverified, scenario-dependent condition that could fail catastrophically in practice.

These issues collectively transform the diagnostic into a test of a *different problem*: convergence on a synthetic posterior rather than the true target. The authors acknowledge these limitations (e.g., lines 203–205) but insufficiently address their implications. Section 5.1 presents simple examples where the approach appears robust, yet these cases lack complexity and do not test the method’s resilience under multimodality, high dimensionality, or model misspecification—precisely the scenarios where existing diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size) fail. Without evidence that the proposed method outperforms standard tools in such regimes, its value remains unproven.

The authors critique prior diagnostics for failing in multimodal or low-summary-statistic settings (lines 203–205) but do not demonstrate how their BMC-based approach overcomes these limitations. For example, if the synthetic posterior is unimodal while the true posterior is multimodal, the BMC bounds may falsely indicate convergence. The paper should explicitly address this risk and provide evidence—via simulations or theoretical arguments—showing superiority in such cases. 

More broadly, the methodology lacks comparative validation. The authors suggest benchmarking against standard diagnostics (Section 4) but omit such comparisons. A compelling case would require simulations where: (1) the true posterior is known (e.g., synthetic data with controlled multimodality), (2) both BMC and standard diagnostics are applied, and (3) diagnostic accuracy is quantified against ground-truth convergence metrics. Without this, the claim that BMC offers "theoretically justified" diagnostics remains unsubstantiated.

While the BMC framework itself is valuable for marginal likelihood estimation, its adaptation to convergence diagnostics here is compromised by the synthetic-data heuristic. The approach effectively shifts the diagnostic target from the *true posterior* to a *simplified surrogate*, which is not a solution to the original problem but a redefinition of it. For the method to be useful, the authors must either: (a) provide a rigorous justification for the synthetic posterior’s equivalence to the true posterior under general conditions, or (b) develop a framework that avoids this assumption entirely (e.g., by integrating BMC with adaptive MCMC).

In conclusion, the paper’s core idea has merit but is undermined by unverified assumptions that directly relate to the convergence problem it aims to solve. The current implementation is not robust to common challenges in Bayesian computation, and the limited examples fail to demonstrate generalizability. To strengthen the work, the authors should: (1) rigorously justify the synthetic posterior assumption, (2) provide comparative simulations against established diagnostics in challenging scenarios (e.g., multimodal, misspecified models), and (3) clarify whether the method’s validity depends on the specific choice of \(\eta_{\text{real}}\) or can be generalized. Without these, the proposed diagnostic remains an interesting but impractical tool for real-world MCMC analysis.
