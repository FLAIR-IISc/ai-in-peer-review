This paper proposes a method—referred to in the draft as AutoBDMC—for assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) with annealed importance sampling (AIS). The core idea is to run AIS forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior) to obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors then show that the expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by the sampler, providing a single scalar diagnostic of sample quality. Because the reverse bound requires an exact posterior sample, the method yields rigorous guarantees on simulated data drawn from the joint model. The paper also proposes a practical protocol for real-data evaluation: fit hyperparameters to the real dataset, simulate data from the fitted model, and validate that AIS behavior on simulated and real data agrees. The authors integrate their approach into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate applications to comparing model representations and to debugging.

Relation to prior work: The method builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001), extending BDMC’s use from estimating log marginal likelihood to bounding divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples. The connection to reverse AIS and thermodynamic integration is well known (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997). The paper’s focus on probabilistic programming languages situates it alongside recent work on assessing posterior approximation quality (e.g., Gorham and Mackey, 2015) and concurrent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016).

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: diagnosing MCMC reliability—especially in black-box systems—is both important and challenging.
- Conceptual simplicity: the bound arises naturally from bidirectional AIS; the resulting scalar diagnostic is easy to interpret.
- Practical relevance: integration with WebPPL and Stan, with instructive case studies comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and a real debugging example.
- Thoughtful real-data protocol: the fitted-hyperparameter procedure and empirical checks for consistency between real and simulated data are pragmatic and well justified.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Clarity in Section 3: This section is substantially harder to follow than the rest. The transition into 3.1 is abrupt, introducing variables and equations before motivating them. I recommend adding a brief high-level roadmap for 3.1 that explains the goal (bounding the Jeffreys divergence in expectation), the role of forward vs. reverse chains, and why conditioning/marginalization steps appear in the derivation.
- Novelty statement: If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 (i.e., that the expected gap between the forward and reverse AIS log-evidence estimators upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the target and approximate sample distributions) is new, please state this explicitly in the introduction and again at the start of Section 3, as the paper’s main contribution hinges on it.
- Terminology/definition: What, precisely, is “AutoBDMC”? The term is used but not defined. Please give a clear definition early (e.g., “AutoBDMC” as the application of forward and reverse AIS—and the resulting BDMC bounds—to quantify sample quality), so that by the time 3.2 refers to “a challenge for AutoBDMC,” readers know exactly what is meant.
- Notation consistency: The paper alternates between y and D for the observed data. Please choose one and use it consistently. Also, check line 149: should p(θ, z | y) be p(θ, z, y) in that context?
- Reproducibility details: For the WebPPL code, please specify which version(s) it supports and provide clear instructions for integration. Ideally, release it as a WebPPL package installable via npm. Similar packaging/instructions for Stan would be highly appreciated.

Empirical evaluation: The experiments are well chosen and reasonably convincing. The validation comparing forward AIS on real vs. simulated data (using fitted hyperparameters) supports the claim that behavior on simulated data can be predictive of real-data performance. The sensitivity study on the number of MCMC steps used to approximate posterior hyperparameters suggests the reverse bound is robust to this approximation. The comparison of model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) yields actionable insights that differ across Stan and WebPPL, highlighting the method’s practical utility. The debugging case where the reverse bound violated the theoretical ordering is particularly compelling as a demonstration of the method’s usefulness beyond benchmarking.

Pros for acceptance:
- Timely and practical contribution with clear utility for probabilistic programming.
- Conceptually neat extension of BDMC with a provable divergence-based diagnostic.
- Strong integration and case studies, including a real debugging win.

Cons for acceptance:
- Section 3 needs clearer exposition and an explicit statement of novelty.
- Terminology and notation should be standardized; define “AutoBDMC.”
- Reproducibility could be improved with versioning and packaging guidance.

Overall assessment: A well-motivated and useful paper that extends BDMC in a practically impactful way. With improved clarity in Section 3 and crisper definitions and notation, this would be a strong contribution. I recommend acceptance after revisions addressing the clarity and reproducibility points above.
