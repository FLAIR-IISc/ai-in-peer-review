- Addresses assessing reliability of MCMC inference and comparing MCMC scheme efficiency.
- Proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood as an MCMC diagnostic.
- Core idea: select the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC bounds for a given computational effort.
- Major challenge: BMC requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.
- Authors propose heuristics to avoid exact-sample requirement by applying diagnostics to posteriors from synthetic data.
- Heuristic depends on prior knowledge of representative hyperparameters (ηreal) estimated beforehand.
- Assumes the estimator for ηreal has itself converged, effectively restating the original convergence problem.
- Assumes the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single ηreal value, a strong and generally unjustified simplification.
- Assumes MCMC convergence behavior on the synthetic-data posterior matches that of the real posterior, which is questionable especially under model misspecification.
- Assumes initializing MCMC at ηreal will quickly yield a near-exact posterior sample, an unguaranteed and optimistic claim.
- Overall critique: the method shifts rather than solves the convergence-assessment problem by diagnosing a different posterior under strong assumptions.
- Validation is limited to simple examples (Section 5.1) and is not convincing evidence of robustness in complex scenarios.
- Paper lacks comparative simulations against established diagnostics to show where the method outperforms existing tools.
- Strengths: technically sound, clearly presented, novel application of BDMC, and practical implementations in WebPPL and Stan.
- Limitations: originality limited (builds directly on BDMC), practical applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-compatible algorithms, and reliance on strong assumptions.
