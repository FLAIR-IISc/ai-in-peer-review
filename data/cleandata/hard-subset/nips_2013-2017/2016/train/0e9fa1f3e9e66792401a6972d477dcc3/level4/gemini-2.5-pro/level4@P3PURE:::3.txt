### Summary

The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—one forward (annealing) and one reverse (heating)—to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The central proposal of this paper is to use the gap between these bounds as a performance metric for MCMC operators. The technical contributions include implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper's primary strength is the simple and elegant proposal to use the BDMC bound gap to assess MCMC operator quality. This idea is easy to implement and has the potential for high impact, particularly within probabilistic programming systems, which are the paper's main target. I agree with the authors that such systems would benefit from implementing this diagnostic tool.

However, my main concerns relate to the novelty and depth of the technical contributions, as well as a significant omission in the discussion of related work. While the authors wisely dedicate space to explaining the underlying BDMC method (from a cited arXiv submission), this leaves the paper's own contributions in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed and lacking in detail, which leads to several clarity issues outlined below.

Setting clarity aside, the technical contributions are modest. The main novelty relative to the existing BDMC work appears to be the observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. While a nice observation, it is arguably not entirely novel.

This leads to a more significant point about related work. The authors do not cite Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366], which is highly relevant in this context. This citation is also missing from the original BDMC manuscript, raising the question of whether the authors are aware of it. This is unfortunate, as there is a missed opportunity to draw connections between the two methods. Tempered Transitions (TT) performs the same forward "annealing" and reverse "heating" sweeps as BDMC, but with the goal of creating a "tempering" MCMC transition move to improve mixing. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep, and the final sample from the forward sweep is used as a proposal, which is accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights from both sweeps.

As far as I can tell, the acceptance ratio in TT is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds calculated in BDMC. Indeed, Section 3.3 of Neal's paper interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound the log partition function difference, noting that a TT sampler accepts with probability 1 when the two estimates converge.

The analogy with TT also raises questions about the choice of the annealing schedule (the βt values), a topic that receives little attention in the paper. It is well-known in the TT literature that the step sizes Δβ = β{t+1} - βt are critical: steps that are too large result in a poor acceptance ratio (and, by analogy, a large BDMC gap), while steps that are too small are computationally wasteful. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this trade-off. For instance, comparisons between MCMC operators are likely unreliable if the gap between the log-bounds is large (e.g., > 1 nat), in which case the schedule should be refined.

This also relates to the "Auto" in "AutoBDMC," which is never clearly defined. It seems there is an opportunity to develop an adaptive schedule that iteratively refines Δβ until the bounds are sufficiently tight for reliable inference. In this respect, the experiments could be more thorough; a systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with the step size would be valuable.

### Conclusion

Despite these criticisms, I believe the paper has merit. The core idea is simple, useful, and has the potential for significant impact. If the authors can convincingly address the issues of clarity and the omission of related work in their response and a revised manuscript, I would be inclined to argue for acceptance.

### Detailed Comments

**Clarity:**
*   The definition of `v` as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise. It presumably refers to {z_{1:T-1}, θ_{1:T-1}}, but it is not unambiguous since the algorithm contains two AIS sweeps.
*   The paper uses inconsistent labels for the AIS sweeps: "rev" and "fwd" in some places, and "for" and "back" in others. It is unclear if this distinction is significant or accidental.
*   Equations (5-7) and (9-11) take an expectation over an estimator p̂(y) which is never defined. This makes it difficult to follow the derivation. Do the authors mean to write E[log R̂_rev] and E[log R̂_fwd]?
*   Equation (6) appears to be incorrect as written, as the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical. Based on Equations (4) and (10), the second term should likely be something like KL[ p(z, θ | y) || q_back(v | z, θ, y) ].
*   The term "AutoBDMC" is never unambiguously defined. Does it refer to (a) the protocol of calculating the bound difference to assess convergence, and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme from Section 3.2?

**Experiments:**
*   It is not clear how the annealing schedule βt is chosen in the experiments. Looking at the code in the appendix, I see `var step = 1/options.steps`. I assume this means βt follows a linear schedule over T steps, but this should be stated explicitly in the main text.
*   In Figures 2 and 3, does "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" refer to the number of steps T used for the BDMC annealing schedule? This should be clarified.
*   The figure captions use undefined acronyms, such as RAIS (presumably Reverse AIS) and the variants (u)c(R)AIS (presumably (un)collapsed variants).
*   The font size for axes and tick labels in the figures should be increased for readability.

**Typos:**
*   In the expression for D_JS, two closing parentheses are missing in the D_KL terms.
*   In the NUTS citation, "Homan" should be "Hoffman".
