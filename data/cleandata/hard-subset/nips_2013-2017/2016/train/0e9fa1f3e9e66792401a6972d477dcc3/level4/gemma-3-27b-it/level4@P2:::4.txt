This paper introduces a method for assessing MCMC convergence based on Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors leverage BDMC’s ability to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood (using annealed importance sampling) and demonstrate that the gap between these bounds provides a bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the approximate samples generated by MCMC. This offers a scalar metric for evaluating MCMC performance. Recognizing the limitation of applying this method directly to real-world data (requiring samples from the true posterior), the authors present a protocol for using synthetic data generated from fitted hyperparameters to approximate real-world inference quality, and demonstrate practical applications in model selection and debugging. 

Overall, this is a well-written and motivated paper. The proposed method appears valuable, and the experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence of its utility. Section 3, particularly the transition into 3.1, proved more challenging to follow than the remainder of the paper. The introduction of variables and equations felt abrupt and lacked sufficient motivation. If the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1 is a novel contribution—and I believe it is—this should be explicitly highlighted, as the paper’s novelty appears to center on this result. The term “AutoBDMC” is used several times, yet the paper never explicitly defines it. Based on my understanding, “AutoBDMC” refers to the approach of using AIS and reverse AIS (rAIS) to analyze MCMC convergence. A clear statement defining “AutoBDMC” would improve clarity. By the time Section 3.2 references challenges for “AutoBDMC,” a formal definition should be readily available.

Several minor points could be addressed: on line 149, *p(theta, z | y)* should likely be *p(theta, z, y)*. There are also notational inconsistencies; the paper occasionally switches between using *y* and *D* to represent data/observations, which caused some confusion. The WebPPL code in the supplemental material should include information specifying compatible WebPPL versions and instructions for integration into the WebPPL distribution. Ideally, releasing this as a readily installable WebPPL package via npm would be highly beneficial. A similar release for the Stan code would also be appreciated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in rigorous mathematical analysis, providing quantifiable bounds on inference accuracy.
*   **Practical Utility:** The proposed approach offers a valuable tool for diagnosing MCMC performance, guiding model selection, and debugging probabilistic programming systems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method and its applicability to real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Section 3:** The mathematical derivations in Section 3 are dense and lack sufficient motivation. The transition to 3.1 is particularly jarring.
*   **Undefined Terminology:** The term "AutoBDMC" is used without a clear definition.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** Switching between *y* and *D* for representing data/observations introduces minor confusion.
*   **Limited Accessibility of Code:** The supplemental WebPPL code lacks clear instructions for installation and usage.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for evaluating MCMC convergence.
*   The method has demonstrated practical utility in diagnosing inference quality and guiding model selection.
*   The empirical validation is compelling and supports the claims made by the authors.
*   The work addresses an important challenge in Bayesian inference – quantifying the reliability of approximate posterior samples.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of Section 3 could be improved with more detailed explanations and better motivation.
*   The undefined terminology (“AutoBDMC”) detracts from the overall clarity of the paper.
*   The lack of accessible code limits the reproducibility and broader adoption of the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. While there are some areas for improvement in terms of clarity and code accessibility, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and practical utility of the proposed method outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the points raised regarding clarity and code dissemination.
