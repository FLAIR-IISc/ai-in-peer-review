- Proposes BREAD: extending BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior.  
- Introduces a protocol to evaluate inference on real data by simulating datasets from hyperparameters estimated on real data.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates practical uses, including model analysis and bug detection.  
- Main technical claim: the gap between stochastic upper and lower marginal likelihood bounds from BDMC upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Contribution is useful and practical but appears as a straightforward extension of BDMC rather than a fundamentally novel idea.  
- Strength: elegant, simple to implement and highly applicable in probabilistic programming frameworks.  
- Strength: demonstrated practical utility, including finding a bug in WebPPL, showing value as an automatic diagnostic.  
- Weakness: fails to cite and discuss Radford Neal’s 1996 Tempered Transitions (TT), which has close conceptual similarities.  
- Technical presentation is rushed and unclear, with ambiguous definitions (e.g., vector v) and inconsistent notation (rev/fwd vs for/back).  
- Key equations (5–7, 9–11) and estimator definitions are unclear; Equation (6) reportedly contains a fundamental error.  
- Fails to define "AutoBDMC" or explain temperature schedule selection; appendix shows β_t = 1/t but this should be in the main text.  
- Experiments lack systematic study of how the bound gap varies with step size, limiting practical guidance.  
- Related-work discussion is weak; the paper should better contextualize against TT and broader MCMC diagnostics literature.  
- Overall: promising and potentially significant for probabilistic programming, but weakened by missing citations, poor exposition, and insufficient experimental analysis.
