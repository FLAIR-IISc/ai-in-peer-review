- Paper proposes BREAD: uses BDMC to get stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood; the gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence and measures sampler quality.
- Core novelty: using BDMC bound gap as a theoretically grounded diagnostic for MCMC/AIS/SMC sampler convergence and efficiency comparison.
- Practical challenge: BDMC upper bound requires an exact posterior sample, unavailable for real data.
- Heuristic solution: run diagnostics on synthetic data generated from a point estimate of hyperparameters so a high-quality approximate sample is obtainable.
- Validation: experiments on models implemented in WebPPL and Stan show the heuristics can work in some cases.
- Main concern—circularity: estimating hyperparameters from a potentially unconverged MCMC run (hat{η}_real) assumes the very convergence being tested.
- Main concern—surrogate problem: diagnostics on synthetic-data posteriors may not reflect convergence behavior on the true posterior, especially under misspecification or multimodality.
- Main concern—initialization reliance: reverse-chain upper bound depends on optimistic assumption that short MCMC from the estimate yields a near-exact posterior draw.
- Experiments provided are limited and do not convincingly demonstrate robustness in complex or challenging inference scenarios.
- Suggested improvement: include direct comparisons to standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, ESS) and show cases where BREAD outperforms them.
