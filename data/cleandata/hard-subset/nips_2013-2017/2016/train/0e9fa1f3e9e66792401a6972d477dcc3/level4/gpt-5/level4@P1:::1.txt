Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS and SMC). By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior), the method produces stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood whose gap, in expectation, upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS. The authors integrate this procedure into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on toy and realistic models, propose a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to approximate the reverse initialization on real data, and demonstrate how the method can guide modeling/algorithmic choices and even uncover implementation bugs.

This work builds on BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation [Grosse et al., 2015] and AIS [Neal, 2001], and is related to SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006], convergence diagnostics [Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Brooks and Gelman, 1998], and measures of sample quality [Gorham and Mackey, 2015]. It is also closely related to contemporaneous work on subjective divergence in probabilistic programming [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016]. Two additional highly relevant validation approaches are Geweke’s “Getting it Right” test and the posterior quantile method, which also evaluate samplers on data generated from the model [Geweke, 2004; Cook, Gelman, and Rubin, 2006].

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing: a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality that ties directly to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical: integrated into two PPLs (WebPPL and Stan), with experiments on several models; the method yields actionable guidance (e.g., choosing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations).
- Useful for debugging: the forward/reverse sandwiching provides a clear invariant; a violation led to discovery of a real bug in WebPPL.
- Thoughtful protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters, with empirical checks suggesting consistency between real and simulated data.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Section 3.1 is hard to follow. The notation introduces q(·) before it is defined. From context, it appears to be the marginal distribution over xT induced by the AIS forward chain after T steps; this should be stated explicitly (e.g., define qfwd(xT) and qrev(v|xT) up front).
- The paper would benefit from an explicit, end-to-end procedure describing how to apply the method (data simulation, forward AIS, reverse AIS initialization, computing the bound, and interpreting the gap), without requiring the reader to piece it together from the main text and appendix.
- Notational clutter: in a general presentation, splitting unobserved variables into z and θ adds cognitive load, since they always appear together. Consider using a single latent variable symbol throughout unless a distinction is essential.
- Minor: Line 165 has a missing closing parenthesis.

Suggestions for revision
- In Section 3.1, define all distributions and conditionals (qfwd(v, xT), qfwd(xT), qfwd(v|xT), qrev(v|xT)) before the derivation, and add a brief intuitive explanation of why marginalization yields the inequalities used to bound the bias.
- Provide a succinct algorithm box or numbered protocol for BREAD on simulated data and for the fitted-hyperparameter workflow on real data, including what guarantees hold in each case.
- Consolidate notation for latent variables and ensure consistency across main text and appendices.
- Fix the noted typo.

Relation to additional literature
- The proposed strategy of validating inference by simulating from the model aligns with [Geweke, 2004] and [Cook, Gelman, and Rubin, 2006], which the authors should cite and discuss. These works test posterior simulators via joint distribution and posterior quantile checks, respectively, whereas the present paper provides an expected upper bound on a divergence between sampler and target. The methods are complementary.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a clear derivation connecting the BDMC bounds to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic; the authors appropriately validate it empirically.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but Section 3.1’s derivation and the lack of a self-contained application recipe hinder readability. Addressing the noted notation and exposition issues would significantly improve clarity.

Originality
- Extends BDMC from marginal likelihood sandwiching to bounding sample quality via Jeffreys divergence and integrates this into PPLs. This is a novel and timely contribution, closely related but not identical to concurrent subjective-divergence work.

Significance
- Potentially high impact for practitioners using black-box inference, providing a concrete diagnostic that can inform modeling and algorithmic choices and catch implementation errors.

Pros
- Rigorous, interpretable metric (expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence).
- Practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan; useful empirical case studies.
- Clear benefits for both algorithm selection (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and debugging.

Cons
- Exposition gaps in Section 3.1 and missing end-to-end guidance.
- Notational choices that reduce readability.
- Minor typographical error.

References mentioned

- Cook, S. R., Gelman, A., & Rubin, D. B. (2006). Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles. JCGS, 15(3), 675–692.
