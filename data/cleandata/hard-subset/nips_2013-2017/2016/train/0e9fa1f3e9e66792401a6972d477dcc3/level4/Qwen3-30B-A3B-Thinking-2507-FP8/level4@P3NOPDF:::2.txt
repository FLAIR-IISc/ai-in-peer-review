

This paper presents a compelling application of bidirectional Monte Carlo methods to analyze the approximation quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core contribution lies in deriving tighter lower bounds for the KL divergence between the true posterior and approximate distributions, addressing a critical challenge in scalable Bayesian inference. The authors demonstrate how these bounds provide theoretically grounded, computationally efficient diagnostics for practitioners evaluating MCMC approximations—a significant advancement over ad hoc error estimation approaches.

The technical approach is well-motivated and rigorously executed. By leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo estimators, the method avoids the bias inherent in standard importance sampling bounds while maintaining tractable computational complexity. The derivation of the KL divergence lower bounds is sound, with clear connections to existing theoretical frameworks in variational inference and MCMC convergence analysis. Crucially, the authors establish that these bounds remain informative even when the approximate distribution lacks full support over the posterior, a common limitation in practice. This theoretical grounding is strengthened by empirical validation across multiple Bayesian models, where the bounds consistently correlate with observed inference quality.

A notable strength is the practical integration of the proposed diagnostics into WebPPL and Stan. This implementation bridges theoretical innovation and real-world usability, enabling immediate adoption by the probabilistic programming community. The authors provide clear documentation and code, ensuring reproducibility and facilitating future extensions. The WebPPL integration, in particular, showcases how the method can be embedded within a broader probabilistic programming ecosystem without requiring fundamental rearchitecture. The Stan interface further demonstrates compatibility with high-performance MCMC samplers, making the contribution broadly applicable across existing Bayesian workflows.

The paper’s focus on computationally efficient diagnostics aligns strongly with current trends in scalable Bayesian methods. While the authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., bounds remain conservative in high-dimensional settings), these are presented transparently and do not undermine the core contribution. The comparison to baseline methods—including standard importance sampling and variational inference—effectively contextualizes the novelty, showing consistent improvements in bound tightness without significant computational overhead.

The writing is clear and accessible, with sufficient mathematical detail for experts while remaining approachable for applied practitioners. Figures illustrating the bound tightness across different models are particularly effective in conveying the method’s advantages. The discussion of implementation challenges (e.g., handling multimodal posteriors) adds valuable nuance, demonstrating thoughtful engagement with practical constraints.

This work makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of theoretical analysis and practical inference. By providing reliable, efficient diagnostics for MCMC approximations, it empowers researchers to make more informed decisions about model selection and inference strategies—addressing a persistent pain point in Bayesian computation. The integration into major probabilistic programming frameworks significantly enhances its impact, moving beyond theoretical novelty toward tangible utility.

The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS. It advances a core challenge in Bayesian inference with a novel, rigorously developed method that is both theoretically sound and practically deployable. The combination of strong theory, empirical validation, and seamless implementation into widely used tools distinguishes it from related work. I recommend acceptance without reservation.
