The authors propose a general method for evaluating MCMC transition operators, building upon the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique. BDMC employs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—analogous to those in Neal’s Tempered Transitions (TT) [Statistics and Computing, 1996]—but reinterprets the resulting probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper suggests leveraging the gap between these bounds as a performance metric for MCMC operators, presenting implementations within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, alongside a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

The core strength of this work lies in the simplicity and potential impact of using BDMC to assess MCMC convergence. Its straightforward implementation suggests broad applicability, particularly within probabilistic programming systems where its adoption could be highly beneficial. 

However, the novelty and depth of the technical contributions warrant further consideration. While the authors acknowledge a related arXiv submission on BDMC and wisely dedicate space to its description, the presentation of their own contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feels somewhat rushed, impacting clarity. This results in several points requiring clarification (detailed below). Beyond clarity, the technical contributions themselves are relatively modest. The main insight—that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence—is a valuable observation, but perhaps not entirely novel. 

A significant omission in the related work is Radford Neal’s seminal paper on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing, 1996]. This omission, also present in the BDMC paper itself, raises questions about the authors’ awareness of the connection. TT shares the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but aims to *tempt* the MCMC transition to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. TT uses the final sample from the reverse sweep to initialize the forward sweep, and the resulting acceptance ratio is, as far as I can tell, directly related to the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper interprets this acceptance ratio as bounding log Z<sub>T</sub> - log Z<sub>1</sub> via thermodynamic integration, accepting when the bounds converge. This analogy also prompts the question of why the authors do not delve deeper into the selection of β<sub>t</sub> values. TT demonstrates that excessively large steps in Δβ = β<sub>t+1</sub> - β<sub>t</sub> reduce acceptance rates, while very small steps are computationally wasteful. The BDMC bounds will also depend on Δβ, suggesting that a sufficiently small Δβ is necessary for reliable inference – if the bounds are not close, the step size should be reduced.  Furthermore, comparisons between MCMC operators may be unreliable if the gap between the bounds exceeds approximately 1 nat, again suggesting a smaller Δβ.  The “auto” functionality of AutoBDMC isn’t fully explained, but an iterative schedule for decreasing Δβ until reliable results are achieved could be a valuable extension.  The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with a systematic study exploring the relationship between the bounds and step size.

Despite these criticisms, the paper presents a potentially useful method. Acceptance hinges on the authors’ ability to address concerns regarding clarity and the discussion of related work in the camera-ready version.

**Clarity Issues:**

*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. The authors likely mean {z<sub>1:T-1</sub>, θ<sub>1:T-1</sub>}, but the two AIS sweeps introduce ambiguity. The inconsistent use of “rev”/“fwd” versus “for”/“back” is also confusing.
*   The estimator \hat{p}(y) used in equations (5-7) and (9-11) is not defined, hindering comprehension. Is it E[log \hat{R}<sub>rev</sub>] and E[log \hat{R}<sub>fwd</sub>]?
*   Equation (6) appears incorrect, as the distributions within the KL divergence are identical. It should likely be something like p(z, θ | y)q<sub>back</sub>(v | z, θ, y)?
*   AutoBDMC remains ambiguously defined. Does it encompass both calculating the bound difference for convergence assessment *and* the fixed hyperparameter scheme in section 3.2?

**Experiments:**

*   The selection of β<sub>t</sub> is unclear. The code in the appendix reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`, suggesting AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps *T* to transition from β<sub>T</sub> = 1 to β<sub>1</sub> = 1/*T*? This should be explicitly stated.
*   In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” – does this refer to the number of steps used *within* BDMC?
*   The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) and its variants (u)c(R)AIS are undefined in the caption.
*   Axes and tick labels are too small.

**Typos:**

*   The expression for D<sub>JS</sub> is missing two closing parentheses in the D<sub>KL</sub> terms.
*   In the NUTS citation, “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”
