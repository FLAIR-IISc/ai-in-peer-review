The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps – essentially the same as those used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, *Statistics and Computing*, 1996] – but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The presented technical contributions are implementations of BDMC for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, and a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference. 

This paper has both strengths and weaknesses. The main strength is that using BDMC to assess MCMC operator convergence is a beautifully simple and readily implementable idea, potentially making this work high impact. This is particularly true within the context of probabilistic programming systems, where I believe all such systems would benefit from implementing this method.

My main concerns relate to the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. While the authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC as existing work, they wisely devote considerable space to reiterating its description. Unfortunately, this means the technical contributions presented in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel somewhat rushed, and the writing quality suffers slightly in those sections. Consequently, some issues of clarity arise (see below). Aside from these clarity issues, the technical contributions are somewhat modest. Relative to existing work on BDMC, the primary novelty appears to be the realization that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence. This is a nice observation, though arguably not entirely novel. 

This leads to a point regarding related work. The authors curiously do not cite Radford Neal’s work on Tempered Transitions [Neal, *Statistics and Computing*, 1996], which is highly relevant. This citation is also missing from the BDMC manuscript itself, which raises the question of whether the authors are aware of it. If not, this is an unfortunate oversight and a missed opportunity to highlight connections between the two methods. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but aims to “temper” the MCMC transition move to improve mixing in multimodal distributions. In TT, the final sample from the reverse sweep initializes the forward sweep, and the final sample from the forward sweep serves as a proposal accepted or rejected according to the ratio of importance weights accumulated in both sweeps. The acceptance ratio in TT, as far as I can tell, is precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Section 3.3 of Neal’s 1996 paper interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log *Z<sub>T</sub>* - log *Z<sub>1</sub>* from above and below, noting that a TT sampler accepts with probability 1 when these estimates converge.

The analogy with TT also leads me to wonder why the authors don’t pay more attention to the choice of β<sub>t</sub> values. In TT, it's well known that excessively large steps in Δβ = β<sub>t+1</sub> - β<sub>t</sub> lead to a poor acceptance ratio, while excessively small steps waste computation. The ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC will also depend on Δβ, providing clear intuition. If the two bounds are not sufficiently close, BDMC should be rerun with a smaller Δβ. Similarly, I suspect comparisons between MCMC transition operators are unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds ~1 nat; in such cases, Δβ should be reduced. 

It hasn't become clear to me what the "Auto" part of AutoBDMC entails, but there's an opportunity to develop a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until reliable inference results are obtained. In this respect, the experiments could be more thorough. A systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with step size would be valuable.

Despite these criticisms, this is not a bad paper. As noted, the method proposed by the authors is potentially quite useful. Depending on whether the authors can convincingly address the clarity issues and discussion of related work in the camera-ready version, I could support acceptance.

**Clarity:**

*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. The authors likely mean {z<sub>1:T-1</sub>, θ<sub>1:T-1</sub>}, but this isn’t immediately unambiguous given the two AIS sweeps. This is compounded by inconsistent labels – “rev” and “fwd” versus “for” and “back” – which may or may not be significant.
*   The authors do not define the estimator \hat{p}(y) used in equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to follow the derivations. Do the authors mean to write E[log \hat{R}<sub>rev</sub>] and E[log \hat{R}<sub>fwd</sub>]?
*   Equation (6), as written, is nonsensical (the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical). Based on equations (4) and (10), I believe the right-hand side should read something like p(z, θ | y) q<sub>back</sub>(v | z, θ, y)?
*   The authors never unambiguously define AutoBDMC. Does this mean (a) calculating the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence *and* (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme described in section 3.2?

**Experiments:**

*   The method for choosing β<sub>t</sub> isn't explicitly stated. Examining the code in the appendix reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`. Therefore, AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps *T* to go from β<sub>T</sub> = 1 to β<sub>1</sub> = 1 / *T*? This should be stated explicitly. In figures 2 and 3, does “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” indicate that BDMC is performed with this number of steps?
*   The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) is not defined in the caption, nor are the variants (u)c(R)AIS, which I assume refer to (un)collapsed variants.
*   Increasing the size of the axes and tick labels would improve readability.

**Typos:**

*   Two closing parentheses are missing in the *D<sub>KL</sub>* terms in the expression for *D<sub>JS</sub>*.
*   In the NUTS citation: Homan -> Hoffman.
