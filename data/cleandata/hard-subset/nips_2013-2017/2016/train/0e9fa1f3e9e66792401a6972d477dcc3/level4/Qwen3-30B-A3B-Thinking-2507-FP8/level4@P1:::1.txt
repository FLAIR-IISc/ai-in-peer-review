

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously evaluating the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The core contribution extends BDMC [GGA15] to upper-bound the expected Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate this on simulated data by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both forward and backward, then integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan to evaluate inference quality across multiple models. Crucially, they propose a protocol to transfer insights from simulated data to real-world applications by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating from these, and comparing inference behavior. The work also reveals a WebPPL bug through BREAD’s diagnostic capability, highlighting its practical utility for debugging probabilistic programming systems.

The paper makes four key contributions: (1) a theoretical derivation showing BDMC upper-bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence; (2) validation on toy distributions where exact Jeffreys divergence is computable; (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real data; and (4) integration into two major probabilistic programming languages with empirical validation. This directly addresses a critical gap in MCMC validation—current diagnostics are heuristic and cannot confirm convergence, whereas BREAD provides rigorous, quantitative bounds under simulated conditions. The approach is highly relevant to NIPS themes of scalable probabilistic inference and reliable algorithmic evaluation, and it complements recent work on MCMC diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and automated inference configuration [RR01].

**Strengths:**  
The core idea is compelling and addresses a genuine pain point in probabilistic modeling: the lack of reliable, quantitative metrics for MCMC convergence. BREAD’s theoretical grounding in BDMC [GGA15] is sound, and the experimental validation across models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.) demonstrates practical utility. The protocol for real-world application (Section 3.2) is well-motivated and validated through empirical consistency checks (Section 5.1.1). The discovery of a WebPPL bug via BREAD further underscores its value for debugging inference engines, a significant practical contribution. The implementation in WebPPL and Stan, alongside the open-sourcing of code, enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary weakness lies in the clarity of the theoretical derivation (Section 3.1). The notation is dense and ambiguous in places: for instance, $q(\cdot)$ is used before its definition (it denotes the marginal distribution of the AIS chain after $T$ steps), forcing readers to consult the supplement for clarity. The derivation’s technical density—particularly the manipulation of $q_{\text{rev}}$ and $q_{\text{fwd}}$—would benefit from a more explicit statement of the key steps. A concise, step-by-step procedure for applying BREAD (e.g., "Step 1: Simulate data from fitted hyperparameters; Step 2: Run forward AIS; Step 3: Initialize reverse AIS with approximate posterior sample...") would greatly improve accessibility. Additionally, the paper uses both $z$ and $\theta$ for latent variables without justification; since these are indistinguishable in the general formulation, this notation choice adds unnecessary complexity (e.g., Line 165 has a missing closing parenthesis).

**Recommendation:**  
This work makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference by providing a rigorous, implementable method for diagnosing MCMC reliability. While the theoretical exposition requires clarification, the method’s novelty, practical implementation, and demonstrated utility (including bug discovery) strongly support acceptance. The weaknesses are resolvable with minor revisions to the main text, and the paper’s technical soundness is well-supported by experiments. 

**Pro Acceptance:**  
- Novel, rigorous approach to MCMC validation with theoretical guarantees.  
- Directly addresses a critical gap in inference reliability assessment.  
- Validated across multiple models and probabilistic programming languages.  
- Practical utility demonstrated through debugging and model selection insights.  

**Con Acceptance:**  
- Theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is technically dense and lacks clear notation.  
- Minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., $z$ vs. $\theta$) reduce clarity.  
- The need to consult the supplement for key definitions detracts from readability.  

This paper advances the state of the art in reliable probabilistic inference, aligning perfectly with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful contributions. It provides a valuable tool for both practitioners and researchers, and its integration into mainstream probabilistic programming systems ensures immediate applicability. I recommend acceptance.
