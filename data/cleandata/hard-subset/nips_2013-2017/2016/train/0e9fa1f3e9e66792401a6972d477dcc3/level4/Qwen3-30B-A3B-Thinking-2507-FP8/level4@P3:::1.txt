

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously measure the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior distribution. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC inference algorithms, particularly those that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The key insight is that BDMC can produce an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence when applied to simulated data with access to exact posterior samples. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility for guiding model representation choices and debugging inference implementations.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded method to bound the Jeffreys divergence using BDMC, extending previous work on marginal likelihood estimation with BDMC [GGA15]. Second, it develops a practical protocol for applying this method to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches. Third, it demonstrates the utility of BREAD through concrete applications in probabilistic programming languages, revealing insights about model representation choices and uncovering a bug in WebPPL.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The method is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical justification showing that BDMC yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The experimental validation across multiple models and probabilistic programming frameworks demonstrates practical utility. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a genuine need in the field: the lack of reliable quantitative measures for MCMC convergence, which is particularly problematic for black-box inference methods. The connection to existing work on MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] is well-articulated, and the paper distinguishes itself by providing a rigorous bound rather than heuristics. The application to debugging WebPPL reveals the method's practical value for developers of probabilistic programming systems.

The paper also excels in significance. By providing a general, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, it addresses a critical barrier to wider adoption of probabilistic programming systems. The ability to quantitatively evaluate inference quality makes probabilistic programming more accessible to non-experts, advancing the field's goal of putting sophisticated probabilistic modeling within reach of a broader audience.

However, the paper has some weaknesses in clarity that hinder understanding of the core method. The derivation in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow, with the notation q(·) used before definition and the relationship between the forward and reverse AIS chains not clearly explained. The authors should explicitly define all notation before using it. The paper also unnecessarily splits unobserved variables into z and θ when they serve the same purpose and appear together in the model. This notational complexity makes the technical contribution harder to grasp than necessary. Additionally, there's a missing closing parenthesis on line 165.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed, step-by-step explanation of how to apply BREAD in practice, rather than requiring readers to piece together the method from scattered details. While the supplement clarifies some aspects, the main text should stand on its own. The connection to related work [CTM16] is mentioned but could be more thorough, particularly regarding the differences between the subjective divergence and Jeffreys divergence.

Regarding the four key criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the experimental validation is thorough, though the paper could better address the limitations of the simulated data approach.
- Clarity: The paper has significant room for improvement in exposition, as noted above.
- Originality: While the BDMC framework [GGA15] is not new, the application to bounding the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC evaluation is novel, and the protocol for real-world data application represents a meaningful extension.
- Significance: The work addresses an important practical problem with clear potential to improve probabilistic programming systems and make them more reliable for practitioners.

The paper is well-positioned to make a meaningful contribution to the field. The main weakness is the unclear exposition of the core technical contribution, which makes it difficult to assess the method's practical implementation. With clearer presentation of the theoretical derivation and a more straightforward description of how to apply BREAD, this paper would be significantly stronger.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper presents a valuable method for a significant problem in probabilistic inference. The technical contribution is sound, the application to probabilistic programming is compelling, and the method has clear potential impact on the field. The clarity issues are fixable with minor revisions to the main text, and the paper's strengths substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The work advances the state of the art by providing a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for evaluating MCMC inference quality, addressing a critical need in probabilistic programming.
