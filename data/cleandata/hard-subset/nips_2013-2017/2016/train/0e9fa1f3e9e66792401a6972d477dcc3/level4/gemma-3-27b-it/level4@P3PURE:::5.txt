The authors address the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes, proposing to leverage the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The core idea is to select, among available MCMC schemes, the one that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. A key challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to generate a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose heuristics to circumvent this limitation, specifically within the context of Bayesian posterior sampling. Their solution involves performing the diagnostic on the posterior generated from synthetic data, rather than the actual data, capitalizing on the relative ease of obtaining good approximate samples for such posteriors. The methodology is then tested on several examples.

The use of stochastic lower bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is an interesting concept. While the methodology appears somewhat specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and relies on diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation suffers from several drawbacks that, in my opinion, diminish the appeal of the proposed diagnostic methodology. These drawbacks stem from the heuristic approach used to address BMC’s requirement for an exact posterior sample.

I identify three key concerns. First, the authors assume prior knowledge of a hyperparameter value, η<sub>real</sub>, representative of the entire posterior – typically obtained from a previously run MCMC. This assumes both that the algorithm used to determine η<sub>real</sub> had already converged (which is the very problem being investigated) and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both are strong and generally unjustified assumptions. Second, they assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated using η<sub>real</sub> exhibits similar convergence properties to the original posterior. This is another unsupported assumption, particularly problematic in contexts involving model misspecification, and represents a potential “change of problem” where diagnostics are performed on a different posterior than the one of interest. Third, the authors optimistically posit that starting from η<sub>real</sub> allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly generate an almost exact sample from the posterior – a possibility, but one that is not guaranteed.

Therefore, the proposed methodology appears to rely on several assumptions critically linked to the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm, the very properties it aims to assess. While Section 5.1 presents examples where these issues do not appear to impact the conclusions, these simple examples are insufficient to demonstrate robustness across more complex scenarios.

The authors critique existing convergence diagnostic tools for potential failures in multimodal cases or when using insufficiently expressive summary statistics (lines 203-205). They should clarify how their method avoids these issues and provide supporting evidence. More generally, simulations comparing their method to commonly used diagnostics (e.g., those described in Section 4) would be valuable to illustrate scenarios where it offers more reliable assessments.
