This paper proposes a novel approach to measuring the reliability of MCMC inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The main idea is to extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), on several models and datasets, and integrate it into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs is an important one. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the proposed solution. The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the method's properties and limitations.

However, I have some concerns regarding the practicality and generality of the proposed methodology. The main challenge is that BDMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose some heuristics to overcome this issue, but these heuristics rely on strong assumptions that may not be justified in general.

Firstly, the authors assume that they can obtain a representative value of the hyperparameters, η_real, from previously run MCMC. This assumes that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged, which is basically the original problem of interest. Furthermore, this assumes that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value, which may not be the case.

Secondly, the authors assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has properties similar to the original posterior. This is an unjustified assumption that should be carefully justified, especially in contexts involving model mis-specifications.

Lastly, the authors adopt an optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. However, this may not be the case, and the proposed methodology seems to rely on various assumptions that are closely related to the actual thing being assessed.

The authors provide some simple examples in Section 5.1, but these examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues recognized by the authors will not be critical in other, maybe more complicated, examples.

To strengthen the paper, I would like to see more evidence that the proposed methodology can handle multimodal cases or cases where few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive. The authors should also provide simulations to compare their method with commonly used diagnostic methods to show scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, but the writing could be improved. The authors could provide more context and background information on the problem and the proposed solution. The paper could also benefit from more visualizations and examples to illustrate the method's properties and limitations.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized, but some sections could be improved. The authors could provide more details on the technical contributions of the paper and the proposed methodology. The paper could also benefit from a clearer conclusion and a more detailed discussion of the limitations and future work.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to measuring the reliability of MCMC inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. However, the authors could provide more context and background information on the problem and the proposed solution to demonstrate the novelty and significance of the contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of probabilistic inference. However, the authors could provide more evidence that the proposed methodology can handle real-world problems and provide more reliable diagnostics than commonly used methods.

Overall, I think the paper has some promising ideas, but it needs more work to demonstrate the practicality and generality of the proposed methodology. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper could make a more significant contribution to the field.
