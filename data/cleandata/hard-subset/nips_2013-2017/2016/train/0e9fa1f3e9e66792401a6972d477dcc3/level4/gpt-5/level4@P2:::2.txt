Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data. The difference between the reverse and forward log-AIS estimates yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior. The authors term the overall approach BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing).

They implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming systems, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and Gaussian mixtures). They also propose a pragmatic protocol to evaluate real-data performance: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from those fitted values, and check that AIS behavior matches between simulated and real data; for the reverse run, they approximate an exact posterior draw via short MCMC on the fitted hyperparameters. Finally, they use BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization and report that the method helped uncover a bug in WebPPL’s multivariate Gaussian sampling.

This work builds on AIS [Neal, 2001], SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006], and BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation [Grosse et al., 2015]. It complements classical convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman–Cowles–Carlin) and recent sample-quality metrics based on Stein’s method [Gorham & Mackey, 2015]. It is closely related to concurrent work on “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016], and the paper clarifies when their estimator upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: derives that the forward/reverse AIS gap upper-bounds (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior samples, with a clean connection to BDMC.
- Practical impact: integration into Stan and WebPPL, with experiments across diverse models. The tool provides actionable guidance (e.g., choosing model representations) and even aids debugging.
- Sensible bridge to real data: the fitted-hyperparameter simulation protocol is carefully validated by comparing forward AIS behavior on real versus simulated datasets; reverse AIS robustness to the number of MCMC burn-in steps for hyperparameters is empirically demonstrated.
- Empirical validation: toy problems show the bound can be close to the true divergence; realistic case studies illustrate usability.
- Scope: applies to AIS/SMC-style algorithms as well as MCMC design choices (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed representations), making it broadly useful in probabilistic programming.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Guarantees only in expectation and only for simulated data with access to an exact posterior draw; the real-data protocol is heuristic. It would help to quantify how the bound degrades when the reverse initialization is approximate, beyond the qualitative robustness study.
- Tightness of the bound is demonstrated on small examples; for the larger models, the paper does not provide quantitative estimates of how loose the bound might be. Reporting empirical gaps and confidence intervals, or variance-reduction strategies (e.g., multiple reverse chains), would strengthen the claims.
- Computational cost: AIS schedules, number of temperatures, and wall-clock budgets significantly affect both bounds and practicality. More systematic guidance or ablations on temperature schedules, kernel choices, and step sizes would be valuable.
- Comparison to existing diagnostics: while the goals differ, including parallel results for standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS) and Stein discrepancies on the same tasks would situate BREAD’s conclusions and show complementarities.
- Clarity and presentation: some notation and references are uneven (e.g., reference formatting like [CGHL+ p]); consolidating the main bound (Equations 6–12) earlier and giving a concise recipe for practitioners (how many chains, temperatures, and checks) would improve readability.

Evaluation against review criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical development is careful and correct to the best of my reading; the expectation bound follows directly from KL computations on forward and reverse chains. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though more quantitative analysis of tightness on larger models would help.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written. Minor issues include reference formatting, lengthy background that could be streamlined, and a need for clearer practitioner guidance on AIS schedules and computational budgets.
- Originality: Novel application of BDMC to bound posterior sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence, plus a practical real-data protocol and integration into major PPLs. The relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriately discussed.
- Significance: High practical significance for probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics. The method can guide modeling/inference choices and provide regression tests for implementations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Provides an expectation-based, theoretically grounded metric for sample quality when exact posterior draws are available (via simulation).
- Demonstrated utility across platforms and models; actionable insights on model representation; debugging value.
- Timely and relevant to the NIPS community focused on probabilistic programming and scalable inference.

Cons for acceptance:
- Guarantees limited to simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Limited analysis of bound tightness and computational trade-offs in complex, high-dimensional settings.
- Sparse comparison to alternative diagnostics on the same tasks.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a solid theoretical extension of BDMC, practical tooling in widely used systems, and compelling demonstrations that will be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working on probabilistic inference and probabilistic programming.
