

The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently introduced technique that performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—essentially identical to those in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996]—but reinterpret the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The core contribution is using the gap between these bounds as a performance metric for MCMC operators, with implementations for Stan and WebPPL and a protocol for posterior inference quality assessment.  

The paper's strongest aspect is the simplicity and implementability of the BDMC-based evaluation framework, which holds significant potential for high impact, particularly in probabilistic programming systems where it is explicitly targeted. All such systems would benefit from adopting this method. However, the novelty and technical depth of the contributions warrant concern. While the authors cite the BDMC arXiv submission and provide a thorough reiteration of its description, this reduces the space available for novel technical exposition in Sections 3.1–3.2, resulting in rushed presentation and diminished clarity.  

The primary technical novelty—observing that the gap between the stochastic bounds upper-bounds the Jensen-Shannon divergence—is insightful but not entirely novel. Crucially, the authors omit citation of Neal’s seminal TT work [Statistics and Computing, 1996], which is highly relevant. TT performs identical reverse "heating" and forward "annealing" sweeps, using the final reverse sample as the initial point for the forward sweep and accepting proposals based on the ratio of accumulated importance weights. This ratio corresponds precisely to the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper explicitly interprets this acceptance ratio via thermodynamic integration bounds on $\log Z_T - \log Z_1$, noting TT accepts with probability 1 when the bounds converge. This oversight misses an opportunity to clarify the TT-BDMC connection, especially since the gap between bounds directly informs $\Delta\beta$ choices: a large gap ($>1$ nat) indicates insufficiently small $\Delta\beta$, necessitating reruns with finer step sizes. The "auto" aspect of AutoBDMC remains undefined; it likely entails (a) using the bound gap to assess convergence and (b) employing the fixed $\beta_t$ schedule in Section 3.2, but this requires explicit definition. An opportunity exists to develop an iterative $\Delta\beta$-reduction schedule until reliable bounds are obtained, which the experiments do not explore systematically.  

**Clarity issues:**  
- Definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous; the authors likely mean $\{z_{1:T-1}, \theta_{1:T-1}\}$, but inconsistent labels ("rev"/"fwd" vs. "for"/"back") compound confusion.  
- Equations (5–7) and (9–11) lack definition of the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ for which expectations are taken; $\mathbb{E}[\log \hat{R}_{\text{rev}}]$ and $\mathbb{E}[\log \hat{R}_{\text{fwd}}]$ are likely intended.  
- Equation (6) is invalid (identical distributions in KL divergence); it should reference $p(z, \theta \mid y)$ and $q_{\text{back}}(v \mid z, \theta, y)$ per Equations (4) and (10).  
- AutoBDMC is never unambiguously defined.  

**Experimental concerns:**  
- $\beta_t$ scheduling is unclear; the appendix code uses `step = 1/options.steps`, implying $T$ steps from $\beta_T = 1$ to $\beta_1 = 1/T$, but this must be stated explicitly.  
- Figure captions omit definitions for RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS, and "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" (e.g., whether BDMC uses this step count).  
- Axes/tick labels in figures are too small.  

**Typos:**  
- Missing closing parentheses in $D_{\text{JS}}$ expressions.  
- "Homan" in NUTS citation should be "Hoffman."  

Despite these issues, the proposed method is potentially valuable. If the authors address the clarity, related work, and experimental deficiencies in the camera-ready version, I would support acceptance. The core idea is compelling, and its implementation simplicity suggests broad applicability to probabilistic programming systems.
