This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The approach extends the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. BDMC uses a pair of annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains—one running forward from the prior to the posterior and one running in reverse from the posterior to the prior—to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The key insight of this paper is that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. The authors implement this technique in two probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, and propose a protocol for using it to diagnose inference quality on real-world data.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Practical Core Idea:** The proposal to use the BDMC bound gap as a quantitative measure of MCMC sample quality is elegant and intuitive. It provides a principled metric grounded in information theory (an upper bound on divergence), which is a significant improvement over commonly used heuristic convergence diagnostics that can only identify specific modes of non-convergence.

2.  **High Potential Significance:** This work has the potential for high impact, particularly for the probabilistic programming community. As inference engines become more automated and complex, it is crucial for both practitioners and developers to have reliable tools to validate their results. BREAD offers a general-purpose and automated procedure to assess the quality of posterior samples, build confidence in "black-box" inference, and debug implementations, as convincingly demonstrated by the authors' discovery of a bug in WebPPL.

3.  **Convincing Application:** The experiments successfully showcase the utility of BREAD for guiding practical modeling decisions. The comparison between collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model in Stan and WebPPPL is a clear example of how this method can provide quantitative insights into the trade-offs between different MCMC algorithms and model parameterizations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's primary weakness is its omission of highly relevant prior work, namely Radford Neal's 1996 paper on Tempered Transitions (TT). The TT algorithm employs a similar mechanism of a forward "annealing" sweep and a reverse "heating" sweep between a target distribution and a simpler reference distribution. The acceptance probability for a TT proposal is based on the ratio of importance weights accumulated during these sweeps, which appears directly analogous to the ratio of the upper and lower bound estimators in BDMC. Neal’s paper also interprets this procedure in the context of thermodynamic integration providing bounds on the log partition function. Acknowledging and thoroughly discussing the relationship between TT, BDMC, and the proposed BREAD method is essential for correctly positioning the paper's contribution. While the insight that the gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence is a valuable contribution, the underlying framework of bidirectional annealing is less novel than the paper currently suggests.

2.  **Lack of Analysis on Key Hyperparameters:** The performance of AIS, and therefore BREAD, is critically dependent on the choice of the annealing schedule (i.e., the intermediate distributions, or temperatures `β_t`). An insufficient number of steps or a poorly chosen schedule can lead to a large variance in the importance weights and, consequently, a loose bound gap, regardless of the quality of the MCMC transition operators. The paper does not provide a systematic study of how the bound gap depends on the annealing schedule. This analysis would be valuable for providing practical guidance to users and for ensuring that comparisons between different MCMC operators are fair and robust.

3.  **Clarity of Technical Presentation:** The derivation of the main theoretical result in Section 3.1 is presented in a condensed manner that is difficult to follow. Ambiguous notation and apparent typos in the equations obscure the steps connecting the BDMC estimators to the KL and Jeffreys divergences, making it difficult for the reader to verify this central claim.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Tempered Transitions:** I strongly recommend that the authors incorporate a discussion of Neal's (1996) work on Tempered Transitions, clarifying its relationship to the BREAD methodology.

*   **Clarity of Derivations (Section 3.1):**
    *   The definition of `v` as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise. Does this refer to the sequence of states `{x_1, ..., x_{T-1}}` generated by the AIS chain? Please clarify this notation.
    *   The derivation in equations (6-11) is difficult to follow. For instance, the estimator whose expectation is being calculated (presumably `log R_hat`) is not explicitly defined in the text leading up to the equations. Furthermore, the KL divergence term in Equation (7) involves a product of four different distributions, and it is not immediately clear how this form arises from the expectation of the log estimator in Equation (6). A more detailed, step-by-step derivation would significantly improve the clarity and rigor of this section.
    *   The expression for the Jeffreys divergence `D_J` is missing two closing parentheses for the `DKL` terms.

*   **Experimental Details:**
    *   Could you please specify how the annealing schedule (the sequence of `β_t` values in Eq. 2) was determined in the experiments? Was a linear spacing used, and was it solely defined by the total number of AIS steps?
    *   In Figures 1 and 2, the x-axis is labeled "Number of AIS steps." Does this refer to `T`, the number of intermediate distributions in the annealing schedule?
    *   The caption for Figure 2 uses undefined acronyms such as `(u)c(R)AIS`. Please define these terms explicitly.
    *   For the camera-ready version, please consider increasing the font size of the text and labels in the figures, as they are currently difficult to read.

### Conclusion

Despite the weaknesses noted above, the core contribution of this paper is valuable and its proposed method could become a very useful tool for the machine learning community. The idea is simple, practical, and addresses a genuine need for rigorous evaluation of MCMC inference. If the authors can convincingly address the concerns regarding the discussion of prior work and improve the clarity of the technical derivations for the final version, I would be inclined to argue for acceptance.
