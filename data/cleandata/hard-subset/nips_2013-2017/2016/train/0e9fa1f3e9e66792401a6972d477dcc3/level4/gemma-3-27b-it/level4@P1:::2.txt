This paper leverages the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference methods. The authors analyze upper bounds on the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. The method is integrated into both WebPPL and Stan, and validated on several models and datasets. Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in Bayesian inference.

The authors build upon recent work, specifically BDMC [GGA15], to tackle the challenging problem of assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples obtained from MCMC methods. Reliable quantitative measures for MCMC convergence and accuracy remain elusive, particularly within “black box” inference systems where internal details are obscured.  By extending BDMC, the authors provide a principled way to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence – a symmetric measure of divergence – between the true posterior and the approximate samples. This is a significant contribution, as it moves beyond heuristic diagnostics towards more rigorous evaluation. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical analysis is sound and well-motivated. The refinement of the BDMC analysis to derive an estimator for the Jeffreys divergence is clearly explained.  Second, the integration of the method (dubbed BREAD) into popular probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan significantly enhances its practical impact. Third, the validation on several models and datasets provides empirical evidence for the method’s effectiveness. Finally, the insightful application of BREAD to analyze the effectiveness of different model representations in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates its utility for guiding algorithmic design.  The identification of a bug in WebPPL is a compelling example of how such rigorous evaluation tools can be invaluable. 

However, the paper also has some minor weaknesses. The assumption that exact posterior samples are available for simulated data is acknowledged, and a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is proposed, but the robustness of this protocol in complex scenarios could be further investigated. While the authors show good matching between bounds and true divergences on toy distributions, it would be valuable to see a more extensive exploration of the accuracy of the bounds in more realistic, high-dimensional settings.  Furthermore, the discussion of related work, while thorough, could be streamlined for conciseness.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian inference – evaluating the reliability of MCMC samples.
*   The proposed method (BREAD) is technically sound and builds upon recent, relevant work.
*   The implementation in WebPPL and Stan increases the practical accessibility of the method.
*   The experimental validation demonstrates the method’s effectiveness and utility.
*   The application to model representation analysis provides valuable insights.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on exact posterior samples for simulated data limits the direct applicability of the method.
*   The accuracy of the bounds in complex, high-dimensional settings requires further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method provides a valuable tool for evaluating the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms, and its integration into widely used probabilistic programming languages enhances its impact. I recommend acceptance.
