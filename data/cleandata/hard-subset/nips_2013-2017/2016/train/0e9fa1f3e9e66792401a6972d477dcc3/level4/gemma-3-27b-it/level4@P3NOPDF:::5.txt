The authors address the challenging problem of assessing the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) outputs and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. Their approach leverages upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood, as provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The core idea is to select the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. A significant hurdle is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to guarantee a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose heuristics to circumvent this requirement, focusing on applying the diagnostic to posteriors generated from synthetic data, exploiting the relative ease of obtaining good approximate samples in such scenarios. The methodology is then demonstrated through several examples. 

The concept of using stochastic lower bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. While the method appears tailored to Approximate Inference Samplers (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) schemes and relies solely on the marginal likelihood for diagnostics, it represents a valuable attempt to develop theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools. However, I find the implementation to be significantly weakened by several drawbacks stemming from the heuristic approach used to address BMC's dependence on exact samples.

Specifically, the proposed methodology introduces assumptions that are closely intertwined with the very problem it aims to solve – the convergence of the original MCMC algorithm. First, the authors assume prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value, denoted as η<sub>real</sub>, obtained, for example, from a previously run MCMC. This presupposes that the initial MCMC run has already converged, and that the posterior distribution can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both are strong and generally unjustified assumptions. 

Second, the authors assume that the posterior distribution induced by synthetic data generated using η<sub>real</sub> exhibits similar convergence properties to the original posterior. This assumption lacks justification, particularly in contexts prone to model misspecification, where synthetic data may not faithfully represent the complexities of the real data. It appears the authors address the difficulties inherent in Bidirectional Monte Carlo by, in effect, shifting the diagnostic problem to a different posterior distribution. 

Third, the methodology optimistically assumes that initializing the MCMC algorithm near η<sub>real</sub> will rapidly lead to an almost exact sample from the posterior. While plausible in some cases, this is not guaranteed and remains an assumption. Therefore, the proposed method’s validity critically depends on the convergence properties of the very MCMC algorithm it seeks to diagnose. 

While Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues appear inconsequential, these cases are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the robustness of the methodology in more complex scenarios. The limited scope of these examples fails to alleviate concerns that these issues could become critical in real-world applications.

The authors critique existing convergence diagnostics (lines 203-205) for potential failures in multimodal distributions or when relying on insufficient summary statistics. To strengthen their contribution, they should articulate why their method overcomes these limitations and provide empirical evidence supporting this claim. More generally, a comparative analysis with commonly used diagnostic tools (such as those described in Section 4) through simulations would be highly beneficial. Such comparisons should highlight scenarios where their method provides more reliable diagnostics, thereby demonstrating its advantages over existing approaches.
