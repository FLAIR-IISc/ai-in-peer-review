This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively assessing the quality of samples from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core challenge addressed is the notorious difficulty of diagnosing the convergence and measuring the accuracy of MCMC, a problem particularly acute for users of "black-box" probabilistic programming languages (PPLs).

The proposed method is an extension of the recently introduced Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. BDMC uses Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to construct stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood of a model. The key theoretical contribution of this paper is to show that the gap between these two bounds serves as an upper bound in expectation on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the distribution of approximate samples generated by AIS and the true posterior.

Because the method requires an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse AIS chain, its rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data where such samples can be generated. To bridge this gap for practical applications, the authors propose a protocol for real-world datasets: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these inferred hyperparameters; and third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset to assess inference quality. The authors implement their method in two popular PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate the framework and provide a case study on choosing between different model representations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly significant problem in machine learning and applied statistics. Providing a quantitative, automated measure of posterior approximation quality would be a major advance for both developers of inference algorithms and practitioners using PPLs. It moves beyond traditional diagnostics, which can often only identify non-convergence, to potentially provide a positive certificate of quality.

2.  **Originality and Quality:** While the work builds directly on BDMC, the insight to use the gap between the bounds to measure posterior divergence is novel and elegant. The derivation in Section 3.1 appears technically sound and provides a clear theoretical foundation for the method. This represents a clever and valuable extension of the original BDMC idea from a tool for estimating marginal likelihoods to one for evaluating posterior accuracy.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct a thoughtful set of experiments.
    *   They validate the proposed protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by showing that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on the actual data for several models (Section 5.1.1).
    *   They demonstrate the robustness of the approach to the necessary approximation of using an MCMC sample to initialize the reverse chain in the real-data protocol (Section 5.1.2).
    *   The application of BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is a compelling case study. It showcases how the method can provide concrete, quantitative guidance for difficult modeling and implementation decisions.
    *   The anecdote about discovering a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's utility as a rigorous debugging tool for complex inference systems.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and does an excellent job of explaining the background material (AIS, BDMC) before presenting its own contributions. The connection between the log partition function estimation and the KL divergence is explained logically.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Simulated Data for Rigorous Guarantees:** The primary limitation, which the authors are upfront about, is that the rigorous upper bound guarantee holds only for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a well-reasoned heuristic, but it is not a formal guarantee. The success of this protocol likely hinges on the assumption that the model is well-specified. If there is significant model misspecification, the inference challenges on the real dataset may not be faithfully reproduced in the simulated one.

2.  **Potential Looseness of the Bound:** The derivation of the bound on Jeffreys divergence relies on the data processing inequality for KL divergence (marginalizing out variables cannot increase KL). While correct, this step could introduce slack, potentially making the bound loose for high-dimensional problems. The paper demonstrates tightness on toy examples (Appendix B), but it is unclear how the tightness scales with the complexity and dimensionality of the model.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires running AIS, which can be computationally intensive, in both a forward and a reverse direction. A more explicit discussion of the computational overhead would be beneficial. For example, how does the cost of obtaining a reliable BREAD estimate compare to the cost of simply running a much longer MCMC chain to begin with? This trade-off is central to its practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in probabilistic inference.
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical contribution connecting BDMC to the measurement of posterior sample quality.
*   The work is supported by a strong and convincing set of experiments, including a practical protocol for real-world data and a compelling case study that demonstrates its utility.
*   The implementation in two major PPLs shows the approach is general and impactful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The rigorous guarantees of the method are confined to simulated data, and its application to real data relies on a heuristic protocol.
*   The tightness of the bound in high-dimensional, complex settings is not fully explored.
*   The computational expense of the method is a practical concern that could be discussed in more detail.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a significant and original contribution to a difficult and important problem. The proposed method, BREAD, is a valuable new tool for researchers and practitioners in probabilistic modeling. I recommend acceptance.
