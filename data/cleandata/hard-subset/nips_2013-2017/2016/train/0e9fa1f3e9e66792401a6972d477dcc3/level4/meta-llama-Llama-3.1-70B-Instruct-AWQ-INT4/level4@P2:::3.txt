This paper proposes a method for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC technique to measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples obtained from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), uses annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of evaluating MCMC inference and the limitations of existing methods. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, including the BDMC method and its application to probabilistic programming languages. The technical contributions of the paper are well-explained, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the BREAD method.

The paper has several strong points. The BREAD method is a novel and useful contribution to the field of probabilistic programming, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in several models. The paper also provides a clear and concise explanation of the BDMC method and its application to probabilistic programming languages.

However, there are some issues with clarity and presentation. The definition of $\vec{v}$ is not clear, and the use of inconsistent labels "rev" and "fwd" may cause confusion. The authors do not define what estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ they are taking the expectation of in equations (5-7) and (9-11), which makes it difficult to follow how these equations arise.

The experiments are well-designed, but there are some issues with presentation. The choice of $\beta_t$ is not clear, and the acronym RAIS is not defined in the caption. The size of the axes and tick labels could be increased for better readability.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the BREAD method and its application to probabilistic programming languages. However, there are some issues with clarity and presentation that need to be addressed.
