The authors address the challenging problem of assessing MCMC reliability and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. They propose leveraging the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The core idea is to select, among competing MCMC schemes, the one that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. A significant obstacle is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To circumvent this, the authors propose several heuristics. Their approach, within the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involves performing the diagnostic on a posterior generated using synthetic data, rather than the actual data. This is motivated by the relative ease of obtaining good approximate samples for these synthetic posteriors. The proposed methodology is then evaluated on a set of examples.

The concept of utilizing stochastic lower bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. While the methodology appears specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and focuses solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation suffers from several drawbacks that, in my opinion, diminish the appeal of the proposed diagnostic. These drawbacks stem from the heuristic strategies employed to address BMC’s requirement for an exact posterior sample.

I identify three key concerns. First, the authors assume prior knowledge of a hyperparameter value, η<sub>real</sub>, representative of the entire posterior distribution, often obtained from a previously run MCMC. This assumption is problematic on two fronts: it presumes the convergence of the algorithm used to find η<sub>real</sub> – essentially restating the original problem of interest – and it suggests the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both are strong and generally unjustified assumptions. Second, they assume the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η<sub>real</sub> exhibits similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior. This is another unsupported assumption, particularly critical in scenarios involving model misspecification, and requires careful justification. It appears the authors attempt to overcome the difficulties inherent in bidirectional Monte Carlo by, in effect, “changing the problem” and performing the diagnostic on a different posterior. Finally, they optimistically assume that initializing the MCMC algorithm with η<sub>real</sub> allows it to quickly generate an almost exact sample from the posterior. While this might be true in some cases, it is not guaranteed. 

Therefore, the proposed methodology relies on several assumptions intrinsically linked to the very thing it aims to assess: the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm. I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not appear to affect the conclusions. However, these limited examples are insufficient to confidently assert that these recognized issues will not be critical in more complex scenarios.

The authors critique existing convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failures in multimodal cases or when insufficient summary statistics are used (lines 203-205). They should clarify how their method overcomes these limitations and provide supporting evidence. More broadly, simulations comparing their method to commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in Section 4) would be beneficial, demonstrating scenarios where it delivers more reliable diagnostics.
