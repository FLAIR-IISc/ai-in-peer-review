

The authors address the challenge of assessing the reliability of MCMC inference outputs and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. They propose leveraging the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea involves selecting among available MCMC approaches the one that achieves the tightest BMC bounds within a given computational budget. A primary technical challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to produce valid stochastic upper bounds. To overcome this, the authors develop a heuristic approach for Bayesian posterior inference: performing diagnostic evaluations on posterior distributions generated from synthetic data rather than real data, as it is more feasible to obtain good approximate samples for synthetic posteriors. The proposed methodology is validated through several examples. The concept of utilizing BMC's stochastic bounds for MCMC convergence diagnosis is promising, representing a valuable effort toward theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic tools, even though the methodology appears restricted to AIS and SMC samplers and focuses primarily on marginal likelihood-based diagnostics.

However, the implementation contains several significant drawbacks that substantially diminish the appeal of the proposed diagnostic methodology. These limitations stem from the heuristic approach designed to circumvent BMC's requirement for exact posterior samples. Specifically:

1. The authors assume they can obtain a representative value of hyperparameters, η_real, from a previously run MCMC algorithm. This assumption is problematic as it requires the prior MCMC algorithm to have already converged (which is the original problem being addressed) and presumes the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point estimate—both strong and unjustified assumptions in general settings.

2. The approach assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real exhibits similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior. This assumption lacks justification, particularly in cases of model mis-specification, where such similarity cannot be guaranteed.

3. The authors adopt an optimistic perspective that starting from η_real enables the MCMC algorithm to rapidly produce near-exact posterior samples. While this might be true in specific cases, it is not guaranteed, and the proposed methodology critically depends on this assumption.

Although Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not appear to impact conclusions, these limited cases are insufficient to demonstrate that the identified assumptions will not prove critical in more complex scenarios. The authors critique existing convergence diagnostics for failing in multimodal settings or when summary statistics are inadequate (lines 203-205), yet they do not sufficiently explain why their method would overcome these limitations or provide evidence supporting this claim. More generally, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparative simulations against commonly used diagnostic methods (as referenced in Section 4), particularly to demonstrate scenarios where the proposed approach yields more reliable diagnostics than established alternatives.
