This paper leverages recent advances in bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference methods. The authors analyze lower bounds on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the true posterior and the approximate distribution produced by MCMC. The method has been integrated into both WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating practical applicability. Overall, this is a strong paper. The authors effectively apply a recent technique – bidirectional Monte Carlo – to analyze the challenging problem of MCMC-based posterior inference. This is a highly relevant and important area of research, and the presented idea is interesting and well-executed. I recommend accepting this paper.

The paper’s central contribution lies in providing a principled way to diagnose convergence issues in MCMC, moving beyond ad-hoc diagnostics. While existing methods often struggle to provide guarantees, this work offers theoretical lower bounds that can inform practitioners about the quality of their approximate posteriors. 

I found the theoretical analysis to be sound and the connection to practical implementations in WebPPL and Stan particularly compelling. The empirical results, while not exhaustive, provide encouraging evidence that the proposed method can detect discrepancies between the true posterior and the MCMC approximation. Specifically, the examples where the method identifies poor mixing and highlights issues with the approximate posterior are valuable.

However, I have a few minor suggestions for improvement. First, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with the bidirectional Monte Carlo evaluation. While the authors acknowledge that it is more expensive than a single MCMC run, a more concrete analysis of the scalability of the method, particularly for high-dimensional problems, would be helpful. 

Second, it would be interesting to explore the sensitivity of the lower bound to the choice of the importance sampling distribution used in the bidirectional Monte Carlo estimator. A discussion of best practices for selecting this distribution, or an analysis of the robustness of the results to different choices, would strengthen the paper.

Finally, while the paper clearly states the limitations of only providing lower bounds (i.e., failing to detect all cases of poor convergence), elaborating on potential avenues for obtaining tighter bounds or complementary diagnostics could provide further insight.

Despite these minor points, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The theoretical framework is well-developed, the implementation is practical, and the results are promising. The clarity of the writing is generally good, and the presentation of the method is accessible. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the NIPS community and has the potential to impact how researchers and practitioners approach MCMC-based posterior inference.
