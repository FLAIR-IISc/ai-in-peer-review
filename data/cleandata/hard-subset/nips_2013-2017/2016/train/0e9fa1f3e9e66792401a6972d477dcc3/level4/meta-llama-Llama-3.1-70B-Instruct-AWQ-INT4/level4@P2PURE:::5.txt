The authors address the problem of evaluating the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The idea is to select the MCMC scheme that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for a given computational effort. However, the main challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. To overcome this issue, the authors propose heuristics, including performing diagnostics on the posterior generated by synthetic data rather than actual data, leveraging the fact that it is easier to produce good approximate samples for such posteriors.

The proposed methodology is then tested on several examples. The concept of using stochastic lower bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing. Although the methodology appears to be specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and only provides diagnostics based on the marginal likelihood, it could still be valuable as it attempts to provide theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. However, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, make the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing. These drawbacks arise from the heuristic methodology attempting to overcome the need for an exact sample from the posterior in Bidirectional Monte Carlo.

I would like to highlight three specific drawbacks. Firstly, the authors assume prior knowledge of a representative value of the hyperparameters, η_real, which is obtained in advance (e.g., from previously run MCMC). This assumption is strong and unjustified in general, as it presupposes that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged (which is the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized by a single value. Secondly, they assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has similar properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) to the original posterior. This assumption is also unjustified and should be carefully justified, particularly in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It appears that the authors are trying to overcome the difficulty posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo by "changing the problem," i.e., performing diagnostics on a different posterior. Finally, they adopt an optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior, which may not always be the case.

The proposed methodology relies on various assumptions that are closely related to the actual thing being assessed, namely the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm. Although Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology, these examples are not convincing enough to claim that the issues will not be critical in more complicated examples. The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failure in multimodal cases or when few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive (lines 203-205). I believe the authors should explain why their method can perform better in such scenarios and provide evidence. Additionally, it would be helpful to have simulations comparing their method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in sections 4) to demonstrate scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.
