Summary
The paper proposes a generic method to evaluate MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC runs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—essentially the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353–366]—but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as a stochastic lower and upper bound on the marginal likelihood. The authors propose using the gap between these bounds as a general performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The main technical contributions are implementations of BDMC in Stan and WebPPL and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

Strengths
- Using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators is a beautifully simple and practical idea. It is easy to implement and potentially high impact, especially for probabilistic programming systems. I expect such systems would benefit from implementing this method.

Concerns about novelty and depth
- The novelty and depth of the technical contribution are modest. Much of the paper reiterates the BDMC method (from the arXiv submission), and as a result the new technical material in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 is rushed; the writing quality also slips there, creating clarity issues (see below).
- Relative to existing BDMC work, the main new insight appears to be that the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen–Shannon divergence. This is a nice observation, though arguably not entirely novel.

Relation to prior work
- It is surprising that the paper does not cite Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353–366], which is highly relevant. The BDMC manuscript also omits this citation. This omission misses an opportunity to highlight connections:
  - TT performs the same reverse and forward sweeps as BDMC, but uses them to propose a tempered MCMC transition to improve mixing in multimodal targets.
  - In TT, the final state of the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep; the final state of the forward sweep is proposed and accepted or rejected according to the ratio of the AIS weights accumulated in both sweeps.
  - As far as I can tell, the TT acceptance ratio is precisely the ratio between the BDMC stochastic lower and upper bounds.
  - Section 3.3 of Neal (1996) interprets this acceptance ratio via two thermodynamic-integration estimates that bound log Z_T − log Z_1 from above and below, noting that acceptance approaches 1 as the two estimates converge.

Annealing schedule and practical guidance
- The analogy with TT suggests the paper should devote more attention to the choice of β_t. In TT it is well known that overly large steps Δβ = β_{t+1} − β_t lead to poor acceptance, while overly small steps waste computation. The BDMC lower–upper gap will also depend on Δβ, providing clear guidance:
  - If the bounds are not close, rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ.
  - Comparisons between MCMC transition operators are likely unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds about 1 nat; in that case, reduce Δβ.
- It is not clear what the “auto” in AutoBDMC entails. There is an opportunity to automate the schedule by iteratively decreasing Δβ until the bounds are sufficiently close, ensuring reliable comparisons.
- The experiments would be stronger with a systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with step size.

Overall assessment
Despite the above concerns, this is not a bad paper. The proposed use of BDMC is potentially very useful. If the authors can convincingly address the clarity issues and the discussion of related work (particularly TT) in the rebuttal and the camera-ready, I could be inclined to argue for acceptance.

Clarity issues
- The definition of v⃗ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is too loose. I presume this means {z_{1:T−1}, θ_{1:T−1}}, but this is ambiguous because the algorithm runs two AIS sweeps. Ambiguity is compounded by inconsistent labels (“rev”/“fwd” vs “for”/“back”). Is this difference meaningful or accidental?
- In Equations (5–7) and (9–11), the estimator p̂(y) whose expectation is taken is not defined, making it hard to follow the derivations. Do you mean E[log R̂_rev] and E[log R̂_fwd]?
- As written, Equation (6) makes no sense: the distributions inside the KL are identical. Based on Equations (4) and (10), I believe the right-hand terms should be something like p(z, θ | y) q_back(v | z, θ, y).
- The paper never unambiguously defines “AutoBDMC.” I assume it means (a) computing the difference between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence, and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme of Section 3.2. Please clarify.

Experimental details and questions
- It is not clear how β_t is chosen. The code in the appendix uses var step = 1/options.steps. I therefore assume AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T, with β_T = 1 and β_1 = 1/T. If so, please state this clearly. In Figures 2 and 3, does “HMC/No-U-Turn steps” indicate that BDMC is performed with this number of steps?
- The acronym RAIS (presumably “reverse AIS”) is not defined in the captions, nor are the variants (u)c(R)AIS (which I assume are uncollapsed/collapsed variants). Please define them.
- Increase the size of axes and tick labels in the figures.

Typos and minor issues
- In the D_JS expression, two closing parentheses are missing in the D_KL terms.
- In the NUTS citation, “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”
