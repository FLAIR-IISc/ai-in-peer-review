Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on algorithms that can be expressed as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS and SMC). The key idea is to run AIS forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data. The gap between the forward stochastic lower bound and reverse stochastic upper bound on the log marginal likelihood yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by the inference algorithm. The authors call the resulting procedure BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing).

Beyond theoretical analysis, the paper integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and evaluates it on a suite of models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and mixtures of Gaussians). The authors further propose a practical protocol to assess inference on real data by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating from the fitted model, and comparing forward/reverse AIS behavior to the real-data forward AIS. They also use BREAD to inform modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and to detect a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL.

This work builds on AIS (Neal, 2001), thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski equality connections, and BDMC’s log-partition bounds. It is closely related to concurrent work on subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), with the key difference that here the reverse chain is initialized from an exact (or approximately exact) posterior sample on simulated data, yielding a bound on the Jeffreys divergence.

Strengths
- Clear and useful contribution: connecting BDMC bounds to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples. This provides a principled, scalar diagnostic of sample quality for AIS/SMC-style samplers.
- Practical impact: integration into two widely used systems (WebPPL, Stan); the method helped surface a real bug; and it offers guidance in choosing model representations.
- Thoughtful protocol for bridging simulated and real data via fitted hyperparameters and consistency checks.
- Empirical validation across several models; the qualitative agreement between simulated and real-data forward AIS behavior supports the proposed practical protocol.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity in Section 3: The presentation jumps into notation and distributions (qfwd, qrev, v) with little intuition. Please precede the derivations with a high-level roadmap: (i) AIS yields a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1; (ii) reverse AIS with an exact posterior draw yields a stochastic upper bound; (iii) the expected gap equals the sum of forward and reverse KL terms after marginalizing auxiliary variables; (iv) thus the expected gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the target.
- Make the new result explicit. State a theorem that the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log-estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence, with assumptions and a short proof sketch. Emphasize “in expectation” in the abstract and main text.
- Scope and guarantees: The method applies directly to AIS/SMC-like procedures and requires exact posterior samples on simulated data. The protocol for real data (using approximate hyperparameter posterior samples to initialize the reverse chain) is practical but weakens guarantees; this should be discussed more explicitly, including when the approximation could mislead (e.g., highly misspecified priors or concentrated posteriors far from fitted hyperparameters).
- Variance and reporting: AIS estimates are heavy-tailed; the paper wisely uses quartiles. It would help to clarify how many chains are needed to obtain stable bounds in practice and to discuss the behavior of the bound across repeats (beyond expectation).
- Notation and consistency: The paper switches between y and D for data; please standardize. Clearly define all symbols when first introduced, including v, qfwd(xT|v), and qrev(v|xT). Ensure geometric-averaging schedules and kernels are specified when reporting experiments.

Quality
- Theoretical claims appear sound and are consistent with AIS/BDMC analyses. The expected Jeffreys bound is a meaningful and interpretable quantity for assessing both under- and over-coverage.
- The empirical evaluation is solid across multiple models and platforms. The real-to-simulated consistency checks are convincing, though more quantitative comparison (e.g., distances between distributions of log weights) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Overall well written and organized, but Section 3 would benefit from a gentler introduction, a formal theorem, and a brief intuitive interpretation of the auxiliary variables and conditionals used in the proof.
- Unify notation for observations (y vs. D); verify symbols in Sec. 2.3 and 3.1. For example, please double-check the instance where p(θ,z|y) vs. p(θ,z,y) appears (line numbers in the draft suggest a possible typo).

Originality
- While grounded in BDMC, the paper’s connection to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence for approximate posterior samples is, to my knowledge, new and well motivated. The discussion of concurrent work on subjective divergence is appropriate.

Significance
- The contribution is useful for practitioners and developers of probabilistic programming systems. Providing a rigorous, interpretable diagnostic that can reveal implementation errors and guide modeling choices is valuable. The integration into WebPPL and Stan increases real-world impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practically relevant theoretical link between BDMC gaps and Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Careful integration and validation across two major systems; actionable insights for model representation choices.
- Practical protocol for real-data evaluation; demonstrated utility in debugging.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees rely on simulated data and exact posterior samples; the real-data protocol provides heuristic rather than formal guarantees.
- Section 3 exposition could be clearer; the main theoretical contribution is not highlighted as a formal statement.
- Applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-like algorithms (though insights may transfer).

Questions and requests for revision
- Please add a formal theorem stating that E[log R̂rev^{-1} − log R̂] ≥ DKL(q||p) + DKL(p||q), with assumptions and a concise proof or pointer.
- Explicitly discuss the “in expectation” nature of the bound and practical implications for single-run diagnostics.
- Clarify the real-data protocol’s limitations and when approximate initialization of the reverse chain could fail.
- Standardize notation (y vs. D). Also verify the suspected typo around p(θ,z|y) vs. p(θ,z,y).
- Reproducibility: include version information for WebPPL and Stan, and provide instructions or packaging (e.g., an npm-installable WebPPL package and an accessible Stan repository) for reproducing experiments.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and useful contribution that extends BDMC to provide interpretable diagnostics of posterior sample quality, with solid empirical validation and practical impact. I recommend acceptance after clarifying and highlighting the main theoretical result and improving the exposition in Section 3.
