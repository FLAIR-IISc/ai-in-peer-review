- Paper proposes using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to assess MCMC reliability and compare schemes.  
- Main challenge: BMC requires an exact target sample to yield a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors propose heuristics: run diagnostics on posterior from synthetic data where good approximate samples are easier to obtain.  
- Central assumption: a representative hyperparameter value η_real is known a priori from previous MCMC runs.  
- Concern: knowing η_real assumes the original MCMC already converged and that the posterior is well summarized by a single value — both strong, unjustified assumptions.  
- Concern: assuming the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior is unjustified, especially under model misspecification.  
- Method effectively "changes the problem" by diagnosing a different posterior, risking irrelevant conclusions for the original target.  
- Method assumes starting from η_real lets the MCMC quickly produce an almost exact sample — another optimistic, potentially unsafe assumption.  
- Authors’ simple examples (Section 5.1) show issues may not affect conclusions there, but are insufficient to generalize to complex settings.  
- Reviewer asks for explanation and evidence why the proposed method should outperform existing diagnostics in multimodal or complex-posteriors scenarios.  
- Suggests simulations comparing the method to standard diagnostics (those in Section 4) to demonstrate advantages and failure modes.  
- Paper is technically sound and clearly explained, but some technical details may be hard for non-experts — more background could help.  
- Originality: novel application of BMC to MCMC diagnostics despite BMC itself not being new.  
- Significance: addresses an important problem and has potential, but needs more empirical support and discussion of limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel methodology, clear exposition, tackles an important Bayesian inference problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: relies on strong/unjustified assumptions, lacks sufficient comparative evidence, and needs a more thorough discussion of limitations and pitfalls.
