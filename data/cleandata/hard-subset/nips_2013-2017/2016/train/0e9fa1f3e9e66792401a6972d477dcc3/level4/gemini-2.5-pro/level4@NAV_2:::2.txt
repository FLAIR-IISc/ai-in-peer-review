This paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of quantifying the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) posterior inference. The authors propose a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the recently developed bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains on simulated data, both in the forward direction (from prior to posterior) and in the reverse direction (from posterior to prior). The authors show that the gap between the resulting stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provides an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior. This work builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015), which introduced BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation, by repurposing the framework to assess sample quality. The authors integrate BREAD into two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and provide a protocol for its application to real-world data.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. As probabilistic programming languages and black-box inference methods become more widespread, the need for rigorous, automated tools to diagnose inference quality is paramount. This work provides a principled and quantitative measure of sample quality, which is a significant advance over common heuristics like visual inspection or the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The primary technical contribution—deriving an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence from the BDMC framework—is novel, elegant, and technically sound. The paper provides a clear derivation and validates the tightness of the bound on toy examples. The proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data (by fitting hyperparameters and then simulating) is a thoughtful and practical solution to a key limitation.
*   **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The authors not only validate their method on several models but also carefully test the key assumptions of their real-data protocol. The consistency checks between real and simulated data (Fig. 1) and the robustness analysis of the approximate posterior for hyperparameters are particularly strong.
*   **Demonstrated Utility:** The application of BREAD to compare collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model in both Stan and WebPPL is an excellent demonstration of the method's practical utility. It provides quantitative insights that can guide modeling decisions. Furthermore, the fact that the method helped uncover a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to its usefulness for developers of inference systems.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for improvement):**

*   **Computational Overhead:** The proposed method requires running many AIS chains in both forward and reverse directions, which can be computationally intensive, potentially much more so than the original inference task itself. A more detailed discussion of the computational cost and its scaling with model complexity or the number of annealing steps would be a valuable addition.
*   **Guarantees on Real Data:** The rigorous theoretical guarantees of BREAD are limited to the simulated data setting where an exact posterior sample is available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a pragmatic heuristic, but it lacks the same guarantees. While the paper is transparent about this limitation and provides empirical validation, it would be helpful to discuss potential failure modes of this heuristic, for instance, in cases of severe model misspecification.
*   **Handling of Multiple Chains:** Footnote 3 notes that the analysis applies to individual AIS chains, and the authors report quartiles of estimates rather than an average. Standard AIS practice involves averaging estimates from multiple runs to reduce variance. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether the theoretical analysis could be extended to provide a bound on the divergence for a mixture distribution obtained from multiple chains, or if averaging the bounds themselves is permissible.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the crucial area of MCMC diagnostics. The method is theoretically sound, practically useful, and thoroughly evaluated. The weaknesses are primarily limitations of the approach rather than flaws in the work itself, and the authors are transparent about them. The paper is well-written, clear, and should be of great interest to both practitioners and developers in the probabilistic machine learning community.
