This paper introduces a general approach to assessing MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC runs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) passes—operationally akin to the forward/backward sweeps used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Stat & Comp 1996]—and interprets the resulting accumulated weights as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The key proposal here is to use the gap between these two bounds as a generic metric of sampler performance. The concrete contributions are BDMC implementations for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming platforms, together with a protocol for evaluating the quality of posterior inference. The work has clear merits and some limitations. On the positive side, leveraging BDMC to gauge MCMC convergence is an elegant and straightforward idea with high potential impact, especially for probabilistic programming, which is the primary application domain considered; in my view, most such systems would benefit from adopting this method. My main reservations concern novelty and depth. Although the authors cite an arXiv preprint on BDMC and (sensibly) recap it at length, Sections 3.1 and 3.2—where the new material resides—feel compressed, and the writing there slips in clarity (details below). Setting clarity aside, the incremental technical advance relative to prior BDMC work seems modest. The principal new observation appears to be that the gap between the lower and upper bounds itself upper bounds the Jensen–Shannon divergence. This is a pleasing insight, though arguably not entirely new. Regarding related work, it is puzzling that Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353–366] is not cited, despite being closely connected. This omission also appears in the BDMC manuscript, raising the question of whether the authors are aware of the connection. That omission is unfortunate, as drawing an explicit link would strengthen the narrative. TT executes the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” pass as BDMC but uses the result as a tempering move to traverse multimodal targets. In TT, the outcome of the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep, and the terminal state of the forward sweep is proposed with an acceptance test based on the ratio of the two accumulated AIS weights. That acceptance probability is, to my eye, exactly the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds. Indeed, Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper interprets this ratio via two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log ZT − log Z1, noting that acceptance tends to 1 as these bounds meet. This analogy also highlights the importance of the temperature schedule {βt}. In TT it is well understood that large Δβ = βt+1 − βt harms acceptance, while very small Δβ wastes compute. The BDMC gap similarly depends on Δβ, providing immediate guidance: if the bounds are too far apart, re-run with a finer schedule (smaller Δβ). Likewise, I expect comparisons among MCMC transition kernels to be unreliable when the log-gap exceeds, say, >1 nat; in that regime, Δβ should be reduced. It remains unclear from the paper what is “auto” in AutoBDMC, but this invites an opportunity to adaptively refine Δβ until the gap indicates sufficiently reliable inference. In this spirit, the experiments could be strengthened by a systematic study of how the bounds vary with step size. Despite these critiques, I find the method useful. As noted, I could advocate for acceptance if the authors can convincingly address clarity and related-work issues in the rebuttal and camera-ready.

Clarity
- The definition of v as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. I presume the authors mean {z1:T−1, θ1:T−1}, but this is ambiguous given the two AIS sweeps. The inconsistency between “rev/fwd” versus “for/back” labels adds to the confusion—is this intentional or accidental?
- In Eqs. (5–7) and (9–11), the estimator whose expectation is taken is not clearly specified. Is the intent to write E[log R̂rev] and E[log R̂fwd]?
- As written, Eq. (6) is puzzling (the distributions inside the KL look identical). Based on Eqs. (4) and (10), I assume the intended terms resemble p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y)?
- The paper never clearly defines “AutoBDMC.” I infer it comprises (a) computing the gap between stochastic lower and upper bounds as a convergence diagnostic and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter approach of Section 3.2.

Experiments
- The choice of βt is not explained. The appendix code shows var step = 1/options.steps; I infer AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T to move from βT = 1 to β1 = 1/T. Please state this explicitly. In Figures 2 and 3, does “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” denote the number of BDMC steps?
- The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) is not defined in the caption, nor are variants like (u)c(R)AIS (which I take to mean (un)collapsed versions).
- Please enlarge axis and tick label fonts for readability.

Typos
- Two closing parentheses are missing in the DKL terms in the D_JS expression.
- NUTS citation: Homan → Hoffman.
