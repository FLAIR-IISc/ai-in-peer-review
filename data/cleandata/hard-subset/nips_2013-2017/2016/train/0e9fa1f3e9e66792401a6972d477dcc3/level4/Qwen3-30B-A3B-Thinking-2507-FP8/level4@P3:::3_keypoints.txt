- Proposes BREAD: uses gap between forward/reverse AIS BDMC bounds to upper bound Jeffreys divergence for MCMC-based posterior inference.  
- Shows in expectation the stochastic marginal likelihood bound gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and proposes a protocol using simulated data from fitted hyperparameters for real-world evaluation.  
- Builds on Grosse et al.'s BDMC (2015); key insight is recognizing bound-gap → Jeffreys divergence.  
- Related work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) uses a reference distribution rather than the true posterior; authors’ analysis is more theoretically grounded for simulated data.  
- Manuscript omits discussion of Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996), despite shared annealing structure — a significant missing related-work connection.  
- Technical approach is sound and supported by derivations (Section 3.1), but some math presentation is unclear or inconsistent.  
- Experimental validation is thorough, checking consistency between real and simulated data and robustness of hyperparameter fitting.  
- Practical utility is demonstrated by implementations in two major probabilistic programming systems.  
- Clarity issues: ambiguous definition of intermediate variable v given dual AIS sweeps and inconsistent forward/reverse labeling.  
- Clarity issues: the relation between bound gap and Jeffreys divergence should be introduced earlier and more clearly.  
- Clarity issues: unexplained acronyms (RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS) appear in figures and AutoBDMC protocol needs clearer explanation.  
- Original contribution (recognizing bound gap as Jeffreys divergence upper bound) is novel but under-emphasized in exposition.  
- Paper would benefit from explicitly connecting to Tempered Transitions to better contextualize novelty.  
- Significance: addresses a major need for automated inference evaluation in probabilistic programming and can guide model choices and reveal implementation bugs.  
- Strengths: simple, elegant idea; practical utility; dual implementations; useful protocol; demonstrated debugging utility.  
- Weaknesses: novelty not sufficiently distinguished from BDMC/Tempered Transitions; rushed math presentation; missing Tempered Transitions citation; ambiguous notation; limited exploration of automatic annealing schedules.  
- Pro acceptance: practical, implementable, theoretically sound method with clear real-world benefits and demonstrated utility.  
- Con acceptance: novelty may be overstated relative to prior work and technical exposition needs clarity improvements.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify novelty, fix mathematical presentation, and contextualize relative to Tempered Transitions.
