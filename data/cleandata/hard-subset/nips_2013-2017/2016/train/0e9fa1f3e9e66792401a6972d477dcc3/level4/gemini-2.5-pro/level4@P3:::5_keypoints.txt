- Paper proposes BREAD, extending BDMC with AIS to produce stochastic lower/upper bounds whose expected gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS approximate and true posterior.
- BREAD requires an exact sample to initialize reverse AIS; for real data authors propose estimating hyperparameters via approximate inference and using simulated data as a surrogate.
- Implemented in WebPPL and Stan; used to compare model representations and to uncover a bug, demonstrating practical utility.
- Strength: provides a principled, quantitative diagnostic that gives a rigorous expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for simulated-data settings.
- Strength: demonstrated usefulness for guiding MCMC design decisions (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed) and for debugging probabilistic programming systems.
- Strength: novel adaptation of BDMC with a clean theoretical derivation linking BDMC bound gap to Jeffreys divergence.
- Weakness: real-data protocol is circular — requires running MCMC to get hyperparameter estimates, assuming the initial MCMC has converged.
- Weakness: diagnosis is performed on a surrogate posterior from simulated data, so conclusions may not transfer if the real posterior differs (e.g., due to misspecification or multimodality).
- Weakness: initializing the reverse chain with approximate samples (few MCMC steps) breaks the theoretical guarantee even on synthetic data.
- Suggestion: include experiments on models with known multimodal posteriors to compare BREAD against standard diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin.
- Suggestion: discuss impact of model misspecification and whether discrepancies in forward AIS curves could serve as a misspecification diagnostic.
- Suggestion: explicitly state and discuss the strong assumptions of the real-data protocol as major limitations.
- Conclusion: theoretically elegant and practically useful for simple cases, but real-data applicability is weakened by strong, unverified assumptions and limited validation on complex models.
