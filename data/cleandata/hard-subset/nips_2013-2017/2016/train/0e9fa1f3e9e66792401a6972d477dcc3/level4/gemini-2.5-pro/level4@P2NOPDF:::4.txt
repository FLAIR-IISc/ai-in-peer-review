This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for assessing the convergence of MCMC samplers. The approach leverages Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, using annealed importance sampling (AIS) and its reverse. The authors demonstrate that the gap between these bounds also provides a bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of the MCMC samples. This yields a single, interpretable scalar quantity that can be used to diagnose convergence.

A key challenge is that this diagnostic can only be computed on synthetic data generated from the model's joint distribution. The authors address this by providing compelling experimental evidence that the method's performance on synthetic data is predictive of the sampler's behavior on real-world data. Furthermore, they demonstrate the utility of AutoBDMC for practical tasks such as model selection and debugging MCMC implementations.

Overall, the paper is well-motivated and clearly written. The proposed method is a valuable contribution for practitioners working with MCMC, and the experimental evaluation provides strong support for its effectiveness.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Framing of Contributions in Section 3:** The core theoretical contribution of the paper appears to be the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1. However, the clarity of this section could be significantly improved. The transition into Section 3.1 is abrupt, introducing new notation and equations without sufficient context or motivation for the reader. If this bound is a novel contribution, this should be stated explicitly, as it forms the theoretical foundation for the entire AutoBDMC method. Clarifying the novelty here would strengthen the paper's overall message.

2.  **Definition of AutoBDMC:** The paper would benefit from a precise and early definition of "AutoBDMC". While a reader can infer that it refers to the overall framework of using AIS and reverse AIS to diagnose MCMC convergence, an explicit definition is missing. The term is used in Section 3.2 ("…this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC…") before it has been formally introduced, which could confuse the reader. A clear, one-sentence definition in the introduction or at the beginning of Section 3 would resolve this ambiguity.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

*   **Notation:** There is an inconsistency in the notation for observed data, which alternates between `y` and `D`. Using a single, consistent symbol throughout would improve readability.
*   **Line 149:** In the expression for the joint distribution, should `p(theta, z | y)` be written as `p(theta, z, y)`?
*   **Supplementary Code:** The provided code is a welcome addition. Its utility could be enhanced by:
    *   Specifying which version(s) of WebPPL the code is compatible with.
    *   Providing clear instructions on how to integrate the code into a WebPPL distribution.
    *   Ideally, releasing the code as a WebPPL package that can be installed via npm would greatly facilitate its adoption. A similar packaged release for the Stan implementation would also be valuable to the community.
