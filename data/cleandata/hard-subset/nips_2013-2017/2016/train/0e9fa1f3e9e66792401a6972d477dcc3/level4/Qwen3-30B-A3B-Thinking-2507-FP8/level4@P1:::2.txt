

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for rigorously evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC-based inference algorithms. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they derive an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence. This approach provides a theoretically grounded, quantitative measure of inference quality that complements existing heuristics for convergence diagnosis.

The paper makes four significant contributions: (1) establishing that BDMC yields an estimator upper-bounding the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) demonstrating that this upper bound is often accurate on small examples where exact computation is possible; (3) proposing a practical protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world datasets by simulating data from hyperparameters inferred on real data; and (4) integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validating it across multiple probabilistic models.

The work addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic inference: the difficulty of reliably measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples, particularly in black-box inference systems where users cannot assess inference reliability. The authors provide a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which is especially valuable for probabilistic programming languages that aim to make sophisticated inference accessible to non-experts.

The paper is technically sound and well-justified. The theoretical analysis carefully extends the BDMC framework to bound the Jeffreys divergence, and the empirical validation demonstrates the method's effectiveness across multiple models and probabilistic programming systems. The authors thoughtfully address the limitations of their approach (notably the requirement for exact posterior samples, which they overcome through their simulation protocol) and provide a clear comparison with related work, particularly the independent contribution by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

Strengths of the paper include its clear problem statement, strong theoretical foundation, practical implementation, and the discovery of a bug in WebPPL through their method, demonstrating the approach's utility for debugging inference algorithms. The integration into both WebPPL and Stan showcases the method's broad applicability, and the experiments provide convincing evidence of its value for model representation analysis, particularly in comparing collapsed versus uncollapsed variants of matrix factorization models.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with sufficient detail to allow reproduction of the results. The connection to previous work is thorough, and the authors properly acknowledge related contributions while clearly distinguishing their method's unique aspects. The discussion of the method's limitations and the proposed protocol for real-world data application demonstrates thoughtful consideration of practical implementation challenges.

Regarding the NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and limitations.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides adequate detail for experts to understand and reproduce the results.
- Originality: While building on BDMC, the authors make a novel contribution by extending it to bound the Jeffreys divergence for posterior inference quality assessment, which is distinct from previous work on marginal likelihood estimation.
- Significance: The work addresses a critical practical problem in probabilistic inference with clear implications for probabilistic programming systems. It provides a rigorous framework that could help non-expert users assess inference reliability, advancing the field toward more accessible and trustworthy probabilistic modeling.

The main limitation is the requirement for exact posterior samples, which necessitates working with simulated data. However, the authors thoughtfully address this by proposing a protocol for real-world data evaluation that appears to be validated in their experiments. This limitation is reasonable given the current state of the field, and the authors acknowledge it without overstating their method's applicability.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a clear, well-justified, and practically relevant advance in probabilistic inference. It aligns perfectly with NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions with both theoretical rigor and practical significance. I recommend acceptance.
