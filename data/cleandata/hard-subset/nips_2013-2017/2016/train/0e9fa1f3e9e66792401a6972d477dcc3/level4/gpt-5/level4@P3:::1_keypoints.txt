- Paper adapts BDMC/AIS to produce an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between AIS/SMC approximate samples and the true posterior by comparing forward and reverse AIS log-p(z) estimates.
- Method implemented in WebPPL and Stan, validated on several canonical models, and shown to guide representation choices and uncover a real bug.
- Builds on BDMC, AIS/SMC, convergence diagnostics, thermodynamic integration, and relates to subjective divergence and probabilistic-programming validation tests.
- Conceptual strength: principled conversion of BDMC into an expected Jeffreys-divergence upper bound for AIS/SMC samplers.
- Practical strength: integration into popular PPLs and demonstrations that yield actionable insights for practitioners.
- Relevance: targets the important problem of evaluating black-box MCMC in probabilistic programming.
- Empirical protocol: proposes a fitted-hyperparameter scheme to transfer simulated-data insights to real data and shows forward-AIS behavior matches closely.
- Clarity issue: Section 3.1 is hard to follow; qfwd and qrev and their conditional factorizations are used before being defined.
- Suggestion: explicitly define qfwd(xT), qfwd(v|xT), qrev(v|xT) and the conditioning structure upfront.
- Suggestion: add a concise, step-by-step BREAD algorithm (inputs, assumptions, forward/reverse AIS, combining runs) in the main text.
- Clarify which expectations the theoretical bound depends on (AIS randomness and, for simulated data, data-generation randomness).
- Notational nitpick: combine latent variables into a single x to reduce clutter; fix minor typos (e.g., missing parenthesis).
- Theoretical limitation: the bound holds only in expectation and requires reversible kernels and exact reverse initialization; per-instance guarantees are not provided.
- Empirical limitation: tightness shown mainly on toy problems; larger-model quantitative results with uncertainty would strengthen claims.
- Real-data protocol caveat: fitted-hyperparameter reverse initialization breaks rigorous guarantees; discuss failure modes (model misspecification, priors, etc.).
- Originality: novel and useful perspective adapting BDMC to bound expected Jeffreys divergence; could further contrast with subjective divergence assumptions.
- Significance: provides a rare quantitative diagnostic for posterior sample quality in black-box settings with immediate PPL utility.
- Experimental concerns: temperature schedules and kernel choices strongly affect AIS—provide guidance and report wall-clock costs for targets.
- Statistical reporting: AIS log-weights are heavy-tailed—justify aggregation method, report variability of the bound across runs, and show uncertainty estimates.
- Comparative suggestion: include comparisons to Geweke’s joint distribution test and posterior-quantile checks to clarify what BREAD uniquely detects.
- Pros for acceptance: principled, implementable technique; integrated in tools; solid theory and supportive experiments; finds real bugs.
- Cons for acceptance: exposition gaps, guarantees only in expectation and primarily on simulated data, and limited applicability to non-annealed MCMC.
- Recommendations: revise Section 3.1, add algorithmic recipe, report numeric bounds with uncertainty, expand real-data protocol discussion and sensitivity analyses, simplify notation, and briefly compare to existing validation tests.
- Overall assessment: strong and relevant contribution that, with clearer exposition and stronger empirical reporting, merits acceptance—reviewer leans toward accept.
