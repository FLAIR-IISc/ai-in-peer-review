This paper proposes a novel method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo, specifically by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors extend the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms, providing a rigorous and general procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors do a good job of explaining the challenges of measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples in MCMC inference. The proposed method, BREAD, is clearly described, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of its properties and limitations. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, demonstrating the effectiveness of BREAD in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in realistic settings.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas and contributions. The background section is informative, and the authors do a good job of situating their work in the context of previous research on MCMC convergence diagnostics and probabilistic programming.

The strengths of the paper include its novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact on the field. The authors provide a rigorous and general method for evaluating the quality of MCMC inference, which could be widely applicable in probabilistic programming and Bayesian statistics. The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of BREAD in debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages.

One potential weakness of the paper is the complexity of the derivation in Section 3.1, which may be difficult for some readers to follow. The authors could consider providing a clearer statement of how to apply the method from start to finish, as well as additional explanations or examples to illustrate the key concepts.

Minor notes include the use of unnecessary notation, such as the distinction between z and theta, which could be simplified or eliminated. Additionally, there is a missing close parenthesis on line 165.

In terms of related work, the authors could consider citing additional references on MCMC convergence diagnostics and probabilistic programming, such as [Geweke, 2004] and [Cook et al., 2012]. These references provide additional context and background on the challenges of evaluating MCMC inference and the importance of developing rigorous methods for monitoring convergence.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. With some minor revisions to address the complexity of the derivation and provide additional context and explanations, the paper has the potential to be a valuable resource for researchers and practitioners working in probabilistic programming and Bayesian statistics.
