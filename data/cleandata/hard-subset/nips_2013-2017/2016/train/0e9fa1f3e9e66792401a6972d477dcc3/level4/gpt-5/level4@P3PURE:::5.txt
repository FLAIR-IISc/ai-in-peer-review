This paper addresses the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC). The core idea is to select, among candidate MCMC schemes, the one that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds given the same computational budget. A key challenge is that BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution to obtain a valid stochastic upper bound. To address this, the authors propose heuristics: in the Bayesian posterior setting, they perform the diagnostic on a posterior induced by synthetic data (rather than the observed data), exploiting the idea that it is easier to generate good approximate samples for such posteriors. They then evaluate the approach on several examples.

The idea of using BMC-based stochastic lower bounds for MCMC convergence diagnostics is interesting. Even though the methodology appears tailored to AIS and SMC schemes and focuses on diagnostics derived from the marginal likelihood, it could still be valuable as an attempt to develop theoretically principled MCMC diagnostic tools.

However, the proposed implementation has several drawbacks that stem from the heuristics introduced to circumvent the need for an exact posterior sample in BMC. In my view, these issues substantially limit the appeal of the diagnostic:

- The method assumes advance knowledge of a hyperparameter value, η_real (e.g., obtained from a prior MCMC run), that is representative of the posterior. This presupposes both that the algorithm used to obtain η_real had already converged (which is the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be adequately summarized by a single value. Both assumptions are strong and generally unjustified.

- It further assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has MCMC convergence properties similar to those of the original posterior. This is again an unjustified assumption, particularly in the presence of model misspecification. In effect, the approach sidesteps the difficulty posed by BMC by changing the problem—diagnosing convergence on a different posterior than the one of interest.

- The method adopts an optimistic stance that initializing at η_real enables the MCMC algorithm to rapidly produce an almost exact sample from the posterior; while this may hold in some cases, it is by no means guaranteed.

Because these assumptions are closely tied to the very property we wish to assess—convergence of the original MCMC algorithm—the overall reliability of the proposed diagnostic is uncertain. I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not appear to undermine the conclusions, but these limited demonstrations are not sufficient to argue that the identified concerns will not be critical in more complex settings.

The authors criticize existing convergence diagnostics for failing in multimodal scenarios or when a few summary statistics are insufficiently expressive (lines 203–205). They should explain why their method is expected to perform better in such scenarios and provide supporting evidence. More generally, it would strengthen the paper to include simulations comparing their diagnostic to commonly used methods (e.g., those discussed in Section 4), highlighting cases where the proposed approach yields more reliable conclusions.
