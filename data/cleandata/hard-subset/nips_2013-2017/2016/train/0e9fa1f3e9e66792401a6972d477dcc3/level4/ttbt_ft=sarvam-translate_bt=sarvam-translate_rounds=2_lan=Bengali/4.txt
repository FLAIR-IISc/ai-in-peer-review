This paper presents a method for evaluating MCMC convergence – known as AutoBDMC. This method calculates the stochastic upper and lower bounds for log-probability using the recently invented two-way Monte Carlo method (which depends on annealed weights). The authors show that the difference between the upper and lower bounds is itself a limit on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the actual posterior and the approximate sample distribution, providing a single scalar quantity for evaluating the behavior of MCMC convergence. Since this method can only analyze convergence on synthetic data generated from a joint distribution, the authors provide experimental evidence that its analysis on such synthetic data can predict the behavior of real-world data. They also demonstrate the use of their method for model selection and debugging. Overall, I found this paper to be quite readable and well-motivated. The presented method seems clearly useful and reasonably compelling for experimental evaluation. I found section 3 to be the most difficult to follow compared to the rest. The conversion to 3.1 is particularly painful, as it enters the definition of variables and equations without any motivation. If the divergence bound in section 3.1 is a new result (which I believe it is), it should be clearly stated, as the novelty/contribution of the paper depends on this result. What exactly is AutoBDMC? This is not clearly stated anywhere in the paper. As far as I understand, AutoBDMC is a method for using AIS and RAISE for MCMC convergence analysis. I hope to see such a statement. When it is said in section 3.2 that "...it poses a challenge to AutoBDMC," AutoBDMC should be clearly defined. A few more details: Line 149: Should p(theta, z | y) be p(theta, z, y)? Symbolic inconsistency: The paper switches between using y and D to represent data/observations, which sometimes seems a bit confusing. Supplementary WebPPL code: It should specify (a) which versions of WebPPL it works with and (b) how it can be integrated into a WebPPL distribution for use. Or better yet, it should be published as a WebPPL package that can be installed via npm. If possible, a similar publication for a Stan version of the code would also be highly appreciated.
