The authors consider the problem of assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of MCMC schemes. They propose to do so by using the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood given by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The idea is for example to choose, among available MCMC schemes, the one that produces the tightest BMC stochastic bounds given the same computational effort.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges in assessing the reliability of MCMC outputs. The proposed methodology is interesting, and the use of stochastic lower bounds given by Bidirectional Monte Carlo to perform MCMC convergence diagnostic is a valuable contribution. However, the implementation has some drawbacks that make the proposed diagnostic methodology not so appealing.

One of the main issues is that the authors assume to know in advance (e.g., from previously run MCMC) a value of the hyperparameters, η_real, which is representative of the whole posterior. This assumes both that the algorithm used to find η_real had converged (which is basically the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be well summarized with a single value. Both assumptions are strong and unjustified in general.

Another issue is that the authors assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from η_real has properties (in terms of MCMC convergence) similar to the original posterior. This again is an unjustified assumption that, for example, should be carefully justified in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It seems that the authors try to overcome the difficulty posed by bidirectional Monte Carlo at the price of "changing the problem," i.e., performing diagnostic on a different posterior.

Furthermore, the authors adopt the optimistic view that starting from η_real allows the MCMC algorithm of interest to be quickly able to draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. This assumption may be true in some cases, but it is not necessarily true in general.

The authors provide some examples in Section 5.1 where these issues appear not to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology. However, these examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues that the authors themselves recognize will not be critical in other, maybe more complicated, examples.

To strengthen their proposal, the authors should provide more evidence that their method can do better in scenarios where previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools fail, such as multimodal cases or cases where few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive. Additionally, it would be helpful to have simulations to compare their method with commonly used diagnostic methods to show scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology. However, the assumptions made in the implementation are not well-justified, and the authors should provide more evidence to support their claims.
