This paper proposes a novel method for estimating the Jensen-Shannon (J-S) divergence between the distribution produced by an MCMC sampler and its target distribution. The primary application is for debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations, particularly within the context of Probabilistic Programming Languages (PPLs).

The core idea of using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to diagnose sampler convergence failures is compelling and potentially very useful, as it addresses known limitations of existing MCMC diagnostics. The motivation is strong, and the proposed technique could be a valuable tool for developers and users of PPLs.

However, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, primarily related to clarity and its connection to prior work.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarity of Derivation (Section 3.1):** The derivation in this section is difficult to follow. Specifically, the notation `q(.)` is used before it is formally defined. While the supplement and surrounding context suggest that `q(.)` represents the marginal distribution of the Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) sampler after T steps, this should be stated explicitly in the main text. The reader should not need to consult the supplement to understand the core methodology of the paper.

2.  **Lack of Procedural Overview:** The paper would benefit significantly from a clear, step-by-step description or an algorithm box that outlines how to apply the proposed method from start to finish. As it stands, the reader must piece together the complete procedure from various descriptions scattered throughout the text. A consolidated overview would greatly improve the paper's accessibility and practical utility.

3.  **Relation to Prior Work:** The authors should discuss the relationship of their work to prior methods that also validate samplers by running them on data simulated from the model's prior distribution. Two highly relevant works in this area are Geweke (2004) [1] and Cook et al. (2012) [2]. A discussion of how the proposed J-S divergence diagnostic compares to or complements these existing "joint distribution tests" would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Notation:** The notational distinction between the unobserved variables `z` and `theta` seems unnecessary, as they are always treated together in the general setting described. To improve clarity and reduce clutter, consider unifying them into a single variable.
*   **Typo:** Line 165 contains a missing closing parenthesis.

**References**

[1] Geweke, J. (2004). Getting it right: Joint distribution tests of posterior simulators. *Journal of the American Statistical Association*, 99(467), 799–804.

[2] Cook, S. R., Gelman, A., & Rubin, D. B. (2006). Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles. *Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics*, 15(3), 675–692.
