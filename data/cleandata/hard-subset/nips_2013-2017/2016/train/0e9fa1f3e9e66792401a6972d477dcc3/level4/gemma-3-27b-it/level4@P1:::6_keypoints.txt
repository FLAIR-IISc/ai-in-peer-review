- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC posterior inference by running AIS chains forward and reverse to upper bound the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence.
- Frames MCMC algorithms as importance sampling on an extended state space, building on AIS and SMC foundations.
- Integrates the BREAD method into WebPPL and Stan for practical use.
- Validates BREAD on multiple models and datasets across the two probabilistic programming systems.
- Demonstrates practical utility by comparing model representations and uncovering a bug in WebPPL.
- Places the work clearly within prior Monte Carlo, convergence diagnostics, and MCMC tuning literature.
- Discusses connections and differences with concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka.
- Strength: provides a theoretically sound, quantitative bound on MCMC accuracy versus heuristic diagnostics.
- Strength: practical implementation makes the method accessible to practitioners in probabilistic programming.
- Strength: cross-system validation strengthens empirical credibility.
- Weakness: technical novelty is incremental, relying heavily on existing BDMC/AIS ideas.
- Weakness: requires exact posterior samples (simulated data), limiting direct applicability to real-world datasets.
- Weakness: computationally expensive due to running AIS in both directions, limiting scalability for complex models.
- Weakness: performance may be sensitive to AIS hyperparameter choices.
- For acceptance: offers a valuable, rigorous tool for evaluating MCMC and aids model development and debugging.
- Against acceptance: limited novelty, reliance on simulated data, and potential scalability issues reduce impact.
- Overall: well-executed and useful contribution suitable for NIPS, though not top-tier due to incremental novelty and practical limitations.
