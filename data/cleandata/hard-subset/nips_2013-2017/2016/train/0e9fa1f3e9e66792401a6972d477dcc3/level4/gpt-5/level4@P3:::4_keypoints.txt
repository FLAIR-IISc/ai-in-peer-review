- Summary: Extends BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence for AIS/SMC-style samplers via forward and reverse AIS on simulated data (BREAD) and integrates into WebPPL and Stan with empirical validation.

- Key idea: Run forward AIS for a stochastic lower bound and reverse AIS initialized from exact posterior on simulated data for a stochastic upper bound; their expected gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence.

- Practical protocol: Fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate from the fitted model, and compare forward/reverse AIS behavior to assess real-data inference.

- Empirical evaluation: Experiments on regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and GMMs show qualitative agreement between simulated and real-data AIS behavior and reveal implementation issues.

- Strength: Theoretical link connecting BDMC gaps to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence provides a principled scalar diagnostic of sample quality.

- Strength: Demonstrated practical impact via integration into WebPPL and Stan and by detecting a real WebPPL bug.

- Strength: Thoughtful protocol for bridging simulated and real data and guidance for modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed).

- Weakness: Section 3 exposition is abrupt and needs a high-level roadmap and more intuition before formal derivations.

- Suggestion: State the main result as a formal theorem (expected gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence) with assumptions and a proof sketch; emphasize “in expectation.”

- Weakness: Method requires exact posterior samples on simulated data; the real-data protocol using approximate initializations weakens formal guarantees and needs explicit discussion of failure modes.

- Weakness: AIS estimates are heavy-tailed; paper should clarify how many chains are needed for stable bounds and report bound variability across repeats.

- Weakness: Notation inconsistencies (y vs. D, undefined symbols like v, qfwd, qrev) and possible typos (e.g., p(θ,z|y) vs. p(θ,z,y)) need fixing.

- Quality: Theoretical claims appear sound and consistent with AIS/BDMC literature; the expected Jeffreys bound is meaningful for assessing coverage.

- Quality: Empirical evaluation is solid and convincing, though more quantitative comparisons (e.g., distances between log-weight distributions) would strengthen results.

- Clarity: Overall well written, but Section 3 needs a gentler introduction, a formal theorem, and intuitive interpretation of auxiliary variables.

- Originality: Novel and well-motivated connection between BDMC gap and expected Jeffreys divergence, distinct from concurrent subjective divergence work.

- Significance: Useful for practitioners and probabilistic programming developers; the diagnostic can reveal implementation errors and guide modeling choices.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical-practical link, careful integration and validation in major systems, and actionable real-data protocol.

- Arguments against acceptance: Guarantees rely on simulated data/exact samples, exposition needs improvement, and applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-like algorithms.

- Requests for revision: Add formal theorem and proof sketch, discuss the “in expectation” nature and single-run implications, clarify real-data protocol limits, standardize notation and fix typos, and provide reproducibility details (WebPPL/Stan versions and packaging).
