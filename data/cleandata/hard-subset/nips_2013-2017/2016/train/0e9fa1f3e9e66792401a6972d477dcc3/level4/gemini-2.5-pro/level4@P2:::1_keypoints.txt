- Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate MCMC/AIS samples and the true posterior.  
- Core idea: run AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data to get an expected upper bound.  
- Particularly useful for probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) where inference is opaque to users.  
- Demonstrated practical utility by integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and finding a bug in WebPPL.  
- Empirically validated across five models and two very different MCMC kernels (HMC/NUTS in Stan, M-H in WebPPL).  
- Used to guide modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) with quantitative comparisons.  
- Significance/novelty: provides a more rigorous, quantitative diagnostic for sampler quality beyond heuristic convergence checks.  
- Main derivation (Section 3.1) is hard to follow; key notation (e.g., qfwd(xT)) is used before being clearly defined.  
- Paper should include a concise, step-by-step algorithmic description of the BREAD procedure in the main text.  
- Missing relevant prior work on sampler validation using simulated data (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006) and should compare to these methods.  
- Minor notational issues: unnecessary separation of θ and z (could combine into a single unobserved-variable block) and a small typographical error (missing parenthesis).  
- Overall assessment: strong and valuable contribution; clarity of derivation and inclusion of related work should be addressed before final acceptance.
