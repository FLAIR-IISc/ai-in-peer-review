

This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors introduce BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which uses reverse annealed importance sampling to provide rigorous bounds on this divergence for simulated data, and present a protocol for applying it to real-world data. The approach is integrated into two probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan), and validated across multiple models including matrix factorization, regression, and clustering.

The work addresses a critical gap in MCMC evaluation, where existing diagnostics can only identify certain forms of non-convergence but cannot rigorously quantify the accuracy of approximate samples. By providing mathematically guaranteed bounds on the divergence between approximate and true posteriors in the simulated data setting, BREAD offers a valuable tool for probabilistic programming systems where users need to assess the reliability of black-box inference.

Strengths:
1. The core idea of using BDMC to diagnose MCMC convergence is novel and highly impactful, addressing a fundamental limitation in current MCMC evaluation methods.
2. The paper demonstrates strong practical applicability through integration into WebPPL and Stan, and thorough validation across multiple models and datasets.
3. The validation protocol for real-world data is well-motivated and carefully evaluated, showing consistent inference behavior between simulated and real data.
4. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, particularly [GGA15] and [CTM16], and provides a thoughtful analysis of connections to prior research.

Weaknesses:
1. Section 3.1, containing the theoretical derivation of the upper bound, is difficult to follow due to unclear notation (e.g., q(.) is used before definition, where it should represent the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after T steps).
2. The paper would benefit from a concise, step-by-step explanation of how to apply BREAD from start to finish, rather than requiring readers to piece this together from multiple sections.
3. The use of both z and theta to represent unobserved variables is unnecessary and confusing; these could be unified as a single set of latent variables.
4. A minor error exists in line 165 (missing close parenthesis), which should be corrected.

Regarding the four criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound with proper theoretical justification and thorough experimental validation. The paper carefully discusses both strengths and limitations.
- Clarity: While the paper has a strong technical foundation, Section 3.1 would benefit from clearer presentation. The theoretical derivation is particularly challenging to follow without consulting the supplement, which should not be necessary for understanding the main text.
- Originality: The extension of BDMC to evaluate MCMC inference is novel, and the paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work.
- Significance: The method addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference with immediate practical applications for probabilistic programming languages, with potential to significantly improve the reliability of inference in these systems.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The minor weaknesses noted can be addressed in the camera-ready version, and I recommend acceptance.
