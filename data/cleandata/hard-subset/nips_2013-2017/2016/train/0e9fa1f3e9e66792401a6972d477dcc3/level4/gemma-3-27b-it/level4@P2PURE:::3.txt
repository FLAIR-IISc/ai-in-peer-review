The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps – functionally equivalent to those in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Stat & Comp 1996] – but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes leveraging the gap between these bounds as a performance measure for MCMC transition operators, presenting implementations of BDMC for Stan and WebPPL, and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality. 

The work possesses notable strengths, primarily the simplicity and ease of implementation of using BDMC to assess MCMC operator convergence. This suggests a potentially high impact, particularly within probabilistic programming systems where its adoption could be beneficial.

However, the novelty and depth of the technical contribution warrant closer examination. While the authors acknowledge a related arXiv submission on BDMC and rightly dedicate space to its description, this results in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling somewhat rushed and impacting writing quality, leading to clarity issues (detailed below). Despite these issues, the core technical contribution – the observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence – is a valuable one, though arguably not entirely novel.

A significant omission in the related work is Radford Neal’s seminal work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353-366]. Its absence from both this manuscript and the original BDMC paper raises concerns about the authors’ awareness of this highly relevant work. TT employs similar reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps to BDMC, but with the aim of tempering the MCMC transition move to enhance mixing in multimodal distributions. Crucially, TT utilizes the final sample from the reverse sweep as the initial sample for the forward sweep, with the final sample from the forward sweep serving as a proposal accepted or rejected based on the ratio of importance weights. This acceptance ratio appears to be precisely the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC.  Neal’s (1996) paper even interprets this acceptance ratio in terms of thermodynamic integration estimates bounding log ZT - log Z1, noting acceptance with probability 1 when the bounds converge.

This analogy with TT suggests a need for greater attention to the selection of βt values. TT demonstrates that overly large steps in Δβ = β{t+1}-βt lead to poor acceptance rates, while excessively small steps waste computation. The BDMC bound difference will also depend on Δβ, providing intuitive guidance: if the bounds are insufficiently close, Δβ should be reduced. Consequently, comparisons between MCMC operators may be unreliable when the gap between the log upper and lower bounds exceeds approximately 1 nat, again necessitating a smaller Δβ.  The functionality of "AutoBDMC" remains unclear, but there is an opportunity to develop a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ until reliable inference is achieved. The experiments would benefit from a systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with step size.

Despite these criticisms, this is a worthwhile paper. The proposed method holds significant potential. Acceptance hinges on the authors’ response convincingly addressing the clarity concerns and providing a more thorough discussion of related work during the camera-ready phase.

**Clarity Issues:**

*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as “all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is imprecise. The authors likely intend ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, but this is ambiguous given the two AIS sweeps. Inconsistent labeling (“rev”/“fwd” vs. “for”/“back”) further complicates understanding.
*   Equations (5-7) and (9-11) lack definition of the estimator \hat{p}(y) for the expectation, hindering comprehension. Do the authors mean E[log \hat{R}{rev}] and E[log \hat{R}{fwd}]?
*   Equation (6), as written, is nonsensical due to identical distributions within the KL divergence. Based on equations (4) and (10), the right-hand side should likely read p(z, θ | y) qback(v | z, θ, y)?
*   The meaning of "AutoBDMC" is never explicitly defined. Does this refer to (a) calculating the bound difference to assess convergence and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme described in section 3.2?

**Experiments:**

*   The selection of βt remains unclear. The code in the appendix reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`. Is AutoBDMC parameterized by the number of steps T to transition from βT = 1 to β1 = 1 / T? This should be stated explicitly.
*   In figures 2 and 3, “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” – does this indicate the number of steps used *within* the BDMC procedure?
*   The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) and its variants (u)c(R)AIS are not defined in the caption.
*   Axes and tick labels should be enlarged for improved readability.

**Typos:**

*   Two closing parentheses are missing in the expression for D_JS.
