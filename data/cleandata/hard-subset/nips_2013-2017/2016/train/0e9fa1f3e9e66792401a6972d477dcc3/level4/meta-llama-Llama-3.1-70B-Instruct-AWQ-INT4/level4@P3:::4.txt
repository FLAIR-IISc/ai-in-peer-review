This paper presents a method for assessing MCMC convergence, which the authors term Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD). BREAD uses the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) method to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds for the log marginal likelihood, relying on annealed importance sampling (AIS). The authors show that the gap between the upper and lower bounds is itself a bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, thus providing a single scalar quantity to assess MCMC convergence behavior.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas in the paper, relating them to previous work on MCMC convergence diagnostics and probabilistic programming. They also provide a detailed description of the BREAD method, including its application to simulated data and real-world data. The paper is well-motivated, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and readability. The authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of the BREAD method, making it accessible to a wide range of readers. The paper is also well-organized, with clear headings and concise paragraphs.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. One of the main issues is the lack of a clear definition of BREAD. While the authors describe the method in detail, they never explicitly state what BREAD is. This makes it difficult to understand the novelty and contribution of the paper. A clear definition of BREAD would help to clarify its relationship to other methods and make the paper more self-contained.

Another issue is the notation inconsistency, where the paper switches between using y and D to denote data/observations. This can be confusing at times, and it would be better to stick to a consistent notation throughout the paper.
