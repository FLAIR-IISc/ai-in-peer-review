The authors address the challenge of quantifying the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of alternative MCMC schemes. They propose leveraging the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), instantiated via annealed importance sampling (AIS), to assess sampler accuracy. In practice, one could select among competing samplers by preferring those that achieve tighter BDMC bounds for a fixed computational budget. A key difficulty is that BDMC requires an exact posterior sample to furnish a valid stochastic upper bound. To mitigate this, the authors introduce heuristics in the Bayesian setting: they run the diagnostic on posteriors induced by simulated data rather than the real dataset, capitalizing on the fact that such settings provide access to exact (or near-exact) posterior samples. They integrate their approach into WebPPL and Stan and present empirical studies. The idea of using BDMC’s stochastic bounds as a theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic is appealing. Although the method is most directly applicable to AIS/SMC-style samplers and focuses on the marginal likelihood, it remains potentially useful as a principled diagnostic tool.

However, the proposed implementation relies on heuristics introduced to circumvent the need for exact posterior samples in real-data scenarios, and these raise concerns that, in my view, limit the practical appeal of the diagnostic. Specifically:
- The approach assumes prior knowledge of a single hyperparameter setting, η_real, that is representative of the posterior (e.g., obtained from previously run MCMC). This presumes both that the earlier algorithm has converged—essentially the original problem—and that the posterior can be summarized well by a single point, which are strong, generally unjustified assumptions.
- It then assumes that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated at η_real exhibits MCMC behavior comparable to that of the original posterior. This is again unvalidated, and could be fragile under model misspecification; effectively, the diagnostic is run on a different posterior than the one of substantive interest.
- It further adopts an optimistic stance that initializing from η_real enables the MCMC algorithm to rapidly produce an almost exact posterior sample (to seed the reverse chain), which may or may not hold in practice.

As a result, the proposed procedure depends on assumptions tightly coupled to the very property we seek to evaluate (convergence of the original MCMC). I appreciate that Section 5.1 presents examples where these issues do not appear to distort conclusions, but these small-scale demonstrations are not sufficient to establish robustness in more challenging or mis-specified settings.

The authors note limitations of standard convergence diagnostics, particularly for multimodal targets or when low-dimensional summaries are uninformative. It would strengthen the paper to explain concretely why the proposed method should perform better in such cases and to provide empirical evidence. More broadly, comparative simulations against commonly used diagnostics (e.g., those surveyed in Section 4) would help identify scenarios where BDMC-based assessments yield more reliable guidance.
