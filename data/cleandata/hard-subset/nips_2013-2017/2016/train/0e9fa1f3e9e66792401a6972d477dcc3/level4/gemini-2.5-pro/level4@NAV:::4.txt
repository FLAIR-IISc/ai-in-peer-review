This work proposes a technique, named AutoBDMC, for evaluating the convergence of MCMC samplers. The method leverages the Bidirectional Monte Carlo framework, utilizing annealed importance sampling (AIS) and its reverse, to establish stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors demonstrate that the difference between these bounds provides a bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the approximate samples and the true posterior, yielding a single scalar metric for convergence assessment. Recognizing that the method can only be applied to synthetic data drawn from the joint distribution, the authors furnish empirical results suggesting that its analysis on such data is predictive of MCMC behavior on real-world datasets. Furthermore, they illustrate applications of the method in model selection and for debugging MCMC implementations.

On the whole, the paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The proposed technique appears to be of significant practical value, and the empirical validation is sufficiently convincing.

However, I found Section 3 to be more challenging to parse than the other sections. The transition to Section 3.1 is particularly abrupt, as it immediately introduces variables and equations without sufficient context or motivation. If the bound on the JS-divergence presented in Section 3.1 represents a novel contribution—which I suspect it does—this should be explicitly claimed, as it appears to be a cornerstone of the paper's contribution.

A clear definition of "AutoBDMC" is also missing. The term is never formally defined anywhere in the text. My interpretation is that AutoBDMC refers to the specific application of AIS and reverse AIS for MCMC convergence analysis, and a statement to this effect would be beneficial. The term should be properly defined before it is used in phrases such as "...this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC" in Section 3.2.

Additional minor points:
*   Line 149: A clarification is needed on whether p(theta, z | y) should be p(theta, z, y).
*   Inconsistent notation: The manuscript alternates between using `y` and `D` to represent the data, which could cause confusion for the reader.
*   Supplemental WebPPL code: The provided code should include (a) the version(s) of WebPPL it is compatible with and (b) clear instructions for integration. Ideally, this would be released as a standard WebPPL package installable via npm. A similar distributable package for the Stan implementation would also be a valuable addition.
