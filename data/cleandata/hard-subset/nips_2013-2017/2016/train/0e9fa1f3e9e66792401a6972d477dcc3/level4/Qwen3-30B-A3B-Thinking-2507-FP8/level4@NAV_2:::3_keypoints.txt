- Paper extends BDMC to measure symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between approximate posterior samples and true posterior using bidirectional AIS.  
- Authors propose BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation by running AIS chains both directions.  
- Implementation integrated into WebPPL and Stan, enabling automatic inference-quality monitoring.  
- Main contributions: derivation of an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.  
- Main contributions: empirical validation of the bound on toy distributions.  
- Main contributions: protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data via hyperparameter fitting.  
- Main contributions: practical implementations in two probabilistic programming systems.  
- Work builds on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and relates to classic convergence diagnostics and probabilistic programming literature.  
- Strength: elegant, simple approach directly applicable to probabilistic programming.  
- Strength: practical utility demonstrated, including discovery of a bug in WebPPL.  
- Potential impact: provides a rigorous, general, automatic procedure for assessing posterior inference reliability.  
- Novelty concern: key insight is a natural extension of existing work and is somewhat incremental.  
- Related work gap: fails to adequately acknowledge Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996) which uses similar forward/reverse sweeps.  
- Technical presentation rushed: Sections 3.1–3.2 lack sufficient theoretical detail for the derivation of bounds.  
- Clarity issues: ambiguous definition of vector v, inconsistent forward/reverse notation, and apparent errors in key equations (e.g., Equation 6).  
- Missing explanation of AutoBDMC and the selection of β_t schedules.  
- Experimental weakness: should systematically vary Δβ (step size) to show how bounds depend on annealing discretization.  
- Recommendation: useful and implementable tool but requires substantial revision on novelty discussion, related work, theoretical clarity, and experiments.
