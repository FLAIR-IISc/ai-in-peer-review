- Proposal: BREAD uses BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler’s output and the true posterior.  
- Motivation: Addresses the hard problem of validating MCMC in “black-box” probabilistic programming languages where diagnostics often fail.  
- Theoretical contribution: Derives an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence grounded in established principles (statistical mechanics / BDMC).  
- Practical implementation: Integrated BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and applied it for model comparison and debugging.  
- Empirical result: Demonstrated utility through comprehensive experiments, including finding a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampler.  
- Novelty: Applying BDMC to directly bound Jeffreys divergence for MCMC evaluation is a new and insightful idea.  
- Practical impact: Implementation in popular PPLs and empirical demonstrations show feasible and useful diagnostics for practitioners.  
- Clarity weakness: Section 3.1’s derivation is hard to follow, with unclear notation (e.g., q(.)) and inadequate stepwise explanation in the main text.  
- Generalizability weakness: Rigorous bounds rely on simulated data; the real-data protocol (fit hyperparameters then simulate) can introduce bias and needs more discussion of its limitations.  
- Notational issue: Distinction between latent variables z and parameters θ is unnecessary and complicates exposition.  
- Minor issue: Typo (missing closing parenthesis on line 165) needs fixing.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a significant validation problem, offers a novel/theoretically sound method, and shows practical feasibility and impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Poor clarity in core derivation and limited direct applicability to real data due to reliance on simulation-based protocol.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper recommended for acceptance contingent on improving clarity in Section 3.1 and discussing real-data protocol limitations.
