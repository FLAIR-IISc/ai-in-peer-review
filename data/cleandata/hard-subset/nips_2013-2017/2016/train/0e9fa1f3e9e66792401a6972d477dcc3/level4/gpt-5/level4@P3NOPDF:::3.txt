Summary
The paper proposes a simple, general method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC runs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—reverse “heating” followed by forward “annealing”—and interprets the resulting stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood as a measure of inference quality. The authors implement BDMC in Stan and WebPPL and propose a practical protocol for assessing posterior inference.

Strengths
- The central idea—using the BDMC gap as a generic performance measure for MCMC operators—is elegant, practical, and easy to implement. It has clear potential impact, especially in probabilistic programming systems.
- Providing working implementations in both Stan and WebPPL increases accessibility and could encourage adoption.
- Framing the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds as an upper bound on the Jensen–Shannon divergence is a useful interpretive link.

Concerns about novelty and depth
- Relative to existing work on BDMC and AIS, the technical contributions are modest. The observation about the JS divergence, while helpful, is not entirely novel.
- Much of the paper reiterates BDMC; as a result, Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel compressed and are where clarity problems arise.

Relation to prior work
- The paper should discuss Tempered Transitions (TT) (Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996). TT performs the same reverse/forward sweeps as AIS/BDMC but uses the ratio of accumulated importance weights as a Metropolis–Hastings acceptance probability for a tempering move. This ratio appears to be the same quantity underlying the BDMC lower/upper bounds. Neal (1996, Sec. 3.3) interprets this ratio via thermodynamic integration bounds and notes acceptance tends to 1 as the bounds converge—directly analogous to using the BDMC gap as a convergence indicator. Making this connection explicit would strengthen the paper and provide readers with well-established intuitions and design guidance.

Choice of temperature schedule
- In both TT and AIS, the schedule β_t is crucial: large steps Δβ degrade the quality of the importance ratios (and TT acceptance), while overly small steps waste computation. The BDMC gap will similarly depend on Δβ. This suggests a straightforward adaptive protocol: if the gap is too large, refine the schedule (smaller Δβ); if the gap is small, coarsen it. A systematic study of how the gap varies with Δβ would be valuable, and would help substantiate “AutoBDMC.”
- It would be helpful to clarify whether AutoBDMC includes any automatic refinement of β_t (e.g., iteratively increasing the number of steps T until the gap falls below a target threshold).

Clarity and presentation issues
- Definition of v: “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is ambiguous given there are two sweeps. Please explicitly define v, e.g., v = {z1:T−1, θ1:T−1} for the forward sweep, and specify the analogous collection for the reverse sweep. Clearly index which sweep each quantity belongs to.
- Labeling: The manuscript inconsistently uses “rev/fwd” and “for/back.” Please choose one convention and adhere to it throughout; if the distinction is meaningful, explain it.
- Estimator notation: In Eqs. (5–7) and (9–11), the paper takes expectations of an undefined estimator p̂(y). Please specify exactly which stochastic estimator is used in each case (e.g., E[log R̂rev] and E[log R̂fwd]) and how it is constructed from the AIS weights.
- Equation (6): As written, the KL divergence is between identical distributions. Based on Eqs. (4) and (10), I believe the intended terms involve the posterior p(z, θ | y) and the reverse path distribution qback(v | z, θ, y). Please correct and check related derivations.
- Definition of AutoBDMC: The term is never unambiguously defined. Please specify whether it refers to (a) computing the lower/upper bound gap as a convergence diagnostic, (b) using a fixed hyperparameter schedule as in Sec. 3.2, and/or (c) any automatic adaptation of β_t or transition kernels.

Experimental evaluation
- Temperature schedule: It is not clear how β_t is chosen. From the appendix code snippet var step = 1/options.steps, I infer β_t = t/T with T = options.steps. Please state this explicitly. Also clarify whether “HMC/No-U-Turn steps” in Figs. 2–3 denotes the number of BDMC temperature steps, the number of dynamic integration steps within each HMC transition, or both.
- Robustness to schedule: Include a systematic study of how the BDMC gap varies with T (or Δβ), ideally across multiple models and transition operators. A rule of thumb might be that gaps > 1 nat indicate an unreliable comparison between operators and warrant a finer schedule.
- RAIS and variants: Define RAIS (reverse AIS) in the captions and text, and clarify the meaning of c/u prefixes (collapsed/uncollapsed).
- Reporting: Consider adding error bars or confidence intervals on the estimated gaps, and include runtime or effective sample size to contextualize trade-offs between tighter bounds and computational cost.
- Visualization: Increase the font sizes in axes and tick labels for readability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the related-work discussion with TT and AIS literature, highlighting the equivalence of sweeps and the acceptance ratio/bounds connection. This will also help motivate design choices for β_t and transition operators.
- Provide a concise, precise definition of all path distributions (forward and backward), weights, and estimators, with consistent variable naming. A compact schematic of the two sweeps with variable indices would help.
- Consider an “AutoBDMC” procedure that adaptively increases T until the median (or high-quantile) BDMC gap falls below a user-specified threshold, with a cap on runtime. This would give practitioners a clear, model-agnostic protocol.

Minor issues and typos
- In the expression for D_JS, two closing parentheses in the D_KL terms are missing.
- In the NUTS citation, “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”
- Define all acronyms on first use (e.g., RAIS, AIS, HMC, NUTS).
- Ensure consistent notation for log marginal likelihood, partition functions, and the directionality of sweeps.

Overall evaluation
This is a useful, practical contribution with strong potential impact in probabilistic programming. The core idea is simple and valuable; the implementations are welcome. However, the paper’s novelty is limited, and clarity and related-work coverage need attention. If the authors can (1) clarify definitions and derivations, (2) discuss the connection to Tempered Transitions and schedule design, and (3) modestly strengthen the empirical study of β schedules and reporting, I would support acceptance.
