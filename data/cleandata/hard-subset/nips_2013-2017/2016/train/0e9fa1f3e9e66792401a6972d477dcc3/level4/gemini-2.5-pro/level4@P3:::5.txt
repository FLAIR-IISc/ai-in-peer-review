This paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of assessing the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors propose a novel diagnostic tool, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to use Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to generate both a stochastic lower bound and a stochastic upper bound on the log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. This offers a quantitative measure of sample quality.

A key requirement for BDMC, and thus for BREAD, is the ability to draw an exact sample from the target distribution to initialize the reverse AIS chain. While this is infeasible for posteriors conditioned on real-world data, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, run approximate inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, generate synthetic data from the model using these estimated hyperparameters. For this synthetic dataset, an exact posterior sample can be generated, allowing BREAD to be applied. The assumption is that the MCMC algorithm's performance on this synthetic data serves as a reliable proxy for its performance on the real data. The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to compare the efficiency of different model representations and to uncover a bug in an implementation.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Principled, Quantitative Diagnostic:** The primary strength of this work is its attempt to move beyond heuristic convergence diagnostics (e.g., visual inspection of trace plots, Gelman-Rubin statistic) towards a more theoretically grounded and quantitative measure of sample quality. For the simulated data setting, BREAD provides a rigorous expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, which is a comprehensive measure of distributional mismatch.

2.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The paper effectively demonstrates the utility of BREAD for guiding MCMC algorithm design. The case study comparing collapsed versus uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model in Stan and WebPPL (Section 5.2.1) is a compelling example of how this tool can yield concrete, actionable insights. Furthermore, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights its potential as a powerful tool for validating and debugging probabilistic programming systems.

3.  **Novel Application of BDMC:** The paper cleverly adapts the BDMC framework, originally proposed for marginal likelihood estimation, to the problem of MCMC diagnostics. The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the gap in BDMC bounds to the Jeffreys divergence, is a clean and valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses / Arguments against Acceptance:**

The main weaknesses of the paper stem from the protocol proposed to handle real-world data, which relies on a series of strong and potentially problematic assumptions.

1.  **Circularity in the Real-Data Protocol:** The proposed heuristic requires first running an MCMC algorithm on the real data to obtain a representative hyperparameter value, `\hat{\eta}_{real}`. This introduces a "chicken-and-egg" problem: to diagnose an MCMC algorithm, one must first run an MCMC algorithm and assume it has converged well enough to produce a meaningful point estimate. This presupposes a solution to the very problem the method aims to solve. Furthermore, it assumes the hyperparameter posterior is well-summarized by a single value, which may not hold for complex or multimodal posteriors.

2.  **Diagnosing a Surrogate Problem:** The method performs its diagnosis not on the original posterior of interest, but on a surrogate posterior conditioned on synthetic data. The crucial assumption that MCMC convergence properties are similar across these two posteriors is strong and not sufficiently justified. This assumption is particularly fragile in the presence of model misspecification, a common scenario in practice. If the real data-generating process differs from the model, the geometry of the true posterior could be substantially different (e.g., more complex, multimodal, or poorly conditioned) than that of the posterior from well-behaved synthetic data. In this case, a successful diagnosis on the synthetic data may provide false confidence about performance on the real data.

3.  **Reliance on Approximate Samples for the Reverse Chain:** The protocol for initializing the reverse chain involves running a few MCMC steps starting from the estimated parameters. This yields an *approximate*, not an exact, posterior sample. While the authors empirically show that the number of steps has little effect in their experiments (Section 5.1.2), this breaks the theoretical guarantee that BREAD provides a valid upper bound, even on the synthetic data.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations of the real-data protocol more directly.

*   The authors critique existing diagnostic tools for potential failures in challenging scenarios like multimodality. It would be highly beneficial to include an experiment on a model with a known multimodal posterior. This would allow for a direct comparison of BREAD's diagnostic capabilities against standard methods (e.g., Gelman-Rubin), demonstrating where the proposed method provides a more reliable assessment.
*   The authors should discuss the potential impact of model misspecification on their protocol. Could the discrepancy between the forward AIS curves on real and simulated data (as mentioned in Appendix D) be developed into a diagnostic for misspecification itself?
*   The assumptions underlying the real-data protocol should be stated more explicitly and discussed as major limitations.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces an interesting and theoretically elegant method for MCMC diagnostics based on solid foundations (BDMC and AIS). The connection between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a nice theoretical result, and the demonstrated applications in comparing model representations and debugging code are valuable. However, the method's applicability to real-world data hinges on a heuristic protocol with strong, unverified assumptions that undermine its rigor. While the validation experiments are encouraging, they are performed on relatively simple models and do not convincingly establish that the conclusions will generalize to more complex, real-world inference problems. The work is a valuable step towards more principled MCMC diagnostics, but the limitations of its current form, particularly concerning the real-data protocol, are significant.
