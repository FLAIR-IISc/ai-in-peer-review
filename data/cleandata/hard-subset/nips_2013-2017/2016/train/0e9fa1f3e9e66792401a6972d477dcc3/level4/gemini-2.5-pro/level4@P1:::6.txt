### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a method, "Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing" (BREAD), for measuring the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The technique is an extension of the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. The core idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains in both the forward direction (from prior to posterior) and the reverse direction (from posterior to prior) on simulated data. The authors show that the gap between the resulting stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood is, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. The authors implement this diagnostic tool in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They validate the method on several models and present a case study using BREAD to compare the performance of different model representations for matrix factorization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles the critical and long-standing problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality. This is particularly challenging and important in the context of "black-box" inference systems like probabilistic programming languages, where users may lack the expertise to detect subtle failures.

2.  **Practical Implementation and Utility:** A key strength is the practical implementation of the BREAD method within two widely recognized probabilistic programming frameworks, Stan and WebPPPL. This makes a potentially complex diagnostic tool accessible to a broader community. The experiments demonstrate its utility not only for validation but also as a tool for making practical modeling decisions (e.g., choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed representations) and even for debugging the inference engines themselves.

3.  **Thorough Validation:** The authors provide a solid empirical validation of their approach. They thoughtfully address the key challenge of applying a technique that requires simulated data to real-world problems by proposing a protocol based on fitting hyperparameters. The experiments in Section 5.1 convincingly show that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data under this protocol, strengthening the case for the method's practical relevance.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its originality. The core theoretical insight—that the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence—is a relatively direct and incremental extension of the analysis already presented for BDMC by Grosse et al. (2015). The authors are transparent about this, but it means the main contribution is the application and empirical validation of this specific use case, rather than the development of a new foundational method.

2.  **Significance for a General NIPS Audience:** While the application is valuable and the engineering is solid, the contribution may not meet the typical threshold for a NIPS paper, which generally emphasizes the introduction of novel methodology. The work reads more like a strong systems or application paper focused on implementing and demonstrating a known technique. The scientific findings presented, such as the comparison of sampler representations, serve primarily to showcase the utility of the BREAD tool, rather than constituting a new, generalizable result in their own right. Consequently, the work might be a better fit for a more specialized venue, such as a workshop on probabilistic programming or MCMC methods, where the focus is more on tools, best practices, and applications.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a well-executed application of the bidirectional Monte Carlo framework to the important problem of MCMC diagnostics. The implementation in Stan and WebPPL is a valuable contribution to the probabilistic programming community, and the experiments are carefully designed and clearly presented. However, the work is largely an application of previously published ideas with limited theoretical novelty. While it is a good paper, its focus on implementation and application makes it feel more suitable for a workshop than for the main NIPS conference.
