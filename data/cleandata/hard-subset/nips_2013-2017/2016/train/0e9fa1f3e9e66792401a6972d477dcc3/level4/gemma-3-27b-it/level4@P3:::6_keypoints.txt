- Extends BDMC to evaluate MCMC posterior inference using forward and reverse AIS to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence (BREAD).
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validates it on multiple models and datasets.
- Uses BREAD to compare model representations and to guide modeling choices, including finding a WebPPL bug.
- Builds on Neal (2001) and Grosse et al. (2015) and situates work within existing MCMC diagnostics and configuration literature; acknowledges CTM16.
- Strength: Provides a theoretically grounded upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, offering a more rigorous diagnostic than heuristics.
- Strength: Practical implementation in widely used probabilistic programming languages increases accessibility.
- Strength: Demonstrated utility for model design and debugging reinforces practical value.
- Strength: Generally clear presentation and appropriate referencing of prior work.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—primarily an implementation and natural extension of prior BDMC results rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.
- Weakness: Theoretical guarantees depend on exact posterior samples, which are only available for simulated data; real-data protocol’s validity is uncertain.
- Weakness: Potentially high computational cost of running forward and reverse AIS chains; scalability concerns not thoroughly addressed.
- Weakness: Use of quartile plots in experiments obscures distribution shapes and limits detailed comparison; alternative summaries/visualizations suggested.
- For acceptance: Offers a rigorous, usable method for assessing MCMC quality and is practically useful and accessible via popular PPLs.
- For rejection: Limited novelty, reliance on simulated-data assumptions, and computational expense may make it unsuitable for a top-tier conference.
- Overall: Solid engineering and implementation contribution but limited novelty and restrictive assumptions; reviewer leans toward rejection unless computational cost and real-data protocol concerns are convincingly addressed.
