

This paper introduces a novel application of bidirectional Monte Carlo methods to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference, making a significant contribution to Bayesian computation. The authors derive and analyze lower bounds for the KL divergence between the true posterior and approximate distributions, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for assessing approximation quality. This approach is particularly valuable as it addresses a critical challenge in scalable Bayesian inference: quantifying the error of approximate posterior methods without requiring access to the true posterior.  

The theoretical framework is well-motivated and clearly presented, with the authors effectively demonstrating how bidirectional Monte Carlo enables tight, computable lower bounds on KL divergence. This advancement allows practitioners to systematically evaluate the fidelity of MCMC approximations, a capability previously limited to heuristic or computationally expensive alternatives. The method is implemented in WebPPL and Stan, two widely used probabilistic programming platforms, significantly enhancing its practical utility. The integration demonstrates concrete benefits for users, enabling straightforward evaluation of inference quality within existing workflows without major modifications to model code.  

The paper's core strength lies in its elegant synthesis of recent advances in Monte Carlo methods with the pressing need for reliable posterior approximation diagnostics. The problem is timely and well-chosen, as MCMC-based methods remain dominant in complex Bayesian modeling despite known approximation challenges. The authors’ solution is both theoretically sound and practically deployable, bridging a key gap between abstract statistical theory and applied inference. The experimental results convincingly validate the approach across diverse models, showing that the derived bounds reliably reflect approximation quality while remaining computationally feasible.  

Minor limitations include the focus on continuous latent variables (though the method may extend to discrete cases), and the lack of comparison to alternative error metrics beyond KL divergence. However, these do not detract from the paper’s primary contribution. The writing is clear and accessible, with sufficient detail to reproduce the methodology and results. The technical depth is appropriate for the NIPS audience, and the paper’s contributions align strongly with the conference’s focus on statistical machine learning.  

Overall, this is a well-executed, impactful paper that advances both the theory and practice of posterior inference. The bidirectional Monte Carlo framework provides a valuable new tool for the community, and its implementation in major probabilistic programming systems ensures broad adoption. The work is original, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful challenge in Bayesian computation. I recommend acceptance.
