This paper addresses the challenge of assessing MCMC output reliability and comparing the efficiency of different MCMC schemes. The authors propose using the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, as provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The core idea is that, for a given computational budget, the MCMC scheme producing the tightest bounds would be deemed the most efficient.

A key challenge is that a valid stochastic upper bound from BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution. To circumvent this, the authors introduce a heuristic approach. In the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, they perform the diagnostic not on the true posterior, but on a surrogate posterior derived from synthetic data. The rationale is that drawing high-quality approximate samples from this synthetic posterior is more feasible. The paper concludes by demonstrating this methodology on several examples.

The central idea of leveraging BMC's stochastic bounds for MCMC diagnostics is interesting. Although the methodology appears limited to AIS and SMC samplers and focuses solely on the marginal likelihood, it represents a valuable attempt to develop more theoretically grounded diagnostic tools. However, the practical implementation suffers from significant drawbacks that, in my view, diminish its appeal. These issues stem directly from the heuristics used to bypass BMC's requirement for an exact posterior sample.

First, the method requires a "representative" hyperparameter value, η_real, presumed to be known from a prior MCMC run. This introduces a circular dependency: it assumes the prior MCMC run has already converged (the very problem we aim to diagnose) and that the entire posterior can be adequately summarized by a single point estimate. Both are strong and generally unjustifiable assumptions.

Second, it is assumed that the posterior derived from synthetic data (generated using η_real) exhibits convergence properties similar to the true posterior. This is another strong, unsubstantiated claim, which would require careful justification, particularly in cases of model misspecification. In essence, the authors circumvent the central difficulty of BMC by "changing the problem"—performing diagnostics on a different, potentially easier, posterior distribution.

Third, the approach optimistically assumes that initializing an MCMC algorithm at η_real will enable it to rapidly produce a near-exact sample from the synthetic posterior. This outcome is not guaranteed. Consequently, the proposed methodology rests on a series of assumptions that are critically intertwined with the very MCMC convergence properties it sets out to assess.

While I appreciate the authors' demonstration in Section 5.1, where these issues do not seem to alter the conclusions, the simple examples provided are insufficient to allay these concerns. They do not provide enough evidence to suggest that the identified problems, which the authors partially acknowledge, would not become critical in more complex and realistic scenarios.

The authors motivate their work by criticizing existing diagnostic tools for their potential failures in multimodal settings or when low-dimensional summary statistics are insufficient (lines 203-205). Given this critique, the paper would be substantially strengthened if the authors explained and empirically demonstrated why their proposed method is more robust in such challenging scenarios. Furthermore, a direct comparison with commonly used diagnostic methods (such as those reviewed in Section 4) is needed. The authors should include simulations that highlight specific cases where their method provides more reliable diagnostics than these established alternatives.
