

This paper proposes a method for evaluating MCMC inference quality by extending bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors present a protocol for using BDMC to assess inference quality on real-world data by simulating datasets from hyperparameters estimated on real data. They integrate their method, named BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its application to model representation analysis and bug detection.

The paper's main technical contribution is showing that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the marginal likelihood (from BDMC) upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. This is a valuable insight, though it appears to be a straightforward extension of the BDMC framework rather than a fundamentally novel contribution. The paper also provides a practical protocol for evaluating inference on real data, which is particularly relevant for probabilistic programming systems where users need reliable diagnostics.

The paper has several strengths. The core idea is elegant and simple to implement, making it highly applicable to probabilistic programming frameworks. The authors demonstrate clear practical utility through their integration into WebPPL and Stan, and their use of BREAD to identify a bug in WebPPL showcases its value as a diagnostic tool. The method's potential impact is significant for the probabilistic programming community, as it provides a rigorous, automatic way to monitor inference quality without requiring domain expertise.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The novelty is questionable, particularly as the authors fail to cite Radford Neal's 1996 work on Tempered Transitions (TT), which shares conceptual similarities with BDMC. TT also uses forward and reverse annealing sweeps with a similar acceptance ratio interpretation, and Neal's paper explicitly discusses how the bounds converge when the two chains are consistent. This omission is particularly problematic since the BDMC paper itself cites neither this work nor the relevant connection.

The technical presentation is also problematic. The derivation in Section 3.1 is rushed and contains several clarity issues. The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is ambiguous, especially with two AIS sweeps in the algorithm. The paper inconsistently uses "rev/fwd" and "for/back" labels without explaining the distinction. Equations (5-7) and (9-11) lack clear definitions of the estimators being used, and Equation (6) as written contains a fundamental error (the distributions inside the KL divergence are identical).

The paper also fails to adequately define "AutoBDMC" or explain how the temperature schedule $\beta_t$ is chosen. While the appendix shows a fixed step size ($\beta_t = 1/t$), this should be clearly stated in the main text. The experiments would benefit from a systematic study of how the gap between bounds varies with step size, which would provide valuable guidance for practitioners.

The connection to related work could be significantly strengthened. The paper's main contribution appears to be applying BDMC to evaluate MCMC inference, but the relationship to existing methods like Tempered Transitions is not properly contextualized. This is a missed opportunity to situate the work within the broader literature on MCMC diagnostics and convergence assessment.

Overall, the paper presents a useful method with clear practical applications, particularly for probabilistic programming systems. However, the lack of novelty attribution, rushed technical presentation, and insufficient exploration of key implementation details limit its scientific contribution. The method itself is promising, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, clearer technical exposition, and more detailed experimental analysis.

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound but the presentation is weak. The paper contains a fundamental error in Equation (6) and lacks sufficient detail on implementation.
- Clarity: The paper suffers from several clarity issues, particularly with definitions and inconsistent notation.
- Originality: The core idea is a straightforward extension of BDMC, and the novelty is questionable without proper contextualization of related work.
- Significance: The method has potential significance for probabilistic programming, but this is not fully realized due to the weak presentation and unaddressed related work.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing these issues, particularly the missing citation to Neal's 1996 work and the clarification of technical details. As it stands, the method is promising but the presentation does not fully convey its value or properly position it within the field.
