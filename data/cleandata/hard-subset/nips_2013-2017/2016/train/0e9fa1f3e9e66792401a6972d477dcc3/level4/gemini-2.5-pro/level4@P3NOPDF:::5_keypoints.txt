- Paper proposes using stochastic upper/lower bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) as an MCMC diagnostic and selector of samplers based on tightness of BMC bounds.
- Core heuristic: run diagnostics on posteriors from synthetic data to avoid BMC’s need for an exact sample from the true posterior.
- Method targets samplers like AIS and SMC and focuses solely on marginal likelihood as the diagnostic quantity.
- Idea is interesting and represents a step toward more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostics.
- Major concern 1: assumes a representative hyperparameter η_real is known a priori, which presumes the initial MCMC has already converged and reduces a complex posterior to a single point.
- Major concern 2: assumes synthetic-data posterior has convergence properties similar to the real posterior; this can fail under model misspecification or when posteriors differ substantially.
- Major concern 3: assumes initializing at η_real yields a nearly exact sample from the synthetic posterior, which is not guaranteed for complex models.
- These assumptions create circularity: the diagnostic relies on the very convergence properties it is supposed to assess, undermining credibility.
- Demonstrations in the paper are limited to simple examples that don’t convincingly address the method’s behavior in challenging, realistic settings.
- Paper lacks empirical comparisons to existing diagnostics (especially on problems where traditional methods fail, e.g., multimodal posteriors), which would be needed to justify practical value.
