## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please fill in*]

This paper presents an implementation and application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) within the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages. The work largely summarizes previously published BMC methodology and demonstrates its integration into these platforms with experiments on established benchmark problems. While the implementation is a valuable engineering contribution, the paper lacks novel theoretical insights. Consequently, a venue focused on practical implementations, such as an MCMC workshop or a software-focused conference, may be more appropriate than NIPS.

**Summary of the Work:**

The authors aim to facilitate the application of bidirectional Monte Carlo – a variance reduction technique for Bayesian inference – by providing implementations within two popular probabilistic programming languages: WebPPL and Stan. BMC, as described, leverages a forward Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence estimator to guide proposal generation, aiming to improve sampling efficiency, particularly in challenging posterior landscapes. The paper details the technical challenges of integrating BMC into these languages and presents empirical results on standard benchmarks including the 8 Schools problem and several models from the Stan diagnostic models repository.

**Strengths:**

* **Practical Implementation:** The implementation of BMC in WebPPL and Stan is a significant practical contribution. These languages are widely used, and providing a readily available implementation of a potentially impactful technique like BMC lowers the barrier to entry for researchers and practitioners.
* **Clear Presentation of BMC:** The paper provides a reasonably clear explanation of the underlying BMC methodology for readers familiar with Bayesian inference and MCMC. The connections to existing variance reduction techniques are also noted.
* **Demonstration on Benchmarks:** Applying BMC to well-known benchmark problems allows for a basic evaluation of its performance and demonstrates its functionality within the target languages. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Novelty:** The core contribution of this paper is an implementation of a known algorithm. There are no novel theoretical developments, extensions to the BMC methodology, or innovative applications presented. The paper primarily reports on applying established techniques to new software platforms.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the authors demonstrate BMC on several benchmarks, the empirical evaluation lacks depth. The performance gains (or lack thereof) are not systematically analyzed. Specifically, there is insufficient comparison against state-of-the-art samplers *within* WebPPL and Stan (e.g., NUTS) using comparable computational budgets. Showing whether BMC consistently outperforms or complements existing samplers is crucial. Simply demonstrating that BMC *works* is not sufficient for a NIPS publication.
* **Missing Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn’t adequately address the limitations of the implementation or the challenges encountered in applying BMC to more complex models. For example, the sensitivity of BMC to the choice of the forward kernel, or the computational cost of the KL divergence estimation, are not discussed in detail. 
* **Presentation Quality:** While generally clear, the paper contains minor grammatical errors (e.g., “previoulsy”). A more thorough proofread is recommended.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2 (Background):** While providing context, the background section could benefit from a more concise description of BMC. Focusing on the key innovations and differences from standard MCMC would be helpful.
* **Section 3 (Implementation):** More details regarding the specific challenges encountered when integrating BMC into WebPPL and Stan would be beneficial. This could include discussions on gradient estimation (for Stan), or handling stochastic computations (for WebPPL).
* **Section 4 (Experiments):**
    * The choice of benchmark problems appears somewhat arbitrary. A more strategic selection, including models specifically known to be challenging for standard MCMC, would strengthen the evaluation.
    * The performance metrics used are not sufficiently detailed. Reporting R-hat, effective sample size, and runtime would provide a more comprehensive assessment.
    * A crucial comparison missing is how BMC performs *relative to* the default samplers (NUTS in Stan, and the standard MCMC tools in WebPPL) within each language.
    * The authors mention that the KL divergence estimator can be computationally expensive. How does this cost impact the overall efficiency of BMC in practice?
* **Future Work:** The suggestions for future work are rather generic. More concrete directions for improvement, based on the limitations observed during the implementation and evaluation, would be more valuable.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper in its current form. While the implementation work is commendable, the lack of novel theoretical contributions and the limited empirical evaluation do not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. The paper would be more suitable for a workshop or conference focused on practical implementations of statistical methods. I encourage the authors to consider submitting a software description to a relevant journal or repository, highlighting the availability of their BMC implementations in WebPPL and Stan. To become suitable for NIPS, the authors would need to present significant new theoretical insights, a substantially more rigorous empirical evaluation – including detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art samplers – and a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach.
