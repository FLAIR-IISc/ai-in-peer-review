The authors introduce a general approach for evaluating MCMC transition operators built on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC runs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) passes—effectively the same forward/backward tempering sweeps as in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996]—and reinterprets the accumulated weights as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This work proposes using the gap between these bounds as a generic performance metric for MCMC kernels. The technical contributions presented are BDMC implementations for the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, along with a protocol for assessing posterior inference quality. The paper has both notable strengths and weaker aspects. The main strength is that using BDMC to diagnose MCMC convergence is an elegant and straightforward idea, easy to implement, and thus potentially high impact—especially for probabilistic programming, the intended setting here. I believe all such systems could benefit from incorporating this method. My primary concern is the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. The authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC and (sensibly) devote substantial space to reviewing it, but this leaves Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feeling rushed, with a noticeable drop in writing clarity that creates confusion (see below). Aside from clarity, the contributions are somewhat modest. Relative to prior BDMC work, the main new point appears to be the observation that the lower–upper bound gap itself upper bounds the Jensen–Shannon divergence. This is a very nice insight, though arguably not entirely new.

Regarding related work, it is puzzling that Radford Neal’s TT paper [Statistics and Computing (1996) 6, 353–366] is not cited, as it is highly relevant. The BDMC manuscript also omits this citation, leaving me unsure whether the authors are aware of TT. If they are, it is unfortunate not to highlight the connections. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but to construct a tempered MCMC move that improves mixing in multimodal targets. In TT, the final state of the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep, and the final state of the forward sweep is proposed and accepted/rejected based on the ratio of the importance weights accumulated across the two sweeps. As far as I can tell, the TT acceptance ratio is exactly the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds. Moreover, Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper interprets this acceptance ratio via two thermodynamic integration estimates that bound log Z_T − log Z_1, noting acceptance tends to 1 as the two estimates coincide. This analogy also raises the question of why the paper does not discuss the choice of β_t values more carefully. In TT it is well known that large Δβ = β_{t+1} − β_t degrades acceptance, while overly small Δβ wastes computation. The BDMC bound ratio will likewise depend on Δβ, giving clear guidance: if the bounds are too far apart, rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ. I also suspect that comparing MCMC kernels is unreliable when the log-gap exceeds >1 nat; in that case Δβ should be reduced again. It is not clear what the “auto” in AutoBDMC entails, but there seems to be an opportunity to automate the schedule by iteratively decreasing Δβ until the gap indicates sufficiently reliable inference. In this respect, the experiments could be more thorough—for instance, a systematic study of how the bounds behave as a function of step size. Despite these criticisms, I think this is a decent paper. The proposed diagnostic is potentially very useful. If the authors can convincingly address the clarity issues and the related work discussion in the rebuttal and camera-ready, I could argue for acceptance.

Clarity
- The definition of vector v as “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is too vague. I assume the authors mean {z_{1:T−1}, θ_{1:T−1}}, but this is not unambiguous given there are two AIS sweeps. This is compounded by inconsistent labels: “rev” and “fwd” versus “for” and “back” (is this distinction meaningful or just accidental?).
- The paper does not specify which estimator \hat{p}(y) is being averaged in equations (5–7) and (9–11), which obscures how these expressions arise. Do the authors intend E[log \hat{R}_{rev}] and E[log \hat{R}_{fwd}]?
- As written, Equation (6) is not coherent (the distributions inside the KL are the same). Based on (4) and (10), I suspect the terms should look like p(z, θ | y) q_{back}(v | z, θ, y).
- I could not find a precise definition of AutoBDMC. Presumably it means (a) computing the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds to assess convergence, and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme in Section 3.2?

Experiments
- It is not clear how β_t is chosen. In the appendix code I see var step = 1/options.steps. I infer AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T, running from β_T = 1 to β_1 = 1/T. If so, please state this explicitly. In Figures 2 and 3, does “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” mean BDMC uses that many steps?
- The acronym RAIS (presumably reverse AIS) is undefined in the caption, as are the (u)c(R)AIS variants (I assume un/collapsed versions).
- Please enlarge axis and tick label fonts.

Typos
- Two closing parentheses are missing in the D_KL terms within the expression for D_JS.
- In the NUTS citation: Homan -> Hoffman.
