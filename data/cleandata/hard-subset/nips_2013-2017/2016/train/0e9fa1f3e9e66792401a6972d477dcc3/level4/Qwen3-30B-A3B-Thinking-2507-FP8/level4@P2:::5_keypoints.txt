- Problem: assesses quality of MCMC outputs by reliably bounding divergence between approximate and true posterior.  
- Proposal: BREAD extends BDMC to provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence.  
- Applicability: method applies to MCMC-based algorithms like AIS and SMC, useful for probabilistic programming.  
- Main theoretical contribution: derives an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Additional contributions: provides exact computation methods for small examples.  
- Practical protocol: proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters.  
- Implementation: integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates practical validation.  
- Experimental validation: demonstrates utility across multiple models and languages and used to debug a WebPPL bug.  
- Motivation and significance: well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and potentially impactful for probabilistic programming reliability.  
- Relation to prior work: clearly relates to BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015) and Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016).  
- Clarity: exposition and methodology are generally clear, but needs clearer differentiation from related subjective divergence work.  
- Originality: moderate—novel application of BDMC to Jeffreys divergence, but technique builds on existing BDMC framework.  
- Major limitation: relies on exact posterior samples for simulated data, limiting practical applicability.  
- Heuristic assumptions for real data: protocol assumes (1) fitted hyperparameters represent the true posterior, (2) synthetic-data posterior shares convergence properties with real posterior, (3) few MCMC steps from fitted hyperparameters yield accurate posterior samples—these are strong and potentially circular.  
- Empirical gaps: insufficient comparisons with existing convergence diagnostics, especially on challenging/multimodal posteriors.  
- Computational concerns: limited discussion of computational cost relative to existing approaches.  
- Validation shortfall: protocol for real-world data lacks rigorous validation beyond simple examples.  
- Recommendation: strengthen discussion of limitations, justify heuristic assumptions, and provide more comprehensive comparisons and validations.
