This paper proposes a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC runs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps—identical in spirit to those used in Tempered Transitions (TT; Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996)—and interprets the accumulated probabilities as stochastic lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. The authors propose using the gap between these bounds as a general performance metric for MCMC kernels. The technical contributions include BDMC implementations for Stan and WebPPL and a protocol for assessing posterior inference quality.

Strengths
- The core idea—using BDMC’s bidirectional bounds to assess MCMC convergence and performance—is elegant, simple, and practical. It is especially compelling for probabilistic programming systems, the target setting here, and could have significant impact if widely adopted.
- The paper’s focus on tooling and protocols for evaluation in real systems (Stan, WebPPL) is useful to the community.

Main concerns
- Novelty and depth: Relative to existing BDMC work, the primary new observation appears to be that the difference between the lower and upper bounds yields an upper bound on the Jensen–Shannon divergence. This is a nice connection but arguably incremental.
- Exposition: While the authors devote space to reintroducing BDMC (a reasonable choice), Sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel rushed and the writing slips, causing avoidable clarity issues (detailed below).

Related work and connections to Tempered Transitions
- The paper does not cite Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996), which is highly relevant. TT performs the same reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps, but uses the resulting ratio of importance weights to construct a tempering move to improve mixing in multimodal targets.
- In TT, the final state from the reverse sweep seeds the forward sweep; the forward sweep’s terminal state is accepted or rejected using the ratio of accumulated weights. This acceptance ratio appears to be exactly the ratio of BDMC’s stochastic lower and upper estimates. Neal (Section 3.3) interprets this via thermodynamic integration bounds on log Z_T − log Z_1 and notes acceptance approaches 1 as the bounds meet. Making this connection explicit would strengthen the paper and situate the contribution.
- This analogy highlights the importance of the inverse-temperature schedule {β_t}. In TT, large Δβ leads to poor acceptance; tiny Δβ wastes computation. The BDMC bound gap will similarly depend on Δβ. Two practical implications:
  - If the bounds are far apart, rerun BDMC with smaller Δβ.
  - Comparisons between MCMC kernels are unreliable when the log-gap exceeds about 1 nat; reduce Δβ in that case.
- The “Auto” in AutoBDMC is not clearly defined. There seems to be room for an adaptive schedule that iteratively decreases Δβ until the bound gap (or a related criterion) indicates reliable inference. A systematic study of bound behavior versus step size would make the experiments more informative.

Overall evaluation
Despite the concerns above, the proposed evaluation methodology is potentially very useful. If the authors can address clarity and related-work issues in the final version, I would be inclined toward acceptance.

Clarity issues
- Definition of v: Describing v as “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is vague. Please define it explicitly (e.g., as the sequence of intermediate latent and parameter states across temperatures for the reverse and forward sweeps). Ambiguity is compounded by there being two AIS sweeps.
- Notation consistency: The paper alternates between “rev/fwd” and “for/back.” If these are distinct objects, clarify; if not, standardize terminology.
- Unspecified estimator: In equations (5–7) and (9–11), the estimator whose expectation is taken for p(y) is not defined. If the intent is E[log R̂_rev] and E[log R̂_fwd], state this clearly and define R̂.
- Equation (6): As written, the KL divergence is between identical distributions and thus ill-defined. Based on equations (4) and (10), it seems the right-hand terms should involve the posterior p(z, θ | y) and the backward path distribution q_back(v | z, θ, y).
- AutoBDMC definition: The paper never clearly defines AutoBDMC. If it means (a) computing the lower–upper bound gap to assess convergence and (b) using the fixed hyperparameter scheme of Section 3.2, please state so explicitly.

Experimental details
- Temperature schedule: It is unclear how {β_t} is chosen. From the code (appendix) showing var step = 1/options.steps, I infer AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T, with a linear schedule from β_T = 1 to β_1 ≈ 1/T. Please document this. In Figures 2 and 3, does “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” denote the number of BDMC temperature steps?
- Acronyms: Define RAIS (reverse AIS) and the variants u/c(R)AIS (presumably uncollapsed/collapsed) in the captions.
- Figures: Increase axis and tick label sizes to improve readability.

Minor comments and typos
- In the displayed expression for the Jensen–Shannon divergence, two closing parentheses in KL terms are missing.
- NUTS citation: “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”

Suggestions for improvement
- Add and discuss the Tempered Transitions citation, drawing explicit connections to BDMC’s bound gap and TT’s acceptance ratio, and discuss thermodynamic-integration interpretations.
- Provide guidance on choosing {β_t}, including practical heuristics (e.g., target bound gaps) and, ideally, an adaptive scheme that adjusts Δβ until a stopping criterion is met.
- Expand the experimental section with a controlled study of how the BDMC bound gap varies with T and with different schedules (linear, geometric), and how this affects conclusions when comparing MCMC kernels.
- Clarify all estimators, path distributions, and expectations; standardize notation across the two sweeps; and precisely define AutoBDMC’s components and defaults.
