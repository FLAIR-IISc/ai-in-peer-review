Summary
The paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jensen–Shannon (JS) divergence between the distribution targeted by an MCMC algorithm and the target posterior, with the aim of debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages. The idea of quantifying convergence failures with a principled, computable divergence is timely and potentially impactful, especially given the well-known limitations of standard MCMC diagnostics.

Strengths
- A clear and pragmatic goal: provide a quantitative measure to detect when an MCMC implementation fails to target the intended posterior.
- BDMC is a natural tool for this purpose, and framing the evaluation in terms of JS divergence is intuitive and interpretable.
- The paper addresses a real need in PPLs, where subtle implementation bugs or modeling mistakes can be hard to diagnose with traditional tools.

Relation to prior work
- The paper is closely related to prior predictive checks for sampler validation, notably Geweke’s “Getting it right” test [1] and the posterior-quantile validation of Cook, Gelman, and Rubin [2]. These approaches also test samplers using synthetic data drawn from the prior. Please situate your method more explicitly relative to [1,2]: what failure modes can BDMC detect that these cannot (and vice versa)? How does the JS-based criterion complement distributional tests or quantile-based checks?

Main concerns and suggestions for clarity
- Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. The notation q(·) is used before it is defined. From context, it appears to denote the marginal distribution of the AIS chain after T steps (i.e., the distribution induced by the transition kernels and annealing schedule at the terminal inverse temperature). Please define q(·) precisely at first use, including initialization, kernels, annealing path, and whether it is the pushforward of the initial distribution through T transitions or the stationary distribution of some kernel.
- The derivation in Section 3.1 would benefit from an explicit, step-by-step presentation. Consider:
  - Stating the assumptions (e.g., access to exact sampling from the prior; tractable evaluation of unnormalized densities; annealing schedule; reversibility conditions).
  - Defining all distributions and kernels before using them (forward and reverse AIS paths, intermediate distributions, proposal and acceptance mechanisms if applicable).
  - Showing how BDMC yields estimators or bounds for the quantities you need, and then how these translate into an estimate or bound on the JS divergence.
  - Summarizing the full procedure in a self-contained algorithm box or checklist (inputs, outputs, and recommended hyperparameters: number of intermediate distributions, T, number of forward/reverse runs).
- Self-contained exposition: Currently, some key definitions and justifications are deferred to the supplement. The main paper should stand on its own, at least at a level where a reader can implement the method without the supplement.
- Practical guidance: Please provide heuristics for choosing annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, chain lengths, and the number of forward/reverse runs; discuss estimator variance and computational cost; and explain how to interpret the results (e.g., what magnitude of JS divergence indicates concern in practice).
- Scope and limitations: Clarify when the proposed procedure is applicable and reliable. For instance:
  - Does it require exact sampling from the prior or prior predictive?
  - How sensitive is it to high dimensionality, poor annealing schedules, or multimodality?
  - When using synthetic data from the prior, how well does performance transfer to real datasets that may be less “typical” under the prior?

Minor comments and nitpicks
- Notation: The split of latent variables into z and θ adds clutter without clear benefit in this general setting; they always appear together in the joint. Consider collapsing to a single block of latent variables (e.g., x or z).
- Line 165: missing closing parenthesis.
- Please define all symbols at first use (e.g., q, p_T, K_t, β_t) and keep notation consistent across the main text and supplement.
- A small, running illustrative example could help anchor the derivation (e.g., a simple conjugate model where ground truth is known).

Overall assessment
This is a promising and useful contribution: diagnosing whether an MCMC sampler targets the intended distribution is central to reliable Bayesian computation, and BDMC provides a principled foundation for such checks. However, the current presentation—especially Section 3.1—makes it hard to reproduce or audit the method without consulting the supplement. With clearer definitions, a self-contained algorithmic description, and stronger positioning relative to [1,2], the paper would be significantly stronger. I lean toward acceptance if the exposition is tightened as suggested.
