The authors tackle the issue of evaluating the reliability of MCMC outputs and comparing the efficiency of various MCMC schemes. They suggest utilizing the upper and lower stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) procedure. The concept is to select the MCMC scheme that yields the tightest BMC stochastic bounds for the same computational effort. However, the main challenge lies in the fact that BMC necessitates an exact sample from the target measure to produce a valid stochastic upper bound. The authors propose several heuristics to address this issue. Their solution, in the context of Bayesian posterior sampling, involves conducting diagnostics on the posterior generated by synthetic data instead of actual data, leveraging the fact that it is easier to produce good approximate samples for such posteriors. The proposed methodology is then tested on several examples. The idea of employing stochastic lower bounds given by Bidirectional Monte Carlo for MCMC convergence diagnostics is intriguing. Although the methodology appears to be specific to AIS and SMC sampler schemes and produces diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood, it still holds value as an attempt to develop theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tools. Nevertheless, the implementation has several drawbacks that, in my opinion, render the proposed diagnostic methodology less appealing. Three primary concerns arise from the heuristic methodology attempting to overcome the requirement of Bidirectional Monte Carlo for an exact sample from the posterior. Firstly, the authors assume prior knowledge of a hyperparameter value, \eta{real}, representative of the entire posterior. This assumption is based on two strong and unjustified premises: that the algorithm used to find \eta{real} had converged (which is essentially the original problem of interest) and that the posterior can be accurately summarized with a single value. Secondly, they assume that the posterior induced by synthetic data generated from \eta{real} exhibits similar MCMC convergence properties as the original posterior. This assumption is also unjustified and may require careful justification, particularly in contexts involving model mis-specifications. It appears that the authors are attempting to circumvent the challenges posed by Bidirectional Monte Carlo at the cost of "altering the problem" by conducting diagnostics on a different posterior. Furthermore, they adopt an optimistic view that starting from \eta{real} enables the MCMC algorithm of interest to quickly draw an almost exact sample from the posterior. Consequently, the proposed methodology relies on various assumptions closely related to the actual aspect being assessed (the convergence properties of the original MCMC algorithm). Although Section 5.1 presents simple examples where these issues do not seem to affect the conclusions drawn from the proposed methodology, these examples are not convincing enough to claim that the various issues acknowledged by the authors will not be critical in more complex scenarios. The authors criticize previously proposed convergence diagnostic tools for their potential failure in multimodal cases or when few summary statistics are not sufficiently expressive (lines 203-205). I believe the authors should explain why their method can perform better in such scenarios and provide supporting evidence. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to include simulations comparing their method with commonly used diagnostic methods (e.g., those described in sections 4) to demonstrate scenarios where it produces more reliable diagnostics.
