This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler’s output and the true posterior distribution. The core idea is to leverage bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound this divergence, with a focus on diagnosing and evaluating MCMC implementations within probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). This is a valuable contribution, as existing MCMC diagnostics often struggle to reliably identify convergence issues. The approach of using BDMC to directly assess sample quality, similar to [GM15] and building on [GGA15, CTM16], offers a promising avenue for robustly evaluating inference procedures.

The paper is structured around a clear motivation: the difficulty of validating MCMC inference, particularly in “black-box” PPL settings. The authors demonstrate the integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and validate its utility by applying it to analyze model representations and even identify a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampling routine. This practical demonstration significantly strengthens the paper's impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying BDMC to directly bound the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC evaluation is a novel and insightful approach.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the estimator, while complex (see weaknesses), is grounded in established statistical mechanics principles. The upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence provides a rigorous measure of sample quality, at least in the simulated data setting.
*   **Practical Impact:** Implementing BREAD in popular PPLs like WebPPL and Stan and demonstrating its use for model comparison and bug detection underscores its practical relevance.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges of MCMC validation and positions BREAD as a potentially valuable solution.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The validation experiments, including comparisons between collapsed and uncollapsed models, provide strong evidence of the method’s utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Derivation:** Section 3.1, which details the core theoretical contribution, is challenging to follow. Notation is introduced without sufficient explanation (e.g., the initial use of *q(.)*), and the flow of the derivation could be improved. While the supplement provides some clarification, the main text should be self-contained. A clearer, step-by-step explanation of how to apply BREAD from start to finish would be beneficial.
*   **Generalizability to Real Data:** The reliance on simulated data for rigorous bounds is a limitation. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (fitting hyperparameters and simulating from them) introduces potential biases, as acknowledged by the authors. More discussion of the conditions under which this protocol is likely to be reliable would be helpful.
*   **Notational Consistency:** The distinction between latent variables *z* and parameters *θ* feels artificial in the general setting presented, as they are always considered jointly. Eliminating one of these variables could simplify the notation and improve readability.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** A missing closing parenthesis on line 165 should be corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian inference: the reliable validation of MCMC samplers.
*   The proposed method, BREAD, offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to quantifying the quality of approximate posterior samples.
*   The practical implementation and validation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrate the method's feasibility and potential impact.
*   The successful use of BREAD to uncover a bug in WebPPL highlights its utility as a diagnostic tool.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The derivation in Section 3.1 lacks clarity, potentially hindering understanding and adoption.
*   The reliance on simulated data for rigorous bounds and the potential biases introduced by the real-data protocol limit the method’s direct applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method offers a promising approach to address the long-standing challenge of validating MCMC samplers, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming. While the clarity of the derivation and the reliance on simulated data are weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and practical impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity issues in Section 3.1.
