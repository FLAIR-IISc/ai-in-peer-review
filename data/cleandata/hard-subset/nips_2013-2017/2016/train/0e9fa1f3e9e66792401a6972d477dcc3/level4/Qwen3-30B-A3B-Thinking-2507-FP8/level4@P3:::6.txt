

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors build upon the BDMC framework proposed by Grosse et al. (2015), which provides stochastic bounds on marginal likelihoods through reverse annealing. Their key contribution is adapting this approach to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. This is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming systems where users need to assess inference quality without expert knowledge.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) a theoretical extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence, (2) a validation protocol for applying this method to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters, (3) integration of the method into WebPPL and Stan, and (4) empirical demonstrations showing how BREAD can guide model representation choices. The authors provide rigorous theoretical derivations showing that the BREAD estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, supported by experiments on toy distributions where exact divergence can be computed.

The paper's strengths are significant. The technical contribution is clear and well-motivated, addressing a genuine need in probabilistic programming: providing users with a reliable, automatic method to assess inference quality. The theoretical extension from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior divergence measurement is sound and logically developed. The practical implementation in two widely used probabilistic programming frameworks demonstrates the method's applicability and utility. The validation protocol for real-world data is thoughtful and addresses a critical limitation of previous approaches that only work on simulated data. The experimental results showing how BREAD can inform model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) provide concrete evidence of the method's practical value.

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The background section thoroughly explains AIS, BDMC, and the necessary probabilistic concepts, making the technical contributions accessible. The derivation of the theoretical bounds is clear and properly contextualized. The experimental section is comprehensive, with careful validation of the method's consistency between simulated and real data, and it demonstrates the method's utility in a practical debugging scenario (identifying a bug in WebPPL).

There are, however, some limitations worth noting. While the authors acknowledge that BREAD is only applicable to inference algorithms like AIS or SMC (which share design choices with MCMC), they could more explicitly address the scope of applicability. The protocol for real-world data relies on the heuristic of using fitted hyperparameters to generate simulated data, which may not always be representative of the true data distribution, though the authors provide reasonable validation of this approach. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains, particularly for larger models.

The significance of the work is strong. The ability to automatically assess the reliability of posterior samples addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic programming, where "black box" inference can produce misleading results without users' awareness. This work provides a rigorous, general-purpose tool that could substantially improve confidence in probabilistic programming systems. The method has clear potential for adoption in practice and could influence how probabilistic programming languages evaluate their inference algorithms.

In terms of originality, the paper extends a previously published framework (BDMC) to a new application domain (posterior divergence measurement), which constitutes a meaningful contribution. While the core technique (BDMC) is not novel, the application to MCMC inference reliability is novel and useful. The related work section appropriately contextualizes the contribution relative to other convergence diagnostics and estimation methods.

The paper is technically sound, with proper theoretical derivations, thorough experimental validation, and clear discussion of both strengths and limitations. The experiments are well-designed to demonstrate the method's utility, and the integration into WebPPL and Stan shows practical relevance.

The main concern I have is whether the scope aligns perfectly with NIPS. While the paper is clearly relevant to probabilistic inference, which is a core NIPS topic, it might be argued that the method is more directly applicable to probabilistic programming systems than to broader machine learning problems. However, given that probabilistic programming is a major area of research at NIPS, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the reliability of inference algorithms (a fundamental concern in machine learning), I believe it fits well within NIPS scope.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The method is theoretically sound, well-implemented, and practically useful. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and addresses a genuine problem in probabilistic programming. While the scope is somewhat narrow (applicable to AIS/SMC-based inference), the insights and tools provided have significant potential impact on how probabilistic programming systems are evaluated and used.

The paper should be accepted as it makes a meaningful contribution to a relevant area of machine learning, with solid theoretical foundations, practical implementation, and clear significance.
