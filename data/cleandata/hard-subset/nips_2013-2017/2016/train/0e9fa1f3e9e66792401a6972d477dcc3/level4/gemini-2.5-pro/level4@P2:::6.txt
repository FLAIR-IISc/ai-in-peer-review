This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantifying the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The work builds directly upon the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015), which was originally developed to estimate log marginal likelihoods. The central idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from the prior to the posterior (forward) and from the posterior to the prior (reverse). The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, generated by these two chains, provides an expected upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior.

Because the reverse chain requires an exact sample from the posterior, the method's theoretical guarantees are limited to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical protocol for real-world datasets: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset using these hyperparameters; and third, use this simulated dataset, for which an exact posterior sample is known, to evaluate the inference algorithm. The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference on several models and to compare the performance of different model representations for matrix factorization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and notoriously difficult problem in Bayesian inference: how to reliably assess the quality of posterior samples from an MCMC algorithm. Standard convergence diagnostics can fail to detect non-convergence, whereas this method provides a quantitative measure of divergence from the true posterior (on simulated data). This is a highly valuable contribution for both practitioners using probabilistic programming languages and for researchers developing new inference methods. The demonstration that BREAD can be used to uncover subtle implementation bugs (as in WebPPL) is a powerful testament to its utility.

2.  **Originality:** While the underlying machinery relies heavily on BDMC, the paper's main contributions are novel. The key theoretical contribution is the explicit connection between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1). This re-frames BDMC from a tool for estimating marginal likelihood into a tool for assessing sample quality, which is a significant conceptual step. Furthermore, the proposed protocol for applying the technique to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters is a novel and pragmatic solution to a major limitation of the core method.

3.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experiments. The derivation connecting the bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence is clear and correct. The experimental validation is thorough, covering five different models from the Stan manual. The authors wisely test the key assumption of their real-data protocol, showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data (Section 5.1.1) and that the reverse bounds are robust to the quality of the approximate posterior sample used for initialization (Section 5.1.2). The case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed samplers provides a concrete example of how BREAD can yield actionable insights for model design.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented clearly, and the derivation of the main result is easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are explained effectively, and the figures are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Applicability of Guarantees:** The rigorous mathematical guarantees of BREAD are confined to the simulated data setting and to inference algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. The extension to real-world data relies on a heuristic protocol. While the authors provide compelling empirical validation for this protocol, it lacks the theoretical guarantees of the core method. The reliance on an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain means the resulting bound is no longer a guaranteed stochastic upper bound. This is a crucial distinction that could be emphasized more clearly.

2.  **Dependence on Model Correctness:** The protocol for real data assumes that the generative model is a good fit for the data. As the authors note in the appendix, model misspecification can lead to a divergence between the sampler's performance on real versus simulated data, potentially rendering the BREAD analysis misleading. This is a significant limitation and it would be beneficial to discuss this caveat more prominently in the main body of the paper rather than just the appendix.

3.  **Incremental Nature:** One could argue that the work is an incremental, though very useful, extension of Grosse et al. (2015). The core algorithm is identical to BDMC; the novelty lies in the new interpretation of its output (as a bound on divergence) and its application to evaluating inference.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   In Section 5.1, the authors mention in a footnote that their analysis does not justify averaging the AIS weights and thus they report statistics on individual chains. This is an important detail. Could the authors elaborate on why averaging is not applicable here and whether the analysis could be extended to accommodate it? Averaging is standard practice for reducing the variance of AIS estimates, and its absence could limit the practical precision of the bounds.
*   The discussion of model misspecification as a failure mode for the real-data protocol is important. I would recommend moving the example from the appendix into the main text or at least dedicating a paragraph in Section 5.1 to discussing this limitation and its implications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. It provides a novel, principled, and practical tool for assessing the reliability of MCMC algorithms. The strengths in significance, quality, and originality far outweigh the weaknesses. The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community and is likely to be influential for users and developers of probabilistic programming systems.
