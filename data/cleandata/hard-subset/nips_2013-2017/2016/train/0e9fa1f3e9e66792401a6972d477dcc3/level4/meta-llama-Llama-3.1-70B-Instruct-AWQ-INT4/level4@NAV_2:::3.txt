This paper proposes a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC method to evaluate certain kinds of MCMC-based inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior distribution. The proposed method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), uses the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators.

The paper's main strength lies in its beautifully simple idea of using BDMC to assess convergence of MCMC operators, which is easy to implement and has the potential to be high-impact. The authors' decision to devote a significant amount of space to reiterating the description of BDMC is wise, given its relevance to the proposed method. However, this also means that the main technical contributions presented in sections 3.1 and 3.2 are somewhat rushed, leading to some issues with clarity.

One of the main concerns with the paper is the novelty and depth of the technical contribution. While the authors cite an arXiv submission on BDMC as existing work, the main technical contributions presented in this paper are somewhat modest. The realization that the difference between the lower and upper bound provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence is a nice observation, but arguably not entirely novel.

The authors also fail to cite Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions, which is highly relevant in this context. This citation is also missing from the manuscript on BDMC, which raises questions about whether the authors are aware of it. If so, this is unfortunate, as there is a missed opportunity to point out connections between the two methods.

The writing quality slips in sections 3.1 and 3.2, leading to some issues with clarity. For instance, the definition of $\vec{v}$ is rather loose and could be clarified. The authors also fail to define what estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ they are taking the expectation of in equations (5-7) and (9-11), making it difficult to follow how these equations arise.

The experiments could be more thorough, particularly in terms of systematically studying how the lower and upper bound vary with the step size. It is also unclear how $\beta_t$ is chosen, and it would be helpful to state this explicitly. Additionally, the acronym RAIS is not defined in the caption, nor are the variants (u)c(R)AIS.

Despite these criticisms, the paper has some strong points, and the method proposed by the authors is potentially quite useful. With some revisions to address the issues with clarity and discussion of related work, this paper could be a valuable contribution to the field.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the BDMC method and its extension to evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms. However, the writing quality could be improved, particularly in sections 3.1 and 3.2.

**Clarity:** The paper could benefit from some revisions to improve clarity, particularly in terms of defining technical terms and explaining the equations.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution is the extension of the BDMC method to evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms, which is a nice observation but arguably not entirely novel.

**Significance:** The paper's significance lies in its potential to provide a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators, which could be high-impact in the field of probabilistic programming.
