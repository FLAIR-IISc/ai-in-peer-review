- Uses Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) upper/lower stochastic bounds to evaluate MCMC reliability and choose the MCMC scheme with the tightest bounds per computational cost.  
- Valid stochastic upper bounds require an exact sample from the target, which the paper circumvents via heuristics.  
- Heuristics include running diagnostics on posteriors from synthetic data, where good approximate samples are easier to obtain.  
- The idea of using BMC stochastic lower bounds as MCMC convergence diagnostics is theoretically interesting.  
- Approach is narrowly tailored to specific samplers and focuses only on marginal-likelihood-based diagnostics.  
- Major concern: the heuristic workaround weakens the formal guarantees provided by BMC.  
- Drawback 1: assumes prior knowledge of a representative hyperparameter value, requiring assumed convergence and that the posterior can be summarized by one value—strong, unjustified assumptions.  
- Drawback 2: assumes synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence to the real posterior, which is questionable—especially under model misspecification—and effectively changes the problem.  
- Drawback 3: assumes starting from the representative hyperparameter lets MCMC quickly produce an almost exact sample, an optimistic and non-general assumption.  
- The method depends on the very convergence properties it aims to assess, creating circularity.  
- The simple examples provided (Section 5.1) are not convincing that the approach will work in more complex, realistic scenarios.  
- Authors should justify why their method would handle multimodality or poor summary statistics better than existing diagnostics and include simulations comparing to common diagnostic tools.
