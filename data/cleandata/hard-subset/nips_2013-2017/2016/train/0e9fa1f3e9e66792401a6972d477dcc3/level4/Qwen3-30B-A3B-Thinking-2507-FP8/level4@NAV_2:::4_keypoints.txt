- Introduces BREAD, extending bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and MCMC samples.  
- Shows BDMC can provide an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence and validates this on toy distributions with exact computation.  
- Proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from estimated hyperparameters and integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan.  
- Distinguishes itself from concurrent work by using exact posterior samples for reverse AIS initialization.  
- Strength: Addresses a practical challenge in assessing MCMC reliability with a theoretically grounded, general-purpose method.  
- Strength: Novel technical contribution—bounding Jeffreys divergence via BDMC—with strong theoretical analysis and experimental support.  
- Strength: Practical utility demonstrated through integration into two probabilistic programming systems and use cases like model comparison and bug detection.  
- Strength: Experimental validation shows consistency between simulated and real-world data, supporting the protocol’s reliability.  
- Weakness: Section 3 is hard to follow; transitions and motivation into technical details (Section 3.1) are unclear.  
- Weakness: Confusing terminology—"AutoBDMC" is used inconsistently and should be replaced by "BREAD."  
- Weakness: Notational inconsistencies (e.g., y vs D for data) are distracting.  
- Weakness: Implementation details would be improved by providing packaged code for WebPPL and Stan rather than supplemental manual integration.  
- Quality: Method is technically sound and well-validated, though approximate reverse-chain initialization in Section 3.2 needs careful interpretation.  
- Clarity: Overall readable but Section 3 requires clearer motivation and a gentler introduction to equations and variables.  
- Significance: Fills a critical gap by providing a rigorous automated tool to assess MCMC reliability, useful for model choices and debugging.
