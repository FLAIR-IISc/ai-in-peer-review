This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by algorithms such as AIS (and, in principle, SMC). The method runs AIS both forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data to provide stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood; the gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The authors integrate the approach into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on toy and realistic models, propose a protocol for applying it to real data via fitted hyperparameters, and use it to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and to uncover an implementation bug.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] and AIS [Neal, 2001], and is related to SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006]. It is complementary to general MCMC diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman) and to recent work on measuring sample quality via Stein’s method [Gorham & Mackey, 2015]. It is also closely related to independent work on “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016]. Importantly, the paper’s simulation-based validation ethos connects to prior software validation approaches that test samplers on data drawn from the generative model [Geweke, 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin, 2006].

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A principled, expectation-level upper bound on Jeffreys divergence derived from the BDMC forward–reverse AIS sandwich. This targets sample quality directly, which standard convergence diagnostics do not.
- Practical impact: Seamless integration into two PPLs (WebPPL, Stan), with evidence that the method can guide choices of model representation and detect subtle implementation bugs.
- Bridge to real data: A pragmatic protocol that fits hyperparameters on real data, simulates data from those estimates, and assesses agreement between real and simulated behavior.
- Empirical validation: Toy problems where the bound tracks the true divergence, and a set of realistic models showing consistent forward-AIS behavior on real vs. simulated data.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Exposition of Section 3.1: The derivation is difficult to follow. The notation q(·) is used before being defined; please define qfwd(xT), qfwd(v|xT), and qrev(v|xT) explicitly before they appear and remind readers that xT is the AIS terminal state (i.e., the approximate sample).
- Missing end-to-end recipe: The main text would benefit from a compact, start-to-finish procedure (or pseudocode) describing how to compute the bounds for a given model, including choices for the temperature schedule, number of AIS steps/chains, and how to report uncertainty.
- Scope of guarantees: The bound holds rigorously only on simulated data where an exact posterior sample is available; the fitted-hyperparameter approach weakens guarantees. The paper acknowledges this and provides empirical support, but clearer discussion of when the approximation might mislead (e.g., under strong prior–likelihood conflict) would help.
- Reporting and variability: Since the gap is an expectation bound, results can be noisy. Please report variability (e.g., across multiple AIS chains) and discuss how many chains are recommended to stabilize the bounds in practice.
- Notation and presentation: Splitting latent variables into θ and z adds clutter without functional distinction in this context; consider collapsing to a single latent vector. There is a missing closing parenthesis around line 165. Use “Jeffreys divergence” consistently to avoid confusion with the Jensen–Shannon divergence.

Quality
- The theoretical link between the BDMC gap and Jeffreys divergence appears sound, and the empirical evidence is convincing for the settings tested. The reliance on AIS is reasonable, though some discussion of nonreversible transitions and alternative intermediate distributions would strengthen the analysis.

Clarity
- With the noted caveats, the paper is generally well written. Improving Section 3.1’s notation, adding an algorithmic checklist, and consolidating the method description in the main text (so the supplement is optional) would substantially improve readability.

Originality
- The core idea—using the BDMC sandwich to bound sample quality—is novel and timely, even if built on known ingredients (AIS, BDMC). The concurrent “subjective divergence” work is acknowledged; the distinction and overlap are appropriately discussed.

Significance
- This is a useful contribution for PPL users and developers. Having a principled, implementable measure that can flag convergence failures and guide design choices is valuable. The bug-discovery case study underscores practical impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a theoretically grounded, broadly applicable diagnostic for MCMC-based inference quality.
- Demonstrates practical utility across two PPLs, with actionable insights into model representation.
- Thoughtful protocol for bridging to real datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation issues (especially Section 3.1) hinder accessibility.
- Guarantees depend on simulated data; the real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Empirical scope (model sizes, diversity of samplers/SMC) could be expanded.

Suggestions for improvement
- Define all distributions (qfwd, qrev) before use; clearly state that xT is the AIS sample.
- Add a concise algorithmic procedure and recommendations for settings (temperatures, chain counts).
- Report variability and, where possible, confidence intervals for the bound estimates.
- Clarify conditions under which the fitted-hyperparameter proxy may fail and how to diagnose that.
- Streamline notation (avoid unnecessary θ/z split) and fix minor typos (e.g., missing parenthesis).
- Discuss computational cost vs. bound tightness and sensitivity to annealing schedules.
