The authors propose a method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps, reinterpreting the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This work leverages the gap between these bounds as a performance measure for MCMC operators, implementing BDMC within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems and developing a protocol for posterior inference quality evaluation.

This paper presents a potentially high-impact idea – a simple and implementable method to assess MCMC convergence, particularly valuable within probabilistic programming systems. Implementing this method within commonly used systems like Stan and WebPPL is a strong point. However, the novelty and depth of the technical contribution warrant careful consideration. While the authors effectively reiterate the BDMC framework, the core technical contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2 feel somewhat rushed, impacting clarity.

A key novelty appears to be recognizing that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. While insightful, this observation may not be entirely novel. Critically, the authors do not cite Radford Neal's work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing (1996)], which is highly relevant as it performs similar “heating” and “annealing” sweeps. The TT method uses the acceptance ratio, which is demonstrably linked to the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC.  A discussion of this connection, and the implications for choosing step sizes (βt), would strengthen the paper. The authors could benefit from exploring a schedule for iteratively decreasing the step size until reliable inference results are obtained—an “AutoBDMC” strategy—and evaluating its effectiveness systematically. 

Clarity issues exist: the definition of  'v' as “all variables sampled in AIS” is imprecise and could be clarified with a specific notation like {z1:T-1, θ1:T-1}. Inconsistent labeling (e.g., “rev” vs. “fwd” vs. "for" and "back") is distracting. Equations (6), (9-11) lack clarity regarding the estimator used in the expectation, and Equation (6) appears to contain an error in the KL divergence terms. The meaning of “AutoBDMC” is never explicitly defined. 

Experiments lack details regarding the choice of βt (the step size parameter), and the connection between "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" and BDMC's steps is unclear. Acronyms like RAIS and (u)c(R)AIS should be defined in figure captions.  Axis labels and tick marks are too small for easy readability.  Minor typos are present (e.g., missing parentheses in the DKL terms, misspellings in citations).

Despite these concerns, this is a worthwhile contribution. The proposed method has the potential to be a valuable diagnostic tool. Depending on the authors’ response to concerns about clarity, related work, and experimental detail, acceptance is plausible.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method is a simple, elegant, and potentially highly impactful approach to evaluating MCMC convergence.
*   Implementation within Stan and WebPPL increases the practical utility of the method.
*   The core idea – utilizing the gap between BDMC bounds as a divergence measure – is insightful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the technical contributions is somewhat limited.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work, particularly regarding Tempered Transitions.
*   Clarity issues detract from the presentation of the method.
*   Experimental details are insufficient to fully assess the method’s performance and sensitivity to parameters.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound overall, although some equations require clarification. The empirical validation is reasonable but could be more extensive. It presents a complete piece of work within the scope defined. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential issues.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from some clarity issues, as detailed above. Improved notation, definitions, and explanations would significantly enhance readability.

**Originality:** The originality is moderate. While the application of BDMC to assess MCMC is novel, the underlying BDMC technique and its connection to tempered transitions are not entirely new.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high. A reliable, automated method for assessing MCMC convergence would be a valuable contribution to the field, particularly within the context of probabilistic programming.
