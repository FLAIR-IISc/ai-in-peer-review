This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler and the target distribution of interest, with a particular eye towards debugging and evaluating MCMC implementations in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). I like the idea of using bidirectional Monte Carlo to diagnose failures of a sampler to converge—this idea seems potentially quite useful since existing MCMC diagnostics are indeed problematic.

The authors extend the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. The method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), is integrated into two PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, and validated on several models and datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges in measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples. The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, I found the derivation in Section 3.1 to be hard to follow. The notation could be improved, and it would be helpful to have a clear statement of how to apply the method from start to finish. The reader has to piece this together a bit, which can be confusing.

Minor notes: It's a little cluttered to break the unobserved variables into z and θ, when in a general setting like this there is no difference between z and θ—they always appear together, so why not eliminate z or θ? Line 165: missing close parentheses.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, particularly [1] and [2], which also test the ability of MCMC samplers by running them on data sampled from the prior.

Overall, I think the paper is a good contribution to the field, and with some revisions to address the above comments, it has the potential to be even stronger.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel method for evaluating MCMC implementations in PPLs.
* The technical contributions are sound, and the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges in measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples.

Weaknesses:

* The derivation in Section 3.1 could be improved for clarity.
* The notation could be improved.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work.
* Minor errors, such as missing close parentheses, should be corrected.

Acceptance:

Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the comments above to improve the clarity and readability of the paper.
