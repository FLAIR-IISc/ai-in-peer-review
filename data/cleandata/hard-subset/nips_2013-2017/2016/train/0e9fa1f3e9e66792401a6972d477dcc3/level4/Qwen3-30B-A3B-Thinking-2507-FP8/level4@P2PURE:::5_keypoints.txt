- Paper proposes selecting MCMC schemes that produce the tightest Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) stochastic bounds on the log marginal likelihood for a given computational budget.  
- BMC requires an exact sample from the target posterior to produce a valid stochastic upper bound, which is a core challenge the paper addresses.  
- Authors introduce heuristics: run diagnostics on a synthetic-data posterior generated using a pre-specified hyperparameter η_real instead of the actual posterior.  
- Premise: approximate samples from the synthetic-data posterior are easier to obtain, enabling practical use of BMC bounds.  
- Empirical evaluation is presented on several examples, showing the idea can work in simple cases.  
- Method is restricted to AIS and SMC samplers and focuses only on marginal-likelihood-based diagnostics.  
- The approach represents a valuable attempt to create theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostics despite its limitations.  
- Major concern 1: method assumes prior knowledge of a representative η_real, presuming the very convergence it aims to diagnose and that the posterior is well summarized by a single value.  
- Major concern 2: it assumes the synthetic-data posterior (from η_real) shares MCMC convergence properties with the original posterior, which is unjustified, especially under model misspecification.  
- Major concern 3: it assumes starting from η_real yields an almost exact posterior sample quickly, which may not hold in general.  
- These assumptions create a circular dependency between diagnostic prerequisites and the convergence properties being assessed.  
- Experiments are limited/simple and insufficient to claim robustness in complex scenarios; authors should clarify why their method overcomes prior diagnostics’ failures (e.g., multimodality) and provide comparative simulations against standard diagnostics.
