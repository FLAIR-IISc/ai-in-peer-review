Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The method runs annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse (posterior to prior), and uses the gap between the resulting stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the exact posterior. The authors integrate the approach into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on simulated and real datasets, and propose a practical protocol for real-data evaluation by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating from the fitted model, and checking consistency of inference behavior across real and simulated data. They also use the tool to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and report a discovered bug in WebPPL revealed by the method.

Relation to prior work: The work builds directly on BDMC and AIS, and is closely related to ideas from thermodynamic integration and non-equilibrium work identities. The connection to Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, 1996] is particularly relevant: TT uses reverse heating and forward annealing sweeps with an acceptance ratio that can be interpreted via bounding arguments on log partition function differences. Drawing out this relationship would strengthen the paper and situate the contributions within the well-established literature on tempering.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, general, and easy to implement. The idea of using BDMC’s bidirectional AIS to produce an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is elegant and broadly useful.
- High potential impact for probabilistic programming, where users need principled, automated diagnostics for black-box inference. The integrations in Stan and WebPPL demonstrate practicality.
- The real-data protocol (fit hyperparameters, simulate, and compare behaviors) is pragmatic and, in experiments, shows encouraging consistency between real and simulated data.
- The method can guide modeling and algorithmic choices (e.g., collapsing vs. not) and even help debug inference implementations.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Novelty and depth: Relative to BDMC, the core novelty is the observation that the forward–reverse gap yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors, plus the practical protocol for real data and PPL integrations. While valuable, the technical additions feel modest and some derivations are presented tersely.
- Related work: The connection to Tempered Transitions [Neal, 1996] deserves explicit discussion. The forward/reverse sweeps in TT and the interpretation of the acceptance ratio via bracketing log ZT − log Z1 are closely aligned conceptually to the BDMC-based analysis here. Highlighting similarities and differences (e.g., objectives, guarantees, and usage) would enrich the paper.
- Schedule design: The choice of inverse-temperature schedule βt critically affects both TT acceptance rates and BDMC gap tightness. The paper provides little guidance or empirical study of how the bound gap varies with Δβ. An “auto” schedule that adaptively refines βt until the gap falls below a target tolerance would be natural and useful.
- Exposition issues that impeded reading:
  - The definition of v as “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is vague given two sweeps; please specify precisely (e.g., the sequence x1:T−1 for the forward chain and its reverse-chain analogue).
  - Notation for forward/reverse (“fwd” vs. “for”, “rev” vs. “back”) is inconsistent; unify terminology.
  - In the expectations in Eqs. (5–7) and (9–11), clearly state the estimator (e.g., E[log R̂fwd] and E[log R̂rev]) to make the derivations traceable.
  - As written, Eq. (6) appears to place identical distributions inside the KL; please check. Based on Eqs. (4) and (10), the intended terms likely involve pT(xT) qrev(v|xT) and qfwd(xT) qfwd(v|xT).
  - “AutoBDMC” is never unambiguously defined; clarify what is automated (e.g., computing the gap, using fitted hyperparameters, adaptive β schedules, stopping criteria).

Quality
The approach is sound, leveraging known AIS/BDMC properties to produce expected bounds on divergences. The derivations would benefit from clearer notation and a few corrections. The experimental protocol is sensible; the consistency checks between real and simulated data are reassuring. A systematic study of how the gap depends on Δβ and transition kernels would further strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written but uneven in the technical core (Sec. 3). Tighten notation, fix equation issues, and define all acronyms and variants on first use. Improve figure readability (axis and tick labels) and ensure captions define acronyms (e.g., RAIS; collapsed/uncollapsed variants).

Originality
Moderate. Building on BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples and deploying it across two PPLs is useful, but the conceptual step from BDMC is incremental. The real-data evaluation protocol is practical.

Significance
High practical significance for PPLs and MCMC users. The method offers a rare, quantitative diagnostic that can inform modeling choices and detect implementation errors.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method with real utility for PPL users and developers.
- Clean integrations in Stan and WebPPL; compelling debugging example.
- Pragmatic protocol to bridge simulated and real data settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Modest technical novelty beyond BDMC; derivations are terse and sometimes unclear.
- Incomplete discussion of closely related tempering literature (e.g., TT).
- Limited guidance and empirical analysis of β schedules and their impact on gap tightness.

Specific questions/suggestions
- Explain how βt is chosen. The appendix code suggests a linear schedule (var step = 1/options.steps). Please state the schedule and its parameterization in the main text, and clarify what “HMC/No-U-Turn steps” in Figures 2–3 denotes relative to BDMC steps.
- Define RAIS and the (u)c(R)AIS variants in figure captions (reverse AIS; uncollapsed/collapsed).
- Consider an adaptive schedule that refines Δβ until the gap is below a target threshold (e.g., < 1 nat), and report how results vary with T and schedule shape.

Minor issues and typos
- Missing closing parentheses in the divergence expression.
- NUTS citation: “Homan” should be “Hoffman.”

Overall evaluation
A useful and timely contribution with strong practical impact, especially for probabilistic programming. If the authors can clarify the derivations, better position the work relative to Tempered Transitions, and provide guidance on schedule design, I would lean toward acceptance.
