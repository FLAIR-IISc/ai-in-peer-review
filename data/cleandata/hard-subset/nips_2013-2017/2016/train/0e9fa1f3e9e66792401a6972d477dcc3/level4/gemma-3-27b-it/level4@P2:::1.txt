This paper proposes a method for estimating the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler’s output and the true target distribution, with the goal of improving the debugging and evaluation of MCMC implementations, particularly within probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The core idea—using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to diagnose sampler convergence—is promising, as existing MCMC diagnostics often fall short. The approach builds on related work, such as [Grosse et al., 2015] and methods for testing sampler quality by evaluating performance on data sampled from the prior [Geweke, 2004; Cook et al., 2012].

The paper extends BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples from MCMC algorithms. This is achieved by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa, particularly for algorithms amenable to importance sampling like AIS and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and validated on several models and datasets. An interesting application of the method is the analysis of different model representations within these PPLs, demonstrating its potential to guide algorithmic design.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel application of BDMC:** Applying BDMC to assess MCMC accuracy, rather than solely for marginal likelihood estimation, is a creative and valuable contribution.
*   **Rigorous bounds:** The method provides, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence, offering a more quantitative assessment than traditional diagnostics.
*   **Practical implementation:** The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method readily accessible to practitioners.
*   **Illustrative experiments:** The application to matrix factorization models demonstrates the practical utility of BREAD in informing modeling decisions.
*   **Debugging potential:** The identification of a bug in WebPPL highlights the usefulness of the method as a tool for verifying software implementations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Derivation clarity:** The derivation in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. The notation is not sufficiently clear, particularly regarding the definition and use of q(.). Expanding on the explanation and potentially moving some details to the appendix would improve readability.
*   **Method application clarity:**  A more concise, step-by-step explanation of how to apply the method would be beneficial. Currently, the reader must synthesize the process from different parts of the text.
*   **Generalizability:** The analysis relies on having access to exact posterior samples (at least for the reverse chain), which limits its direct applicability to all MCMC algorithms and real-world scenarios. The proposed protocol for using simulated data mitigates this issue, but its robustness deserves further investigation.
*   **Variable notation:** The distinction between latent variables *z* and parameters *θ* feels artificial in the general setting presented.  Consolidating these into a single variable representation might streamline the notation.
*   **Minor Errors**: A missing closing parenthesis on line 165.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for evaluating MCMC accuracy.
*   The rigorous bounds provided by BREAD address a long-standing challenge in Bayesian inference.
*   The practical implementation in widely-used PPLs enhances the impact of the work.
*   The illustrative experiments demonstrate the potential for BREAD to guide model and algorithm design.
*   The identified bug in WebPPL highlights the method’s utility for software verification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the derivation and method explanation could be improved.
*   The reliance on exact posterior samples limits the immediate applicability of the method.
*   The validation experiments, while encouraging, could be expanded to a wider range of models and datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method offers a valuable tool for diagnosing and improving MCMC algorithms, particularly within the context of probabilistic programming. While the clarity of the technical details could be enhanced, the potential impact of this work is substantial.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the identified weaknesses regarding clarity and providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the method.
