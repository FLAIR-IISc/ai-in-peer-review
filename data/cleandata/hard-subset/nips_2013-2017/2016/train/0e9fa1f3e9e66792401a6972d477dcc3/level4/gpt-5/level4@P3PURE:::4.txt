This paper introduces AutoBDMC, a method for assessing MCMC convergence that leverages Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood via annealed importance sampling (AIS). The authors show that the gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jensen–Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples, yielding a single scalar diagnostic for MCMC convergence. Because the approach evaluates convergence only on synthetic data drawn from the joint distribution, the paper provides experimental evidence that performance on such synthetic data is predictive of behavior on real-world data. The method is also demonstrated for model selection and debugging.

Overall assessment:
- The paper is well written and well motivated. The method appears genuinely useful, and the experimental evaluation is reasonably compelling.
- A key strength is the reduction of convergence assessment to a single, interpretable scalar bound via the BDMC gap.
- The demonstrated applications to model selection and debugging are practical and persuasive.

Clarity and exposition:
- Section 3 is substantially harder to follow than the rest of the paper. The transition into Section 3.1 is especially abrupt: it introduces variables and equations without sufficient motivation or context.
- If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 is a new result (which I believe it is), the paper should state this explicitly, as the novelty and contribution of the work hinge on it.
- The paper never explicitly defines “AutoBDMC.” As I understand it, AutoBDMC refers to the approach of using AIS and reverse AIS (rAIS) within BDMC to analyze MCMC convergence. Please define this clearly and early. By the time Section 3.2 states that “this poses a challenge for AutoBDMC,” the reader should already have a precise definition in mind.

Limitations and empirical claims:
- The method’s reliance on synthetic data drawn from the joint is a real limitation; the paper appropriately addresses this with experiments suggesting that conclusions drawn from synthetic data are predictive for real data. It would help to emphasize this caveat and the scope of the predictive claim.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Line 149: Should p(θ, z | y) be p(θ, z, y)?
- Notation: The paper alternates between y and D for the observed data. Please standardize to a single symbol to avoid confusion.
- Reproducibility: The WebPPL code in the supplement should specify (a) the WebPPL version(s) with which it is known to work and (b) clear instructions for integrating it into a WebPPL installation. Even better, consider releasing it as a WebPPL package installable via npm. If possible, provide a similar packaged release for the Stan implementation as well.

In summary, this is a well-motivated and useful contribution. Clarifying the definition of AutoBDMC, smoothing the exposition in Section 3 (especially 3.1), and explicitly stating the novelty of the divergence bound would substantially strengthen the paper. Standardizing notation and improving the code packaging/documentation would further enhance clarity and reproducibility.
