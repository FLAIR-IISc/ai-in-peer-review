- Proposes BREAD: uses forward/reverse AIS to sandwich log marginal likelihood and upper-bound expected Jeffreys divergence between AIS samples and the true posterior.  
- Applies to AIS/SMC-style algorithms and integrates implementations into WebPPL and Stan with experiments and a debugging case study.

Strengths:
- Conceptually appealing and theoretically grounded via stochastic upper/lower bounds.  
- Clear technical result: expected AIS gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence for simulated data.  
- Practical integration and illustrative examples, including a compelling bug-detection anecdote.  
- Useful for informing modeling/algorithmic choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed).

Main weaknesses / concerns:
- Method requires an exact posterior sample for a valid reverse bound, which is impractical for real data.  
- Protocol relies on a representative hyperparameter point estimate η_real, presuming convergence of prior MCMC.  
- Assumes synthetic-data posteriors mimic real-data posterior behavior, which fails under misspecification or weak priors.  
- Optimistic reverse-chain initialization (short MCMC from η_real) may not produce near-exact posterior samples in hard problems.  
- Empirical evidence is limited and may not generalize to challenging, multimodal, or poorly mixing targets.

Relation to prior work / requested comparisons:
- Authors should compare BREAD to standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS, autocorrelation, multichain tests) on hard targets and show where BREAD is superior or more conservative.  
- Need controlled experiments demonstrating BREAD’s advantages in multimodal, heavy-tailed, or ill-conditioned problems.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Sound theoretical derivations for simulated data, but practical protocol weakens formal guarantees; experiments are limited.  
- Clarity: Well written and organized; connections between BDMC, AIS/SMC, and Jeffreys divergence are clear; real-data limitations should be highlighted more.  
- Originality: Novel extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence for AIS samples and PPL integration; real-data protocol is pragmatic but heuristic.  
- Significance: Potentially valuable for AIS/SMC benchmarking and PPL debugging, but broader MCMC convergence impact limited by reliance on synthetic data and approximate reverse samples.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide theory or empirical conditions under which synthetic-data protocol preserves relative algorithm rankings.  
- Run sensitivity analyses over η_real, number of reverse MCMC steps, and model misspecification to quantify impacts.  
- Directly compare BREAD to standard diagnostics on a suite of hard targets and highlight failure modes of the baselines.  
- Assess and report tightness of the expected bound versus true Jeffreys divergence on additional toy and multimodal problems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical foundation linking BDMC gaps to Jeffreys divergence on simulated data.  
- Practical implementations and useful demonstrations, including a real debugging success.  
- Potentially valuable tool for PPL developers and for comparing AIS/SMC algorithmic choices.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Real-data protocol depends on strong, unvalidated assumptions (representative η_real, synthetic-data fidelity, near-exact reverse initialization).  
- Limited empirical validation with few/simple examples and no direct comparisons to common diagnostics.  
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers and marginal-likelihood-based diagnostics rather than general MCMC workflows.
