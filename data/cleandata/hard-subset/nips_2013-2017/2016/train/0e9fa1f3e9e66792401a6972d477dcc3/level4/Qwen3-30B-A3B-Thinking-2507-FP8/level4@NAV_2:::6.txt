

The paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [Grosse et al., 2015] to measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. This builds upon the authors' previous work on BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods and extends it to evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly those that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors integrate their method, called BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), into WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several probabilistic models.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis showing how BDMC can be used to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence. The paper provides a clear derivation of the estimator and demonstrates its accuracy on toy distributions where exact computation is possible. However, the method is limited to simulated data (with exact posterior samples), and the protocol for applying it to real-world data relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters from real data produce representative simulated data. This limitation is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed. The experimental validation on five models shows consistency between real and simulated data, but the paper doesn't explore cases where model misspecification might lead to misleading results.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough explanation of the theoretical background and methodological details. The connection between BDMC and the new application to MCMC inference is well-motivated, and the experimental section provides sufficient details for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate the consistency between real and simulated data results.

Originality: While the core technique (BDMC) is from prior work [Grosse et al., 2015], the application to evaluating MCMC inference represents a novel use case. However, the paper does not demonstrate significant conceptual innovation in how the technique is applied, as the extension to MCMC inference follows naturally from the BDMC framework. The authors acknowledge related work [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016] that also uses BDMC for similar purposes, noting that their method differs in that it uses the true posterior for the reverse chain rather than a reference distribution. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from this prior work in terms of the technical contribution.

Significance: The work addresses an important practical problem in probabilistic inference—evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples, particularly in black-box inference systems. The method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which could be valuable for probabilistic programming users. However, the practical utility is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained (i.e., simulated data), and the authors' protocol for real-world data remains heuristic. The paper demonstrates this technique's utility in guiding model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization), but the significance of these findings for the broader field is somewhat limited, as they represent incremental improvements rather than fundamental advances.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper provides a theoretically sound extension of an existing method to a practically important problem, demonstrates the technique's effectiveness on multiple models, and integrates it into widely used probabilistic programming frameworks. The work has clear practical value for probabilistic programming users who need to assess the reliability of inference results.

Arguments against acceptance: The technical contribution is incremental rather than transformative, as it applies an existing technique to a new domain rather than introducing a novel method. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from related work [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016], and the method's applicability is limited to simulated data (with the real-world protocol being heuristic). The significance of the findings for the broader machine learning community is modest, as the method doesn't fundamentally advance the state of the art in MCMC inference.

Overall, while the paper presents a useful tool for practitioners of probabilistic programming, its technical contribution is limited to an application of existing methodology, and the significance of the work is modest for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The paper would be more appropriate for a venue focused on probabilistic programming or MCMC methods rather than a general machine learning conference.
