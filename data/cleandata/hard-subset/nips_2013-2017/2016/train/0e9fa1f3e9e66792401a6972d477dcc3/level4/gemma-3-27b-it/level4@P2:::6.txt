This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the true posterior and the approximate samples. BDMC, originally proposed for estimating the log marginal likelihood, is refined to provide an upper bound on this divergence, applicable to algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors integrate their method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p], and validate it on several models and datasets. They further demonstrate its utility by analyzing the effectiveness of different model representations within these frameworks.

The work builds on a strong foundation of existing techniques in probabilistic inference, notably AIS [Nea01] and SMC [MDJ06], and extends the BDMC framework introduced by Grosse et al. [GGA15]. The authors correctly identify the challenge of validating MCMC inference and propose a method for quantifying the quality of approximate posterior samples. The connection to existing work on stochastic bounds [BGS15] and related approaches like Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka [CTM16] is also acknowledged. The application to two widely used probabilistic programming languages is a positive aspect, potentially providing practical tools for users to assess inference reliability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian inference: reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples obtained via MCMC.
*   **Rigorous Approach:** Leveraging BDMC provides a theoretically grounded method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence, offering more than just heuristic diagnostics.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan enhances its accessibility and potential impact.
*   **Validation:** The experiments on various models and the comparison of different model representations demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.
* **Debugging Application:** The discovery of a bug in WebPPL highlights BREAD’s potential beyond model evaluation to code verification.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the implementation and application are valuable, the core idea builds directly upon existing work and does not introduce fundamentally new theoretical concepts. The paper primarily demonstrates an application of previously published techniques.
*   **Applicability Restrictions:** The method is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, limiting its broader applicability to all MCMC methods.
*   **Simulated Data Dependency:** The rigorous bounds rely on simulated data, requiring a protocol (parametric bootstrap-like) to extend it to real-world datasets, which introduces potential complexities and limitations.
*   **Computational Cost:** BDMC and AIS can be computationally intensive, potentially limiting the scalability of BREAD to very complex models.
* **Presentation:** The paper would benefit from a clearer separation of the theoretical contributions from the implementation details and experimental results. The narrative sometimes feels dense and assumes a high level of prior knowledge.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a valuable and rigorous tool for evaluating MCMC inference, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field.
*   Practical implementations in widely used probabilistic programming languages enhance its impact.
*   Demonstrates the utility of BREAD through insightful experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks significant theoretical novelty. The core ideas are extensions of existing techniques.
*   The applicability is limited to a specific class of inference algorithms.
*   Potentially high computational cost.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid engineering effort to apply existing techniques to a practical problem. While the theoretical novelty is limited, the implementation and validation are well-executed. Given the lack of significant theoretical breakthrough, the paper may be better suited for a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, rather than a main NIPS conference. However, the practical utility and rigorous approach are commendable.

**Recommendation:** Soft Reject. While valuable, the paper’s incremental nature and limited novelty do not meet the high bar for acceptance at NIPS. Encouraging submission to a more specialized workshop is recommended.
