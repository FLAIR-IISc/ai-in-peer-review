The authors propose a generic method for evaluating MCMC transition operators based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a recently proposed technique. BDMC performs two annealed importance sampling (AIS) sweeps – analogous to those used in Tempered Transitions (TT) [Neal, Statistics and Computing, 1996] – but reinterprets the accumulated probabilities as lower and upper bounds on the marginal likelihood. This paper proposes using the gap between these bounds as a performance measure for MCMC operators, presenting implementations within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, alongside a protocol for posterior inference quality evaluation.

The core strength of this work lies in the simplicity and potential impact of using BDMC to assess MCMC convergence. The method is easily implemented, and its application within probabilistic programming systems, where it could be widely adopted, is particularly promising. However, the novelty and technical depth of the contribution require further consideration.

While the authors acknowledge a related arXiv submission on BDMC and wisely dedicate space to its description, this emphasis leads to a somewhat rushed presentation of their contributions in sections 3.1 and 3.2, and a decline in writing clarity. This impacts the understanding of certain technical aspects (detailed below). Despite these issues, the observation that the difference between the lower and upper bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence is insightful, though not entirely novel. 

A significant omission is the lack of citation to Radford Neal’s foundational work on Tempered Transitions [Statistics and Computing, 1996]. This omission extends to the BDMC manuscript itself, raising concerns about the authors’ awareness of the connection. TT employs similar reverse “heating” and forward “annealing” sweeps as BDMC, but aims to improve mixing in multimodal distributions by tempering the transition move. Critically, the TT acceptance ratio aligns precisely with the ratio of the stochastic lower and upper bounds in BDMC. Section 3.3 of Neal’s paper interprets this ratio as bounding log Z<sub>T</sub> - log Z<sub>1</sub> via thermodynamic integration, accepting when these bounds converge. 

The connection to TT also raises questions about the attention given to the choice of β<sub>t</sub> values. TT’s performance is sensitive to the step size Δβ = β<sub>t+1</sub> - β<sub>t</sub>; too large steps reduce acceptance, while too small steps waste computation.  The BDMC bound difference will similarly depend on Δβ, providing a clear intuition: if the bounds are not sufficiently close, a smaller Δβ is necessary. A reliable comparison of MCMC operators likely requires the gap between the log upper and lower bounds to be less than approximately 1 nat. Exploring a schedule for iteratively decreasing Δβ based on this gap – a potential extension of the “AutoBDMC” functionality – would be valuable. 

The experiments could be more thorough. A systematic study of how the lower and upper bounds vary with the step size would significantly strengthen the analysis. The current description of AutoBDMC is also unclear. Is it solely about calculating the bound difference for convergence assessment, or does it encompass the hyperparameter scheme in section 3.2? The documentation of the AutoBDMC parameterization (specifically, the role of the “steps” parameter in relation to β<sub>t</sub>) could be more explicit. In figures 2 and 3, it is unclear whether “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” relates to the number of steps used *within* the BDMC evaluation itself. 

Specific clarity issues include:

*   The definition of $\vec{v}$ as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise. The authors likely mean ${z{1:T-1}, \theta{1:T-1}}$, but this is ambiguous given the two AIS sweeps. The inconsistent use of “rev”/“fwd” versus “for”/“back” further contributes to the confusion.
*   Equations (5-7) and (9-11) lack definition of the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ used in the expectation. Are the authors intending to write E[log $\hat{R}$<sub>rev</sub>] and E[log $\hat{R}$<sub>fwd</sub>]?
*   Equation (6) is currently nonsensical due to identical distributions within the KL divergence. Based on equations (4) and (10), the right-hand side should likely be p(z, θ | y) q<sub>back</sub>(v | z, θ, y).
*   The meaning of AutoBDMC remains ambiguously defined.

Regarding the experiments: 

*   The choice of β<sub>t</sub> is unclear. The code appendix reveals `var step = 1/options.steps`, suggesting AutoBDMC is parameterized by the number of steps T to go from β<sub>T</sub> = 1 to β<sub>1</sub> = 1 / T. This should be stated explicitly.
*   The acronym RAIS, and its variants (u)c(R)AIS, require definition in the figure captions.
*   Increasing the size of the axes and tick labels in the figures would improve readability.

Finally, some minor errors exist:

*   Two closing parentheses are missing in the D<sub>KL</sub> expression for D<sub>JS</sub>.
*   The NUTS citation incorrectly lists "Homan" instead of "Hoffman."



Despite these criticisms, this is a worthwhile paper. The proposed method has the potential to be a valuable tool for assessing MCMC convergence. Camera-ready revisions addressing the clarity issues, the discussion of related work (specifically Tempered Transitions), and a more thorough experimental analysis of the step size Δβ would significantly strengthen the manuscript and could incline me towards acceptance.
