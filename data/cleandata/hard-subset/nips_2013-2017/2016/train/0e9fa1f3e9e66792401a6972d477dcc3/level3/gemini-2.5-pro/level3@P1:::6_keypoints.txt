- Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC by using forward and reverse AIS to evaluate MCMC approximate posterior samples.
- Main theoretical claim: the gap between BDMC log-marginal bounds upper-bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence between forward AIS samples and the true posterior.
- Implements the diagnostic in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates practical utility.
- Rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, so they hold only for simulated data.
- For real data the authors propose a heuristic: infer hyperparameters, simulate data, then apply BREAD, which weakens formal guarantees.
- Experiments and a matrix factorization case study show BREAD can guide modeling choices and debugging (it uncovered a WebPPL bug).
- Paper is technically sound; derivation is a correct extension of Grosse et al. (2015).
- Writing and exposition are clear and the background material is accessible and well organized.
- Primary originality is limited: method is an incremental reinterpretation of BDMC rather than a novel technique.
- Much of the contribution is engineering/implementation work, making it more of a systems/application paper.
- Significance is practical for probabilistic programming developers and algorithm debugging, but limited for practitioners with single real datasets.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, provides a principled diagnostic (on simulated data), solid implementations, good experiments, and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient methodological novelty, focus on application/engineering, and lack of rigorous applicability to real-world data.
- Overall assessment: solid, useful work but borderline for a top-tier conference due to limited novelty and practical constraints.
