Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL) between the approximate sampling distribution produced by AIS/SMC-style samplers and the true posterior. The core idea is to run AIS forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data. The gap between the reverse upper bound and the forward lower bound on log marginal likelihood yields an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between q (the marginal distribution of the AIS sample after T steps) and p (the true posterior). The authors integrate this into WebPPL and Stan, propose a protocol to assess real-data performance via fitted hyperparameters and simulation, show toy examples where the bound is tight, validate behavior across several models, compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations, and demonstrate utility for debugging by uncovering a bug in WebPPL.

This work builds on BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse, Ghahramani & Adams 2015), AIS (Neal 2001) and reverse AIS/Jarzynski equalities, and relates to convergence diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin 1992; Brooks & Gelman 1998; Cowles & Carlin 1996) and sample quality metrics (Gorham & Mackey 2015). A closely related contemporaneous effort is Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) on “subjective divergence,” which this paper discusses. Importantly, there is prior work on simulation-based validation of MCMC that should be cited and contrasted: Geweke (2004) “Getting it right” and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006) “Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles.” Both advocate validating inference by running on data generated from the prior; the present paper offers a quantitative, divergence-based approach in that same spirit.

Strengths
- Timely and useful idea: a principled, quantitative measure of posterior sample quality, especially valuable in PPLs where black-box inference obscures failures.
- Clear theoretical link from BDMC log-ML bounds to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between q and p; analysis uses only AIS factorization assumptions.
- Practical impact: integration with WebPPL and Stan, a pragmatic real-data protocol via fitted hyperparameters, and compelling case studies (matrix factorization representation choices).
- Diagnostic utility: the method helped uncover a real bug in WebPPL.
- Experiments demonstrate consistency between simulated and real data behavior across diverse models.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees require an exact posterior sample; thus rigorous bounds apply only to simulated data. The fitted-hyperparameter trick for real data is heuristic (though empirically supported).
- The bound is in expectation; finite-sample concentration and reporting practices (e.g., heavy-tailed AIS log-weights) are not fully addressed. Guidance on replication and averaging is limited.
- Section 3.1 is hard to follow: q(·) is used before being defined. Notational choices and relegation of key derivations to the supplement impede readability.
- Some experimental choices (e.g., quartiles rather than means; sensitivity to annealing schedule, number of intermediate temperatures, and transition kernels) deserve more systematic analysis.
- Minor presentation issues (variable split, typo).

Quality
The theoretical claim (expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence via the BDMC gap) is correct and crisply derived. The toy cases demonstrating tightness are reassuring. The empirical validations are careful and relevant, though results depend on AIS design choices that are not fully explored. The real-data protocol is plausible and the sensitivity study over MCMC steps for hyperparameters is a nice touch.

Clarity
Generally well-written, but Section 3.1 needs clearer notation and a more self-contained derivation. Important steps are pushed to the supplement; the main text should provide a complete, step-by-step recipe for practitioners. The paper should explicitly define qfwd(xT) as “the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler’s final state after T steps,” and define qrev(v|xT) before use. Splitting (z, θ) throughout adds clutter; a unified latent x would streamline exposition. Fix the typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis). Also clarify that the paper bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence, not the Jensen–Shannon divergence.

Originality
The adaptation of BDMC from log-ML to bounding posterior-sample divergence is novel and nontrivial, and the expected bound on Jeffreys divergence is a clear conceptual advance for diagnosing MCMC quality in PPLs. Relation to CTM (2016) is appropriate; please make the differences and practical tradeoffs even more explicit.

Significance
High potential impact. Providing a quantitative, implementation-agnostic measure of posterior sample quality for AIS/SMC-style samplers could influence PPL practice, model representation choices, and debugging workflows. While applicability is constrained by the need for exact posterior samples (hence simulated data), the proposed real-data protocol is pragmatic and likely to see use.

Detailed suggestions
- Cite and discuss Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006), comparing their simulation-based checks to BREAD’s divergence-based guarantee.
- Section 3.1:
  - Define qfwd(xT), qfwd(v|xT), and qrev(v|xT) before first use; state clearly that q(·) refers to the marginal of the AIS sampler after T steps.
  - Move key derivation steps (Eqs. 6–11) motivation and factorization into the main text; add a numbered algorithm summarizing forward and reverse AIS and the computation of the gap.
- Provide practical guidance on:
  - Choosing temperature schedules and number of intermediate distributions.
  - Number of AIS chains needed for a stable bound; discuss variance and whether averaging log-weights across chains is justified for the bound.
  - Sensitivity of the bound to transition-kernel mixing and annealing choices.
- Experiments:
  - Report computational cost (wall-clock) for forward and reverse AIS and how it compares to baseline MCMC runs.
  - Where possible, add one or two cases with known ground-truth Jeffreys divergence to further calibrate tightness.
- Presentation:
  - Use a single latent x instead of (z, θ) unless there is a compelling reason to separate them.
  - Fix the typo at line 165.

Pros
- Conceptually strong, rigorous connection from BDMC to sample-quality assessment.
- Practical integration into two PPLs; demonstrated debugging value.
- Useful guidance for model representation choices; clear and relevant experiments.

Cons
- Rigorous guarantees require simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Bound only in expectation; limited discussion of concentration and stability.
- Some clarity issues in Section 3.1 and reliance on supplement for key derivations.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The idea is sound and useful, the execution largely convincing, and the potential impact on PPL practice is substantial. Addressing the clarity and related-work issues, and adding more practitioner guidance on AIS design and bound stability, would strengthen the final version.
