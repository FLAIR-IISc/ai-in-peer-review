## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper presents a compelling extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference, specifically by providing upper bounds on the KL divergence between the true posterior and the approximate distribution generated by the inference algorithm. The core idea – leveraging the strengths of BDMC to quantify inference reliability – is both timely and important, given the increasing use of “black-box” probabilistic programming systems where assessing convergence and accuracy can be challenging. The work builds on [GGA15] and extends it to provide a novel estimator for this crucial divergence measure.

The authors effectively contextualize their work within the broader landscape of MCMC diagnostics and model evaluation. They correctly identify the limitations of traditional convergence diagnostics and highlight the need for quantitative measures of approximation quality. The related work section is thorough, accurately placing their method alongside recent efforts like [CTM16] and addressing subtle differences in approach. 

A significant strength of this paper lies in its practical implementation. Integrating BREAD into both WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the method’s versatility and accessibility. The validation experiments, including the analysis of model representations in both frameworks, provide tangible evidence of BREAD’s utility in guiding modeling decisions. Furthermore, the authors proactively address potential issues with applying BREAD to real-world data by proposing a protocol for simulating data from fitted hyperparameters, and validating its consistency. Discovering and reporting a bug in WebPPL is a bonus, showcasing the method's potential as a robust testing tool. 

While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of BREAD, particularly as it scales to more complex models. A clearer articulation of the assumptions underlying the applicability of their method to algorithms beyond AIS/SMC would also be helpful. However, these are minor points.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference: assessing the reliability of MCMC.
*   Offers a novel and theoretically grounded approach using BDMC.
*   Demonstrates practical utility through implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages.
*   Provides compelling experimental validation and insights into model representation choices.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost could be a limitation for very complex models (though this is acknowledged).
*   Generalizability to algorithms outside AIS/SMC could be further clarified.



**Overall:** This is a well-executed and significant contribution to the field. The method is sound, the implementation is impressive, and the results are insightful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
