- Paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) with annealed importance sampling (AIS) to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between AIS sample distribution and the true posterior via the forward–reverse log-marginal-likelihood gap (BREAD).  
- Implementations in Stan and WebPPL and experiments show use for simulated-data validation, a protocol to transfer diagnostics to real data, and guidance for modeling choices and debugging.  
- The central derivation that the forward–reverse gap upper bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence is sound and practically useful.  
- The simulated-data-to-real-data protocol is well motivated and empirically supported across five models.  
- Integrations and the debugging example demonstrate clear practical value for probabilistic programming.  
- Technical novelty is modest relative to prior BDMC/AIS work, but the reframing as an expected bound on Jeffreys divergence is a neat, not previously explicit contribution.  
- Relation to Neal’s tempered transitions and other thermodynamic approaches should be discussed more concretely to clarify differences in assumptions and guarantees.  
- Major concern: the bound holds only in expectation, but the paper lacks guidance on estimating E[gap] uncertainty (variance, CIs, multiple runs), so practitioners cannot judge when a small observed gap is meaningful.  
- Experimental details critical to AIS performance are under-specified (β schedule, number/placement of temperatures, Δβ, number/type of transitions per temperature), undermining reproducibility and fairness of comparisons.  
- Use of fitted-hyperparameter reverse-chain initialization breaks exactness of guarantees; more stress tests (misspecification, heavy-tailed priors) are needed to support robustness claims.  
- Some derivations and equations (Eqs. 6–11) contain ambiguities/typos and may misstate KL arguments; the AIS weight/ratio estimator should be explicitly defined.  
- Notation is ambiguous (definition of v, forward vs reverse joint/conditional distributions); forward/reverse labels should be made consistent and explicit.  
- Acronyms and variant labels (RAIS, (u)/(c)-(R)AIS) are not defined in figure captions; clarify what "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" refers to within AIS.  
- Figures have small axes/tick labels and unclear legends; improve readability and labeling.  
- Typos and citation errors (e.g., "Homan" vs. "Hoffman") and mismatched parentheses in divergence expressions must be fixed.  
- Significance: a practical, principled diagnostic for black-box MCMC is valuable—useful for model representation choices, catching implementation bugs, and tuning algorithms—though applicability is limited to AIS/SMC-like samplers.  
- Pros summarized: useful BDMC reframing, practical Stan/WebPPL integrations, sound simulated-to-real protocol, empirical validations, and model-representation guidance.  
- Cons summarized: expectation-only bound without uncertainty quantification, under-specified AIS design choices, weakened guarantees from approximate reverse initialization, and clarity/derivation issues.  
- Suggested improvements: provide an adaptive "AutoBDMC" schedule refinement procedure (e.g., bisection or ESS/KLD-based placement) and wall-clock normalization.  
- Suggested improvements: quantify uncertainty of estimated expected gap via repeated runs, CIs, or concentration bounds and discuss averaging vs. reporting quantiles.  
- Suggested improvements: fully detail AIS configurations (temperature counts/placement, transitions per temperature, HMC/NUTS params, number of chains) and explore schedule sensitivity.  
- Suggested improvements: deepen related-work discussion on tempered transitions, thermodynamic integration, and subjective divergence, and strengthen misspecification/robustness experiments for real-data protocol.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept conditional on addressing clarity issues, systematically documenting AIS design choices, and providing an adaptive/uncertainty-aware protocol; reviewer confidence: moderate.
