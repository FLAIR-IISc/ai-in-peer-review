- Proposes BREAD, a novel method to quantitatively assess MCMC reliability (e.g., AIS, SMC) by extending the BDMC framework.  
- Core idea: produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to derive an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors.  
- Strength: offers a principled, theoretically grounded diagnostic for MCMC convergence, addressing a difficult and important problem.  
- Theoretical contribution: elegant connection between BDMC bounds and Jeffreys divergence.  
- Practical application relies on performing diagnostics on synthetic data due to BDMC's need for an exact posterior sample.  
- Issue 1: Requires a representative hyperparameter η_real from prior MCMC on real data, introducing circularity and assuming those runs have converged.  
- Issue 2: Assumes synthetic posterior convergence mirrors real-data posterior convergence, an unjustified assumption especially under model misspecification.  
- Issue 3: Assumes initializing chains from η_real quickly yields a proxy for an exact sample, which is optimistic.  
- These intertwined assumptions undermine validity in complex, multimodal, or poorly specified scenarios where the diagnostic is most needed.  
- Empirical validation is limited to simple models and does not demonstrate robustness in challenging settings.  
- Recommendations: provide rigorous justification for reliability in difficult cases (e.g., multimodal posteriors) and include comparative studies against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS).  
- Conclusion: Without addressing these foundational concerns and comparative evaluations, the practical utility of BREAD remains unclear.
