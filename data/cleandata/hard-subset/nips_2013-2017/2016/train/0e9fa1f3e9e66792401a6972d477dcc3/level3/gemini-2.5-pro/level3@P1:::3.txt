### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a method, named Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15]. BDMC uses Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both a forward direction (prior to posterior) and a reverse direction (posterior to prior) to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

Since the reverse pass requires an exact sample from the true posterior, which is unavailable for real data, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, perform approximate inference on the real data to find plausible hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these hyperparameters, which provides an exact posterior sample by construction; finally, run BREAD on this simulated data to assess inference quality. The authors implement their method in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference on several models and to compare the performance of different model parameterizations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound for the most part, building directly upon the established theory of AIS and BDMC. The derivation showing that the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is a key contribution. The experiments effectively validate the proposed protocol for real-world data, showing that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on the actual data. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a strong testament to the method's utility as a diagnostic tool.

However, the paper's quality is diminished by several clarity issues and a lack of rigor in the experimental description. A critical parameter in any AIS-based method is the annealing schedule (the sequence of inverse temperatures, β_t). This is under-discussed. The choice of schedule significantly impacts the tightness of the bounds and the rate of convergence, making it essential for any comparison between different MCMC operators or model representations. The paper does not specify how this schedule was chosen in the experiments, which compromises the reproducibility and interpretation of the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the clarity degrades significantly in Section 3.1, which contains the main theoretical argument. This section feels rushed and contains several ambiguities and errors that make the derivations difficult to follow.
*   The notation is sometimes loose. For example, the vector `v` is defined as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage," which is ambiguous given that there are two separate AIS sweeps (forward and reverse).
*   The estimator for the partition function ratio, which is the random variable inside the expectation in equations (6)-(11), is not explicitly defined in terms of the sampling distributions `qfwd` and `qrev`. This makes it hard to verify the derivation.
*   Equation (6) appears to be incorrect as written, as the arguments to the KL divergence are identical. It seems the authors intended to relate the expectation of the log importance weight to a KL divergence, but the expression is flawed.
*   The writing uses inconsistent terminology (e.g., "fwd"/"rev" vs. "forward"/"reverse") which can be confusing.
*   The figures have presentation issues, such as axis and tick labels that are too small to be easily legible. Acronyms used in figure captions (e.g., RAIS, ucRAIS) are not defined.

**Originality:** The paper builds heavily on the existing BDMC framework [GGA15]. The main novel contribution is the observation that the gap between the BDMC bounds can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence. While this is a nice and useful insight, it is a relatively modest extension of the prior work. The proposed protocol for applying the method to real-world data is a practical contribution but is a heuristic procedure based on established ideas like the parametric bootstrap.

Furthermore, the paper misses a key piece of related work: Radford Neal's 1996 paper on "Sampling from multimodal distributions using tempered transitions." This work discusses the use of heating and cooling (annealing) paths and relates the acceptance probability of a tempered transition to the ratio of partition functions, an idea closely related to the non-equilibrium physics connection underlying AIS and BDMC. A discussion of this foundational work would better situate the paper in its proper context.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high. The probabilistic programming community is in great need of general, reliable, and automated tools for diagnosing the quality of black-box inference. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality that goes beyond heuristic convergence diagnostics like R-hat. If integrated into major PPLs, it could become a standard tool for both developers (for debugging and testing) and users (for gaining confidence in their results). The experiments comparing model representations demonstrate its value for guiding modeling decisions.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Proposes a simple, intuitive, and much-needed quantitative metric for MCMC sample quality.
*   The method is general and applicable to any inference scheme based on AIS or SMC.
*   The implementation and validation in Stan and WebPPL demonstrate its practical utility.
*   The use-case of comparing model representations is compelling and addresses a common challenge for practitioners.
*   The work has high potential impact for the growing field of probabilistic programming.

**Arguments against:**
*   The core technical novelty beyond existing work on BDMC is limited.
*   The paper fails to cite and discuss highly relevant prior work (Neal, 1996).
*   The central theoretical section (3.1) is poorly written, containing ambiguous notation and apparent errors in key equations, hindering understanding and trust in the result.
*   Crucial experimental details, particularly the choice of annealing schedule, are missing, which harms reproducibility and the validity of comparisons.

### Recommendation and Justification

I am recommending **Marginal Accept**. The paper presents a valuable and potentially high-impact idea for the probabilistic programming community. The proposed diagnostic tool addresses a real and important problem. However, its acceptance is currently hampered by significant issues in clarity, a failure to cite key related work, and a lack of experimental detail. The errors and ambiguities in Section 3.1 are particularly concerning and must be fixed. I believe the paper could be acceptable for NIPS if the authors can thoroughly address these points in a revision. The core idea is strong enough to warrant publication, provided the presentation and experimental rigor are brought up to the conference standard.

### Detailed Comments for Authors
1.  **Related Work:** Please add a discussion of Neal's (1996) work on Tempered Transitions and its connection to estimating partition function ratios via annealing.
2.  **Clarity in Section 3.1:** This section requires a major revision. Please:
    *   Provide an explicit definition for the estimators whose expectation you are taking in equations (6) and (9).
    *   Correct equation (6). The arguments to the KL divergence should be `qfwd(v, xT)` and `pT(xT) qrev(v|xT) / Z_T`, or a similar correct formulation.
    *   Clarify the definition of `v` to avoid ambiguity.
3.  **Experimental Details:** Please explicitly state how the annealing schedule (the `β_t` values) was chosen for your experiments. Was it a simple linear schedule? How was the number of steps determined? This is crucial for interpreting the results, especially the comparisons in Figure 2.
4.  **Typos/Minor Issues:**
    *   The citation for NUTS should be Hoffman and Gelman (2014), not Homan and Gelman.
    *   There is a missing closing parenthesis in the definition of Jeffreys divergence in Section 3.
    *   Please increase the font size of axis labels and ticks in the figures for better readability.
    *   Define all acronyms used in figure captions.
