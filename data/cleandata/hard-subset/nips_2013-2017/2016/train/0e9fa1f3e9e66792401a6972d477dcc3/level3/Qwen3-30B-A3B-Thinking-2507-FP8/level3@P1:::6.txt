

This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors derive an estimator that upper bounds this divergence in expectation, which they term BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing). They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on various models, and demonstrate its utility in comparing model representations and debugging inference implementations.

The paper builds directly on the BDMC framework introduced by Grosse et al. [GGA15], which provides stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihoods by running AIS chains in both forward and reverse directions. The key technical contribution is adapting this framework to bound the Jeffreys divergence for posterior approximation quality rather than marginal likelihood estimation. The authors show that the BDMC bounds can be interpreted as upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples. They also propose a protocol for using BREAD to evaluate inference quality on real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

The paper demonstrates a clear application of established techniques rather than introducing novel theoretical contributions. While the implementation in WebPPL and Stan is well-executed, the core method is a straightforward extension of BDMC. The authors acknowledge related work [CTM16] which similarly uses BDMC for posterior evaluation, noting that their approach differs in using exact posterior samples for reverse initialization rather than a reference distribution. However, this difference does not constitute a significant theoretical advance.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound and well-executed in its implementation. The validation experiments on simulated data and the comparison of model representations in WebPPL and Stan are thorough. The debugging example in WebPPL demonstrates practical utility, though this is more of a byproduct than a core contribution. The paper correctly acknowledges the limitations of their approach (requiring exact posterior samples on simulated data) and proposes a reasonable protocol for real-world application.

Clarity is generally good, with clear explanations of the technical framework and well-organized experiments. The presentation of the BREAD method and its integration into probabilistic programming languages is accessible to readers familiar with MCMC and probabilistic programming.

Originality is the paper's main weakness. The core idea is a direct application of BDMC to a new problem domain, with no substantial theoretical innovation. The paper does not advance the state of the art in MCMC or posterior evaluation beyond what was already possible with BDMC. The authors cite relevant prior work, but the contribution falls short of the novel theoretical or methodological advances expected at NIPS.

Significance is limited. While the method provides a useful diagnostic tool for probabilistic programming users, it addresses a niche problem within a specific context (simulated data with exact posterior samples). The paper does not demonstrate how this method advances the broader field of probabilistic inference or enables new applications that were previously impossible. The connection to model representation choices is interesting but does not represent a fundamental advance in inference methodology.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Well-executed implementation in two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Provides a useful diagnostic tool for practitioners evaluating posterior inference quality
- Demonstrates practical utility through debugging and model representation analysis
- Clear presentation of the method and validation experiments

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Core technique is not novel; represents an extension of existing work rather than a new contribution
- Lacks theoretical innovation or significant methodological advancement
- The problem addressed (evaluating MCMC inference quality) is important, but the solution is not sufficiently novel for NIPS
- The contribution would be more appropriate for a workshop on MCMC or probabilistic programming

The paper's main contribution is a practical implementation of a known technique for a specific application. While technically sound and well-executed, it lacks the originality and significance expected for a NIPS publication. The work would be better suited for a workshop on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, where the focus is on practical implementation rather than theoretical advancement. The authors have made a useful contribution to the toolchain for probabilistic programming, but it does not represent a significant scientific advance that would warrant publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a more compelling demonstration of how this approach enables new insights or applications that were previously unattainable.
