This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for quantitatively evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally developed by Grosse et al. (2015) for estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors show that by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains on simulated data—both in the forward direction (from prior to posterior) and the reverse direction (from an exact posterior sample back to the prior)—one can obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key insight of this work is that the expected gap between these bounds serves as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the true posterior and the approximate distribution produced by the forward AIS chain. This provides a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality. The authors integrate this method into two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility for validating inference and guiding modeling decisions. The problem of assessing MCMC convergence and sample quality is a long-standing challenge in Bayesian statistics, with most existing methods being heuristic (e.g., Gelman-Rubin diagnostics). This work provides a more rigorous alternative, building on recent advances in Monte Carlo methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and highly important problem: how can we trust the output of MCMC-based inference systems? This is especially critical for probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) which aim to automate inference for non-experts. BREAD provides a powerful and automated tool for developers and sophisticated users to rigorously benchmark and debug inference algorithms.
2.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The extension of the BDMC framework from estimating a scalar value (the marginal likelihood) to bounding a divergence between distributions is a clever and significant contribution. The theoretical derivation that connects the "sandwich" gap to the Jeffreys divergence is clean and insightful, providing a well-motivated metric for sample quality that accounts for both mode-covering (via DKL(p||q)) and mode-seeking (via DKL(q||p)) behavior.
3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the expected bound on the Jeffreys divergence is clear and well-explained. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors wisely address potential pitfalls of the method, such as its reliance on simulated data, and propose a sensible protocol for applying it in the context of real-world datasets. The validation experiments in Section 5.1 successfully show that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on real data, strengthening the practical case for the proposed protocol.
4.  **Practical Utility:** The implementation and evaluation within two very different PPLs (WebPPL with M-H and Stan with HMC/NUTS) demonstrates the method's generality. The case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is an excellent example of how BREAD can provide concrete, quantitative guidance for making modeling choices that impact inference performance. Furthermore, the anecdote about using BREAD to uncover a bug in WebPPL's sampler is a powerful testament to its value as a tool for ensuring correctness.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The primary limitation, clearly acknowledged by the authors, is that rigorous guarantees hold only for simulated data where an exact posterior sample is accessible. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a pragmatic and well-justified heuristic, but it ultimately lacks the rigor of the simulated-data setting. It relies on the assumption that the model is well-specified, as performance on simulated data may not be representative of performance on real data under model misspecification (as noted in Appendix D).
2.  **Applicability to Standard MCMC:** The method is directly applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC that can be framed as importance sampling in an extended space. It is not directly applicable to standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs. While the authors argue that insights gained from evaluating AIS can inform the design of general MCMC algorithms (since they share components like transition operators), this is an indirect form of evaluation. This distinction could be emphasized more clearly.
3.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires running many AIS chains, each of which can be computationally intensive, in two separate directions. A brief discussion of the computational cost and the trade-off between the number of AIS steps/chains and the quality of the divergence bound would be a valuable addition for practitioners looking to adopt this method.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed and important piece of work. It tackles a timely and difficult problem with a novel and elegant idea, provides a sound theoretical analysis, and demonstrates its practical value through extensive experiments and integration into major PPLs. The weaknesses are inherent to the problem domain rather than flaws in the work itself, and the authors are transparent about these limitations. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. It provides a much-needed tool for increasing the reliability and trustworthiness of MCMC methods and probabilistic programming systems.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
