- Proposes BREAD, a method for quantitatively measuring reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference via reverse annealing.  
- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) by running forward and reverse AIS to produce stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood.  
- Main technical result: the expected gap between AIS bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and forward AIS sample distribution.  
- Provides a principled, quantitative metric for sample quality rather than heuristic diagnostics.  
- Unlike classical diagnostics, BREAD can give a positive certificate of convergence on simulated data.  
- Built on AIS and BDMC theory and explicitly distinguished from concurrent related work (CTM16).  
- Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and evaluated on multiple models.  
- Significance: addresses a major need for reliable, automated posterior-quality diagnostics in probabilistic programming.  
- Originality: novel and insightful use of bidirectional log-ML gap to bound posterior divergence.  
- Technical quality: derivation appears correct, limitations are acknowledged, and the real-data protocol is pragmatic and empirically validated.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with clear background, method development, and effective figures.  
- Practical impact: tools integrated into popular PPLs; useful for guiding modeling choices and found a real WebPPL bug.  
- Limitation: rigorous guarantees apply only to simulated data; the real-data heuristic could fail under severe model misspecification.  
- Applicability: method targets importance-sampling-based extended-state algorithms (AIS/SMC) and is not a general diagnostic for all MCMC samplers.  
- Computational cost: bidirectional AIS is expensive; paper should discuss overhead relative to the original inference task.  
- Analysis limitation: theoretical bound applies to individual chain pairs, preventing averaging of importance weights for lower-variance log-ML estimates—an avenue for future work.  
- Overall: high-quality, useful contribution whose strengths outweigh minor weaknesses; reviewer recommendation: Accept.
