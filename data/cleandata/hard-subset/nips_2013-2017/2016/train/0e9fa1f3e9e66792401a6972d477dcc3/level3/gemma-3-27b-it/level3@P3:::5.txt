This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference by using bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. This builds upon previous work on estimating marginal likelihoods with BDMC [GGA15] and extends it to provide a diagnostic for MCMC convergence, particularly valuable in “black box” inference settings. The approach applies primarily to AIS and SMC samplers, leveraging the ability to generate exact samples from the target distribution in simulated data scenarios.

The core idea is sound: providing a theoretically justified diagnostic for MCMC is a significant challenge, and bounding the divergence offers a principled way to assess sample quality. The application to probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan is also practical and addresses a real need – users of these tools often lack reliable methods to determine if results are trustworthy. The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing MCMC diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and related approaches for estimating marginal likelihoods [Nea01, Jar97]. The recognition of independent, concurrent work [CTM16] demonstrates awareness of the broader research landscape.

However, despite the interesting premise, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that significantly limit its practical applicability and theoretical validity. The most serious issue is the reliance on a pre-existing, converged MCMC run to obtain a representative hyperparameter value (η_real). This fundamentally undermines the diagnostic’s purpose; if a prior MCMC run *has already converged*, there’s no need for a convergence diagnostic. Assuming a single summary statistic is sufficient from this initial run is also questionable – particularly for complex posteriors. 

Further, the assumption that the synthetic-data posterior possesses similar convergence properties to the original posterior is a major flaw. This is especially problematic under model misspecification, where the synthetic data, generated from estimated parameters, may not accurately reflect the complexities of the real-world data. The claim that starting from η_real allows the MCMC to quickly produce an almost exact sample is optimistic and lacks empirical justification. In reality, even with a good starting point, MCMC can struggle with challenging posteriors. These assumptions directly tie the diagnostic’s validity to the very problem it aims to solve – convergence.

The empirical evaluation is limited and unconvincing. The simple examples in Section 5.1, while demonstrating the method’s functionality, do not provide sufficient evidence that the identified issues won't manifest in more complex, real-world scenarios. The finding of a bug in WebPPL is a positive outcome, but feels somewhat incidental to the core contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Attempts a theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic.
*   Application to probabilistic programming languages is relevant.
*   Identified a bug in WebPPL, showcasing practical utility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a pre-converged MCMC run renders the diagnostic largely redundant.
*   Unjustified assumption of similar convergence properties between real and synthetic data.
*   Optimistic and unverified assumption about rapid convergence from η_real.
*   Limited and unconvincing empirical evaluation.
*   Fails to address how the method handles multimodality or poor summary statistics.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is currently **weak reject**. While the core idea is interesting and the effort to integrate the method into probabilistic programming languages is commendable, the fundamental flaws in the assumptions and the limited empirical support significantly outweigh the strengths. 

**Recommendations to the authors:**

1.  **Address the reliance on prior MCMC runs:** The core diagnostic problem is assessing convergence *without* assuming prior knowledge of convergence. Explore alternative approaches that don’t require an initial, converged run.
2.  **Justify the synthetic data assumption:** Provide a theoretical or empirical justification for why the synthetic-data posterior would exhibit similar convergence behavior to the real-world posterior, especially under model misspecification. Consider scenarios where this assumption breaks down and how the diagnostic might perform.
3.  **Investigate multimodality and poor summary statistics:** Explain how the method performs with multimodal posteriors or when using poor summary statistics for hyperparameter estimation. 
4.  **Provide more rigorous simulations:** Conduct simulations with a wider range of model complexities and posterior characteristics to demonstrate the robustness of the diagnostic. Compare BREAD’s performance to standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size) in these simulations.
5.  **Clarify the applicability:** More clearly define the scenarios where BREAD is most likely to be useful and the limitations of the method.
