- Paper extends BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence between target posterior and AIS sampling distribution via ˆB = log ˆR − log ˆRrev and introduces BREAD integrated into WebPPL and Stan.  
- Strength: Clear, principled theoretical extension giving an in-expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence under AIS assumptions.  
- Strength: Practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan with useful case studies (collapsed vs uncollapsed factorization, debugging).  
- Strength: Thoughtful protocol for bridging simulated and real data, with empirical checks (e.g., reverse-chain warmup S).  
- Weakness: Rigorous guarantees require exact posterior samples (simulated data); real-data use is heuristic.  
- Weakness: ˆB upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence only in expectation, so single-run outputs are not guaranteed bounds and uncertainty reporting is unclear.  
- Weakness: Real-data protocol depends on strong, unverified assumptions about fitted hyperparameters, posterior geometry similarity, and near-exact initialization after few MCMC steps.  
- Weakness: Method directly evaluates AIS/SMC samplers and only indirectly assesses standalone MCMC (e.g., NUTS) unless used as AIS kernels.  
- Weakness: Empirical validation is limited and does not probe hard failure modes (multimodality, misspecification, high-dimensional pathologies).  
- Quality: Theoretical derivations appear correct and well-explained; experimental design is sensible but not sufficiently robust.  
- Clarity: Paper is well written but should emphasize "in expectation" vs per-run guarantees and practical implications (CIs, chain counts).  
- Clarity: Provide concise pseudocode for the real-data BREAD protocol with defaults for S, AIS steps/particles, and failure diagnostics.  
- Clarity: Clarify implementation details of AIS kernels in Stan/WebPPL (e.g., use of NUTS/HMC at intermediate temperatures) and computational overhead.  
- Originality: Moderate; natural extension of BDMC with practical PPL integration, but needs clearer distinction from Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016).  
- Significance: Potentially significant for PPL benchmarking and debugging, but limited immediate impact for real-data diagnostics under current assumptions.  
- Suggestions: Test BREAD on challenging posteriors (well-separated multimodal, funnels, heavy tails) and under misspecification to validate the real-data protocol.  
- Suggestions: Compare BREAD to standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, Geweke, Brooks-Gelman) and Stein discrepancies to show complementary or superior detection.  
- Suggestions: Quantify sensitivity to η_real and reverse-chain steps S, propose automated checks (multiple η_real draws, bootstraps).  
- Suggestions: Report computational cost vs diagnostic tightness (time-normalized curves, AIS steps/particles, annealing schedule guidance).  
- Suggestions: When ground truth exists, systematically compare ˆB to true Jeffreys divergence to assess conservativeness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Solid theoretical contribution, practical PPL integration, and promising case studies addressing an important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Guarantees limited to simulated data and expectation bounds; empirical evidence lacks robustness and scope is limited to AIS/SMC.  
- Overall recommendation: Leaning reject; promising but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer real-data guidance, and comparisons to established diagnostics.
