The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper bounding (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC-like samplers. The core idea is to run AIS both forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior). The forward AIS log-weight gives a stochastic lower bound on the log partition function ratio, while reverse AIS (initialized from an exact posterior sample, available on simulated data via ancestral sampling) gives a stochastic upper bound. The expected gap between the two provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The authors call the procedure BREAD and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. Empirically, they (i) validate that simulated-data behavior mirrors real-data behavior when simulating from fitted hyperparameters, (ii) show the reverse bound is insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the required posterior sample over hyperparameters, (iii) explore modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization), and (iv) demonstrate debugging value by uncovering a sampling bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work: The work builds directly on BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al., 2015) and AIS (Neal, 2001), connecting to nonequilibrium work bounds (Jarzynski, 1997). It is complementary to sample-quality measures via Stein’s method (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). The concurrent “subjective divergence” of Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) is closely related; when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior, their estimator also upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, matching the authors’ theory. Importantly, there is earlier simulation-based validation of Bayesian inference that should be cited and discussed: Geweke’s “Getting it right” (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006) on posterior-quantile checks. Both advocate validating MCMC by simulating from the prior and checking calibration; BREAD shares this philosophy but contributes a quantitative upper bound on divergence, with a direct tie to AIS/BDMC and probabilistic programming.

Quality: The theoretical contribution is sound and well-motivated: deriving that the expected bidirectional AIS gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, and showing how one-sided bounds estimate one-sided KLs in the presence of an external upper/lower bound. The guarantees are rigorous for simulated data with exact posterior initialization; for real data the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic. The empirical validation across five models is sensible and the consistency between real and simulated data is reassuring. However:
- The bound holds in expectation; the paper would benefit from reporting uncertainty (e.g., multiple independent bidirectional runs with CIs on the gap), and discussing concentration or tail behavior of the estimator.
- AIS weights are heavy-tailed; the choice to visualize quartiles rather than means sidesteps variance issues. A more principled aggregation (e.g., log-mean-exp, or multiple-chain averaging with error bars) would strengthen claims.
- The tightness study is relegated to the appendix with toy examples; adding at least one main-text quantitative example would help calibrate users.

Clarity: Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. The notation q(·) appears before it is fully defined. Please:
- Explicitly define qfwd(xT) as the marginal distribution of the AIS final state after T steps, and qrev(v|xT) as the reverse-chain conditional induced by AIS under exact posterior initialization. Introduce v = (x1,…,xT−1) up front.
- Present a step-by-step, self-contained derivation in the main text from E[log R̂] and E[log R̂rev] to the inequalities (Eqs. 8, 11) and to the expected-gap ≥ Jeffreys-divergence result, with clear statements of assumptions (ergodicity, reversibility, exact-posterior initialization).
- Provide a concise algorithm box for BREAD: inputs, forward/reverse schedules, how to run on simulated vs real data, how to aggregate multiple runs.
- Minor clarity: splitting unobserved variables into θ and z clutters notation, since they always appear together; consider using a single x throughout.

Originality: The extension of BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding sample-quality via Jeffreys divergence is a neat and practically useful perspective. While much of the machinery (AIS, BDMC, reverse AIS) is established, the specific expected-gap bound and its operationalization in PPLs are novel enough for NIPS, especially with the pragmatic diagnostic/debugging angle.

Significance: High practical relevance. A quantitative, automatable bound on sample quality can materially aid users of probabilistic programming, guide modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed), and surface implementation bugs. The simulated-data requirement is a limitation, but the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is a reasonable bridge, and the paper documents both successes and a failure mode under misspecification.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Cite and contrast with Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006), and clarify how BREAD complements SBC-style calibration (e.g., BREAD provides a divergence bound vs rank-uniformity tests).
- Report variability and confidence intervals of the bidirectional gap across runs; discuss how many runs are needed for a stable estimate.
- Provide guidance on temperature schedules and number of intermediate distributions; assess sensitivity and computational overhead.
- Compare to standard diagnostics on the same runs (R̂, ESS) and to Stein discrepancies to show complementary information.
- Add one substantive main-text example quantifying tightness (J vs bound) beyond the appendix.
- Clarify the scope: when reverse initialization is approximate, the procedure estimates a subjective divergence; articulate what is still guaranteed.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, useful contribution with rigorous guarantees (on simulated data).
- Integration into two major PPLs; practical impact demonstrated.
- Insightful case study on model representations; real debugging win.

Cons for acceptance:
- Some theoretical and algorithmic details are deferred; Section 3.1 is hard to parse.
- Guarantees are in expectation; empirical uncertainty not fully characterized.
- Reliance on simulated data and heuristic real-data protocol may limit adoption without more guidance.

Minor comments and typos:
- Define qfwd, qrev, v, and xT before using them in Section 3.1; avoid introducing θ and z separately unless needed.
- Fix typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).
- Standardize references (e.g., “CGHL+ p” looks malformed); include full venues/years.
- Consider renaming BREAD expansion once for clarity and use the acronym thereafter.

Overall: A promising and practically valuable paper. With improved exposition and a bit more empirical characterization of estimator variability and schedule sensitivity, it would make a strong addition to the program.
