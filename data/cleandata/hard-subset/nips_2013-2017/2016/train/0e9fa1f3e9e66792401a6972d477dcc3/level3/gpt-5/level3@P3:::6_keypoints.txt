- Paper revisits BDMC/AIS to assess reliability of MCMC posterior inference by running AIS forward and reverse to get stochastic lower/upper bounds on log p(y).
- Shows the expected gap between reverse and forward log-AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS sample distribution and the true posterior.
- Integrates the procedure into WebPPL and Stan, validates on toy and several small realistic models, and proposes a protocol for real-data diagnostics via simulation from fitted hyperparameters.
- Approach closely connects to prior thermodynamic/annealing and SMC work and overlaps with prior/concurrent BDMC uses for marginal likelihood estimation.
- Independent concurrent work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka) derives a related “subjective divergence” that reduces to the same Jeffreys bound when initialized from the true posterior.
- Clear and accessible exposition of BDMC/AIS and how expectations relate to divergences.
- Strong engineering contribution: implementations in two PPLs and a range of example models.
- Practical protocol for linking simulated-data guarantees to real-data workflows with useful consistency checks.
- Demonstrated real utility as a QA tool (found a bug in WebPPL).
- Empirical comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations provides actionable guidance.
- Limited novelty: main theoretical observation follows directly from known BDMC identities and overlaps with prior work; “BREAD” is largely rebranding.
- Guarantees only hold on simulated data with exact posterior initialization; the real-data fitted-hyperparameter procedure is heuristic and unquantified.
- Evaluation is small-scale, largely qualitative, and lacks evidence of scalability, high-dimensional or multimodal robustness, or comparisons to standard diagnostics.
- Important design choices (annealing schedule, number of temperatures, transition kernels) crucially affect results but receive limited guidance or ablation.
- The bound is only in expectation; no per-instance guarantees are provided, complicating practical interpretation.
- Technical presentation is sound and careful, but practical claims on real data lack rigorous control or calibration.
- Writing and organization are strong, with clear recaps and explicit assumptions; recommend emphasizing expectation-only nature earlier.
- Originality is low; primary contribution is systems/implementation and practical protocol rather than new theory.
- Significance is higher as a PPL/systems contribution than as a NIPS main-track theoretical advance.
- Pros for acceptance: useful tooling, clear exposition, and empirical case studies that inform practitioners.
- Cons for acceptance: minimal theoretical novelty, guarantees limited to simulated data, and limited empirical depth/comparisons.
- Suggested improvements: quantitative calibration on tractable ground-truth problems, comparisons to R-hat/ESS and Stein discrepancy, sensitivity analyses for annealing/kernel choices, and development of a principled reference distribution for reverse AIS on real data.
- Recommendation: Reject for NIPS main track; recommend presentation at an MCMC/PPL workshop focused on tooling and engineering insights.
