Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) with annealed importance sampling (AIS) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key idea is to run AIS forward (prior → posterior) and reverse (posterior → prior) and use the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS. The method (BREAD) is implemented in Stan and WebPPL and used to (i) validate inference on simulated data; (ii) propose a protocol to transfer these diagnostics to real data by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating from the fitted model, and checking consistency; and (iii) guide modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and detect implementation errors.

This builds on prior BDMC work (Grosse et al., 2015), AIS (Neal, 2001), thermodynamic/annealing ideas (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997), and connects to recent diagnostics for sample quality (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). The paper also relates to independent work on “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Quality
- Positive: The central derivation that the forward–reverse gap upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between q and p is sound and useful. The protocol for simulated-data validation of real-data inference is carefully motivated and supported empirically. Integrations into Stan/WebPPL and the debugging example show practical value.
- Concerns:
  - The bound is only in expectation; guidance on how to estimate E[gap] with uncertainty (e.g., variance estimates, multiple runs, CIs via bootstrap) is missing. Practitioners need to know when a small observed gap is meaningful.
  - The experimental section under-specifies key AIS design choices. The β schedule (number and placement of intermediate temperatures, Δβ) and number/type of transitions per temperature are crucial for both bound tightness and fair comparisons; these should be stated and systematically explored.
  - The “fitted hyperparameters” reverse-chain initialization breaks the exactness needed for guarantees. While Section 5.1.2 shows robustness to the number of burn-in steps S, more stress tests (misspecification, heavy-tailed priors) would strengthen the claim.
  - Some derivations and equations have clarity issues (see detailed comments).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and accessible, but Sections 3.1–3.2 are rushed and contain ambiguities:
- Notation: v defined as “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage” is ambiguous when comparing forward and reverse sweeps; please explicitly define the joint distributions and conditionals for both directions and keep “fwd/rev” vs. “for/back” consistent.
- Missing definitions: The estimator inside expectations in Eqs. (6–11) (e.g., the precise form of the AIS weight/ratio) should be explicitly defined. As written, Eq. (6) appears to lead to a KL with identical arguments; likely a typographical error—please correct and verify all KL arguments in Eqs. (6–11).
- Acronyms/labels: Define RAIS and any (u)/(c)- (R)AIS variants in figure captions; clarify whether “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” in figures denotes steps used within AIS at each temperature.
- Figures: Axes/tick labels are too small to read; please increase font sizes and clarify legends.
- Typos: The NUTS citation attributes “Homan” instead of “Hoffman.” Ensure parentheses are balanced in divergence expressions and throughout.

Originality
The paper’s main conceptual move—reinterpreting the BDMC forward–reverse gap as an estimator that upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between AIS sample distribution and the true posterior—is neat and, to my knowledge, not explicitly developed in prior work. That said:
- The technical novelty over BDMC (and AIS analyses) is modest; much of the machinery is known. The contribution is a careful refinement applied to posterior-sample quality measurement, plus a practical protocol and implementations.
- The relation to Neal’s tempered transitions could be discussed more concretely, beyond noting equilibrium assumptions: draw a clearer connection between the acceptance-ratio/energy landscape view and the current bounds, and explain differences in assumptions and guarantees.

Significance
Given the difficulty of validating black-box MCMC, a practical, automated, and principled diagnostic is valuable. The ability to:
- provide actionable guidance on model representations across systems,
- catch subtle implementation bugs,
- and offer a quantitative target (gap) for algorithm tuning,
makes this work relevant to both researchers and practitioners, especially in probabilistic programming. The restriction to AIS/SMC-like samplers limits universality, but many design choices overlap with general MCMC, so the insights can transfer.

Pros
- Clear, useful reframing of BDMC as an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical integration in Stan and WebPPL; compelling debugging example.
- Sensible protocol for leveraging simulated data to say something about real data; empirical validation across five models.
- Demonstrates how the tool can inform modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed).

Cons
- Bound is only in expectation; no guidance on uncertainty quantification or stopping rules.
- AIS design choices (β schedule, transitions per temperature) are under-specified and not systematically studied; comparisons may be sensitive to these choices.
- Use of approximate reverse initialization weakens guarantees; more extensive stress-testing needed.
- Clarity issues in derivations, notation, and figures; some equation/citation errors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an “AutoBDMC” procedure: adaptively refine the β schedule (e.g., bisection on Δβ, or KLD/ESS-based placement) until the forward–reverse gap (with uncertainty bars) falls below a user threshold; report wall-clock-normalized results.
- Quantify uncertainty of the estimated expected gap by repeating AIS runs and reporting means with CIs, or concentration bounds for the log-estimators; discuss when to average vs. report quantiles.
- Detail AIS configurations: number and placement of temperatures, transitions per temperature (HMC/NUTS parameters), and number of chains. Explore schedule sensitivity and show how tighter schedules tighten bounds.
- Deepen the related-work discussion of tempered transitions, thermodynamic integration, and subjective divergence; clarify when each approach provides bounds vs diagnostics.
- Strengthen the real-data protocol: include explicit misspecification experiments (beyond the appendix) and scenarios with diffuse hyperpriors to probe the robustness of reverse-chain initialization.
- Fix derivation/notation issues, define all acronyms, and improve figure readability.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The technical novelty is modest, but the idea is sound, useful, and well validated, and the implementations could have high practical impact in probabilistic programming. I would support acceptance if the authors address the clarity issues, systematically document AIS design choices, and provide an adaptive schedule/uncertainty-aware protocol. Confidence: moderate.
