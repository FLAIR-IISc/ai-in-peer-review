This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The core idea is to leverage the recent BDMC technique, originally designed for estimating log marginal likelihood, to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. This allows for a theoretically grounded assessment of MCMC performance. The authors integrate BREAD into the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages and demonstrate its use in comparing different model representations.

The work builds upon the foundation of annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), extending the BDMC framework [GGA15] to assess the quality of approximate posterior samples. While traditional MCMC diagnostics often rely on heuristics, BREAD provides a quantitative bound on the divergence, potentially offering a more robust evaluation. The authors address the challenge of requiring an exact posterior sample for the upper bound by proposing a protocol for using simulated data based on hyperparameters estimated from real data.

A key strength of this paper lies in its attempt to provide a theoretically justified diagnostic tool for MCMC. The existing landscape of MCMC convergence diagnostics often lacks strong theoretical guarantees. BREAD's connection to BDMC and the bounding of the Jeffreys divergence offer a promising avenue for developing a more rigorous assessment of inference quality. The integration into popular probabilistic programming languages like WebPPL and Stan increases its potential impact and accessibility. Furthermore, the use of BREAD to inform modeling choices, as demonstrated with the matrix factorization example, highlights its practical utility.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that significantly limit its contribution. The reliance on hyperparameters estimated from initial MCMC runs is a major concern. The method assumes that these initial runs have already converged and that a single summary (the estimated hyperparameters) is sufficient to represent the posterior. This assumption directly circumvents the very problem BREAD aims to solve: ensuring reliable inference. If the initial MCMC runs are flawed, the subsequent analysis on simulated data is unlikely to be informative about the actual performance on real data.

Furthermore, the claim that the behavior observed on simulated data reflects that on real data is largely unjustified. The authors acknowledge the potential for discrepancies, particularly under model misspecification, but provide limited evidence to support the validity of this assumption.  It is unclear why the simulated data, generated using potentially biased hyperparameters, would accurately represent the complexities of the true posterior.  

The assumption that the MCMC chain can quickly converge to an almost exact sample starting from the estimated hyperparameters is also optimistic and lacks empirical validation. This is particularly problematic in high-dimensional or complex models where achieving accurate convergence can be challenging. 

The empirical evidence presented in Section 5.1, while demonstrating consistency between real and simulated data for the chosen models, is insufficient to convince the reader that these issues won’t arise in more complex or realistic scenarios. The selected examples are relatively simple and may not capture the full range of challenges faced in Bayesian inference. 

Additionally, the method primarily focuses on assessing inference quality through the marginal likelihood. While valuable, this provides an incomplete picture of the posterior, especially in cases of multimodality or complex dependencies. The paper does not adequately address how BREAD handles such scenarios or how it compares to other established diagnostic techniques.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel application of BDMC to MCMC diagnostics.
*   Offers a theoretically grounded approach for bounding the Jeffreys divergence.
*   Integrates the method into widely used probabilistic programming languages.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on potentially flawed initial MCMC runs to estimate hyperparameters.
*   Assumes a questionable correspondence between simulated and real data.
*   Lacks sufficient empirical validation, particularly for complex models.
*   Primarily focuses on the marginal likelihood, neglecting other aspects of the posterior.
*   Does not adequately address the limitations of the method in the presence of multimodality or model misspecification.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the idea of leveraging BDMC for MCMC diagnostics is intriguing, the methodological weaknesses and limited empirical support significantly detract from the paper's contribution. The core assumptions underlying the method are questionable and undermine its potential validity. The paper requires substantial revision, including a more rigorous justification of its assumptions, more extensive empirical validation with diverse models, and a clearer comparison to existing diagnostic techniques.  **I recommend rejection.** The authors should address concerns about handling multimodality and model misspecification and provide simulations comparing their diagnostic to standard methods.
