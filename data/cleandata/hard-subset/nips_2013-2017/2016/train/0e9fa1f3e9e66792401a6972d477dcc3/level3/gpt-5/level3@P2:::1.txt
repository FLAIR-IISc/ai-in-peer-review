Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC-style samplers. The key idea is to run AIS both forward (prior → posterior) and in reverse (posterior → prior), where the reverse chain is initialized from an exact posterior draw available on simulated data. The gap between the reverse upper and forward lower stochastic bounds on log p(y) then yields an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between qfwd(xT) (the approximate sampling distribution of the final AIS state) and the target pT. The authors integrate this diagnostic (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, validate tightness on toy distributions, and propose a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data at those settings, and use an approximate posterior draw of (η, θ) to initialize reverse AIS, empirically validating that forward/reverse curves align across real and simulated datasets.

This work is well-situated within a line of research on partition function estimation and MCMC validation: Neal’s AIS (2001) and reverse-AIS connections to Jarzynski (1997); BDMC for marginal likelihood sandwiching (Grosse et al., 2015); conservative log-likelihood estimation via reverse annealing (Burda et al., 2015); and sample-quality diagnostics via Stein’s method (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). The paper positions itself relative to concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Two closely related prior validation paradigms should also be cited and compared: (1) Geweke’s “Getting it right” joint distribution tests of posterior simulators (2004), and (2) Cook, Gelman, and Rubin’s posterior-quantile software validation (2006). Both validate MCMC by running on data simulated from the prior and comparing known reference distributions; they are highly relevant to the simulated-data protocol here and provide complementary checks that do not rely on AIS bridging.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation that the BDMC gap upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is sound and useful. It is clearly stated that the bound is in expectation and relies on an exact posterior draw (available for simulated data). The one-sided bounds with external L/U are also helpful.
- Empirics: The toy examples demonstrate reasonable tightness; the multi-model Stan experiments show that real/simulated forward AIS curves closely match when simulating from fitted hyperparameters, and that reverse bounds are insensitive to the number of hyperparameter MCMC steps. The matrix factorization case study is informative, and the discovered bug in WebPPL underscores practical value.
- Limitations: The guarantees are only rigorous for simulated data with exact posterior draws; the real-data protocol is heuristic. The bound may be loose in hard multimodal settings, and the paper acknowledges heavy-tailed AIS weights and avoids averaging in the analyses. More quantitative characterization of variability (e.g., confidence intervals across repeated bidirectional runs, median-of-means or methods from Gogate et al., 2007) would strengthen the empirical support.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but Section 3.1 is hard to follow on first read. q(·) is used before being explicitly defined. Please define qfwd(xT) up front as the marginal distribution over the final AIS state after T stages, and define qfwd(v|xT) and qrev(v|xT) before using them in Eqs. (6–11).
- Important derivation details are pushed to the supplement; the main text should include a clear, step-by-step argument from the AIS extended-state perspective to the expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, ideally with a concise boxed algorithm for BREAD (inputs, outputs, and how to compute the gap).
- Notation: splitting unobserved variables into θ and z is unnecessary since they always appear together; using a unified latent vector x (or (θ, z) only when needed) would declutter.
- Minor: typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

Originality
- The core novelty is recasting the BDMC sandwich to bound the Jeffreys divergence between sample distributions and the true posterior, and deploying it as a practical diagnostic within PPLs. Relative to BDMC for log-ML and to the concurrent “subjective divergence,” this work emphasizes expected bounds on Jeffreys divergence using exact posterior initialization and demonstrates practical usage in Stan/WebPPL. The real-data proxy protocol is a pragmatic contribution.

Significance
- This contributes a rigorous, interpretable diagnostic for AIS/SMC-style samplers, with actionable insights for modeling and inference choices in PPLs (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations). The debugging example shows clear utility. Given the prevalence of black-box MCMC and the limitations of standard convergence heuristics, this is likely to be adopted by PPL practitioners and researchers.

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of BDMC to bound sample quality (Jeffreys divergence) in expectation.
- Practical integrations into WebPPL and Stan; real case studies; actionable insights on model representation.
- Clear simulated-data guarantees; sensible real-data proxy with empirical validation.
- Useful for debugging; found a real bug.

Cons
- Guarantees rely on exact posterior draws (simulated data). Real-data protocol is heuristic and may be optimistic under misspecification or poorly informed hyperpriors.
- Section 3.1 is dense; key definitions appear late; main derivation should be more self-contained.
- Tightness characterization is limited; no formal finite-sample/tail guarantees for the divergence gap; AIS schedule sensitivity could be discussed more.
- Limited comparison to alternative diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS, Geweke 2004, Cook–Gelman–Rubin 2006) on the same runs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add discussion and citations to Geweke (2004) joint distribution tests and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006) posterior-quantile validation, contrasting their goals and assumptions with BREAD.
- Revise Section 3.1 to: (a) define qfwd(xT), qfwd(v|xT), qrev(v|xT) before use; (b) present a step-by-step derivation in the main text; (c) include a concise algorithmic description of BREAD.
- Provide guidance on practical settings: number of intermediate temperatures, kernel choices, and diagnostics of schedule adequacy; show sensitivity plots where the gap narrows with more T.
- Report variability across repeated bidirectional runs (e.g., interquartile ranges, median-of-means) and consider combining multiple chains to tighten stochastic bounds (cf. Gogate et al., 2007).
- Include a head-to-head comparison with standard MCMC diagnostics (R-hat, ESS) and with Geweke/CGR tests on the same models to show complementary strengths.
- Discuss potential failure modes (e.g., highly multimodal targets where reverse AIS is fragile) and how users should interpret large gaps.
- Clean up notation (avoid splitting θ and z unless needed), and fix the noted typo.

Recommendation
This is a solid and useful contribution to practical validation of MCMC in probabilistic programming. With improved clarity in Section 3.1, added discussion of related validation methods, and a bit more empirical characterization of variability and sensitivity, the paper would be a strong accept.
