

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously evaluate MCMC inference quality by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] to provide upper bounds in expectation on this divergence for MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly applicable to AIS and SMC. By running AIS chains both forward and backward on simulated data, they sandwich the true log marginal likelihood, yielding rigorous bounds on the divergence. The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, validated across multiple models, and used to analyze model representation tradeoffs in matrix factorization.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded method for measuring MCMC reliability, addressing a critical gap in probabilistic inference where existing convergence diagnostics are heuristic and often fail to detect specific inference failures. This is particularly valuable for black-box inference in probabilistic programming languages, where users need confidence in results without deep expertise. Second, the authors demonstrate practical utility by uncovering a bug in WebPPL through their method, showing how BREAD can serve as a debugging tool. Third, the method reveals meaningful insights about model representation choices in probabilistic programming, showing how collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations affect inference speed and efficiency differently across WebPPL and Stan.

The technical contribution is strong and well-supported. The derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1) is theoretically sound, though the presentation could be clearer. The authors properly acknowledge related work [CTM16], noting that their method uses exact posterior samples for initialization, whereas Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka use reference distributions, which has different theoretical guarantees. However, the paper should have cited and discussed earlier work on validating MCMC by running inference on data sampled from the prior [Geweke 2004; Cook et al. 2006], which shares conceptual similarities with their proposed protocol for real-world data.

The paper's quality is high, with thorough experimental validation across five models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, time series, and Gaussian mixtures). The protocol for using simulated data to evaluate real-world inference (Section 3.2) is well-justified and validated in Section 5.1.1-5.1.2, showing that inference behavior on simulated data closely matches real data when hyperparameters are fitted. The matrix factorization case study (Section 5.2.1) effectively demonstrates how BREAD can guide practical modeling decisions, showing that collapsed representations improve convergence in WebPPL but not in Stan.

Clarity is a notable weakness. The key derivation in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow due to unclear notation (e.g., q(.) is used before being defined as the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after T steps). The paper pushes important details to the supplement rather than presenting them in the main text, making the method less accessible. The unnecessary splitting of unobserved variables into z and θ (instead of treating them as a single vector) adds clutter. There's also a typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

The originality is strong for NIPS. While BDMC [GGA15] was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, this paper's application to bounding posterior divergence is novel. It differs significantly from prior work on MCMC diagnostics [CC96; GR92; BG98], which cannot confirm convergence but only identify specific forms of non-convergence. The method also differs from Gorham and Mackey [GM15], which requires strong convexity assumptions, while BREAD makes no distributional assumptions.

The significance is high. As probabilistic programming becomes more widespread, the need for reliable inference quality assessment grows. BREAD provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, addressing a critical gap in the field. The method's ability to guide model representation choices and uncover implementation bugs (as demonstrated in WebPPL) shows tangible practical value. While limited to simulated data, the proposed protocol for real-world application is well-justified and validated.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound and practically useful method for evaluating MCMC inference reliability. The main weaknesses are in clarity and presentation, which could be addressed with minor revisions. The method has significant potential to improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems and guide better modeling decisions. I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a critical problem with a novel solution, provides strong theoretical and empirical validation, and demonstrates clear practical utility.
