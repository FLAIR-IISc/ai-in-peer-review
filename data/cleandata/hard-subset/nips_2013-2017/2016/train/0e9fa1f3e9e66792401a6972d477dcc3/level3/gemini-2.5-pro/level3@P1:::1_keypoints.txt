- Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC to give a stochastic upper bound (in expectation) on the Jeffreys divergence between AIS approximate posteriors and the true posterior.  
- Method requires an exact posterior sample, so it's directly applicable only to simulated data; authors propose a heuristic protocol for real data that fits hyperparameters then simulates data.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates utility in experiments (inference consistency checks, model-representation comparison, and bug detection).  
- Builds on established simulation-based validation traditions but provides a quantitative, theoretically grounded divergence measure rather than visual/parameter-recovery checks.  
- Technical contribution: sound extension of BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding Jeffreys divergence for AIS.  
- High practical significance for PPL users and researchers: a principled tool to debug and compare samplers and model representations.  
- Validation experiments (Sec. 5.1) support that simulated-data results are relevant to real-data performance.  
- Matrix factorization experiment (collapsed vs uncollapsed) gives concrete guidance on representation choices.  
- Practical impact emphasized by discovery of a real bug in WebPPL via BREAD.  
- Key limitation: reliance on simulated data; the real-data protocol uses an approximate posterior to initialize reverse chain, breaking formal guarantees despite empirical effectiveness.  
- Originality: novel use of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence, though BDMC itself is recent prior work.  
- Missing related work: fails to cite important prior papers on simulation-based MCMC validation (Geweke 2004; Cook et al. 2006) and should situate the method historically.  
- Clarity issues: Section 3.1 (derivation of the bound) is hard to follow; notation like qfwd(xT) is used before definition and key derivation steps lack explanation.  
- Not self-contained: crucial AIS and BDMC details are relegated to the appendix; the main text needs clearer, step-by-step intuition.  
- Minor presentation issues: unnecessary theta vs z distinction and a typographical error (missing parenthesis) noted.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important problem, presents a novel principled method, strong experiments, and immediate practical utility via PPL integration.  
- Rejection arguments: central theoretical section lacks clarity and the paper omits key prior related work, slightly overstating novelty.  
- Recommendation: accept, with required revisions to clarify Section 3.1 and to properly cite/discuss prior simulation-based MCMC validation literature.
