- Extends BDMC to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and AIS-produced approximate samples using forward and reverse AIS estimates.
- Proposes a practical real-data protocol using fitted hyperparameters and simulated datasets, with implementations for WebPPL and Stan.
- Theoretical result: expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence; emphasize that the bound holds in expectation.
- Request to report uncertainty for the gap estimator and guidance on how many AIS runs are needed for finite-sample use.
- Formal guarantees require an exact posterior sample and algorithms expressible as importance sampling on an extended state space (AIS/SMC).
- The real-data protocol is a reasonable but heuristic substitute for exact guarantees; highlight and discuss potential failure modes (e.g., prior-data conflict).
- Experiments show simulated-data AIS behavior tracks real-data behavior across five Stan models and reverse AIS is robust to hyperparameter MCMC steps.
- Case study (matrix factorization) yields actionable modeling/implementation insights and uncovered a WebPPL Gaussian sampler bug.
- Recommend additional sensitivity analyses for annealing schedules, number of tempering steps, transition kernels, and reporting confidence intervals for the bound gap.
- Section 3 is hard to follow; introduce all variables before use and give more intuition for conditioning and marginalization steps.
- State the main theorem early in Section 3.1 with clear assumptions and a boxed statement that the bound holds in expectation.
- Ensure consistent terminology (BDMC, BREAD, AutoBDMC) and define any end-to-end tool name on first use.
- Fix notation inconsistencies (e.g., y vs. D) and minor typos like p(θ,z|y) vs. p(θ,z,y).
- Clarify repeatedly that the bound targets Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence, not Jensen–Shannon divergence.
- Novelty: reframes BDMC sandwich as an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, giving a single scalar sample-quality metric and a practical real-data protocol.
- Significance: timely and useful for probabilistic programming and MCMC practice, though limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers.
- Pros: solid theoretical link, practical integrations, empirical validation, useful scalar metric, and demonstrated practical impact (debugging/model choices).
- Cons: formal guarantees depend on exact posterior samples, Section 3 clarity issues, insufficient sensitivity/uncertainty analysis, and terminology/notation inconsistencies.
- Suggested improvements: foreground main result and assumptions, provide variance/CIs and guidance on chain counts, add sensitivity studies and ablations, compare more to Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), and clean exposition/notation.
- Software suggestions: provide clear WebPPL integration instructions, consider an npm package, and release Stan code and reproduction scripts.
- Overall recommendation: strong, useful paper recommended for acceptance after clarifying Section 3, explicitly stating novelty, fixing terminology/notation, and minor software packaging guidance.
