- Extends BDMC to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and AIS/SMC sample distributions using forward and reverse AIS.  
- Reverse AIS is initialized from exact posterior draws on simulated data; the gap between reverse upper and forward lower log p(y) bounds yields an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.  
- Integrates the diagnostic (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, validates on toy problems, and proposes a simulated-data protocol for real datasets by fitting hyperparameters and simulating data.  
- Well-situated with prior work: builds on Neal’s AIS, BDMC for marginal likelihoods, reverse-annealing likelihood work, and relates to Stein-method diagnostics.  
- Should cite and compare to Geweke’s joint distribution “Getting it right” tests (2004) and Cook–Gelman–Rubin posterior-quantile validation (2006).  
- Theory: derivation that the BDMC gap upper-bounds expected Jeffreys divergence is sound and clearly stated as an expectation dependent on exact posterior draws.  
- Provides useful one-sided bounds with external lower/upper estimators.  
- Empirics: toy examples show reasonable tightness; Stan experiments show forward/reverse alignment when simulating from fitted hyperparameters.  
- Empirics: reverse bounds are insensitive to number of hyperparameter MCMC steps; matrix factorization case study is informative and found a real WebPPL bug.  
- Limitations: guarantees only rigorous for simulated data with exact posterior draws; real-data protocol is heuristic and may be optimistic under misspecification.  
- Limitations: bounds may be loose for hard multimodal targets and AIS weights can be heavy-tailed; avoidance of averaging is noted.  
- Recommends more quantitative variability characterization (confidence intervals, repeated runs, median-of-means, methods like Gogate et al. 2007).  
- Clarity: Section 3.1 is hard to follow; q(·) and qfwd(xT) are used before definition and should be defined up front.  
- Clarity: main derivation details are pushed to the supplement; include a step-by-step derivation and a concise boxed algorithm for BREAD in the main text.  
- Notation: splitting latent variables into θ and z is unnecessary; use a unified latent vector x to declutter.  
- Minor typo: missing closing parenthesis at line 165.  
- Originality: novelty is recasting BDMC sandwich to bound sample quality (Jeffreys divergence) and deploying it as a practical diagnostic in PPLs, distinct from BDMC log-ML work and concurrent subjective divergence.  
- Significance: provides a rigorous, interpretable diagnostic for AIS/SMC samplers with practical value for PPL users and demonstrated debugging utility.  
- Pros: conceptually clean extension, practical integrations, simulated-data guarantees, real case studies, and debugging impact.  
- Cons: reliance on exact posterior draws for guarantees makes the real-data protocol heuristic; Section 3.1 needs clarity; limited tightness characterization and comparisons to other diagnostics.  
- Suggestions: add discussion/citations to Geweke (2004) and Cook–Gelman–Rubin (2006); revise Section 3.1 to define quantities and present derivation and algorithm; clean up notation and typo.  
- Suggestions: provide guidance on practical settings (number of temperatures, kernel choices), show sensitivity plots of gap vs T, report variability across repeated runs, and compare head-to-head with R-hat/ESS/Geweke/CGR tests.  
- Suggest discussing failure modes (e.g., highly multimodal targets) and how to interpret large gaps.  
- Recommendation: solid, useful contribution; accept after improving clarity in Section 3.1, adding related-methods discussion, and strengthening empirical variability and sensitivity characterization.
