This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), to evaluate the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to measure the accuracy of approximate posterior samples obtained from MCMC algorithms. The method is applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC).

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the BREAD method and its theoretical foundations. The idea of using bidirectional Monte Carlo to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples is interesting and has the potential to provide a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tool.

However, the paper has several major drawbacks. Firstly, the method assumes a known representative hyperparameter value η_real from prior MCMC runs, presuming those runs already converged and a single summary is sufficient. This assumption is problematic, as it directly relates to the original convergence problem and undermines the diagnostic's validity if it fails.

Secondly, the method assumes that the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior, which is unjustified, especially under model misspecification. This assumption is critical, as it allows the authors to run diagnostics on posteriors for synthetic data instead of the real-data posterior.

Thirdly, the method assumes that starting from η_real lets the MCMC quickly produce an almost exact sample, an optimistic and unverified assumption. This assumption is also critical, as it allows the authors to use the synthetic-data posterior as a proxy for the real-data posterior.

The empirical evidence provided in the paper is limited, and the simple examples in Section 5.1 are not convincing that the issues won't matter in more complex settings. The authors should provide more comprehensive simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, including comparisons to standard MCMC diagnostic tools.

Furthermore, the authors should explain why their method handles multimodality/bad summary-statistic cases better. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential pitfalls of the BREAD method.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the theoretical foundations of the BREAD method. However, the paper's claims are not fully supported by the empirical evidence, and the authors should provide more comprehensive simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the BREAD method and its theoretical foundations. However, some of the notation and technical details may be challenging for non-experts to follow.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel method for evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. However, the method builds on existing work in the field, and the authors should provide more context and discussion of the relationships between their method and existing MCMC diagnostic tools.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to provide a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tool, which is an important contribution to the field. However, the paper's claims are not fully supported by the empirical evidence, and the authors should provide more comprehensive simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.
