- Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC with forward and reverse AIS to produce stochastic bounds and an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between AIS posterior and true posterior.  
- Theoretical guarantees require reverse AIS to start from an exact posterior sample, so they only hold for simulated data with known ground-truth parameters.  
- Practical protocol for real data: infer hyperparameters on real data, simulate a dataset from the model with those hyperparameters, then apply BREAD to the simulated data.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validates on multiple models, showing consistent inference behavior between real and simulated data under the protocol.  
- Demonstrates practical utility by comparing model representations for matrix factorization and uncovering a WebPPL sampler bug.  
- Builds on BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015); novelty is providing a quantitative bound on divergence (Jeffreys) rather than qualitative checks.  
- Misses discussion and citation of related simulation-based MCMC validation work (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006), weakening literature positioning.  
- Core theoretical derivation (linking BDMC gap to expected Jeffreys divergence) appears sound and is the paper’s key contribution.  
- Authors are transparent about the simulated-data limitation and provide experiments validating their protocol for real-data applicability.  
- Clarity issues in Section 3.1: notation q(.) is used before definition (e.g., q_fwd(x_T) should be defined as the marginal).  
- Logical leap from Eq. (7) to Eq. (8) needs explicit justification that KL does not increase under marginalization.  
- Notation is sometimes cluttered (theta and z always paired); combining into a single unobserved variable would simplify exposition.  
- Minor editorial issue: typo at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).  
- Originality is solid—clever extension of BDMC to quantitative diagnostics and useful application to PPL debugging; concurrent related work is cited.  
- Significance is high—provides a principled, automated diagnostic for MCMC/PPL users and developers that can detect non-convergence and implementation issues.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled metric, addresses an important problem, implemented in two PPLs, and supported by compelling experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: main derivation lacks clarity and the paper omits discussion of relevant prior simulation-validation literature.  
- Overall assessment: strong, practical, and impactful contribution with fixable weaknesses in clarity and literature framing.
