This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantifying the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015), which uses forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The main theoretical contribution is showing that the expected gap between these two bounds serves as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples generated by AIS.

The authors implement BREAD within two probabilistic programming systems, WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its utility. As the method formally requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain (and is thus limited to simulated data), the paper also proposes a practical protocol for its application to real-world data: first infer hyperparameters on the real data, then use these to generate a simulated dataset for which BREAD can be applied. The authors validate this protocol and use BREAD to compare different model representations for matrix factorization, as well as to uncover a bug in WebPPL.

This work relates to a long line of research on MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Brooks and Gelman, 1998). However, unlike traditional diagnostics which can only identify non-convergence, BREAD provides a quantitative, rigorous bound on the divergence from the true posterior for simulated data. The method is a direct application and extension of BDMC [GGA15]. A significant piece of related work, Neal's (1996) work on Tempered Transitions, is not cited. Tempered Transitions uses a similar annealing/heating process and its analysis of acceptance ratios relates closely to the ratio of forward and reverse path probabilities central to BDMC/AIS. The connection to this foundational work should be discussed.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** The paper addresses the critical and notoriously difficult problem of assessing the quality of MCMC samples. This is especially important for probabilistic programming systems, which aim to automate inference for non-experts. A reliable, quantitative measure of sample quality would be a major contribution to the field, and BREAD is a promising step in this direction.
2.  **Practicality and Usefulness:** The implementation and validation within two popular systems (Stan and WebPPL) is a major strength. The case studies are compelling: using BREAD to choose between model representations and, perhaps most convincingly, to identify a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL, demonstrates its immediate practical value.
3.  **Simplicity:** The core idea is simple and elegant. Using the gap between the BDMC bounds as a proxy for sample quality is intuitive and leverages an existing, powerful technique.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Technical Presentation:** The method's description in Section 3.1 is rushed and difficult to follow, which significantly detracts from the paper's quality.
    *   The derivations in equations (6)-(11) are hard to parse. The estimator for the partition function ratio (let's call it `\hat{R}`) is not explicitly defined within the expectation, making it difficult to verify the steps. For instance, what is the precise form of `\hat{R}` whose expectation is taken in equation (6)?
    *   The notation is ambiguous at times. The vector `v` is loosely defined as "all of the variables sampled in AIS before the final stage," which is confusing given there are two chains (forward and reverse). A more precise definition is needed. The use of "fwd"/"rev" and "for"/"back" is also inconsistent.
    *   Equation (7) introduces `DKL(qfwd(xT ) qfwd(v |xT ) k pT (xT ) qrev(v |xT ))`. It is unclear how this follows from equation (6) without a more detailed derivation. The relationship between the AIS importance weight and this specific KL divergence needs to be clearly laid out.

2.  **Originality:** The paper's technical novelty is modest. The core machinery is from BDMC [GGA15]. The main new insight is the connection between the BDMC gap and the Jeffreys divergence. While this is a nice observation, it appears to be a relatively direct consequence of the definition of BDMC and properties of KL divergence under marginalization. The omission of Neal's (1996) work on Tempered Transitions also weakens the positioning of the paper's contribution.

3.  **Experimental Rigor and Reproducibility:** The experiments, while demonstrating potential, lack crucial details about a key hyperparameter: the annealing schedule (the sequence of `\beta_t`).
    *   The choice of schedule (i.e., the number of steps and the spacing of temperatures) critically affects the tightness of the bounds and the efficiency of the AIS chains. Without a systematic discussion of how this schedule was chosen, it is difficult to interpret the results or compare performance across different settings (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed models). A poor schedule could unfairly disadvantage one approach over another.
    *   For this method to be truly practical, guidance on setting the schedule is needed. A good addition would be to propose and evaluate a simple adaptive scheme, for instance, one that iteratively increases the number of steps until the bound gap `\hat{B}` is below a desired threshold.
    *   The figures are also unclear. For instance, in Figure 2, what does "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" on the x-axis refer to? Is it the number of AIS annealing steps, or the number of leapfrog steps within each MCMC transition? Captions also contain undefined acronyms (e.g., "(u)c(R)AIS"). Finally, the axes and tick labels are too small to be easily legible.

### Minor Issues
*   The definition of Jeffreys divergence in Section 3 has a missing closing parenthesis: `DJ(qkp) = DKL(qkp) + DKL(pkq)`.
*   The citation for the NUTS sampler should be Hoffman and Gelman (2014), not Homan and Gelman.

### Recommendation

This paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution. It tackles an important problem with a simple and practical approach, backed by compelling demonstrations. However, the current manuscript is not yet ready for publication. The weaknesses in clarity, particularly in the core methods section, are significant and must be addressed. Furthermore, the lack of discussion around the critical annealing schedule hyperparameter and the omission of key related work detract from the paper's thoroughness and novelty.

I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors can substantially revise the paper to:
1.  Completely rewrite Section 3.1 for clarity, with precise definitions and derivations.
2.  Add a proper discussion of related work, especially Neal (1996).
3.  Provide full details of the annealing schedules used in experiments and discuss the sensitivity of the method to this choice.

Given the potential impact of the work, I lean towards encouraging a major revision rather than an outright rejection.
