- Extends BDMC to upper-bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between true posterior and AIS/SMC sample distributions by running forward and reverse AIS; method named BREAD.  
- Key idea: forward AIS gives a stochastic lower bound on log p(y) and reverse AIS gives an upper bound; their expected gap upper-bounds DJ(p||q).  
- Requires exact posterior samples, so rigorous guarantees apply on simulated data; proposes a fitted-hyperparameter (parametric-bootstrap-style) protocol to transfer to real data.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating practical tooling and applicability to PPLs.  
- Theoretical contribution: clean derivation showing BDMC gap bounds Jeffreys divergence using AIS’s extended-state-space view and KL monotonicity.  
- Empirical validation: tightness checks on toy problems with exact DJ; protocol validation across five Stan models showing similar forward-AIS behavior on real vs simulated data.  
- Shows reverse AIS bound is robust to number of MCMC steps for approximating posterior hyperparameters.  
- Demonstrates practical utility: guides representation choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and uncovers a WebPPL implementation bug.  
- Clarity: well-written presentation with limitations stated; some missing practical details like schedule choice and variance guidance.  
- Originality: reframes BDMC log-evidence sandwich as a quantitative posterior-sample-quality bound and provides a pragmatic real-data workflow.  
- Significance: provides interpretable, rigorous sample-quality bounds for AIS/SMC-like samplers, valuable for probabilistic programming and MCMC reliability.  
- Main limitation: needs exact posterior samples, so real-data use relies on a heuristic fitted-hyperparameter transfer with no formal guarantees under misspecification.  
- Statistical limitation: bound holds only in expectation and can be noisy/heavy-tailed for single runs; concentration/CI discussion is lacking.  
- Practical limitation: tightness depends on annealing schedules and kernels; guidance or adaptive schemes are not provided.  
- Experimental limitation: experiments are moderate scale and focus on AIS; lacking SMC case study and high-dimensional/multimodal stress tests.  
- Suggestions: provide heuristics/auto-tuning for temperature schedules, sensitivity analyses, and replication/CI procedures for BDMC gaps.  
- Suggestions: report wall-clock cost versus standard diagnostics (ESS, R-hat) and show cases where BREAD detects failures they miss.  
- Suggestions: add SMC example and larger/multimodal benchmarks to test robustness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, solid theory, practical integrations, careful experiments, and demonstrated real-world debugging utility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: guarantees limited to simulated data, expectation-bound may be high-variance or loose, and experimental scope (SMC/high-dim) is limited.  
- Overall recommendation: well-executed, novel, and practically useful; recommendation to accept.
