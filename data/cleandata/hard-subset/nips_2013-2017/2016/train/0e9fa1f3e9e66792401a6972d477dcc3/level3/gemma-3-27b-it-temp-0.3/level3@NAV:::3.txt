## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes utilizing the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds obtained via Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The authors contribute implementations of BDMC within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, alongside a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality. The core idea – leveraging BDMC’s bounds for diagnostic purposes – is simple and potentially impactful, particularly for “black box” inference scenarios common in probabilistic programming. 

The strength of this work lies in its practicality. Providing concrete implementations in popular tools like Stan and WebPPL immediately increases accessibility and potential adoption. The proposed protocol for assessing inference quality on real-world data, while heuristic, addresses a critical challenge in applying BDMC, which natively requires access to exact posterior samples. The experiments demonstrating the use of BREAD to analyze model representation choices in both frameworks are a good illustration of the method’s utility.

However, the novelty of the technical contributions feels modest. While the observation regarding the Jensen-Shannon divergence bound is insightful, it doesn’t represent a significant theoretical leap. A key omission is the lack of discussion of Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996), which is directly related to BDMC and its interpretation in terms of acceptance ratios. This oversight weakens the positioning of the work within the existing literature. 

Several aspects require clarification and improvement. The choice of β_t (the annealing schedule) is under-discussed. The impact of Δβ on the bounds and resulting comparisons should be investigated more systematically, potentially with an iterative scheme to tighten the bounds. The term “AutoBDMC” remains ambiguous; its meaning and mechanism are never clearly defined. Furthermore, sections 3.1-3.2 feel rushed, leading to ambiguities in notation (e.g., the definition of vector *v*) and inconsistent labeling (“rev/fwd” vs. “for/back”). Crucially, the estimator \hat{p}(y) used in equations 5-7 and 9-11 is not defined, hindering comprehension. Equation (6) appears incorrect as written.

The experiments lack sufficient detail regarding the practical choice of β_t. The figure captions are unclear (e.g., RAIS definition), and presentation issues like small axis labels detract from readability. Minor issues include typos (missing parentheses, misspelling of Hoffman).

**Arguments for acceptance:** The method is conceptually sound, practically implemented, and addresses a relevant problem in probabilistic programming. It offers a potentially valuable diagnostic tool for MCMC inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The novelty is limited, related work is not fully addressed, and several aspects of the presentation require significant clarification.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity issues, expanding the related work discussion (specifically including Neal, 1996), and providing more thorough experimental details. A more systematic exploration of the β schedule and a clear definition of “AutoBDMC” are also essential.
