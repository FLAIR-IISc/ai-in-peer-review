- Introduces BREAD, an extension of BDMC that bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence to assess MCMC posterior sample quality.
- Uses BDMC’s stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to derive an estimator that upper-bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence.
- Builds on Grosse et al. (2015) BDMC framework, repurposing it from marginal-likelihood estimation to posterior-sample quality evaluation.
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, showing practical utility for model representation comparisons and debugging.
- Theoretical derivation is technically sound and well-motivated, proving the expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Empirical implementation provides actionable insights; the WebPPL debugging example revealed a subtle multivariate Gaussian sampling bug.
- Major limitation: relies on a synthetic-data protocol that assumes hyperparameters estimated from real-data MCMC are reliable, creating a circular dependency.
- Paper does not adequately justify that synthetic-data inference behavior matches real-data behavior, especially under model misspecification or multimodality.
- Validation is limited to five simple models, lacking tests on complex or multimodal scenarios where diagnostics are most needed.
- Method applies directly only to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended state spaces (e.g., AIS/SMC), not to general MCMC samplers.
- Authors acknowledge scope limits but do not sufficiently address how to extend the diagnostic to broader MCMC methods.
- Practical applicability is undermined by the synthetic-data assumptions, making the claim of a “rigorous, general, and automatic” procedure overstated.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and connects appropriately to existing convergence-diagnostic and probabilistic-programming literature.
- Recommendation: clarify the specific conditions for reliable use and provide stronger theoretical/empirical justification for the synthetic-data protocol in challenging inference settings.
