This paper tackles the long-standing challenge of assessing the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. Building on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015), the authors run annealed importance sampling (AIS; Neal, 2001) both forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) to sandwich the log marginal likelihood and relate the bidirectional gap to an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sampling distribution. They further discuss how one-sided gaps can bound individual KL terms, and propose a pragmatic protocol for real data via fitted hyperparameters. The method is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, validated on multiple models (regression, time series, matrix factorization, mixtures), and used to compare collapsed/uncollapsed representations and to uncover a subtle implementation bug—demonstrating practical impact. The work is well-situated relative to prior diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998), sample-quality testing (Gorham & Mackey, 2015), and closely related subjective divergence ideas (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Quality: The theoretical derivation is careful and extends BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to posterior sample quality. Empirical results are convincing, spanning toy settings where ground truth is available and realistic models in two PPLs. Limitations are acknowledged: guarantees are rigorous on simulated data requiring an exact posterior sample; the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic, and bounds hold in expectation rather than with high probability.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with helpful background on AIS/BDMC and precise statements of what is and is not guaranteed. Suggestions: report computational overheads, detail annealing schedules and sensitivity analyses, and consolidate notation for forward vs. reverse chains.

Originality: The insight that the bidirectional AIS gap controls the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples is novel and useful. Integration into WebPPL and Stan and the real-data protocol are impactful contributions beyond theory.

Significance: Practitioners lack robust, general diagnostics for MCMC in black-box settings. This work offers a principled, implementable tool that informs model/inference choices and even aids debugging, making it timely and likely to influence practice.

Pros:
- Strong theory connecting BDMC to sample-quality bounds
- Solid empirical validation across tools and models
- Practical protocol for real data and demonstrated utility
- Clear positioning vs. related work

Cons:
- Expectation (not high-probability) guarantees
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-like settings with simulated data
- Limited exploration of bound tightness and schedule sensitivity at scale

Recommendation: Accept.
