## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper proposes utilizing Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to assess the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference and compare sampler efficiency by evaluating the tightness of BMC bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The core idea – selecting MCMC schemes that yield tighter bounds for equivalent computational effort – is interesting and builds upon recent work in bounding marginal likelihoods (GGA15). The authors extend this framework to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the approximated samples, presenting a diagnostic tool for MCMC convergence. 

The work is related to broader efforts in MCMC diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996) but distinguishes itself by attempting a theoretically grounded approach, rather than relying on heuristics. Related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) also leverages BDMC, though with a different initialization strategy for the reverse chain.  The authors demonstrate implementation within WebPPL and Stan, showcasing practical applicability.

However, the paper suffers from several significant drawbacks. The reliance on a pre-existing, assumed-converged hyperparameter value (η_real) is a major limitation. This assumption inherently begs the question of convergence, undermining the diagnostic’s validity if the initial MCMC runs were flawed. Furthermore, the assumption that the synthetic data posterior accurately reflects the convergence properties of the original posterior, particularly under model misspecification, is unjustified. The claim that starting from η_real allows for rapid production of an “almost exact” sample is optimistic and lacks empirical verification. 

The empirical evidence presented (Section 5.1) is limited, and the simple examples do not convincingly demonstrate the robustness of the method to more complex scenarios. While the bug discovered in WebPPL is a positive outcome, it doesn’t validate the broader efficacy of the proposed diagnostic. The authors do not adequately address how their approach handles multimodal posteriors or cases with poor summary statistics, areas where standard diagnostics struggle. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**  The paper presents a novel application of BMC, offering a theoretically grounded diagnostic for MCMC. The integration with WebPPL and Stan is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The core assumptions are problematic and weaken the theoretical foundation. The empirical validation is insufficient. The paper doesn’t clearly differentiate itself from existing diagnostics in challenging scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**  I lean towards **rejection**. While the idea has merit, the critical assumptions and limited empirical validation raise serious concerns about the reliability and practical utility of the proposed method. To strengthen the paper, the authors should address the concerns regarding the assumptions, provide a more comprehensive simulation study comparing their diagnostic to established methods, and demonstrate robustness to multimodality and poor summary statistics.
