- Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to rigorously evaluate MCMC posterior inference quality by bounding Jeffreys divergence.  
- Key theoretical result: BDMC/AIS run forward and reverse yields an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Contribution 1: Theoretical analysis proving BDMC provides an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.  
- Contribution 2: Method for exact computation of true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound in small tractable examples.  
- Contribution 3: Practical protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter fitting.  
- Contribution 4: Integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan with empirical validation across multiple models.  
- Experimental validation shows strong agreement between simulated and real-data results and uncovers a WebPPL implementation bug.  
- Technical rigor: careful derivations building on prior BDMC work and thorough theoretical and empirical validation.  
- Clarity: well-structured exposition, clear definitions (AIS, BDMC, Jeffreys divergence), and effective figures (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Originality: novel application of BDMC to inferential quality assessment, distinct from prior marginal-likelihood use and related work.  
- Practical significance: provides a general, rigorous, and automatic way to monitor MCMC quality useful for probabilistic programming practitioners.  
- Limitation: currently applicable only to inference algorithms representable as importance sampling on extended state spaces (e.g., AIS/SMC).  
- Practical caveat: real-data protocol depends on hyperparameter fitting, which may be challenging for complex models.  
- Overall assessment: well-motivated, technically sound, practically useful work that merits acceptance.
