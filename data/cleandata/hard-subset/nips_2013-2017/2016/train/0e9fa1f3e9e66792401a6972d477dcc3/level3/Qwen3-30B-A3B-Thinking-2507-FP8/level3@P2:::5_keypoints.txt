- Paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to bound Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate MCMC samples.  
- Builds on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015) and uses BDMC bounds to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence.  
- Four main contributions: (1) theoretical derivation that BDMC estimates upper-bound Jeffreys divergence in expectation.  
- Contribution (2): technique for exact computation of true divergence and upper bound on small examples.  
- Contribution (3): protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.  
- Contribution (4): integration and validation within WebPPL and Stan across multiple models.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, reproducible, and connects appropriately to related work (e.g., Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016).  
- Implementation in two probabilistic programming languages demonstrates practical relevance and insights on model representation choices.  
- Technical derivation is sound but depends on three strong, under-justified assumptions about representativeness and convergence of η_real.  
- Key assumptions: (1) single η_real from prior runs represents true posterior, (2) synthetic data from η_real mirrors real-data MCMC convergence, (3) MCMC from η_real quickly yields good posterior samples.  
- Empirical validation is limited to five simple models with data generated from the true process and does not address model misspecification or weak priors.  
- Originality is moderate—novel extension of BDMC but restricted to AIS/SMC-based inference, limiting applicability to general MCMC methods.  
- Paper fails to convincingly compare BREAD to standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) or show advantages for multimodality/badly conditioned posteriors.  
- Acceptance arguments: theoretically interesting extension with rigorous measurement approach and practical implementation insights.  
- Rejection arguments: reliance on unverified assumptions, limited applicability, and insufficient empirical validation in challenging scenarios.  
- Recommendation: strengthen empirical validation to address key assumptions or clarify the specific scenarios where BREAD is applicable; as presented, contributions are interesting but not broadly validated.
