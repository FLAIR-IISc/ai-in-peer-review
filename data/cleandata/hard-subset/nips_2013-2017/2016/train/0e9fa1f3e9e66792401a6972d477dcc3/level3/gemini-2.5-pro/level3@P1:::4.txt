This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively assessing the quality of samples from MCMC-based inference algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS). The method builds directly upon the recently proposed Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15], which uses forward and reverse AIS chains to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds, respectively, on the log marginal likelihood of a model. The key contribution of this work is to show that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate sample distribution produced by AIS and the true posterior. This yields a single, quantitative scalar metric for evaluating the quality of approximate inference.

A significant limitation of this method is that running the reverse AIS chain requires an exact sample from the true posterior, which is generally intractable for real-world data. The authors address this by limiting the rigorous analysis to simulated data, where an exact posterior sample can be obtained by first sampling from the joint distribution over parameters and data. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, they propose a protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset; second, simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters; and third, run BREAD on this simulated dataset. The paper provides compelling experimental validation for this protocol, showing that inference behavior on the simulated data closely matches that on the real data. The authors implement their method in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility for guiding model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers) and for debugging inference implementations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation that the gap between the BDMC bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a valuable theoretical contribution. The claims are well-supported by a thoughtful set of experiments. The authors are careful to address the primary weakness of their method—its reliance on simulated data—by designing and validating a protocol for its application to real-world problems (Section 5.1). This validation, which checks for consistency between real and simulated data behavior and tests the sensitivity to the approximate posterior initialization, significantly strengthens the paper. The scientific finding comparing model representations in Stan and WebPPL is a complete and interesting application of the proposed technique.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-motivated. However, the clarity could be improved in a few key areas. The transition from the background in Section 2 to the main theoretical contribution in Section 3.1 is abrupt. The derivation in equations (4) through (11) is dense, and the variables `v` and `xT` are introduced without sufficient context, making this central section difficult to parse on a first reading. A few sentences motivating the decomposition of the forward and reverse chains and defining the notation more explicitly before presenting the equations would greatly aid readability. The paper is also marred by a few minor presentational issues, such as inconsistent notation for the data (sometimes `y`, sometimes `D`) and a potential typo in Section 2.3, where the text states one can obtain posterior samples for "simulated observations using the elementary fact that p(y) p(θ, z|y) = p(θ, z, y)". This is correct, but perhaps `p(θ, z, y)` was intended in the first part of the sentence to align with the ancestral sampling description.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds directly on BDMC [GGA15], the insight to use the gap between the bounds to quantify the divergence from the true posterior is novel and significant. This reframes BDMC from a tool for estimating marginal likelihoods into a powerful diagnostic for MCMC convergence. The application of this idea to evaluate and debug probabilistic programming languages is also a novel and important contribution. The authors appropriately cite and discuss related work, including concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], clearly delineating the relationship between the two approaches. If the Jeffreys divergence bound is indeed a novel contribution of this paper, it would be beneficial for the authors to state this more explicitly, as it is a central result.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. The lack of reliable, quantitative, and general-purpose diagnostics for MCMC is a major outstanding problem in computational statistics and machine learning. This work provides a principled method that moves beyond heuristics. It is likely that researchers developing new MCMC algorithms and PPLs will find this tool extremely useful for evaluation and debugging, as demonstrated by the authors' own experience finding a bug in WebPPL. Practitioners could also use this method to gain confidence in the results of a black-box inference engine or to make principled choices about model representation. The implementation and demonstration in both Stan and WebPPL showcases the method's potential for broad impact.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and principled method for quantitatively measuring MCMC sample quality by bounding the Jeffreys divergence to the true posterior.
*   **Pro:** It addresses a significant and long-standing problem in the field, moving beyond common convergence heuristics.
*   **Pro:** The proposed methodology is thoroughly validated with well-designed experiments that address its primary limitations.
*   **Pro:** The work has high potential impact, providing a valuable tool for PPL developers, MCMC researchers, and sophisticated practitioners. The demonstration of its use for model selection and debugging is very compelling.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The clarity of the main theoretical section (3.1) could be substantially improved, as it is currently difficult to follow.
*   **Con:** The method's rigorous guarantees are limited to simulated data, and its application to real data relies on a protocol whose validity may be model-dependent. (Note: The authors do a good job of mitigating this concern with their validation experiments).
*   **Con:** Minor issues with notation and presentation detract from the overall quality of the manuscript.

### Detailed Comments

1.  I strongly suggest revising Section 3.1 to improve its clarity. Before introducing Equation (4), it would be helpful to briefly explain the "extended state space" view of AIS, defining `xT` as the final sample and `v` as the collection of intermediate states. This would make the subsequent derivations much more accessible.

2.  The paper's central theoretical contribution appears to be the connection between the BDMC gap and the Jeffreys divergence bound. I recommend making this claim more explicit in the introduction and methods sections. For instance, stating "Our key theoretical contribution is to show that..." would highlight this for the reader.

3.  The experiments showing that the method can be used to debug a PPL implementation are excellent. This is a very powerful and practical application that strengthens the paper's overall message.

4.  To maximize the impact of this work, I encourage the authors to make their code extensions for WebPPL and Stan easily accessible. For WebPPL, this could be an `npm` package. For Stan, clear instructions on how to apply the necessary patch or use the extended version would be very beneficial for the community.
