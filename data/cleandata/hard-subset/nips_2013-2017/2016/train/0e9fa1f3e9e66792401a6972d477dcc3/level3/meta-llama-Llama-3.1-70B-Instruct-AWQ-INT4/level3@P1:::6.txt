This paper presents an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to evaluate the accuracy of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms. The authors propose a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples produced by MCMC algorithms. The method is based on the idea of running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data.

The authors provide a thorough background on AIS, stochastic lower bounds on the log partition function ratio, and BDMC. They then describe their method, BREAD, which uses BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The authors also propose a protocol for using BREAD to evaluate the accuracy of posterior inference on real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

The paper reports on experiments using BREAD to evaluate the accuracy of posterior inference in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The authors validate the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data and demonstrate the effectiveness of BREAD in diagnosing issues with MCMC samplers.

However, the paper appears to be more of an implementation and application of previously published results on BDMC rather than a novel contribution. The authors do not seem to introduce any new ideas or methods, but rather demonstrate the usefulness of existing techniques in the context of probabilistic programming languages.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a clear and thorough explanation of the background material, making it accessible to readers who may not be familiar with AIS and BDMC.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of BREAD in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in two probabilistic programming languages.
* The paper includes a detailed description of the experiments and results, making it easy to follow and reproduce.

Weaknesses:

* The paper appears to lack originality, as it primarily describes the implementation of known techniques rather than introducing new methods.
* The authors do not provide a clear justification for why BREAD is a better approach than existing methods for evaluating MCMC inference algorithms.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential drawbacks of BREAD.
