

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for evaluating MCMC inference quality by estimating the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide an upper bound in expectation on this divergence. The key insight is that by running AIS chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, one can obtain rigorous bounds on the divergence. This approach is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) where users need reliable diagnostics for black-box inference.

The paper relates well to previous work, though it could better acknowledge two key prior approaches for MCMC validation. Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006) proposed validating MCMC by running inference on data sampled from the prior distribution. These methods provide a gold standard for checking whether an inference algorithm can recover the true parameters when the data is generated from the prior. The authors' approach is complementary, as it provides a quantitative measure of divergence rather than just a qualitative validation. These should be explicitly cited and discussed in the introduction.

The paper's main strengths include:
- A novel application of BDMC for MCMC diagnostics, addressing a real need in PPLs
- Theoretical derivation showing that the BDMC method provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence
- Practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan, with validation on multiple models
- Demonstrated utility in debugging (exposing a bug in WebPPL)
- Clear protocol for applying the method to real-world data

The paper also has several weaknesses that need addressing:

1. **Clarity issues in Section 3.1**: The derivation in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow because q(.) is used before being properly defined. The authors should explicitly state that q(.) refers to "the marginal of the AIS sampler after T steps" or similar. The derivation would be much clearer if the authors defined all terms before using them.

2. **Insufficient detail in main text**: Key derivation details are pushed to the supplement, making the main text less self-contained. The authors should include the most important steps in the main text to help readers follow the method's application.

3. **Unnecessary variable splitting**: The paper splits unobserved variables into z and theta, though they always appear together. This is unnecessary clutter that could be simplified to a single variable notation.

4. **Typographical error**: There's a missing closing parenthesis at line 165.

5. **Insufficient discussion of prior work**: While the paper mentions related work on MCMC diagnostics, it fails to adequately contrast with Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006), which directly address the problem of validating MCMC by running on data from the prior.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field of probabilistic programming and MCMC diagnostics. The BREAD method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic way to monitor inference quality, which is particularly valuable for non-expert users of PPLs. The demonstration of debugging a WebPPL bug shows practical utility.

The paper meets all quality criteria: the claims are theoretically supported, the method is validated on multiple models, and the authors are careful to discuss limitations. The significance is high as it addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic programming where users cannot verify inference quality.

Regarding originality: While the core BDMC method is from Grosse et al. (2015), the application to MCMC diagnostics for PPLs is novel. The paper properly cites this prior work and clearly differentiates its contribution.

For clarity, the paper would benefit from:
- Rewriting Section 3.1 with clear definitions before usage
- Including key derivations in the main text
- Simplifying the variable notation
- Correcting the typo
- Adding discussion of Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006) in the introduction

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant practical value for the probabilistic programming community. The minor issues noted above can be easily addressed in a revision. The paper should be accepted as it makes a valuable contribution to the field with clear applications and rigorous methodology. The proposed BREAD method provides a much-needed tool for evaluating MCMC inference quality in PPLs, addressing a critical gap in current diagnostic approaches.
