

This paper proposes "BREAD" (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to measure MCMC reliability by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that BMC provides stochastic upper bounds on log marginal likelihood, which can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence when exact posterior samples are available. To overcome the limitation that BMC requires exact samples (which is typically unavailable for real data), they propose using synthetic data generated from fitted hyperparameters, with the assumption that the synthetic posterior's convergence properties mirror those of the real posterior.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretically grounded approach to MCMC diagnostics and its practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan. The authors provide a clear derivation showing how BMC bounds the Jeffreys divergence, and the empirical validation on toy models demonstrates reasonable accuracy. This work builds upon the BMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015) and relates to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), though it distinguishes itself by focusing on divergence bounds rather than subjective divergence.

However, the paper suffers from three major methodological weaknesses that undermine its core claims. First, it assumes a known representative hyperparameter value η_real from prior MCMC runs, presuming those runs already converged and a single summary is sufficient—a circular assumption when diagnosing convergence. Second, it assumes synthetic-data posteriors have similar convergence properties to real posteriors, which is unjustified under model misspecification. Third, it assumes starting from η_real allows MCMC to quickly produce near-exact samples, an optimistic and unverified claim. These assumptions directly relate to the convergence problem the paper aims to solve, making the diagnostic's validity questionable if they fail.

The empirical evidence is limited to simple examples in Section 5.1, which are insufficient to demonstrate robustness in complex settings. The paper lacks comparisons to standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin) and fails to address how their method handles multimodality or poor summary statistics.

Quality: The theoretical foundation is sound, but the assumptions undermine the practical validity.  
Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written.  
Originality: The application of BMC to MCMC diagnostics is novel, though the core BMC technique is not.  
Significance: The potential utility is limited by the unverified assumptions, reducing its impact on real-world MCMC reliability assessment.

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework, the fundamental assumptions about synthetic data and hyperparameter fitting severely limit its practical applicability. I recommend rejection due to these critical methodological flaws, though the authors should be encouraged to address these limitations in future work.
