This paper proposes a novel method, AutoBDMC, for evaluating the convergence of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) techniques. The authors extend the recently introduced BDMC technique to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve. The proposed method is also well-described, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of its properties. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in evaluating the convergence of MCMC algorithms.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a single scalar convergence metric, which is a significant improvement over existing methods. The authors also demonstrate the additional uses of their method for model selection and debugging.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The method is limited to analysis on synthetic data sampled from the joint, and the authors acknowledge that this may not be representative of real-world data. While they provide experiments showing synthetic-data analyses predict real-data behavior, more work is needed to fully understand the limitations of the method.

Additionally, some of the notation and variable definitions are introduced without sufficient motivation, making it difficult to follow the paper in some sections. The authors should consider adding more explanations and justifications for their notation and variable choices.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a clearer definition of AutoBDMC. While the authors describe the method in detail, they never explicitly define what AutoBDMC denotes. This could lead to confusion for readers who are not familiar with the method.

Finally, there are some minor issues with the paper, such as potential typos and inconsistent notation for data. These issues are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on existing work on BDMC and AIS, but the authors provide a novel application of these techniques to evaluate MCMC convergence. The paper is well-referenced, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of MCMC convergence evaluation. While there are some limitations and minor issues, the paper is a valuable addition to the literature and has the potential to be widely used in the field.
