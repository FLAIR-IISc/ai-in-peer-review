- Summary: Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between MCMC approximate and true posteriors by using the gap between BDMC stochastic bounds and applying it to AIS/SMC with a synthetic-data protocol.

- Strength: Tackles an important problem—diagnosing MCMC convergence and measuring posterior sample quality beyond heuristic checks.

- Strength: Provides a novel theoretical link showing BDMC bounds give an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, yielding a principled distributional quality measure.

- Strength: Demonstrates practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan and uses BREAD to guide modeling choices and debug implementations.

- Weakness: Protocol is circular—requires an initial (assumed well-converged) MCMC estimate of hyperparameters ˆη_real, relying on the very convergence it aims to assess.

- Weakness: Assumes posterior behavior on synthetic data generated from ˆη_real matches that of the real data posterior, a strong unvalidated assumption especially under model misspecification.

- Weakness: Initialization of the reverse AIS chain from generative parameters plus a few MCMC steps is optimistic and may not yield a representative posterior sample for hard posteriors.

- Weakness: Empirical validation is limited to standard models and does not show robustness in challenging high-dimensional or multimodal settings where diagnostics are most needed.

- Suggestion: Address circularity—e.g., use an ensemble of ˆη_real estimates or other strategies to assess robustness to the initial hyperparameter estimate.

- Suggestion: Empirically investigate the posterior-similarity assumption, including studies under known model misspecification.

- Suggestion: Compare BREAD directly to standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, multiple chains) on multimodal or failure cases to show added value.

- Suggestion: Analyze how bound tightness and estimator behavior scale with dimensionality and problem difficulty.

- Overall evaluation: Theoretical contribution is strong and promising, but practical applicability is limited by strong, largely unverified assumptions in the proposed protocol and insufficient empirical validation on challenging inference problems.
