This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) upper and lower stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood. The authors extend the BMC technique to assess MCMC reliability and compare sampler efficiency by choosing MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC bounds for equal computational effort. The key idea is to use BMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the target distribution and the distribution of approximate samples produced by Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) samplers.

The paper makes several contributions. First, it shows that BMC yields an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence of approximate samples from the true posterior. Second, it presents a technique for exactly computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, and shows that the upper bound is often a good match in practice. Third, it proposes a protocol for using BMC to evaluate the accuracy of approximate inference on real-world datasets by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters. Finally, it extends both WebPPL and Stan to implement the proposed method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), and validates BREAD on a variety of probabilistic models in both frameworks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. The experimental results are also impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of BREAD in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in various settings.

However, there are some limitations and potential drawbacks to the proposed method. One major assumption is that the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior, which may not always be justified, especially under model misspecification. Additionally, the method assumes that starting from the true hyperparameter value η_real lets the MCMC quickly produce an almost exact sample, which is an optimistic and unverified assumption. These assumptions directly relate to the original convergence problem, undermining the diagnostic's validity if they fail.

Furthermore, the empirical evidence is limited, and the simple examples in Section 5.1 are not convincing that the issues won't matter in more complex settings. The authors should provide more extensive experiments to demonstrate the robustness of BREAD in various scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper builds upon previous work on BMC and AIS, but the extension to bounding the Jeffreys divergence and the proposed protocol for evaluating real-world datasets are novel contributions.

In conclusion, the paper presents a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic tool, which is interesting and has the potential to be useful in practice. However, the authors should address the limitations and potential drawbacks mentioned above to increase the confidence in the proposed method.
