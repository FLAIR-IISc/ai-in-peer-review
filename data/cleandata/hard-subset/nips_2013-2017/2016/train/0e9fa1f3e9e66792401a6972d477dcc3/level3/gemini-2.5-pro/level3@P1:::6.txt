This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by certain MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method is a direct extension of the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique introduced by Grosse et al. (2015). The core idea of BDMC is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both a forward direction (from prior to posterior) and a reverse direction (from posterior to prior) to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood, respectively. The key contribution of this paper is to show that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples from the forward AIS chain and the true posterior distribution. The authors implement this diagnostic tool within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. Because the reverse chain requires an exact posterior sample, the method's guarantees only hold for simulated data. To address this, the paper proposes a heuristic protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets by first inferring hyperparameters, then simulating new data from these parameters. The utility of the method is demonstrated through a series of validation experiments and a case study comparing different model representations for matrix factorization.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation that connects the BDMC log-likelihood gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a correct and straightforward extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015). The experiments are well-conducted and provide evidence for the utility of the proposed diagnostic. For instance, the case study comparing collapsed and uncollapsed samplers for matrix factorization provides a clear example of how BREAD can guide modeling decisions. The fact that the method helped uncover a bug in WebPPL is a strong practical testament to its usefulness as a validation tool for developers of inference systems.

However, a key weakness is the method's applicability to real-world data. The rigorous bound on Jeffreys divergence is only valid on simulated data where an exact posterior sample can be obtained to initialize the reverse chain. The proposed protocol to circumvent this for real data is a heuristic: it uses an *approximate* posterior sample to initialize the reverse process. While the experiments suggest this may work well in practice, it undermines the primary advantage of the method—its mathematical guarantees. The paper should be more upfront about the fact that for real data, BREAD becomes another diagnostic heuristic, rather than a rigorous bounding tool.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The background section provides an excellent and concise overview of AIS and BDMC, making the paper self-contained and accessible. The main idea is communicated effectively, and the experimental setup and results are presented in an easy-to-understand manner.

**Originality:** The primary weakness of this paper is its limited originality. The core methodological machinery—using forward and reverse AIS to sandwich the log marginal likelihood—was entirely proposed in Grosse et al. (2015). This paper's main theoretical contribution is the observation that the gap between the BDMC bounds also bounds the Jeffreys divergence. While this is a useful insight, it is an incremental step built directly upon the existing BDMC analysis. The bulk of the paper is dedicated to the implementation and application of this technique in Stan and WebPPL. While such engineering work is valuable to the community, it feels more like an application paper or a systems demonstration rather than a work introducing a novel method. The contribution does not seem substantial enough for a top-tier conference like NIPS, which typically requires a greater degree of methodological novelty.

**Significance:** The work addresses the highly significant and challenging problem of diagnosing the convergence and sample quality of MCMC algorithms. This is particularly crucial in the context of probabilistic programming, where inference engines can be treated as black boxes by users. Providing a quantitative tool that goes beyond standard heuristics like the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic is a valuable contribution. Researchers developing new inference algorithms or probabilistic programming languages could find this tool extremely useful for debugging and evaluation.

However, the overall significance is tempered by the originality and practical limitations mentioned above. Since the rigorous guarantees are restricted to simulated data, the direct utility for a practitioner analyzing a single, real-world dataset is limited. The practitioner is more interested in the quality of inference on their actual data, not on a simulated facsimile. While the authors argue persuasively that consistency between real and simulated data provides confidence, it remains an indirect form of validation.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses an important and difficult problem: reliably measuring MCMC sample quality.
2.  The proposed method, BREAD, provides a more principled diagnostic than many existing heuristics by bounding a formal divergence metric (at least on simulated data).
3.  The implementation in popular frameworks like Stan and WebPPL is a useful contribution to the community and increases the practical impact of the work.
4.  The experiments are well-executed and demonstrate the utility of the method for tasks like comparing model representations and debugging inference code.
5.  The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of novelty:** The work is a minor and incremental extension of the previously published BDMC method [Grosse et al., 2015]. The main contribution is re-interpreting an existing result rather than proposing a new technique.
2.  The paper's focus is largely on the application and implementation of an existing idea, which may not meet the bar for a NIPS publication.
3.  The method's rigorous guarantees do not apply to real-world data, which is the setting of primary interest for most practitioners. The proposed workaround is a heuristic that weakens the method's main appeal.

In summary, this is a solid piece of work that presents a useful application of an existing technique. However, its lack of significant methodological novelty makes it a borderline case for NIPS. The contribution feels more appropriate for a specialized venue, such as a workshop on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, or a journal focusing on scientific software.
