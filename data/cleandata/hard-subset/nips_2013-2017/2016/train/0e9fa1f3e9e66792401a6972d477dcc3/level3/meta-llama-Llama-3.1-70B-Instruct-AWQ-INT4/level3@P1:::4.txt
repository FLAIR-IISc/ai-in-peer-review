This paper proposes a novel method, AutoBDMC, for measuring the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The method extends the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, the authors upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper is well-motivated and presents a useful method with reasonably compelling experiments. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method by integrating it into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validating it on several models and datasets. They also show that their method can be used to guide the design of inference algorithms and to analyze the effectiveness of different model representations.

However, there are some minor issues with the paper. Section 3, especially the transition into 3.1, is hard to follow, and variable/equation definitions are introduced without sufficient motivation. Additionally, the paper refers to "AutoBDMC" before it is clearly defined, and the notation for data is inconsistent. Furthermore, the supplemental WebPPL code needs to be released as an npm-installable WebPPL package, and the Stan code should also be released.

In terms of the quality of the paper, it is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is also well-written, and the authors provide enough background information for non-experts to understand the context and significance of their work.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference. The method is also significant, as it addresses a long-standing problem in the field of probabilistic inference. The paper also demonstrates the potential of the method for model selection and debugging.

The clarity of the paper is good, but there are some areas where the writing could be improved. The authors could provide more motivation for the variable and equation definitions, and the notation could be made more consistent. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more explanations and examples to help non-experts understand the technical details.

With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference.
