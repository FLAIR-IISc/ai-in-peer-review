This paper proposes Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), a method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based inference in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The core idea is to extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide a quantitative measure of sampler performance. By running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) in both the forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions on simulated data, the authors derive an estimator that provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. Since this requires an exact posterior sample, the method is primarily applicable to simulated data. The authors propose a protocol to extend its utility to real-world datasets by first fitting hyperparameters and then generating simulated data from the inferred posterior predictive distribution. The method is implemented and validated in two popular PPLs, Stan and WebPPL.

This work addresses the important and challenging problem of MCMC diagnostics. Existing methods are often heuristic and can only detect specific modes of non-convergence, whereas this paper offers a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality, which is a significant strength. The theoretical connection between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence is novel and elegant. The experiments are compelling, particularly the case study comparing model representations in Stan and WebPPL, which showcases the method's practical utility for guiding modeling decisions. The fact that BREAD was used to uncover a bug in WebPPL further underscores its value as a rigorous debugging tool.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed. First, the discussion of related work is incomplete. The protocol for validating inference on simulated data is similar in spirit to established techniques. The authors should cite and discuss prior work in this area, such as Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman, and Rubin (2006), which also advocate for validating MCMC samplers by running them on data generated from the model's prior.

Second, the clarity of the methodology in Section 3.1 could be significantly improved. The distribution of the approximate samples, denoted `qfwd(xT)`, is used in the derivation before it is explicitly defined as the marginal distribution of the AIS sampler's output after T steps. Furthermore, placing key derivation details in the supplement makes the main text difficult to follow and not self-contained. Presenting a clearer, step-by-step application would strengthen the paper.

Minor issues include cluttered notation from splitting unobserved variables into z and theta when they always appear together, and a typographical error (missing closing parenthesis) around line 165.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a significant contribution. The weaknesses are addressable through revision.
