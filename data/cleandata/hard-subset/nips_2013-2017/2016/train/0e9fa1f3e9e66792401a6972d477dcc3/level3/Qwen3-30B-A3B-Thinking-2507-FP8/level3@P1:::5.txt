

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) for assessing the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms. The authors leverage BDMC's ability to produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to derive an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. This builds on Grosse et al.'s (2015) BDMC framework, which was originally developed to estimate marginal likelihoods. The key innovation is applying this to evaluate the quality of posterior samples from MCMC-based inference, rather than just marginal likelihoods. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its application to model representation comparisons and debugging.

The paper's main strengths include its theoretically grounded approach to a long-standing problem in MCMC inference. The derivation showing that the BDMC estimator upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is technically sound and well-motivated. The integration into two major probabilistic programming languages demonstrates practical utility, and the empirical validation on matrix factorization models reveals actionable insights about model representation choices. The debugging example with WebPPL (revealing a subtle bug in multivariate Gaussian sampling) powerfully demonstrates BREAD's potential as a diagnostic tool for implementation correctness.

However, the paper's most significant limitation is its reliance on a synthetic data protocol to evaluate inference quality on real-world data. The authors assume that hyperparameters estimated from real data (via potentially unreliable MCMC) can generate synthetic data whose inference behavior matches the real data. This creates a circular dependency: the method assumes the real-data MCMC has already converged to produce reliable hyperparameters, which is precisely the problem it aims to diagnose. The paper does not adequately justify why the synthetic data would have similar convergence properties to the real data, particularly under model misspecification or multimodality. The validation experiments in Section 5.1.1 show promising consistency between real and synthetic data for five simple models, but these are not complex enough to demonstrate robustness to challenging inference scenarios.

Another concern is the method's applicability scope. BREAD is directly applicable only to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over extended state spaces (like AIS or SMC), not to general MCMC samplers. The paper claims broader relevance through the "design choices" of these algorithms, but this connection remains somewhat tenuous. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently address how their diagnostic could be extended to more general MCMC methods.

The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the practical utility is limited by the synthetic data assumptions. While the paper provides a theoretically justified diagnostic tool, the assumptions required to apply it in practice undermine its validity in the most critical scenarios where MCMC convergence is uncertain. The authors should provide more rigorous justification for their synthetic data protocol, particularly regarding multimodal posteriors or cases where the prior is highly diffuse relative to the data.

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by these limitations. While the approach is interesting and the implementation is well-executed, the method's practical applicability is questionable given the assumptions required. The authors' claim that BREAD "provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring the quality of posterior inference" is overstated, as the protocol depends on the very MCMC convergence it aims to diagnose.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with thorough technical derivations and careful experimental validation. The related work section adequately covers convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996) and connects to recent work on probabilistic programming diagnostics (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to MCMC diagnostics but is undermined by critical assumptions that limit its practical applicability. The authors should clarify the specific conditions under which their method can be reliably applied and provide more rigorous justification for their synthetic data protocol, particularly for challenging inference scenarios. The method shows promise but requires more substantial validation before it can be considered a reliable diagnostic tool for general MCMC inference.

Pro: Theoretical derivation of BREAD is sound and extends BDMC to a useful diagnostic; practical integration into WebPPL and Stan; demonstrates valuable insights for model representation and debugging.

Con: Relies on unverified assumptions about synthetic data matching real data; assumes real-data MCMC has already converged (the problem being diagnosed); limited to AIS/SMC samplers; lacks validation on complex, multimodal models where diagnostics are most needed.
