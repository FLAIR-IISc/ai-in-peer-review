Summary and relation to prior work
The paper revisits bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) and applies it to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) both forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) to obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on log p(y). Building on standard AIS/BDMC identities (Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015), the authors note that the expected gap between the reverse and forward log-AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate AIS samples and the true posterior. They then integrate this procedure into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on toy examples and several small realistic models, and propose a practical protocol for “real data” diagnostics by fitting hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulating from the fitted model (akin to a parametric bootstrap; Efron & Tibshirani, 1998), and running BDMC on the simulated data. They also report that the method helped uncover a bug in WebPPL.

The approach is closely related to thermodynamic/annealing arguments (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997) and sequential Monte Carlo (Del Moral et al., 2006), and to prior/concurrent uses of BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al., 2015; Burda et al., 2015). Independent work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) introduces a “subjective divergence” estimator derived from the same BDMC machinery; when the reverse chain is initialized at the true posterior, their bound reduces to the same Jeffreys-divergence control discussed here. The general theme contrasts with traditional MCMC diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996) and with direct sample-quality measures such as Stein discrepancy (Gorham & Mackey, 2015).

Strengths
- Clear, accessible summary of BDMC/AIS and how their expectations relate to divergences between approximate and true posteriors.
- Solid engineering effort: integration in two probabilistic programming systems (WebPPL and Stan), with examples across linear/logistic regression, AR models, mixture models, and matrix factorization.
- Practical protocol for connecting simulated-data guarantees to real-data workflows via fitted hyperparameters; the consistency checks between real and simulated datasets are useful.
- Demonstrated utility as a testing/QA tool (bug discovery in WebPPL), which is valuable for the PPL community.
- Empirical study comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations across platforms, providing actionable guidance for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the key observation that the forward/reverse AIS gap upper bounds Jeffreys divergence follows directly from BDMC importance-sampling identities; closely overlapping results appear in prior/concurrent work (Grosse et al., 2015; Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016). The “BREAD” rebranding does not introduce new methodology.
- Guarantees hold only on simulated data with exact posterior initialization; for real data, the fitted-hyperparameter procedure breaks the guarantee and becomes heuristic. The paper does not quantify how conservative the bound remains in this setting or how it correlates with true error.
- Evaluation is small-scale and largely qualitative (quartiles of AIS logs). There is little evidence of scalability, robustness in high dimensions or multimodal settings, or comparison against standard diagnostics (R-hat/ESS) and Stein-based measures.
- The choice of annealing schedules, number of temperatures, and transition kernels crucially affects estimates; the paper provides limited guidance or ablation on these design choices.
- The bound is only in expectation; per-instance guarantees are not provided, which complicates interpretation for practitioners.

Quality
Technically sound as a summary/implementation of known results. The derivations mirror standard BDMC analyses; toy examples support tightness in simple cases. The empirical sections are careful about potential pitfalls (e.g., shared initial state in reverse chains). However, claims about practical reliability on real data rest on a heuristic protocol without rigorous control; additional quantitative validation (e.g., correlation between BDMC gap and held-out predictive metrics or distances to gold-standard posterior) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Well written and well organized, with helpful recaps of AIS/BDMC and clear statements of assumptions and scope. Suggestions:
- Emphasize earlier and more explicitly that bounds are expectation-only and require exact posterior samples; clarify implications for per-dataset decisions.
- Provide concrete guidance on temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and kernel choices, and how these affect the bound.
- Report not only quartiles but also stabilized estimates (e.g., averaging log-weights via log-sum-exp) where justified and compare variability across seeds.

Originality
Low. The key theoretical statements are straightforward consequences of BDMC and appear concurrently in closely related work. The primary contribution is an application/implementation in WebPPL and Stan and an engineering protocol for real-data usage.

Significance
As a systems/practice contribution for PPL users, the tooling is useful and could encourage better diagnostics. As a NIPS main-track paper, the lack of conceptual novelty and limited empirical depth reduce impact. This seems more suitable for an MCMC/PPL workshop or a systems venue emphasizing tooling and engineering insights.

Pros for acceptance
- Useful, general-purpose diagnostic tooling integrated in widely used PPLs.
- Clear exposition that may help practitioners adopt BDMC-based checks.
- Empirical case study informing modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed).

Cons for acceptance
- Minimal novelty beyond prior BDMC literature; overlaps with concurrent work.
- Guarantees restricted to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic without calibration.
- Limited empirical scale and lack of comparisons to established diagnostics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative calibration on problems with known ground truth (e.g., small models where exact posteriors are tractable) showing correlation between the BDMC gap and true divergences or predictive errors.
- Compare against R-hat/ESS and Stein discrepancy, and analyze where BDMC adds value.
- Explore sensitivity to annealing schedules, T, kernels, and dimensionality; offer practical defaults.
- For real data, develop a principled reference distribution for reverse AIS (cf. subjective divergence), and bound the additional bias.

Recommendation
Reject for NIPS main track due to limited originality; recommend presentation at an MCMC/PPL workshop where the implementation insights and practical protocol would find the appropriate audience.
