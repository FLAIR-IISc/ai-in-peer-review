This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference by leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The core idea is to utilize the strengths of BDMC – providing stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood – to quantify the accuracy of MCMC algorithms, particularly Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validate it on several models, using it to guide design choices related to model representation.

The paper builds upon the recent work on BDMC [GGA15] and extends it to the problem of posterior inference evaluation. The motivation is strong: rigorously validating MCMC inference is notoriously difficult, and current diagnostics often lack quantitative guarantees. The attempt to provide a theoretically grounded diagnostic tool is a valuable contribution, particularly in the context of “black box” inference methods employed in probabilistic programming languages. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates a practical application of the methodology.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses that limit its impact. A central assumption – and a major point of concern – is the reliance on hyperparameters estimated from initial MCMC runs on real data to generate simulated datasets. The authors acknowledge this introduces potential issues, but the justification for assuming these initial runs provide a “representative” hyperparameter estimate is weak. If the initial MCMC runs *fail* to converge properly (the very problem BREAD aims to address!), the resulting simulated data will be unrepresentative, and any conclusions drawn from BREAD will be misleading. This creates a circularity: the diagnostic's validity hinges on the assumption that the inference it is evaluating is already reasonably accurate.

Furthermore, the assumption that inference behavior on simulated data accurately reflects behavior on real data is questionable, especially in the presence of model misspecification. The authors attempt to address this by running a few MCMC steps from the estimated hyperparameters to generate a better starting point for the reverse chain, but the theoretical justification for this procedure is limited. It is not clear why a few MCMC steps will reliably produce a sample sufficiently close to the true posterior to ensure the validity of the upper bound.

The applicability of the method is also limited. BREAD directly applies to AIS and SMC algorithms, and the authors acknowledge its indirect relevance to general MCMC methods. The focus on bounding the Jeffreys divergence, while theoretically sound, might not be the most informative metric for practical users. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate why this metric is superior to existing diagnostics, such as R-hat or effective sample size, particularly given the strong assumptions required for its application.

The experimental validation, while thorough for the chosen examples, is not particularly convincing. The models used are relatively simple, and the results presented in Section 5.1, while showing consistency between real and simulated data, do not address the concerns about the underlying assumptions. The demonstration of BREAD’s usefulness in guiding model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) is interesting but relies heavily on the specific models and datasets used.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretically motivated approach to evaluating MCMC inference.
*   Demonstrates a practical implementation within popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan).
*   Highlights the importance of rigorous diagnostic tools for black-box inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on strong, potentially invalidating assumptions about the accuracy of initial MCMC runs and the representativeness of simulated data.
*   Limited applicability beyond AIS and SMC algorithms.
*   The focus on the Jeffreys divergence might not be the most informative metric for practical users.
*   Experimental validation is limited and does not fully address the concerns about the underlying assumptions.
*   Does not adequately address how the method performs in cases of multimodality or with poorly chosen summary statistics.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the significant weaknesses and strong assumptions undermine its practical value. While the theoretical framework is sound, the reliance on potentially flawed initial inferences and the questionable applicability to real-world scenarios raise serious concerns. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the assumptions more thoroughly, provide simulations demonstrating robustness to model misspecification and initial inference failures, and clearly articulate the advantages of using the Jeffreys divergence over existing diagnostic tools. A comparison to established diagnostics (R-hat, effective sample size) in a wider range of scenarios is essential.
