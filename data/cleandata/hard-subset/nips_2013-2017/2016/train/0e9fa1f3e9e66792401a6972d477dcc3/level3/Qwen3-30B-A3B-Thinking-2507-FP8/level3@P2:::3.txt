

# Review for "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors derive that the gap between BDMC's stochastic lower and upper bounds upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. They integrate this method into WebPPL and Stan, and propose a protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters.

The paper builds on Grosse et al.'s BDMC work (GGA15) and relates to recent efforts in convergence diagnostics. While related work on convergence diagnostics (Brooks and Gelman 1998; Cowles and Carlin 1996) and methods for estimating sample quality (Gorham and Mackey 2015) are mentioned, the paper misses a crucial connection to Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which shares conceptual similarities with BDMC and provides an acceptance ratio interpretation that would have strengthened the theoretical foundation.

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution that is well-supported by experimental results on simulated data. The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound (Section 3.1) is the core technical contribution, though it contains several issues: Equation (6) appears incorrect as written (KL arguments appear identical), the definition of vector v is ambiguous, and the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ is never defined. The experiments validate the method on five models and demonstrate its utility in debugging WebPPL and comparing model representations. However, the paper lacks sufficient detail on the $\beta$ schedule choice (a critical parameter affecting bounds and comparisons), and the "AutoBDMC" concept is never clearly defined.

**Clarity:** The writing quality suffers from several issues that significantly hinder comprehension. Notation is inconsistent (using "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back"), key terms are undefined (RAIS, u/cRAIS), and critical equations contain errors (missing parentheses in D_JS expression). Sections 3.1-3.2 feel rushed and contain ambiguities that would make replication difficult. Figures have poor labeling and small axes/tick labels. The connection between the theoretical contribution and practical implementation is not clearly articulated.

**Originality:** While the application of BDMC to evaluate MCMC inference is valuable, the core insight that the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence is not clearly novel. The paper should have explicitly acknowledged the connection to Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which provides a similar framework for analyzing convergence. The method itself is a relatively straightforward application of existing BDMC techniques rather than a novel theoretical contribution.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in probabilistic programming: reliably measuring the quality of MCMC inference. The method has high potential impact for practitioners using probabilistic programming languages, as demonstrated by its use in debugging WebPPL and providing insights into model representation choices. The protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data (Section 3.2) is particularly valuable for practitioners. However, the method's applicability is limited to certain inference algorithms (AIS, SMC), and the requirement for simulated data somewhat restricts its immediate utility.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a rigorous, general method for evaluating MCMC inference quality
- Demonstrates practical utility through debugging and model representation analysis
- Integrates into two major probabilistic programming frameworks (Stan, WebPPL)
- Validates the method through careful experiments on multiple models

**Weaknesses:**
- Missing key related work (Neal 1996 Tempered Transitions)
- Technical issues in derivations and notation
- Inadequate discussion of critical parameters (β schedule)
- Ambiguous definition of key concepts ("AutoBDMC")
- Insufficient experimental detail (e.g., β_t choice in practice)
- Several typos and presentation issues

**Recommendation:** The paper addresses an important problem with potential high impact for probabilistic programming practitioners. However, the technical contributions are modest and the paper contains significant clarity issues that would impede adoption. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address the theoretical and presentation issues before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. Specifically, they should: 1) clarify the theoretical contribution by connecting to Neal's work, 2) fix all technical errors and ambiguities in derivations, 3) provide more detail on implementation choices, and 4) clarify the meaning of "AutoBDMC."

Given the current state of the paper, I recommend rejection. The work has potential value but requires significant revision to meet NIPS standards for technical quality and clarity. With the suggested revisions, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
