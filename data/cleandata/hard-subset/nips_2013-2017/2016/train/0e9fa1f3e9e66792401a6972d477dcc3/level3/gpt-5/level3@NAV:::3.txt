This paper proposes using the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) gap—the difference between stochastic lower and upper bounds on log p(y) obtained via forward and reverse AIS—as a generic, implementation-agnostic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The authors show that, on simulated data, the expected BDMC gap upper bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the AIS sample distribution and the true posterior. They implement the method in Stan and WebPPL, introduce a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to proxy real-data behavior, and demonstrate applications to model representation choices and debugging. The work builds directly on BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), AIS (Neal, 2001), and connects to contemporaneous subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), as well as broader MCMC diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998) and Stein-based sample quality measures (Gorham & Mackey, 2015).

Strengths: The core idea—turning the BDMC gap into a quantitative, comparable measure of inference quality—is simple, practical, and potentially high-impact for probabilistic programming. The implementations for Stan and WebPPL and the real-data proxy protocol are valuable. The validation examples and the bug-finding anecdote illustrate genuine utility.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Quality/clarity: Sections 3.1–3.2 feel rushed. Equation (6) is incorrect as written (the KL arguments appear identical); the estimator p̂(y) used inside expectations in eqs. (5)–(7) and (9)–(11) is never defined; the vector v is loosely defined given two AIS sweeps; labels “rev/fwd” vs “for/back” are inconsistent. RAIS and (u)c(R)AIS are undefined in figure captions. Figures have tiny axes/ticks; one caption is ambiguous about whether “HMC/NUTS steps” refers to BDMC chains with that step count. Fix these and the typos (missing parenthesis in DJS; “Homan” → “Hoffman”).
- Originality: The observation that the expected BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys (or JS) divergence is useful but not clearly novel; please clarify novelty relative to BDMC (2015) and subjective divergence (2016).
- Related work: The paper should discuss Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996) and its acceptance-ratio interpretation, which is closely related to heating/cooling bounds and to BDMC’s reverse AIS.
- β schedule: The choice of βt is under-discussed; Δβ directly affects both bounds and cross-method comparisons. I recommend an iterative scheme that decreases Δβ until the gap stops shrinking (with stopping criteria and computational budget reporting), and experiments that explore schedule sensitivity.
- AutoBDMC: The “auto” mechanism is not defined; clarify whether it (a) uses the gap to assess convergence, (b) uses the fixed hyperparameter scheme of §3.2, or both.

Pros for acceptance:
- Practical, low-friction metric with broad applicability.
- Solid integrations in major PPLs; useful protocol; actionable experimental insights.

Cons against acceptance:
- Modest novelty; several technical/clarity errors impede verification and reproducibility.
- Insufficient treatment of annealing schedules and experimental details.

Recommendation: Borderline accept, contingent on fixing clarity/notation/equation issues, adding missing related work (especially Neal, 1996), and strengthening the schedule analysis by camera-ready.
