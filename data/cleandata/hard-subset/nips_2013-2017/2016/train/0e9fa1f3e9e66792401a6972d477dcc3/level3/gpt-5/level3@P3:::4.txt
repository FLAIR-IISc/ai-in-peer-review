Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior→posterior) and in reverse (posterior→prior) on synthetic datasets sampled from the joint. The log-marginal likelihood is sandwiched between stochastic lower and upper bounds; crucially, the expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS, yielding a single, scalar convergence metric. The method is instantiated in two probabilistic programming systems (WebPPL and Stan), and the paper further proposes a practical protocol to relate synthetic-data analyses to real data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters. Empirically, the bound tracks true divergence on toy cases, synthetic-data conclusions predict real-data behavior across several models, and the tool supports model representation decisions and debugging.

The work builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015) BDMC and Burda et al. (2015) reverse annealing, connects to AIS (Neal, 2001), SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006), and non-equilibrium estimators (Jarzynski, 1997), and complements convergence diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998) and sample-quality criteria (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). It overlaps with concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which also leverages BDMC ideas.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated goal: a quantitative, model-agnostic convergence metric for black-box MCMC.
- Solid theoretical contribution: expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence via AIS/rAIS gap; tightness validated on controlled examples.
- Practical impact: tool integrated into WebPPL and Stan; useful for model selection (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and for debugging (identifying a WebPPL bug).
- Thoughtful protocol to bridge simulated and real data; empirical evidence that fitted-hyperparameter simulations mirror real-data inference behavior.
- Readable overall, with useful experimental breadth across models and platforms.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope limitation: rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample, so the method is restricted to synthetic data drawn from the joint. While the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is reasonable and empirically supported, the guarantee becomes heuristic on real data; this should be emphasized crisply in the abstract and conclusions.
- Novelty clarity: If the divergence bound in Section 3.1 (that the expected AIS/rAIS gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence between p and the AIS sample distribution) is new, please state this explicitly and contrast with [GGA15] and [CTM16]. It is a central contribution and should be highlighted.
- Terminology and definition: “AutoBDMC” is referenced but never defined. Please clearly define what AutoBDMC denotes (it appears to be the automated use of AIS and reverse AIS to bound log-ML and thereby assess sample quality/convergence), and use a single term consistently (BREAD vs. AutoBDMC) to avoid confusion.
- Exposition in Section 3: The transition into 3.1 is hard to follow. Variables and densities (qfwd, qrev, v, xT, conditionals) appear with minimal motivation. Consider:
  - Starting with a high-level sketch of the proof idea (forward/reverse AIS as IS on extended space; gap equals sum of two KLs in expectation).
  - Introducing all random variables and conditionals in a single definitions box before equations (4–11).
  - Stating upfront that the bounds hold in expectation and clarifying when averaging across AIS chains is or is not justified by the analysis.
- JS divergence: Since the result upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence, and practitioners often prefer Jensen–Shannon (JS) divergence, clarify the relationship (e.g., whether the bound also controls JS up to a constant). If you claim a JS bound, state and prove it or provide a citation.
- Real-data protocol: The Section 3.2 “approximate posterior” initialization of the reverse chain is well-motivated and the sensitivity study vs. S steps is reassuring. Please discuss potential failure modes (e.g., strong prior–likelihood tension; hyperparameters concentrated away from fitted values) and diagnostics to detect them.
- Experimental details: For reproducibility, report AIS schedules (number and placement of temperatures), transition kernels and their tuning, number of repeats, and variance estimates for the bound gap. Clarify whether any adaptation is used during AIS transitions.
- Minor issues:
  - Possible typo around Line 149: p(theta, z | y) vs. p(theta, z, y).
  - Inconsistent notation for data (y vs. D); please standardize.
  - Clearly state that bounds are in expectation; where figures show single-chain quantiles, explain interpretation and when averaging is appropriate.

Reproducibility and code
- WebPPL: provide compatible versions, installation/integration instructions, and ideally release as an npm-installable package (with examples mirroring the paper).
- Stan: release full code and data generation scripts; pin Stan and CmdStan versions and compiler flags.
- Include random seeds and instructions to reproduce all figures.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The theoretical derivation appears correct and is connected to AIS importance sampling on an extended space. The empirical study is careful, and the fitted-hyperparameter validation is a strong point. Clarifications in Section 3 would further strengthen rigor and readability.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-structured, with a notable dip in clarity at the start of Section 3. Define AutoBDMC, unify terminology (AutoBDMC vs. BREAD), and clean up notation.

Originality
A novel and useful reframing of BDMC to yield an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence for AIS-like samplers, with an automated workflow. Relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is acknowledged; delineate precise differences.

Significance
High practical significance: a scalar, implementation-agnostic convergence metric that works across PPLs; helpful for model selection and debugging. Likely to be adopted by practitioners and to influence tooling in probabilistic programming.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, useful method with theoretical grounding and practical value.
- Cross-platform implementations and convincing experiments.
- Addresses a long-standing pain point in MCMC diagnostics; supports model comparison and debugging.

Cons against acceptance
- Guarantees limited to synthetic data; real-data use is heuristic.
- Some exposition gaps and undefined terminology (AutoBDMC).
- Novelty of the key bound not stated forcefully enough relative to prior/concurrent work.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. With improved clarity in Section 3, explicit positioning of the main theoretical contribution, a precise definition of AutoBDMC, and minor fixes, this paper would make a valuable addition to the NeurIPS program.
