This paper discusses the application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the accuracy of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms. The authors extend the BDMC technique to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples produced by annealed importance sampling (AIS). They integrate their method, called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets.

The main idea of the paper is to use BDMC to provide a rigorous and general method for monitoring the quality of posterior inference. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it to study the impact of different model representations on the performance of MCMC algorithms in WebPPL and Stan. They also show that BREAD can be used to debug the implementation of probabilistic programming languages.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the underlying concepts and techniques. The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature and position their work within the context of existing research on MCMC convergence diagnostics and probabilistic programming.

However, the paper's contributions are primarily limited to the application and implementation of existing techniques rather than the introduction of new methods or ideas. The authors acknowledge that their work builds on previously published results on BDMC, and the novelty of their contribution lies in the extension of these techniques to bound the Jeffreys divergence and their implementation in probabilistic programming languages.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their methods and results. However, the paper's significance and impact are somewhat limited by the fact that it primarily describes the implementation of known techniques rather than introducing new ideas or methods.
