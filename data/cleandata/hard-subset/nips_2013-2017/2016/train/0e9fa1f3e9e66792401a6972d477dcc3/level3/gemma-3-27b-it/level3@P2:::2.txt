This paper presents a novel method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. Building upon bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15], the authors extend the technique to provide a rigorous, expectation-based upper bound on this divergence, particularly relevant for “black box” inference methods where assessing quality is challenging. The method is integrated into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan and validated through experiments, including a compelling demonstration of its utility in guiding model representation choices.

The problem addressed is crucial. MCMC remains a cornerstone of probabilistic inference, yet assessing the quality of the resulting samples remains difficult, especially within the context of automated inference systems like probabilistic programming languages. Existing diagnostics often fall short of providing rigorous quantitative measures. This work directly tackles this gap, offering a potentially powerful tool for both end-users and developers. The connection to BDMC and the refinement of its analysis to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a significant contribution.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The technical analysis appears sound, carefully building upon prior work [GGA15, Nea01]. The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a substantial undertaking, demonstrating the practical applicability of the method. The experimental validation is well-designed, using both simulated and real-world data, and the protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data (using hyperparameter inference and simulation) is a clever solution to the problem of requiring exact posterior samples. The results presented—specifically, the insights gained regarding the choice of model representations in WebPPL and Stan—showcase the method’s potential to guide algorithmic and modeling decisions. The identification of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the utility of BREAD as a testing and validation tool. The clarity of the presentation is also commendable; the paper is well-organized and logically structured, making the complex methodology accessible.

However, there are some areas for potential improvement. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the method, further discussion on the computational cost of BREAD would be valuable. The runtime of running AIS chains both forward and reverse, and the need for exact samples (even for simulated data) can be significant, particularly for complex models. A more detailed discussion of scalability and potential approximations to reduce computational burden would strengthen the paper. Additionally, while the validation on five models is good, expanding this to a more diverse set of models and datasets would further solidify the method’s robustness. The discussion of the limitations of using approximate posterior samples for hyperparameter fitting is well-addressed, but exploring alternative strategies for generating more representative hyperparameter samples could be considered.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The method offers a novel approach to a long-standing problem in Bayesian inference. Providing a rigorous bound on the quality of approximate samples is a significant advance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is well-grounded and builds upon established techniques.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the method’s practical applicability and potential impact.
*   **Compelling Experiments:** The experimental results are convincing and illustrate the utility of BREAD for guiding model design and debugging implementations.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The computational demands of BREAD, particularly for complex models, could limit its widespread adoption.
*   **Scalability:** While validated on a reasonable number of models, further investigation into scalability to much larger and more complex models is warranted.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-developed and rigorously analyzed solution. While the computational cost is a concern, the benefits of having a rigorous, quantitative measure of MCMC inference quality outweigh this drawback. The method has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of probabilistic programming systems and advance the field of Bayesian inference.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should consider expanding the discussion of computational cost and scalability, and potentially exploring strategies for reducing the computational burden. A slightly more extensive validation on a wider range of models could also be beneficial.
