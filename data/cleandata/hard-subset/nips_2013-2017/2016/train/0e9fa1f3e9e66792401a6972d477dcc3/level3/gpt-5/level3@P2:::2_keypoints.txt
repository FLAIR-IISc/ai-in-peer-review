- Paper extends BDMC to bound the quality of approximate posterior samples from AIS/SMC-like MCMC by relating AIS forward/reverse log-partition gaps to Jeffreys divergence.
- Main theoretical result: expected gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS upper-bounds the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) between true posterior and AIS-produced sample distribution.
- Shows one-sided KL bounds in expectation when combined with external upper or lower bounds, and evaluates tightness on toy problems with exact Jeffreys computations.
- Reverse AIS requires an exact posterior start, so rigorous guarantees hold for simulated data only.
- Practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters, simulate data at fitted values, initialize reverse AIS from an approximate posterior via short MCMC chains.
- Empirically validates alignment of forward AIS behavior between real and simulated data across five models and finds negligible impact from number of reverse-init MCMC steps.
- Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and applied it to validate sampler accuracy, guide representation choices, and debug a WebPPL bug.
- Connects to Neal’s AIS, thermodynamic integration, Jarzynski relations, convergence diagnostics, and recent sample-quality measures; compares to concurrent subjective divergence work.
- Derivations are technically sound and correctly extend BDMC’s analysis; limitations and conditions are clearly stated.
- Empirical evaluation includes diverse models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR, GMM) and case studies in two PPLs.
- Limitation: tightness assessments beyond toy problems are empirical; large-scale behavior and variance not fully characterized.
- Paper is well-written and organized; background and main results are clearly presented.
- Minor clarity suggestions: emphasize expectation-based bounds, give guidance on annealing schedules and resource allocation, summarize wall-clock costs and variance, and move a model-misspecification example into main text.
- Original contribution: novel extension of BDMC to posterior-sample quality and a practical real-data protocol, plus PPL integrations and applied demonstrations.
- Significance: provides an actionable, theoretically grounded tool for assessing MCMC-based inference in PPLs with demonstrated practical utility.
- Strengths: clear theoretical result, practical implementation, diverse empirical validation, actionable model/representation insights, and careful relation to prior work.
- Weaknesses: guarantees are expectation-based and rely on simulated data, applicability limited to AIS/SMC-structured algorithms, and limited analysis of computational cost, variance, and schedule sensitivity.
- Suggested extensions: heuristics/adaptive schedule selection, wall-clock and compute-efficiency reporting, extensions to SMC and pseudo-marginal methods, and best-practice guidance when exact reverse samples are unavailable.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, rigorous and practical approach, strong theory and validation, clear benefits for PPL community.
- Arguments against acceptance: expectation-bound nature and simulated-data reliance limit real-data guarantees; scope restricted to AIS/SMC; need for more comprehensive practical guidance.
- Reviewer recommendation: Accept (high confidence).
