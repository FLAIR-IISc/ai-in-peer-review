This paper proposes a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The main idea of the paper is to use the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds of bidirectional Monte Carlo as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The authors contribute implementations of bidirectional Monte Carlo for Stan and WebPPL and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the challenges in measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples. The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, and it has the potential to be high-impact for probabilistic programming systems.

However, there are some concerns about the novelty of the technical contributions. The Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation is nice, but it is not clearly novel. Additionally, the authors do not cite or discuss Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which closely relates to bidirectional Monte Carlo and its acceptance ratio interpretation.

The paper also lacks a systematic treatment of the choice of β_t, which affects the bounds and comparisons. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of this issue and propose an iterative scheme to decrease Δβ until the bounds are sufficiently tight.

Furthermore, the paper has some clarity problems, particularly in sections 3.1-3.2. The writing quality slips, causing several ambiguities. The notation is also not well-defined, particularly the definition of vector v. The use of "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back" is inconsistent and unclear.

The experiments lack detail, and it is unclear how β_t is chosen in practice. The code implies that step = 1/options.steps, but this should be stated explicitly. The figures also have some presentation issues, such as small axes and tick labels.

In terms of related work, the authors should discuss Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996) and its relationship to bidirectional Monte Carlo. They should also clarify the meaning and mechanism of "auto" in AutoBDMC.

Overall, the paper has some strengths, but it also has some significant weaknesses. The authors should address the concerns about novelty, clarity, and related work to make the paper more acceptable.
