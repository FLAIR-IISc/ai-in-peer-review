

This paper proposes an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors apply this technique to two probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) and validate it on several models. The core idea builds directly on Grosse et al.'s (2015) BDMC framework, extending it to measure inference quality rather than estimating marginal likelihoods.

The paper demonstrates a technically sound implementation of known techniques, with clear descriptions of the methodology and validation on simulated data. The application to probabilistic programming languages provides practical utility for practitioners, and the paper is well-organized with thorough experimental validation. The authors present a compelling case for how BREAD can guide model representation choices, as demonstrated in their matrix factorization experiments.

However, the paper's main limitation is its lack of original contribution. The core technique is a straightforward application of previously published BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015), with the extension to Jeffreys divergence being a minor technical refinement rather than a novel methodological advance. The paper fails to distinguish itself from related work, particularly Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which achieves similar goals with different technical choices. The significance is limited to demonstrating an implementation of existing techniques in new contexts rather than advancing the field.

The paper meets quality standards through careful implementation and validation, and it's clearly written. However, it lacks sufficient originality to warrant publication at NIPS, which requires significant novelty. The significance is modest as the technique doesn't provide new theoretical insights or substantially improve existing methods.

Pro: Practical implementation in two widely used probabilistic programming languages, thorough experimental validation, useful for practitioners to assess inference quality.

Con: No novel contributions beyond applying existing BDMC framework, minimal theoretical advancement, closely related to prior work without clear differentiation.

Given the lack of substantial novelty and the availability of more appropriate venues (such as MCMC workshops), I recommend rejection from NIPS. The work is valuable for practitioners but does not meet NIPS' threshold for significant scientific contribution.
