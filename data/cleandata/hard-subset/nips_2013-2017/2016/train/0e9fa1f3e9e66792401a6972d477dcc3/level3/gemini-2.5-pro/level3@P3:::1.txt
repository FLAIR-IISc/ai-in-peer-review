This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively evaluating the reliability of MCMC inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate a stochastic upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution generated by a sampler (specifically, Annealed Importance Sampling) and the true posterior. Because this method requires an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse chain, its rigorous guarantees apply only to simulated data. The authors propose a practical protocol for applying this to real-world problems: first, fit hyperparameters on the real data, then generate simulated data from these fitted parameters to evaluate the sampler's performance. The method is implemented and validated within two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and is used to provide practical insights, such as comparing different model parameterizations and even discovering an implementation bug.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the critical and notoriously difficult problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence. Existing diagnostics are often heuristic and can fail to detect serious convergence issues. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative measure of sampler quality by bounding the divergence to the true posterior. This is a significant step beyond qualitative checks or metrics like effective sample size. The idea of repurposing the BDMC framework, originally for marginal likelihood estimation, to bound a divergence metric is both clever and elegant.
*   **Practical Utility for PPLs:** The integration into PPLs like Stan and WebPPL is a major strength. As "black-box" inference becomes more common, users have fewer tools to validate the results. BREAD offers a path towards automated, rigorous quality control for PPLs, which could be invaluable for both developers (for debugging and benchmarking) and practitioners (for gaining confidence in their models). The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful demonstration of this utility.
*   **Compelling Experiments:** The experiments effectively validate the proposed protocol. The authors show that inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters closely matches behavior on real data across several models. They also demonstrate how BREAD can guide practical modeling decisions, such as choosing between a collapsed and uncollapsed representation for a matrix factorization model, by providing a clear quantitative trade-off between convergence speed and per-iteration cost.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Missing Relevant Literature:** The paper's positioning could be significantly strengthened by discussing its relationship to prior work on MCMC validation using simulated data. Two highly relevant works are Geweke (2004, "Getting it Right: MCMC for Econometrics") and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006, "Validation of Software for Bayesian Models Using Posterior Quantiles"). Both propose a "simulate-and-fit" procedure where data is generated from the prior, and the MCMC algorithm is tested on its ability to recover the known parameters that generated the data. The authors should cite these papers and discuss how BREAD provides a different and potentially more powerful validation tool (e.g., a single summary statistic in the J-S divergence vs. checking posterior quantile coverage for all parameters).
*   **Clarity of Derivations:** Section 3.1, which presents the core theoretical result, is difficult to follow. The notation `qfwd(xT)` is used to denote the marginal distribution of approximate samples from the AIS chain, but this is not explicitly defined when it first appears. A clearer, more step-by-step introduction to the notation and the derivation would greatly improve the paper's readability.
*   **Self-Containment:** The paper relies heavily on the supplement for important details. For instance, the main text states that the bound `B` and the true divergence `J` were found to be a good match on toy examples, but all details are in Appendix B. While space is limited, the main text should be more self-contained, perhaps by including a concise algorithmic description or a more detailed walkthrough of the application of the method. This would allow an expert reader to grasp the key mechanics without constant reference to the appendix.
*   **Minor Notational Clutter:** The distinction between unobserved variables `z` and parameters `θ` seems unnecessary, as they always appear together as `(θ, z)`. Combining them into a single variable representing all unobserved quantities would simplify the notation and make the equations less cluttered.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** The paper would benefit from another proofreading pass to catch minor errors, such as a missing closing parenthesis noted in the first paragraph of Section 3.1.

**Overall Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The proposed method, BREAD, provides a much-needed tool for the rigorous evaluation of MCMC samplers, a problem of great importance to the NIPS community, especially with the rise of probabilistic programming. The main weaknesses relate to clarity and positioning within the existing literature, but these are all addressable. The core idea is sound and the potential impact is high. I recommend acceptance.
