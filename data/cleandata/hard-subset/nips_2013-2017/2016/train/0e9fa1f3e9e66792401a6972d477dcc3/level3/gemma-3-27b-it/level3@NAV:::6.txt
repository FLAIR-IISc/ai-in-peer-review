## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper explores the application of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), originally proposed for marginal likelihood estimation, to assess the quality of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors extend the existing BDMC framework to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples obtained from algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). They demonstrate this by integrating the method into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan, and validate it through experiments on several models. 

The work is grounded in a solid foundation of established techniques. It effectively summarizes the relevant background on AIS, stochastic bounds on log-partition functions, and the core ideas of BDMC [GGA15, Nea01]. The authors appropriately connect their approach to related work on MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92] and alternative sample quality measures [GM15, CTM16], correctly positioning their contribution as a method for bounding divergence rather than directly diagnosing convergence. 

However, the primary weakness of this paper lies in its lack of substantial novelty. The core idea – applying BDMC to assess posterior accuracy – is a direct extension of existing work, and the technical contribution primarily involves adapting and implementing known techniques within WebPPL and Stan. While the implementations are valuable, they do not constitute a significant methodological advancement. The experiments, while showcasing the method's functionality, largely serve to validate the existing theory, rather than demonstrating a breakthrough in either inference accuracy or efficiency.  The bug found in WebPPL, while interesting, feels more like a byproduct of implementation than a central result.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a functional implementation of BDMC within two popular probabilistic programming languages.
*   Provides a clear and well-written explanation of BDMC and its application to posterior assessment.
*   Highlights a potential use case for evaluating and debugging probabilistic programming systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks significant original ideas or novel theoretical contributions.
*   Primarily focuses on implementation rather than introducing new methods.
*   The experimental results are largely validation of existing concepts.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper is better suited for a workshop focused on MCMC methods or Bayesian inference (as suggested by the authors) rather than a main NIPS conference. While the work is technically sound and clearly presented, it doesn't meet the threshold for originality and significance expected at NIPS. The authors have successfully applied a known technique to a new context, but the resulting contribution feels incremental rather than transformative. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to submit to a more specialized venue.
