This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is an extension of the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15]. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains in both a forward direction (from prior to posterior) and a reverse direction (from posterior to prior) on simulated data, the method produces stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The key technical contribution of this work is to show that the expected gap between these bounds provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples generated by the forward AIS chain. This provides a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality.

The work is well-situated within the context of MCMC diagnostics. Unlike classical diagnostics [e.g., GR92, BG98] which can only identify specific modes of non-convergence, BREAD can provide a positive certificate of convergence (on simulated data) by showing that the divergence is small. The method is built upon the theoretical foundations of AIS [Nea01] and BDMC [GGA15]. The authors also acknowledge and clearly differentiate their work from concurrent, related research [CTM16]. To demonstrate its practical utility, the authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to analyze several models.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The problem of evaluating the quality of approximate posterior inference is of paramount importance, particularly with the proliferation of "black-box" inference engines in probabilistic programming. The lack of reliable, automated diagnostics is a major bottleneck for both expert and non-expert users. This paper makes a significant contribution by providing a quantitative, interpretable metric (an upper bound on Jeffreys divergence) for sample quality.
*   **Originality:** The idea of using the gap in a bidirectional Monte Carlo estimate to bound the divergence of the resulting samples is novel and insightful. The derivation in Section 3.1, which refines the analysis of [GGA15] to connect the log-ML bounds to the Jeffreys divergence, is a clever and valuable theoretical contribution.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bound is clear and appears correct. The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly the requirement of an exact posterior sample, which restricts rigorous guarantees to the simulated data setting. The protocol proposed in Section 3.2 to handle real-world data (by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters) is a pragmatic and well-reasoned heuristic. The experimental validation of this protocol (Section 5.1) is thorough and convincing, showing consistency between real and simulated data behavior and robustness to the number of MCMC steps for initializing the reverse chain.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The background sections provide the necessary context on AIS and BDMC, and the main method is developed logically. The experimental sections are clear, and the figures effectively communicate the results.
*   **Practical Impact:** The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a major strength that grounds the theoretical work in practical application. The use cases presented are compelling. The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization (Section 5.2.1) is an excellent example of how BREAD can guide concrete modeling and algorithmic decisions. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL (Section 5.2.2) is a powerful testament to the method's utility as a tool for validating the correctness of inference implementations.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Real-world Data Protocol:** The primary limitation is that the rigorous bound only applies to simulated data. The proposed heuristic for real-world data is sensible, and the validation is good. However, it would strengthen the paper to discuss potential failure modes of this heuristic. For instance, in cases of severe model misspecification, the posterior landscape for the real data could be qualitatively different from that for the simulated data, potentially making the BREAD results misleading. A brief discussion on this point would be beneficial.
*   **Applicability:** The method is directly applicable to importance sampling-based algorithms over an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. The authors argue that insights gained can inform the design of other MCMC algorithms, which is a fair point. However, it is worth explicitly stating that BREAD is not a general-purpose diagnostic for *any* MCMC sampler (e.g., a standard Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampler) but is tailored to this specific, albeit important, class of algorithms.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running bidirectional AIS is computationally intensive. It requires running many AIS chains, each of which involves many MCMC steps. A short discussion of the computational overhead of BREAD relative to the cost of the original inference task would be helpful for practitioners looking to adopt this method.
*   **Averaging of Estimates:** As noted in Footnote 3, the theoretical analysis of the bound on the Jeffreys divergence applies to the gap computed from individual forward/reverse chain pairs. This prevents the common practice of averaging the importance weights from multiple chains to obtain a lower-variance estimate of the log marginal likelihood. This might make the empirical bounds more variable than they could be. While likely beyond the scope of this paper, it would be interesting future work to see if the analysis could be extended to accommodate averaged estimates, potentially leading to tighter or more stable bounds.

**Summary**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful method for tackling the important problem of MCMC quality assessment. The strengths in significance, originality, and technical execution far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The integration into popular probabilistic programming languages and the compelling experimental results, including guiding model representation choices and discovering a software bug, demonstrate the clear value of this work to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
