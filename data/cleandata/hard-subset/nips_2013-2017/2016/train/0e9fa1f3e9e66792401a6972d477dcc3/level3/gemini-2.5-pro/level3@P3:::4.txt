This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively assessing the convergence of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which uses forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors' key theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between these two bounds provides an expected upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon (Jeffreys) divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution generated by AIS. This yields a single, interpretable scalar metric for sample quality.

The work builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015), who introduced BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation, and extends it to the novel application of evaluating sample quality. It also fits into the broader literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics, but offers a more rigorous alternative to common heuristics, at least in certain settings. A key limitation is that the reverse AIS chain requires an exact sample from the posterior, restricting the method's formal guarantees to synthetic data sampled from the joint distribution. To address this, the authors propose and test a protocol for applying the method to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters and then running the analysis on data simulated from this fitted model. The method is implemented and validated in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and used to provide insights into model representation choices and for debugging.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the crucial and long-standing problem of MCMC convergence diagnostics. The primary contribution—linking the BDMC bounds gap to the Jensen-Shannon divergence—is an elegant and important theoretical result. It provides a principled, quantitative, and interpretable measure of sample quality, which is a significant step beyond existing heuristics like R-hat. This is particularly valuable for users of black-box probabilistic programming systems.
*   **Practical Utility:** The authors demonstrate clear practical applications beyond simple convergence checking. The experiments comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model provide concrete guidance on model design. Furthermore, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL powerfully illustrates the method's value as a rigorous debugging and validation tool for inference engine developers.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental work is sound. The authors astutely identify the main limitation (reliance on synthetic data) and make a convincing effort to bridge the gap to real-world applicability. The validation experiments in Section 5.1, which show that inference behavior on real data is closely mirrored by behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters, lend strong support to their proposed protocol.
*   **Overall Quality and Readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a complete piece of work. The motivation for needing better convergence diagnostics is compelling, and the overall approach is communicated effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity in Section 3:** The theoretical exposition in Section 3, especially the introduction to 3.1, is difficult to follow. The notation for the forward and reverse chain distributions (e.g., `q_fwd(v, x_T)`) is introduced abruptly without sufficient intuition. A clearer explanation of what `v` represents (the history of the AIS chain) and a more gradual derivation would significantly improve readability.
*   **Highlighting the Core Contribution:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound in Section 3.1 is the paper's central theoretical novelty. This should be stated more explicitly. The current presentation makes it seem like a straightforward extension of Grosse et al. (2015), whereas this connection between the bounds gap and sample divergence is a key insight that deserves to be highlighted as such.
*   **Clarity of Terminology:** The method is named BREAD, but the term is introduced without a clear, concise definition. A sentence like "We refer to the use of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence as Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD)" would be helpful.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors should be commended for releasing code. However, its usability could be improved. The WebPPL code should specify compatible versions and include instructions for integration. Ideally, both the WebPPL and Stan implementations would be released as easy-to-install packages (e.g., an npm package for WebPPL and inclusion in a Stan library), which would greatly increase the method's adoption and impact.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The manuscript uses inconsistent notation for the data, switching between `y` and `D` (e.g., `p(theta, z, y)` in Eq. 2 vs. `p(theta, eta | D)` in Sec 3.2). This should be standardized.
*   The authors should double-check all equations for notational consistency, particularly in the distinction between joint distributions like `p(theta, z, y)` and conditional (posterior) distributions `p(theta, z | y)`.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and practically useful method for a significant problem in machine learning and statistics. The theoretical connection between the BDMC bounds and sample divergence is a valuable contribution, and the experiments are compelling. The primary weaknesses relate to the clarity of the theoretical presentation, which are readily addressable. The work is likely to be of high interest to practitioners using MCMC and developers of probabilistic programming languages.
