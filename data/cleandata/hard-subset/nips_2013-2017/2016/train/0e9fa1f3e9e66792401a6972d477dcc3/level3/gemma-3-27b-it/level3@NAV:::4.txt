## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper presents AutoBDMC, a method leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – specifically Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and its reverse – to assess MCMC convergence by bounding the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution. The core idea is compelling: utilizing the gap between upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood as a scalar convergence metric. This offers a potentially valuable tool for diagnosing inference quality, particularly in “black box” probabilistic programming settings. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and demonstrates the method’s utility through experiments in WebPPL and Stan, including a successful debugging application uncovering a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampling. 

The paper builds upon previous work in convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks & Gelman, 1998) and marginal likelihood estimation (e.g., Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015).  It’s a significant extension of BDMC, moving beyond marginal likelihood estimation to directly assess sample quality. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work like Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), highlighting the differences in their approaches. 

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues in Section 3, particularly the transition into 3.1. Variable and equation definitions are introduced abruptly without sufficient context or motivation, hindering understanding. The novelty of the divergence bound derived in Section 3.1 is not explicitly stated; it *should* be emphasized if it represents a significant contribution. Furthermore, “AutoBDMC” is never formally defined, leading to ambiguity. While clear from context, the manuscript should explicitly state that AutoBDMC refers to the application of AIS and rAIS for MCMC analysis. Minor issues include a potential typo on line 149 and inconsistent notation for data (y vs. D). 

Despite these shortcomings, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  The method has the potential to be a valuable addition to the toolkit for evaluating MCMC inference.  The validation of consistency between simulated and real data behaviour is reassuring. The release of code (though requiring improvement – see below) is a positive step.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of BDMC to assess MCMC convergence.
*   Provides a scalar convergence metric based on a theoretically grounded bound on divergence.
*   Demonstrated utility in debugging probabilistic programming language implementations.
*   Clear and generally well-written paper.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity issues in Section 3.
*   Lack of explicit definition for “AutoBDMC”.
*   Limited to simulated data for rigorous bounds (though real-data validation is presented).



**Recommendations:** The authors should address the clarity issues in Section 3, explicitly define "AutoBDMC," and ensure consistent notation.  Critically, the supplemental WebPPL code needs improved documentation regarding compatible versions and clear integration instructions. Ideally, releasing both the WebPPL and Stan code as installable packages would greatly enhance reproducibility and impact.  Addressing these points would significantly strengthen this already promising contribution.
