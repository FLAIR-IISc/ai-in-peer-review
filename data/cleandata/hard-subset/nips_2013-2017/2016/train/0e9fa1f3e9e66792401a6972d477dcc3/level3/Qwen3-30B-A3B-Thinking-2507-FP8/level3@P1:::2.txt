

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for measuring the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15] to provide rigorous upper bounds on this divergence, which is particularly valuable for black-box inference systems where traditional convergence diagnostics are inadequate. The method builds on annealed importance sampling (AIS) and reverse AIS to sandwich the true log marginal likelihood, with the gap between bounds providing an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.

The work makes four key contributions: (1) deriving an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, (2) demonstrating the accuracy of this bound on toy examples, (3) proposing a protocol for applying the method to real-world data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, and (4) integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan for practical evaluation. The authors validate their approach through extensive experiments, including analyzing model representation choices in matrix factorization and uncovering a bug in WebPPL's Gaussian sampling implementation.

The paper excels in quality, providing technically sound theoretical analysis and thorough experimental validation. The derivation in Section 3.1 is rigorous, extending [GGA15] to show how the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. The experimental validation (Section 5) is comprehensive, demonstrating consistency between simulated and real data across five different models and showing that approximate posterior samples from fitted hyperparameters yield reliable bounds. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to practitioners.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors carefully introduce necessary background on AIS, BDMC, and Bayesian inference before presenting their method. The exposition of BREAD is well-structured, with clear mathematical derivations and intuitive explanations. The experimental setup is described thoroughly, and the figures (particularly Figures 1-2) effectively communicate the results. The paper provides sufficient detail for replication, including model specifications and implementation details.

The originality is strong, addressing a timely problem with a novel application of BDMC. While related work on convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92] and automatic MCMC configuration [RR01] exists, BREAD provides a fundamentally different approach that offers rigorous bounds rather than heuristic diagnostics. The connection to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16] is appropriately discussed in Section 4, highlighting how BREAD differs by requiring exact posterior samples (which are available in simulated data settings).

The significance of this work is substantial. MCMC reliability is a critical challenge in probabilistic inference, particularly for black-box systems like probabilistic programming languages. BREAD provides a general, automatic, and rigorous method for evaluating inference quality, which could significantly improve the trustworthiness of inference results. The paper demonstrates how BREAD can guide practical decisions, such as choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed model representations in probabilistic programming. The method's ability to uncover implementation bugs (as in the WebPPL case) further underscores its practical value.

The primary limitation is that BREAD is limited to settings where exact posterior samples can be obtained, which requires simulated data. While the authors propose a protocol for real-world data using fitted hyperparameters, this introduces an approximation that could potentially affect the bound's accuracy. However, the validation in Section 5.1.2 shows this approximation has negligible impact in practice, making this limitation manageable.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution by extending BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation to posterior inference evaluation, which was not previously explored. The connection to the "parametric bootstrap" for validating inference on real data is insightful and practical.

Overall, this is a well-executed, significant contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic inference. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical integration into popular probabilistic programming frameworks makes it immediately useful. The paper's clarity and technical depth align well with NIPS standards.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Rigorous theoretical contribution addressing a critical problem in MCMC inference; comprehensive experimental validation across multiple models; practical integration into widely used probabilistic programming systems; clear demonstration of how the method can guide modeling decisions; significant potential impact on practitioners.

Con arguments against acceptance: Limited to simulated data settings (though the authors provide a practical protocol for real-world data); requires exact posterior samples for the bounding technique (though this is feasible in simulated data contexts).

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in evaluating MCMC inference reliability in a demonstrable way, with clear practical applications for probabilistic programming users. The authors have carefully considered related work and provided thorough validation of their approach. This paper should be accepted as it addresses a fundamental problem with a novel, rigorous, and practical solution.
