- Paper extends BDMC to quantify MCMC posterior sample quality using forward/reverse AIS to produce stochastic lower/upper bounds on log marginal likelihood.
- Proves that the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log-bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and approximate sample distribution.
- Introduces BREAD (BDMC for divergence bounding) and integrates it into WebPPL and Stan, plus a pragmatic real-data protocol using simulated data from fitted hyperparameters.
- Experiments on five canonical models and a matrix factorization case study demonstrate utility (representation choices, debugging) and uncovered a WebPPL sampler bug.
- The theoretical derivation (expectation bound) appears correct under stated assumptions (exact posterior sample for reverse AIS, ergodic kernels, AIS as IS on extended space).
- Toy examples show the bound is often tight, but more systematic characterization across multimodality, conditioning, and temperature schedules is needed.
- Real-data protocol validated empirically (simulated behavior matches real data) and is robust to number of MCMC steps used to initialize reverse chain.
- Empirics are convincing, but the paper should report wall-clock overhead, AIS schedule choices, variance of estimates, and required number of AIS chains for stability.
- Writing is generally clear and well-motivated, but Section 3 introduces variables abruptly—add a roadmap for forward/reverse chain factorization and roles of q_fwd/q_rev.
- The manuscript should explicitly highlight the novelty of the Jeffreys-divergence expectation bound as distinct from BDMC’s log-ML sandwiching.
- Terminology/inconsistency issues: use one name (BDMC vs. BREAD vs. AutoBDMC), unify notation (y vs. D), and fix instances conflating p(θ,z|y) and p(θ,z,y).
- Figures: showing quartiles is reasonable, but annotate with number of chains, temperature schedules, and total computation since averages of log AIS weights aren’t justified by the bound.
- Originality: applying BDMC to posterior sample-quality and the Jeffreys-divergence expectation bound are novel contributions; relation to “subjective divergence” discussed appropriately.
- Significance: provides a single interpretable scalar bound useful for PPL practitioners and system builders, though limited by requirement for AIS/SMC samplers and exact posterior samples.
- Pros: rigorous extension of BDMC, practical PPL integrations, useful protocol linking simulated and real data, strong case studies including bug discovery.
- Cons: rigorous bounds only for simulated data, limited tightness guidance, presentation/notation issues, and limited empirical comparison to other diagnostics (R̂, ESS, Stein).
- Requests: provide practical guidance on AIS schedules/spacing/adaptive methods, number of intermediates, diagnostics for bound stability, and wall-clock/memory costs.
- Questions: can the approach be adapted to SMC/NUTS hybrids or produce per-parameter diagnostics; compare against standard MCMC diagnostics and Stein discrepancies; clarify averaging multiple chains for concentration.
- Minor: define and use single terminology, fix typos/notation, and make WebPPL/Stan code reproducible with versions and installation instructions.
- Overall recommendation: solid, well-executed contribution; recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and reproducibility points.
