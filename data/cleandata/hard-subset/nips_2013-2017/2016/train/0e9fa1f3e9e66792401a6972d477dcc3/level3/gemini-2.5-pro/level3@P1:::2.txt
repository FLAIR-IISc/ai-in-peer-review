This paper presents Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), a method for quantitatively evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by certain MCMC-based algorithms. The core problem this work addresses is the long-standing difficulty of diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality, a challenge that is particularly acute for users of "black-box" probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The proposed method is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [GGA15], which was originally developed to provide tight, sandwiching bounds on the marginal likelihood. The authors' key insight is that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained by running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) from the prior to the posterior (forward) and from the posterior to the prior (reverse), provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior.

This method provides a principled, quantitative measure of inference quality. A significant hurdle is that the reverse chain requires an exact sample from the true posterior, which is typically unavailable. The authors address this by restricting the rigorous analysis to simulated data, where one can generate an exact posterior sample by first sampling from the joint distribution. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, they propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate a new dataset from these fitted hyperparameters, and finally, run BREAD on this simulated data. The paper provides an empirical validation of this protocol.

The contributions are both theoretical and practical. The paper refines the analysis of BDMC to formally derive the Jeffreys divergence bound. It then integrates the BREAD method into two popular but distinct PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its applicability. Finally, it uses the tool to conduct scientific investigation, comparing different model parameterizations for matrix factorization and, compellingly, identifying a subtle bug in WebPPL's sampler. This work is related to a large body of literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics [e.g., GR92, BG98], but differs by providing a quantitative bound on a statistical divergence rather than a heuristic test for non-convergence. It is also closely related to concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16], which the authors appropriately discuss.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Importance and Timeliness:** The problem of evaluating and trusting inference in PPLs is of critical importance. As these tools become more widespread, users (both expert and non-expert) need reliable methods to ensure the validity of their results. This paper tackles this head-on.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The connection between the BDMC marginal likelihood gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a clever and insightful contribution. It repurposes an existing technique for a different, arguably more practical, goal: assessing sample quality rather than just estimating a single number (the evidence).
3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors do an excellent job of validating their method. They test the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data, check the robustness of their proposed real-data protocol to the number of MCMC steps, and provide a compelling case study comparing model representations. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the method's utility.
4.  **Practical Implementation:** By implementing BREAD in both WebPPL and Stan, the authors have created a tool that can be immediately used by the community. This greatly increases the impact of the work beyond a purely theoretical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited to Simulated Data:** The rigorous theoretical guarantees of BREAD apply only to simulated data where exact posterior samples are available. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a sensible heuristic but lacks the same guarantees. While the empirical validation is encouraging, it remains an approximation. The paper is upfront about this limitation.
2.  **Applicability to Specific Samplers:** The method is directly applicable to samplers that can be viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space, such as AIS and SMC. The paper evaluates AIS chains using the native MCMC kernels of Stan (NUTS) and WebPPL (M-H) as transitions. While insights gained from this analysis are argued to be broadly useful, the method does not directly evaluate a standard, non-annealed MCMC run.
3.  **No Averaging of Estimates:** As noted in Footnote 3, the theoretical analysis bounds the divergence for a single AIS chain. Standard practice for reducing the variance of AIS estimates involves averaging the importance weights from multiple runs. It is not clear from the paper's analysis if an averaged estimate would maintain the bounding property, which could impact the tightness and stability of the bound in practice.

### Detailed Assessment

**Quality: High.** The paper is technically sound. The derivation extending the BDMC analysis to bound the Jeffreys divergence is clear and correct. The experimental work is comprehensive and well-designed, addressing key potential concerns about the method's applicability and validating its usefulness in a real-world scientific context (model comparison). The authors are careful to state the limitations of their approach, particularly the reliance on simulated data.

**Clarity: Excellent.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and provides the necessary background on AIS and BDMC before introducing the novel contributions. The methodology is explained logically, and the experiments are presented with informative figures and clear takeaways. The paper provides enough detail to understand both the theory and its practical implementation.

**Originality: High.** While building directly on the BDMC framework of [GGA15], the core contribution—using the bound gap to measure sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence—is novel. This re-framing is a significant conceptual step. The application as a diagnostic and debugging tool for PPLs is also a highly original and valuable contribution to the field.

**Significance: High.** This work has the potential for significant impact. It provides a much-needed, principled tool for a problem that currently relies mostly on heuristics. Researchers designing new inference algorithms or PPLs can use BREAD for rigorous testing and debugging. Practitioners can use it to gain confidence in their models and to make principled choices about model representation and sampler settings. This work represents a tangible step forward in making automated probabilistic inference more reliable and trustworthy.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that presents an elegant and practical solution to an important problem. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are inherent to the problem domain and are addressed thoughtfully by the authors. The paper is a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
