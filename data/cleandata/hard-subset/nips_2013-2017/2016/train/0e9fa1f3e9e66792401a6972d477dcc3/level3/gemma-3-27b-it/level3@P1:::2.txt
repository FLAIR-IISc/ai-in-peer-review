This paper introduces a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for evaluating the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The core idea leverages bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples generated by MCMC algorithms, particularly those amenable to an importance sampling interpretation like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) or Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). By running AIS chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, the authors derive an estimator that provides an upper bound on this divergence.  They then integrate this method into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility through several models and datasets, even using it to guide design choices within these frameworks.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a long-standing challenge in Bayesian inference: reliably assessing the quality of MCMC samples. Existing convergence diagnostics often provide insufficient guarantees. The authors build directly upon recent work on BDMC [GGA15] and extend it to this new application, making a valuable contribution. The connection to AIS and SMC provides a concrete context, and the extensions to WebPPL and Stan demonstrate the practicality of the approach. This work relates to a significant body of research on MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and automatic MCMC configuration [RR01], offering a different perspective – a direct bound on the divergence rather than indirect measures of convergence. Independent work [CTM16] also utilizes BDMC for similar purposes, and the paper clearly articulates the relationship and differences between the two approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the estimator for the Jeffreys divergence bound appears rigorous, building logically on the existing BDMC framework. The analysis is carefully presented and mathematically sound.
*   **Novelty and Originality:** While building on existing work, the application of BDMC to directly quantify the accuracy of MCMC samples is novel. The extension to probabilistic programming languages adds significant practical value. The approach of using simulated data with fitted hyperparameters to approximate real-world performance is also a clever contribution.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The integration with WebPPL and Stan makes the method directly usable by practitioners. The demonstration of its utility in guiding model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed models) highlights the potential for real-world impact.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide validation on several models and datasets, demonstrating the method's robustness.  The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed models provides a compelling example of how BREAD can inform modeling decisions.  Furthermore, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates the potential for BREAD to serve as a testing tool.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the concepts and provide sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Simulated Data Requirement:** The method, in its current form, relies on access to simulated data for rigorous bounds. While the authors propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data, the validity of this protocol is dependent on the accuracy of the hyperparameter estimation and the similarity between simulated and real data. This could be a limitation in some scenarios.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains in both directions can be computationally expensive, particularly for complex models. While the authors acknowledge this, the computational cost might limit the applicability of the method to very large-scale problems.
*   **Sensitivity to Initial Conditions:** As noted in the discussion of real-world data, the choice of initialization for the reverse chain (using approximate hyperparameters) could influence the results. Though the authors conduct validation showing limited sensitivity, this remains a potential concern.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a crucial problem in Bayesian inference with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The method is practical, as demonstrated by its integration into widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the potential for real-world impact.
*   The authors are careful to discuss limitations and potential issues.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on simulated data, and the associated assumptions, could limit the generalizability of the results.
*   The computational cost may preclude application to very large problems.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed method, BREAD, provides a rigorous way to assess the reliability of MCMC-based inference. The integration into WebPPL and Stan, combined with the clear presentation and thorough evaluation, make this a significant and impactful work.  The weaknesses are not substantial enough to outweigh the strengths, and the potential benefits of this method are considerable.  I recommend acceptance.
