- Introduces BREAD, an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) using reverse annealing to evaluate MCMC posterior inference quality.
- Key theoretical insight: the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution.
- Four main contributions: theoretical derivation, toy validation of the bound, protocol for applying BREAD to real data via hyperparameter fitting and simulated data, and integration into WebPPL and Stan.
- Demonstrates accuracy of the Jeffreys divergence upper bound on toy distributions where exact values are available.
- Validates consistency between simulated-data results and real-world models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.).
- Shows practical utility: guides model representation choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and uncovers implementation bugs in probabilistic programming languages.
- Provides a single scalar, rigorous metric for MCMC convergence without requiring exact posterior samples, useful for black-box inference systems.
- Relates clearly to prior work (BDMC, subjective divergence) and distinguishes itself by offering mathematical guarantees for Jeffreys divergence.
- Theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) is technically sound with careful derivations supporting the main claim.
- Experimental validation is comprehensive and includes a compelling debugging example in WebPPL.
- Clarity issues: abrupt transition into Section 3.1, insufficient motivation for variables, and "AutoBDMC" introduced without clear definition.
- Notational inconsistencies and minor typos (e.g., p(θ, z | y) vs p(θ, z, y); inconsistent use of y vs D).
- Supplemental code shortcomings: needs explicit WebPPL version compatibility, integration instructions, better documentation, and packaging (e.g., npm).
- Originality is strong: novel application of BDMC to bound posterior approximation error; authors should state this novelty explicitly in Section 3.1.
- High significance: could increase confidence in PPLs for non-experts and assist in model selection and debugging.
- Weaknesses summarized: ambiguous terminology, clarity and notation problems, minor typos, and insufficient code documentation.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, practically important contribution; recommend acceptance.
