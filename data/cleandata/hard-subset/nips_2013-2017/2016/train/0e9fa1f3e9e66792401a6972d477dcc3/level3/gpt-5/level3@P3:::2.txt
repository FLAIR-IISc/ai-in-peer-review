Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse, Ghahramani, Adams 2015) to quantitatively evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. The key idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS; Neal 2001) both forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data. The gap between the reverse and forward log AIS estimates is shown to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by the inference algorithm and the true posterior. The analysis also shows how, given an auxiliary bound on log normalizing constants, one can bound one-sided KL divergences in expectation. Because reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, the paper uses simulated datasets generated from the model (posterior samples arise from ancestral joint samples), and proposes a pragmatic protocol for real-data assessment: fit hyperparameters to real data, simulate datasets at those hyperparameters, and verify that AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data (akin in spirit to a parametric bootstrap).

The authors integrate BREAD into two probabilistic programming systems—WebPPL (Goodman & Stuhlmüller) and Stan (Carpenter et al.)—and validate on a suite of models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, mixture of Gaussians). They further demonstrate how the tool can guide modeling/inference design (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization in Stan vs. WebPPL) and even uncover implementation bugs (a multivariate Gaussian sampling bug in WebPPL). The work is related to convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman), to Stein discrepancies for sample quality (Gorham & Mackey 2015), to thermodynamic integration and nonequilibrium work (Neal 1996; Jarzynski 1997), and to independent contemporaneous work on subjective divergence in PPLs (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016), which also builds on BDMC.

Strengths:
- Timely and important problem: assessing reliability of black-box MCMC is central for both practitioners and PPL users.
- Elegant idea: reusing BDMC with AIS forward/reverse to produce expectation upper bounds on Jeffreys divergence of the approximate sampler.
- Solid theory: clear derivation of how the forward/reverse gaps bound DKL(q||p), DKL(p||q), and their sum, with careful treatment of stochastic lower/upper bounds on log partition ratios.
- Practical integration: implemented in WebPPL and Stan; experiments span multiple canonical models; concrete guidance on model representation tradeoffs; useful for debugging.
- Sensible real-data protocol with empirical validation that simulated-data behavior tracks real-data behavior.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Guarantees require an exact posterior sample; thus the rigorous bound applies to simulated data. The fitted-hyperparameter heuristic for real data is plausible but lacks formal guarantees or error quantification.
- Bounds are in expectation; per-run guarantees are not provided, and heavy-tailed AIS log-weights make single-run estimates noisy. Practical guidance on aggregation and uncertainty of the bound could be strengthened.
- Applicability is limited to algorithms expressible as importance sampling over extended state spaces (AIS/SMC). For general long-run MCMC (e.g., HMC/NUTS alone), BREAD provides only indirect insight via AIS variants and design choices.
- Potential sensitivity to annealing schedule, choice of transition kernels, and number of intermediate distributions; more systematic schedule adaptation or ablation would help.
- Empirical scope is modest in dimensionality; more large-scale or strongly multimodal cases would further stress-test tightness of the bounds.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is high. The derivations connecting forward/reverse AIS to expectation bounds on KL/Jeffreys divergences are correct and well-motivated, building cleanly on BDMC. The empirical studies substantiate the practical utility: (a) tightness on toy problems, (b) real-vs-simulated consistency, (c) insensitivity to the number of hyperparameter MCMC steps for reverse initialization, (d) actionable guidance on collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations, and (e) bug detection.
- Suggestions: quantify variability of the bound across runs (e.g., confidence intervals for B), and provide more guidance on schedule design and diagnostics when the upper and lower AIS bounds misbehave.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The notation separating forward and reverse AIS chains is helpful. The simulated-data caveat and real-data protocol are explained candidly. Minor suggestions: include a concise algorithmic summary of BREAD, explicitly state the assumptions under which each guarantee holds, and provide more intuition for when the bound is expected to be tight or loose.

Originality:
- Novel and useful reuse of BDMC: shifting from benchmarking marginal likelihood estimators to evaluating posterior sample quality. The expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posteriors via AIS gaps is a nice conceptual contribution. Integration into two distinct PPLs and demonstrating impact on modeling choices adds practical originality. The relation to subjective divergence is properly acknowledged.

Significance:
- High. If adopted, BREAD can become a standard tool for PPL developers and users to sanity-check inference quality, tune annealing schedules and kernels, compare model representations, and uncover implementation errors. The approach addresses a longstanding gap in MCMC diagnostics, complementing ESS and R-hat with a distributional discrepancy measure.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Can you provide practical guidance or adaptive methods for choosing the annealing schedule and number of intermediate distributions that improve bound tightness?
- Do you observe systematic relationships between bound tightness and dimensionality, multimodality, or condition numbers?
- Could you report aggregate uncertainty (e.g., bootstrap CIs) for B across multiple AIS runs?
- How does BREAD perform with SMC variants and resampling—any pitfalls or advantages relative to AIS?
- Are the Stan/WebPPL implementations and scripts publicly available for reproduction?

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous analysis yielding actionable, interpretable bounds on posterior sample quality.
- Practical integration into mainstream PPLs; useful empirical demonstrations and a real bug found.
- Addresses a pressing need in black-box Bayesian inference.

Cons:
- Theoretical guarantees limited to simulated data and expectation bounds.
- Applicability constrained to AIS/SMC-style samplers; scalability and tightness in high dimensions remain to be fully characterized.
- Heuristic real-data protocol lacks formal error bounds.

Overall assessment: well done. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem with an interesting and useful idea, backed by thoughtful experiments.

Reviewer recommendation: Accept.
