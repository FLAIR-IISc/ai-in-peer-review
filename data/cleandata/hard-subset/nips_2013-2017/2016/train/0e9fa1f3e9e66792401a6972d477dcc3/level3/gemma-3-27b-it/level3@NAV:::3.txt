## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes utilizing the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) as a general performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The authors contribute implementations of BDMC within the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming systems, alongside a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality. The core idea – leveraging BDMC’s existing bounds for a diagnostic tool – is simple and potentially impactful, especially given the “black box” nature of many modern inference systems. 

The strength of this work lies in its practicality. Providing implementations in popular probabilistic programming languages lowers the barrier to adoption and allows practitioners to assess the reliability of their inferences. The proposed protocol for evaluating real-world data, through hyperparameter estimation and simulated data validation, is a reasonable approach to address the limitation of requiring exact posterior samples. 

However, the novelty of the technical contributions feels modest. While the observation regarding the Jensen-Shannon divergence bound is interesting, it doesn’t represent a significant theoretical leap. A crucial omission is the lack of discussion of Radford Neal’s 1996 work on Tempered Transitions, which is deeply related to BDMC and the interpretation of acceptance ratios within the annealing framework. This oversight weakens the positioning of the work within the existing literature.

Several areas require clarification and improvement. The authors under-discuss the choice of β_t in the annealing schedule (Δβ), and how this impacts the resulting bounds. A systematic exploration of different Δβ values, potentially with an iterative scheme to tighten the bounds, would strengthen the analysis. “AutoBDMC” is vaguely defined; it’s unclear whether it refers to a convergence assessment based on the bound gap or the fixed hyperparameter scheme detailed in Section 3.2. 

Furthermore, the clarity of sections 3.1-3.2 suffers, resulting in ambiguities. The definition of vector 'v' is loose, and the inconsistent use of “rev/fwd” versus “for/back” is confusing. Crucially, the estimator \hat{p}(y) used within equations 5-7 and 9-11 is never defined, hindering understanding of the derivations. Equation (6) appears incorrect as written, with identical arguments in the KL divergence terms. The experimental section lacks detail on the practical choice of β_t, and the figure labeling is ambiguous (e.g., the meaning of "HMC / No-U-Turn steps"). Several minor issues, like undefined acronyms (RAIS) and typos, also detract from the polish.

**Arguments for acceptance (with revisions):** The method is conceptually sound, practically implemented, and addresses a genuine need in probabilistic programming.
**Arguments against acceptance:** The novelty is limited, and significant clarity issues impede understanding. The related work discussion is incomplete.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance *conditional* on the authors addressing the clarity issues, expanding the related work discussion (specifically citing Neal, 1996), and providing a more thorough exploration of the β schedule and AutoBDMC. A revised version addressing these points would represent a valuable contribution to the field.
