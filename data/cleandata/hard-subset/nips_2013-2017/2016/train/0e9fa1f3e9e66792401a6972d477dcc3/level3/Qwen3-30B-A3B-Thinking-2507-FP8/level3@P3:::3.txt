

# Review: Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo

This paper proposes using the gap between bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) stochastic lower and upper bounds as a performance measure for MCMC transition operators. The authors extend the BDMC technique [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. They implement this in WebPPL and Stan, and propose a protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data.

The paper builds on Grosse et al. (2015)'s BDMC framework, which provides stochastic bounds on marginal likelihood estimates by running AIS chains in both directions. The authors refine this analysis to derive a bound on the Jeffreys divergence, which is a more comprehensive measure of posterior approximation quality than either KL divergence alone. This work relates to the broader literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92], automatic MCMC configuration [RR01], and methods for estimating sample quality [GM15].

**Strengths:** The method is simple to implement and potentially high-impact for probabilistic programming systems, as it provides a rigorous, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality. The authors successfully integrate BREAD into both WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility through experiments on multiple models and its role in debugging a WebPPL implementation (issue #473). The validation protocol for real-world data is well-motivated and provides a practical approach for using BDMC on actual inference problems.

**Weaknesses:** The technical contributions are modest, with the key insight (Jensen-Shannon divergence bound) not clearly novel. The paper fails to cite or discuss Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which closely relates to BDMC and its acceptance ratio interpretation. The choice of β_t schedule is under-discussed, with Δβ critically affecting the bounds and comparisons but not treated systematically. The meaning of "auto" in AutoBDMC is never unambiguously defined, and the paper lacks a clear iterative scheme to decrease Δβ until bounds are tight.

Several clarity issues undermine the paper's presentation: Sections 3.1-3.2 feel rushed, with ambiguous notation (definition of vector v is loose, inconsistent use of "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back"), and missing definition of estimator \hat{p}(y) in equations 5-7 and 9-11. Equation (6) contains an error where the KL divergence arguments appear identical. The experiments lack detail on β_t choice (code implies step = 1/options.steps but this should be stated), and Figure 1's labeling is ambiguous regarding "HMC / No-U-Turn steps." Undefined acronyms (RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS) and small figure axes/tick labels further reduce clarity.

**Recommendation:** The paper has significant potential impact but requires substantial revisions to address the weaknesses. The modest technical novelty and lack of related work discussion are concerning, but the method's simplicity and practical utility for probabilistic programming systems are strong points. If the authors address the clarity issues, incorporate the missing related work, and provide a systematic treatment of β_t scheduling (including a suggested iterative scheme to tighten bounds), the paper would be suitable for acceptance. The current version contains too many ambiguities and omissions to be accepted in its present form.

**Summary of key concerns:**
- *Novelty*: Key observation not clearly novel; missing critical related work (Neal 1996)
- *Clarity*: Ambiguous notation, rushed sections, undefined terms, equation errors
- *Experiments*: Insufficient detail on β_t scheduling, unclear figure interpretation
- *Technical rigor*: Under-discussed β_t choice, AutoBDMC definition ambiguity

The paper's core idea has merit, but the presentation and technical execution require significant improvement. With the suggested revisions, this work could provide a valuable tool for the probabilistic programming community. As it stands, it would benefit from additional work to strengthen its theoretical foundation and clarify its contributions relative to prior work.
