This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC-like algorithms. The key idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) “forward” (prior-to-posterior) and “reverse” (posterior-to-prior) on simulated data. The stochastic lower bound from forward AIS on log p(y) and the stochastic upper bound from reverse AIS sandwich the marginal likelihood; their gap has expected value that upper bounds DJ(p||q). The authors call the resulting procedure BREAD. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate tightness on toy examples with exact DJ, propose a practical protocol for real-data assessment via fitted hyperparameters (parametric bootstrap-style), and use BREAD to study model representations (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and to debug an implementation error in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work: BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] introduced sandwiching log-evidence via reverse AIS/SMC given an exact posterior sample; here the analysis is sharpened to show that the BDMC gap upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS output distribution and the true posterior. This complements classic MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin [1992], Brooks–Gelman [1998]) that assess convergence but do not yield quantitative bounds on discrepancy, and connects to thermodynamic integration and Jarzynski’s equality [Neal 1996; Jarzynski 1997] underpinning AIS [Neal 2001]. Compared to Stein-based sample quality measures [Gorham & Mackey, 2015], BREAD requires exact posterior samples (available on simulated data) but makes no convexity assumptions and applies to multimodal targets. Independent work on “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016] is closely related; with the true posterior as reference, their estimator matches the same upper bound; this paper emphasizes the BDMC-derived expectation bound and a pragmatic real-data protocol.

Quality: The theoretical result is clear and correct: the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log-evidence estimators equals an upper bound on DJ(p||q), with one-sided bounds attainable when an external upper/lower bound is available. The derivation cleanly uses AIS’s extended-state-space view and KL monotonicity under marginalization. Empirically, the authors (i) compute exact DJ and BDMC gaps on small distributions to assess tightness; (ii) validate a fitted-hyperparameter protocol by showing close agreement between forward AIS behavior on real vs simulated data across five Stan models; (iii) show the reverse AIS bound is robust to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the posterior hyperparameters; (iv) demonstrate practical guidance on model representation (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) in WebPPL and Stan; and (v) uncover and fix a PPL bug when the bounds violate ordering. While most experiments are modest scale, they are carefully executed and aligned with the claims.

Clarity: The paper is well written, with a crisp presentation of AIS/BDMC preliminaries and the new analysis for DJ. The limitations (need for an exact posterior sample) are stated, and the real-data protocol is explained and empirically supported. Some additional clarity could help: more guidance on choosing annealing schedules and number of temperatures; explicit discussion of variance/concentration of the stochastic bounds and recommended replication strategy; and a brief summary of computational overhead.

Originality: The fundamental novelty is reframing BDMC’s log-evidence sandwich into a quantitative bound on posterior-sample quality (Jeffreys divergence) for AIS/SMC-type samplers, plus a practical workflow for real-data evaluation. This is a nontrivial and useful conceptual step beyond prior BDMC usage for marginal likelihood benchmarking. The integration into two PPLs and the representation-study application further increase novelty for the community.

Significance: Measuring MCMC reliability remains a central challenge, especially for black-box inference in probabilistic programming. A method that (on simulated data) yields rigorous, interpretable bounds on sample quality, and that transfers to real data via a validated protocol, is valuable to both practitioners and researchers. The representation study shows concrete design implications; the debugging story underscores engineering impact. While the method is restricted to AIS/SMC-like settings (or MCMC kernels embedded in AIS), these are widely used and share design choices with standalone MCMC.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Requires an exact posterior sample; rigorous guarantees apply only on simulated data. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol is compelling but heuristic, with no formal guarantees when the posterior over hyperparameters is broad or misspecified.
- The DJ bound holds in expectation; single-run gaps can be noisy due to heavy-tailed AIS weights. More discussion of concentration/CI construction and recommended replication would help.
- Tightness can degrade with poor annealing schedules or kernels; guidance or adaptive schedules would strengthen the practical story.
- Empirical validations are on moderate-size models; evaluation on higher-dimensional, multimodal posteriors would further support robustness.
- The SMC applicability is stated but experiments focus on AIS; a concrete SMC case study would be useful.

Suggestions:
- Provide heuristics or automatic tuning for temperature schedules and number of intermediate distributions, and report sensitivity analyses.
- Report wall-clock overhead vs. conventional diagnostics (ESS, R-hat) and show scenarios where BREAD detects failures they miss.
- Include confidence intervals for the BDMC gap via repeated runs and tail bounds combining multiple samples.
- Add an SMC-based example and a higher-dimensional/multimodal benchmark to stress-test tightness.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a rigorous, interpretable measure of sample quality.
- Solid theory linking BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence; clear and correct derivations.
- Practical impact demonstrated via integration into WebPPL and Stan, guidance on model representations, and real-data protocol validation.
- Empirically careful; even finds a real bug, indicating utility as a testing tool.
- Relevant and useful for the NeurIPS community working on inference and probabilistic programming.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Guarantees rely on simulated data; real-data protocol lacks formal guarantees.
- Bound is in expectation and may be loose or high-variance in challenging settings.
- Experimental scope is moderate; limited exploration of SMC and very high-dimensional/multimodal targets.

Overall assessment: Well-executed paper with a compelling theoretical insight and practical methodology that can materially improve how we assess MCMC-based inference in practice. Recommendation: accept.
