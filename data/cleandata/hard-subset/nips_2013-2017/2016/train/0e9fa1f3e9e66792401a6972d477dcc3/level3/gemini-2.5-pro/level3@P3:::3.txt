### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained by running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains from the prior to the posterior (forward) and vice versa (reverse), provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior. Since the reverse chain requires an exact posterior sample, this method is directly applicable only to simulated data. The authors propose a protocol to extend its utility to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters, then simulating data from the model using these parameters. The method is implemented and tested within two probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL, and used to compare different model representations for a matrix factorization problem.

The work builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015) who introduced BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation, and Neal (2001) who developed AIS. The main contribution is repurposing the BDMC framework not for estimating a single value (the marginal likelihood), but for quantifying the quality of the samples produced by the underlying inference machinery. This connects it to a long line of research on MCMC convergence diagnostics, but offers a more quantitative and rigorous measure, albeit one limited to simulated data. The authors correctly cite the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which explores a similar idea.

**Strengths**

1.  **Practical Significance:** The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality is a fundamental and persistent challenge in applied Bayesian statistics. A reliable, automated method for this purpose would be a significant contribution, especially for users of probabilistic programming systems who may not be inference experts. The proposal is simple, intuitive, and directly addresses this need.

2.  **Simplicity and Impact:** The proposed method is elegant and easy to implement within any framework that supports AIS. By integrating it into Stan and WebPPL, the authors demonstrate its practicality. The use case of debugging a subtle bug in WebPPL's sampler is a particularly compelling demonstration of the method's utility as a correctness-checking tool for inference engine developers.

3.  **Quantitative Evaluation:** Unlike many traditional diagnostics which are heuristic (e.g., Gelman-Rubin R-hat), this method provides a quantitative bound on a standard statistical divergence (Jeffreys divergence). This allows for a more direct and interpretable comparison of different MCMC transition operators, model parameterizations, or hyperparameter settings, as shown in the matrix factorization experiment.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, particularly in Section 3. The writing feels rushed and several key definitions and derivations are ambiguous, hindering reproducibility and understanding.
    *   **Undefined Terms:** The estimator `\hat{p}(y)`, which appears in the expectations in equations (5)-(7) and (9)-(11), is never defined. This makes it impossible to follow the derivation.
    *   **Equation Error:** Equation (6) is incorrect as written. The two arguments to the KL divergence, `qfwd(xT ) qfwd(v |xT )` and `pT (xT ) qrev(v |xT )`, are not properly defined or related in a way that makes the expression meaningful. It seems the authors intended to relate the joint forward path distribution to a joint reverse path distribution, but the expression is confusing.
    *   **Ambiguous Notation:** The definition of the vector `v` as "all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage" is imprecise.
    *   **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper inconsistently uses "fwd"/"rev" and "for"/"back" to refer to the two AIS chains.
    *   **Undefined Acronyms:** Figure captions use acronyms like "RAIS" and "uc(R)AIS" without defining them.
    *   **Presentation:** The figures have axes and tick labels that are too small to be easily legible.

2.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to cite or discuss Radford Neal's (1996) paper, "Sampling from multimodal distributions using tempered transitions." This is a major omission. Tempered transitions involve a similar process of annealing/tempering and can be analyzed in terms of detailed balance over an extended state space, which is closely related to the underlying justification for the forward/reverse estimators in BDMC. A discussion of this work is essential for properly contextualizing the paper's contributions.

3.  **Methodological Gaps:**
    *   **Annealing Schedule:** The choice of the annealing schedule (the sequence of β_t values, or equivalently, the step size Δβ) is a critical hyperparameter that directly impacts both the computational cost and the tightness of the bounds. The paper gives this crucial choice very little attention. A more systematic treatment is needed.
    *   **"AutoBDMC" Unclear:** The paper introduces the term "AutoBDMC" but never provides a clear and unambiguous definition. It is unclear if this refers to the general method of using the bound gap, the specific heuristic for real-world data, or some automated procedure for setting the annealing schedule.

4.  **Originality:** The technical novelty of the work is modest. The main contribution is the observation that the existing BDMC framework can be re-interpreted to bound the Jeffreys divergence. While useful, this is a relatively small conceptual step from the original BDMC paper. The observation itself is nice but its novelty is not clearly established.

5.  **Experimental Detail:** The experiments lack sufficient detail for reproducibility. For instance, it is not stated how the annealing schedules (number of steps) were chosen for the experiments. Figure captions are also ambiguous (e.g., it is unclear if "HMC / No-U-Turn steps" refers to the number of AIS steps or the number of leapfrog steps within each transition).

**Constructive Suggestions**

*   **Improve Clarity:** The authors must undertake a major revision of Section 3. This includes: defining all terms (especially `\hat{p}(y)`), correcting Equation (6), and clarifying the notation for `v`.
*   **Discuss Related Work:** A thorough discussion of Neal (1996) and its relationship to the BDMC framework should be added.
*   **Address Annealing Schedule:** The authors should discuss the impact of the β schedule more formally. I would suggest proposing and experimenting with an iterative scheme where Δβ is decreased until the bound gap is smaller than a desired threshold, which would make the method more practical and robust.
*   **Define "AutoBDMC":** The authors need to explicitly define what they mean by "AutoBDMC" or remove the term.
*   **Add Experimental Details:** All hyperparameters and settings for the experiments, especially the annealing schedules, must be clearly specified. Figures should be improved for legibility.
*   **Correct Typos:** The missing parenthesis in the definition of Jeffreys divergence and the misspelling of Hoffman as "Homan" should be corrected.

**Recommendation: Borderline Accept**

This paper presents a simple and potentially high-impact idea for evaluating MCMC sample quality. The implementations in Stan and WebPPL and the demonstrated use cases are valuable. However, the paper is significantly undermined by poor clarity in its core methodological section, a major omission of related work, and a lack of detail in its experimental methodology. These issues are severe but potentially fixable. If the authors can thoroughly address the clarity, related work, and experimental detail issues in a camera-ready version, the paper would be a solid contribution to the NIPS community. Without these fixes, the paper falls below the required standard for publication.
