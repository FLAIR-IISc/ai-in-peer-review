This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess MCMC-based posterior inference by using annealed importance sampling (AIS) in both directions to sandwich the log marginal likelihood on simulated data, and then interprets the expected gap between the forward and reverse stochastic bounds as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior. The method (BREAD) applies when the inference algorithm can be cast as IS on an extended space (AIS/SMC). The authors integrate the technique into WebPPL and Stan, propose a pragmatic protocol for approximating the reverse-chain initialization on real data (fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data, and run reverse AIS from an approximate posterior sample), and validate on a set of models (linear/logistic regression, AR, matrix factorization, MoG). They also use BREAD to compare collapsed vs uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization and report a debugging win in WebPPL where inconsistent bounds exposed a multivariate Gaussian sampling bug.

Relation to prior work: The analysis builds directly on BDMC and AIS (Neal, 2001), and is conceptually related to Jarzynski’s non-equilibrium work relations and SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006). It complements convergence diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998) and sample-quality measures like Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). It is close to concurrent work on subjective divergence in probabilistic programming (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016). The connection to tempering/tempered transitions (Neal, 1996) and acceptance-ratio interpretations is under-discussed and would strengthen context.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, general, and practically valuable: provides a quantitative diagnostic for inference quality that integrates naturally with probabilistic programming toolchains.
- Clear, useful observation: the forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds in expectation the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) between approximate and true posterior samples.
- Implementations in both WebPPL and Stan with multiple models; the collapsed vs uncollapsed case study is actionable for practitioners.
- Real-data protocol is thoughtful, and the consistency checks between real and simulated data are convincing for the tested cases.
- Demonstrated utility for debugging inference implementations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty is modest: the key inequality follows from BDMC/AIS identities; the contribution is a careful repurposing for sampler evaluation. This is still useful, but the paper should better delineate what is new versus what follows from BDMC.
- Related work: tempered transitions (Neal, 1996) and thermodynamic integration schedules deserve discussion, especially regarding schedule design and acceptance behavior; expand the comparison to subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) beyond a brief mention.
- β-schedule under-specified: the choice of βt (and Δβ) crucially affects both the tightness of bounds and fairness of comparisons. Please:
  - Specify the schedule used in all experiments (e.g., linear βt = t/T) and how T is chosen.
  - Explore schedule sensitivity (linear vs moment averaging; cf. Grosse et al., 2013) and report how the forward–reverse gap evolves with T and with adaptive schedules aimed at equalizing incremental log-weight variance.
  - Provide results as a function of both number of transitions and wall-clock time.
- “AutoBDMC” is not defined: clarify what “auto” does—does it adapt β, number of steps, transition kernels, or stopping criteria? If it refers to the fitted-hyperparameter protocol, say so explicitly.
- Clarity issues in Sections 3.1–3.2:
  - Define all estimators inside expectations (e.g., the AIS weight estimator for p(y)); currently \hat{p}(y) is not introduced.
  - Notation for v as “all variables sampled before the final stage” is ambiguous given two AIS sweeps; explicitly index forward vs reverse chains and conditionals.
  - Consistency of labels: standardize on “fwd/rev” (or “forward/reverse”) throughout; avoid mixing with “back/for.”
  - Equation (6) as printed appears incorrect (KL arguments look identical or mis-ordered). Please correct the expressions in Eqs. (6)–(11) and include a derivation or appendix with full steps.
- Real-data protocol: while empirically stable across S ∈ {10,…,10000}, discuss failure modes when fitted hyperparameters are poor (multimodality or weakly informative priors). Consider initializing the reverse chain with multiple approximate posterior draws to reduce dependence on a single seed, and quantify any bias by varying S and reporting confidence intervals on the bound.
- Experimental details:
  - Precisely state how βt and number of intermediate distributions were chosen for each figure; code implies a fixed step of 1/options.steps—document this.
  - Clarify figure axes: does “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” mean the number of NUTS trajectories per β, total leapfrog steps, or total transitions? Define RAIS (reverse AIS) and u/c(R)AIS in captions.
  - Improve figure readability (axis labels/ticks) and ensure captions are self-contained.
- Minor presentation issues: fix typos (e.g., missing parenthesis in the divergence expression; NUTS author is Hoffman, not “Homan”).

Quality
- The theoretical argument is sound given BDMC/AIS foundations, with the important caveat that the bound holds in expectation and, rigorously, only with exact posterior initialization. The empirical validation is reasonable but could be strengthened with more schedule analysis and tighter numerical checks on toy problems where Jeffreys divergence is exactly computable (the appendix direction is good; consider integrating a main-text figure).
- The real-data heuristic is plausible and tested, but its limits should be discussed.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but the key derivations and notation need tightening; figures and captions require more detail. Define all acronyms on first use (AIS/RAIS/SMC/NUTS).

Originality
- Incremental but useful: reframing BDMC’s bidirectional bounds as a direct measure of sample quality for AIS/SMC samplers and integrating into PPLs. The idea is close to concurrent subjective-divergence work.

Significance
- High practical impact for users of probabilistic programming and MCMC developers; offers a concrete, quantitative tool to compare representations and catch bugs.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, implementable diagnostic with integrations in two major systems.
- Clear empirical utility (model representation tradeoffs, debugging).
- Bridges a gap in evaluating black-box samplers.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to BDMC; derivations need cleanup.
- Schedule choice and experimental protocol under-specified.
- Bounds are only rigorous on simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. With improved clarity, fuller discussion of related work and β-schedule design, and clearer experimental reporting, this would be a solid and useful NIPS paper.
