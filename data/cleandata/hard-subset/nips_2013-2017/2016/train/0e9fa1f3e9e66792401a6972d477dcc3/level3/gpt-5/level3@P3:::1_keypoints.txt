- Paper extends BDMC to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between AIS/SMC sampler output and the true posterior using forward and reverse AIS on simulated data.
- Integration into WebPPL and Stan with a protocol for assessing real-data performance via fitted hyperparameters is presented.
- Builds on BDMC, AIS, reverse AIS/Jarzynski equalities, and relates to convergence diagnostics and sample-quality metrics.
- Should cite and contrast prior simulation-based MCMC validation work (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006).
- Strength: principled, quantitative measure of posterior sample quality valuable for PPLs and debugging.
- Strength: clear theoretical derivation linking BDMC log-ML gap to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence under AIS factorization assumptions.
- Strength: practical impact demonstrated through tooling, case studies, and a real bug found in WebPPL.
- Strength: experiments show consistency between simulated and real-data behavior across models.
- Weakness: rigorous guarantees require exact posterior samples, so formal bounds apply only to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Weakness: bound holds in expectation; concentration, finite-sample behavior, and heavy-tailed AIS log-weight issues are not fully addressed.
- Weakness: Section 3.1 is hard to follow; key notation and derivations are introduced too late or relegated to the supplement.
- Weakness: experimental choices (quartiles vs means, sensitivity to annealing schedule, number of temperatures, transition kernels) lack systematic analysis.
- Minor issues: presentation clutter from (z, θ) splitting, a typo (line 165), and unclear divergence naming (Jeffreys vs Jensen–Shannon).
- Quality: theoretical claim is correct and derivation crisp; toy tightness examples reassuring; empirical validation is careful but depends on AIS design choices.
- Clarity: main text should define qfwd(xT), qrev(v|xT), and provide a self-contained derivation and an algorithmic recipe for practitioners.
- Originality: novel adaptation of BDMC to bound posterior-sample divergence and a meaningful advance for diagnosing MCMC in PPLs.
- Significance: high potential impact for PPL practice, model representation choices, and debugging workflows despite limitations to simulated-data guarantees.
- Suggested additions: cite Geweke (2004) and Cook et al. (2006); move key derivations into main text; add a numbered forward/reverse AIS algorithm.
- Suggested practical guidance: advice on temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, number of AIS chains, variance and averaging practices, and sensitivity to transition kernels.
- Suggested experiments: report wall-clock costs for AIS runs, compare to baseline MCMC, and add cases with known ground-truth Jeffreys divergence.
- Presentation fixes: use a single latent x where possible, define notation before use, and fix the noted typo.
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — sound, useful, and impactful work that would benefit from clarity, related-work additions, and more practitioner guidance.
