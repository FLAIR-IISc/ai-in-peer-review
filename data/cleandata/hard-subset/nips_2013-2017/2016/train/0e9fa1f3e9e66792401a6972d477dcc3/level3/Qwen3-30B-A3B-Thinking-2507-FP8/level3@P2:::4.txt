

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by MCMC-based inference algorithms. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples obtained via annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The key insight is that the gap between forward and reverse AIS chains provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation, which can be used as a single scalar metric for evaluating inference quality.

The paper builds on Grosse et al. (2015) who introduced BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods, and connects to related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) who also use BDMC for evaluating posterior inference. The authors extend this to provide a rigorous convergence metric for MCMC inference, which is particularly valuable for black-box inference systems where users cannot easily assess inference quality.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound method with well-supported theoretical analysis. The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound (Section 3.1) is correct and extends the BDMC framework in a meaningful way. The experimental validation on multiple models in both WebPPL and Stan is thorough, with convincing results demonstrating that synthetic data analysis predicts real data behavior (Section 5.1). The method has been successfully applied to model selection (Section 5.2.1) and debugging (Section 5.2.2), revealing a bug in WebPPL that would have otherwise gone unnoticed. The authors carefully address limitations, particularly the requirement for simulated data, and provide a practical protocol for applying the method to real-world data.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though Section 3.1 presents significant readability challenges. The transition into the theoretical analysis is abrupt, with key variables and equations introduced without sufficient motivation. The term "AutoBDMC" appears multiple times before being explicitly defined (or even consistently used), creating confusion. The notation for data (y vs. D) is inconsistent, and there's a potential typo on Line 149 (p(θ, z | y) vs p(θ, z, y)). While the supplementary materials address some code integration issues, the paper would benefit from clearer definitions of key concepts and more consistent notation.

**Originality**: The core contribution—using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between true and approximate posteriors—is novel for the purpose of MCMC convergence assessment. While related work (Grosse et al., 2015; Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016) has explored BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation, the specific application to bounding the divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior represents a meaningful extension. The paper should more explicitly state this novelty in Section 3.1, as it is a central contribution.

**Significance**: This work addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference: the lack of rigorous metrics for evaluating MCMC convergence. The method provides a practical, general-purpose tool for probabilistic programming users to assess inference quality without requiring expert knowledge. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to a wide range of users. The demonstration of how the method can guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and reveal implementation bugs (Section 5.2.2) further underscores its practical significance.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**:

*Pro*:
- Provides a rigorous, general method for evaluating MCMC inference quality
- Successfully applied to two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Demonstrates practical utility for model selection and debugging
- Well-validated through multiple experiments
- Addresses a significant gap in the field

*Con*:
- Section 3.1 is difficult to follow due to poor exposition of theoretical foundations
- Key terms like "AutoBDMC" are undefined or inconsistently used
- Notation inconsistencies and minor technical errors
- Limited discussion of when the method might fail or provide misleading results

The paper is strong in its technical contribution and practical relevance, but suffers from significant clarity issues in the theoretical presentation. The authors should revise Section 3 to more clearly motivate the key definitions and explicitly state the novelty of their divergence bound. The term "AutoBDMC" should be properly defined early in the paper, and the notation should be made consistent throughout.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution that addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference with a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically useful. The clarity issues are significant but fixable with revision. The paper advances the state of the art in MCMC diagnostics by providing a rigorous, general-purpose metric that can be applied to a wide range of inference algorithms and models.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and precision in the theoretical presentation. The method is compelling, the experiments are solid, and the practical applications are well-demonstrated.
