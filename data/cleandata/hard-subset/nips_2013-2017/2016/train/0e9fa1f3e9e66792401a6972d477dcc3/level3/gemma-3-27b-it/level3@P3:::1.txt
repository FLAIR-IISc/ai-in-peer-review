This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), leveraging bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler’s output and the true posterior distribution. The motivation is strong: existing MCMC diagnostics are often insufficient for reliable assessment, particularly within “black box” probabilistic programming systems. The core idea – utilizing BDMC to bound this divergence – is promising and directly addresses a critical need in the field.

The work builds on the recent BDMC framework [GGA15] initially developed for marginal likelihood estimation. It extends this by deriving an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, demonstrating its accuracy on toy distributions, and providing a protocol for applying the method to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and simulated data generation.  The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and use it to explore tradeoffs in model representation, and even uncover a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampling implementation.

This is a significant contribution.  Related work in MCMC diagnostics ([CC96, GR92, BG98]) largely focuses on convergence *detection*, rather than *quantification* of error as BREAD attempts. The method is distinct from approaches like [GM15] which rely on strong convexity assumptions.  The paper correctly identifies and discusses related work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka [CTM16], highlighting a connection to subjective divergence and clarifying differences in approach. Importantly, the authors acknowledge and appropriately credit the foundational work of Neal [Nea01] on Annealed Importance Sampling. However, the paper would be strengthened by explicitly citing and discussing Geweke [2004] and Cook, Gelman & Rubin [2006]. These prior works offer established methods for validating MCMC by running samplers on data generated from the prior predictive distribution, providing a conceptually similar benchmark for assessing sampler performance, and offering a useful point of comparison.

Despite the strengths, there are areas for improvement. Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. The variable `q(.)` is used *before* being defined, leaving the reader unsure of its meaning.  Explicitly stating that `q(.)` represents the marginal of the AIS sampler after T steps would improve clarity. More broadly, while the authors mention the derivation details are in the supplement, the main text should strive to be more self-contained.  A more step-by-step explanation of how to apply BREAD would benefit readers unfamiliar with the details of BDMC.  Pushing essential details to the supplement hinders understanding and reproducibility.

A minor point: splitting unobserved variables into `z` and `θ` throughout the paper feels unnecessarily cluttered, as they consistently appear together. Using a single variable (e.g., `x`) would simplify the notation without losing information.  Additionally, a typographical error exists on line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence represents a novel approach to MCMC diagnostics.
* **Significance:**  Addressing the reliability of MCMC inference is a critical problem, particularly within probabilistic programming.  This work provides a rigorous (for simulated data) and potentially valuable tool for assessing sampler performance.
* **Soundness:**  The theoretical foundation is well-developed, and the validation on toy distributions is encouraging.
* **Practicality:** The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the feasibility of the method, and the bug discovery highlights its potential as a debugging tool.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Clarity:**  Section 3.1 is difficult to follow, and the reliance on the supplement for key derivation details reduces accessibility.
* **Completeness:** The lack of discussion of Geweke [2004] and Cook, Gelman & Rubin [2006] represents a missed opportunity to contextualize the work within existing validation methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. While improvements to clarity and completeness would be beneficial, the potential impact of this work is significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.  The authors have provided a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and probabilistic programming.
