

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution, specifically targeting MCMC inference in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The authors demonstrate that BDMC can provide rigorous upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence for simulated data, and they develop a protocol to apply this method to real-world data by using fitted hyperparameters to generate representative simulated datasets. The approach is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and validated across multiple models.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of MCMC diagnostics, which suffers from a lack of reliable quantitative measures. The core idea is compelling, as existing convergence diagnostics often fail to provide rigorous guarantees, and this work offers a theoretically grounded alternative. The method is well-motivated by the need to evaluate "black box" inference systems, and the experimental validation on real models demonstrates practical utility.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Crucially, it omits discussion of two key prior works: Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006), which established the practice of validating MCMC by running inference on data sampled from the prior. This omission weakens the paper's contextualization within the field. Section 3.1 is particularly problematic, as it uses q(·) without definition, making the derivation difficult to follow. The authors should explicitly define q as "the marginal of the AIS sampler after T steps" as suggested. While the supplementary material contains important derivation details, the main text should be self-contained and present a clear step-by-step explanation of the method. Additionally, the paper unnecessarily splits unobserved variables into z and θ when they always appear together, and contains a typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

Despite these issues, the core idea is strong and potentially impactful for practitioners evaluating MCMC reliability in PPLs. With revisions to address the missing citations, clarify the technical presentation, and fix minor issues, this work could make a significant contribution to the field. The significance of the method for debugging inference algorithms (as demonstrated by uncovering a WebPPL bug) further supports its value.
