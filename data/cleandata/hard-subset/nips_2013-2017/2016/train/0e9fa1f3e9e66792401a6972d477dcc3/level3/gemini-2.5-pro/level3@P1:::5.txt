This paper proposes a new method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods, to the problem of MCMC diagnostics. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC can be used to form an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and the true posterior. A key challenge for BDMC is that its stochastic upper bound requires an exact sample from the target distribution (the posterior), which is unavailable for real-world data. The paper's main methodological proposal is a protocol to circumvent this: first, run approximate inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, generate synthetic data from the model using these hyperparameters; finally, run the BREAD diagnostic on this synthetic data, for which an exact posterior sample can be generated. The authors implement their method in WebPPL and Stan and use it to compare the efficiency of different model parameterizations.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its attempt to develop a more theoretically grounded and quantitative diagnostic tool for MCMC convergence, a notoriously difficult and important problem. The field heavily relies on heuristics like the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic, which can fail to detect certain types of non-convergence.
*   **Originality:** The connection drawn between the BDMC log-likelihood gap and the Jeffreys divergence is a novel and elegant theoretical contribution. It provides a principled way to measure the quality of samples from AIS/SMC, moving beyond simple convergence checks to quantify the distributional mismatch.
*   **Significance:** If the proposed method were reliable, it could be a significant contribution. A tool that allows users of probabilistic programming languages to automatically and rigorously assess inference quality would be extremely valuable, both for practitioners and for developers of inference algorithms. The application of BREAD to debug a subtle bug in WebPPL is a compelling demonstration of its potential utility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the background concepts of AIS and BDMC before presenting its own contributions. The derivations in Section 3.1 are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

While the theoretical foundation of BREAD on simulated data (with a true posterior sample) is sound, the proposed protocol for applying it to real-world data rests on a series of strong and questionable assumptions. These assumptions undermine the method's claim to be a rigorous diagnostic tool in practice.

*   **Quality - Methodological Flaws:** The protocol for real-world data seems to suffer from a fundamental circularity.
    1.  It requires first performing approximate posterior inference on the real dataset to obtain an estimated hyperparameter `ˆη_real`. This initial step presumes that the MCMC run has already converged sufficiently well to produce a representative point estimate. If the initial inference failed, the entire basis for the diagnostic is flawed. The diagnostic is thus conditioned on the success of the very process it is meant to diagnose.
    2.  The method assumes that the MCMC convergence properties of the posterior for the synthetic data `p(θ, η | D_sim)` are representative of the convergence properties for the real-data posterior `p(θ, η | D_real)`. This is a strong, unjustified assumption. The geometry of the posterior (e.g., multimodality, correlations, conditioning) can be highly sensitive to the specific dataset. This is particularly problematic in cases of model misspecification, which is common in practice, as the real data may induce a posterior landscape qualitatively different from any that can be generated by the model itself.
    3.  The protocol uses a short MCMC run initialized from `(ˆη_real, θ)` to generate an *approximate* posterior sample to seed the reverse AIS chain. This optimistically assumes that the sampler can quickly reach the high-probability region and produce a sample that is a good proxy for an exact one. This is another strong assumption about the very mixing properties under investigation.

These three assumptions mean that the diagnostic's validity hinges on conditions that are as difficult to verify as the original problem of MCMC convergence. The paper does not provide strong arguments or evidence to support them.

*   **Quality - Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical validation in Section 5.1 is insufficient to allay these concerns. The experiments are conducted on five standard, relatively well-behaved models. While they show consistency between real and simulated data for these specific cases, they do not test the method in more challenging scenarios where diagnostics are most needed—for instance, on models with significant multimodality, strong posterior correlations, or where model misspecification is a known issue. The validation fails to demonstrate that the method is robust precisely when its core assumptions are most likely to be violated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses a highly significant problem in machine learning and statistics: the reliable diagnosis of MCMC convergence.
2.  The theoretical contribution of linking the BDMC gap to an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is novel and interesting.
3.  The demonstration of using the method to debug a probabilistic programming language implementation is a valuable practical result.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The proposed protocol for real-world data relies on strong, unverified, and circular assumptions that compromise its validity as a rigorous diagnostic.
2.  The empirical evaluation is limited to simple models and does not provide convincing evidence that the method works in the challenging cases where it would be most useful.
3.  The paper does not compare its diagnostic to existing standard methods (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, ESS) to show where it provides superior or complementary information.

**Recommendation:**

While the core idea is interesting, the methodological flaws in the proposed protocol for real-world data are significant. The paper would be much stronger if the authors could provide a more robust justification for their assumptions. I would recommend that the authors:
*   Explicitly acknowledge and discuss the circularity of the assumptions in the real-data protocol.
*   Provide a more compelling argument or evidence for why inference on synthetic data is a reliable proxy for inference on real data, especially addressing potential pitfalls like multimodality and model misspecification.
*   Conduct experiments on more challenging models where MCMC is known to be difficult and standard diagnostics may be misleading.
*   Perform a direct comparison of BREAD's findings with those from standard diagnostics like `R-hat` and effective sample size to demonstrate its relative strengths and weaknesses.

Without addressing these fundamental issues, the practical utility of the proposed method remains in question.
