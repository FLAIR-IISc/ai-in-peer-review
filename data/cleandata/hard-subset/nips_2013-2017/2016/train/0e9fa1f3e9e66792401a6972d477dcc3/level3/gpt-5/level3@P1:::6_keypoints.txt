- Adapts bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess MCMC-based posterior sample quality via forward/reverse AIS gaps that bound Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Focuses on AIS/SMC-like algorithms viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space and uses ancestral simulation to get exact posterior samples on simulated data.
- Integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan, validates on toy problems, and demonstrates use on regression, time series, mixture models, and matrix factorization.
- Proposes a pragmatic real-data protocol: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data at those hyperparameters, compare AIS behavior on real vs simulated data, and initialize reverse AIS with short MCMC moves.
- Clear derivation showing the BDMC sandwich for log partition functions translates into an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions.
- Practical integration into two probabilistic programming systems, demonstrating the method’s utility in realistic workflows and regression testing.
- Protocol shows empirical evidence that forward AIS behaves similarly on real and simulated data under fitted hyperparameters.
- Demonstrated diagnostic value by uncovering a subtle multivariate Gaussian sampling bug in WebPPL.
- Empirical case study shows BREAD can inform modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed) differently across platforms.
- Limited originality: main theoretical idea follows prior BDMC analysis; contribution is mainly repurposing known bounds and system integration, with overlap with concurrent CTM16 work.
- Guarantees only hold for simulated data with exact posterior samples; the real-data protocol is heuristic and breaks rigorous guarantees.
- Applicability is constrained to AIS/SMC-style samplers (or MCMC wrapped in such schemes), limiting direct relevance to general MCMC like HMC/NUTS.
- Experimental scope is modest: small models/data, limited exploration of challenging multimodal or high-dimensional posteriors where convergence diagnostics are most needed.
- Tightness of the bound is demonstrated mainly on toy problems where exact quantities are computable.
- Limited comparison to alternative diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, Geweke, Stein discrepancies); lacks systematic empirical head-to-head evaluations.
- Technical quality: derivations are sound and consistent with BDMC; caveats are acknowledged.
- Empirical methodology is reasonable with helpful sensitivity analyses on reverse-chain initialization length, but lacks broader quantitative calibration of bound tightness.
- Clarity: well written and organized; AIS/BDMC background and method-to-Jeffreys connection are clear; suggests clearer checklist of assumptions and consistent dispersion-aware summaries.
- Originality assessment: incremental contribution focused on tooling rather than new theory or algorithms.
- Significance: useful for PPL users/developers as a diagnostic and regression test; limited broader MCMC community impact due to AIS reliance and simulated-data requirement.
- Suggested improvements: broader empirical evaluation (multimodal, high-dim), quantify bound tightness distributionally (coverage/calibration).
- Suggested improvements: compare empirically against R-hat/ESS/Stein discrepancies and analyze sensitivity to annealing schedules, intermediate distributions, and kernels.
- Suggested improvements: explore extensions for per-instance probabilistic confidence (multiple reverse runs or concentration bounds) and clarify distinctions from CTM16 with empirical comparisons.
- Arguments for acceptance: practical, actionable diagnostic integrated into popular PPLs; clear exposition and demonstrated real-world value via debugging and modeling guidance.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, guarantees restricted to simulated data, modest experiments, and limited comparisons to established diagnostics.
- Recommendation: borderline reject for main conference; better fit for an MCMC/probabilistic programming workshop unless strengthened with broader empirical comparisons and deeper bound-analysis.
- Reviewer confidence: medium.
