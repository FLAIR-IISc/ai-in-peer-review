- Introduces BREAD, an extension of BDMC to measure reliability of MCMC posterior inference by bounding the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence.  
- Builds on Grosse et al. (2015) and connects to literature on MCMC diagnostics and BDMC-based evaluation.  
- Main technical contributions: (1) estimator that upper-bounds expected Jeffreys divergence, (2) technique to compute true divergence and bound on small examples, (3) protocol for applying method to real data, (4) integration and validation in WebPPL and Stan.  
- Experimental validation compares model representations, guides modeling choices, and uncovered a real WebPPL bug, demonstrating practical utility.  
- Strength: tackles an important, timely problem in probabilistic programming with a rigorous, general, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality.  
- Strength: solid theoretical foundation with proper derivation of the Jeffreys divergence upper bound.  
- Strength: practical relevance shown by integration into two popular probabilistic programming languages and multiple case studies.  
- Major weakness: significant clarity issues in Section 3.1, notably using q(·) before defining it and not stating the AIS marginal q(x_T) explicitly.  
- Presentation problems: key derivation details are left to the supplement, making the main text less self-contained.  
- Notational clutter: unnecessary splitting of unobserved variables into z and θ when they always appear together reduces readability.  
- Minor error: typographical mistake (missing closing parenthesis) at line 165.  
- Quality assessment: strong technical content and experiments, but real-data validation protocol needs better justification.  
- Clarity assessment: needs substantial revision to improve exposition and include key derivations in the main text.  
- Originality: novel extension of BDMC to evaluate MCMC via Jeffreys divergence, distinct from prior work like Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).  
- Significance: addresses a critical practical problem with potential impact for research and industry; demonstrated tangible debugging utility.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, theoretically grounded, practically implemented method with real-world applicability.  
- Rejection/concern arguments: presentation and clarity issues (especially theoretical exposition and notation) must be fixed before acceptance.
