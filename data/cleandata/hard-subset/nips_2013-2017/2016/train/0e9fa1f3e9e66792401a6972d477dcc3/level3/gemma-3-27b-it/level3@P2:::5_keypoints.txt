- Proposes BREAD: using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate MCMC samples.  
- Leverages BDMC’s stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood to quantify AIS/SMC accuracy.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validates on several models; used to guide model-representation choices.  
- Strong motivation: fills need for theoretically grounded diagnostics for black-box MCMC inference.  
- Central weakness: depends on hyperparameters from initial MCMC on real data to generate simulated datasets, creating circularity if those runs did not converge.  
- Mitigation (running a few MCMC steps from estimated hyperparameters) lacks strong theoretical justification for producing representative reverse-chain starts.  
- Questionable assumption that inference behavior on simulated data reflects behavior on real data, especially under model misspecification.  
- Applicability is limited: directly applies to AIS and SMC and is only indirectly relevant to general MCMC methods.  
- Focus on Jeffreys divergence may not be the most practically informative metric and isn’t shown superior to R-hat or effective sample size.  
- Experimental validation uses relatively simple models and does not sufficiently address the method’s key assumptions.  
- The model-representation guidance example is interesting but may rely heavily on the specific models/datasets used.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically motivated approach, practical implementations in popular PPLs, highlights need for rigorous diagnostics.  
- Arguments against acceptance: strong/possibly invalidating assumptions, limited scope, questionable metric utility, insufficient experiments, unclear behavior with multimodality or poor summaries.  
- Overall: interesting idea but significant weaknesses undermine practical value; recommendation is rejection pending stronger justification, robustness tests to misspecification and initial failures, and comparisons to established diagnostics.
