This paper proposes a method to measure the reliability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend the BDMC technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms and integrate it into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The method provides a rigorous way to bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors clearly explain the problem of evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples in MCMC inference. The proposed method is simple and easy to implement, making it a potentially high-impact contribution to probabilistic programming systems.

However, the technical contributions of the paper are modest, and the authors do not clearly discuss the novelty of their approach. The Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation is nice, but it is not clear if it is entirely new. Additionally, the authors do not cite or discuss Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which closely relates to BDMC and its acceptance ratio interpretation.

The paper also has some clarity issues, particularly in sections 3.1-3.2, which feel rushed and contain several ambiguities. The notation is sometimes unclear, and the authors use inconsistent labels, which can make the paper hard to follow. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a clear definition of the estimator \hat{p}(y), which makes the derivations hard to understand.

The experiments are also lacking in detail, and it is unclear how the authors chose the β_t values in practice. The figures are not well-labeled, and the axes and tick labels are too small.

In terms of related work, the authors do not provide a comprehensive discussion of the existing methods for evaluating MCMC inference. They mention some previous work, but the discussion is limited, and they do not clearly explain how their approach differs from others.

Overall, the paper has some interesting ideas, but it needs significant improvements in terms of clarity, novelty, and related work discussion. With some revisions, the paper could be a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic programming.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a new method for evaluating MCMC inference, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The method is simple and easy to implement, making it a potentially high-impact contribution to probabilistic programming systems.
* The paper provides some interesting experimental results, particularly in terms of comparing different model representations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The technical contributions of the paper are modest, and the authors do not clearly discuss the novelty of their approach.
* The paper has some significant clarity issues, particularly in sections 3.1-3.2.
* The notation is sometimes unclear, and the authors use inconsistent labels, which can make the paper hard to follow.
* The experiments are lacking in detail, and it is unclear how the authors chose the β_t values in practice.
* The related work discussion is limited, and the authors do not clearly explain how their approach differs from others.

**Recommendation:**

The paper could be acceptable if the authors address the following issues:

* Clarify the novelty of their approach and discuss the related work more comprehensively.
* Improve the clarity of the paper, particularly in sections 3.1-3.2.
* Provide a clear definition of the estimator \hat{p}(y) and explain the derivations in more detail.
* Provide more detail about the experiments, including how the authors chose the β_t values in practice.
* Improve the figures and labels to make them more readable.
