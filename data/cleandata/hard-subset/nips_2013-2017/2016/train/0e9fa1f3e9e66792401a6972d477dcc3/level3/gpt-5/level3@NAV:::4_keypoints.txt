- Proposes AutoBDMC: run AIS and reverse-AIS on simulated data to get stochastic lower/upper bounds on log marginal likelihood and use their gap as a single-number convergence diagnostic for MCMC posterior inference.
- Theoretical claim: the expected forward–reverse gap upper-bounds a symmetrized divergence between the true posterior and the sampler’s approximation (stated as Jeffreys/symmetrized KL).
- Builds on BDMC and AIS literature and positions itself against classical MCMC diagnostics and recent sample-quality tests; relates to concurrent BDMC work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).
- Integrates tool into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates utility for model selection and debugging (e.g., found a WebPPL bug).
- Quality: theory is carefully argued and matches toy cases; experiments on five models indicate synthetic-data analyses correlate with real-data behavior.
- Main limitation: method relies on simulated-data setting and requires an exact posterior draw; the hyperparameter-fitting protocol for real data is heuristic.
- Clarity issues: Section 3 (esp. transition to 3.1) introduces variables/factorizations with insufficient setup and lacks intuition before Eqs. (6–11).
- Request to define all symbols on first use and include a schematic of forward/reverse chains and conditionals for clarity.
- “AutoBDMC” is never crisply defined; paper should state precisely what constitutes AutoBDMC and use the term only after definition.
- Originality: extending BDMC to bounding posterior-sample error is a valuable contribution; clarify whether the divergence bound is novel relative to CTM (2016).
- Ambiguity on divergence type: ensure consistency on whether the bound is Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) or Jensen–Shannon.
- Significance: provides a practical, quantitative convergence metric useful for probabilistic programming and MCMC practitioners.
- Minor/editorial fixes: likely typo around Line 149 (p(θ, z | y) vs p(θ, z, y)); unify data notation (y vs D).
- Software suggestions: specify compatible WebPPL versions and installation steps; consider releasing an npm-installable WebPPL package and providing Stan code.
- Pros: clear/useful metric, solid theory and tooling, compelling cross-platform experiments, practical debugging value.
- Cons: dependence on simulated-data and heuristic posterior-initialization for real data, clarity/terminology issues (AutoBDMC, divergence type), and weak software packaging.
