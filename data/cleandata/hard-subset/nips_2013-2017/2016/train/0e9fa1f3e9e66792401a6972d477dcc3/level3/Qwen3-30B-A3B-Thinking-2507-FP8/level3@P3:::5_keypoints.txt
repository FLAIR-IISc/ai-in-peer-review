- Introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to bound Jeffreys divergence between MCMC approximations and the true posterior.
- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood for bounding inference error.
- Proposes a protocol for real data: infer hyperparameters, simulate data from them, and evaluate inference on simulated data.
- Builds on prior BDMC work and relates to convergence diagnostics and automatic MCMC tuning, while offering theoretically justified bounds.
- Novelty: directly applies BDMC to evaluate posterior approximation quality, with implementations in WebPPL and Stan.
- Theoretical derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence is mathematically sound.
- Practical implementation and empirical validation on several models demonstrate applicability.
- Real-world value shown by discovering a bug in WebPPL using BREAD.
- Main weakness: relies on critical assumptions in the synthetic-data protocol that can invalidate the diagnostic when they fail.
- Assumption 1: requires a representative hyperparameter η_real from prior MCMC runs, creating circularity since those runs must already have converged.
- Assumption 2: assumes synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the real posterior, which may fail under misspecification or multimodality.
- Assumption 3: assumes starting from η_real yields an almost exact sample quickly, an optimistic and unverified claim.
- Empirical validation is limited to simple examples where assumptions likely hold and does not demonstrate performance in complex scenarios.
- Paper does not adequately address handling cases where synthetic and real posteriors differ substantially.
- Quality: theoretical development is solid but practical utility is undermined by unvalidated assumptions tied to convergence.
- Clarity: paper is well-written, with precise derivations and a clear experimental setup.
- Significance: offers a theoretically justified diagnostic but practical impact is limited by the synthetic-data assumptions.
- Arguments for acceptance: sound theoretical framework, novel BDMC application, practical implementations, useful for debugging inference.
- Arguments against acceptance: unreliable under key assumptions, limited empirical stress-testing, lacks methods to validate assumptions, and circular protocol for real data.
- Recommendation: reject pending stronger empirical evidence, validation of assumptions, and direct comparisons to standard diagnostics.
