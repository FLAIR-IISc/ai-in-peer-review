- Contribution: Extends BDMC to upper-bound (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and AIS/SMC-like approximate samples via bidirectional AIS.
- Core idea: Run AIS forward (prior→posterior) for a stochastic lower bound and reverse (posterior→prior) initialized from exact posterior samples for a stochastic upper bound; their expected gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence.
- Implementation: Integrated the BREAD procedure into WebPPL and Stan.
- Empirical results: Validates that simulated-data behavior mirrors real-data when simulating from fitted hyperparameters.
- Empirical results: Shows the reverse bound is insensitive to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate required posterior samples over hyperparameters.
- Empirical results: Explores modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) and demonstrates debugging value by uncovering a WebPPL sampling bug.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015), AIS (Neal 2001), nonequilibrium work bounds (Jarzynski 1997), and complements Stein-method diagnostics.
- Concurrent work: The “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016) is closely related and matches the authors’ theory when reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior.
- Missing citations: Should cite and contrast prior simulation-based MCMC validation (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006) and clarify how BREAD complements SBC-style checks.
- Theoretical quality: Derivation that expected bidirectional AIS gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence is sound and well-motivated for simulated data with exact posterior initialization.
- Limitations: Guarantees hold in expectation and require exact posterior initialization (simulated data); fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data is heuristic.
- Statistical concerns: Need to report variability and confidence intervals for the bidirectional gap, discuss concentration/tail behavior, and quantify how many runs are needed for stability.
- AIS-specific concern: AIS log-weights are heavy-tailed; visualizing quartiles instead of means sidesteps variance issues—recommend principled aggregation (log-mean-exp, multiple-chain averaging with error bars).
- Tightness study: Tightness results are relegated to the appendix; add at least one main-text quantitative example comparing the bound to true divergence.
- Clarity issues: Section 3.1 is hard to follow—q(·) and v are introduced late; recommend explicitly defining qfwd(xT), qrev(v|xT), v = (x1,…,xT−1) up front.
- Presentation requests: Provide a step-by-step, self-contained derivation in the main text (from E[log R̂] and E[log R̂rev] to the Jeffreys bound) with clear assumptions (ergodicity, reversibility, exact initialization).
- Algorithmic clarity: Add a concise algorithm box for BREAD (inputs, schedules, simulated vs real data protocol, aggregation over runs).
- Notation cleanup: Avoid splitting unobserved variables into θ and z if they always appear together; consider using a single x throughout.
- Practical guidance requested: Provide guidance on temperature schedules, number of intermediate distributions, sensitivity analysis, and computational overhead.
- Comparative evaluation: Compare BREAD diagnostics to standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS) and Stein discrepancies to show complementary information.
- Acceptance pros: Rigorous guarantees for simulated data, practical integration into PPLs, useful debugging and modeling insights.
- Acceptance cons: Some theoretical and algorithmic details deferred, estimator variability not fully characterized, reliance on simulated data and heuristic real-data protocol may limit adoption.
- Minor fixes: Fix notation and typos (e.g., line 165), standardize references, and define/introduce the BREAD acronym once and use consistently.
