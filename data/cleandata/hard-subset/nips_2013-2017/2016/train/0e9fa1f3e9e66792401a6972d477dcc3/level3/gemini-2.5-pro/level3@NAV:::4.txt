This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively evaluating MCMC inference quality. Building upon the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework [GGA15], the approach uses forward (AIS) and reverse AIS to compute stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood for synthetic data. The central theoretical contribution is showing that the gap between these bounds upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. This provides a valuable single-scalar metric for convergence, moving beyond common heuristics for MCMC diagnostics.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-motivated and presents a method of significant practical importance. The quality of the work is high; the approach is technically sound, and the claims are supported by a compelling set of experiments in two widely-used probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL. The authors are transparent about the primary limitation—that the method provides rigorous bounds only on synthetic data—and they propose and validate a reasonable protocol for using these analyses to predict performance on real-world data. The paper’s significance is further enhanced by demonstrating BREAD’s utility not only for convergence analysis but also for practical tasks like comparing model representations and debugging inference code, as evidenced by the discovery of a bug in WebPPL.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness lies in its clarity, particularly in Section 3. The derivation of the divergence bound, which is a core contribution, is difficult to follow, as key equations and variables are introduced without sufficient intuition or motivation. Regarding originality, if the divergence bound presented in Section 3.1 is a novel contribution, this should be stated explicitly to properly frame its importance. There are also several minor issues that hinder clarity: a potential typo on Line 149 (p(theta, z | y) should likely be p(theta, z, y)) and inconsistent notation for data (y vs. D). Finally, to improve reproducibility and impact, the supplemental code should be better documented with compatible WebPPL versions and integration instructions. Ideally, the code for both platforms would be released as easily installable packages.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a useful and well-validated method. The weaknesses are primarily related to presentation and could be addressed in a revised version.
