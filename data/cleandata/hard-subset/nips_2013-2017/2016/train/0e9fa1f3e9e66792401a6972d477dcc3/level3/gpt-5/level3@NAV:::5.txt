This paper adapts Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based inference by using stochastic upper/lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between an algorithm’s approximate posterior and the true posterior. The practical idea is to compare samplers (primarily AIS/SMC variants) by the tightness of the BDMC “sandwich” under equal computational budgets. Because BDMC requires an exact posterior draw to form a valid stochastic upper bound, the authors propose a synthetic-data protocol: infer a representative hyperparameter η_real on the real dataset, simulate data from these hyperparameters, and then run forward/reverse AIS to produce bounds. They integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan and present small-scale experiments, including representation choices for matrix factorization. The work relates to prior diagnostics (Gelman & Rubin, 1992; Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996) and to sample-quality measures (Gorham & Mackey, 2015), as well as contemporaneous “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Quality: The derivation that the BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is sound and clearly builds on BDMC/AIS theory (Neal, 2001). However, the central protocol relies on strong assumptions that are not justified empirically in complex settings: (1) a single η_real, obtained from prior MCMC runs, is assumed representative, implicitly presuming those runs converged; (2) the synthetic-data posterior is assumed to mirror the real-data posterior’s geometry/mixing difficulty, which can fail badly under model misspecification; (3) initializing reverse AIS from η_real and a few MCMC steps is assumed to yield an almost exact sample. These assumptions target the very convergence issues the diagnostic seeks to assess, undermining validity if they fail. The empirical section focuses on simple examples; evidence that these failure modes will not matter at scale is limited.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized; the exposition of AIS/BDMC is careful. The protocol details (choice of temperatures, transitions, compute-equality criteria, and how bounds are aggregated across chains) could be specified more precisely for reproducibility.

Originality: The use of BDMC to bound sample quality and the “tightest bounds” selection criterion are interesting, though related to concurrent subjective divergence. Positioning relative to that work could be expanded.

Significance: If robust, this could be a useful, theoretically motivated diagnostic for AIS/SMC (and for design choices in PPLs). The current reliance on synthetic-data proxies and limited experiments reduce immediate impact.

Suggestions:
- Justify and stress-test the η_real and synthetic-data assumptions: ablations that perturb η_real, deliberate model misspecification, multimodal mixtures, and funnel geometries.
- Compare against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, energy/BFMI, Geweke) and Stein discrepancies; report correlations with downstream posterior errors.
- Clarify how the method handles multimodality and poor summaries; show cases where BDMC succeeds while standard diagnostics fail.
- Provide fairness controls for “equal compute” (e.g., wall-clock, gradient evals).

Pros for acceptance:
- Theoretically grounded diagnostic with clear ties to BDMC
- Practical integration into PPLs; potential to guide modeling/algorithmic choices
- Clear derivations and interesting idea of using bound tightness as a selection metric

Cons against acceptance:
- Key assumptions tied to convergence are unverified and may fail in realistic scenarios
- Limited empirical validation on challenging posteriors and under misspecification
- Applicability mainly to AIS/SMC; less clear for general-purpose MCMC without AIS wrapping
