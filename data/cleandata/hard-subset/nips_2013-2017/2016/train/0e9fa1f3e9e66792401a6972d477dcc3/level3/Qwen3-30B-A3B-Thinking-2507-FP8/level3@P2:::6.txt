

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a method to evaluate the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), extending the work of Grosse et al. (2015) to bound the symmetrized KL divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors integrate their approach into WebPPL and Stan, and apply it to study model representations in matrix factorization.

The paper builds on the BDMC framework introduced in Grosse et al. (2015), which provides stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors extend this to bound the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. They present a protocol for using BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to evaluate inference quality on real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters and using approximate posterior samples to initialize reverse chains.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with careful derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The experimental validation on multiple models and datasets demonstrates the method's applicability to real-world probabilistic programming systems. However, the paper does not provide new theoretical results beyond extending Grosse et al. (2015), and the experimental validation consists primarily of applying known techniques to new platforms rather than demonstrating novel insights. The paper does not address important limitations of the approach, such as the computational cost of running both forward and reverse AIS chains, or how the method would scale to larger models.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail to understand the method and reproduce the experiments. The derivation in Section 3.1 is mathematically rigorous, and the experimental setup is well-described. The figures effectively illustrate the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data.

**Originality:** The main contribution of this paper is application rather than innovation. The authors extend BDMC to a new use case (evaluating MCMC inference), but this extension follows directly from the theoretical framework established in Grosse et al. (2015). The protocol for evaluating inference on real-world data is straightforward and not novel. The paper cites related work, including the concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also builds on BDMC for posterior evaluation. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from this related work.

**Significance:** The method provides a useful tool for probabilistic programming practitioners to assess inference quality, which is an important practical problem. However, the paper does not demonstrate significant advances over existing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin statistics) or other methods for evaluating MCMC reliability. The debugging example in Section 5.2.2 is interesting, but it represents a minor application of the method rather than a major contribution. The paper's significance is limited to the specific context of probabilistic programming languages, and the results do not advance the broader field of MCMC methodology.

**Strengths:**
- Well-executed implementation in two major probabilistic programming platforms (WebPPL and Stan)
- Clear demonstration of the method's ability to validate inference behavior between real and simulated data
- Useful debugging application that uncovered a bug in WebPPL
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple models and datasets

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks novel theoretical contributions beyond extending existing BDMC work
- The method is limited to specific inference algorithms (AIS/SMC) and requires simulated data
- The protocol for real-world data evaluation relies on strong assumptions about the posterior approximation
- Does not sufficiently distinguish contributions from concurrent work (CTM16)
- Fails to demonstrate significant advantages over existing MCMC convergence diagnostics

**Recommendation:** This paper is a solid engineering contribution that provides a useful tool for practitioners of probabilistic programming. However, it does not present sufficient theoretical novelty or significant advances in the state of the art to justify acceptance at NIPS. The paper primarily describes implementing known techniques rather than introducing new methods. The contributions would be more appropriately presented at a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, rather than at NIPS. While the implementation work is valuable, the lack of novel theoretical insights and the limited scope of the method make it a marginal contribution for a top-tier machine learning conference.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing convergence diagnostics, a clearer statement of how the proposed method advances beyond the state of the art, and a more thorough discussion of limitations. The authors should also more explicitly distinguish their work from the concurrent CTM16 paper.
