

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique introduced by Grosse et al. (2015) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors develop a method they term "Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing" (BREAD) and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. The approach leverages the fact that BDMC can produce both stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, which can be used to bound the Jeffreys divergence when exact posterior samples are available (typically through simulation).

The paper builds directly on Grosse et al. (2015), which introduced BDMC as a method for estimating log marginal likelihoods by sandwiching the true value between stochastic upper and lower bounds. The authors' contribution consists primarily of applying this existing technique to a new problem domain (evaluating MCMC inference quality) and implementing it in two probabilistic programming languages. The paper also proposes a protocol for using BREAD with real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters.

Regarding quality, the technical content is sound and the implementation appears correct. The authors validate their method on toy examples and demonstrate its application to matrix factorization models in both WebPPL and Stan. The experiments show that BREAD can provide useful insights into model representation choices and even uncovered a bug in WebPPL. However, the paper does not provide theoretical guarantees beyond what was already established in Grosse et al. (2015), and the empirical results are somewhat limited in scope.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to probabilistic programming researchers. The implementation details are sufficiently described to allow reproduction, though the paper would benefit from more detailed documentation of the WebPPL and Stan extensions.

The originality of this work is limited. The core technique (BDMC) was introduced in Grosse et al. (2015), and the paper's main contribution is applying this technique to a new domain rather than developing novel theoretical results. The connection to related work (particularly Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016) is acknowledged, though the relationship could be clarified more explicitly. The NIPS 2015 proceedings already contain several papers on bidirectional Monte Carlo and related techniques (e.g., Grosse et al., 2015), making this work somewhat incremental.

The significance of the work is modest. While BREAD provides a useful diagnostic tool for probabilistic programming practitioners, it is limited to simulated data and requires exact posterior samples for validation. The paper does not demonstrate how BREAD can be used to improve inference algorithms beyond the matrix factorization case study, nor does it provide a compelling argument for why this method would be widely adopted by the broader machine learning community. The approach is valuable as a diagnostic tool, but it does not advance the state of the art in MCMC methods or probabilistic inference.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a useful implementation of existing techniques in two popular probabilistic programming languages
- Demonstrates practical application of BDMC to a new problem (evaluating MCMC inference quality)
- Uncovers a bug in WebPPL, showing the method's utility for debugging
- Presents a clear protocol for applying the technique to real-world data

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks original theoretical contributions; primarily implements known techniques
- The core technique (BDMC) was already presented at NIPS 2015
- The paper does not provide compelling evidence that BREAD will be widely adopted or significantly advance the field
- The application to real-world data is limited and lacks thorough validation
- The venue (NIPS) is typically reserved for more substantial theoretical or methodological advances

This paper represents a solid engineering contribution that could be valuable for practitioners using probabilistic programming languages, but it does not contain sufficient novelty or significance to warrant publication at NIPS. The work would be more appropriately presented at an MCMC or probabilistic programming workshop (such as the International Workshop on Probabilistic Programming), where the focus is more on practical implementations and applications rather than theoretical advances. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification for why this specific application of BDMC is novel and impactful, rather than simply demonstrating its implementation.
