- Proposes BREAD: Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing, extending BDMC to quantitatively evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference via forward and reverse AIS on simulated data.  
- Shows the expected gap between stochastic lower and upper bounds on log marginal likelihood upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and forward AIS approximation.  
- Provides a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality that accounts for both mode-covering and mode-seeking behavior.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating generality across PPLs and inference engines (M-H, HMC/NUTS).  
- Empirical validation is thorough; experiments show inference behavior on simulated data (from fitted hyperparameters) matches real data and the method can guide modeling choices.  
- Practical utility illustrated by a case study comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization and by detecting a bug in WebPPL’s sampler.  
- Strength: addresses a fundamental problem—trustworthiness of MCMC outputs—and offers an automated tool for benchmarking and debugging inference.  
- Strength: novel, elegant theoretical connection from BDMC sandwich gap to Jeffreys divergence.  
- Strength: technically sound derivation and clear exposition of pitfalls and protocols for real-world use.  
- Weakness: rigorous guarantees hold only for simulated data with access to exact posterior samples; protocol for real data is heuristic and assumes well-specified models.  
- Weakness: not directly applicable to standard MCMC (e.g., Metropolis-Hastings, Gibbs); applicability is mainly to AIS/SMC-like extended-space importance samplers.  
- Weakness: computationally intensive—requires many AIS chains in both directions; trade-offs between computation and bound quality need discussion.  
- Overall: well-executed, significant contribution with transparent limitations; recommendation to accept.
