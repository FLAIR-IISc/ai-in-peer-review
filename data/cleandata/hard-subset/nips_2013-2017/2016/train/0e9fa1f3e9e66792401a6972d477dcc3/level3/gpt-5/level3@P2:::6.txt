Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies how to quantify the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference. It builds on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse, Ghahramani, Adams 2015), which sandwiches the log marginal likelihood by running AIS forward (stochastic lower bound in expectation) and reverse (stochastic upper bound in expectation given access to exact posterior samples). The main technical claim here is that the expected gap between reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS and the true posterior. The authors call the resulting procedure BREAD. They also propose a heuristic “fitted-hyperparameter” protocol to assess real datasets: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data from them, and apply BREAD on the simulated dataset to diagnose inference behavior.

They integrate the method into WebPPL (MH over execution traces) and Stan (NUTS/HMC), validate the bound’s behavior on toy problems where exact divergences can be computed, and conduct experiments on several models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures). They illustrate how BREAD can guide modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and report that it helped uncover a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampling. Related work is discussed, including BDMC (AISTATS’15) and independent concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), classical MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman), and Stein discrepancy methods (Gorham–Mackey, NIPS’15).

Strengths
- Clear, careful exposition of BDMC/AIS mechanics and how to turn the bidirectional AIS gap into an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence for AIS-produced samples.
- Practical tooling: integration into WebPPL and Stan; experiments on multiple canonical models; use as a debugging aid.
- Useful practitioner guidance: insights into collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations across two systems; protocol to relate simulated and real-data behavior.
- Polite, thorough discussion of related work, including concurrent efforts.

Weaknesses
- Originality is limited. The core theoretical result is a fairly direct consequence of BDMC’s analysis for AIS; the idea that the bidirectional gap upper bounds a symmetrized KL in expectation is close to known results, and concurrent work [CTM16] covers closely related ground.
- The most rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample, confining the method to simulated data. The real-data “fitted hyperparameter” protocol is heuristic with no guarantees and relies on limited empirical validation.
- Tightness of the bound beyond toy cases is unclear. The paper shows qualitative alignment but lacks systematic assessment of looseness, error bars, or conditions under which the gap meaningfully reflects sample quality.
- Limited comparison to alternative diagnostics (e.g., R̂, ESS, Stein discrepancies) on the same tasks to contextualize practical benefits.
- Some bibliographic/citation formatting issues and minor notational rough edges.

Quality
The derivations appear correct and consistent with BDMC/AIS analyses. The expectation bound is stated carefully. Experiments are sensible and reproducible in principle (integration into two PPLs is a plus). However, the empirical evaluation does not thoroughly quantify bound tightness or compare to other diagnostics. The real-data protocol lacks theoretical backing; while the consistency checks are reassuring, they do not establish reliability in broader settings.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with helpful background and motivation. The exposition of forward/reverse AIS and how the Jeffreys divergence arises from the expected bidirectional gap is clear. A few references are incomplete and should be cleaned up. It would help to more explicitly summarize computational costs (e.g., number of temperatures, steps, wall-clock) and provide guidance on schedule design.

Originality
Moderate to low. The main idea follows directly from BDMC and AIS, with concurrent related work arriving at similar conclusions. The practical integration and case studies are valuable but not methodologically novel.

Significance
Moderate for practitioners of probabilistic programming and Bayesian computation: a tool that can flag convergence issues, guide modeling choices, and even catch implementation bugs is useful. However, the requirement of exact posterior samples limits the scope to simulated data; the heuristic extension to real data reduces the strength of the guarantees. Without stronger evidence of tightness and broader applicability beyond AIS/SMC settings, impact is likely incremental.

Reasons to accept
- Solid, careful articulation and packaging of a useful evaluation technique for AIS/SMC-style inference.
- Practical integration into widely used PPLs; demonstrates real debugging value.
- Clear experiments showing how the method can inform modeling/inference choices.

Reasons to reject
- Limited novelty relative to BDMC and concurrent “subjective divergence” work; main theoretical result is a straightforward extension.
- Guarantees apply only to simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic and unproven.
- Insufficient comparative evaluation and quantification of bound tightness.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a more systematic study of bound tightness across problem classes (dimensionality, multimodality, conditioning), with confidence intervals and wall-clock analysis.
- Compare against Stein discrepancy metrics, R̂, ESS, and other diagnostics to show when BREAD offers distinct advantages.
- Clarify the computational overhead of reverse AIS and guidance on temperature schedules/transition kernels.
- Discuss applicability to SMC and other extended-state samplers, and to non-AIS MCMC workflows via shared design choices.
- Clean up citations and ensure related work is accurately positioned vis-à-vis CTM’16.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). While the paper is well executed and practically useful, the methodological novelty is limited and concurrent work diminishes the contribution. A workshop on MCMC/probabilistic programming may be a better venue. Confidence: 0.7.
