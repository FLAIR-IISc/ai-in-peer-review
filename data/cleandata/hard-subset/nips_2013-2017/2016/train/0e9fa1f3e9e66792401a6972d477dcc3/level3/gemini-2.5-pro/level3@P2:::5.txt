### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a novel method, "Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing" (BREAD), for evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, originally proposed by Grosse et al. (2015) for estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provided by BDMC can be used to form an estimator that upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by a sampler and the true posterior.

This method is applicable to MCMC algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). A key challenge is that BDMC requires an exact sample from the target (posterior) distribution to compute the stochastic upper bound, which is generally unavailable for real-world problems. The authors propose a protocol to circumvent this: first, run approximate inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters; finally, run BREAD on this synthetic dataset, where an exact posterior sample is available from the simulation process. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference on several models and to compare the performance of different model representations for matrix factorization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a critical problem:** The paper tackles the highly important and challenging problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples. This is a major pain point for users of probabilistic programming systems and practitioners of Bayesian modeling, where existing diagnostics are often heuristic and can fail silently.

2.  **Novel theoretical contribution:** The connection between the BDMC bounds and the Jeffreys divergence is a clever and elegant theoretical insight. It provides a more principled and quantitative measure of sample quality than many existing diagnostics, moving beyond simple convergence checks to assess the distributional discrepancy.

3.  **Practical implementation and use cases:** The integration of BREAD into two popular and distinct probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan) is a valuable contribution, demonstrating the method's potential for practical use. The experiments showing how BREAD can be used to guide modeling decisions (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and to debug a probabilistic programming implementation are compelling demonstrations of its utility.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weaknesses of this work lie in the protocol proposed for applying BREAD to real-world data, which relies on a series of strong and potentially problematic assumptions.

1.  **Circular reasoning in the proposed protocol:** The protocol requires an initial MCMC run on the real dataset to obtain an estimated hyperparameter `ˆ⌘real`. The validity of this estimate hinges on the assumption that this initial MCMC run has already converged to a representative part of the posterior. This is fundamentally circular: the diagnostic tool designed to assess convergence requires a well-converged initial run to function properly. The paper does not address how to handle situations where the hyperparameter posterior is complex (e.g., multimodal or heavy-tailed), making a single point estimate `ˆ⌘real` a poor summary.

2.  **Unsubstantiated assumption of posterior similarity:** The method evaluates sampler performance on a synthetic dataset generated using `ˆ⌘real`. The core assumption is that the MCMC sampler's convergence behavior on the posterior of this synthetic data is representative of its behavior on the true posterior of the real-world data. This is a very strong claim that is not theoretically justified or empirically validated in challenging scenarios. This assumption is particularly likely to fail in the common case of model misspecification, where the generative process of the real data differs from the model, potentially leading to very different posterior geometries.

3.  **Optimistic assumption for initializing the reverse chain:** To run the reverse AIS chain, the protocol starts from the known parameters used to generate the synthetic data and applies a few MCMC steps, assuming this is sufficient to produce a sample that is "plausible" and close to an exact posterior sample. This is an optimistic assumption. If the posterior is difficult to explore, a short chain initialized this way may not be representative, undermining the validity of the resulting upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. This step essentially assumes that obtaining a good posterior sample is easy, which is the very problem the diagnostic is meant to help solve.

4.  **Limited empirical validation:** The validation experiments in Section 5.1 use relatively standard models. While they show consistency between inference on real and simulated data for these specific cases, they do not provide compelling evidence that the critical assumptions will hold in more complex, high-dimensional, or multimodal settings where reliable diagnostics are most crucial. A more convincing validation would involve demonstrating the method's effectiveness on problems where standard diagnostics (like the Gelman-Rubin statistic) are known to fail.

**Questions and suggestions for improvement**

*   The authors should more directly address the circularity in their proposed protocol. How can a user trust the initial estimate `ˆ⌘real` if they do not yet trust their MCMC sampler? Perhaps an ensemble of `ˆ⌘real` values could be used to assess robustness.
*   The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough investigation of the "posterior similarity" assumption. For instance, the authors could study a case of known model misspecification and show whether BREAD still provides useful insights or if its results become misleading.
*   The authors should consider providing a direct comparison of BREAD to standard convergence diagnostics. For example, on a model with known multimodality, does BREAD correctly identify that a sampler has failed to explore all modes, and how does its signal compare to that of, e.g., running multiple chains for a Gelman-Rubin test?
*   The analysis of the bound tightness in Appendix B is useful. It would be valuable to understand how this tightness scales with the dimensionality of the problem.

**Overall evaluation**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting approach to the significant problem of MCMC diagnostics. The core idea of using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a solid contribution. However, the paper's practical significance for real-world data analysis is limited by the strong, and largely unverified, assumptions underlying its proposed application protocol. While the work is promising, its current form overstates the reliability of the method for real-world datasets without more substantial justification and empirical support in challenging inference scenarios.
