- Proposes BREAD: apply BDMC by running forward and reverse AIS to bound the marginal likelihood.  
- Shows the expected gap between forward/reverse bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS sample distribution and the true posterior.  
- Reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, so rigorous guarantees only hold for simulated data.  
- For real data the authors use a heuristic (initialize reverse chain from approximate posterior), which breaks formal upper-bound guarantees.  
- Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, representing a valuable engineering contribution.  
- Empirically validates bound tightness on toy problems and assesses the protocol on real-world data.  
- Demonstrates practical utility: comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations and finding a bug in WebPPL.  
- Strength: addresses the important problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence with a principled, quantitative measure (Jeffreys divergence).  
- Strength: paper is clear, well-written, and provides easy-to-follow derivations and experiments.  
- Strength: technically sound with solid empirical evaluation and useful implementations for PPL users/developers.  
- Weakness: limited originality—the core technique is BDMC from Grosse et al. (2015); theoretical extension is incremental.  
- Weakness: similar in spirit to concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).  
- Weakness: contribution is largely application/implementation, making it better suited to a workshop or computational-statistics venue than NIPS.  
- Weakness: practical limitation that rigorous guarantees do not extend to real-world data without an exact posterior sample.  
- For acceptance: addresses a significant problem, provides a practical tool, and delivers non-trivial PPL implementations.  
- Against acceptance: little methodological novelty, mainly an application/implementation of existing techniques, and limited theoretical applicability to real data.  
- Overall: well-executed and useful application of BDMC but lacking significant originality, so better fit for a specialized or application-focused venue.
