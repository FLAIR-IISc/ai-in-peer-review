- Proposal: Introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing) to bound the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate MCMC samples using bidirectional Monte Carlo, extending BDMC to provide an MCMC diagnostic.  
- Scope: Applies mainly to AIS and SMC samplers and requires the ability to generate exact target samples in simulated-data settings.  
- Core merit: Offers a theoretically justified diagnostic for MCMC convergence, addressing a real need for reliability checks in black-box inference.  
- Practical relevance: Targets probabilistic programming systems (WebPPL, Stan) where users lack reliable convergence diagnostics.  
- Positioning: Correctly situated relative to existing MCMC diagnostics and marginal likelihood estimation literature; acknowledges concurrent work.  
- Major flaw: Method depends on a pre-existing, converged MCMC run to obtain η_real, which undermines the diagnostic’s purpose.  
- Questionable summary statistic use: Assuming a single summary statistic from the initial run is sufficient is doubtful, especially for complex posteriors.  
- Synthetic-data assumption issue: Assuming synthetic-data posterior has similar convergence properties to the real posterior is unjustified and problematic under model misspecification.  
- Optimistic convergence claim: Claim that starting from η_real yields an almost exact sample is unverified and may fail for challenging posteriors.  
- Empirical shortcomings: Evaluation is limited and uses overly simple examples; findings (including the WebPPL bug) are insufficient evidence of robustness.  
- Failure modes: Paper does not adequately address multimodality or the impact of poor summary statistics on the diagnostic.  
- Overall conclusion: Reviewer recommends weak reject due to fundamental assumptions and limited empirical support.  
- Recommendations: Remove reliance on prior converged runs, justify synthetic-data assumptions theoretically/empirically, study multimodality and summary-statistic sensitivity, run broader simulations and compare to standard diagnostics, and clarify applicability and limitations.
