This paper proposes a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD) to quantitatively measure the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which uses forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains to produce stochastic lower and upper bounds on the model's log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound, in expectation, on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior.

Since the reverse AIS chain must be initialized from an exact posterior sample, the method's theoretical guarantees only hold for simulated data where the ground truth parameters are known. To bridge this gap for real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, run inference on the real data to estimate hyperparameters; second, simulate a new dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters; and third, apply BREAD on this simulated dataset to evaluate the inference quality. The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. They validate the approach on several models, demonstrating that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated data under their protocol. Finally, they showcase the utility of BREAD by using it to compare the performance of different model representations for matrix factorization and to uncover a bug in WebPPL's sampler.

This work builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015). The idea of validating MCMC samplers by running them on simulated data, where the true posterior is known (or at least the data-generating parameters are), is a well-established practice in Bayesian statistics. However, the paper misses the opportunity to connect its contribution to this line of work, such as the posterior predictive checks and simulation studies proposed by Geweke (2004, "Getting it right: P-values for MCMC") and Cook, Gelman, and Rubin (2006, "Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles"). These methods provide a philosophical underpinning for the paper's general approach, and discussing them would better situate the specific quantitative contribution of BREAD within the existing literature.

**Quality**
The paper appears technically sound. The derivation connecting the BDMC gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a key theoretical contribution and seems correct. The work is a complete piece of research, presenting the core idea, a practical protocol for its application, and extensive experiments on multiple models and platforms. The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of the method—namely its direct applicability only to simulated data—and the experimental validation of their proposed protocol for real-world data is a thoughtful and necessary addition.

**Clarity**
While the paper is generally well-written, the clarity of the central derivation in Section 3.1 could be significantly improved. The notation `q(.)` is used in equations (4) and (5) before being explicitly defined. For instance, `q_fwd(x_T)` should be clearly stated as the marginal distribution over the final state of the forward AIS chain. The logical leap from Eq. (7) to Eq. (8) relies on the property that KL divergence does not increase upon marginalization, which should be explicitly mentioned for the reader's benefit. Making this section more self-contained and walking through the derivation step-by-step would strengthen the paper considerably.

On a minor note, the notation is sometimes cluttered. For example, the unobserved variables are consistently split into parameters `theta` and latent variables `z`, but they almost always appear together as a pair `(theta, z)`. Combining them into a single variable would simplify the notation without loss of generality. There is also a typo in line 165 (a missing closing parenthesis).

**Originality**
The core idea of using the BDMC framework to bound the divergence between an approximate and true posterior distribution is novel and clever. While the use of simulated data for MCMC validation is not new, BREAD offers a more rigorous, quantitative measure of sample quality than the qualitative or heuristic checks common in prior work. The application of this technique as a diagnostic tool for probabilistic programming languages is a valuable and original contribution. The concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) is appropriately cited and discussed. As mentioned, the originality claim could be sharpened by explicitly differentiating the work from earlier simulation-based validation methods (Geweke 2004; Cook et al. 2006).

**Significance**
The potential impact of this work is high. Reliable, quantitative, and automated diagnosis of MCMC sampler performance is a major challenge, especially for users of black-box inference systems like PPLs. Existing diagnostics are often heuristic and can fail to detect non-convergence. BREAD provides a principled tool that can help PPL users gain confidence in their results and guide PPL developers in designing and debugging their systems. The paper’s examples of comparing model representations and finding an implementation bug are powerful demonstrations of the method's practical utility. This work is likely to be built upon and used by both researchers and practitioners in the probabilistic modeling community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and principled method for quantitatively evaluating MCMC sampler quality by bounding the Jeffreys divergence to the true posterior.
*   Addresses a significant and long-standing problem in probabilistic inference, which is particularly relevant for the growing field of probabilistic programming.
*   The method is implemented and validated in two widely-used PPLs (Stan and WebPPL) on a variety of models.
*   The experiments provide compelling use cases, including guiding model representation choices and debugging inference engines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The key theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow and would benefit from greater clarity and detail.
*   The paper fails to cite and discuss highly relevant prior work on MCMC validation using simulated data, weakening its positioning in the literature.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. The core idea is innovative and the practical utility is clearly demonstrated. The primary weaknesses are in the clarity of the main derivation and the omission of some relevant literature, both of which are readily addressable.
