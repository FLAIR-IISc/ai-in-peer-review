This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to quantify the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference. Using annealed importance sampling (AIS) run in both directions (prior→posterior and posterior→prior), the authors obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood for simulated datasets. They show that the expected gap between the reverse and forward AIS log-bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence (i.e., the symmetrized KL) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS. They call this use of BDMC for divergence bounding BREAD and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. They also propose a pragmatic protocol for real data: infer hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from those hyperparameters, and verify that behavior on simulated data mirrors the real-data behavior. Experiments span five canonical models and a matrix factorization case study comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations; BREAD also uncovered a bug in WebPPL’s multivariate Gaussian sampler. The paper situates the work relative to BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015], AIS [Neal, 2001], SMC [Del Moral et al., 2006], convergence diagnostics [Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Brooks and Gelman, 1998], Stein discrepancies [Gorham and Mackey, 2015], and “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016].

Quality:
- Theory: The derivation that E[log R̂rev−1 − log R̂fwd] upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior (Eqs. analogous to their (8) and (11)) appears correct and clearly extends BDMC’s analysis from log-ML to sample-quality. The assumption set (exact posterior sample for reverse AIS, ergodic kernels, AIS as IS over an extended state space) is stated, and the expectation bound is appropriately qualified.
- Tightness/diagnostics: The toy examples showing that the bound is often tight are helpful; more systematic characterizations (e.g., across multimodality/conditioning and temperature schedules) would strengthen confidence. The real-data protocol (fitted hyperparameters) is sensible and validated empirically by showing close agreement between real and matched simulated data, and insensitivity to the number of MCMC steps used to initialize the reverse chain.
- Empirics: The five-model study and matrix factorization case are convincing in demonstrating utility (representation choices, debugging) across two systems. It would be useful to report wall-clock overhead, AIS schedule choices, variance of estimates, and how many AIS chains are needed to achieve stable bounds in practice.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and well-motivated. However, the transition into Section 3 and the introduction of the variables v and x_T come abruptly; a short roadmap of the forward/reverse chain factorization and the role of each term (q_fwd(v|x_T), q_rev(v|x_T)) would help.
- The manuscript should explicitly and prominently state the novelty of the Jeffreys-divergence expectation bound, since it is a central contribution distinct from BDMC’s original log-ML sandwiching.
- Terminology: please use one name consistently. The paper uses BDMC and BREAD; if “AutoBDMC” appears anywhere, define it precisely at first use and align it with the rest of the paper.
- Notation consistency: unify y vs. D for data; check the occurrence where p(θ,z|y) and p(θ,z,y) might be confused (around the discussion of Eq. (2) and Eq. (3)).
- Figures: because averages of log AIS weights are not justified by the bound, your choice to show quartiles is reasonable; still, annotate figures with the number of chains, temperatures, and total computation.

Originality:
- The application of BDMC to bound posterior sample quality and the expectation bound on the Jeffreys divergence are, to my knowledge, novel contributions. The relation to concurrent “subjective divergence” work is appropriately discussed; emphasizing the exact-posterior-sample condition under which both upper-bound the Jeffreys divergence clarifies the distinction.

Significance:
- Reliable, quantitative assessment of MCMC sample quality—especially in probabilistic programming—is important. The method provides a single, interpretable scalar bound, is rigorous on simulated data, and proves useful for model representation choices and implementation debugging. The main limitation is the requirement for AIS/SMC-style samplers and access to exact posterior samples (hence simulated data), but the real-data protocol partially mitigates this and is itself of practical value.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous extension of BDMC yielding an expectation upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical integration into two widely used PPLs; impactful case studies (representation trade-offs; bug discovery).
- Sensible and validated protocol to connect simulated and real data.
- Related work positioned fairly; useful to both practitioners and system builders.

Cons:
- Bounds are only rigorous for simulated data; applicability to pure MCMC on real data remains indirect.
- Tightness characterization limited; more guidance on AIS temperature schedules, number of steps, and cost-benefit trade-offs would help.
- Some presentation/notation issues in Section 3 and inconsistent terminology.
- Limited comparison to alternative diagnostics (e.g., R̂, ESS) and to Stein discrepancies beyond high-level discussion.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide practical guidance for choosing AIS schedules (geometric vs. moment averaging, adaptive spacing), number of intermediate distributions, and diagnostics for when the bound is stable.
- Report wall-clock cost and memory overhead for BREAD relative to baseline sampling.
- Can the approach be adapted to SMC/NUTS hybrids or to produce per-parameter diagnostics?
- Consider comparing the bound against standard MCMC diagnostics and Stein discrepancies on the same runs to contextualize its sensitivity and failure modes.
- Clarify whether averaging multiple reverse/forward chains (vs. reporting individual-chain gaps) can yield concentration bounds on the expected gap.

Minor comments:
- Define and use a single term (BDMC/BREAD). If “AutoBDMC” is used, define it once.
- Check the potential typo where p(θ,z|y) and p(θ,z,y) are conflated; also standardize y vs. D.
- Please make the WebPPL and Stan code easy to reproduce: indicate compatible versions, integration instructions, and consider releasing as an npm-installable WebPPL package and a versioned Stan repository with scripts.

Overall: A solid, well-executed contribution that offers a rigorous and useful metric for MCMC sample quality in probabilistic programming. I recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and reproducibility points.
