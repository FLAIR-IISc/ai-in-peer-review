This paper proposes using the gap between BDMC’s stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood as a generic performance measure for MCMC transition operators, with implementations in Stan and WebPPL, and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality. The core idea – leveraging BDMC for MCMC diagnostics – is appealing and addresses a critical need for reliable measures of inference quality in probabilistic programming. The work builds on recent advances in bidirectional Monte Carlo and provides a practical framework for evaluating and comparing different inference strategies.

**Strengths:** The method is conceptually simple and potentially high-impact. Its direct applicability to probabilistic programming systems like Stan and WebPPL is a significant advantage. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in evaluating MCMC performance and offer a tangible tool for addressing them. The identification of a bug in WebPPL via BREAD highlights the utility of the method for software testing and validation.  The presented protocol for using BREAD on real-world data, by simulating from fitted hyperparameters, is a practical workaround for the requirement of exact posterior samples.

**Weaknesses:** While the idea is sound, the technical contributions feel modest. The observation that the Jensen-Shannon divergence can be bounded using BDMC is interesting, but not demonstrably novel; the connection to existing work isn’t sufficiently explored. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work, most notably omitting Radford Neal’s seminal 1996 paper on Tempered Transitions, which directly relates to BDMC and the interpretation of its acceptance ratio. The choice of β<sub>t</sub> in the annealing schedule is under-discussed; Δβ significantly impacts the bounds and comparisons, and a more systematic treatment is needed.  The meaning of “AutoBDMC” is left ambiguous – it’s unclear whether it refers to automated convergence assessment *using* the bound gap, or the specific fixed-hyperparameter scheme from Section 3.2. 

**Clarity & Presentation:** Sections 3.1–3.2 suffer from a rushed feel, with a noticeable slip in writing quality leading to ambiguities. The definition of vector *v* (“all variables sampled in AIS before final stage”) is loose and confusing, given the two AIS sweeps. Inconsistent labeling ("rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back") detracts from clarity. Crucially,  \hat{p}(y) – the estimator inside the expectations in equations 5–7 and 9–11 – is not defined, making the derivations difficult to follow. Equation (6) appears incorrect as written, with identical KL divergence arguments; the intended terms are unclear. The experimental section lacks detail regarding the practical choice of β<sub>t</sub>; the code implies `step = 1/options.steps`, but this should be explicitly stated in the paper. Figure captions lack definitions for RAIS and its variants (u)c(R)AIS. Axes and tick labels in figures are too small, hindering readability.  Minor issues include a missing closing parenthesis in the D<sub>JS</sub> expression and a misspelling of Hoffman as "Homan" in the NUTS citation.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel *application* of existing techniques (BDMC and KL divergence bounding) rather than fundamentally new algorithms. While the combination is valuable, the core ideas are not groundbreaking. The lack of engagement with closely related work like Neal (1996) further weakens the perceived originality.

**Significance:**  The work’s potential significance lies in its practical impact on probabilistic programming. If the method is refined and widely adopted, it could become a standard tool for assessing the reliability of MCMC-based inference. However, its current form feels more like a proof-of-concept than a definitive solution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a practical tool for evaluating MCMC performance in probabilistic programming.
*   Identifies a bug in WebPPL, demonstrating the method’s utility for debugging.
*   Offers a potential path towards more automated and reliable inference systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Technical contributions are relatively modest.
*   Lacks thorough discussion of related work.
*   Suffers from clarity issues and ambiguities.
*   Experimental details are insufficient for reproducibility.
*   The choice of β schedule requires further investigation.




**Recommendation:** This paper could be acceptable with major revisions before the camera-ready stage. The authors *must* address the clarity and presentation issues, provide a more comprehensive discussion of related work (particularly Neal 1996), and elaborate on the experimental details, especially regarding the β schedule. I suggest an iterative scheme to decrease Δβ until the bounds are sufficiently tight, and experiments exploring this approach would strengthen the paper considerably.  Addressing these concerns would significantly enhance the paper’s quality and increase its potential impact.
