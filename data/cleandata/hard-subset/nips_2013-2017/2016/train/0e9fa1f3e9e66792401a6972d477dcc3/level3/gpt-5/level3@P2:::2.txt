Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al. 2015) from bounding log marginal likelihoods to assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC-based algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling on an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC). The core result shows that the expected gap between reverse-AIS and forward-AIS stochastic bounds on the log partition function ratio upper bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the target posterior p(·|y) and the distribution q of approximate samples produced by AIS. The paper also shows how one-sided KLs can be bounded in expectation when combined with an external upper or lower bound, and evaluates tightness on toy problems where exact Jeffreys divergences are computable.

Because reverse AIS requires an exact posterior sample, the method yields rigorous guarantees on simulated data. To bridge to real datasets, the authors propose a practical protocol: estimate hyperparameters on the real data, simulate data at those fitted hyperparameters, and then use reverse AIS initialized by an approximate posterior sample obtained via MCMC steps starting from the fitted instantiation. They validate that forward AIS behavior aligns between real and simulated data across five models and that the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the reverse initialization has negligible impact on reverse bounds.

The method—termed BREAD—is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and used to (i) validate sampler accuracy, (ii) guide design choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization representations), and (iii) debug a subtle implementation bug in WebPPL.

This work connects to Neal’s AIS, thermodynamic integration and Jarzynski’s nonequilibrium work relations, convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman), and recent measures of sample quality (Stein discrepancies; Gorham & Mackey). It also relates to concurrent work on subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), which is similar in spirit but uses an arbitrary reference distribution for the reverse chain; the present paper emphasizes guarantees tied specifically to exact posterior starts and provides a practical pathway via simulated data.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations connecting expected AIS bounds to symmetrized KL are correct and clearly extend BDMC’s analysis. The conditions and limitations (need for exact posterior sample for rigorous guarantees) are stated. Empirical validation covers toy cases with exact comparisons and several realistic models. The real-data protocol is well-motivated and stress-tested (sensitivity to the number of reverse-initialization MCMC steps and alignment between real and simulated data).
- Empirics: Multiple models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR, GMM) in Stan; representation study in both Stan and WebPPL; diagnostic case uncovering a bug. The figures suggest practical usefulness. One limitation is that tightness assessments beyond toy cases remain empirical and the computational overhead is not systematically quantified.
- Carefulness: The paper is honest about scope and limitations and cites concurrent related work.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and readable. Background on AIS/BDMC is appropriate, the main result is clearly stated, and the practical protocol is explicit. Minor suggestions:
- Emphasize early and repeatedly that bounds are in expectation (and what that implies for finite runs).
- Provide guidance on choosing annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and number of AIS chains vs. computational budget.
- Summarize wall-clock cost and variance behavior across models to aid practitioners.
- Surface at least one model-misspecification example in the main text (currently in the appendix).

Originality
The extension from BDMC’s marginal likelihood sandwich to bounding posterior sample quality (Jeffreys divergence) and the associated practical protocol for real data constitute a novel and timely contribution. The integration into two PPLs and the demonstration of how this informs modeling/algorithmic choices add to the paper’s originality and practical relevance. The connection and contrasts to concurrent subjective divergence work are appropriately discussed.

Significance
Assessing reliability of MCMC-based automatic inference is a pressing need, especially for probabilistic programming. Providing an actionable, general-purpose, and largely automated evaluation tool with theoretical grounding (on simulated data) is impactful. The case studies demonstrate that BREAD can guide representation choices and even uncover implementation bugs, suggesting real utility for both researchers and practitioners. The ideas are likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to SMC variants, schedule design, adaptive diagnostics).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical result: expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence from AIS forward/reverse gap.
- Practical, implementable, and demonstrated in two widely used PPLs.
- Convincing empirical validation across diverse models; protocol to relate simulated and real data.
- Actionable insights into model representation choices; demonstrated debugging utility.
- Careful positioning relative to prior and concurrent work.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees are expectation-based and require exact posterior samples; real-data protocol, while sensible, lacks formal guarantees.
- Applicability restricted to algorithms with AIS/SMC structure; not directly applicable to generic MCMC outputs without a compatible path and reverse initialization.
- Limited analysis of computational cost, variance, and schedule sensitivity; guidance for practitioners could be expanded.
- Tightness of the bound is empirically evaluated only on small toy problems; large-scale behavior remains heuristic.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide practical heuristics or adaptive methods for choosing temperatures/schedules and number of intermediate distributions to improve tightness and stability.
- Report wall-clock time and compute-efficiency trade-offs when using BREAD, including the number of chains and steps required for stable estimates.
- Discuss potential extensions to SMC and to cases where only biased but consistent estimators are available (e.g., pseudo-marginal methods).
- Clarify best practices for constructing the reverse-chain reference when exact samples are unavailable, and elaborate on differences from subjective divergence beyond the brief comparison.

Arguments for acceptance
- Important, timely problem with a rigorous and practical solution.
- Strong theoretical foundation and careful empirical validation.
- Clear benefits for the PPL community; demonstrated impact (guiding design, debugging).
- Well-written and likely to stimulate follow-up work and adoption.

Arguments against acceptance
- Expectation-bound nature and dependence on simulated data limit formal guarantees on real data.
- Scope limited to AIS/SMC-like methods; broader MCMC settings not directly covered.
- Practical guidance on computational cost and parameterization could be more comprehensive.

Recommendation: Accept

Confidence: High (I am familiar with AIS/BDMC, MCMC diagnostics, and PPLs).
