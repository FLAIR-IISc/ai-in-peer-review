- Paper adapts BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between an approximate and true posterior using stochastic upper/lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood and compares samplers by the tightness of this “sandwich” under equal compute.  
- Proposes a synthetic-data protocol: fit a representative hyperparameter η_real on real data, simulate data from it, and run forward/reverse AIS to produce bounds.  
- Implemented the method in WebPPL and Stan and presents small-scale experiments (including matrix factorization).  
- Theoretical derivation that the BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is sound and builds on AIS/BDMC theory.  
- Central protocol relies on strong, untested assumptions: a single η_real is representative (assuming prior runs converged), synthetic-data posterior mirrors real posterior geometry, and reverse AIS initialization yields an almost exact sample.  
- Those assumptions target the very convergence issues the diagnostic aims to detect, undermining validity if they fail, especially under misspecification or complex posteriors.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to simple examples, providing little evidence these failure modes won’t matter at scale.  
- Writing and exposition are generally clear and careful on AIS/BDMC, but protocol details (temperatures, transitions, compute-equality, aggregation across chains) need more precise specification for reproducibility.  
- The use of BDMC and the “tightest bounds” selection criterion is interesting but closely related to contemporaneous “subjective divergence” work and should be better positioned relative to it.  
- If robust, the method could be a useful, theoretically motivated diagnostic for AIS/SMC and PPL design choices, but current reliance on synthetic proxies and limited experiments reduces immediate impact.  
- Suggested experiments: stress-test η_real and synthetic-data assumptions via perturbations, deliberate misspecification, multimodal mixtures, and funnel geometries.  
- Suggested comparisons: evaluate against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, energy/BFMI, Geweke), Stein discrepancies, and report correlations with downstream posterior errors.  
- Suggested clarifications: show how the method handles multimodality and poor summaries and provide cases where BDMC succeeds while standard diagnostics fail.  
- Suggested fairness controls: define “equal compute” clearly (e.g., wall-clock time, gradient evaluations).  
- Pros: theoretically grounded diagnostic, practical PPL integration, clear derivations, and useful idea of using bound tightness as a selection metric.  
- Cons: key convergence-related assumptions are unverified and may fail in realistic scenarios; limited empirical validation on challenging posteriors and under misspecification; applicability mainly to AIS/SMC rather than general-purpose MCMC.
