

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for rigorously evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior distribution. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains in both forward and reverse directions on simulated data, they provide a theoretically grounded upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) a refined theoretical analysis demonstrating the upper bound property; (2) exact computation of both true divergence and upper bound on toy examples; (3) a protocol for applying the method to real-world data; and (4) integration into WebPPL and Stan for practical use.

The work builds upon Grosse et al.'s (2015) BDMC framework but applies it to a critical problem in probabilistic inference: measuring the reliability of MCMC samples. The authors demonstrate how BREAD can guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even uncover implementation bugs, as evidenced by their discovery of a WebPPL sampling error.

The paper excels in quality: the theoretical analysis is sound, supported by careful derivations, and the experimental validation is comprehensive across multiple models and probabilistic programming languages. Clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation of both theoretical and practical aspects, and sufficient detail for reproducibility. Originality is strong—the application of BDMC to posterior evaluation is novel, distinct from related work like Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), and properly contextualized in the literature. Significance is high, addressing a fundamental challenge in probabilistic modeling that affects both practitioners and researchers. The method provides a practical, rigorous tool for evaluating inference quality in black-box probabilistic programming systems.

The paper is well-written, technically complete, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The integration into widely used tools (WebPPL and Stan) significantly enhances its practical impact. No major weaknesses were identified; the protocol for real-world data evaluation is thoughtful and validated through extensive experiments. This work represents a substantial advancement in the evaluation of MCMC inference, with clear potential to improve both research and practice in probabilistic modeling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
