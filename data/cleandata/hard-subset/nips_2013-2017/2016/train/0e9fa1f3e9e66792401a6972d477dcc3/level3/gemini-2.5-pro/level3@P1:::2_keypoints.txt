- Problem: Diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality in black-box PPLs is difficult and lacks principled quantitative tools.
- Method: BREAD extends BDMC by using the AIS forward–reverse log-evidence gap to upper-bound the expected Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior.
- Theoretical limitation: Rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample for the reverse chain, so they hold only for simulated data where such samples are available.
- Practical protocol: For real data, infer hyperparameters, simulate a dataset from those hyperparameters, and run BREAD on the simulated data as a heuristic approximation.
- Implementation: BREAD is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, enabling immediate community use.
- Empirical validation: Experiments show consistency between real and simulated-data inference, robustness to MCMC steps, useful model-comparison insights, and the method uncovered a subtle WebPPL sampler bug.
- Strength — Importance: Tackles a critical, timely problem for trustworthy probabilistic inference in PPLs.
- Strength — Novelty: Clever repurposing of BDMC to obtain a quantitative divergence bound (Jeffreys divergence) for sample quality.
- Strength — Empirical rigor: Thorough experiments validate the protocol and demonstrate practical utility.
- Strength — Practical impact: Working implementations increase the method’s immediate usefulness and impact.
- Weakness — Simulated-data requirement: Theoretical guarantees do not extend to real data; the proposed real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Weakness — Sampler applicability: Directly applicable only to samplers expressible as importance sampling on extended spaces (e.g., AIS/SMC), not standard non-annealed MCMC runs.
- Weakness — Averaging unclear: Analysis bounds a single AIS chain; it’s unclear if averaged AIS estimates preserve the bounding property.
- Overall assessment: Paper is technically sound, clearly written, original, and highly significant — strengths outweigh inherent limitations.
