This paper proposes AutoBDMC, an instantiation of bidirectional Monte Carlo that runs AIS and reverse AIS on simulated data to produce stochastic lower/upper bounds on log marginal likelihood, and uses the gap as a single-number convergence diagnostic for MCMC-based posterior inference. Building on BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] and AIS [Neal, 2001], the authors show that the expected gap between forward and reverse bounds upper-bounds a symmetrized divergence between the true posterior and the sampler’s approximate distribution (the manuscript states Jeffreys/symmetrized KL), thereby yielding a principled, scalar measure of sample quality. The method is integrated in WebPPL and Stan and is further used for model selection and debugging. The paper positions itself against classical MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman) and recent sample-quality tests (e.g., Stein discrepancies [Gorham & Mackey, 2015]) and relates to concurrent BDMC-based work [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016].

Quality: The theoretical link from the bidirectional gap to a divergence bound is carefully argued and matches toy cases; experiments on five models suggest that synthetic-data analyses correlate with real-data behavior, and the tool surfaces real issues (e.g., a WebPPL bug). The main limitation is reliance on simulated data and an exact posterior draw; the proposed hyperparameter-fitting protocol is reasonable and empirically supported but remains heuristic.

Clarity: The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but Section 3 (especially the transition to 3.1) introduces variables and factorizations with insufficient setup. Please add intuition before Eqs. (6–11), define all symbols upon first use, and include a schematic of forward/reverse chains and conditionals. “AutoBDMC” is never crisply defined; state precisely what constitutes AutoBDMC (AIS+rAIS bounds on log p(y) plus the divergence-gap estimator) and use this term only after definition. By Section 3.2 you refer to challenges “for AutoBDMC” before defining it.

Originality: Extending BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding posterior-sample error is a valuable twist. If the divergence bound in §3.1 is novel (beyond [CTM, 2016]’s subjective divergence when initialized at the true posterior), please state this explicitly and clarify whether the bound is Jeffreys or Jensen–Shannon (the text says Jeffreys; ensure consistency).

Significance: A practical, quantitative convergence metric that doubles for model selection and debugging is useful for both probabilistic programming and MCMC practitioners.

Minor/editorial:
- Likely typo around Line 149: p(θ, z | y) vs p(θ, z, y).
- Unify notation for data (y vs D).
- Software: specify compatible WebPPL versions and installation/integration steps; consider releasing an npm-installable WebPPL package and provide Stan code similarly.

Pros:
- Clear, useful metric; solid theory and tooling.
- Compelling cross-platform experiments; practical debugging value.
- Good positioning relative to BDMC/AIS literature.

Cons:
- Dependence on simulated-data setting; heuristic posterior-initialization for real data.
- Clarity issues in §3; ambiguous terminology (AutoBDMC, divergence type).
- Software packaging could be stronger.
