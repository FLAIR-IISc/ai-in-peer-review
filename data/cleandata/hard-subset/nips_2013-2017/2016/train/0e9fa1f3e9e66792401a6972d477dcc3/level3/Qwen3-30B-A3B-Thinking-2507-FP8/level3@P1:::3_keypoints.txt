- Introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to bound Jeffreys divergence between MCMC samples and the true posterior.  
- Extends BDMC theoretically to provide an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence via the gap between forward and reverse AIS chains.  
- Presents four contributions: theoretical extension, exact computation for small examples, a protocol for real data with fitted hyperparameters, and implementations in WebPPL and Stan.  
- Demonstrates practical utility by integrating into two probabilistic programming systems and validating on several models.  
- Shows experiments where bounds guide model representation choices and provides debugging examples.  
- Practical, simple approach valuable for assessing black-box inference reliability in probabilistic programming.  
- Technical novelty is modest; core observation not clearly distinguished from prior work (notably Neal’s Tempered Transitions, 1996).  
- Fails to properly cite or discuss closely related work, weakening the novelty claim.  
- β schedule choice (Δβ) is under-discussed; no systematic method or exploration of its impact on bound quality.  
- The "AutoBDMC" automation component is ambiguously defined and not clearly explained.  
- Clarity issues: inconsistent notation (e.g., "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back") and missing key definitions (e.g., estimator ĥp(y) in several equations).  
- Technical error reported in Section 3.1 (equation (6)) and ambiguity in vector v definition given two AIS sweeps.  
- Experimental details insufficiently specified, particularly how the β schedule is chosen in practice.  
- Presentation problems: small figure axes, typos (e.g., "Homan" vs "Hoffman"), and other readability issues.  
- Overall assessment: useful practical tool but insufficiently novel, technically shallow, and poorly contextualized to merit NIPS acceptance as-is.  
- Recommendations: address clarity and notation, properly engage related work (Neal), and systematically study β schedule effects before resubmission.  
- Notes potential future work: more rigorous theoretical foundation and extensions to complex inference scenarios.
