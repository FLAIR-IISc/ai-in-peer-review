- Proposes BREAD, a novel method using bidirectional Monte Carlo to evaluate MCMC reliability by bounding Jeffreys divergence.  
- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo to measure accuracy of approximate posterior samples for algorithms seen as importance sampling on an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC).  
- Paper is well-written and provides clear theoretical foundations for the BREAD method.  
- The idea of bounding Jeffreys divergence as a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic is interesting and potentially valuable.  
- Major concern: method assumes a known representative hyperparameter value η_real from prior MCMC runs, which presumes convergence and undermines the diagnostic if that assumption fails.  
- Major concern: assumes synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the real-data posterior, an unjustified and critical assumption especially under model misspecification.  
- Major concern: assumes starting from η_real yields an almost exact sample quickly, an optimistic and unverified assumption that enables using synthetic-data posterior as proxy.  
- Empirical evidence is limited; simple examples (Section 5.1) are not convincing that issues won’t arise in complex settings.  
- Authors should provide more comprehensive simulations and comparisons to standard MCMC diagnostics to support claims.  
- Paper lacks detailed discussion of how BREAD handles multimodality or poor summary-statistic cases and should clarify this.  
- Paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations and potential pitfalls of the method.  
- Technically sound and clear overall, but some notation and technical details may be hard for non-experts.  
- Originality: novel contribution but builds on existing bidirectional Monte Carlo work; relationships to prior MCMC diagnostics should be elaborated.  
- Significance: has potential as a theoretically justified diagnostic tool, but current empirical support is insufficient to fully back the claims.
