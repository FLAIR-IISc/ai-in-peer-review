

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo" (NIPS 2016)

This paper proposes a method for assessing MCMC convergence by using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to generate stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The authors show that the gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution, providing a single scalar convergence metric. The method, which the authors refer to as "AutoBDMC" (though this term is never explicitly defined), is limited to analysis on synthetic data but demonstrates that synthetic data analyses can predict real-data behavior. The paper integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan, demonstrating its use for model selection and debugging.

The paper builds on the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework introduced by Grosse et al. (2015), which provides stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood. It extends this work to evaluate MCMC-based inference by showing how the gap between upper and lower bounds relates to the Jeffreys divergence. The paper is closely related to independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also uses BDMC for inference quality assessment, though the authors' approach differs in that it uses exact posterior samples for initialization rather than a reference distribution.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method with reasonable theoretical analysis. The derivation in Section 3.1 showing that the bound upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence is the core theoretical contribution, though it's not explicitly stated as novel. The experiments are well-designed and compelling, particularly the validation that synthetic data behavior matches real data behavior and the debugging example that uncovered a WebPPL bug. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the method's applicability only to simulated data). The paper is a complete work, not a position paper, with adequate theoretical and empirical support.

**Clarity:** The paper has several clarity issues that hinder understanding. The term "AutoBDMC" is never explicitly defined, despite being used extensively. The transition to Section 3.1 is particularly confusing, with variable definitions introduced without sufficient motivation. The paper refers to "challenges for AutoBDMC" in Section 3.2 before the term is properly defined. There are minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., p(θ, z | y) vs p(θ, z, y) on Line 149, inconsistent use of y vs D for data). The supplemental code lacks documentation about compatible WebPPL versions and integration instructions, and would benefit from being released as an npm-installable package.

**Originality:** The paper's key contribution—showing that the gap between BDMC bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence—is novel and significant. However, this novelty is not explicitly stated, which weakens the paper's contribution claim. The authors' approach differs from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), but the relationship between the methods isn't clearly articulated. While the method extends existing BDMC work, the specific theoretical connection to Jeffreys divergence is a clear advance.

**Significance:** The method addresses a real and important problem in probabilistic inference: how to reliably assess the quality of MCMC samples, particularly in black-box inference systems. The ability to use this method to guide model selection (as demonstrated with the matrix factorization example) and debug inference implementations (as demonstrated with the WebPPL bug) makes it practically valuable. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes it immediately applicable to real-world probabilistic programming. The paper's significance is further enhanced by demonstrating that synthetic data analysis can reliably predict real data behavior, which is crucial for practical application.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-motivated, presents a useful method with compelling experiments, and demonstrates additional applications for model selection and debugging. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental validation is thorough.

**Weaknesses:** The lack of explicit definition for "AutoBDMC" and the unclear presentation of Section 3.1 are significant issues that need addressing. The paper doesn't clearly state the novelty of the divergence bound, which is a central contribution. The supplemental code requires better documentation.

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference by providing a rigorous method for assessing MCMC convergence. While the clarity issues and lack of explicit novelty statement are problematic, they are fixable in a revised version. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and has clear practical applications. The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in MCMC inference that impacts both practitioners and researchers.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental problem in MCMC inference with a rigorous solution
- Theoretical contribution (bound on Jeffreys divergence) is novel and significant
- Demonstrates practical utility through model selection and debugging examples
- Integrates with widely used probabilistic programming frameworks
- Experiments provide strong validation of the method

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Key term "AutoBDMC" is never defined
- Section 3.1 is difficult to follow due to poor motivation of definitions
- Novelty of the divergence bound is not explicitly stated
- Minor but noticeable notational inconsistencies
- Supplemental code lacks documentation for practical use

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The clarity issues are significant but fixable, and the method's practical utility and theoretical soundness make it deserving of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and definition issues.
