- Proposes AutoBDMC: uses Bidirectional Monte Carlo (AIS + reverse AIS) to produce stochastic upper/lower bounds on log marginal likelihood for MCMC convergence assessment.
- Shows the gap between these bounds upper-bounds a divergence between true and approximate posteriors (review cites Jensen–Shannon / Jeffreys), yielding a single scalar convergence metric.
- Method limited to settings with exact posterior samples (synthetic-data analyses), but authors show synthetic analyses predict real-data behavior when using fitted hyperparameters.
- Demonstrated practical use cases: model selection and debugging of inference implementations.
- Builds on BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015) and connects to tempering/thermodynamic integration literature (Neal, Jarzynski).
- Distinguishes itself from prior MCMC diagnostics by providing quantifiable bounds on divergence instead of heuristic checks; related concurrent work differs in reverse-chain reference distribution.
- Strength: novel application of BDMC to directly bound posterior divergence; derived relationship between bound gap and divergence is significant.
- Strength: appears theoretically rigorous with clear derivations and justification.
- Strength: high practical relevance for MCMC practitioners and probabilistic programming systems.
- Strength: experiments convincingly validate that synthetic analyses predict real-data behavior; integration uncovered a real bug.
- Strength: implementation work—integrated into WebPPL and Stan—demonstrates impact.
- Weakness: Section 3 (especially transition into 3.1) is hard to follow with new variables and equations introduced without sufficient context or motivation.
- Weakness: “AutoBDMC” is not explicitly defined early; references to challenges “for AutoBDMC” appear before the method is clearly described.
- Weakness: minor technical issues—possible typo (p(theta, z | y) vs p(theta, z, y)) and inconsistent data notation (y vs D).
- Weakness: supplemental software lacks stated compatible WebPPL versions, clear integration instructions, and should be released as an npm-installable package; Stan code availability could be improved.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound method with compelling experiments and practical integrations that demonstrate utility.
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity problems in Section 3, missing precise definition of AutoBDMC, and insufficient software release details hinder usability and readability.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper likely to make significant impact; recommend acceptance pending revisions to clarify Section 3, formally define AutoBDMC, and improve software release/documentation.
