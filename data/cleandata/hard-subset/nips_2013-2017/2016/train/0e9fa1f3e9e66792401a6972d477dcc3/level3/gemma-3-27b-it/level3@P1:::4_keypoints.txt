- Introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), extending BDMC to evaluate MCMC posterior inference quality.
- Uses forward and reverse AIS to produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood.
- The gap between bounds yields an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate samples.
- Provides a principled, quantitative convergence/accuracy measure for MCMC, especially useful in black-box inference systems.
- Directly applicable to AIS and SMC, argued to give insights for broader MCMC methods.
- Implemented BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, enabling practical use and analysis of modeling choices.
- Demonstrates method utility via experiments, including model representation effects and a matrix factorization case study.
- Validates consistency between simulated-data results and real-data analyses; used to discover a WebPPL bug.
- Builds on BDMC [GGA15] and situates contributions relative to convergence diagnostics and automatic MCMC configuration literature.
- Acknowledges independent similar work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16].
- Main limitation: rigorous bounds require simulated data; authors propose a protocol for applying to real datasets via simulation/hyperparameter estimation.
- Method is computationally intensive, which is an acknowledged tradeoff for principled guarantees.
- Strength: clear motivation, technical derivation of the Jeffreys divergence estimator, and strong experimental validation.
- Weakness: Section 3 (and transition into 3.1) is hard to follow with insufficient context and motivation for variables/equations.
- Weakness: novelty of the divergence bound in Section 3.1 is not explicitly highlighted enough.
- Weakness: term “AutoBDMC” is used without a clear definition or early explanation.
- Minor issues: inconsistent notation (e.g., 'y' vs 'D'), a potential typo around line 149, and awkward repetition before defining terms.
- Reproducibility concern: supplemental WebPPL and Stan materials need clearer versioning, integration instructions, and ideally package-based distribution.
- Acceptance arguments: novel principled approach, quantitative posterior discrepancy measure, practical implementations, experimental usefulness, and solid writing.
- Rejection arguments: unclear presentation in key derivation section, under-emphasized novelty, undefined terminology, minor errors, and insufficient supplemental clarity.
- Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on improving clarity in Section 3 and enhancing supplemental materials for reproducibility.
