- Paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) upper and lower stochastic bounds on log marginal likelihood to evaluate MCMC reliability and compare sampler efficiency.  
- Key idea: choose MCMC schemes that yield the tightest BMC bounds for equal computational effort.  
- Method runs annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains forward (prior→posterior) and backward (posterior→prior) on simulated data to bound the symmetrized KL between true posterior and approximate samples.  
- Applicable to algorithms that can be cast as importance sampling on an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC).  
- Major challenge: method requires an exact sample from the target to produce a valid stochastic upper bound.  
- Authors’ mitigation: run diagnostics on synthetic-data posteriors instead of the real-data posterior, using synthetic data as a proxy.  
- Critical assumption 1: a known representative hyperparameter value η_real exists from prior MCMC runs and those runs already converged.  
- Critical assumption 2: the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the real-data posterior, which may fail under misspecification.  
- Critical assumption 3: starting from η_real allows MCMC to quickly produce an almost exact sample — optimistic and unverified.  
- These assumptions directly relate to the original convergence problem, so if they fail the diagnostic’s validity is undermined.  
- Empirical evidence is limited; simple examples (Section 5.1) are not convincing for complex settings.  
- Paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and well-organized, though some technical details may be hard for non-experts.  
- Original idea is novel and significant but builds on existing BMC/AIS work and needs stronger practical validation.  
- Recommended revisions: more thorough discussion of limitations/pitfalls, additional empirical comparisons to standard diagnostics, and simulations on harder problems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically justified diagnostic, solid technical presentation, potential for significant impact if validated.  
- Arguments against acceptance: strong/unverified assumptions, limited empirical support, and inadequate discussion of limitations.
