- Paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC posterior inference using forward AIS and reverse AIS on synthetic datasets to sandwich the log-marginal likelihood.
- The expected gap between stochastic lower and upper bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and AIS approximate-sample distribution, yielding a scalar convergence metric.
- Method instantiated in WebPPL and Stan, with a protocol to relate synthetic-data analyses to real data by simulating from fitted hyperparameters.
- Empirically, the bound tracks true divergence on toy problems, synthetic-data conclusions predict real-data behavior across several models, and the tool aids model representation decisions and debugging.
- Builds on BDMC (Grosse et al. 2015), reverse annealing (Burda et al. 2015), AIS (Neal 2001), SMC, and non-equilibrium estimators; complements existing MCMC diagnostics.
- Clear, well-motivated goal: a quantitative, model-agnostic convergence metric for black-box MCMC.
- Solid theoretical contribution: expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence via the AIS/rAIS gap; validated tightness on controlled examples.
- Practical impact: integrated tools in WebPPL and Stan; useful for model selection and debugging (e.g., detected a WebPPL bug).
- Thoughtful protocol to bridge simulated and real data, with empirical support that fitted-hyperparameter simulations mirror real-data behavior.
- Readable overall with useful experimental breadth across models/platforms.
- Scope limitation: rigorous guarantees require exact posterior samples, so formal results only hold for synthetic data drawn from the joint; real-data use is heuristic.
- Novelty clarity needed: explicitly state if the Section 3.1 divergence bound is new and contrast with Grosse et al. (2015) and Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016).
- Terminology issue: “AutoBDMC” is referenced but never defined; unify terms (AutoBDMC vs. BREAD) and define clearly.
- Exposition in Section 3 is hard to follow; introduce a high-level proof sketch, define all variables/conditionals before equations, and clarify expectation vs. averaging across chains.
- Clarify relationship to Jensen–Shannon divergence if practitioners prefer JS; provide proof or citation if claiming a JS bound.
- Real-data protocol: discuss potential failure modes (e.g., strong prior–likelihood tension, mismatched hyperparameters) and diagnostics to detect them.
- Experimental details missing for reproducibility: report AIS temperature schedules, transition kernels/tuning, repeats, variance estimates, and whether adaptation was used.
- Minor issues: possible typo p(theta,z|y) vs. p(theta,z,y); inconsistent data notation (y vs. D); state explicitly that bounds are in expectation and interpret single-chain plots accordingly.
- Reproducibility/code requests: provide WebPPL compatible versions, install instructions, npm package, full Stan code/data scripts, pinned Stan/CmdStan versions, compiler flags, seeds, and instructions to reproduce figures.
- Technical soundness: derivation appears correct and linked to AIS on extended space; empirical study is careful.
- Clarity: generally readable but Section 3 needs cleanup; define AutoBDMC and standardize notation.
- Originality: a novel and useful reframing of BDMC giving an expected Jeffreys divergence bound, but delineate differences from concurrent subjective-divergence work.
- Significance: high practical significance as a scalar, implementation-agnostic convergence metric likely useful for practitioners and tooling.
- Pros for acceptance: useful method with theory, cross-platform implementations, convincing experiments, and addresses a key MCMC diagnostic problem.
- Cons against acceptance: guarantees limited to synthetic data, exposition gaps and undefined terminology, and the main bound’s novelty not emphasized relative to prior work.
- Overall recommendation: lean accept, contingent on clarifying Section 3, explicitly positioning the theoretical contribution, defining AutoBDMC, and making minor fixes.
