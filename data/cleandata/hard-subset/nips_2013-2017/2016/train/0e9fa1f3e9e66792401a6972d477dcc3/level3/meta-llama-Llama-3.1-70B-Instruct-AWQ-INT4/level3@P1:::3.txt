This paper proposes a novel approach to measuring the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the recently introduced bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, they upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper makes several key contributions. Firstly, it provides a simple and easy-to-implement method for evaluating the performance of MCMC transition operators. Secondly, it presents a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The method is applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as AIS or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The authors also integrate their method into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets.

The paper's strengths lie in its simplicity, ease of implementation, and potential high impact for probabilistic programming systems. However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the technical contributions, particularly the Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation. Additionally, the paper lacks discussion of related work, including Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which closely relates to bidirectional Monte Carlo and its acceptance ratio interpretation.

One of the main concerns is the choice of β schedule, which affects the bounds and comparisons. The authors under-discuss this choice and do not provide a systematic treatment of the issue. Furthermore, the meaning and mechanism of "auto" in AutoBDMC are not clearly defined, and the experiments do not explore this aspect thoroughly.

The paper also suffers from clarity problems, particularly in sections 3.1-3.2, where the writing quality slips, causing several ambiguities. The notation is also ambiguous, particularly the definition of vector v, which is loose and confusing given two AIS sweeps. Additionally, the use of "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back" is inconsistent and unclear.

In terms of originality, the paper's technical contributions are modest, and the Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation is not clearly novel. However, the paper's strength lies in its simplicity and ease of implementation, making it a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic programming.

To improve the paper, the authors should address the clarity problems, provide a more systematic treatment of the β schedule issue, and explore the iterative scheme to decrease Δβ until bounds are sufficiently tight. Additionally, they should define the estimator inside expectations in equations 5-7 and 9-11, correct the equation error in equation 6, and clarify the meaning and mechanism of "auto" in AutoBDMC.

In conclusion, while the paper has some weaknesses, its simplicity and ease of implementation make it a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic programming. With some revisions to address the clarity problems and provide a more systematic treatment of the β schedule issue, the paper could be acceptable for publication.
