This paper proposes using the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds from Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) as a generic performance measure for MCMC-based inference. Building on the BDMC framework, which uses forward and reverse Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains, the authors show that this gap provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the approximate and true posteriors. The main contributions include this protocol for evaluating inference quality, along with implementations in the Stan and WebPPL probabilistic programming languages.

The primary strength of this work lies in its simplicity and potential for high impact. Providing a quantitative, theoretically-grounded measure of sampler quality is a significant challenge in probabilistic modeling. By integrating this method into popular frameworks like Stan, the authors offer a practical tool that could be widely adopted by both researchers and practitioners to diagnose inference and guide algorithm design. The protocol for applying this to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters is a pragmatic solution to a key limitation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its contribution. The technical novelty is modest, as the approach is a direct application of existing BDMC methods, and the observation linking the bound gap to the Jensen-Shannon divergence is not clearly established as a novel contribution. A more significant issue is the omission of highly relevant prior work, notably Radford Neal's (1996) paper on Tempered Transitions, which has strong conceptual links to the AIS annealing process used here.

The manuscript also suffers from significant clarity issues. Sections 3.1-3.2 are rushed and ambiguous. Key terms like "AutoBDMC" are not clearly defined, the notation for vector `v` is imprecise, and the crucial estimator `\hat{p}(y)` is never explicitly defined. Furthermore, equation (6) appears incorrect as written, hindering the reader's ability to follow the derivation. The experimental details are sparse, particularly concerning the choice of the annealing schedule (β_t), which critically affects the bounds and any comparisons made. Figures are difficult to interpret due to small labels and undefined acronyms.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable and useful idea, but the current manuscript is not ready for publication. If the authors can thoroughly revise the paper to address the clarity issues, properly situate their work with respect to related literature, and provide more rigorous experimental details, it could become an acceptable contribution.
