

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The authors build upon the BDMC framework proposed by Grosse et al. (2015), which uses annealed importance sampling (AIS) to sandwich the log marginal likelihood between stochastic upper and lower bounds. The key insight is that the gap between the forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the true posterior and the approximate distribution of samples. This provides a single scalar metric to assess MCMC convergence without requiring exact posterior samples, which is particularly valuable for black-box inference systems.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) deriving the theoretical connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence bounding; (2) demonstrating the accuracy of the upper bound on toy distributions; (3) proposing a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter fitting and simulated data generation; and (4) integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validating it on multiple models. The authors show how BREAD can guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even uncover implementation bugs in probabilistic programming languages.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic inference: the lack of rigorous, quantitative measures for MCMC convergence. While convergence diagnostics like Gelman-Rubin exist, they cannot confirm convergence, whereas BREAD provides rigorous bounds in the simulated data setting. The paper relates well to previous work on convergence diagnostics (e.g., Brooks and Gelman, 1998) and related techniques like the subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016), clearly distinguishing their contribution by providing mathematical guarantees for the Jeffreys divergence bound.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is technically sound, with careful derivation showing that the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence. The experimental validation is comprehensive, including both toy examples where exact Jeffreys divergence can be computed and real-world models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.) where the authors demonstrate consistency between simulated and real data. The debugging example in WebPPL is particularly compelling, showing how BREAD can serve as a tool for implementation validation.

The clarity of the paper could be improved. The transition into Section 3.1 is abrupt, with variable definitions introduced without sufficient motivation. The term "AutoBDMC" appears without clear definition (it seems to refer to the automated application of BDMC for MCMC analysis), and the paper refers to "challenges for AutoBDMC" before the term is properly introduced. There are also minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., p(θ, z | y) vs p(θ, z, y) on line 149) and inconsistent use of data notation (y vs D). The supplemental code should include specific WebPPL version compatibility and integration instructions, and ideally be made available as an npm package.

The originality of the work is strong. While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al., 2015), the connection to bounding the Jeffreys divergence for posterior approximation is novel. The authors should explicitly state this novelty in Section 3.1, as it's a central contribution. The approach is distinct from Gorham and Mackey's (2015) method for directly estimating sample quality, which makes strong convexity assumptions, whereas BREAD makes no assumptions about the posterior structure.

The significance of the work is high. Probabilistic programming languages like Stan and WebPPL are increasingly used by non-experts who lack the expertise to assess inference reliability. BREAD provides a rigorous, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which could significantly increase confidence in probabilistic programming systems. The method's applications to model selection (comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and debugging demonstrate its practical utility beyond just convergence measurement.

Strengths: 
- Novel theoretical connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence bounding
- Rigorous validation on toy examples and comprehensive experiments on real models
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan
- Demonstrated utility for model selection and debugging
- Clear motivation addressing a significant challenge in MCMC inference

Weaknesses:
- Ambiguous terminology ("AutoBDMC" not defined)
- Clarity issues in Section 3.1, particularly the transition into the theoretical analysis
- Notational inconsistencies and minor typos
- Supplemental code requires better documentation and packaging

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a critical need in probabilistic inference. While the paper would benefit from improved clarity in the theoretical section and better documentation of the code, the core ideas are novel, technically sound, and of significant practical importance. The method provides a valuable tool for both researchers developing inference algorithms and practitioners using probabilistic programming languages. I recommend acceptance.
