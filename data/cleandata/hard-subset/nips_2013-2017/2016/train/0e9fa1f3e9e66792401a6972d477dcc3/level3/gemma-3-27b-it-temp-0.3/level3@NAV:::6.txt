## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper explores the application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), originally proposed for marginal likelihood estimation, to evaluate the quality of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors extend the existing BDMC framework to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples obtained via algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). They implement this method within the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its use on several models, including an analysis of model representation choices. 

The work builds directly on GGA15 and Neal’s work on AIS, and correctly positions itself within the broader context of MCMC convergence diagnostics and model evaluation. The authors acknowledge related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (CTM16) which also leverages BDMC for posterior evaluation, highlighting the current interest in this approach. 

The primary strength of the paper lies in its thorough implementation and validation. Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a significant undertaking, and the experiments demonstrate its functionality. The validation experiments, particularly those comparing results on simulated and real data, are well-designed and provide confidence in the method’s practical utility. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL through the use of BREAD is a compelling example of its potential as a testing tool.

However, the paper’s main weakness is a lack of substantial novelty. The core contribution is an application of an existing technique (BDMC) to a related, but not fundamentally new, problem (evaluating MCMC samples). While the extension to KL divergence bounding is a logical step, it doesn’t represent a breakthrough in methodology. The analysis of model representations, while interesting, doesn’t offer particularly surprising insights. The paper largely describes *how* to implement known techniques rather than presenting new ones. The reliance on simulated data, even with the validation protocol, limits the generalizability of the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Demonstrates a functional implementation of a potentially useful diagnostic tool within popular probabilistic programming languages. Provides a concrete example of how such a tool can be used to inform modeling choices and even identify implementation errors.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lacks significant originality. Primarily an engineering effort rather than a novel research contribution. The scope feels more appropriate for a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming tools than a flagship conference like NIPS.



**Overall:** I recommend **rejection**. While the work is technically sound and well-executed, it doesn’t meet the originality and significance criteria for NIPS. The authors should consider submitting to a more specialized venue, such as an MCMC workshop or a conference focused on probabilistic programming.
