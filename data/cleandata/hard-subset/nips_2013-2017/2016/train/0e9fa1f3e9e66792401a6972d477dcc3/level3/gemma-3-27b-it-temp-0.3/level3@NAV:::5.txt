## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior and the true posterior. The core idea – choosing MCMC schemes that yield tighter BMC bounds for equal computational effort – is a valuable pursuit, as robust MCMC diagnostics remain a significant challenge. The work builds on the recent BMC framework [GGA15] and attempts to extend it to provide a theoretically grounded diagnostic tool, applicable particularly to AIS and SMC samplers. 

The authors correctly identify the key hurdle: BMC requires an exact sample from the target distribution for a valid stochastic upper bound. Their workaround, running diagnostics on posteriors for *synthetic* data, is a pragmatic approach given this limitation. Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a commendable engineering effort. 

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. The reliance on a pre-determined hyperparameter value (η_real) obtained from prior MCMC runs is a major concern. This assumes those initial runs *already converged* – directly undermining the diagnostic’s purpose. Furthermore, assuming a single summary statistic is sufficient to represent the prior is a strong and potentially invalid assumption.  The central premise that synthetic data posterior convergence properties mirror those of the original data posterior is also unjustified, especially in the presence of model misspecification, which is a common scenario. The assumption that starting from η_real allows MCMC to quickly reach an almost exact sample is optimistic and lacks empirical support. 

The empirical validation (Section 5.1) is limited. While the authors demonstrate consistency between real and simulated data for the models tested, the simple examples are insufficient to convince that the identified issues won’t manifest in more complex, high-dimensional settings. The bug found in WebPPL, while interesting, is more a demonstration of BREAD’s potential for code verification than a validation of its diagnostic capabilities.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The attempt to develop a theoretically justified MCMC diagnostic is a worthwhile goal. The integration into probabilistic programming languages is a positive contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The core assumptions are problematic and directly relate to the convergence problem the method aims to solve. The empirical evidence is weak. The method's applicability is limited to AIS and SMC.

**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection**. While the idea is intriguing, the fundamental assumptions and limited validation raise serious doubts about the reliability and generalizability of BREAD. The authors should address the concerns regarding the reliance on prior MCMC runs, the synthetic data assumption, and provide more extensive simulations comparing BREAD to standard convergence diagnostics, including cases with multimodality and model misspecification. A clearer explanation of how BREAD handles these challenging scenarios is crucial.
