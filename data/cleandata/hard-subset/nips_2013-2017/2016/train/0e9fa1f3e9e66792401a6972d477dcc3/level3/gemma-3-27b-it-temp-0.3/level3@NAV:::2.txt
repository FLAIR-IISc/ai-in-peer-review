## Review of “Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo”

This paper presents a compelling extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference. The core idea – leveraging BDMC to upper bound the KL divergence between the true posterior and the approximate distribution generated by MCMC – addresses a critical and notoriously difficult problem in Bayesian statistics. The work builds upon [GGA15] and elegantly adapts their framework to this new context, offering a principled way to assess the reliability of inference, particularly within “black box” probabilistic programming systems. 

The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on MCMC convergence diagnostics (e.g., [CC96, GR92]) and marginal likelihood estimation (e.g., [Nea01]). They clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods, notably highlighting the ability to provide rigorous bounds (in the simulated data setting) rather than merely diagnostic heuristics. The related work section is thorough, acknowledging concurrent work by [CTM16] and detailing the nuances of their respective approaches. 

A significant strength of the paper lies in its practical integration into two prominent probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. This demonstrates the method’s usability and potential impact. The validation experiments, while limited in scope, provide encouraging evidence that the method can accurately assess inference quality and even uncover implementation bugs, as demonstrated with WebPPL. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, using simulated data generated from fitted hyperparameters, is a clever and necessary adaptation.

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost associated with BREAD. While the authors acknowledge the need for significant computation, a more detailed analysis of the scaling behavior with respect to model complexity and data size would be valuable. Furthermore, the assumption that the fitted hyperparameters accurately represent the true posterior, even with the MCMC refinement step, warrants further scrutiny. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a timely and important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   Provides a rigorous method for evaluating MCMC inference, offering a significant improvement over existing heuristics.
*   Demonstrates practical applicability through integration with WebPPL and Stan.
*   Well-written and clearly presented, with a thorough review of related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost is a potential limitation that requires further investigation.
*   The reliance on fitted hyperparameters for real-world data analysis introduces a potential source of error.



**Overall:** This is a well-executed and significant contribution to the field. The strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** to the conference.
