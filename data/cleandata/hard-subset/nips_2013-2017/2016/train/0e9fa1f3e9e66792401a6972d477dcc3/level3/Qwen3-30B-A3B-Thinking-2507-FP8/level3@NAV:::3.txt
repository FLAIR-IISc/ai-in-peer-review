

This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure MCMC inference quality by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend BDMC to create a performance measure for MCMC transition operators, implement it in WebPPL and Stan, and propose a protocol for evaluating inference quality on real data. While the core idea builds on Grosse et al. (2015), the paper's application to MCMC inference quality is interesting.

The primary strength lies in the simplicity and potential high-impact for probabilistic programming systems. The method is straightforward to implement and could provide practitioners with a valuable tool for validating inference quality. However, the technical contributions appear modest, and the Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation isn't clearly novel. The paper fails to cite Radford Neal's seminal 1996 work on Tempered Transitions, which closely relates to the BDMC approach and its acceptance ratio interpretation.

Several critical issues undermine the paper's quality. The β schedule choice (Δβ) is under-discussed despite its significant impact on bounds and comparisons. The meaning of "auto" in AutoBDMC remains ambiguous throughout. Sections 3.1-3.2 are rushed with notation ambiguities (e.g., vector v definition, inconsistent "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back" labels), and equation (6) contains an error where KL arguments appear identical. The estimator \hat{p}(y) is never defined, making derivations hard to follow. Experiments lack detail about β_t selection, and figures have small axes and undefined acronyms like RAIS.

While the paper's protocol for evaluating inference quality on real-world data is valuable, the presentation issues and technical omissions prevent it from being a strong contribution. The authors should systematically address the β schedule, clarify AutoBDMC, fix the equation errors, and properly contextualize their work against Neal's 1996 paper. With these revisions, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to probabilistic programming evaluation.

The paper shows promise but currently lacks sufficient rigor and clarity to meet NIPS standards. I recommend rejection unless major revisions address these concerns.
