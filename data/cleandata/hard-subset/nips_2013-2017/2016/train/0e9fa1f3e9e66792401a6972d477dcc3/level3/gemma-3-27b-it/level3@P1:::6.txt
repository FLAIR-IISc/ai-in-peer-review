This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference algorithms, specifically aiming to bound the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors leverage annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) as mechanisms for applying BDMC, building upon previous work in importance sampling and marginal likelihood estimation [Nea01, MDJ06].  A key contribution lies in refining the analysis of [GGA15] to derive an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence, validated on toy distributions. They integrate this method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+p] and apply it to analyze the impact of model representation on inference effectiveness.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the background necessary to understand the proposed method. It appropriately contextualizes BDMC within the broader landscape of MCMC convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and automated MCMC parameter tuning [RR01].  The authors acknowledge related work on estimating sample quality [GM15] and alternative approaches using BDMC [CTM16], demonstrating awareness of the current research landscape. The extensions to WebPPL and Stan are a practical and valuable addition, increasing the accessibility of this evaluation technique.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of the BREAD method, building logically from background material.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan makes the technique readily available to practitioners.
*   **Rigorous Approach:** The attempt to provide rigorous bounds on the Jeffreys divergence is commendable, offering a stronger guarantee than typical MCMC convergence diagnostics.
*   **Useful Application:** Applying BREAD to analyze model representation choices in probabilistic programming is a compelling example of its utility.
*   **Thorough Validation:** The validation experiments, including consistency checks between real and simulated data, add confidence in the method's reliability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core innovation—applying BDMC to assess posterior accuracy—is an extension of existing ideas, rather than a fundamentally new methodological contribution. While the analysis and implementation are valuable, the paper doesn’t present a breakthrough in theoretical understanding.
*   **Applicability Restrictions:** The method's direct applicability is limited to algorithms amenable to importance sampling (AIS, SMC), restricting its broader use. While the authors acknowledge the implications for MCMC more generally, the core technique remains tied to these specific inference methods.
*   **Simulated Data Dependence:** Even with the protocol for real-world data, the reliance on simulated data introduces potential biases if the simulation accurately represents the real-world data.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains, especially in the reverse direction, can be computationally expensive, potentially limiting the practicality of BREAD for complex models.
*   **Comparison to Existing Diagnostics:** A more detailed comparison of BREAD to existing MCMC diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size) would strengthen the argument for its adoption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a practical and potentially valuable tool for evaluating the quality of approximate inference, particularly in the context of probabilistic programming.
*   The integration with WebPPL and Stan lowers the barrier to entry for using this technique.
*   Demonstrates a rigorous approach to bounding the error in approximate inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks significant novelty, primarily extending existing techniques rather than presenting groundbreaking new ideas.
*   The limited applicability of the method to specific inference algorithms might restrict its broader impact.
*   Given the incremental nature of the contribution, it might be more suitable for a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming rather than a flagship conference like NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid, well-written paper that presents a practical extension of existing methods for evaluating MCMC inference. While the work is technically sound and the implementation is valuable, the limited novelty and applicability raise concerns about its suitability for NIPS. I recommend **weak reject**.  The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of its limitations and a more detailed comparison to existing diagnostic tools. It might be more appropriately positioned for a more specialized workshop.
