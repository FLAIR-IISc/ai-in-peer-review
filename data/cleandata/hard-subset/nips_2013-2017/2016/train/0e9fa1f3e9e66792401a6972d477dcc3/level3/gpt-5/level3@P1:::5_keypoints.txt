The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound the expected Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and AIS/SMC approximate sample distributions.

Core method: run forward AIS (prior→posterior) and reverse AIS (posterior→prior) on simulated data; the expected gap between log-weight bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence.

Integrates the “BREAD” procedure into WebPPL and Stan and validates tightness on toy problems with computable ground-truth Jeffreys divergence.

Proposes a real-data protocol: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate synthetic data at those estimates, and evaluate forward/reverse AIS on the synthetic data.

Demonstrates practical utility in case studies, including guiding representation choices in matrix factorization and detecting a WebPPL sampling bug.

Builds on prior work: BDMC, AIS, thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski identities, and relates to the subjective divergence estimator by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka.

Offers a different, likelihood-based pathwise diagnostic complementary to R̂, ESS, and recent Stein discrepancy methods.

Theoretical strength: a careful derivation showing the expected forward/reverse AIS gap upper-bounds DJ(p‖q), connected to extended-state AIS.

Engineering strength: pragmatic implementation in two PPLs with exemplar case studies and a thoughtful real-data protocol.

Main limitation: the rigorous guarantee only holds for simulated datasets with exact posterior samples; real-data protocol replaces exact samples with short MCMC runs, breaking the guarantee.

Key assumptions of the real-data protocol are weakly justified: representativeness of fitted hyperparameters, similarity of synthetic and real posterior geometry, and sufficiency of few MCMC steps for near-exact samples.

Method is limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers or MCMC embedded within them and does not directly evaluate standalone MCMC chains like plain HMC/NUTS.

Bound is in expectation; paper gives limited finite-sample guidance on how many forward/reverse chains or temperatures are needed for stable, practical estimates.

Empirical validation is narrow: five relatively simple models and visual comparisons (vertical shifts of AIS curves) that do not directly validate Jeffreys-control under misspecification or multimodality.

Clarity is good overall, but the paper should better distinguish formal guarantees from heuristics and clarify finite-sample interpretation, numerical stability, and aggregation for heavy-tailed weights.

Originality is moderate: extends BDMC to Jeffreys divergence and PPL tooling, but closely related prior work exists.

Significance: promising as a benchmarking and debugging tool for PPL inference developers, but limited as a general-purpose MCMC diagnostic on real data.

Suggested improvements: compare to standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS, Geweke) and Stein discrepancies on challenging targets (Neal’s funnel, high-dim mixtures, heavy tails, misspecification).

Suggested improvements: calibrate finite-sample behavior (concentration of empirical gap, confidence intervals) and report sensitivity to number of chains and temperatures.

Suggested improvements: stress-test the real-data protocol by varying MCMC initialization steps, hyperparameter estimates, and exploring known multimodal cases.

Suggested improvements: discuss applicability of embedding HMC/NUTS kernels within AIS and when such embedding reflects standalone HMC behavior.

Arguments for acceptance: clear theoretical link from BDMC to an interpretable posterior-sample quality measure, useful PPL integrations, and concrete developer-facing use cases.

Arguments against acceptance: guarantees depend on conditions rarely met for real data, the heuristic workaround is weakly validated, and empirical breadth and finite-sample guidance are insufficient.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject—interesting and useful for PPLs but needs stronger empirical validation on challenging problems and clearer finite-sample guidance for NIPS publication.
