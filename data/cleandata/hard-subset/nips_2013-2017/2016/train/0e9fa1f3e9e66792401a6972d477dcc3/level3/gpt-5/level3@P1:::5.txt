This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC-style algorithms. The core idea is to run AIS forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) on simulated data; the forward AIS log-weight is a stochastic lower bound on log ZT/Z1 and reverse AIS yields a stochastic upper bound via a lower bound on the reverse ratio. The expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the target p and the approximate sample distribution q. The authors integrate this “BREAD” procedure into WebPPL and Stan, validate tightness on toy cases where ground-truth J is computable, and propose a protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate synthetic data at those estimates, and evaluate AIS forward/reverse on the synthetic data. They use BREAD to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in a matrix factorization model and report that the tool helped uncover a sampling bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work: The work builds directly on BDMC [Grosse et al., AISTATS 2015], AIS [Neal 2001], and thermodynamic integration/Jarzynski identities [Jarzynski 1997; Neal 1996]. It is closely related to the subjective divergence estimator of Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka [2016], which also uses BDMC-style forward/reverse paths; the present paper emphasizes an expectation bound on Jeffreys divergence and applies it within PPLs. It complements MCMC diagnostics such as Gelman–Rubin R̂ and ESS [Gelman & Rubin 1992; Brooks & Gelman 1998; Cowles & Carlin 1996] and recent Stein discrepancies [Gorham & Mackey, NIPS 2015], offering a different, likelihood-based, pathwise evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: the expected gap between forward and reverse AIS bounds upper-bounds DJ(p‖q). The derivation is careful and connects naturally to the extended-state AIS perspective.
- Pragmatic integration into WebPPL and Stan, with case studies across several standard models and a useful debugging anecdote, demonstrating engineering utility in PPL settings.
- The real-data protocol is thoughtful and acknowledges the necessity of exact posterior samples for reverse AIS; the fitted-hyperparameter bootstrap is a reasonable heuristic with empirical checks.
- The matrix factorization study shows how BREAD can guide model representation choices and reveals system-specific trade-offs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy reliance on simulated data and exact posterior samples: the rigorous guarantee only holds for simulated datasets. The real-data protocol replaces the exact posterior with a sample obtained by short MCMC runs initialized at fitted hyperparameters. This breaks the guarantee and rests on assumptions that are weakest precisely in the hard cases: multimodality, weak identifiability, or severe model misspecification.
- Assumptions in the real-data protocol are insufficiently justified: (i) that the fitted hyperparameter point estimate is representative of the posterior; (ii) that the synthetic data have similar posterior geometry and mixing difficulty as the real data; and (iii) that a small number of MCMC updates produce an “almost exact” posterior sample. The paper provides some empirical checks across five relatively simple models, but these do not convincingly cover challenging regimes where MCMC reliability is most questionable.
- Scope limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers or MCMC steps embedded within them. The paper argues that design choices transfer to conventional MCMC (e.g., HMC), but the diagnostic itself does not directly evaluate plain chains without an annealing/SMC scaffold.
- The bound is in expectation. Guidance is limited on finite-sample usage: how many forward/reverse chains are needed to obtain a stable, practically meaningful estimate of the Jeffreys upper bound? The visualizations focus on quartiles of log-weights rather than a calibrated reporting of the bound gap.
- Empirical section emphasizes agreement of forward AIS curves on real vs. synthetic data after a vertical shift. This is suggestive but not a direct validation of the claimed Jeffreys divergence control, and could fail under misspecification (as the authors note in an appendix).

Quality
- The theoretical results appear sound and well-situated within existing AIS/BDMC analyses. However, the main practical claims for real data rest on heuristics. More systematic stress-testing and calibration would improve confidence.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Suggestions: clarify the finite-sample interpretation of the bound gap and recommended practice (number of temperatures, chains, and diagnostics to detect when reverse-chain initialization is inadequate); discuss numerical stability and aggregation strategies for heavy-tailed AIS weights; and more explicitly distinguish guarantees (simulated data with exact posterior sample) from heuristics (fitted-hyperparameter protocol).

Originality
- Moderate. Extends BDMC to a new target (Jeffreys divergence for approximate posteriors) and packages it for PPLs. Related independent work is acknowledged and contrasted.

Significance
- For benchmarking and debugging inference within PPLs, the approach is promising. As a general-purpose diagnostic for MCMC on real datasets, its impact is limited by the need for simulated data and approximate posterior initializations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to standard diagnostics (R̂, ESS, Geweke) and Stein discrepancy across challenging targets: Neal’s funnel, high-dimensional Gaussian mixtures, heavy-tailed posteriors, and misspecified models. Report cases where BREAD flags issues that traditional diagnostics miss (and vice versa).
- Calibrate the finite-sample bound: report how the empirical gap concentrates with increasing numbers of forward/reverse chains and temperatures; provide confidence intervals or concentration diagnostics.
- Stress-test the real-data protocol: vary the number of MCMC steps used to initialize the reverse chain under known multimodality; measure sensitivity to different hyperparameter estimates and priors.
- Discuss applicability to HMC/NUTS in practice (e.g., using HMC kernels within AIS), and when this embedding meaningfully reflects the behavior of standalone HMC.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical link from BDMC to an interpretable posterior-sample quality measure.
- Practical integration into two PPLs with concrete use cases, including revealing an implementation bug.
- Potentially valuable benchmarking tool for inference developers.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees hinge on conditions rarely met for real data; the proposed workaround is heuristic and weakly validated.
- Limited empirical breadth; lacks head-to-head comparisons with widely used diagnostics on challenging targets.
- Finite-sample guidance and calibration of the bound are underdeveloped.

Overall recommendation
- Borderline reject. The paper is interesting and potentially useful for the PPL community, but stronger empirical validation on challenging problems and clearer finite-sample guidance are needed to justify publication at NIPS in its current form.
