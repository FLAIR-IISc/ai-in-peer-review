This paper extends the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique [GGA15] to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors integrate their method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), into WebPPL [GS] and Stan [CGHL+ p] and validate it on several models. They also use it to study the effectiveness of different model representations in both probabilistic programming languages.

The work builds upon recent advances in bounding the marginal likelihood using BDMC, adapting the approach to instead quantify the accuracy of posterior samples. This is a valuable endeavor, as assessing the reliability of MCMC inference remains a significant challenge, particularly within “black box” inference systems. The paper correctly positions this work within the context of existing convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and methods for automatic MCMC parameter tuning [RR01], highlighting that BREAD offers a potentially more rigorous approach, albeit under specific conditions. The connection to related work on stochastic lower bounds [BGS15] and alternative divergence estimators [CTM16, GM15] is also clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the difficulty of validating MCMC inference and the need for quantitative measures beyond heuristics.
*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is well-motivated and builds logically upon previous work [GGA15]. The analysis appears sound, and the extension to divergence bounding is a natural progression.
*   **Practical Implementation:** Integrating BREAD into two widely used probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) demonstrates the practical applicability of the method.
*   **Validation on Multiple Models:** The experiments cover a reasonable range of models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.) and provide some evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
*   **Diagnostic Application:** The use of BREAD to compare model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed in the matrix factorization example) provides a compelling demonstration of its utility in guiding modeling decisions. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the potential of the method as a testing tool.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core contribution – adapting BDMC for divergence bounding – feels incremental. The authors acknowledge this, framing the work as an application of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new method. The theoretical derivation, while sound, largely extends prior work.
*   **Simulated Data Dependence:** The method currently requires simulated data to obtain rigorous bounds, limiting its direct applicability to real-world problems. The proposed protocol for using fitted hyperparameters to generate representative simulated data is a reasonable approach, but the validation provided (Section 5.1.1) is not entirely conclusive.
*   **Computational Cost:** Running AIS chains in both directions is likely to be computationally expensive, particularly for complex models. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the computational overhead or provide guidance on when the benefits of BREAD outweigh the costs.
*   **Quartile Visualization:** While acknowledging the presence of outliers, the choice to visualize quartiles rather than means or medians in some plots (e.g., Fig. 1) obscures the central tendency of the distributions.
*   **Missing Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method, such as its sensitivity to the choice of intermediate distributions in AIS or the potential for inaccurate bounds when the reverse chain is initialized from an approximate posterior.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a rigorous method for evaluating MCMC performance, filling a gap in the existing toolbox.
*   Demonstrates practical utility through implementations in two popular probabilistic programming languages.
*   Offers a novel application of BDMC beyond marginal likelihood estimation.
*   Highlights a bug in WebPPL, showcasing the method’s value as a debugging tool.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea is incremental and lacks substantial novelty.
*   The reliance on simulated data limits the immediate applicability of the method.
*   The computational cost of running BDMC may be prohibitive for large-scale problems.
*   The presented results, while positive, do not demonstrate a transformative impact on the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed application of existing techniques to a challenging problem. While the novelty is limited, the implementation in WebPPL and Stan and the demonstration of its utility in diagnosing model representations are valuable contributions. However, the dependence on simulated data and the potential computational cost raise concerns. I believe the paper is a good contribution but might be more appropriate for a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, rather than a main NIPS conference.

**Rating:** Weak Accept (leaning towards Borderline Reject) – I would be comfortable with either decision, depending on the program committee’s overall priorities and the availability of more novel submissions.
