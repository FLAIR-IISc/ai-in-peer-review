This paper proposes a novel method, BREAD, for quantitatively assessing the reliability of MCMC inference, particularly for samplers like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015). By generating stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, the authors derive an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the sampler's approximate posterior and the true posterior. The goal of providing a more theoretically grounded MCMC diagnostic than existing heuristics is a valuable contribution to the field.

The primary strength of this work lies in its attempt to build a principled, quantitative diagnostic for MCMC convergence, a significant and notoriously difficult problem. The theoretical connection drawn between the BDMC bounds and the Jeffreys divergence is elegant and interesting.

However, the paper's practical application to real-world data relies on a series of strong and questionable assumptions. The BDMC framework requires an exact posterior sample to produce a valid upper bound, which the authors work around by performing diagnostics on synthetic data. This workaround introduces three critical issues:
1.  It requires first obtaining a representative hyperparameter, η_real, from prior MCMC runs on the real data, which presumes those initial runs have already converged and that a single point estimate is a sufficient summary. This introduces a degree of circularity.
2.  It assumes that the MCMC convergence properties on the synthetic posterior are similar to those on the real-data posterior. This is a strong, unjustified assumption, especially under the common scenario of model misspecification.
3.  It optimistically assumes that starting an MCMC chain from η_real quickly yields a sample that is a sufficient proxy for an exact sample needed to initialize the reverse chain.

These assumptions are deeply intertwined with the original convergence problem and undermine the diagnostic's validity precisely in the complex, multimodal, or poorly-specified scenarios where it would be most useful. The empirical validation in Section 5.1 is limited to simple models and does not convincingly demonstrate that these foundational issues are negligible in more challenging settings.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a more rigorous justification for why their method is reliable in difficult cases, such as posteriors with significant multimodality where a point-estimate for hyperparameters is a poor summary. Furthermore, a crucial missing piece is a comparative study. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it included simulations comparing BREAD against standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin R-hat, ESS) on models known to be difficult for MCMC, demonstrating where the proposed method offers a clear advantage. Without addressing these fundamental concerns, the proposed diagnostic's practical utility remains unclear.
