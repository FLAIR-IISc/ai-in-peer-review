This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), based on bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples obtained from MCMC algorithms. The core idea is to leverage the strengths of BDMC, originally designed for marginal likelihood estimation, to provide a quantitative measure of MCMC sample quality, particularly crucial in “black-box” probabilistic programming language (PPL) settings. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate its utility through validation experiments and an analysis of different model representations in matrix factorization.

The motivation for this work is strong. Existing MCMC diagnostics often lack rigorous guarantees, and assessing the reliability of inference in PPLs is challenging for non-expert users. BDMC offers a potentially powerful approach to address these issues by providing a quantifiable bound on the divergence between the approximate and true posterior. The paper builds directly on prior work on BDMC [GGA15, Nea01] and extends it to the specific problem of evaluating posterior inference. It’s a natural and logical extension of existing techniques. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and practical demonstration. The derivation of the estimator bounding the Jeffreys divergence appears sound, and the implementation within popular PPLs like WebPPL and Stan is significant. The experiments, while not extensive, demonstrate the method’s feasibility and illustrate its potential to inform model design choices. The identification of a bug in WebPPL through the application of BREAD is a particularly compelling example of its practical value.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. Section 3.1, detailing the theoretical development, is difficult to follow. The notation is dense, and the introduction of distributions like q(.) before their explicit definition creates confusion.  The authors should prioritize clarity and ensure the core argument is readily accessible to a broad audience. While the authors mention pushing details to the supplement, essential steps in the derivation should be presented in the main text to enhance reproducibility and understanding.  The use of both 'z' and 'θ' to represent unobserved variables feels cluttered and unnecessary, as they consistently appear together, and simplifying the notation would improve readability. Finally, there's a minor typographical error on line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

A key area where the paper could be strengthened is through a more thorough discussion of related work in MCMC diagnostics. The authors acknowledge existing methods, but a more detailed comparison with techniques like Geweke’s diagnostics [Geweke, 2004] and the work of Cook, Gelman, and Rubin [Cook, Gelman & Rubin, 2006] – which focus on validating MCMC by running chains on data sampled from the prior – would provide valuable context and demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the field. These methods share the goal of assessing MCMC performance and acknowledging their limitations in comparison to BREAD's bounds would be helpful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:**  The application of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence for evaluating MCMC samples is a novel and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation appears solid, and the integration with existing PPLs demonstrates practical feasibility.
*   **Potential Significance:** BREAD could become a valuable tool for both researchers developing inference algorithms and users of PPLs seeking to validate their results.
*   **Demonstrated Utility:**  The identification of a bug in WebPPL showcases the method's ability to uncover subtle errors in inference implementations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:**  The presentation of the theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is challenging and requires improvement.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experimental evaluation, while helpful, could be more extensive and explore a wider range of models and datasets.
*   **Missing Related Work Discussion:** The discussion of related work on MCMC diagnostics could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel approach to a challenging problem. While the clarity of the theoretical presentation needs improvement, the underlying ideas are strong, and the practical demonstration is compelling.  The potential impact of this work on the field of probabilistic programming and MCMC is significant. I recommend **acceptance** contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity concerns in Section 3.1 and providing a more comprehensive discussion of related work in MCMC diagnostics.
