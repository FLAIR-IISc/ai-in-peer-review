

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors propose using BDMC to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior, addressing a critical challenge in probabilistic programming where black-box inference methods make it difficult to validate results. The work builds upon Grosse et al. (2015), extending their BDMC framework from marginal likelihood estimation to posterior quality assessment. The paper is well-positioned in the NIPS literature, connecting to recent work on MCMC diagnostics (Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Brooks and Gelman, 1998) and complementary to Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which also leverages BDMC for inference evaluation but with different theoretical guarantees. The authors correctly note that existing MCMC diagnostics cannot confirm convergence, while their method provides rigorous bounds under simulated data.

The paper makes four main technical contributions: (1) an estimator that upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence; (2) a technique for computing both the true Jeffreys divergence and upper bound on small examples; (3) a protocol for applying the method to real-world data; and (4) integration into WebPPL and Stan with validation across multiple models. The experimental validation demonstrates the method's utility in comparing different model representations (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and uncovering a bug in WebPPL, highlighting its practical value for debugging inference algorithms.

The paper's strengths are significant. The problem addressed is important and timely, given the growing use of probabilistic programming for non-experts. The method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which could greatly benefit practitioners. The integration into two popular probabilistic programming languages demonstrates practical relevance, and the case studies effectively illustrate how the method can guide modeling and algorithmic decisions. The theoretical foundation is sound, with proper derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The most significant issue is the lack of clarity in Section 3.1, where the authors use q(·) before defining it. The marginal distribution of the AIS sampler after T steps should be explicitly stated as q(x_T) = ∫ q(v,x_T) dv to make the derivation comprehensible. This is a critical part of the paper's theoretical contribution, and its poor exposition undermines the technical quality. The paper also pushes essential derivation details to the supplement rather than including them in the main text, making the paper less self-contained. The unnecessary splitting of unobserved variables into z and θ (which always appear together) creates clutter and reduces readability. Additionally, there is a typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The technical content is strong, with good theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The method is carefully evaluated on multiple models, though the real-world data validation protocol could be more thoroughly justified.
- Clarity: The paper has significant clarity issues, particularly in Section 3.1, which needs substantial revision for better exposition. The main text should be self-contained regarding key derivations.
- Originality: The extension of BDMC to evaluate MCMC inference is novel and significantly different from previous NIPS papers on MCMC diagnostics. The work distinguishes itself from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) by focusing on the Jeffreys divergence rather than subjective divergence.
- Significance: The method addresses a critical practical problem in probabilistic programming, with potential to improve the reliability of inference in both research and industry applications. The debugging example (uncovering a WebPPL bug) demonstrates tangible utility.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a novel solution to an important problem, with strong theoretical foundation and practical implementation. The method provides a rigorous alternative to heuristic MCMC diagnostics, and the integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates real-world applicability.

Arguments against acceptance: The current presentation has significant clarity issues that need to be addressed, particularly in the theoretical development. The paper would benefit from including key derivation details in the main text and fixing the variable notation.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. With revisions to improve clarity and exposition, particularly in Section 3.1, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the technical contribution is significant but the presentation detracts from its impact. The authors should address the clarity issues before final acceptance.
