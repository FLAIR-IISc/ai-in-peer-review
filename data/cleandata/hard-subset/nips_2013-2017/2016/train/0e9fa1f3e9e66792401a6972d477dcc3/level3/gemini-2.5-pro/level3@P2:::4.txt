### **Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of assessing the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, particularly in the context of "black-box" probabilistic programming languages. The authors propose a method called Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015). The core idea is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains on simulated data, both in the "forward" direction (from prior to posterior) and the "reverse" direction (from posterior to prior). This process yields stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood.

The main theoretical contribution of this work is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds serves as an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate sample distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. This provides a single, quantitative, and interpretable scalar metric for the quality of the approximate samples.

A key requirement of the method is access to an exact sample from the posterior, which limits its direct application to simulated data. To bridge this gap for real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real dataset, then simulate a new dataset from the model using these fitted hyperparameters, and finally apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. The paper provides experimental evidence suggesting that inference behavior on such simulated data is a good proxy for behavior on the original real data.

The authors implement BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on a variety of models. They demonstrate its utility not only for diagnosing convergence but also for guiding modeling decisions (e.g., choosing between a collapsed and uncollapsed representation of a matrix factorization model) and for debugging inference implementations, as evidenced by their discovery of a bug in WebPPL. The work is well-situated with respect to prior work on MCMC diagnostics, BDMC, and concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The theoretical derivation connecting the BDMC bound gap to the Jeffreys divergence is elegant and well-founded. Claims are substantiated with a good range of experiments across five different models and two distinct inference frameworks (Metropolis-Hastings in WebPPL and HMC/NUTS in Stan). The authors are careful to acknowledge the primary limitation of the method—its reliance on simulated data—and propose a reasonable, albeit heuristic, protocol to address it. The validation of this protocol adds confidence in the method's practical applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with a clear introduction that motivates the problem effectively and a solid background section. The experimental results are presented clearly. However, the clarity could be improved in Section 3.1, which presents the core theoretical result. The derivation is quite dense, and the transition from the background material is abrupt. The reader would benefit from a clearer statement of the goal at the beginning of the section (i.e., to bound the Jeffreys divergence) and more motivation for the definitions and algebraic steps. The novelty of the Jeffreys divergence bound, which is a central contribution, could also be more explicitly highlighted. There are also minor notational inconsistencies, for instance, using both 'y' and 'D' to refer to data.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution. While it builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015), the extension from estimating marginal likelihoods to bounding the divergence of posterior approximations is a novel and impactful idea. This reframing of BDMC as a diagnostic tool for sample quality, rather than just a tool for model comparison, is a key insight. The application to and integration with modern probabilistic programming languages is also timely and important. The paper does a good job of acknowledging and differentiating itself from concurrent related work.

**Significance:** The work is of high significance to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and statistics. The problem of validating MCMC output is fundamental, and the lack of reliable, general-purpose tools hinders the broader adoption of Bayesian methods, especially via probabilistic programming. BREAD offers a principled and quantitative measure of inference quality that is more rigorous than common heuristics like the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic. The demonstrated applications for comparing model representations and for debugging inference engines are compelling and highlight the method's broad utility. By integrating their method into Stan and WebPPL, the authors have made a valuable contribution that others are likely to use and build upon. The main practical limitation is the computational cost and the reliance on simulated data, which may prevent its use as a routine, default check.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a major, unsolved problem:** Provides a new, quantitative tool for the crucial task of diagnosing MCMC convergence and sample quality.
2.  **Strong theoretical contribution:** The paper originally proves that the expected BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence, providing a principled foundation for the method.
3.  **Convincing empirical validation:** The method is successfully demonstrated on a variety of models within two widely-used and structurally different probabilistic programming systems (Stan and WebPPL).
4.  **Demonstrated practical utility:** The paper shows the method is useful not just for convergence checking but also for model engineering and software debugging, which are high-impact applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limitation to simulated data:** The method's core guarantees only hold for simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a heuristic, and its reliability may be context-dependent.
2.  **Clarity of the core method:** Section 3.1 is difficult to follow and could obscure the paper's main contribution for some readers.
3.  **Computational cost:** The method requires running many AIS chains, which can be computationally intensive, potentially limiting its widespread adoption.

### **Detailed comments**

Overall, I am in favor of accepting this paper. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the core idea, clearly outweigh the weaknesses. Below are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Improving Section 3.1:** This section would be much clearer if it were prefaced with a high-level summary of the goal and the steps involved. For example: "We now show how the expectations of the forward and reverse AIS log-likelihood estimators can be combined to form a bound on the Jeffreys divergence. Our derivation proceeds in two steps: first, we relate the bias of the forward estimator to DKL(q||p), and second, we relate the bias of the reverse estimator to DKL(p||q)." This would help orient the reader before diving into the equations.
*   **Code Release:** The authors have made their code available, which is commendable. To maximize its impact, they should consider making the Stan and WebPPL extensions easily installable (e.g., as a package for WebPPL) and provide clear instructions on compatible software versions.
*   **Minor Fixes:** Please unify the notation for observed data (either 'y' or 'D' throughout).
