- Proposes using BDMC to evaluate MCMC posterior inference reliability by bounding the symmetrized (Jeffreys) KL divergence.
- Extends Grosse et al. (2015) BDMC framework to derive an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior.
- Integrates the method into WebPPL and Stan and applies it to matrix factorization models.
- Presents a protocol (BREAD) for evaluating inference on real data via simulation from fitted hyperparameters and reverse-chain initialization.
- Technical derivation is sound and mathematically rigorous, and experiments show applicability to real probabilistic programming systems.
- Experimental contribution is mainly applying known techniques to new platforms rather than providing new theoretical insights.
- Paper omits discussion of important limitations such as computational cost of forward/reverse AIS chains and scaling to larger models.
- Well-written and clearly organized with reproducible experimental details and effective figures.
- Originality is limited; the extension follows directly from prior BDMC work and overlaps with concurrent CTM16 work without sufficient differentiation.
- Practical significance is useful for probabilistic programming practitioners but does not convincingly outperform existing MCMC diagnostics.
- Strengths: solid implementation in WebPPL and Stan, consistent validation between real and simulated data, useful debugging example, thorough experiments.
- Weaknesses: lacks novel theory, limited to AIS/SMC and simulated-data protocol, relies on strong posterior-approximation assumptions, insufficient distinction from CTM16, and no strong advantage over existing diagnostics.
- Recommendation: solid engineering work but insufficient theoretical novelty for top-tier acceptance; better suited to a workshop or more focused venue.
- Suggested improvements: compare with existing convergence diagnostics, clarify advances over prior work, fully discuss limitations and distinguish from concurrent CTM16.
