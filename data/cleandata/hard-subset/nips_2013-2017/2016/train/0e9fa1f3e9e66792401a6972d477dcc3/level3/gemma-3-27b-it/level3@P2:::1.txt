This paper proposes a novel method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for evaluating the quality of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, particularly within probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The core idea is to leverage bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to upper bound the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrate its utility in guiding model representation choices.

The motivation is strong. Assessing MCMC convergence and accuracy remains a significant challenge, especially in “black box” PPLs where internal workings are obscured. Existing diagnostics often fall short of providing rigorous guarantees. The proposed approach, building on recent work on BDMC, offers a potentially valuable tool for quantifying the reliability of inference.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to bounding the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC evaluation is a novel contribution. While BDMC has been used for marginal likelihood estimation, this work extends its applicability to posterior accuracy assessment.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical justification for the method, demonstrating an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
*   **Practical Implementation:** The authors successfully integrated BREAD into two widely used PPLs, WebPPL and Stan, making the method accessible to practitioners.
*   **Demonstrated Utility:** The experimental results showcase the practical utility of BREAD in identifying differences in the effectiveness of different model representations, and even uncovering a bug in WebPPL.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of MCMC validation and positions BREAD as a potential solution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Derivation (Section 3.1):** The derivation in Section 3.1 is somewhat difficult to follow. The notation `q(.)` is used before the distributions are explicitly defined, hindering comprehension. A clearer, step-by-step presentation of the derivation within the main text, rather than relying heavily on the supplement, would significantly improve readability. Specifically, explicitly stating that `qfwd(xT)` is the marginal distribution obtained after T steps of the AIS sampler would be helpful.
*   **Dependence on Simulated Data:** The method fundamentally relies on the ability to generate simulated data with known posterior samples, which is not always feasible in real-world applications. While the authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via hyperparameter estimation and re-simulation, the validity of this approach depends on the accuracy of the estimated hyperparameters and the representativeness of the simulated data, which isn't fully addressed.
*   **Limited Scope of Applicability:** BREAD, as currently formulated, is applicable to algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (AIS, SMC). Its direct applicability to all MCMC algorithms is limited, although the authors acknowledge this and argue its relevance for informing broader MCMC design choices.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work on MCMC convergence diagnostics and validation methods. Specifically, the authors should cite and discuss related work on validating MCMC by running chains on data sampled from the prior [Geweke, 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin, 2006], as this offers a similar spirit of validating against known data.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some clarity issues and limitations in scope, this is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The proposed method offers a rigorous approach to evaluating MCMC inference, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. The practical implementation in WebPPL and Stan, along with the demonstrated utility in guiding model representation choices and debugging, strengthens the paper's value.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MCMC evaluation.
*   Practical implementation in widely used PPLs.
*   Demonstrated utility in real-world scenarios.
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity of derivation could be improved.
*   Reliance on simulated data limits applicability.
*   Missing discussion of relevant related work on MCMC validation.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding clarity in the derivation (Section 3.1) and incorporating a discussion of related work on MCMC validation techniques, such as those by Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006).
