This paper proposes a novel method, termed Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD) – building upon Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – for evaluating the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The core idea is to use annealed importance sampling (AIS) both forward and in reverse to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The authors integrate this method into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets, demonstrating its utility for guiding model and algorithmic design.

The paper addresses a critical challenge in probabilistic inference: reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples, particularly within “black box” inference systems. Existing convergence diagnostics often lack quantitative rigor, and the proposed method offers a principled, albeit computationally intensive, approach to quantifying the discrepancy between the approximate and true posteriors. The connection to recent work on BDMC [GGA15] is clearly articulated, and the authors effectively position their contribution as an extension enabling posterior evaluation rather than solely marginal likelihood estimation.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The theoretical grounding is sound, and the derivation of the upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is a significant contribution, although its novelty could be more explicitly stated. The practical implementation within WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the method’s applicability to real-world inference systems. The validation experiments, showcasing consistency between simulated and real data, and the use case of comparing model representations, are compelling and illustrate the method’s potential impact. The identification of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the diagnostic power of BREAD. The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated, making it accessible to researchers familiar with MCMC and probabilistic programming.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. Section 3, particularly the transition into Section 3.1, can be challenging to follow. The introduction of variables and equations feels abrupt, lacking sufficient motivation and context. A more gradual build-up of the mathematical framework would enhance clarity. The term “AutoBDMC” is used without explicit definition, which is confusing. It's unclear what precisely "AutoBDMC" refers to – it appears to represent the application of BDMC principles to MCMC analysis but this should be stated directly. References to challenges “for AutoBDMC” further exacerbate this issue. There are minor inconsistencies in notation, such as the use of both ‘y’ and ‘D’ to represent data. While the authors acknowledge the limitation to simulated data, more discussion on the assumptions required for transferring insights from simulated data to real-world scenarios would be beneficial. Finally, providing clearer instructions for reproducing the results, including compatible versions of WebPPL and Stan and potentially releasing the code as installable packages, would significantly improve the paper’s reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The method provides a novel and theoretically grounded approach to bounding the divergence between approximate and true posteriors.
*   **Significance:** The ability to rigorously assess MCMC convergence is crucial for reliable probabilistic inference. This method has the potential to improve the trustworthiness of results obtained from probabilistic programming systems.
*   **Practicality:** The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the method’s applicability to real-world tools.
*   **Impact:** The demonstration of its use for model selection and debugging showcases the broader impact of this work.
*   **Clarity (Overall):** Despite some minor issues in Section 3, the paper is generally well-written and understandable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity (Section 3):** Section 3 requires significant improvement in terms of clarity and motivation.
*   **Limited Applicability:** The current method is primarily applicable to simulated data, requiring careful validation for real-world datasets.
*   **Reproducibility:** The lack of clear instructions and packaged code hinders reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While Section 3 requires significant improvement in terms of clarity, the core idea is novel and potentially impactful. The practical implementation and validation experiments are well-executed and demonstrate the method’s utility. I recommend **acceptance** with the request that the authors address the concerns regarding clarity in Section 3, provide a precise definition of "AutoBDMC", and improve the reproducibility of their results by providing clear instructions and packaged code. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
