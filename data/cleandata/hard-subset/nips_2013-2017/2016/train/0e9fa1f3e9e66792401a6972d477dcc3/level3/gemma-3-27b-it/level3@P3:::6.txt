This paper presents an application of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, specifically bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the approximate samples obtained from algorithms like annealed importance sampling (AIS) and sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). While the authors correctly summarize existing work on BDMC and related techniques, and successfully implement the method within WebPPL and Stan, the paper lacks significant novelty and reads more as an engineering report than a scientific contribution suitable for NIPS.

The core idea – extending BDMC to assess MCMC quality – is a logical progression from the original BDMC work [GGA15], and the authors accurately describe the theoretical basis for bounding the Jeffreys divergence. The paper meticulously details the implementation in two probabilistic programming languages, which is a commendable effort. However, the primary contribution is the *implementation* of a known technique, rather than the development of new methodology. The application to matrix factorization, while illustrative, doesn't represent a substantial advancement in the field. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a positive outcome, but it’s more a testament to the value of thorough testing than a novel research finding.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear exposition of existing methods:** The paper provides a comprehensive and understandable summary of BDMC, AIS, and related concepts.
*   **Successful implementation:** Integrating BDMC into WebPPL and Stan is a significant engineering undertaking and demonstrates the feasibility of the approach.
*   **Practical validation:**  The experiments on simulated data and the comparison of model representations provide some practical insights, although not groundbreaking.
*   **Bug discovery:** Identifying a bug in WebPPL demonstrates a valuable application of the implemented framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of originality:** The paper presents no new theoretical insights or algorithmic innovations. It’s primarily an application of existing techniques.
*   **Limited scientific impact:** The results, while technically sound, do not significantly advance the state-of-the-art in MCMC diagnostics or probabilistic programming.
*   **Restricted applicability:** The method, as presented, is limited to algorithms amenable to AIS-style analysis.
*   **Overly detailed exposition:**  Some sections (e.g., background) are excessively detailed and could be condensed to focus on the core contribution, which remains limited.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the implementation appears correct. The claims are generally well-supported by the experiments, although the scope of those experiments is limited. It feels incomplete in the sense that it stops at implementation rather than pushing the boundaries of the field. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though verbose in places. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are reasonably clear.  More concise writing would improve readability.

**Originality:** This is the primary weakness. The paper builds upon existing work without presenting any novel ideas.  The related work section is thorough, which further highlights the lack of novel contributions.

**Significance:** The results are unlikely to have a broad impact on the field. While useful for debugging and understanding the behavior of MCMC algorithms in specific contexts, the method doesn’t offer a fundamentally new approach to MCMC diagnostics or inference.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a practical application of BDMC to a relevant problem.
*   Successfully integrates a complex method into two popular probabilistic programming languages.
*   Identified a bug in WebPPL, highlighting the utility of the approach for testing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks substantial novelty and scientific contribution.
*   Primarily an engineering effort rather than a research breakthrough.
*   The limited scope and applicability of the method.
*   More suitable for a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming tooling than a major conference like NIPS.



**Overall:**  I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically competent and well-written, it falls short of the originality and significance criteria expected for NIPS. The work would be more appropriately presented at a workshop focused on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming implementations.  The authors should consider framing the work as a technical report detailing the implementation and validation of BDMC within WebPPL and Stan, rather than a novel research contribution.
