### Summary

This paper proposes a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD) for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, which was originally used for estimating marginal likelihoods. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both forward (from the prior to the posterior) and backward (from the posterior to the prior) on simulated data, the authors derive an estimator that provides a stochastic upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. The method requires an exact sample from the true posterior, limiting its direct application to simulated data. The authors propose a protocol for extending its use to real-world data by first fitting hyperparameters, then simulating data from the model conditioned on these hyperparameters. The BREAD framework is integrated into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and its utility is demonstrated through several experiments, including validating inference consistency, comparing different model representations for matrix factorization, and identifying a bug in WebPPPL's sampler.

The general approach of validating inference algorithms on simulated data, where the ground truth is known, has a history in the MCMC literature. This work builds on that tradition by providing a more quantitative and theoretically grounded measure of discrepancy (the Jeffreys divergence) rather than relying on visual checks or simple parameter recovery statistics.

### Quality and Significance

The paper addresses the crucial and long-standing problem of diagnosing the reliability of MCMC samplers. Existing diagnostics, such as the Gelman-Rubin statistic, are heuristic and can fail to detect certain modes of non-convergence. This work offers a more principled approach by providing a quantitative bound on a formal statistical divergence. The technical contributions are sound, extending the analysis of BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding the Jeffreys divergence.

The significance of this work is high. For practitioners using probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) as black-box inference engines, a reliable tool for assessing sample quality is invaluable. For researchers developing new models or inference algorithms, BREAD provides a powerful methodology for debugging and comparing different approaches. The experiments effectively demonstrate this utility:
1.  The validation experiments in Section 5.1 provide confidence that the method's results on simulated data are relevant to performance on real data.
2.  The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed samplers for matrix factorization is a compelling use case, offering concrete guidance on model representation choices that can be difficult to make a priori.
3.  The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a very strong testament to the practical utility of this method for ensuring the correctness of PPL implementations.

A key limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is the reliance on simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a reasonable heuristic, but it relies on an approximate posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, which breaks the formal guarantee of the bound. However, the empirical validation in Section 5.1.2 suggests this approximation is effective in practice.

### Originality and Related Work

The primary contribution is the novel application and extension of the BDMC framework to bound the Jeffreys divergence for evaluating AIS-based samplers. While BDMC itself is a recent proposal [GGA15], its use for this purpose is new.

However, the paper misses an opportunity to connect its validation protocol to important prior work on MCMC diagnostics. The idea of generating data from the model's prior, running the MCMC sampler, and checking if the posterior can recover the "true" parameters is a well-established validation technique. Key papers in this area that should be cited and discussed include:
*   Geweke, J. (2004). Getting it right: A new approach to motivation and evaluation. *Journal of the American Statistical Association*.
*   Cook, S. R., Gelman, A., & Rubin, D. B. (2006). Validation of software for Bayesian models using posterior quantiles. *Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics*.

These papers lay the conceptual groundwork for using simulation to validate Bayesian inference. The present work can be framed as providing a much more sophisticated and quantitative tool within this established paradigm. Acknowledging this connection would strengthen the paper by placing it in its proper historical context.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the experiments are well-explained. However, there are areas where clarity could be significantly improved.

1.  **Section 3.1 is difficult to follow.** The derivation of the bound on the Jeffreys divergence is the central theoretical contribution of the paper, but its presentation is too compressed. The notation `qfwd(xT)` is used in equations (4) through (11) before its meaning is made clear. It should be explicitly defined as the marginal distribution of the final state `xT` produced by the forward AIS chain. The logical steps in the derivation, particularly the application of the data processing inequality for KL divergence (from eq. 7 to 8 and from eq. 10 to 11), are presented without sufficient explanation.

2.  **The paper is not self-contained.** Crucial details for understanding the AIS algorithm and the derivation of the BDMC bounds are relegated to the appendix. While space is limited, the main text should provide a clear, step-by-step intuition for how the forward and reverse chains are constructed and how they lead to the bounds. A more self-contained presentation in Section 3.1 would greatly enhance the paper's impact and readability.

3.  **Minor Points:**
    *   The notational distinction between parameters `theta` and latent variables `z` seems unnecessary, as they are always treated together as `(✓, z)`. Simplifying this to a single variable representing all unobserved quantities would reduce clutter.
    *   There is a typographical error in line 165: `Eqfwd(v,xT )  log(...)` is missing a closing parenthesis after the log expression.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   Addresses a highly significant and difficult problem in modern machine learning and statistics: how to trust MCMC output.
*   Proposes a novel and principled method (BREAD) based on a clever extension of BDMC.
*   The practical utility is convincingly demonstrated through strong experiments, including a real-world debugging success story in a popular PPL.
*   The integration into Stan and WebPPL makes the contribution immediately useful to a wide audience.

### Arguments for Rejection
*   The clarity of the core theoretical section (3.1) is low, making the central contribution difficult to understand without significant effort from the reader.
*   The paper fails to cite and discuss highly relevant prior work on MCMC validation using simulated data, which slightly overstates its conceptual novelty in that area.

### Recommendation

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The contribution is important, original, and of high practical value. The clarity issues, while notable, are correctable. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to improve the clarity of Section 3.1 and to properly situate their work with respect to the prior literature on simulation-based MCMC validation.
