This paper adapts bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference, focusing on AIS/SMC-like algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling on an extended state space. Leveraging forward and reverse AIS on simulated data (with an exact posterior sample obtainable via ancestral simulation), the authors show that the gap between stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. They integrate the method into WebPPL and Stan, validate on toy problems (where tightness can be checked), and demonstrate practical use on a range of models (regression, time series, mixture models, matrix factorization). They propose a pragmatic protocol for real datasets: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data at those hyperparameters, and check that AIS behavior on real and simulated data aligns; they also initialize reverse AIS by short MCMC moves from the fitted hyperparameters and empirically assess insensitivity to the number of steps. Finally, they use the methodology to compare model representations (collapsed vs uncollapsed) and uncover an implementation bug in WebPPL. The work builds on Neal (1996; 2001), Jarzynski (1997), BDMC for marginal likelihood sandwiching (Grosse et al., 2015), and relates to concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016).

Strengths
- Clear articulation of how the BDMC sandwich for log partition functions translates into an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions.
- Practical integration into two probabilistic programming systems, showing the method’s utility in realistic workflows.
- Sensible and useful empirical protocol for bridging simulated and real data, with evidence that forward AIS behaves similarly in both settings under fitted hyperparameters.
- Demonstrated diagnostic value: identifying a subtle multivariate Gaussian sampling bug in WebPPL.
- Empirical case study illustrating how BREAD can inform modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed representations) differently across platforms.

Weaknesses
- Originality is limited. The core theoretical ingredient—using forward/reverse AIS gaps to control divergences—largely follows from prior BDMC analysis; the main contribution is repurposing known bounds for sample quality rather than marginal likelihood, plus system integration. Overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” [CTM16] further narrows novelty.
- The guarantees hold only for simulated data with exact posterior samples; for real data, the fitted-hyperparameter protocol is heuristic and breaks the rigorous bound. While empirical checks are reassuring, the lack of theoretical control in the real-data setting is a key limitation.
- Applicability is constrained to AIS/SMC-style samplers (or MCMC wrapped in such schemes). Claims about informing general MCMC design are indirect; there is no direct assessment of generic HMC/NUTS outputs absent AIS scaffolding.
- Experimental scope is modest: relatively small models/data; limited exploration of challenging multimodal, high-dimensional posteriors where convergence diagnostics are most needed. Tightness of the bound is shown only on toy distributions where exact quantities are computable.
- Limited comparison to alternative convergence/sample-quality diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, ESS, Geweke diagnostics, Stein discrepancies [Gorham & Mackey, 2015]); the paper would benefit from a more systematic empirical head-to-head.

Quality
- Technically sound derivations, consistent with BDMC foundations. The expectation bound is clearly stated, and the caveats (simulated data requirement; approximate posterior initialization for hyperparameters) are acknowledged.
- Empirical methodology is reasonable; sensitivity analyses on the reverse-chain initialization length are helpful. However, the paper stops short of providing quantitative calibration (e.g., how often the bound is tight or conservative across a broader suite).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The background on AIS/BDMC is clear, and the Methods section makes the connection to Jeffreys divergence explicit. The practical protocol for real data is described plainly. Suggestions: summarize assumptions and limitations in a concise checklist; report dispersion-aware summaries (e.g., medians, IQRs) consistently, and clarify when multiple-chain averaging is or is not justified by the analysis.

Originality
- Incremental. The main novelty is the specific repurposing of BDMC to bound sample quality and its integration into PPL tooling, rather than new theory or algorithms. Concurrent related work further reduces originality.

Significance
- Useful in practice for PPL users and developers as a diagnostic and regression-testing tool; the ability to catch implementation bugs is a strong selling point. However, impact on the broader MCMC community may be limited by the reliance on AIS-like scaffolding and the need for simulated data to retain guarantees.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide broader empirical evaluation including multimodal targets and higher dimensions; quantify bound tightness distributionally (coverage, calibration).
- Compare against R-hat/ESS and Stein discrepancies; show cases where BREAD detects failures those miss (or vice versa).
- Analyze sensitivity to annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels.
- Explore extensions to obtain per-instance probabilistic confidence statements (e.g., via multiple reverse runs or concentration bounds).
- Clarify the relationship and distinctions from [CTM16], including empirical comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, actionable diagnostic integrated into widely used PPLs.
- Clear exposition that may help standardize reliability checks for AIS/SMC-based inference.
- Demonstrated real value via debugging and guiding modeling choices.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond applying BDMC to a closely related metric; overlapping concurrent work.
- Guarantees only for simulated data; heuristic nature on real data.
- Modest experimental scope and limited comparisons to established diagnostics.

Recommendation
- Borderline reject for the main conference due to limited originality and scope; stronger fit for an MCMC/probabilistic programming workshop. If strengthened with broader empirical comparisons and deeper analysis of bound tightness and real-data performance, it could merit reconsideration. Reviewer confidence: medium.
