This paper revisits bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) and applies it to assess the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference by running AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) to produce stochastic lower/upper bounds on log p(y). The gap is used to upper bound in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between approximate AIS samples and the true posterior. The authors integrate this diagnostic into WebPPL and Stan, propose a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to approximate the reverse initialization on real data, and present case studies (regression, mixtures, matrix factorization), including an illustration that the tool can reveal a bug. The work is positioned relative to AIS (Neal, 2001), SMC (Del Moral et al., 2006), standard diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman), and sample-quality measures (Stein discrepancies; Gorham–Mackey, 2015). Closely related concurrent work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016) reaches essentially the same Jeffreys-divergence guarantee via “subjective divergence.”

Quality: The analysis follows directly from BDMC and AIS importance-sampling identities; for simulated data the arguments are sound. However, for real data the fitted-hyperparameter reverse initialization breaks the rigorous guarantee, and the empirical validation of this approximation is limited. Comparisons against standard diagnostics and Stein discrepancies are missing, and tightness-of-bound studies are small-scale.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The relationship to BDMC and to CTM16 should be made more explicit, with a precise statement of what (if anything) is new beyond reinterpreting BDMC’s bias terms as a Jeffreys bound.

Originality: Limited. The main ideas—forward/reverse AIS sandwiching and the expectation bound—are already in BDMC or are straightforward corollaries; CTM16 independently presents an equivalent formulation. The contribution is primarily an implementation and application in WebPPL and Stan.

Significance: As engineering, the tool could be useful to PPL users (e.g., for debugging and model-representation choices). As a NeurIPS main-track contribution, the advances are modest and better suited to an MCMC/Probabilistic Programming workshop or a systems/demo venue.

Pros:
- Clear exposition; good integration into WebPPL and Stan
- Practical case studies; actionable insights; nice debugging example
- Reproducible and potentially useful to practitioners

Cons:
- No substantial novelty beyond BDMC; overlap with CTM16
- Guarantees hold only on simulated data; real-data protocol heuristic
- Limited empirical breadth; no head-to-head against established diagnostics

Recommendation: Reject for the main track; consider an MCMC/ProbProg workshop after clarifying novelty, broadening benchmarks, and adding comparisons to R-hat/ESS and Stein-based diagnostics.
