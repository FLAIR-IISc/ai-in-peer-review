- Proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), extending BDMC to evaluate MCMC posterior inference via forward and reverse AIS on simulated data.
- Shows the gap between reverse and forward log-AIS estimates upper-bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between approximate sampler output and the true posterior.
- Derives how auxiliary bounds on log normalizing constants yield one-sided KL divergence bounds in expectation.
- Requires an exact posterior sample for reverse AIS, so rigorous guarantees apply to simulated-data experiments only.
- Proposes a pragmatic real-data protocol: fit hyperparameters, simulate datasets, and check AIS behavior matches real data (parametric-bootstrap style).
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validates on linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, and mixture-of-Gaussians.
- Demonstrates practical utility: guides model representation choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and uncovered a multivariate Gaussian sampling bug in WebPPL.
- Relates to convergence diagnostics (R-hat), Stein discrepancies, thermodynamic integration, and contemporaneous work on subjective divergence.
- Strength: addresses a timely, important problem for black-box MCMC and PPL users.
- Strength: elegant reuse of BDMC/AIS to produce expectation upper bounds on Jeffreys divergence.
- Strength: solid theoretical derivations connecting forward/reverse AIS gaps to DKL(q||p), DKL(p||q), and their sum.
- Strength: practical integration and experiments provide actionable guidance and debugging benefits.
- Strength: sensible real-data protocol with empirical validation that simulated behavior tracks real data.
- Limitation: rigorous guarantees require simulated-data exact samples; the real-data heuristic lacks formal guarantees or error quantification.
- Limitation: bounds are in expectation; no per-run guarantees and AIS log-weights can be heavy-tailed and noisy.
- Limitation: applicable mainly to AIS/SMC-style samplers; offers only indirect insight for long-run MCMC like HMC/NUTS alone.
- Limitation: potential sensitivity to annealing schedule, transition kernels, and number of intermediates; more schedule adaptation/ablation needed.
- Limitation: empirical evaluation is modest in dimensionality and multimodality; scalability and tightness in high dimensions remain untested.
- Quality: technically sound derivations and empirically substantiated practical utility (tightness on toy problems, real-vs-sim consistency, robustness checks).
- Suggested improvements: quantify bound variability across runs (e.g., CIs), provide guidance on annealing schedule design, and state assumptions explicitly.
- Clarity: well written with helpful notation; recommend adding a concise algorithmic summary and intuition about tightness conditions.
- Originality: novel reuse of BDMC for posterior sample quality assessment and useful integration into two PPLs.
- Significance: high — could become a standard tool for PPL developers and users to sanity-check inference quality and detect implementation errors.
- Questions for authors: adaptive schedule selection, relationships between bound tightness and dimensionality/multimodality, aggregate uncertainty reporting, SMC/resampling behavior, and code availability.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, useful, and well-presented work; recommendation: Accept.
