This paper proposes a method, Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), for measuring the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference by bounding the symmetrized Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. The approach extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) and is validated through implementations within the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan, alongside experiments demonstrating its utility in guiding model representation choices.

The core idea builds upon recent work on BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] to provide an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa, and leveraging the properties of stochastic bounds, the authors aim to quantify the accuracy of MCMC-based inference.  The contribution lies in adapting BDMC for this specific purpose, providing a practical tool for evaluating inference algorithms, especially within “black box” probabilistic programming systems. This is a valuable endeavor, as assessing MCMC convergence and accuracy remains a significant challenge in Bayesian inference. 

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The proposed method appears conceptually simple and potentially impactful, offering a way to assess the quality of approximate inference without relying on convergence diagnostics which often only identify *potential* problems. The integration of BREAD into both WebPPL and Stan demonstrates its practicality, and the experimental results suggesting its ability to guide modeling decisions (specifically, the choice of collapsed vs. uncollapsed variable representations) are promising. The identification of a bug in WebPPL further highlights the potential of this approach for verifying software implementations.

However, the paper also suffers from several weaknesses. While the core idea is sound, the technical contributions feel somewhat incremental. The observation that the gap between BDMC’s lower and upper bounds can serve as a performance measure for MCMC transition operators isn't entirely novel. Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work, notably failing to cite Radford Neal’s Tempered Transitions [Neal, 1996], which shares conceptual similarities with BDMC and its acceptance ratio interpretation. The authors also under-discuss the crucial choice of the annealing schedule (β_t), and the effect of the step size (Δβ) on the tightness of the bounds – a more systematic exploration of this parameter would strengthen the analysis. 

The clarity of the presentation is a significant concern. Sections 3.1–3.2 are particularly rushed and the writing quality suffers, introducing ambiguities. For example, the definition of the vector *v* is vague and potentially confusing given the two AIS sweeps involved. There’s also inconsistency in notation (e.g., “rev”/“fwd” vs “for”/“back”). Crucially, the estimator \hat{p}(y) appearing in equations (6), (9), (10) and (11) is never defined, hindering a clear understanding of the derivations. Equation (6) appears incorrect as written, and the meaning of “AutoBDMC” remains ambiguous – is it a convergence assessment or the hyperparameter fitting scheme from Section 3.2?

The experiments, while valuable, lack sufficient detail.  The method for choosing β_t in practice isn’t clearly stated, and it’s unclear whether the “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” in the figures refers to the number of steps *used with* BDMC. Acronyms like RAIS are used without definition in figure captions, and the figures themselves suffer from overly small axes and tick labels. Finally, there are minor typos (e.g., a missing closing parenthesis in the Jeffreys divergence expression and a misspelling of Hoffman in the Stan citation).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a practical and potentially valuable tool for assessing MCMC inference quality, especially in probabilistic programming.
*   Demonstrates successful implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan).
*   Shows the method can inform modeling decisions (e.g., variable collapsing).
*   Uncovered a bug in WebPPL, highlighting the utility of the approach for software verification.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Technical contributions are incremental and lack significant novelty.
*   Presentation is unclear, with rushed sections, ambiguous notation, and undefined terms.
*   Related work discussion is incomplete, missing key references (e.g., Neal, 1996).
*   Experiments lack sufficient detail and thoroughness, particularly regarding the annealing schedule.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a worthwhile idea with practical potential. However, its current form is hampered by clarity issues, a lack of thoroughness in related work discussion and experimentation, and modest technical novelty. I believe that with significant revisions to address these weaknesses – particularly improving clarity, expanding the related work section, and providing more detailed experimental analysis – this paper could be acceptable for NIPS. As it stands, I lean towards **reject** but would be open to reconsidering with a major revision. The authors should prioritize clarity and completeness in a revised manuscript.
