This paper proposes a novel method for assessing the reliability of MCMC inference, specifically for samplers like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) and Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea is to extend the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, previously used for estimating log marginal likelihoods, into a diagnostic tool. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood produced by BDMC serves as an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior sample distribution and the true posterior. The proposed criterion for comparing MCMC schemes is that for a fixed computational budget, the one yielding the tightest bounds (smallest gap) is superior.

A key challenge in applying BDMC is that the reverse procedure, which generates the stochastic upper bound, requires an exact sample from the target posterior distribution. This is generally infeasible for real-world data, as obtaining such a sample is the original inference problem. To circumvent this, the authors propose a protocol they call BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing): first, they run an initial MCMC on the real data to obtain a point estimate for the model's hyperparameters. Then, they generate a synthetic dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters. The BDMC diagnostic is then run on this synthetic data, for which an approximate posterior sample for initializing the reverse chain can be more easily obtained. The paper demonstrates this approach by integrating it into the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages and uses it to compare different model representations for matrix factorization.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Grounding:** The paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of MCMC convergence diagnostics. The proposed approach is a creative and welcome attempt to move beyond heuristics towards a more theoretically principled tool. The connection derived between the BDMC bound gap and the Jeffreys divergence is an elegant and interesting theoretical contribution.

2.  **Potential for Practical Application:** The authors have implemented their method in two prominent probabilistic programming languages, Stan and WebPPL. The experiments showing its use for comparing model parameterizations and even for debugging a sampler implementation highlight the potential practical value of such a tool for both practitioners and developers of inference systems.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this work lies in the set of strong, unverified assumptions required to make the method applicable to real-world data. These assumptions appear to circumvent the very challenges that a convergence diagnostic is supposed to solve, casting doubt on the reliability of the proposed BREAD protocol.

1.  **Circular Reliance on a Converged MCMC Run:** The protocol begins by performing approximate posterior inference on the real dataset to obtain an estimated hyperparameter, `η_real`. This step implicitly assumes that this initial MCMC run has already converged and that a single point estimate is a sufficient summary of the hyperparameter posterior. This is a form of circular reasoning: a reliable diagnostic should not depend on the prior success of the very method it is meant to diagnose. If the initial run failed to converge or if the posterior over hyperparameters is multimodal or diffuse, `η_real` could be unrepresentative, invalidating the entire subsequent analysis.

2.  **Unjustified Assumption of Similar Posterior Geometries:** The method relies on the crucial assumption that the MCMC sampler's convergence behavior on the synthetic dataset is representative of its behavior on the original, real-world dataset. This is a very strong claim that is not theoretically or empirically justified. In practice, real-world data often arises from a process that is not perfectly captured by the model (i.e., model misspecification). The resulting posterior given real data can have a much more complex geometry (e.g., sharp ridges, multiple modes, funnels) than the posterior from "clean" data generated by the model itself. A sampler could therefore perform well on the synthetic problem, leading to a tight diagnostic bound, while still failing to explore the more challenging true posterior.

3.  **Optimistic Initialization of the Reverse Chain:** The protocol for initializing the reverse chain on the synthetic data—starting from `η_real` and running a few MCMC steps—is another optimistic and unverified step. It assumes that this short run is sufficient to produce a sample that is a good proxy for an exact posterior sample. However, if the MCMC mixes slowly (the very condition we want to detect), this initial sample could be highly biased and far from the true posterior, which would compromise the validity of the stochastic upper bound and thus the entire diagnostic.

4.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments in Section 5.1 are performed on relatively simple models. While they show consistency between real and simulated data in these cases, they do not provide convincing evidence that the critical assumptions will hold in more complex, high-dimensional, or multimodal settings where reliable diagnostics are most needed.

### Qualitative Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The theoretical derivation linking the BDMC gap to Jeffreys divergence (assuming an exact posterior sample) is technically sound. However, the practical protocol proposed for real-world data is built upon a cascade of questionable assumptions. This disconnect between the core theory and the proposed application significantly undermines the technical quality and trustworthiness of the method as a reliable diagnostic.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is presented effectively, and the derivation of the main result in Section 3.1 is easy to follow.

*   **Originality:** The idea of repurposing the BDMC framework as a quantitative MCMC diagnostic is highly original. The link to Jeffreys divergence is a novel insight that distinguishes this work from prior MCMC diagnostics and builds creatively upon the work of Grosse et al. (2015).

*   **Significance:** The problem of MCMC diagnostics is of immense importance to the machine learning community. A reliable, automated tool would be a major contribution. While this paper makes a conceptually interesting proposal, the fundamental flaws in its underlying assumptions for real-data application limit its practical significance in its current form. Researchers and practitioners are unlikely to trust a diagnostic whose validity depends on the problem of interest being "easy enough" for its assumptions to hold.

### Recommendations for the Authors

The paper presents an interesting idea, but its claims of practical utility are not well-supported. To strengthen the paper, the authors should directly address the critical assumptions underpinning their BREAD protocol:
1.  Provide a more robust justification for why MCMC behavior on synthetic data should be expected to mirror that on real data, especially under potential model misspecification.
2.  Investigate the sensitivity of the diagnostic to the choice of `η_real`, particularly in cases with multimodal or broad hyperparameter posteriors.
3.  Conduct more challenging experiments on models known to have difficult posterior geometries (e.g., multimodality, funnels). A crucial addition would be a direct comparison of the BREAD diagnostic to standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin's R-hat, effective sample size) in scenarios where those diagnostics are known to either succeed or fail. This would help establish where BREAD offers a tangible advantage.
