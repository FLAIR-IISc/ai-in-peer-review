This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference algorithms, bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior and the approximate samples. The core idea leverages the recent BDMC technique [GGA15] by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa, providing an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The method is integrated into the probabilistic programming languages WebPPL and Stan, validated on several models and datasets, and used to analyze model representations.

The paper’s significance stems from the inherent difficulty in validating MCMC inference, especially within “black box” probabilistic programming systems. Existing convergence diagnostics are often heuristics and lack rigorous quantitative measures. This work provides a principled approach to assess the quality of approximate posteriors, offering users greater confidence in results and aiding researchers in developing more robust algorithms. The extension to two widely used probabilistic programming languages is particularly valuable.

A key strength of the paper is its simplicity and potential for practical impact. The technique is relatively straightforward to implement and integrate into existing inference frameworks. The demonstrated application to Stan and WebPPL suggests immediate usability for a broad range of practitioners. The observation that BDMC can be used to guide modeling decisions, illustrated with the matrix factorization example, is compelling. 

However, the technical contributions, while solid, are incremental rather than revolutionary. The core idea of using the gap between BDMC bounds to assess MCMC performance is reasonable, but its novelty is limited. The authors acknowledge related work on bounding the marginal likelihood, but omit crucial connections to earlier work, such as Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions [Neal96], which shares a conceptual link with BDMC through acceptance ratio estimation. A more thorough discussion of related approaches would strengthen the paper.

Several areas require improvement regarding clarity and completeness. Sections 3.1-3.2 feel rushed, leading to ambiguities. The notation is occasionally imprecise (e.g., the definition of ‘v’), and inconsistencies in labeling (e.g., “rev”/”fwd” vs “for”/”back”) hinder comprehension. Crucially, the estimator  \hat{p}(y) used in several equations is not defined, making the derivations difficult to follow.  Equation (6) appears incorrect as written. Furthermore, the meaning and mechanism of “AutoBDMC” are never fully explained. The choice of the annealing schedule (β_t) and the step size Δβ are under-discussed; a systematic exploration of their impact on the bounds would be beneficial, potentially with an iterative scheme to tighten the bounds.

The experimental evaluation, while validating the method, lacks sufficient detail. It is unclear how β_t is chosen in practice, and the relationship between “HMC / No-U-Turn steps” and BDMC steps is ambiguous. The figures suffer from small axes labels, reducing their readability. Several minor issues, such as typos (missing parentheses, misspelling of Hoffman) and undefined acronyms (RAIS, (u)c(R)AIS) detract from the paper's polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in Bayesian inference.
*   Provides a principled and relatively simple approach for evaluating MCMC algorithms.
*   Demonstrates practical application within popular probabilistic programming languages.
*   Offers potential for guiding model development and debugging inference systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Technical contributions are incremental and lack significant novelty.
*   Omission of relevant related work (e.g., Neal’s Tempered Transitions).
*   Clarity and completeness issues in the technical sections.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup and presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the technical novelty is limited, the practical implications and potential impact are substantial. However, the paper requires significant revisions to address the clarity, completeness, and related work issues. If the authors address these concerns – particularly by expanding the related work discussion, clarifying the technical details, and providing a more thorough experimental analysis – the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, it is borderline acceptable, leaning towards rejection, pending substantial revisions.
