This paper proposes AutoBDMC, a method leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – specifically annealed importance sampling (AIS) and its reverse – to assess MCMC convergence by providing stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. A key contribution is demonstrating that the gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution, offering a single scalar convergence metric. While limited to analysis on synthetic data due to the requirement of exact posterior samples, the authors persuasively show that analyses performed on synthetic data generated from hyperparameters fit to real data predict real-data behavior. The method is demonstrated for model selection and debugging, and the paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a useful method supported by reasonably compelling experiments.

This work builds upon the BDMC framework introduced by Grosse et al. (2015) for estimating log marginal likelihoods and extends it to the assessment of posterior sample quality. It relates to a broader body of work on MCMC convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996; Gelman & Rubin, 1992) but distinguishes itself by offering quantifiable bounds on divergence rather than relying on heuristic checks. Recent concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) also leverages BDMC, differing in the reference distribution used for the reverse chain. The paper also connects to literature on tempering and thermodynamic integration (Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997) which provide related bounds on log-ratios of partition functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to directly bound the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior and the true posterior is a valuable contribution.  The derived relationship between the bound gap and the divergence is particularly significant.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, with clear derivations and justifications for the proposed method.
* **Practical Relevance:** Providing a quantifiable convergence metric is highly desirable for MCMC practitioners, especially within probabilistic programming systems.  The demonstrated applications to model selection and debugging further enhance practicality.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's ability to predict real-data behavior from synthetic data analyses, addressing a crucial limitation of requiring exact posterior samples.
* **Implementation & Impact:** Integrating BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and uncovering a bug in WebPPL, highlights the practical value of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Section 3):**  Section 3, particularly the transition into 3.1, is challenging to follow. New variables and equations are introduced without sufficient context or motivation. The logical flow could be improved with more explicit explanations of the reasoning behind each step.
* **Explicit Definition of AutoBDMC:** The term “AutoBDMC” is used throughout the paper without being explicitly defined. It’s crucial to clearly state what AutoBDMC represents – the use of AIS and rAIS for MCMC analysis – to avoid ambiguity.
* **Premature Reference to AutoBDMC:** Section 3.2 refers to challenges “for AutoBDMC” before the method is clearly defined, exacerbating the issue above.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** A potential typo exists on Line 149 (p(theta, z | y) vs p(theta, z, y)). The notation for data is inconsistent (y vs D).
* **Software Release:** The supplemental WebPPL code lacks stated compatible WebPPL versions and clear integration instructions. Ideally, it should be released as an npm-installable WebPPL package. Similarly, the Stan code should be readily available for use.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for assessing MCMC convergence.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.
* The integration into WebPPL and Stan, along with the bug discovery, underscores the method’s utility.
* The paper is generally well-written and addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity issues in Section 3 hinder understanding and detract from the paper’s overall readability.
* The lack of a precise definition of “AutoBDMC” and premature references to it are distracting.
* The software release details are insufficient for easy adoption by the research community.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant impact on the field of Bayesian inference.  The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and experimentally validated.  However, addressing the clarity issues in Section 3, providing a formal definition of AutoBDMC, and improving the software release would significantly strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon these revisions.
