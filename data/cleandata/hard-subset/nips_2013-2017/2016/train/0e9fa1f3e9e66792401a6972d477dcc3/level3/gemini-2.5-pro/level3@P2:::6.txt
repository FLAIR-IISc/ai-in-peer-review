This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of assessing the quality of samples from Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference algorithms. The authors propose a method called Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing (BREAD), which extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015). The core idea is to use annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains run in both a forward direction (from prior to posterior) and a reverse direction (from posterior to prior). This process yields stochastic lower and upper bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The main theoretical contribution of this work is to show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the distribution of the approximate samples (generated by the forward AIS chain) and the true posterior distribution.

Since the reverse chain requires an exact sample from the posterior, the method's theoretical guarantees are limited to simulated data where such samples can be generated. To bridge the gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a protocol: first, run approximate inference on the real dataset to estimate hyperparameters; second, generate a simulated dataset from the model using these estimated hyperparameters; and finally, apply BREAD to this simulated dataset to diagnose inference quality. The authors implement BREAD within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate the protocol and to compare the performance of different model representations for a matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation that connects the BDMC gap to an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is correct. The experimental work is a significant strength of the paper. The authors conduct a careful validation study across five different models to show that inference behavior on data simulated via their protocol is consistent with behavior on real data. The application of BREAD to compare collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a matrix factorization model is a compelling use case that demonstrates the practical utility of the method for guiding modeling decisions. Furthermore, the fact that the method helped uncover a bug in the WebPPL library is a strong piece of evidence for its usefulness as a debugging and validation tool for inference systems.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It provides sufficient background on AIS and BDMC for the reader to understand the proposed method. The motivation is strong, and the description of the experimental protocol and results is easy to follow.

**Originality:** This is the main weakness of the paper. The work is presented as an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo [GGA15], and indeed, the core technical machinery is taken directly from this prior work. The primary novel contribution is the refinement of the analysis in [GGA15] to show that the expected gap between the bounds relates to the Jeffreys divergence. While this is a useful insight, it is a relatively incremental theoretical step. The majority of the paper's contribution lies in the application and implementation of this known technique in the context of probabilistic programming, rather than in the development of fundamentally new methodology.

**Significance:** The problem of MCMC diagnostics is of high importance to the NIPS community, especially with the rising popularity of "black-box" inference in probabilistic programming. A tool that provides quantitative, rigorous guarantees on sample quality would be a significant contribution. The BREAD framework is a step in this direction, and its implementation in popular languages like Stan and WebPPL increases its potential impact. However, the significance of the contribution is somewhat limited by two factors. First, the limited originality means the paper is more of a demonstration of a powerful application than a new research advance. Second, the reliance on a heuristic protocol for real-world data (where rigorous diagnostics are most needed) means the method's guarantees do not extend to the setting of most practical interest, although the authors' validation is encouraging.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and practical problem in modern machine learning: the need for reliable MCMC diagnostics.
*   The implementation and thoughtful experimental validation in two popular probabilistic programming systems (Stan and WebPPL) is a valuable contribution to the community.
*   The paper provides compelling use cases, such as comparing model representations and debugging inference code, which clearly demonstrate the method's utility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The methodological novelty is very limited. The core ideas are almost entirely from prior work on Bidirectional Monte Carlo [GGA15], with the main contribution being a minor, albeit useful, extension of the existing analysis.
*   The paper's contribution feels more like a strong systems/applications paper than a novel research paper, consisting primarily of implementing a known technique.
*   The rigorous guarantees only hold for simulated data, which limits the method's direct applicability for users interested in analyzing a specific real-world dataset.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality application of an existing technique to the important problem of MCMC diagnostics. The implementation and experiments are well-executed and demonstrate clear value. However, the work contains very little in the way of novel methodology. While the application is compelling, the paper reads more like a report on the implementation of known ideas than a new research contribution suitable for NIPS. This work may be better suited for a venue focused more on probabilistic programming systems or an MCMC-themed workshop.
