- Introduces BREAD, a method using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and MCMC sample distributions.  
- Leverages bidirectional AIS/SMC (forward and reverse chains) on simulated data to produce an upper-bound estimator.  
- Applicable to MCMC algorithms that admit an importance sampling interpretation, especially AIS and SMC.  
- Integrated implementations in WebPPL and Stan to demonstrate practicality and usability.  
- Demonstrated utility on several models/datasets, including guiding modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and discovering a WebPPL bug.  
- Well-motivated contribution addressing the challenge of reliably assessing MCMC sample quality beyond standard diagnostics.  
- Builds on BDMC and situates clearly within existing MCMC convergence and automatic configuration literature, noting related independent work.  
- Technical derivation of the estimator is rigorous and mathematically sound.  
- Novel application of BDMC to directly quantify MCMC accuracy, with a clever protocol using simulated data and fitted hyperparameters.  
- Practical relevance enhanced by integration into popular probabilistic programming languages.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation showing robustness across examples.  
- Paper is clearly written and reproducible.  
- Main weakness: method requires simulated data, so applicability to real-world data depends on quality of hyperparameter estimation and simulation fidelity.  
- Main weakness: running AIS in both directions is computationally expensive, limiting scalability to very large models.  
- Main weakness: potential sensitivity to reverse-chain initialization, though empirical validation suggests limited impact.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important problem with a novel, theoretically sound, and practical approach; strong experiments and careful discussion of limitations.  
- Rejection arguments: reliance on simulated-data assumptions and computational cost may limit generalizability and applicability.  
- Overall assessment: strong, impactful paper with minor limitations; recommended for acceptance.
