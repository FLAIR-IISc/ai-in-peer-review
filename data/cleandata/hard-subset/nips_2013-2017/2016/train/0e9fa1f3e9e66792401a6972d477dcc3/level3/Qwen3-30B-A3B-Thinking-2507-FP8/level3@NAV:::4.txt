

This paper proposes a method for assessing MCMC convergence using bidirectional Monte Carlo (AIS and reverse AIS) to provide stochastic upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood, which the authors term "AutoBDMC" (though the term is never explicitly defined). The key contribution is demonstrating that the gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and approximate sample distribution, yielding a single scalar convergence metric. The authors validate their approach on synthetic data, showing that synthetic-data analyses predict real-data behavior, and demonstrate applications for model selection and debugging within WebPPL and Stan.

The paper is well-motivated and presents a useful method with compelling experiments. The authors make a strong case for the practical utility of their approach in probabilistic programming contexts, where reliable convergence diagnostics are particularly challenging. The demonstration of how the method can identify optimal model representations (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) provides valuable insights for practitioners.

However, significant clarity issues undermine the paper's impact. The term "AutoBDMC" is never explicitly defined, and the paper refers to "challenges for AutoBDMC" before defining it. Section 3.1, which contains the central theoretical contribution, is difficult to follow due to insufficient motivation for variable definitions and the lack of explicit statement regarding the novelty of the divergence bound. The manuscript also contains minor issues, including a potential typo on Line 149 and inconsistent notation for data (y vs D).

While the paper's limitation to synthetic data is acknowledged, the authors' protocol for validating on real-world data is reasonably well-justified. The supplemental code should be more clearly documented and released as npm-installable packages for WebPPL and Stan.

The work represents a meaningful contribution to MCMC diagnostics, with clear potential to advance the field. However, the clarity issues and lack of explicit novelty statement for the key theoretical contribution prevent it from being as strong as it could be. The paper would benefit from substantial revisions to improve exposition before publication.
