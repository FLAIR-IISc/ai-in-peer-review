

# Review: "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique of Grosse et al. (2015) to create stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, which they then use to bound the Jeffreys divergence. The key insight is that when exact posterior samples are available (e.g., from synthetic data), BDMC produces rigorous bounds on inference quality. The authors address the practical challenge of applying this to real-world data by proposing a protocol: infer hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from these hyperparameters, and evaluate inference on the simulated data.

The paper builds on several strands of prior work. It extends the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015) for marginal likelihood estimation to directly evaluate posterior approximation quality. It also relates to convergence diagnostics (Brooks & Gelman, 1998; Cowles & Carlin, 1996) and automatic MCMC tuning methods (Roberts & Rosenthal, 2001), while distinguishing itself by providing theoretically justified bounds rather than heuristic diagnostics. The work also connects to independent concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016), which similarly leverages BDMC for inference evaluation but uses a different reference distribution for the reverse chain.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically grounded contribution to the challenging problem of MCMC reliability assessment. The derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence (Section 3.1) is mathematically sound and provides a novel perspective on using BDMC for inference quality assessment. The implementation in WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical applicability, and the empirical validation on multiple models (Section 5) provides reasonable evidence of the method's utility. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL through BREAD application is a compelling real-world demonstration of the method's value.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weakness lies in its critical assumptions about the synthetic data protocol (Section 3.2), which undermine the validity of the diagnostic when they fail:

1. The method assumes a known representative hyperparameter value η_real from prior MCMC runs, presuming those runs already converged. This is circular since the method is meant to diagnose convergence.

2. It assumes the synthetic-data posterior has similar MCMC convergence properties to the original posterior, which is unjustified, especially under model misspecification (as noted in Appendix D).

3. It assumes starting from η_real lets MCMC quickly produce an almost exact sample, an optimistic and unverified assumption. The paper shows in Section 5.1.2 that this approximation has negligible impact on the upper bound, but this only validates the specific implementation, not the underlying assumption.

The empirical evidence in Section 5.1 is limited to simple examples where the assumptions are likely to hold. The paper does not provide convincing evidence that the method would work in more complex scenarios, especially those with multimodality or poor summary statistics. The authors do not address how their method would handle cases where the synthetic-data posterior differs substantially from the real-data posterior.

**Quality:** The theoretical development is sound, but the practical implementation relies on assumptions that directly relate to the convergence problem the method aims to solve. The paper acknowledges these limitations but does not sufficiently address how to validate the assumptions in real-world settings.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from theoretical development to implementation and validation. The mathematical derivations are precise, and the experimental setup is well-described.

**Originality:** The paper extends BDMC to directly bound inference quality, which is novel. While the underlying BDMC technique is from Grosse et al. (2015), the application to MCMC reliability assessment is new. The connection to probabilistic programming languages is also a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The method offers a theoretically justified approach to MCMC diagnostics, which is a valuable contribution to the field. However, the practical utility is limited by the critical assumptions about synthetic data, which may not hold in many real-world applications. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how BREAD would outperform standard diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size) in challenging scenarios.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically sound framework for bounding inference quality
- Extends BDMC to a novel application domain
- Demonstrates practical implementation in two probabilistic programming languages
- Has potential value for debugging inference implementations

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Critical assumptions about synthetic data make the diagnostic unreliable in many practical settings
- Empirical validation is limited to simple examples that don't test the method's core limitations
- Does not adequately address how to validate the key assumptions in real-world applications
- The proposed protocol for real-world data assumes the very convergence problem it aims to diagnose

**Recommendation:** While the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution, the critical assumptions underlying the real-data protocol significantly undermine its practical value. The authors should provide more convincing evidence that the synthetic data protocol works in complex scenarios, address how to validate their key assumptions, and directly compare their diagnostic to standard methods. Without these improvements, the paper's claims about reliably assessing MCMC inference quality are overstated.

The paper is technically sound but the practical limitations are too severe for this to be considered a strong contribution to the field. I recommend rejection.
