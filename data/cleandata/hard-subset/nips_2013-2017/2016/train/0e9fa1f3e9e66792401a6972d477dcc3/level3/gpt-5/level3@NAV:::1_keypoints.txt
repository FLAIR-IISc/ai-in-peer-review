- Contribution: Proposes BREAD, using bidirectional Monte Carlo with forward and reverse AIS to upper-bound (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between MCMC approximations and the true posterior for PPL debugging/evaluation.  
- Method: Leverages simulated data to obtain exact posterior draws for reverse AIS and integrates implementations with WebPPL and Stan.  
- Experiments: Demonstrated on multiple models, including a collapsed vs. uncollapsed representation study and a real-data protocol via fitted hyperparameters.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on BDMC for marginal likelihood (Grosse et al. ’15), AIS/SMC estimators (Neal ’01; Del Moral et al. ’06), and sample-quality measures (Gorham & Mackey ’15); closely related to concurrent “subjective divergence” (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka ’16).  
- Missing citations: Should discuss earlier validation-by-simulation diagnostics (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006).  
- Theoretical quality: Core derivation that the forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds expected Jeffreys divergence is sound and useful.  
- Presentation issues: Too many essential details deferred to the supplement; Section 3.1 is hard to follow and uses q(.) before defining it.  
- Notation fixes: Explicitly define q(x_T) as the marginal of forward AIS after T steps and state q(v|x_T); consider combining z and θ into a single block to reduce clutter.  
- Empirical calibration: “In expectation” nature and potential high variance need clearer calibration beyond toy cases—recommend reporting B vs. J across more synthetic targets and comparisons to ESS/R̂.  
- Clarity improvements: Provide step-by-step derivation of Eqs. (6–12) in the main text with clear notation; fix minor typos (e.g., missing parenthesis at line 165).  
- Originality: Novel extension of BDMC to bound posterior sample quality, timely and appropriate for PPLs; relation to subjective divergence noted but Geweke/Cook et al. should be added.  
- Significance: High practical impact—reliable quantitative evaluation of MCMC in black-box settings and actionable debugging via WebPPL/Stan integration.  
- Pros: Conceptually elegant and practically useful bound; clear PPL integrations; insightful representation comparison (collapsed vs. uncollapsed).  
- Cons: Heavy reliance on simulated-data setting; real-data protocol is heuristic; main derivations not in paper and limited calibration beyond small toy examples.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on improving clarity, moving key derivations into the main text, and addressing related-work omissions.
