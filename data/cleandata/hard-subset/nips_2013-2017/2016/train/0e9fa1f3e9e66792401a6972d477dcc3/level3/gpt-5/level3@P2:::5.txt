Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) [Grosse et al., 2015] to evaluate the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by AIS/SMC-type algorithms. The key theoretical contribution shows that the gap between forward and reverse AIS log normalizing constant estimates, ˆB = log ˆR−log ˆRrev, upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the target posterior p and the AIS sampling distribution q. The authors call the resulting procedure BREAD, and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan. Because BDMC requires an exact posterior sample to run reverse AIS, the paper proposes a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from those fitted hyperparameters, then use an approximate posterior sample (obtained by short MCMC) to initialize the reverse chain. The empirical section validates several aspects on five standard models (linear/logistic regression, matrix factorization, AR time series, Gaussian mixtures), and demonstrates how BREAD can guide modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed factorization) and even help debug a probabilistic programming system. Related work discussed includes classical MCMC diagnostics [Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Cowles and Carlin, 1996; Brooks and Gelman, 1998], AIS/SMC [Neal, 2001; Del Moral et al., 2006], thermodynamic integration and nonequilibrium work [Neal, 1996; Jarzynski, 1997], Stein discrepancies [Gorham and Mackey, 2015], and closely related “subjective divergence” work [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016].

Strengths
- Clear and principled extension of BDMC: the in-expectation upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is simple, well-motivated, and technically sound given AIS assumptions.
- Practical integration into two systems (WebPPL and Stan), with useful case studies. The collapsed/uncollapsed matrix factorization comparison is insightful and the debugging example is compelling.
- Thoughtful attention to the “simulated vs real data” gap, including a concrete protocol and empirical checks (e.g., varying the number of reverse-chain warmup steps S).
- The work highlights an underexplored but important need: rigorous, quantitative evaluation of black-box inference in PPLs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on simulated data and exact posterior samples: the rigorous guarantee applies only when a true posterior sample is available (i.e., simulated data). For real data, the bounds become heuristic.
- The key diagnostic quantity ˆB only upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation. Thus, per-run outputs are not guaranteed upper bounds, and the manuscript should be clearer about how to report uncertainty and how many chains are needed to make the estimate reliable.
- The real-data protocol hinges on strong assumptions: (i) fitted hyperparameters η_real are representative of the true hyperparameters; (ii) simulated data at η_real has similar posterior geometry and mixing as the real data (which may fail under misspecification); (iii) a small number of MCMC steps from (η_real, θ) yields a “nearly exact” posterior sample. Section 5.1 provides some support on five models, but the assumptions remain optimistic and unverified in harder, multimodal, or heavy-tailed settings.
- Scope: the method directly evaluates AIS/SMC-style samplers; it does not directly measure the reliability of a standalone MCMC method (e.g., NUTS) unless embedded as kernels within AIS. The paper motivates that design choices transfer, but this is only indirectly supported.
- Empirical evaluation is limited relative to the ambition. The toy validations in Appendix B and the five Stan examples in Section 5.1 are useful but do not probe challenging failure modes (e.g., strong multimodality, severe misspecification, high-dimensional funnel/pathologies).

Quality
The theoretical derivations are careful and correct as far as I can tell; the connection to Jeffreys divergence and the expectation bound are clearly explained. The stochastic bound properties on log Z ratios are appropriately cited. The practical protocol is reasonable but rests on assumptions that directly relate to the original convergence problem. Experimental design is sensible for a first pass, but does not yet establish robustness in hard cases or provide strong guidance about when the heuristic real-data protocol can be trusted.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. A few suggestions:
- Be explicit and prominent about “in expectation” vs “per-run” guarantees for DJ and what this implies for practice (confidence intervals over ˆB, number of chains, when to trust a reported gap).
- Provide a concise algorithmic description (pseudocode) of BREAD on real data, including defaults for S, number of AIS steps/particles, and diagnostics to detect protocol failure.
- Clarify how AIS kernels are implemented in Stan and WebPPL (e.g., NUTS/HMC at intermediate temperatures), and the computational overhead.

Originality
Moderate. Extending BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence for sample quality is a natural and useful idea, and integrating it into PPLs is practically valuable. The overlap with [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016] should be discussed more deeply; please sharpen the distinctions and implications when using non-exact reference distributions.

Significance
Potentially significant for the PPL community as a rigorous tool for simulated-data benchmarking, model representation choices, and regression testing. As a general-purpose real-data diagnostic, current assumptions and limited evidence reduce immediate impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please evaluate BREAD on deliberately challenging posteriors (multimodal mixtures with well-separated modes, hierarchical funnels, heavy tails), including misspecified models, and report how often the real-data protocol’s conclusions align with simulated-data conclusions.
- Compare against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS per parameter/statistic, Geweke/Brooks-Gelman) and Stein discrepancies [Gorham and Mackey, 2015]. Show when BREAD detects failures those miss, and vice versa.
- Quantify sensitivity to the choice of η_real and to the number of reverse-chain steps S beyond the five models, and propose automated checks (e.g., multiple η_real draws or bootstraps).
- Report computational cost vs diagnostic tightness: time-normalized curves, number of AIS steps/particles needed, and how to set annealing schedules robustly.
- When ground truth is available (small models), systematically compare ˆB to the true DJ to calibrate conservativeness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with clear connection to sample quality.
- Practical integration and promising case studies; useful for PPL development and debugging.
- Addresses an important problem with a principled approach.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees are limited to simulated data and expectation bounds; real-data protocol rests on unverified assumptions.
- Empirical evidence does not yet demonstrate robustness on challenging, realistic problems or clear advantages over established diagnostics.
- Scope limited to AIS/SMC; indirect applicability to common MCMC workflows.

Overall recommendation
Leaning reject. The paper is thoughtful and promising, but requires stronger empirical validation (especially on difficult posteriors), clearer guidance for real-data use, and comparisons to standard diagnostics to substantiate its practical value. I encourage resubmission after addressing these points.
