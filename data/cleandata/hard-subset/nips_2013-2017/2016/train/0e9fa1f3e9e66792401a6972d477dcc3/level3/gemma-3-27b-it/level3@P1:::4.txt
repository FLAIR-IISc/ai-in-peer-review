This paper introduces a method, termed BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), extending Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)-based posterior inference. The core idea is to use Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) both forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse to obtain stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. The gap between these bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution, offering a quantifiable measure of convergence and accuracy. While directly applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC, the authors argue the method provides insights applicable to broader MCMC techniques. They demonstrate the approach by integrating it into the WebPPL and Stan probabilistic programming languages and use it to analyze the impact of model representation choices.

The work builds upon the recent BDMC framework [GGA15] and addresses the critical challenge of reliably assessing MCMC convergence, a long-standing problem in probabilistic inference. Numerous heuristics exist, but robust quantitative measures are lacking, particularly in “black-box” inference systems. This paper directly tackles this issue by offering a principled, albeit computationally intensive, way to quantify the discrepancy between approximate posterior samples and the true distribution. The authors also acknowledge and address the limitation that rigorous bounds are only achievable with simulated data, proposing a protocol for applying the method to real-world datasets via hyperparameter estimation and data simulation.  Related work, such as convergence diagnostics [CC96, GR92, BG98] and automatic MCMC configuration [RR01], are discussed, clearly positioning this contribution within the broader landscape of inference validation. The paper also correctly identifies related, independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka [CTM16] using a similar BDMC-based approach.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The technical contributions – deriving the estimator for bounding Jeffreys divergence and implementing it in widely used probabilistic programming systems – are valuable. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the method can not only assess convergence but also guide modeling decisions, as shown by the matrix factorization experiment. The inclusion of both WebPPL and Stan increases the practical impact and accessibility of the work. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and the experimental results are compelling. The validation experiments, demonstrating the consistency of results between simulated and real data, are particularly important. Further, the unexpected discovery and debugging of a bug in WebPPL underscores the utility of BREAD as a testing tool. 

However, the paper also exhibits some weaknesses. Section 3, particularly the transition into Section 3.1, is challenging to follow. The introduction of variables and equations lacks sufficient context and motivation, making it difficult for the reader to fully grasp the derivation. The novelty of the divergence bound in Section 3.1 isn’t explicitly highlighted, which is a significant oversight considering it’s a central contribution. The term "AutoBDMC" is used without clear definition. While it appears to refer to the application of BDMC to MCMC analysis, the authors should state this explicitly. The repeated reference to “AutoBDMC” before defining it feels awkward. There are also minor inconsistencies in notation (using both 'y' and 'D' for data) and a potential typo in line 149.  Finally, the supplemental materials (WebPPL and Stan code) require greater clarity regarding compatible versions, integration instructions, and ideally, package-based distribution for ease of reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to assessing MCMC convergence.
*   Provides a quantifiable measure of the difference between approximate and true posteriors.
*   Integration into widely used probabilistic programming systems (WebPPL and Stan).
*   Demonstrated utility in guiding modeling choices and debugging software.
*   Clear writing and strong experimental validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Section 3 is difficult to follow due to unclear presentation and lack of motivation.
*   Novelty of the divergence bound isn’t clearly emphasized.
*   Lack of a precise definition of “AutoBDMC.”
*   Minor inconsistencies and a potential typo.
*   Supplemental materials need improvement for reproducibility.



Overall, despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be practically useful. The authors have identified and addressed potential limitations, and the experimental results are convincing. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the clarity issues in Section 3 and providing improved supplemental materials.
