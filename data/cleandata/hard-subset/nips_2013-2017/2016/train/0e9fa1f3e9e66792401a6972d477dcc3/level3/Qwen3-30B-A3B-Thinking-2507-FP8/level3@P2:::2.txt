

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for rigorously evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples produced by MCMC-based inference algorithms. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework from Grosse et al. (2015) to provide an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples. This represents a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference, addressing a critical challenge in MCMC: the difficulty of measuring the quality of approximate posterior samples, particularly in black-box inference methods.

The paper builds on BDMC, which was originally developed to estimate log marginal likelihood by sandwiching the true value between stochastic upper and lower bounds. The authors refine this analysis to derive a new estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples. They demonstrate this technique on simulated data where exact posterior samples are available, and present a protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating data from inferred hyperparameters. The method is integrated into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validated on multiple models including linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, and mixture-of-Gaussians.

The paper's strengths are substantial. Methodologically, it provides a technically sound derivation of the upper bound on Jeffreys divergence, with careful theoretical analysis that extends the work of Grosse et al. (2015). The experimental validation is thorough, with the authors not only demonstrating the accuracy of their bound on toy distributions but also showing how BREAD can be used to guide model representation choices in probabilistic programming. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to real-world inference problems. The paper also demonstrates practical utility by uncovering a bug in WebPPL's implementation of multivariate Gaussian sampling through the method's diagnostic capabilities.

In terms of originality, while BDMC was introduced in Grosse et al. (2015), the application to MCMC inference quality evaluation represents a novel and significant extension. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work, particularly Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) who proposed a similar but different approach using "subjective divergence." The authors' method provides mathematically guaranteed upper bounds on the Jeffreys divergence, whereas the subjective divergence approach has less clear guarantees when the reference distribution isn't the true posterior.

The significance of this work is high. The paper directly addresses a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference: how to measure the reliability of MCMC-based posterior samples. This is particularly important for probabilistic programming systems, where users need to trust the results of black-box inference. The authors demonstrate that BREAD can provide valuable insights into model representation choices, potentially improving inference efficiency across different probabilistic programming frameworks. The method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality, which aligns well with NIPS' focus on impactful contributions to machine learning.

The paper is well-written and clear, with good organization and sufficient detail to understand the method and its implementation. The experimental section is particularly strong, with careful validation of the method's protocol for real-world data and compelling examples of how BREAD can guide modeling decisions. The only minor weakness is that the paper could provide slightly more detail on the implementation challenges of integrating BREAD into Stan and WebPPL, though the provided examples are sufficient for understanding.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a fundamental, important problem in probabilistic inference
- Provides theoretically sound, rigorous bounds on inference quality
- Demonstrates practical utility through integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks
- Offers valuable insights for model representation choices
- Includes thorough experimental validation

Arguments against acceptance:
- The method is limited to cases where exact posterior samples can be obtained (simulated data), though the authors provide a reasonable protocol for real-world data
- The focus is primarily on AIS-based inference, though the authors convincingly argue that insights generalize to MCMC more broadly

Overall, this is a well-executed, high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The method is technically sound, the experiments are thorough, and the significance of the work is clear. The paper represents a meaningful advance in the ability to measure and improve the reliability of MCMC inference, which has broad implications for practitioners using probabilistic programming systems. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their approach through both theoretical analysis and practical application.

I recommend acceptance.
