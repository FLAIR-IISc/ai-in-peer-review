This paper proposes a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for evaluating the quality of samples from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The method is a direct application of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework introduced by Grosse et al. (2015). The core idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains in both a "forward" direction (from prior to posterior) and a "reverse" direction (from posterior to prior). The forward chain provides a stochastic lower bound on the log marginal likelihood, while the reverse chain provides a stochastic upper bound. The authors show that the expected gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution generated by AIS and the true posterior. Since the reverse chain requires an exact posterior sample, the method's rigorous guarantees apply only to simulated data where such a sample can be generated. The authors implement BREAD in two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and use it to validate inference and compare the performance of different model representations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence and quantifying the quality of approximate posterior samples is of fundamental importance, particularly for users of probabilistic programming languages (PPLs) who may treat inference as a black box. Existing diagnostics are often heuristic and cannot confirm convergence. This paper offers a principled, quantitative measure of sample quality (the Jeffreys divergence) which is a significant step forward from qualitative diagnostics.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a clear and concise background on AIS and BDMC before presenting the BREAD method. The derivations in Section 3.1 are easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors provide a solid empirical evaluation, including validating the tightness of their bound on toy examples (Appendix B) and carefully assessing the proposed protocol for real-world data (Section 5.1). The implementation in two distinct and widely-used PPLs (Stan and WebPPL) is a valuable engineering contribution. The use cases presented—comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations and identifying a bug in WebPPL—are compelling demonstrations of the method's practical utility for both PPL users and developers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of methodological novelty. The core technique, bidirectional Monte Carlo using forward and reverse AIS chains to bound the marginal likelihood, was published by Grosse et al. at NIPS 2015. The main theoretical extension in this paper—showing that the expected gap between the bounds also bounds the Jeffreys divergence—is a straightforward and incremental extension of the analysis already present in the original BDMC paper (as acknowledged in Section 3.1). The work is therefore primarily an application and implementation of a known technique rather than the introduction of a new one. The paper is also very similar in spirit to the concurrent work of Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), which it cites.
*   **Scope & Venue:** Given the limited originality, the contribution feels more aligned with a specialized workshop on MCMC methods or PPLs, or a journal focused on computational statistics and software, rather than a top-tier machine learning conference like NIPS that prioritizes novel algorithmic and theoretical contributions. The paper's strength lies in demonstrating the utility of an existing tool within specific software ecosystems, which is valuable but may not meet the bar for a NIPS publication.
*   **Practical Limitations:** The method's rigorous guarantees are confined to simulated data, as it requires an exact sample from the true posterior to initialize the reverse chain. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a heuristic that relies on initializing the reverse chain from an approximate posterior sample, which breaks the formal upper-bound guarantee. While the authors present empirical evidence that this heuristic works well in their experiments, it remains an approximation and a key limitation for real-world applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a highly significant and challenging problem in applied machine learning: how to trust the output of black-box MCMC inference.
2.  Provides a practical tool that is demonstrated to be useful for important tasks like model selection (choosing a representation) and software debugging.
3.  The implementations in Stan and WebPPL are non-trivial and represent a valuable contribution to those communities.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The work contains very little methodological novelty. The core ideas are almost entirely drawn from the NIPS 2015 paper on BDMC.
2.  The main contribution is the application and implementation of an existing technique, which may be out of scope for NIPS. A more appropriate venue might be an MCMC or PPL workshop.
3.  The method's rigorous guarantees do not apply to real-world data, where its use relies on a heuristic that lacks theoretical justification.

In summary, this is a well-executed and clearly-written paper on an important topic. The authors demonstrate the utility of applying BDMC to the problem of evaluating MCMC sample quality. However, the lack of significant originality is a major concern. The paper essentially reports on the successful implementation and application of a previously published method, with only a minor theoretical extension. While valuable, this contribution seems better suited for a more specialized or application-focused venue.
