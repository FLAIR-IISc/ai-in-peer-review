

The paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and approximate samples. The authors build upon Grosse et al. (2015), who introduced BDMC for estimating marginal likelihoods by sandwiching them between stochastic upper and lower bounds. The key insight is that these bounds can be used to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence, providing a more direct measure of inference quality than marginal likelihood alone. The paper makes four main contributions: (1) derivation showing BDMC yields an estimator upper bounding Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) technique for exact computation of both true divergence and upper bound on small examples; (3) protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters; and (4) integration into WebPPL and Stan with validation on multiple models.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical contributions, with appropriate connections to related work, particularly noting the relationship with Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016). The implementation in two popular probabilistic programming languages demonstrates practical relevance, and the application to model representation choices provides valuable insights for practitioners.

The quality of the work is technically sound, with a clear theoretical derivation building on Grosse et al. (2015). However, the validity of the proposed method depends on three strong assumptions that are not adequately justified: (1) a single hyperparameter value η_real from prior MCMC runs is representative of the true posterior; (2) synthetic data generated from η_real has similar MCMC convergence properties to real data; and (3) MCMC starting from η_real quickly produces good approximate posterior samples. These assumptions directly relate to the convergence problem the method aims to solve, potentially undermining its validity when the assumptions fail. The empirical validation (Section 5.1) is limited to five simple models where synthetic data is generated from the same underlying process as real data, without addressing cases of model misspecification or weakly informative priors.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized exposition and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The originality is moderate - while extending BDMC to evaluate MCMC reliability is novel, the approach is limited to AIS/SMC-based inference rather than general MCMC, significantly narrowing its applicability. The significance is limited by these constraints, as the method cannot be broadly applied to the many MCMC implementations used in practice.

The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how BREAD compares to standard convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, Brooks-Gelman) in terms of practical utility, which is a significant omission for a diagnostic tool. The authors should provide evidence that their method handles multimodality or badly conditioned posteriors better than existing approaches, as suggested in their related work discussion.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of BDMC, offers a rigorous approach to measuring MCMC quality, and provides practical insights into model representation choices for probabilistic programming.

Arguments against acceptance: The method relies on unverified assumptions that undermine its validity, has limited applicability to only AIS/SMC-based inference, and lacks convincing empirical validation in more complex scenarios. The paper does not adequately address how the method compares to standard diagnostics or handles challenging cases where the assumptions might fail.

While the problem of evaluating MCMC quality is important, the practical limitations of this specific approach are substantial. The authors should either strengthen their empirical validation to address the key assumptions or clarify the specific scenarios where their method is applicable. As presented, the paper's contributions are interesting but insufficiently validated to warrant acceptance as a general diagnostic tool for the NIPS audience.
