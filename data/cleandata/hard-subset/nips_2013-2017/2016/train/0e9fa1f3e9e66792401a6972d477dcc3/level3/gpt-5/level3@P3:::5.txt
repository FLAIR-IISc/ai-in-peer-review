Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse et al., 2015) from bounding log marginal likelihoods on simulated data to assessing the quality of posterior samples from AIS/SMC-style samplers. The key technical observation is that the gap between a stochastic lower bound (forward AIS) and a stochastic upper bound (reverse AIS initialized from an exact posterior draw) upper-bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the sampler’s output distribution and the true posterior. The authors call this application BREAD and integrate it into WebPPL and Stan, using the bounds to compare modeling choices (e.g., collapsed vs uncollapsed representations) and to debug inference implementations. They also propose a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on the real dataset, simulate data from those fitted values, and run BDMC on the synthetic posterior as a proxy for the real posterior. This connects to longstanding MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Cowles & Carlin; Brooks & Gelman), path sampling/TI and AIS (Neal), and recent sample-quality measures (Stein discrepancy; Gorham & Mackey). The paper is closely related to contemporary “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016), and clearly positions its contribution relative to that line.

Quality
- Theory: For simulated data with an exact posterior sample, the derivation that the BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is sound and well-connected to prior BDMC/AIS analyses. The bound is informative on toys where both sides can be computed.
- Scope: The approach primarily applies to AIS/SMC (importance sampling on an extended state space) and yields diagnostics based solely on the marginal likelihood. While the authors motivate broader relevance to MCMC design choices, the guarantees do not extend to arbitrary MCMC outputs without embedding them in AIS/SMC.
- Real-data protocol: The practical workaround for real data rests on strong assumptions: (i) a single fitted hyperparameter vector η_real summarizes the real posterior adequately; (ii) the synthetic posterior induced by η_real has similar convergence properties to the true posterior; (iii) starting from η_real and running a short MCMC produces an almost-exact posterior draw for reverse AIS. These assumptions are unverified and directly tied to the original convergence concerns—if they fail (e.g., under multimodality, heavy tails, or model misspecification), the diagnostic can be misleading. The experiments in §5.1 are suggestive but limited in complexity and do not convincingly rule out such failures.
- Empirics: The integration into Stan/WebPPL and the comparison of collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization are useful. The debugging example underscores practical value. However, the validation is largely on relatively simple models; the synthetic-real matching is shown via overlapping forward AIS curves rather than downstream posterior discrepancy metrics, and the sensitivity to η_real and reverse-chain initialization is not stress-tested under harder conditions.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with careful derivations and a useful background section on AIS/BDMC. The description of the real-data protocol is candid about limitations. It would help to:
- Clarify precisely when the “upper bound in expectation” can be interpreted as a reliable diagnostic for finite K, T, given heavy-tailed weight distributions.
- Provide guidance on annealing schedules, kernel choices, and computational budgeting to make bound comparisons fair and reproducible.

Originality
The use of BDMC’s bidirectional log-ML bounds to upper-bound the Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples is a neat and nontrivial extension. Positioning this as a diagnostic/benchmarking tool for probabilistic programming systems is novel and timely. Relation to subjective divergence is appropriately discussed.

Significance
If robust, the method offers a theoretically grounded diagnostic and a principled way to compare samplers by “tightness of BDMC bounds per unit compute.” This could influence practice in probabilistic programming, model representation choices, and regression testing. However, the current reliance on fitted-synthetic data and approximate reverse initialization limits confidence in real-data conclusions. The impact would be higher with stronger empirical evidence and robustness analyses under challenging posteriors.

Pros
- Rigorous, interpretable quantity (Jeffreys divergence upper-bounded in expectation) for simulated data.
- Clear extension of BDMC theory and practical integration into two major PPLs.
- Useful case study on representation trade-offs; compelling debugging story.
- Polite and accurate positioning relative to prior work (AIS/SMC, BDMC, Stein discrepancy, subjective divergence).

Cons
- Major assumption: availability of a representative η_real from prior MCMC runs, presuming convergence and sufficiency of a single summary.
- Major assumption: synthetic-data posterior mimics real-data posterior mixing behavior; this can break badly under misspecification or multimodality.
- Major assumption: short MCMC from η_real produces a near-exact sample to seed reverse AIS; optimistic and unverified.
- Applicability mainly to AIS/SMC; diagnostics reflect log-ML behavior, not arbitrary MCMC outputs.
- Empirical validation limited; Section 5.1 examples are too simple to dispel concerns about complex, multimodal, or ill-conditioned posteriors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theoretically or empirically quantify the bias introduced when reverse AIS is initialized from an approximate posterior sample; provide bounds or diagnostics to detect when this bias is large.
- Stress-test on challenging targets: multimodal mixtures (with label switching), hierarchical “funnel” posteriors, and deliberately misspecified models where synthetic and real behavior diverge.
- Compare against standard diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, Geweke, path-based thermodynamic integration checks), and show where BREAD catches failures they miss and vice versa.
- Replace the single η_real with a distribution: sample multiple η from an approximate posterior over hyperparameters; report variability in BDMC bounds across these draws to reflect posterior uncertainty.
- Provide compute-normalized comparisons with clear annealing schedules and kernel settings; report bound tightness vs wall-clock.
- Explain why the method should handle multimodality or poor summary-statistic initializations better than standard practices; include counterexamples and ablations.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is interesting and the theory for simulated data is solid, but the central practical contribution—using fitted-synthetic data and approximate reverse initialization to diagnose real-data performance—rests on untested assumptions closely tied to the very convergence issues being diagnosed. With stronger empirical validation on hard posteriors and a principled treatment of reverse-initialization approximation, this could be a valuable contribution.
