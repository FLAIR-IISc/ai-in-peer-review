This paper presents BREAD, a method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate and true posterior distributions. The core technique is a direct application of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework previously introduced by Grosse et al. (2015). By running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains in both a forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) direction on simulated data, the authors derive an expected upper bound on the divergence. The main contribution of this work is the implementation and application of this known technique within two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The authors validate their approach on several models and use it to compare different model representations.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clarity and practical utility. The problem of diagnosing MCMC convergence is of significant importance to the community, and the proposed BREAD method provides a more rigorous tool than common heuristics. The implementation within Stan and WebPPL makes this tool accessible to a wide audience of practitioners. The experiments are well-conducted, demonstrating how the method can be used to validate inference consistency between real and simulated data and provide concrete insights, such as choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed model representations. The discovery of a bug in WebPPL is a compelling testament to the method's utility as a diagnostic tool.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its limited originality. The theoretical foundation is almost entirely drawn from previous work on BDMC, with the extension to bounding Jeffreys divergence being a minor and straightforward analytical step. The paper is therefore less a presentation of a novel method and more a report on the application and implementation of an existing one. While valuable, this contribution does not appear to advance the state of the art in a fundamental way, as required for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

In summary, while the work is technically sound, well-executed, and addresses an important practical problem, its lack of a significant novel contribution makes it a borderline case. The paper presents a high-quality application study rather than new research. For this reason, the work might be better suited for a more specialized venue, such as a workshop on MCMC methods or probabilistic programming, where the focus is more on tooling, implementation, and practical application.
