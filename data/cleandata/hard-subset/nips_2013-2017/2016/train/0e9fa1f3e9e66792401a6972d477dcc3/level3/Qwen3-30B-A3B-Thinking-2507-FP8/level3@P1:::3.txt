

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), which extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the accuracy of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate their method by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models. The key insight is that the gap between the forward and reverse AIS chains provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence, which can be used as a performance measure for MCMC transition operators.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) a theoretical extension of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence; (2) an exact computation method for small examples to validate the bound; (3) a protocol for applying the method to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters; and (4) implementations in WebPPL and Stan. The approach is particularly valuable for probabilistic programming systems where users need to assess the reliability of black-box inference.

The paper's strengths include its practical utility for evaluating inference quality in probabilistic programming, its simplicity, and the clear demonstration of how the method can guide model representation choices. The validation experiments showing consistency between real and simulated data (Section 5.1.1) and the debugging example (Section 5.2.2) provide compelling evidence of the method's usefulness. The integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks demonstrates practical applicability.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The technical novelty is modest: the core observation that the BDMC gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence is not clearly distinguished from prior work, particularly Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which shares conceptual similarities. The paper fails to properly cite or discuss this related work, which weakens the novelty claim. The β schedule choice (critical for the quality of bounds) is under-discussed, with the paper not addressing how to choose Δβ systematically or exploring its impact on the bounds. The "AutoBDMC" component is ambiguously defined and never clearly explained, creating confusion about how the method is automated.

The paper also suffers from several clarity issues that hinder understanding. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back"), and key definitions are missing (e.g., the estimator $\hat{p}(y)$ in equations 5-7 and 9-11). Section 3.1 contains an error in equation (6), and the definition of vector $v$ is ambiguous given the two AIS sweeps. The experimental details are insufficiently specified, particularly regarding how the β schedule is chosen in practice (the paper states "step = 1/options.steps" but doesn't clarify this choice). Presentation issues like small axes in figures and typos (e.g., "Homan" instead of "Hoffman" for NUTS) further diminish the paper's quality.

While the method has practical value for probabilistic programming systems, the paper's contributions are not sufficiently novel or technically deep to justify acceptance as a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical extension is minor, the implementation details are insufficient, and the lack of proper engagement with related work weakens the paper's foundation. The authors could strengthen the paper by addressing the clarity issues, properly contextualizing their work within Neal's Tempered Transitions, and providing more systematic exploration of the β schedule's impact.

The paper has potential for future work, particularly in developing a more rigorous theoretical foundation for the bound and exploring how the method can be extended to more complex inference scenarios. However, as presented, it falls short of the quality expected for NIPS. The paper would benefit significantly from a thorough revision addressing the clarity, novelty, and experimental detail issues outlined above.

In summary, the paper presents a useful tool for evaluating MCMC inference quality, but the technical contributions are modest, the novelty is not clearly established, and the paper suffers from several clarity and presentation issues. While the method could be valuable for probabilistic programming practitioners, the paper as written does not make a strong enough scientific contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors should address the identified weaknesses before resubmission.
