Summary
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference. By running annealed importance sampling (AIS) both forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data, the authors use the stochastic lower/upper bounds on log p(y) to upper bound in expectation the symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between the sampler’s marginal q(xT) and the true posterior pT. They implement the method in Stan and WebPPL, propose a protocol to transfer insights from simulated to real data (fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate from them, and check consistency), and show uses for modeling choices and debugging. The paper relates to AIS and BDMC, Jarzynski’s nonequilibrium work relations, SMC, and concurrent “subjective divergence” work (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka).

Relation to prior work
The idea of bounding log partition ratios with forward/reverse annealing connects to Neal’s AIS (2001), Jarzynski (1997), and BDMC (Grosse et al., 2015). The acceptance-ratio perspective and heating/cooling schedule are closely related to Tempered Transitions (Neal, 1996), which is not cited but should be, as it clarifies the forward–reverse connection and acceptance probabilities. The paper also complements sample-quality approaches such as Stein discrepancies (Gorham & Mackey, 2015). The bound on symmetrized KL appears aligned with concurrent subjective divergence work; any new Jensen–Shannon divergence observation is not clearly novel and needs precise positioning.

Strengths
- Simple, broadly applicable idea: use the BDMC gap as a generic metric of sample quality for AIS/SMC-style transition operators.
- Practical implementations in Stan and WebPPL, with a clear potential impact on probabilistic programming workflows.
- Sensible protocol to relate simulated-data guarantees to real data; useful for users and system developers.
- Demonstrated utility for model representation choices and for debugging (WebPPL Gaussian bug).

Weaknesses and requested fixes
Technical correctness and clarity
- Sections 3.1–3.2 feel rushed. Several derivations are hard to follow due to missing definitions and notation inconsistencies.
- Missing estimator definition: the paper uses ĥp(y) (inside expectations in eqs. 5–7 and 9–11) but never defines it. Please define precisely the AIS estimator and its reverse counterpart used inside the expectations, and state which expectations are taken over which chains.
- Equation (6) appears incorrect as written: the KL arguments look identical. Please correct the expression; as written it obscures the intended decomposition into DKL(qfwd(xT,v) || pT(xT) qrev(v|xT)) and the marginalization step leading to (8).
- Notation ambiguity: v is defined as “all variables sampled in AIS before the final stage,” but there are both forward and reverse sweeps. Define v for each direction and clarify whether v includes all intermediate states x1:T−1 and any auxiliary randomness.
- Inconsistent labels: the manuscript alternates between rev/fwd and for/back. Please standardize throughout text, equations, and figures.
- AutoBDMC is undefined: it is unclear what “auto” denotes. Clarify whether it means (a) using the bound gap to assess convergence/stopping; (b) the fixed hyperparameter protocol of Sec. 3.2; or (c) an automated schedule/tuning procedure.

Related work and novelty
- Cite and discuss Tempered Transitions (Neal, 1996) and the acceptance-ratio viewpoint; situate BDMC’s reverse AIS bound relative to tempered pathways.
- The bound on symmetrized divergences is nice but appears to overlap with concurrent “subjective divergence” work. If you also mention Jensen–Shannon divergence, please give a precise statement and novelty claim, or reframe as a known consequence.

β schedule and evaluation protocol
- The choice of βt schedule is under-discussed. Since Δβ directly affects both bounds and comparisons, please:
  - State the schedule used in all experiments (code suggests βt = t/T).
  - Analyze sensitivity to Δβ and number of stages T.
  - Provide guidance for choosing schedules across models and transition operators.
  - Suggested addition: an iterative refinement scheme that adaptively decreases Δβ until the forward–reverse gap is below a user tolerance. Practical heuristics include equalizing variance of incremental log-weights, using ESS thresholds per stage, or placing more β points where the log-likelihood curvature is greatest (e.g., temperature ladder adaptation).
- Clarify whether “AutoBDMC” includes an automated Δβ refinement and a stopping rule based on the stochastic gap.

Experiments and presentation
- Explicitly report how βt is chosen, number of AIS stages, and how many HMC/NUTS steps per stage. Some figure captions suggest “HMC / No-U-Turn steps”; please clarify whether these are transitions per AIS stage or total steps.
- Define RAIS (reverse AIS) and other acronyms such as (u)c(R)AIS in-text and in captions.
- Provide numerical details (chains, seeds, time budgets). Since the bounds are in expectation, discuss how many AIS runs are needed to get tight estimates and whether you aggregate via mean or robust summaries.
- Improve figure readability: axis titles and tick labels are too small.

Typos and minor issues
- Close a missing parenthesis in the DJS expression; check whether you really intend JSD or Jeffreys where referenced.
- NUTS citation spells Hoffman as “Homan.”
- Ensure consistent use of Jeffreys divergence notation DJ and its definition.

Quality
Conceptually sound and valuable; however, derivational slips (eq. 6), missing definitions, and ambiguity around AutoBDMC and β schedules reduce reproducibility. The simulated-versus-real protocol is reasonable and partially validated, but broader stress tests would improve confidence.

Clarity
Writing is generally readable, but Sections 3.1–3.2 need tightening. Standardize notation, define estimators, and expand the β-schedule discussion. Figures need clearer labels.

Originality
Moderate. The main idea leverages existing BDMC/AIS machinery; the expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence overlaps with concurrent work. The implementation and protocol contributions for PPLs are useful but not deeply novel.

Significance
High potential impact for PPL users and developers: a simple, implementable yardstick for inference reliability that also helps debugging and model choice.

Pros
- Simple, general, and practical idea with real implementations.
- Useful protocol bridging simulated and real data.
- Demonstrated diagnostic value (bug discovery) and modeling insights.

Cons
- Modest novelty; missing related work on Tempered Transitions.
- Clarity issues: derivations, undefined estimators, inconsistent labels.
- Under-specified β schedule; AutoBDMC meaning unclear.
- Experimental details and figure clarity need work.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. I would support acceptance if the authors fix the clarity issues (esp. Sections 3.1–3.2, eq. 6, estimator definitions), add Tempered Transitions to related work, systematically address β-schedule choice (ideally with an adaptive Δβ refinement study), and clarify AutoBDMC. The method’s practicality and potential impact in probabilistic programming make it worth including once these issues are addressed.
