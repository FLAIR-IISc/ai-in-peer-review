

# Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo" (NIPS 2016)

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of the bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) technique to rigorously evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. Building on Grosse et al.'s NIPS 2015 work on BDMC for marginal likelihood estimation, the authors demonstrate how to bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The key insight is that by running annealed importance sampling (AIS) chains both from prior to posterior and vice versa on simulated data, one can upper bound in expectation the Jeffreys divergence. This approach extends the BDMC framework to provide a theoretically grounded method for assessing inference quality, addressing a critical gap in probabilistic modeling.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) a theoretical analysis showing BDMC yields an estimator that upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; (2) a technique for exact computation of both the true Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples; (3) a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data through hyperparameter fitting; and (4) integration into WebPPL and Stan, validated across multiple models.

Regarding quality, the paper demonstrates strong technical rigor. The theoretical analysis builds carefully on previous work while extending it in a novel direction. The authors provide clear derivations showing how the BDMC technique can bound the Jeffreys divergence, and they validate their approach through both theoretical analysis on small examples (where exact computation is possible) and empirical evaluation on multiple models. The paper also addresses potential weaknesses by carefully considering the limitations of the method (particularly its applicability to simulated data) and proposing a practical protocol for real-world application. The experimental validation on both WebPPL and Stan, including the discovery of a bug in WebPPL through the method's application, demonstrates practical utility.

The paper excels in clarity. The exposition is well-structured, with careful definitions of key concepts (AIS, BDMC, Jeffreys divergence) and a clear progression from theoretical development to practical implementation. The integration of the method into existing probabilistic programming frameworks is well-explained, and the experimental section provides sufficient detail to reproduce results. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing consistency between real and simulated data) effectively communicate key findings.

The originality is strong. While BDMC was previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, this paper's application to evaluate MCMC inference quality represents a novel contribution. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches, including the concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), and provide a theoretically grounded justification for their method. The approach of using BREAD to guide model representation choices (as demonstrated in the matrix factorization case study) represents a valuable new application of the technique.

The significance is high. MCMC reliability is a fundamental challenge in probabilistic inference, and the paper provides a rigorous, general method for addressing it. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the technique immediately applicable to practitioners, potentially improving confidence in probabilistic programming systems. The ability to use BREAD to guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed models) offers concrete value to modelers. The paper also demonstrates how the method can be used to identify implementation bugs, as evidenced by the WebPPL bug discovery.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a timely problem. MCMC is a cornerstone of probabilistic inference, yet validation of inference quality remains challenging, particularly in black-box probabilistic programming systems. The authors convincingly argue that their method provides a rigorous, general, and automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality.

One minor limitation is that the method is currently applicable only to inference algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS or SMC), though the authors persuasively argue that this still provides valuable insights for broader MCMC methods. The protocol for real-world data requires fitting hyperparameters first, which may be challenging for some complex models, but the authors provide a thorough validation of this approach.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem with a novel and useful approach. It advances the state of the art in probabilistic inference by providing a rigorous method for evaluating inference quality, with immediate practical applications in probabilistic programming systems. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates the method's practical utility, and the experimental results provide strong validation of the approach.

In conclusion, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. It addresses a fundamental problem with a technically sound and novel approach, provides rigorous theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates practical utility through integration into widely used probabilistic programming frameworks. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and represents a significant advance in our ability to evaluate MCMC inference quality.

I recommend acceptance.
