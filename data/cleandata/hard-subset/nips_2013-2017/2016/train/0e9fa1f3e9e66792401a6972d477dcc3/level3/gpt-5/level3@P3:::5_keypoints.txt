- Paper extends Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to bound Jeffreys divergence of AIS/SMC posterior samples by the gap between forward and reverse AIS log-marginal-likelihood bounds (BREAD), integrated into WebPPL and Stan.  
- Theoretical result: for simulated data with an exact posterior draw, the BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation; derivation is sound and well-connected to prior work.  
- Scope limitation: guarantees apply mainly to AIS/SMC (importance sampling on extended state space) and do not extend to arbitrary MCMC outputs without embedding them in AIS/SMC.  
- Real-data protocol relies on strong, unverified assumptions: a single fitted hyperparameter η_real summarizes the real posterior; synthetic posterior from η_real has similar convergence properties; and a short MCMC from η_real yields an almost-exact draw for reverse AIS.  
- These real-data assumptions can fail under multimodality, heavy tails, or model misspecification, making the diagnostic potentially misleading.  
- Empirical validation is limited: experiments use relatively simple models, synthetic-real matching is shown mainly via overlapping AIS curves, and sensitivity to η_real and reverse-chain initialization is not stress-tested.  
- Clarity is good with careful derivations, but the paper should clarify when the expectation bound is a reliable finite-sample diagnostic and provide practical guidance on annealing schedules, kernels, and compute budgeting.  
- Originality: novel and nontrivial extension of BDMC to posterior-sample diagnostics and useful positioning relative to subjective divergence and related work.  
- Significance conditional: could influence PPL practice and regression testing if robustness to real-data issues is demonstrated; currently limited by reliance on fitted-synthetic protocol and approximate reverse initialization.  
- Pros: rigorous interpretable quantity for simulated data; clear theoretical extension; practical integration into major PPLs; useful modeling and debugging case studies.  
- Cons: major dependence on representative η_real, on synthetic-data posterior mimicking real posterior, and on near-exact reverse initialization; applicability mainly to AIS/SMC; limited empirical stress-testing on hard posteriors.  
- Suggested improvements: quantify bias from approximate reverse initialization; stress-test on challenging targets (multimodal, funnel, misspecified); compare to standard diagnostics; sample multiple η_real to reflect hyperparameter uncertainty; provide compute-normalized comparisons and clear annealing/kernel settings; include counterexamples and ablations.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject—interesting theory for simulated data but practical real-data claims rest on untested assumptions and need stronger empirical validation.
