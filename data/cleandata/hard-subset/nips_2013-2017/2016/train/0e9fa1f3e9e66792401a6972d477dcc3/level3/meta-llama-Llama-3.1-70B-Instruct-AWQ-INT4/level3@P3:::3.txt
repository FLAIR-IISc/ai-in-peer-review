This paper proposes the use of the gap between bidirectional Monte Carlo's (BDMC) stochastic lower and upper bounds as a generic performance measure for Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) transition operators. The authors extend the BDMC technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The main contributions of the paper are the implementations of BDMC for Stan and WebPPL and a protocol for evaluating posterior inference quality. The authors also propose a technique for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the target distribution and the distribution of approximate samples produced by annealed importance sampling (AIS).

The paper has several strengths. The method is simple, easy to implement, and potentially high-impact for probabilistic programming systems. The authors provide a clear explanation of the BDMC technique and its application to posterior inference. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference.

However, there are some concerns about the novelty of the technical contributions. The Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation is nice, but it is not clearly novel. The authors do not cite or discuss Radford Neal's Tempered Transitions (1996), which closely relates to BDMC and its acceptance ratio interpretation.

Additionally, the paper has some clarity problems. Sections 3.1-3.2 feel rushed, and the writing quality slips, causing several ambiguities. The notation is sometimes ambiguous, and the use of "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back" is inconsistent and unclear.

The paper also lacks detail in some experiments. It is unclear how βt is chosen in practice, and the code implies step = 1/options.steps, but this should be stated explicitly. The figure captions are unclear, and the axes and tick labels in figures are too small.

Furthermore, the paper has some typos and undefined acronyms. The missing closing parentheses in the D_JS expression and the misspelling of Hoffman as "Homan" in the NUTS citation are examples of this.

Overall, the paper has some strengths, but it also has some weaknesses that need to be addressed. The authors should clarify the notation, provide more detail in the experiments, and fix the typos and undefined acronyms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new method for evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The method is simple, easy to implement, and potentially high-impact for probabilistic programming systems.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The technical contributions are modest, and the Jensen-Shannon divergence bound observation is not clearly novel.
* The paper lacks detail in some experiments and has some clarity problems.
* The notation is sometimes ambiguous, and the use of "rev"/"fwd" vs "for"/"back" is inconsistent and unclear.

Recommendation:

The paper could be acceptable if the authors fix the clarity, related-work discussion, and experiment thoroughness by camera-ready. The authors should also address the novelty concerns and provide more detail in the experiments.
