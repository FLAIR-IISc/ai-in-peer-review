- Proposes BREAD, extending BDMC by running forward/reverse AIS to obtain stochastic lower/upper bounds on log marginal likelihood.
- Proves the expected gap between these bounds upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence between AIS approximate samples and the true posterior.
- Requires exact posterior samples, so theoretical guarantees apply to simulated data only; authors propose simulating data from fitted hyperparameters as a heuristic for real data.
- Empirically validated in WebPPL and Stan across multiple models; demonstrated usefulness for convergence diagnosis, model representation choices, and debugging (found a WebPPL bug).
- Theoretical derivation is elegant and well-founded; experiments cover five models and two inference frameworks.
- Authors acknowledge the main limitation (simulated-data requirement) and validate the proposed protocol to increase practical confidence.
- Section 3.1 (core theory) is dense and abrupt; needs clearer statement of goals and motivation for algebraic steps.
- Notational inconsistencies (e.g., using both 'y' and 'D' for data) should be unified.
- Novel contribution: reframes BDMC to bound posterior approximation quality (Jeffreys divergence) rather than only estimate marginal likelihoods.
- Significant practical impact: offers a principled scalar diagnostic for MCMC quality that is stronger than common heuristics like Gelman–Rubin.
- Computational cost is high due to many AIS chains, which may limit routine adoption.
- For acceptance: addresses a major unsolved problem with strong theory, convincing experiments, and clear practical utility.
- Against acceptance: core guarantees limited to simulated data, clarity issues in exposition, and potentially prohibitive computational demands.
- Suggested improvement: preface Section 3.1 with a high-level summary and a two-step sketch relating forward/reverse estimator biases to DKL terms.
- Suggested improvement: make code easily installable (e.g., WebPPL package) and provide clear instructions and compatible software versions.
- Minor editorial fix: unify notation for observed data throughout the paper.
