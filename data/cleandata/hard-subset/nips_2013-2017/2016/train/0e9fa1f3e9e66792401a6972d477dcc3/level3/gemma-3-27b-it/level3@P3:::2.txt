This paper presents a novel and valuable technique, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for evaluating the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The core idea – leveraging BDMC to upper bound the KL divergence between the true posterior and the approximate distribution obtained from MCMC – is both theoretically sound and practically impactful. The authors demonstrate the utility of their method by integrating it into WebPPL and Stan, and validating it on a range of models and datasets. This work tackles a crucial problem in Bayesian inference, namely, assessing the trustworthiness of results from “black box” inference procedures.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It effectively situates the work within the broader context of MCMC diagnostics (e.g., Brooks & Gelman, 1998), marginal likelihood estimation (e.g., Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015), and probabilistic programming (e.g., Goodman et al., 2008). The authors acknowledge and relate their work to concurrent research by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), highlighting the common foundation in BDMC while distinguishing their approaches. The detailed explanation of the mathematical derivation, while dense, is crucial for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of BREAD.  The inclusion of Appendices with supplementary details and code snippets is also appreciated.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. First, the theoretical contribution – refining the analysis of Grosse et al. (2015) to derive an estimator for the Jeffreys divergence – is significant. The rigorous bound on the divergence provides a more quantitative and reliable measure of inference quality than traditional convergence diagnostics. Second, the practical integration of BREAD into two popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) dramatically increases its accessibility and potential impact. Third, the experimental validation on diverse models and datasets, including the demonstration of consistency between simulated and real data, bolsters confidence in the method's effectiveness. Finally, the discovery of a bug in WebPPL underscores BREAD's potential as a valuable testing tool.

However, the paper isn’t without minor limitations. The requirement of being able to sample from the true posterior for the reverse AIS chain restricts its direct applicability to scenarios with simulated data. While the authors propose a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters, the success of this protocol relies on the accuracy of the hyperparameter estimation and the representativeness of the simulated data. Though validated, this remains an approximation.  Further investigation into the robustness of the protocol under varying degrees of model misspecification would be beneficial. Additionally, the analysis in Section 3.1 focuses on bounding the Jeffreys divergence; exploring other divergence measures or error metrics might reveal additional insights into the quality of approximate inference.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by the experimental results.
*   **Novelty:** The application of BDMC to bound the KL divergence of MCMC samples is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian inference and provides a valuable tool for assessing the reliability of results.
*   **Practical Impact:** The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan makes it readily accessible to practitioners.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on simulating data limits direct application to all real-world problems, though the proposed workaround is reasonable and validated.
*   The mathematical complexity might make the paper challenging for readers unfamiliar with BDMC and KL divergence concepts.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-done paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The method is theoretically sound, practically useful, and clearly presented. The authors have thoroughly validated their approach and demonstrated its potential for improving the reliability of probabilistic programming systems.  Despite the minor limitations, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
