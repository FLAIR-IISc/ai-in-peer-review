This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) with forward and reverse AIS to upper bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys (J–S) divergence between an MCMC-derived approximate sampling distribution and the true posterior, primarily for debugging and evaluating inference in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The method (BREAD) leverages simulated data to obtain exact posterior draws for reverse AIS, integrates with WebPPL and Stan, and is demonstrated on several models, including a study of collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and a real-data protocol via fitted hyperparameters. The work relates to prior BDMC uses for marginal likelihood estimation (Grosse et al., AISTATS’15), AIS/SMC-based estimators (Neal’01; Del Moral et al.’06), and sample-quality measures (Gorham & Mackey, NIPS’15). It is also closely related to concurrent “subjective divergence” ideas (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka’16). The paper should discuss earlier validation-by-simulation diagnostics that sample data from the prior and test inference correctness (Geweke 2004; Cook, Gelman & Rubin 2006).

Quality: The core derivation that the AIS forward–reverse gap upper bounds the expected Jeffreys divergence is sound and useful. However, too many essential details are deferred to the supplement. Section 3.1 is hard to follow: q(.) is used before being defined; please explicitly define q(x_T) as the marginal of the forward AIS after T steps and state q(v|x_T). The “in expectation” nature of the bound and potential high variance deserve clearer empirical calibration beyond toy cases (e.g., report B vs. J across more synthetic targets; compare to ESS/R̂).

Clarity: The presentation is generally good but could be more self-contained. Provide a step-by-step derivation of Eqs. (6–12) in the main text, with clear notation. Splitting unobserved variables into z and θ clutters equations given they appear together; consider using a single block. Minor: missing closing parenthesis at line 165.

Originality: Extends BDMC to bound posterior sample quality, which is novel and timely for PPLs. Relation to subjective divergence is appropriately noted; add discussion of Geweke (2004) and Cook, Gelman & Rubin (2006).

Significance: High. Reliable quantitative evaluation of MCMC in black-box settings is important. The WebPPL/Stan integration and the debugging example underscore practical impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually elegant, practically useful bound on sample quality
- Clear integration into two PPLs; actionable debugging win
- Insightful representation comparison (collapsed vs. uncollapsed)

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on simulated-data setting; real-data protocol remains heuristic
- Main derivations should be in the paper; Section 3.1 clarity issues
- Limited calibration of bound tightness beyond small toys

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarity and related-work fixes.
