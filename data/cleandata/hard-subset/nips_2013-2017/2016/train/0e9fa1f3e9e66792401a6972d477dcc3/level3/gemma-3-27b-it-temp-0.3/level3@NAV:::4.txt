## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper presents AutoBDMC, a method leveraging Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) – specifically annealed importance sampling (AIS) and its reverse – to assess MCMC convergence by establishing stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood. A key contribution is demonstrating that the gap between these bounds provides an upper bound on the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the true posterior and the approximate sample distribution, offering a single scalar convergence metric. The authors integrate their method into WebPPL and Stan, validating it on synthetic data and demonstrating its predictive power for real-world datasets. 

The work builds upon prior research in MCMC diagnostics (e.g., Brooks & Gelman, 1998) and marginal likelihood estimation (e.g., Neal, 2001; Grosse et al., 2015). It distinguishes itself by providing a quantifiable, expectation-based bound on the divergence between the approximate and true posterior, rather than relying on heuristic convergence checks. The related work section appropriately acknowledges concurrent efforts by Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka (2016) which also utilize BDMC, highlighting the differences in their approaches. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its technical soundness, clear motivation, and practical demonstration. The experimental validation, particularly the consistency check between simulated and real data, is compelling. The application to model selection and debugging, including the identification of a bug in WebPPL, showcases the method’s utility beyond pure convergence assessment. 

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. Section 3, especially the transition into 3.1, is difficult to follow. Variable and equation definitions are introduced without sufficient context or motivation. The novelty of the divergence bound in Section 3.1 isn’t explicitly stated, which is a significant oversight given its centrality to the contribution. Furthermore, "AutoBDMC" is never formally defined, leaving the reader to infer its meaning. This is compounded by references to challenges "for AutoBDMC" before the term is clarified. Minor issues include a potential typo (line 149) and inconsistent notation for data (y vs. D). The supplemental material requires clearer instructions regarding WebPPL version compatibility and ideally, should be packaged for easier installation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The method offers a valuable, theoretically grounded approach to assessing MCMC reliability, addressing a long-standing challenge in probabilistic inference. The practical implementation and validation in widely used probabilistic programming languages enhance its impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The clarity issues in Section 3 hinder understanding and detract from the paper’s overall presentation. The lack of explicit definition for "AutoBDMC" is a significant omission.



**Overall:** Despite the clarity issues, the technical contribution and practical demonstration are strong. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. The authors should prioritize clarifying Section 3, defining "AutoBDMC", and improving the supplemental material.
