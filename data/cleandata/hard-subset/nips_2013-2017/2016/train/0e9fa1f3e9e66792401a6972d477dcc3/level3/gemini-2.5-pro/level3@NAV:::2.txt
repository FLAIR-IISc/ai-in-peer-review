This paper introduces BREAD, a novel method for evaluating the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), a technique previously used for marginal likelihood estimation [Grosse et al., 2015]. By running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains in both the forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions on simulated data, the authors derive an estimator that provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior. The method's utility is demonstrated through its integration into two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan.

This is a well-executed paper that addresses a significant and notoriously difficult problem. The reliable diagnosis of MCMC sample quality is a critical challenge for the community, especially with the rise of black-box probabilistic inference systems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper presents a principled, quantitative measure of inference quality that moves beyond common convergence heuristics. The central idea of using the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimates to bound the symmetrized KL divergence is both elegant and novel, providing a much-needed tool for assessing sampler performance.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The theoretical derivation clearly extends prior work, and the experimental validation is comprehensive. The authors validate their method on several models, provide a compelling use case in comparing model representations, and even demonstrate its utility for debugging by discovering a bug in WebPPL.
*   **Clarity & Impact:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. By integrating the method into widely-used frameworks like Stan and WebPPL, the authors have made a significant practical contribution that is likely to be used and built upon by researchers and practitioners alike.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The primary limitation, which the authors openly acknowledge, is that the rigorous bounds require an exact posterior sample, restricting its formal guarantees to simulated data. While the proposed protocol for applying it to real-world data is a sensible heuristic, it lacks the same theoretical backing.
*   The method is directly applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC, and its utility for diagnosing more standard MCMC methods is indirect.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. It makes a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to an important problem. The authors are careful to state the limitations of their approach while convincingly demonstrating its value. I strongly recommend acceptance.
