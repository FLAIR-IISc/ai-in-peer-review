- Proposes using the BDMC gap (difference between forward/reverse AIS log p(y) bounds) as an implementation-agnostic performance measure for MCMC transition operators.
- Shows on simulated data that the expected BDMC gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the AIS sample distribution and the true posterior.
- Implements the method in Stan and WebPPL, introduces a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to proxy real-data behavior, and demonstrates model-selection and debugging use cases.
- Builds on prior work (BDMC 2015, AIS 2001) and connects to subjective divergence (2016) and broader MCMC diagnostics and sample-quality measures.
- Strength: simple, practical, and potentially high-impact metric for probabilistic programming.
- Strength: useful implementations in major PPLs and a real-data proxy protocol.
- Strength: validation examples and a bug-finding anecdote illustrate practical utility.
- Clarity/quality issues: Sections 3.1–3.2 feel rushed and contain errors (Eq.6 incorrect, p̂(y) never defined, vector v loosely defined, inconsistent labels “rev/fwd” vs “for/back”).
- Presentation problems: RAIS and (u)c(R)AIS undefined in captions, figures have tiny axes/ticks, ambiguous caption about “HMC/NUTS steps,” and several typos (e.g., missing parenthesis, “Homan”→“Hoffman”).
- Originality concern: the novelty of the expected BDMC gap bounding Jeffreys/JS divergence is not clearly distinguished from BDMC (2015) and subjective divergence (2016).
- Related work missing: should discuss Neal’s Tempered Transitions (1996) and its acceptance-ratio interpretation, which relates to heating/cooling bounds and reverse AIS.
- β-schedule issues: choice of βt (Δβ) is under-discussed; schedule directly affects bounds and comparisons and needs sensitivity experiments.
- Recommendation for β: propose an iterative scheme to reduce Δβ until the gap stabilizes, with stopping criteria and budget reporting.
- AutoBDMC unclear: authors must define whether “auto” uses the gap for convergence assessment, fixed-hyperparameter scheme, or both.
- Pros for acceptance: practical, low-friction metric; solid PPL integrations; useful protocol; actionable insights.
- Cons against acceptance: modest novelty and several technical/clarity errors that impede verification and reproducibility; insufficient treatment of annealing schedules and experimental details.
- Recommendation: borderline accept, contingent on fixing clarity/notation/equation issues, adding missing related work (especially Neal 1996), and strengthening schedule analysis for camera-ready.
