- Problem addressed: assessing reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference in black-box settings.  
- Method: run AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) to form a bidirectional gap.  
- Theoretical result: bidirectional AIS gap gives an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between true posterior and approximate samples.  
- Additional theory: one-sided gaps can bound individual KL terms (forward or reverse).  
- Real-data protocol: propose a pragmatic fitted-hyperparameter approach to apply the method without exact posterior samples.  
- Implementation: integrated into WebPPL and Stan.  
- Empirical validation: convincing experiments across regression, time series, matrix factorization, and mixture models.  
- Practical impact: used to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations and to uncover a subtle implementation bug.  
- Positioning: clearly related to BDMC, AIS/SMC literature and existing diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, sample-quality tests).  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and precise about guarantees and limitations.  
- Suggested improvements: report computational overhead, detail annealing schedules and sensitivity, and consolidate forward/reverse notation.  
- Originality: novel insight linking bidirectional AIS gap to Jeffreys divergence; integration and real-data protocol are significant contributions.  
- Significance: provides a principled, implementable diagnostic likely to influence practice for MCMC reliability.  
- Main strength: strong theoretical connection extending BDMC to sample-quality bounds.  
- Empirical strength: solid cross-tool and cross-model empirical support.  
- Practical strength: offers a usable protocol and demonstrated debugging utility.  
- Primary limitation: guarantees hold in expectation, not with high probability.  
- Applicability limitation: method requires AIS/SMC-like settings and simulated-data guarantees need exact posterior samples.  
- Empirical limitation: limited exploration of bound tightness and annealing-schedule sensitivity at scale.  
- Recommendation: accept.
