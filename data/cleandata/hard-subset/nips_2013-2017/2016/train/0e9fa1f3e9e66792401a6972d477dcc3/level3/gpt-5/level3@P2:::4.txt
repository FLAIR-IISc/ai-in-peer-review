Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. Using annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior), the method sandwiches the log marginal likelihood and shows that the gap between the reverse and forward AIS log estimates upper-bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL) between the true posterior and the distribution q of approximate samples produced by AIS. The authors propose a practical protocol to use this idea on real data via fitted hyperparameters and simulated datasets, and they integrate the approach into WebPPL and Stan. Experiments validate that simulated-data behavior tracks real-data behavior, illustrate use for choosing model representations (collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization), and even uncover a bug in WebPPL’s Gaussian sampler.

The work builds directly on BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015], AIS [Neal, 2001], reverse AIS/Jarzynski [Jarzynski, 1997], and connects to convergence diagnostics [Gelman and Rubin, 1992; Cowles and Carlin, 1996; Brooks and Gelman, 1998], and Stein-based sample quality measures [Gorham and Mackey, 2015]. It is closely related to concurrent “subjective divergence” work [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016], which also leverages reverse chains; the present paper emphasizes an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence when the reverse chain is initialized from an exact posterior sample and provides a real-data evaluation protocol.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation that E[log R_hat] and E[−log R_hat^rev] bound the two one-sided KL terms, and that the expected gap bounds the Jeffreys divergence, is clean and valuable. Please explicitly emphasize that the bound is in expectation and clarify implications for finite-sample use (e.g., report uncertainty on the gap estimator and discuss how many AIS runs are needed).
- Scope and assumptions: Rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample—available on simulated data (via ancestral sampling from p(θ,z,y))—and algorithms that can be written as importance sampling on an extended state space (AIS/SMC). The real-data protocol with fitted hyperparameters is a reasonable heuristic and empirically validated, but it removes the formal guarantee; please state this limitation prominently and discuss potential failure modes (e.g., prior-data conflict).
- Experiments: The validation on five models in Stan shows that simulated-data forward AIS behavior mirrors real-data behavior, and that reverse AIS results are robust to the number of hyperparameter MCMC steps. The matrix factorization case study provides actionable insights across WebPPL and Stan. It would help to add sensitivity analyses to annealing schedules, number of tempering steps, and transition kernel choices, and to report variability/confidence intervals of the bound gap across runs.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but Section 3 (especially the transition into 3.1) is hard to follow. Consider:
  - Introducing all variables (v, x_T, q_fwd, q_rev) before using them and adding more intuition for the conditioning steps and the marginalization inequality.
  - Stating the main theorem early in 3.1 with assumptions and a clear boxed statement that the bound upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Terminology: Ensure consistent naming and definitions. The manuscript uses BDMC and BREAD; if “AutoBDMC” is intended as the end-to-end tool or protocol, define it precisely on first use and use the term consistently. Avoid introducing terms before they are defined (e.g., Section 3.2 refers to challenges “for AutoBDMC”).
- Notation: Unify the notation for data (y vs. D). There appears to be a minor mismatch around Section 2.3; please check for typos such as p(θ,z|y) vs. p(θ,z,y) in the equations and surrounding text.
- Divergence: Throughout, make clear that the bound targets the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence, not the Jensen–Shannon divergence, to avoid confusion.

Originality
- Conceptually novel and useful is the reinterpretation of the BDMC sandwich as an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between p and q for AIS/SMC approximate samples, yielding a single scalar sample-quality metric. Exact evaluation on toy problems and a practical real-data protocol strengthen the contribution. The relation and differences to concurrent “subjective divergence” [CTM16] are acknowledged; a clearer positioning (assumptions, guarantees, and practical implications) would further highlight novelty.

Significance
- Important and timely for probabilistic programming and MCMC practice. Provides a rigorous diagnostic on simulated data, a pragmatic protocol for real data, and concrete guidance on modeling and implementation decisions (e.g., collapsing). The debugging example underscores practical impact. While limited to AIS/SMC-style samplers, many design choices transfer to broader MCMC settings.

Pros
- Clear theoretical link from BDMC to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence between posterior and approximate samples.
- Practical integration into WebPPL and Stan; method reveals real issues (bug) and informs model representation choices.
- Empirical evidence that simulated-data analyses reflect real-data behavior across multiple models.
- Useful, scalar, comparable metric for convergence/sample quality.

Cons
- Formal guarantees require exact posterior samples; real-data protocol is heuristic without bounds.
- Section 3 could be clearer; main result and assumptions should be stated more explicitly.
- Limited exploration of sensitivity to AIS schedules and kernels; uncertainty quantification for the bound estimator is not fully developed.
- Terminology and notation inconsistencies (e.g., AutoBDMC undefined; y vs. D).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and foreground the main theoretical result: assumptions, statement, and consequences of an expectation bound; discuss practical estimation (variance, CIs, number of chains).
- Provide guidance and sensitivity studies on annealing schedules, number of intermediate distributions, and transition kernels; include ablations showing their effect on the gap.
- Expand the comparison to [Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka, 2016]: when are the two approaches equivalent, and how do guarantees differ when using a reference distribution vs. an exact posterior?
- Strengthen Section 3 exposition; define all variables early and add intuition for the inequalities.
- Clean up notation and minor typos; ensure consistent use of data symbols and correct expressions around Section 2–3.
- Software: Provide clear integration instructions and version compatibility for WebPPL; consider releasing as an npm-installable package. Similarly, release Stan code and scripts for reproducing figures.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong and useful paper that offers a principled, practical diagnostic for MCMC-based inference with solid empirical support and real utility for probabilistic programming. I recommend acceptance after clarifications to Section 3, explicit statement of the main novelty, and improved consistency in terminology/notation, plus minor software packaging guidance.
