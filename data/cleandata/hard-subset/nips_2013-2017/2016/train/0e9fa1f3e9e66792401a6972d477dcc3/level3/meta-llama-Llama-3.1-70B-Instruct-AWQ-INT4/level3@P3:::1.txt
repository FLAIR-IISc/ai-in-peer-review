This paper proposes using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to estimate the Jeffreys divergence between an MCMC sampler and the target distribution for debugging/evaluating MCMC in probabilistic programming languages (PPLs). The idea is interesting and potentially useful because existing MCMC diagnostics are problematic. The authors provide a clear explanation of the BDMC technique and its application to bounding the Jeffreys divergence.

The paper builds on previous work by Grosse et al. (2015) and extends it to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The authors also discuss related work on MCMC diagnostics and automatic configuration of MCMC algorithms.

However, there are some areas for improvement. Section 3.1 is hard to follow, and the notation q(.) is used before being defined. It would be helpful to explicitly state that q(.) refers to the marginal of the AIS sampler after T steps. Additionally, important derivation details are pushed to the supplement, which makes it difficult to follow the main text. It would be better to present a clear step-by-step application of the method in the main text.

The authors also split unobserved variables into z and theta, which seems unnecessary since they always appear together. This notation could be simplified to improve readability.

The paper includes a minor typographical error at line 165 (missing closing parenthesis).

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel application of BDMC to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. While the technique itself is not new, its application to PPLs is a significant contribution.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the BDMC technique and its application. However, the notation and terminology could be improved to make the paper more accessible to readers who are not familiar with PPLs or MCMC.

The results are significant, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in evaluating the accuracy of posterior inference in realistic settings. The paper also includes a thorough discussion of related work and provides a clear explanation of the contributions of the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of PPLs and MCMC. The authors provide a clear explanation of the BDMC technique and its application to evaluating MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper is well-organized, and the results are significant.
