- Extends BDMC to bound the expected Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between true posterior and AIS sampler distribution via forward and reverse AIS.
- Core idea: gap between forward stochastic lower bound and reverse stochastic upper bound on log p(y) yields an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence when initialized from an exact posterior draw.
- Proposes a practical "fitted-hyperparameter simulation" protocol to approximate reverse initialization for real datasets.
- Integrates the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan and uses it to validate inference and find an implementation bug.
- Builds directly on AIS and BDMC, and relates to thermodynamic integration, non-equilibrium work identities, and sample-quality metrics like Stein discrepancies.
- Clarifies relation to concurrent "subjective divergence" work, showing reduction to Jeffreys divergence with true-posterior initialization.
- Strength: clear theoretical connection between BDMC sandwich and an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Strength: practical protocol and cross-platform implementation make the method usable in real PPLs.
- Strength: empirical validation across multiple models shows consistency between simulated and real AIS behavior and robustness of reverse initialization.
- Strength: provides actionable guidance (e.g., choosing collapsed vs uncollapsed representations) and helps debug inference engines.
- Weakness: rigorous guarantees require an exact posterior sample, so guarantees hold only on simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Weakness: applicability limited to samplers expressible as AIS/SMC over extended states; does not directly bound arbitrary MCMC outputs like raw NUTS.
- Weakness: divergence bound is in expectation; single-run variability and estimator variance are not fully quantified beyond toy cases.
- Weakness: bound tightness depends on annealing schedules, kernels, and number of temperatures; more guidance on schedule design is needed.
- Weakness: tightness demonstrated mainly on small examples; limited systematic characterization in higher-dimensional models and computational cost vs tightness.
- Quality: theoretical derivations appear correct and experiments/methodology are sensible; quartile reporting is reasonable but more formal uncertainty quantification would strengthen claims.
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized, but could report implementation details (temperature schedules, kernel/tuning choices) more systematically.
- Originality and significance: novel extension of BDMC to bound sample quality, useful fitted-hyperparameter protocol, and practical impact for PPLs; recommended for acceptance with suggested clarifications.
- Suggested improvements: provide adaptive/sensitivity strategies for annealing schedules, quantify estimator variance and CI, discuss failure modes of fitted-hyperparameter approach, clarify kernel/HMC/NUTS settings, and compare to alternative diagnostics (ESS, R-hat, Stein).
