### **Summary**

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method for quantitatively evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples generated by MCMC-based algorithms like Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS). The core technical contribution is an extension of the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework. Where BDMC was originally proposed to produce stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, this work demonstrates that the gap between these two bounds provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate sample distribution and the true posterior.

Because the method, in its rigorous form, requires an exact sample from the posterior, its direct application is limited to simulated data. To bridge this gap for practical use, the paper proposes a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world datasets: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data; second, simulate a new dataset from these inferred hyperparameters; and third, run BREAD on this simulated data to assess inference quality. The method is implemented and validated within two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and is used to provide insight into model representation choices for a matrix factorization problem.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper presents a high-quality, technically sound piece of work. The central theoretical result—that the expected gap between the forward and reverse AIS log-likelihood estimates bounds the Jeffreys divergence—is a clever and well-supported extension of the analysis from Grosse et al. (2015). The derivation is clear and appears correct. The work is empirically validated on a variety of models, and the experiments are thoughtfully designed to test the core claims, such as the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data and the robustness of the real-data protocol to the number of burn-in steps. The discovery and reporting of a bug in WebPPL serves as a powerful testament to the method's practical utility for verifying the correctness of inference implementations. The authors are also commendably transparent about the primary limitation of the method (the need for an exact posterior sample) and propose a reasonable heuristic to address it.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides the necessary context on AIS and BDMC in a clear and concise manner, making the paper accessible even to readers not intimately familiar with these techniques. The derivation of the main result in Section 3.1 is presented logically. The figures are informative and effectively communicate the experimental results, particularly the quartile plots which adeptly handle the high variance of AIS estimates. The overall structure guides the reader from theory to practical application smoothly.

#### **Originality**

The work builds directly upon existing methods, namely AIS and BDMC. However, the originality lies in the novel application and re-interpretation of these tools. The insight to use the BDMC bound gap not just to bracket the marginal likelihood but to quantify sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence is a significant and non-obvious contribution. This recasts BDMC from a tool for model comparison into a powerful diagnostic for inference itself. The proposed protocol for real-world data, while heuristic, is also a novel contribution to making these types of diagnostics practical. The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself relative to prior work, including a clear discussion of the concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

#### **Significance**

The significance of this work is high. The problem of reliably diagnosing the convergence and quality of MCMC samplers is a fundamental and persistent challenge in Bayesian statistics and machine learning. This is especially true with the rise of "black-box" probabilistic programming languages, where users may have limited ability to assess the trustworthiness of the output. BREAD provides a principled, quantitative, and automatable method to address this need. By integrating it into popular frameworks like Stan and WebPPL, the work demonstrates a clear path to broad impact. The use case of comparing model representations shows that the method can provide concrete guidance on modeling decisions that directly affect inference performance. This tool is likely to be adopted by developers of PPLs for testing and by sophisticated users for validating complex models.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but could be improved in a few areas:

1.  **Discussion of the Real-Data Protocol's Assumptions:** The protocol for real-world data is a pragmatic and necessary contribution. However, it relies on the assumption that an approximate posterior sample (for the hyperparameters) is "close enough" to an exact one to initialize the reverse chain effectively. While the experiments in 5.1.2 provide compelling evidence that this works well in practice, a more explicit discussion of the potential failure modes would strengthen the paper. For instance, what happens if the model is badly misspecified, or if the initial approximate inference on the real data fails to find the main posterior mass for the hyperparameters? Acknowledging these potential issues would add nuance.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The practicality of any diagnostic tool depends on its computational overhead. A brief discussion of the cost of BREAD would be a valuable addition. How does the runtime of running K forward and K reverse AIS chains compare to the cost of running a standard MCMC analysis for the same model? This would help potential users gauge when and where the method is most applicable.

3.  **Averaging AIS Chains:** Footnote 3 notes that the theoretical analysis applies to individual AIS chains, not averages. Standard practice with AIS is often to average the importance weights (not their logs) to obtain a lower-variance estimate. The presented analysis, which relies on the log of a single estimate, may be noisier. It would be helpful to clarify whether the theoretical bounds could be extended to accommodate some form of averaging across chains to produce more stable estimates of the divergence bound. If not, a brief explanation of the theoretical obstacle would be insightful.

### **Final Assessment**

This paper presents a significant and timely contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. It provides a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical tool for assessing the quality of MCMC samples. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and its importance is well-demonstrated through compelling experiments and implementations in major PPLs. The primary limitation regarding simulated data is acknowledged and thoughtfully addressed. The suggestions above are intended for minor refinement rather than indicating any fundamental flaws. The work is of high quality and is highly likely to be influential. It is a clear candidate for acceptance.
