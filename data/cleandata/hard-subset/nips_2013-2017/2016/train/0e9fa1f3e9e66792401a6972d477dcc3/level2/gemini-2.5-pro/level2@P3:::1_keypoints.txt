- Introduces BREAD, extending BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence between AIS-derived approximate posteriors and the true posterior.  
- Theoretical result: expected gap between forward and reverse AIS log estimates upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence; derivation is clear and correct.  
- Direct method requires an exact posterior sample, so applicability is limited to simulated data.  
- Proposes a practical real-data protocol: infer hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from them, then run BREAD on the simulated data.  
- Implemented and validated in WebPPL and Stan, with experiments across multiple models.  
- Empirical results show consistency between behavior on real and simulated data and robustness to burn-in choices.  
- Practical utility demonstrated by discovering a bug in WebPPL, showing value for implementation verification.  
- Paper is well-written and organized; background on AIS/BDMC and main derivation are accessible.  
- Figures (especially quartile plots) effectively convey high-variance AIS results.  
- Originality lies in reinterpreting BDMC gap as a diagnostic for sample quality rather than only marginal likelihood bounding.  
- Real-data protocol is a novel, pragmatic contribution to make the diagnostic usable beyond simulations.  
- High significance: addresses a fundamental MCMC diagnostic problem and has clear adoption potential in PPLs and modeling decisions.  
- Suggestion: add explicit discussion of failure modes/assumptions of the real-data protocol (e.g., model misspecification or poor hyperparameter inference).  
- Suggestion: include discussion of computational cost (K forward and K reverse AIS chains) relative to standard MCMC analyses.  
- Suggestion: clarify whether theoretical bounds can accommodate averaging across AIS chains or explain obstacles to extending analysis beyond single-chain logs.  
- Final assessment: technically solid, practically validated, acknowledges limitations, and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
