This paper introduces a method for measuring the reliability of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference using bidirectional Monte Carlo. The authors extend the bidirectional Monte Carlo technique to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence between the true posterior distribution and the distribution of approximate samples.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the background and related work. The technical contributions of the paper are significant, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on several models and datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a rigorous and general method for monitoring the quality of posterior inference. The authors integrate their method into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validate it on several models and datasets. The results show that the method can provide valuable insights into the choice of model representation and can help guide the design of inference algorithms.

The paper also discusses the limitations of the method, including the requirement of an exact sample from the target distribution and the potential for the upper bound to overestimate the true Jeffreys divergence. However, the authors provide a protocol for using the method to analyze sampler accuracy on real-world data and demonstrate its effectiveness in practice.

The experiments are well-designed, and the results are clearly presented. The authors validate the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in guiding the choice of model representation.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it relies on the availability of an exact sample from the target distribution, which may not always be feasible. However, the authors discuss this limitation and provide a protocol for using the method to analyze sampler accuracy on real-world data.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic inference and provides a valuable tool for evaluating the quality of MCMC inference.
