

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC). The authors extend BDMC [GGA15] to evaluate MCMC-based inference algorithms like AIS and SMC, providing an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence in expectation. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on simulated data, and propose a protocol for real-world application by fitting hyperparameters to real data and simulating datasets. The method is demonstrated to guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even uncovered a bug in WebPPL.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical contribution is strong and well-motivated. The derivation in Section 3.1 rigorously establishes that the BDMC upper bound sandwiches the Jeffreys divergence, with tight empirical performance on toy examples (Appendix B). The integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks (WebPPL and Stan) demonstrates practical utility, and the debugging example (Section 5.2.2) powerfully illustrates BREAD's value for identifying implementation errors. The real-world validation protocol (Section 3.2) is well-considered, and the experiments (Section 5.1) convincingly show consistency between simulated and real data for multiple models. The paper clearly situates itself against related work (e.g., [CTM16], convergence diagnostics), avoiding overclaiming.

**Weaknesses:**  
The method is limited to inference algorithms amenable to AIS/SMC (e.g., not directly applicable to standard Metropolis-Hastings without modification), though the authors reasonably argue this covers key design choices. The real-world validation (Section 5.1.1) relies on five models from the Stan manual; expanding to more diverse or challenging models (e.g., multimodal posteriors) would strengthen the case for general applicability. While the hyperparameter fitting protocol is practical, the paper does not rigorously quantify *how often* fitted hyperparameters yield representative simulated data (e.g., across different model classes or datasets), which is a potential limitation for broader adoption. The comparison to established convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, [Gelman and Rubin, 1992]) is missing, making it harder to assess BREAD's relative advantage.

**Significance:**  
This work addresses a critical gap in probabilistic programming: the lack of reliable, automatic metrics for posterior inference quality. By providing a theoretically grounded, implementable method to quantify reliability, BREAD directly supports the goal of making probabilistic modeling accessible to non-experts. The ability to guide model representation choices (Section 5.2.1) and debug implementations (Section 5.2.2) offers tangible, immediate value to practitioners. While not a "breakthrough" in theory, it is a significant practical contribution that advances the usability of probabilistic programming systems.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The technical soundness is excellent, the experiments are thorough, and the practical impact is clear. The minor weaknesses (scope limitations, need for broader validation) do not undermine the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance. To further improve the paper, the authors could briefly discuss the computational cost of running BREAD (e.g., doubling AIS chains) and clarify the conditions under which the hyperparameter fitting protocol fails (e.g., in cases of model misspecification).
