Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and a sampler’s approximate distribution. The key idea is to run annealed importance sampling (AIS) forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior). On simulated data, where an exact posterior draw is available by ancestral sampling, the gap between a forward stochastic lower bound and a reverse stochastic upper bound on log p(y) yields, in expectation, an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the target and the AIS sample distribution. The paper refines the BDMC analysis to formalize this bound, evaluates tightness on toy distributions, proposes a practical protocol (“fitted-hyperparameter simulation”) to approximate the reverse initialization on real datasets, and integrates the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan. Experiments across five models validate consistency between real and simulated data, illustrate robustness to approximate reverse initialization, guide representation choices in a matrix factorization example, and uncover an implementation bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on AIS [Neal 2001] and BDMC [Grosse et al. 2015], leveraging the fact that log AIS estimates yield stochastic (one-sided) bounds on log partition function ratios and that reverse AIS enables an opposite bound when initialized at an exact posterior sample (available on simulated data). It relates to thermodynamic integration and non-equilibrium work identities [Jarzynski 1997; Neal 1996], and to sample-quality metrics that do not assume the sampler (e.g., Stein discrepancies [Gorham & Mackey, NIPS 2015]). The authors also discuss independent concurrent work on “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016], clarifying that their bound reduces to the Jeffreys divergence when the true posterior initializes the reverse chain.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean connection between the BDMC sandwich and an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence for the AIS sample distribution.
- Practicality: a concrete protocol to assess inference accuracy on real problems via fitted hyperparameters; integration into two widely used PPLs (WebPPL, Stan).
- Empirical validation: multiple models/datasets; checks that real vs simulated AIS behavior matches; robustness of the reverse bound to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate posterior hyperparameters.
- Actionable insights: using BREAD to choose between collapsed vs uncollapsed representations and to diagnose implementation errors.
- Careful positioning: limitations of convergence diagnostics are discussed; relation to concurrent work is addressed.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope: the guarantees require at least one exact posterior sample and thus are rigorous only on simulated data; for real data, the fitted-hyperparameter scheme is heuristic (albeit empirically reasonable).
- Applicability: the method evaluates samplers that can be cast as importance sampling over an extended state (AIS/SMC). It does not directly bound the divergence of arbitrary MCMC output (e.g., raw NUTS) unless used within a bridging scheme.
- Nature of the guarantee: the divergence bound holds in expectation; a single run can be optimistic or pessimistic. The variance of the gap estimator and finite-sample behavior are not fully quantified beyond toy cases.
- Sensitivity and design choices: the tightness of bounds depends on annealing schedules, MCMC kernels and their tuning, and the number of temperatures; more guidance on robust schedule design would help.
- Empirics: tightness is demonstrated on small toy examples; in higher-dimensional models the potential looseness is not systematically characterized. Reporting of computational cost vs bound tightness could be expanded.

Quality
Theoretical derivations appear correct and extend BDMC in a meaningful way to bound a relevant divergence between approximate and true posterior sample distributions. The experimental methodology is sensible, and the cross-platform integration is a strong practical contribution. The use of quartiles (rather than means) is reasonable given heavy tails of AIS logs, but more formal uncertainty quantification for the bound gap would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The derivation linking the bidirectional log-ML gap to the Jeffreys divergence is concise and understandable. The real-data protocol is explained clearly, and the experimental sections are easy to follow. A few implementation details (e.g., temperature schedules, kernel choices, step-size/trajectory settings) could be reported more systematically.

Originality
Extending BDMC from marginal likelihood benchmarking to bounding sample quality via the Jeffreys divergence is novel and useful. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol for real data, and the practical PPL integrations, are original contributions. The overlap with concurrent work is acknowledged and the differences are explained.

Significance
Reliable, quantitative measures of MCMC sample quality are a longstanding need, especially for probabilistic programming and black-box inference. A method that can provide rigorous bounds (on simulated data) and informative diagnostics on real data is valuable. Even with scope limitations, the approach can influence model representation choices and help validate and debug inference engines.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Provide guidance or an adaptive strategy for selecting the annealing schedule and number of temperatures to balance cost and tightness, and sensitivity analyses to these choices.
- Quantify the variance of the gap estimator and report confidence intervals for the bound in the experiments; consider combining multiple runs to tighten stochastic bounds (e.g., via techniques like Gogate et al. 2007).
- Discuss failure modes for the fitted-hyperparameter protocol (e.g., weakly informative priors or model misspecification); propose diagnostics to detect mismatch between real and simulated data beyond visual alignment of AIS curves.
- Clarify the transition kernels used within Stan for AIS (e.g., HMC/NUTS settings, reversibility requirements) and potential applicability to SMC.
- Explore comparisons with alternative diagnostics (ESS, R-hat) and with Stein discrepancy-based assessments to position BREAD’s practical benefits.

Pros
- Clear theoretical link to an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.
- Practical, implementable diagnostic integrated into two PPLs.
- Empirical validation across models; provides actionable modeling insights.
- Useful for debugging inference implementations.

Cons
- Guarantees require simulated data; real-data protocol is heuristic.
- Bound is in expectation; finite-sample variability not fully characterized.
- Applicability limited to AIS/SMC-style algorithms and chosen schedules.
- Limited evidence on tightness in higher dimensions and computational trade-offs.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated contribution that extends BDMC to a practically useful diagnostic of posterior sample quality, with clear theory, thoughtful experiments, and meaningful impact for probabilistic programming. I recommend acceptance, with the above clarifications to strengthen the empirical characterization and practical guidance.
