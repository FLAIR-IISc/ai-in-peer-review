- Introduces BREAD, a method to bound Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate MCMC/importance-sampling samples using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC).
- Leverages BDMC to obtain upper and lower bounds on log marginal likelihood and relates these to the Jeffreys divergence.
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates use for diagnosing inference and guiding modeling choices.
- Provides a protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data via simulated data and hyperparameter estimation.
- Theoretical connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence is clearly presented and appears sound.
- Applying BDMC to measure posterior-sample quality is novel and unexplored prior to this work.
- Practical implementations in WebPPL and Stan increase the method’s real-world impact.
- Demonstrated practical benefit by discovering a bug in WebPPL.
- Paper is generally well-written, well-organized, and uses consistent notation.
- BREAD (and BDMC) is computationally expensive, requiring forward and reverse AIS chains, which may limit applicability.
- Method depends on the ability to simulate data from the model, limiting use when the generative model is ill-defined or intractable.
- Assumptions (e.g., viewing algorithms as importance sampling, validity of AIS intermediate distributions, requirement for exact prior samples) need deeper discussion.
- Empirical evaluation is limited—mainly validation plus one matrix factorization example—and should cover more models/datasets.
- Presentation choices (using quartiles) obscure magnitude of differences; error bars or distributions would be more informative.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practically implemented, addresses an important inference challenge, and has demonstrated diagnostic value.
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost, reliance on simulated data, and insufficient empirical breadth.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution deserving acceptance with minor revisions.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to (1) detail computational cost and mitigation strategies, (2) expand discussion of assumptions, (3) broaden empirical evaluation, and (4) present results with error bars or distributions.
