This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), a method for bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples obtained from MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly those formulated as importance sampling (like AIS and SMC). The core idea is to leverage bidirectional Monte Carlo [GGA15] to obtain upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, and relate these bounds to the Jeffreys divergence. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, and demonstrate its utility in diagnosing inference quality and guiding modeling choices.

The paper addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian inference: reliably assessing the quality of approximate posterior samples. While convergence diagnostics exist, they often lack quantitative guarantees. BREAD provides a principled way to upper bound the divergence, offering a more robust assessment. The extension of BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to divergence measurement is a clever and valuable contribution. The authors also tackle the practical issue of applying this technique to real-world data through a bootstrapping-like approach involving hyperparameter estimation and simulated data. The integration with two prominent probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, significantly enhances the practical impact of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation connecting BDMC to the Jeffreys divergence is clearly presented and appears to be logically sound. The derivation builds upon existing work [GGA15] in a natural way.
*   **Novelty:** Applying BDMC to measure the quality of approximate posterior samples is a novel contribution. While BDMC itself is established, this particular application hasn’t been explored before.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The integration with WebPPL and Stan and the demonstration of its use in model comparison (matrix factorization) show the practical utility of the method. The identification of a bug in WebPPL demonstrates a real-world benefit beyond just diagnosing convergence.
*   **Addressing Real-World Challenges:**  The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data is a significant addition, acknowledging the limitation of requiring exact posterior samples for the theoretical guarantees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are reasonably clear.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** BDMC, and thus BREAD, is computationally expensive. Running AIS chains in both directions requires significant computation, potentially limiting its applicability to complex models. This cost isn't fully explored or discussed in terms of its practical implications.
*   **Dependence on Simulated Data:** Despite the protocol for applying BREAD to real data, the method fundamentally relies on being able to simulate data from a model. This might be problematic in situations where the generative model is not well-defined or is intractable.
*   **Assumptions and Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., algorithms needing to be viewable as importance sampling), a more thorough discussion of the assumptions underlying the method (e.g., the validity of the intermediate distributions in AIS) would be beneficial.  The requirement for an exact sample from the prior (and the impact of approximating the posterior for the reverse chain) should be further investigated and highlighted.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation focuses mainly on validation and a single example (matrix factorization). A more comprehensive set of experiments, including a wider range of models and datasets, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Presentation of Results:** Quartiles, while mitigating outlier effects, somewhat obscure the magnitude of the differences observed in the AIS estimates. Showing error bars or distributions would give a more nuanced picture.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for quantifying the quality of approximate posterior samples.
*   The practical implementation and demonstration in WebPPL and Stan significantly enhance the value of the work.
*   The paper addresses an important and long-standing challenge in Bayesian inference.
*   The identification of a bug in WebPPL highlights the potential of BREAD as a diagnostic tool.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of BREAD may limit its practical applicability.
*   The reliance on simulated data introduces potential limitations.
*   The empirical evaluation, while promising, is not extensive enough to fully demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The technical soundness, novelty, and practical relevance of the work are commendable. While the computational cost and reliance on simulated data are legitimate concerns, the authors have acknowledged these limitations and proposed a reasonable approach for applying the method to real-world data. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational cost of BREAD in more detail and explore potential strategies for mitigating it.
2.  Provide a more thorough discussion of the assumptions underlying the method.
3.  Expand the empirical evaluation to include a wider range of models and datasets.
4.  Consider presenting results with error bars or distributions in addition to quartiles.
