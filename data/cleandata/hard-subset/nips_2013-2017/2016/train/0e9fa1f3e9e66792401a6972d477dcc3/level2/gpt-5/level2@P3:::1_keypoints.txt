- Extends BDMC to assess MCMC-based posterior inference: shows forward–reverse AIS log-gap upper-bounds (in expectation) the Jeffreys divergence between AIS terminal distribution and the target posterior (BREAD).

- Proposes a practical protocol for applying BREAD to real data by fitting hyperparameters, simulating matched data, and checking AIS behavior alignment.

- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and demonstrates use on standard models, model-choice comparisons, and discovery of an implementation bug.

- Builds on AIS/BDMC and relates to concurrent subjective divergence work; complements existing diagnostics like R-hat and Stein discrepancies.

- Clear, principled derivation linking expected forward/reverse AIS log estimates to one-sided KLs and the Jeffreys divergence.

- Addresses a practically important problem (quantitative diagnostic for automatic inference) and integrates into widely used probabilistic programming systems.

- Provides a sensible empirical protocol and evidence that forward-AIS behavior aligns between real and matched-simulated datasets across several models.

- Demonstrates real utility: guiding modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and detecting a WebPPL bug.

- Analysis acknowledges limitations (expectation-only nature, heavy-tailed AIS behavior) and includes toy cases to check tightness.

- Applicability limited to samplers expressible as AIS/SMC on an extended space and requires at least one exact posterior sample or proxy for reverse AIS.

- The bound holds only in expectation; single-run forward–reverse gaps are not high-probability upper bounds and estimator variance can be large.

- Limited quantitative study of tightness on moderately high-dimensional, multimodal, or strongly correlated posteriors.

- The fitted-hyperparameter protocol lacks theoretical guarantees and may fail under weak identification or strong prior–likelihood conflict.

- Computational overhead and required number of forward/reverse AIS runs are not reported in detail; practical guidance on AIS design and budgets is limited.

- Comparisons to alternative diagnostics are qualitative; no quantitative head-to-head evaluations demonstrating complementary or superior detection capability.

- Theoretical derivations appear sound; empirical validation is reasonable but needs better uncertainty quantification and runtime reporting.

- Paper is well organized and readable but would benefit from pseudocode, a summary table of datasets/models/AIS settings, and cleaned references.

- Novelty lies in repurposing BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence in expectation and providing a pragmatic real-data protocol with system integrations.

- The contribution is significant for probabilistic programming and Bayesian ML by offering a quantitative, implementation-agnostic sample-quality diagnostic.

- Essential suggested improvements: report confidence intervals for E[gap], provide runtime/compute sensitivity to AIS choices, and include at least one moderate-dimensional challenging posterior with ground-truth or tighter tightness study.

- Optional suggestions: add BREAD pseudocode and AIS scheduling advice, quantitative comparison with other diagnostics (e.g., Stein discrepancy), and illustrate SMC instantiation.

- Arguments for acceptance: solid theoretical contribution, practical system integrations, demonstrated utility and bug discovery, and relevance to probabilistic inference reliability.

- Arguments against acceptance: expectation-only guarantees with high variance, restricted applicability to AIS/SMC, and empirical validation could be deeper on tightness and computational cost.

- Overall recommendation: leaning toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening empirical quantification of estimator stability and reporting computational budgets.
