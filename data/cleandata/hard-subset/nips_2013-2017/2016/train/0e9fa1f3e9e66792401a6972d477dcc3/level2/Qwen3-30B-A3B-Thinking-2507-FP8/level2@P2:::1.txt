

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method that extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to measure the reliability of MCMC-based posterior inference algorithms. The key contribution is showing how BDMC can upper bound the expected Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples, providing a rigorous measure of inference quality. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on several models, and demonstrate its utility in comparing model representations and debugging inference algorithms.

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong. The derivation in Section 3.1 carefully extends the BDMC framework to bound the Jeffreys divergence, with a clear mathematical justification. The authors properly acknowledge related work, including the independent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016), and clarify how their approach differs (particularly regarding the reverse chain initialization). The validation on toy examples (Appendix B) demonstrates that the upper bound closely matches the true Jeffreys divergence in simple cases, providing confidence in the theoretical claims.

The experimental validation is thorough and well-designed. The authors address important practical concerns through their protocol for real-world data evaluation (Section 3.2), demonstrating through five real models that inference behavior on simulated data matches that on real data (Section 5.1.1). They also validate that their approximate posterior initialization for the reverse chain (using MCMC steps from fitted hyperparameters) has negligible impact on the upper bound (Section 5.1.2). The scientific findings in Section 5.2.1 provide concrete evidence of BREAD's utility, showing how it can guide model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization), with different optimal choices in WebPPL versus Stan. The debugging example (Section 5.2.2) further demonstrates practical value.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate background on AIS and BDMC. The connection to probabilistic programming is particularly relevant for NIPS, as it addresses a key challenge in making these systems more reliable for non-expert users. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method immediately applicable to real-world probabilistic programming.

However, some limitations should be noted. The paper's empirical validation is limited to relatively small-scale models (e.g., N=50, D=25 in the matrix factorization example), leaving open questions about scalability to larger problems. While the authors address the "simulated data vs. real data" issue through their protocol, they don't explore how sensitive the results are to the choice of hyperparameters used for simulation. The paper also doesn't provide a thorough comparison of BREAD's computational cost versus other convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin statistics), which would be valuable for practitioners.

The significance of the work is substantial. Reliable evaluation of MCMC inference is a longstanding challenge, and BREAD provides a theoretically grounded, general-purpose solution that can be integrated into probabilistic programming systems. Its ability to guide modeling decisions and uncover implementation bugs (as demonstrated in the WebPPL debugging case) adds practical value beyond just providing a diagnostic metric.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive for the scope of the work, and the practical applications are well-illustrated. The integration into two major probabilistic programming frameworks demonstrates the method's immediate relevance to the NIPS community.

Minor suggestions for improvement: The paper could better quantify the computational overhead of running BREAD compared to standard MCMC diagnostics. A discussion of when BREAD might be impractical (e.g., for extremely large models where AIS is prohibitively expensive) would strengthen the practical assessment. The connection to related work could be further strengthened by more explicitly comparing BREAD's guarantees to those of Gorham and Mackey's method (2015), particularly regarding their convexity assumptions.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the evaluation of MCMC inference. The theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical applications demonstrate clear value. The paper is well-written and appropriately contextualized within the broader literature on MCMC diagnostics and probabilistic programming. I recommend acceptance.
