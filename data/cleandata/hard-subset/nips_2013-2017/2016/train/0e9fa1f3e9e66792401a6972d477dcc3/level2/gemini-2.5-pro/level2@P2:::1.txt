### Summary

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the quality of samples from MCMC-based inference algorithms. The key idea is an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC), which was originally developed for estimating marginal likelihoods. The authors show that the gap between the stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood, obtained by running Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) from the prior to the posterior (forward) and vice versa (reverse), provides an upper bound in expectation on the Jeffreys divergence (symmetrized KL divergence) between the approximate posterior distribution of the samples and the true posterior.

Since the method requires an exact sample from the posterior to initialize the reverse chain, its theoretical guarantees hold only for simulated data. To bridge this gap to real-world applications, the authors propose a practical protocol: first, infer hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate a new dataset from the model using these hyperparameters, and finally run BREAD on this simulated dataset. The paper provides an implementation of BREAD in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan. The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments to validate their protocol and demonstrate the utility of BREAD for guiding modeling decisions (e.g., choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed model representations) and for debugging inference implementations.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the expected gap in bidirectional log-likelihood estimates to the Jeffreys divergence, is a simple but powerful extension of the analysis in Grosse et al. (2015) and appears to be sound. This provides a much-needed, theoretically-grounded tool for assessing sample quality in a field that has long relied on heuristics.

The experimental validation is a major strength. The authors thoughtfully address the main practical limitations of their method.
- The protocol for applying the method to real data is well-motivated, and the experiments in Section 5.1.1 provide convincing evidence that for the models tested, inference behavior on data simulated from fitted hyperparameters is a good proxy for behavior on real data.
- The validation in Section 5.1.2, showing that the reverse AIS estimates are robust to the number of MCMC steps used to generate the initial sample, strengthens the credibility of this protocol.

One minor point that could be clarified is the nature of the bound. The paper shows that the expectation of the gap `E[B]` bounds the Jeffreys divergence `J`. However, practitioners will compute a single estimate `B` (or an average of a few). It would be helpful to include a discussion on the variance of the estimator `B` and how many chains might be needed to obtain a reliable estimate of this bound. The use of quartiles in the plots suggests this variance can be high, which is an important practical consideration.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The background sections on AIS and BDMC provide the necessary context for understanding the main contribution. The motivation for the work is compelling, and the proposed method is explained clearly. The figures are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

A small point of clarification could be made regarding footnote 3. It states that the analysis of Section 3.1 does not justify averaging the weights from multiple AIS chains, which is common practice for reducing the variance of the log marginal likelihood estimate. This is a crucial detail. If individual estimates are used, the resulting bound estimator may be very high variance. A brief explanation in the main text of why averaging is not applicable here and what the implications are for the practitioner would be beneficial.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While it builds directly on the BDMC framework of Grosse et al. (2015), the application of this framework to bound the divergence of posterior samples is a significant and novel conceptual contribution. It reframes a tool for model comparison (marginal likelihood estimation) as a tool for algorithm evaluation (sample quality assessment). The authors also do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to classical convergence diagnostics and recent related work, including a commendably transparent discussion of the concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

### Significance

The significance of this work is very high. The lack of reliable, quantitative, and automatic methods for validating posterior inference is a major bottleneck in the practical application of Bayesian methods, particularly with the rise of "black-box" probabilistic programming languages. BREAD offers a principled solution to this problem.

The paper's two main use cases demonstrate its potential impact:
1.  **Guiding modeling and algorithmic choices:** The analysis of collapsed vs. uncollapsed matrix factorization provides a concrete example of how BREAD can yield non-obvious, quantitative insights that help users write more efficient models for a given inference engine.
2.  **Debugging:** The anecdote about discovering a bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to the utility of having a method with strong theoretical guarantees. As new PPLs are developed, such tools for verification will become indispensable.

The primary limitation, as the authors acknowledge, is the reliance on simulated data and the assumption of a well-specified model for the real-data protocol to be meaningful. However, this is a reasonable and well-justified approach, and the proposed validation checks (e.g., comparing AIS curves) provide a way to assess if the simulation is a faithful proxy.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable and impactful contribution that is likely to be widely used by both researchers developing inference algorithms and practitioners using PPLs to solve real-world problems. It is a strong paper that clearly merits publication.
