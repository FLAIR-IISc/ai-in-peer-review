- Introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to bound symmetrized KL (Jeffreys) divergence between approximate and true posteriors using bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC).  
- Extends BDMC to evaluate MCMC-based algorithms like AIS and SMC, providing an expected upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, enabling practical use and validation across probabilistic programming systems.  
- Proposes a real-world validation protocol: fit hyperparameters to real data, simulate datasets, and apply BREAD to assess representativeness.  
- Demonstrates BREAD guiding model representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed factorization) and identifying implementation bugs.  
- Theoretical contribution is strong and rigorously derived (Section 3.1), with empirically tight bounds on toy examples.  
- Practical utility is shown through integration into major frameworks and a powerful debugging example.  
- Experiments show consistency between simulated and real data across multiple models, supporting the validation protocol.  
- Situates work well relative to prior methods and avoids overclaiming.  
- Limitation: applies only to inference algorithms compatible with AIS/SMC (not standard Metropolis-Hastings without modification).  
- Limitation: real-world validation uses only five Stan-manual models; broader, more challenging (e.g., multimodal) models would strengthen generality.  
- Limitation: paper lacks rigorous quantification of how often fitted hyperparameters produce representative simulated data across model classes.  
- Limitation: missing direct comparison to established convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman-Rubin), leaving relative advantage unclear.  
- Suggestion: authors should discuss computational cost of BREAD (e.g., doubling AIS chains) and clarify failure modes of the hyperparameter fitting protocol.  
- Overall significance: fills an important practical gap by providing theoretically grounded, implementable posterior-quality metrics useful to practitioners.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, well-executed, and practically impactful despite minor scope and validation limitations.
