- Extends BDMC/AIS to bound expected Jeffreys divergence between AIS/SMC-style sampler outputs and the true posterior via forward/reverse AIS gap (BREAD).  
- Integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and proposes a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to transfer diagnostics from simulated to real data.  
- Theoretical derivation that the forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds Jeffreys divergence in expectation is sound and clearly presented.  
- Empirical validation includes exact checks on toy problems, multiple realistic models, and examples where BREAD guides modeling choices and finds bugs.  
- Guarantees for real data rely on the fitted-hyperparameter protocol and are therefore heuristic rather than formal.  
- Tightness of the bound is demonstrated only on small problems; limited assessment of bound tightness at realistic scales.  
- Practical concerns about AIS estimator variance and outlier behavior; uncertainty quantification is lacking (quartiles reported instead of principled intervals).  
- Paper is well written and organized; derivations and limitations are stated plainly.  
- Reproducibility could be improved by providing more guidance on annealing schedules and transition kernels.  
- Original and nontrivial extension of BDMC to posterior sample quality; practical novelty in the fitted-hyperparameter protocol and PPL integrations.  
- Useful contribution for practitioners and PPL developers as an automated diagnostic for sampler reliability and debugging.  
- Scope limited to samplers expressible as importance-sampling over extended state spaces (AIS/SMC-like methods).  
- Suggested improvements: add uncertainty quantification for bounds, analyze sensitivity to temperature schedules and kernels, report computational budgets, and compare to standard diagnostics (R-hat/ESS, Stein discrepancy).  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear theory with practical impact, careful empirical validation, and actionable insights for modeling and debugging.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on simulated-data guarantees for real data, limited evaluation of tightness/variance at scale, and narrow applicability with few comparisons to existing diagnostics.
