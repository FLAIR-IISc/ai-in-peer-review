- Introduces BREAD, a method (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing) to quantitatively measure reliability of MCMC-based inference by extending BDMC to assess approximate posterior samples.  
- Core idea: run AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data and use the expected gap between stochastic log marginal likelihood bounds to upper-bound the Jeffreys divergence.  
- Applicable exactly only to simulated data because the reverse chain needs an exact posterior sample; proposes a pragmatic protocol for real data by estimating hyperparameters, simulating data, and applying BREAD.  
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and provides extensive experiments validating robustness and close matching of inference behavior between real and simulated data.  
- Demonstrates practical utility via case studies: guides model/representation choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed) and discovered a bug in WebPPL.  
- Novel and significant: moves beyond heuristic MCMC diagnostics to a quantitative divergence measure, with the BDMC–Jeffreys divergence connection as a key insight.  
- Practical: empirically shown to be useful for guiding modeling and algorithmic decisions and for software validation.  
- Theoretical rigor: for simulated-data scenarios, the expected BDMC bound gap is a clear, sound upper bound on Jeffreys divergence.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation across five Stan-manual models supports the protocol’s assumptions and robustness to approximate reverse-chain initialization.  
- Limited applicability: directly applies to AIS/SMC-like algorithms but not standard MCMC (MH/HMC/NUTS); connection to performance of standard MCMC is indirect and requires further formalization.  
- Computational cost concern: running many AIS chains in both directions is potentially expensive; scaling behavior and intended use frequency (diagnostic vs. one-off) need discussion.  
- Variance and estimation issue: E[B_hat] bounds J, but practical single-sample B_hat variance and why averaging log-weights across AIS runs isn’t justified require clarification.  
- Heuristic limitation for real data: the simulate-from-estimated-hyperparameters protocol is pragmatic but can fail under model misspecification; this limitation should be emphasized and diagnostics suggested.  
- Overall: high-quality, impactful work with solid theory and experiments; weaknesses are mainly limitations of scope and computational cost rather than flaws, and the reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
