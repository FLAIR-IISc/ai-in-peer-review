This paper addresses the critical and notoriously difficult problem of measuring the reliability of approximate posterior samples generated by Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The authors propose Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing (BREAD), a technique that extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework of Grosse et al. (2015). The core idea is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains in both the forward (prior to posterior) and reverse (posterior to prior) directions on simulated data, for which an exact posterior sample is available. The authors show that the gap between the forward and reverse AIS estimators provides an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the approximate posterior produced by AIS and the true posterior. To extend this method's utility to real-world data, they propose a practical protocol: fit hyperparameters on the real data, simulate new data from this posterior, and then apply BREAD.

The paper has numerous strengths. The primary contribution—re-purposing the BDMC bound gap as a measure of sample quality—is both novel and elegant. The theoretical derivation is sound and clearly articulated. The work is of high quality, supported by a thorough experimental validation that convincingly demonstrates the method's utility. The authors wisely validate their protocol for real-world data, showing that inference behavior is consistent between real and simulated datasets and that the results are robust to the use of approximate posterior samples for initializing the reverse chain. The practical case studies, which use BREAD to compare model representations in Stan and WebPPL and to uncover a subtle implementation bug, are particularly compelling and highlight the significant practical impact this work could have for both developers and users of probabilistic programming languages. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

The main weakness, which the authors forthrightly acknowledge, is that the rigorous mathematical guarantees apply only to simulated data. The proposed protocol for real-world data is a sensible and well-justified heuristic, but it is a heuristic nonetheless. Furthermore, the method is directly applicable only to inference algorithms like AIS or SMC, though the authors argue persuasively that insights gained can inform the design of MCMC algorithms more broadly.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, important, and unsolved problem in probabilistic inference.
*   The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and well-supported by strong experiments.
*   Demonstrates clear practical value for evaluating, comparing, and debugging modern inference systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Rigorous bounds are limited to simulated data, and direct applicability is limited to certain classes of samplers.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. It is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in MCMC diagnostics and is likely to be highly influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
