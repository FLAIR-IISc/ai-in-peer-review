Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC; Grosse, Ghahramani, Adams 2015) from marginal likelihood evaluation to assessing the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. Specifically, for algorithms expressible as importance sampling over an extended state space (notably AIS and SMC), the work shows that the gap between reverse- and forward-AIS log estimates upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the target posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by the sampler. The method—termed BREAD—thus provides a quantitative, asymptotically tight, and implementation-agnostic diagnostic of sample quality on simulated data. The paper further proposes a practical protocol to approximate this assessment for real datasets by fitting hyperparameters on real data, simulating data at those estimates, and checking that AIS behavior matches between real and simulated data. BREAD is integrated into WebPPL and Stan, and is used to validate inference on several standard models, compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations for matrix factorization across systems, and even uncover a sampling bug in WebPPL.

The approach builds directly on AIS (Neal 2001) and BDMC’s reverse-AIS idea, but goes beyond prior analyses by deriving expectation bounds on DJ(p||q)+DJ(q||p) for the marginal distribution of AIS terminal states. It is complementary to classical MCMC convergence diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin 1992; Brooks–Gelman 1998) and to recent measures of sample quality such as Stein discrepancies (Gorham–Mackey 2015). It is closely related to concurrent work on subjective divergence in probabilistic programming (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka 2016), which becomes equivalent to BREAD when the true posterior is used as the reverse-chain initializer.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled derivation showing that the forward–reverse AIS log-gap upper bounds in expectation the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS terminal distribution and the target posterior.
- Focus on a practically important problem—quantitatively diagnosing reliability of automatic inference—and integration into two widely used probabilistic programming systems (WebPPL and Stan).
- Sensible and carefully validated protocol for bridging from simulated to real data via fitted hyperparameters; empirical evidence that forward-AIS behavior aligns between real and matched-simulated datasets across several models.
- Demonstrated utility beyond theory: guiding modeling choices (collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and detecting an implementation bug.
- Analysis acknowledges the “in expectation” nature of the bound and heavy-tailed behavior of AIS, and provides toy cases where tightness can be checked exactly.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Applicability is limited to inference schemes that can be cast as AIS/SMC over an extended space and require at least one exact posterior sample (or a proxy) to run reverse AIS. This excludes many standard MCMC configurations in their native form.
- The reported bound is only in expectation; the single-run forward–reverse gap is not itself a high-probability upper bound on Jeffreys divergence. Variance can be large, and the paper primarily visualizes quartiles rather than providing sampling-error control for the expectation estimate.
- Tightness is validated on toy distributions and via qualitative alignment on real/simulated data, but there is limited quantitative study of tightness in higher-dimensional, realistically challenging posteriors where multimodality and strong correlations arise.
- The fitted-hyperparameter protocol lacks theoretical guarantees and relies on the assumption that approximate posterior samples after a small number of transitions are “close enough” for reverse AIS. While the empirical study across five models is reassuring, guidance on when this can fail (e.g., weakly identified hyperparameters or strong prior–likelihood conflict) would help.
- Computational overhead is not reported in detail; the method may be expensive due to multiple forward and reverse AIS runs. Practical guidance on how many chains and intermediate distributions are needed to obtain a stable expectation estimate is limited.
- Comparisons to alternative diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, Stein discrepancies) are qualitative; there is no side-by-side quantitative evaluation demonstrating cases where BREAD reveals failures that others miss or vice versa.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears sound: the derivations for E[log R̂] and E[log R̂rev] and the use of marginalization to relate the expectations to one-sided KLs are standard and correctly applied, and the expectation bound for the Jeffreys divergence follows cleanly. The empirical validation is sensible given constraints, though more thorough quantification of uncertainty of the expectation estimates and runtime/compute budgets would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The exposition of AIS/BDMC prerequisites is clear, and the contribution is easy to follow. Notational consistency is good, though an explicit pseudo-code box summarizing the BREAD procedure (inputs, outputs, and AIS scheduling choices) would improve reproducibility. A table summarizing the datasets/models and AIS settings would also help. Some references appear with placeholder formatting; these should be cleaned.

Originality:
The main novelty is repurposing BDMC to bound, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between AIS terminal distributions and the true posterior, together with a pragmatic protocol for real data and system integrations that demonstrate actionable benefits. While conceptually close to BDMC and concurrent subjective divergence work, the contribution is nontrivial and useful.

Significance:
The ability to quantify sample quality for black-box inference is impactful for probabilistic programming and Bayesian ML. The presented applications—modeling decisions and debugging—underscore practical significance. With wider adoption, the method could meaningfully improve reliability of MCMC-based tools.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Report statistical uncertainty of the estimated expectation bound: provide confidence intervals for E[gap] via multiple independent runs and discuss the number of chains required for stable estimates.
- Provide runtime/compute overhead and sensitivity to AIS design choices (temperature schedule, number of intermediate distributions, transition kernels).
- Include at least one moderate-dimensional, challenging posterior (e.g., correlated Gaussian with strong conditioning or a hierarchical logistic regression) with ground-truth KL where possible, or a tighter empirical tightness study.

Optional:
- Add pseudo-code for BREAD and practical recommendations for setting AIS schedules in Stan/WebPPL.
- Compare quantitatively with at least one alternative diagnostic (e.g., Stein discrepancy) on the same simulated tasks.
- Explore SMC instantiation to illustrate generality beyond AIS.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contribution with clear derivation and a practically relevant diagnostic.
- Demonstrated utility across systems, models, and an identified real bug.
- Addresses an important gap in probabilistic programming: trustworthy, quantitative sample-quality assessment.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Expectation-only nature of the bound and limited analysis of estimator variance may limit immediate practical adoption.
- Applicability restricted to AIS/SMC-style samplers and simulated-data settings; guarantees do not directly extend to real datasets.
- Empirical validation could be deeper on tightness and computational cost.

Overall recommendation:
Leaning toward acceptance. The contribution is methodologically sound, useful in practice, and well-aligned with NIPS interests in probabilistic inference and probabilistic programming. Strengthening the empirical quantification of estimator stability and reporting computational budgets would further solidify the case.
