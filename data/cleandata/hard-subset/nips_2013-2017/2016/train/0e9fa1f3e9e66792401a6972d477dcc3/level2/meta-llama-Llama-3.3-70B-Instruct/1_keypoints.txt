- Introduces BREAD, a method for evaluating approximate posterior inference specifically for MCMC/AIS-style algorithms.
- Extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide bounds on the Jeffreys divergence between true posterior and approximate samples.
- Method applies to algorithms viewable as importance sampling over an extended state space (e.g., AIS, SMC).
- Proves BDMC yields an estimator that upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation.
- Gives a technique to compute exact Jeffreys divergence and the upper bound on small examples, showing close practical agreement.
- Proposes a protocol for evaluating inference on real data by simulating datasets from fitted hyperparameters.
- Implements BREAD in WebPPL and Stan and validates it on a variety of probabilistic models.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis underpinning the method.
- Strength: comprehensive, well-designed experimental evaluation across models and datasets.
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation that aids understanding.
- Limitation: directly applicable only to certain algorithm classes (not universally applicable).
- Limitation: real-data evaluation relies on simulated data from fitted hyperparameters, which may not reflect real data distributions.
- Limitation: additional work needed to broaden applicability and further validate on more models/datasets.
- Novelty: new and useful use of BDMC to bound Jeffreys divergence, advancing evaluation of approximate inference.
- Pro-acceptance points: novel, rigorous, effective in experiments, and useful for debugging probabilistic programming.
- Anti-acceptance points: limited algorithmic scope, reliance on simulation-based protocol for real data, and need for wider validation.
