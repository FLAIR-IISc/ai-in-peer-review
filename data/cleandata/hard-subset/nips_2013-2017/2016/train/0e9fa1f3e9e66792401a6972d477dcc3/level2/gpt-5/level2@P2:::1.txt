Summary
This paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to assess the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by upper bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples produced by AIS/SMC. The key idea is to run AIS in both directions (prior→posterior and posterior→prior) on simulated data to obtain stochastic lower and upper bounds on log marginal likelihood; the gap between these bounds has an expected value that upper bounds DJ(q||p). The authors (i) formalize this connection (deriving bounds for both one-sided KLs as well), (ii) show tightness on toy cases, (iii) propose a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to transfer diagnostics from simulated to real datasets, and (iv) implement and evaluate the method in WebPPL and Stan on several models. They further use the method to compare collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations in matrix factorization and to identify a bug in WebPPL.

Relation to prior work
BDMC [Grosse et al., 2015] and AIS [Neal, 2001] form the technical basis. The paper connects BDMC’s log-partition bounds to posterior sample quality (DJ), complementing convergence diagnostics (e.g., Gelman–Rubin, Brooks–Gelman) and recent sample-quality measures (e.g., Stein discrepancies [Gorham & Mackey, 2015]). The independent work on “subjective divergence” [Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka, 2016] is discussed; the equivalence when the reverse chain is initialized from the true posterior matches the analysis here. The paper’s contribution is to (a) make the Jeffreys-divergence link explicit, (b) provide a practical protocol for real data, and (c) deliver implementations in two PPLs.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation showing that E[log R̂−log R̂rev] ≥ DJ(q||p) is sound and clearly presented, reusing the AIS extended-state-space view. The one-sided KL bounds with auxiliary upper/lower bounds are also correct. However, the guarantee is in expectation over AIS randomness; the paper does not provide concentration results translating finite-sample estimates of the gap into high-probability upper bounds on DJ. This limits the per-dataset diagnostic strength.
- Practicality: The fitted-hyperparameter simulation protocol is reasonable, and the empirical checks (consistency of forward AIS on real vs. simulated data; insensitivity of reverse AIS to the number of MCMC steps used to approximate the posterior hyperparameters) support its use. The experiments cover five standard models and a matrix factorization case study.
- Methodological care: Important implementation subtleties deserve clarification. AIS requires kernels that leave each intermediate pt invariant. If Stan/NUTS is used inside AIS, adaptivity during sampling can violate invariance; the paper should explicitly state that all NUTS adaptation was disabled while running AIS transitions and discuss reversibility/acceptance-correction details. Similarly, numerical integrator error and step-size schedules could affect correctness if not handled carefully.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The background on stochastic bounds and reverse AIS is helpful. The BREAD estimator and the fitted-hyperparameter protocol are clearly described. Figures prioritizing quartiles are reasonable given heavy-tailed AIS estimates, but it would be helpful to report means and standard errors of log-weights (or gap estimates) as well, since expectations are the quantities in the guarantees. More detail on the intermediate temperature schedule, number of transitions per temperature, and computational budget would improve reproducibility and aid interpretation.

Originality
The main novelty is repurposing BDMC’s two-sided log-ML bounds into an estimator that upper bounds, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence of approximate posterior samples, and integrating this into PPL toolchains. While related to concurrent “subjective divergence,” the paper’s derivation, implementations, and real-data protocol constitute a distinct and useful contribution.

Significance
The work provides a principled, general-purpose diagnostic that is more interpretable than standard MCMC heuristics and can catch real issues (e.g., the WebPPL bug). The ability to compare model representations quantitatively is valuable for PPL users. Limitations remain: applicability is strongest for AIS/SMC-style samplers and simulated data; the fitted-hyperparameter approach is heuristic; and the expectation-only guarantee weakens per-run certification. Nevertheless, the approach should influence practice in probabilistic programming and spur further research on actionable sample-quality guarantees.

Pros
- Clear theoretical link: expected bound on DJ via BDMC gap; extensions to one-sided KLs.
- Practical protocol bridging simulated and real data; empirical validation across five models.
- Implementations in WebPPL and Stan; demonstrates usefulness for model representation choices and debugging.
- Careful discussion of related work and limitations.

Cons
- Only expectation guarantees; no finite-sample, high-probability bounds on DJ or guidance on the number of AIS runs needed to estimate E[gap] with confidence.
- Real-data evaluation relies on heuristic fitted-hyperparameter initialization for reverse chains; while validated empirically, it lacks theoretical guarantees.
- AIS kernel correctness under Stan/NUTS requires explicit handling of adaptivity and reversibility; the paper should detail this.
- Limited exploration of multimodal/hard posteriors and SMC implementations; most experiments are AIS-based and relatively standard models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample uncertainty quantification for the DJ bound estimate: report the sample mean and confidence intervals for the gap (using multiple independent pairs of forward/reverse AIS runs), and discuss bias/variance trade-offs.
- Clarify AIS kernel construction with HMC/NUTS: confirm that adaptation is off during sampling, that proposals leave pt invariant via a Metropolis correction, and discuss any practical deviations and their impact.
- Analyze computational cost: report wall-clock time vs. bound tightness and offer guidelines for choosing number of temperatures, transitions per temperature, and number of AIS chains.
- Expand experiments to include a clearly multimodal posterior and an SMC-based implementation to support the claimed generality.
- Discuss how to use the method to tune annealing schedules or transition kernels automatically (closing the loop from diagnosis to configuration).

Recommendation
Lean toward acceptance. The paper provides a principled and practical diagnostic with clear theoretical grounding, useful implementations, and convincing case studies. Addressing the points above—especially kernel correctness details and uncertainty quantification of the bound—would further strengthen the contribution.
