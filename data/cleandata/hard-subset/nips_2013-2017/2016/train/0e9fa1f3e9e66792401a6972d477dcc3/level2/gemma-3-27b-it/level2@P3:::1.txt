This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) designed to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to use annealed importance sampling (AIS) in both forward and reverse directions to upper bound the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the true posterior and the approximate samples generated by the inference algorithm. The work integrates BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and validates its use on several models, showcasing its ability to guide modeling and algorithmic choices.

The paper addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian inference: reliably assessing the accuracy of approximate posterior samples. Current diagnostics are often heuristics, lacking quantitative guarantees. This work directly tackles this issue with a mathematically grounded approach. The extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a significant contribution, particularly its applicability to algorithms like AIS and SMC, which share characteristics with traditional MCMC methods. The successful integration of BREAD into popular probabilistic programming languages (WebPPL and Stan) increases its accessibility and potential impact. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, by simulating data from inferred hyperparameters, is a clever solution to the problem of requiring exact posterior samples.

A strength of the paper is the thoroughness of the validation. The authors not only demonstrate the accuracy of the bound on toy distributions but also perform extensive experiments on models within WebPPL and Stan, verifying the consistency of results between real and simulated data. The identification and debugging of a bug in WebPPL further highlight the practical utility of BREAD as a testing tool. The use of quartiles to visualize the distributions of AIS estimates is appropriate given the potential for outliers. The analysis of matrix factorization model representations with BREAD provides a clear example of how the method can inform model design decisions.

However, several areas could be improved. The presentation, while generally clear, suffers from a somewhat disjointed flow, especially within Section 3. The derivation in Section 3.1 feels dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key steps. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the method for non-simulated data, further discussion on the sensitivity of the hyperparameter fitting protocol to model misspecification would be valuable. The experiments focus heavily on Stan and WebPPL. Demonstrating the applicability of BREAD to other inference frameworks would strengthen the generalizability of the findings. The connection to related work [CTM16] is adequately discussed, but a deeper comparison of the strengths and weaknesses of both approaches would be beneficial. The notation could also be made more consistent throughout the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach for quantifying the quality of approximate posterior samples.
*   Successful integration into widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Thorough validation on multiple models and datasets.
*   Demonstrated practical utility for debugging and informing model design.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian inference with a rigorous methodology.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Presentation can be dense and somewhat disjointed.
*   Limited exploration of applicability beyond Stan and WebPPL.
*   Discussion of robustness to model misspecification could be expanded.



Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian inference. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses. Addressing the suggested improvements would further enhance the paper's clarity and impact. A minor revision is recommended.
