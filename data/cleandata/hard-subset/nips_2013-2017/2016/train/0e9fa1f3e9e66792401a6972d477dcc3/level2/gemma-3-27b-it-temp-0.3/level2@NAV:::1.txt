## Review of "Measuring the reliability of MCMC inference with bidirectional Monte Carlo"

This paper presents a method, BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), for upper-bounding the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and approximate samples obtained from MCMC-based inference algorithms, particularly those leveraging annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The core idea extends bidirectional Monte Carlo [GGA15] to provide a quantifiable measure of inference reliability, addressing a long-standing challenge in probabilistic inference.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of validating MCMC convergence and proposes a rigorous approach using BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence. The theoretical derivation is well-presented, building upon existing work on stochastic bounds and AIS. A key contribution is the extension of BDMC to this specific context and the demonstration of its accuracy on toy distributions. The integration of BREAD into WebPPL and Stan is a significant practical step, and the validation on several models is commendable. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data, involving hyperparameter inference and simulated data generation, is a clever workaround for the requirement of exact posterior samples. 

A strength of the paper lies in its thoroughness. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the applicability to algorithms amenable to importance sampling and the need for simulated data for rigorous bounds. The experimental validation, including the discovery of a bug in WebPPL, highlights the utility of BREAD as a diagnostic tool. The comparison of collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations is a valuable application demonstrating the practical insights BREAD can provide. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of computational cost. While the authors mention per-iteration costs, a more detailed analysis of the overall computational burden of BREAD, particularly for high-dimensional models, would be helpful. Additionally, the reliance on Stan and WebPPL limits the immediate generalizability of the implementation. While these are reasonable choices for demonstration, exploring applicability to other probabilistic programming frameworks would strengthen the work. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound method for quantifying MCMC inference reliability.
*   Provides a practical implementation integrated into widely used probabilistic programming languages.
*   Demonstrates the utility of the method through validation and a compelling case study.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost could be a limiting factor for complex models.
*   Implementation is currently limited to Stan and WebPPL.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound solution. The practical demonstration and validation are convincing. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational cost discussion and potentially broaden the scope of implementation.
