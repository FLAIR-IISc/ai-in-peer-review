This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference. The core idea is to upper bound the Jeffreys divergence between the true posterior and the distribution of approximate samples obtained from algorithms like AIS or SMC. This is achieved by running AIS chains both forward (prior to posterior) and in reverse, leveraging the properties of BDMC to sandwich the log marginal likelihood and, consequently, bound the divergence. The authors integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan, validate it on several models, and demonstrate its use in guiding model representation choices.

The paper is well-motivated. Evaluating the reliability of MCMC is notoriously difficult, and the rise of probabilistic programming necessitates automated, robust diagnostics. BDMC provides a promising framework, and extending it to bound the Jeffreys divergence is a natural and valuable step. The connection to existing work on stochastic lower/upper bounds and related techniques like tempered transitions is clearly outlined. The integration into WebPPL and Stan demonstrates practical applicability, and the validation experiments, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence of effectiveness.  The work builds nicely on [GGA15] and [CTM16], clearly differentiating their approach from Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka’s work based on the reference distribution used in the reverse chain. The discussion of the potential to debug implementations via inconsistencies in forward/reverse bounds is a particularly insightful observation.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the Jeffreys divergence bound appears rigorous, building on the established framework of BDMC. The connection between bounding the log marginal likelihood and bounding the divergence is clearly explained.
* **Practical Relevance:** The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the method directly usable by practitioners. The application to model representation choice illustrates a tangible benefit beyond simply quantifying accuracy.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background material on AIS and BDMC is concise but sufficient.  The descriptions of the experiments are clear and the results are presented in a digestible format.
* **Novelty:** Extending BDMC to this specific purpose (bounding the Jeffreys divergence for MCMC validation) is novel. The protocol for applying BREAD to real-world data using fitted hyperparameters is a pragmatic contribution.
* **Debugging Application:** Identifying the potential use of BREAD for debugging probabilistic programming language implementations is a significant and often overlooked benefit of such a diagnostic tool.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper acknowledges the computational demands of running AIS chains, the practical implications for large, complex models are not fully explored. The authors don’t discuss strategies for mitigating this cost (e.g., using shorter chains, adaptive chain lengths).  This is a significant barrier to adoption in many real-world scenarios.
* **Simulated Data Reliance:** The method relies heavily on the ability to generate simulated data, which isn’t always possible or straightforward. While the proposed protocol for using fitted hyperparameters mitigates this issue, its effectiveness relies on the assumption that the fitted hyperparameters accurately capture the underlying data generating process.  The sensitivity of the results to the quality of the hyperparameter fit is not fully investigated.
* **Limited Scope of Validation:** The validation experiments are limited in scope. The authors consider only a handful of models and datasets. It’s unclear how well the method generalizes to more challenging inference problems (e.g., highly multimodal posteriors, models with many parameters).
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Diagnostics:** The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison of BREAD to existing MCMC diagnostics (e.g., R-hat, effective sample size). It would be valuable to know how BREAD performs relative to these established techniques in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and ease of use.
* **Assumptions of the Method:** The paper mentions that the method requires having access to an exact posterior sample. This is only possible in simulated data or when strong simplifying assumptions are made. A deeper discussion of the impact of using approximate posterior samples for initializing the reverse chain would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical problem in probabilistic inference (reliable evaluation of MCMC).
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach based on established principles (BDMC).
* Demonstrates practical applicability through integration into widely used probabilistic programming languages.
* Offers potential for debugging inference engines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Relatively high computational cost may limit applicability to complex models.
*  Dependence on simulated data or accurate hyperparameter fitting.
*  Limited validation on a diverse set of models and datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic inference. While the computational cost and reliance on simulated data are legitimate concerns, the novelty, technical soundness, and practical relevance of the work outweigh these drawbacks.  The paper clearly demonstrates the potential of BREAD as a robust and reliable diagnostic tool for MCMC-based inference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more thorough discussion of computational complexity and comparisons to existing diagnostic techniques.  Specifically, the authors should include a discussion of strategies for scaling BREAD to larger models and an expanded validation section.
