This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a novel method for quantitatively measuring the reliability of MCMC-based inference algorithms. The work extends the Bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) framework, previously used for marginal likelihood estimation, to the problem of evaluating the quality of approximate posterior samples. The core idea is to run Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) chains on simulated data, both in the forward direction (from prior to posterior) and in the reverse direction (from posterior to prior). The authors show that the expected gap between the resulting stochastic upper and lower bounds on the log marginal likelihood provides a principled upper bound on the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between the distribution of approximate samples and the true posterior.

Since the method requires an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, it is strictly applicable only to simulated data. To bridge this gap, the paper proposes a practical protocol for evaluating inference on real-world datasets: first, estimate hyperparameters on the real data, then simulate a new dataset from these hyperparameters, and finally apply BREAD to this simulated dataset. The authors implement their method in two popular probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and provide a thorough experimental validation. The experiments show that the method is robust and that inference behavior on data simulated via their protocol closely matches behavior on real data. Finally, the utility of BREAD is demonstrated through a case study comparing different model representations for matrix factorization and by identifying a bug in the WebPPL implementation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** This paper addresses the critical and long-standing problem of evaluating the quality of approximate MCMC inference. Moving beyond heuristic convergence diagnostics to a quantitative measure of divergence from the true posterior is a significant contribution. The connection between the BDMC bounds and the Jeffreys divergence is a key novel insight that makes this work possible. This tool is of immense value to both developers and users of probabilistic programming languages and other black-box inference systems.

2.  **Practical Utility:** The proposed method, BREAD, is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical tool. The experiments convincingly demonstrate its utility for guiding important modeling and algorithmic decisions, such as choosing between collapsed and uncollapsed representations of a model. The fact that it was used to find a real implementation bug in WebPPL is a powerful testament to its usefulness for software validation.

3.  **Rigorous Foundations:** For the simulated data case, the method rests on a solid theoretical foundation. The derivation that the expected gap between the BDMC bounds is an upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence is clear and sound. This provides a level of rigor that is often absent in MCMC diagnostics.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength of the paper. The authors thoughtfully design experiments to validate the key assumptions of their proposed protocol for real-world data, namely the consistency of inference behavior between real and simulated data and the robustness to using an approximate posterior sample for the reverse chain initialization. The validation across five different models from the Stan manual is comprehensive and adds credibility to the approach.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Applicability to General MCMC:** The method is directly applicable to algorithms like AIS and SMC, but not to more standard MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings or HMC/NUTS. The paper argues that insights gained from evaluating AIS can inform the design of other MCMC methods, as they share design choices (e.g., transition operators, model parameterization). While this is a reasonable claim, the evaluation remains indirect. It would be helpful if the authors could elaborate on how one might more formally connect the performance of an AIS chain with the performance of a standard MCMC algorithm using the same transition kernels.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The BREAD procedure requires running many AIS chains, each potentially with many intermediate steps, in two directions. This seems computationally intensive. A discussion of the computational overhead and how it scales with the number of AIS steps or model complexity would be a valuable addition. Is this method intended as a frequent diagnostic, or as a more intensive, one-off check during model development?

3.  **The Nature of the Bound:** The paper shows that the expectation of the bound gap, `E[B_hat]`, upper bounds the Jeffreys divergence `J`. However, in practice, one obtains one or more samples of `B_hat`. Could the authors comment on the variance of `B_hat`? A bound with very high variance might be less useful in practice. Relatedly, Footnote 3 states that the analysis does not justify averaging weights from multiple AIS chains and that estimates from individual chains are reported. This is slightly confusing, as one typically averages estimates to reduce variance. Could the authors clarify why averaging `log(R)` and `log(1/R_rev)` over multiple independent runs is not valid here for producing a tighter estimate of their respective expectations, and thus a tighter estimate of `E[B_hat]`?

4.  **Heuristic for Real Data:** The protocol for real-world data is pragmatic and well-motivated. However, it remains a heuristic, as the reverse chain is not initialized from a true posterior sample. The success of this protocol rests on the similarity of the inference problem on real vs. simulated data. The paper acknowledges this and provides an example of failure due to model misspecification in the appendix. It would be beneficial to highlight this limitation more prominently in the main text and perhaps suggest diagnostics for when the protocol might be expected to fail.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-founded, and highly useful method for evaluating the quality of MCMC inference. The work is a significant step forward from traditional convergence diagnostics and provides a much-needed tool for the growing community of probabilistic programming users. The paper is clearly written, and the claims are supported by strong theoretical arguments and comprehensive experiments. The identified weaknesses are primarily limitations of the approach rather than flaws in the work, and the authors are generally upfront about them. I believe this paper will have a significant impact and will be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
