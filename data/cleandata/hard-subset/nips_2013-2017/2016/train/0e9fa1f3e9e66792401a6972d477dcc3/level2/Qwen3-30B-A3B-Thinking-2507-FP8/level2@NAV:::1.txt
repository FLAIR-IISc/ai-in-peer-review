

This paper proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), an extension of bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to rigorously measure the quality of MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate samples and the true posterior. The authors demonstrate that BDMC can be used to derive an upper bound in expectation on this divergence, validate the bound on toy distributions, and propose a protocol for applying it to real-world data by simulating data from fitted hyperparameters. They integrate BREAD into WebPPL and Stan and apply it to analyze model representations and detect implementation bugs.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear derivation showing how BDMC bounds the Jeffreys divergence. The validation on toy distributions demonstrates the bound's accuracy, and the experiments on real-world models in two probabilistic programming languages provide practical evidence of the method's utility. The ability to detect a bug in WebPPL through inconsistent reverse AIS estimates is particularly compelling evidence of the method's practical value.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured theoretical derivations and thorough experimental validation. It appropriately situates itself within the literature, acknowledging related work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) while clearly distinguishing their contributions. The method addresses a significant problem in probabilistic programming: the lack of reliable metrics to assess MCMC inference quality.

The main limitations are that the method applies only to algorithms like AIS or SMC (not general MCMC), and the real-world protocol relies on the assumption that fitted hyperparameters yield representative simulated data. While the authors validate this assumption empirically, the paper could more thoroughly explore the conditions under which the protocol fails.

Strengths: Novel theoretical contribution, practical implementation, clear validation, useful for probabilistic programming users. Weaknesses: Limited applicability to specific inference algorithms, reliance on simulated data protocol for real-world evaluation.

This work represents a significant advance in the field of probabilistic inference quality assessment. The method provides a rigorous, automatic procedure for monitoring inference quality in probabilistic programming systems, addressing a critical need for non-expert users. The paper is well-executed and makes a strong case for the value of BREAD. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to further clarify the limitations of the real-world protocol.
