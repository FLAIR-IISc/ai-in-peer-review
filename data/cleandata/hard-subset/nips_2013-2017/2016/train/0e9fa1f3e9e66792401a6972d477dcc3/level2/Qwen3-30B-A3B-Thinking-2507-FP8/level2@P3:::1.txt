

This paper introduces BREAD (Bounding Divergences with Reverse Annealing), a method to rigorously measure the reliability of MCMC inference by bounding the symmetrized KL divergence (Jeffreys divergence) between approximate posterior samples and the true posterior. The authors extend bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to provide an upper bound in expectation on this divergence, which is applicable to inference algorithms that can be viewed as importance sampling over an extended state space, such as annealed importance sampling (AIS) or sequential Monte Carlo (SMC). The method is integrated into two probabilistic programming languages, WebPPL and Stan, and validated on multiple models.

The main technical contributions include: (1) deriving a theoretical bound on the Jeffreys divergence using BDMC, (2) developing a protocol for applying the method to real-world data through hyperparameter estimation and simulation, (3) implementing BREAD in WebPPL and Stan, and (4) using the method to analyze model representations in both frameworks.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of the problem it addresses: MCMC inference is widely used but notoriously difficult to validate. The authors provide a theoretically sound approach that yields rigorous bounds, which is a significant advancement over heuristic convergence diagnostics. The connection between BDMC and Jeffreys divergence is well-motivated and carefully derived, with the key insight that running AIS both forward and backward provides a sandwich bound on the log marginal likelihood, which can be translated to a bound on the Jeffreys divergence. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the paper appropriately references related work, including the independent concurrent work by Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016).

The experimental validation is thorough and well-executed. The authors demonstrate the method's accuracy on toy distributions where exact Jeffreys divergence can be computed, and they validate the protocol for real-world data through multiple models (linear regression, logistic regression, matrix factorization, etc.). The comparison between simulated and real data shows consistent inference behavior, and the sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter sampling procedure demonstrates robustness. The practical application to comparing collapsed vs. uncollapsed model representations in WebPPL and Stan provides concrete insights that would be valuable to practitioners. The debugging of a WebPPL bug through the method's failure to produce expected bounds is particularly compelling evidence of the method's practical utility.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The background on AIS and BDMC is sufficient for readers familiar with MCMC, and the methodological contributions are explained with appropriate mathematical rigor. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the work immediately applicable to practitioners, and the open-source implementation would facilitate adoption.

The primary limitation is that the method is restricted to inference algorithms that can be framed as importance sampling over an extended state space (like AIS and SMC), which excludes some common MCMC approaches. However, the authors reasonably argue that these algorithms involve many of the same design choices as traditional MCMC methods, and the insights gained can inform broader inference algorithm development. The method's applicability to probabilistic programming languages, where users often lack expertise to validate inference quality, is a significant practical contribution.

The paper addresses an important problem in probabilistic inference with a theoretically sound and practically useful approach. The method provides a rigorous alternative to heuristic convergence diagnostics, and the validation experiments demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple models and frameworks. The integration into WebPPL and Stan makes the contribution immediately applicable to the broader community. The comparison with related work, particularly the concurrent Cusumano-Towner and Mansinghka (2016) work, is appropriately handled and highlights the paper's distinct contributions.

This work advances the state of the art in MCMC inference validation by providing a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically implementable. The insights gained from applying the method to model representation choices demonstrate its utility for guiding algorithmic and modeling decisions. The paper meets all four criteria for NIPS: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, original in its extension of BDMC to bound the Jeffreys divergence, and significant in providing a rigorous tool for practitioners to assess inference quality in probabilistic programming systems.
