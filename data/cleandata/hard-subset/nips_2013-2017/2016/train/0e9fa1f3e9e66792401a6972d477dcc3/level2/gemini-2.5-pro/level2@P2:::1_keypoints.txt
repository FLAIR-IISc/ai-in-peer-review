- Introduces BREAD, a method extending BDMC to quantitatively bound sample quality from MCMC by relating bidirectional AIS log-marginal gaps to Jeffreys divergence.
- Proves the expected gap between forward and reverse AIS bounds provides an upper bound on the Jeffreys (symmetrized KL) divergence.
- Theoretical guarantees require an exact posterior sample to initialize the reverse chain, so they hold only for simulated data.
- Proposes a practical real-data protocol: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from the fitted model, and run BREAD on that simulation.
- Provides implementations of BREAD in WebPPL and Stan.
- Experimental validation is thorough and shows inference on simulated-from-fitted data is a good proxy for real data in tested models.
- Experiments show reverse AIS estimates are robust to the number of MCMC steps used to generate the initial sample.
- Highlights usefulness for guiding modeling/algorithmic choices (e.g., collapsed vs. uncollapsed representations) and for debugging inference implementations.
- Notes a practical concern: practitioners compute single/finite B estimates, so estimator variance and required number of chains should be discussed.
- Points out averaging AIS weights (common practice) is not justified by their analysis and may reduce the bound's validity or change variance properties.
- Writing, organization, and figures are excellent and make the contribution clear and accessible.
- Work is original (novel use of BDMC for sample-quality assessment), significant, and merits publication despite reliance on simulated-data assumptions.
