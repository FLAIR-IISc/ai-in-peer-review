- Addresses the hard problem of measuring reliability of approximate posterior samples from MCMC methods.
- Proposes BREAD (Bounding Divergences with REverse Annealing), extending BDMC from Grosse et al. (2015).
- Core idea: run AIS chains forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data with an exact posterior sample.
- Shows the gap between forward and reverse AIS estimators gives an expected upper bound on the Jeffreys divergence between the AIS approximate posterior and the true posterior.
- Introduces a practical protocol for real data: fit hyperparameters on real data, simulate data from that posterior, then apply BREAD.
- Novel and elegant re-purposing of the BDMC bound gap as a sample-quality measure.
- Theoretical derivation is sound and clearly presented.
- Thorough experimental validation demonstrating utility and robustness of the method.
- Validates protocol for real-world data, showing consistent inference behavior between real and simulated datasets and robustness to approximate reverse-chain initialization.
- Strong practical case studies: comparing Stan and WebPPL and discovering a subtle implementation bug.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
- Main weakness: rigorous guarantees hold only for simulated data; the real-data protocol is a justified but heuristic approach.
- Applicability is directly limited to samplers like AIS/SMC, though findings may inform broader MCMC design.
- Overall assessment: strengths substantially outweigh limitations; strong recommendation for acceptance.
