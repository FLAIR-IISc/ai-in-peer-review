Summary and relation to prior work: The paper extends bidirectional Monte Carlo (BDMC) to evaluate MCMC-based posterior inference by bounding, in expectation, the Jeffreys divergence between approximate samples (from AIS/SMC-style samplers) and the true posterior. The core idea—running AIS forward (prior→posterior) and reverse (posterior→prior) on simulated data to sandwich log marginal likelihood—yields an expected upper bound on DJ(q||p) via the gap between stochastic upper and lower bounds. The authors integrate the method (BREAD) into WebPPL and Stan, propose a fitted-hyperparameter protocol to transfer diagnostics to real data, validate on several models, and show how BREAD informs modeling choices (collapsed vs uncollapsed matrix factorization) and even uncovers an implementation bug. The work builds directly on AIS (Neal), BDMC (Grosse et al.), and connects to thermodynamic integration, MCMC diagnostics (Gelman–Rubin; Brooks–Gelman), and sample-quality measures (Stein discrepancy; Gorham–Mackey). It acknowledges concurrent work on subjective divergence (Cusumano-Towner & Mansinghka).

Quality: The derivation that the forward–reverse AIS gap upper-bounds the Jeffreys divergence in expectation is sound and clearly presented. Empirical support is careful: exact checks on toy problems, multiple realistic models, and a sensibly designed protocol for fitted hyperparameters. However, guarantees for real data are heuristic; tightness is demonstrated only on small cases; and variance/outlier behavior of AIS estimates is a practical concern (quartiles reported rather than principled uncertainty).

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized, with derivations and limitations stated plainly. Integration details and experimental setups are mostly clear; adding more guidance on annealing schedules and transition kernels would improve reproducibility.

Originality: Extending BDMC from marginal likelihood estimation to bounding posterior sample quality is a neat and nontrivial advance. The fitted-hyperparameter protocol and end-to-end integration in two PPLs add practical novelty; concurrent related work is appropriately cited.

Significance: The contribution is useful for practitioners and PPL developers: it offers a quantitative, largely automated diagnostic for sampler reliability, informs representation choices, and aids debugging. Scope is limited to samplers expressible as IS over extended state spaces.

Limitations and suggestions: Provide uncertainty quantification for bounds (e.g., via repeated runs with confidence bands), analyze sensitivity to temperature schedules and kernels, report computational budgets, compare against standard diagnostics (R-hat/ESS, Stein discrepancy), and broaden evaluation (e.g., SMC, more challenging multimodal posteriors). Formalizing the bias induced by the fitted-hyperparameter protocol would strengthen claims for real data.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical contribution with practical impact; integrated in widely used tools.
- Careful empirical validation; actionable insights for model representation and debugging.
- Addresses an important gap in rigorously assessing MCMC reliability.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Guarantees hinge on simulated data and become heuristic for real data.
- Limited assessment of bound tightness and variance at realistic scales.
- Narrow applicability (AIS/SMC-like samplers) and limited comparisons to existing diagnostics.
