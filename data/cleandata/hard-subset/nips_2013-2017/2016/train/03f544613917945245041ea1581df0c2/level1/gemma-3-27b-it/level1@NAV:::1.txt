## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce the bias inherent in methods like SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, commonly used in numerical analysis, by running two SGLD chains in parallel with differing step sizes and then combining the results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE, claiming improved convergence rates compared to SGLD. Experiments on both synthetic and real (matrix factorization) datasets support these claims.

The paper builds directly on the well-established line of work in SG-MCMC, referencing seminal papers like Welling & Teh (2011) and subsequent extensions (e.g., Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014). It also appropriately connects to work on higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) and the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation itself (Talay & Tubaro, 1990). The presentation clearly articulates how SGRRLD leverages existing techniques to address the bias-variance trade-off in SG-MCMC.

**Strengths:** The primary strength lies in the novelty of applying RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing asymptotic guarantees and non-asymptotic bounds. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating improved performance over SGLD and, in one experiment, competitive results with a higher-order integrator. The authors thoughtfully address the potential for increased variance through correlated Brownian motions.  The exploration of SGRRHMC further demonstrates the generality of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:** The paper is dense and at times difficult to follow. The mathematical notation is heavy, and while rigorous, could benefit from more intuitive explanations. While the authors mention parallelization as an advantage, a more detailed discussion of computational cost and scalability would be valuable. Further, the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., step sizes) in the experiments isn't fully justified and relies somewhat on trial and error as hinted in the text.  A comparison with other bias-reduction techniques specifically designed for SG-MCMC would also strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with rigorous analysis.
*   Convincing experimental results demonstrating performance improvements.
*   Potential for practical impact in large-scale Bayesian inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Dense and challenging presentation, potentially limiting accessibility.
*   Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability.
*   Lack of comparison to other existing bias-reduction techniques in SG-MCMC.



**Overall:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While the presentation could be improved, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and experimental validation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity of the exposition and expanding the discussion of computational considerations.
