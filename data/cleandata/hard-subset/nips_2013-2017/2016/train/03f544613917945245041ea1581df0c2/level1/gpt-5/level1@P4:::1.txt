Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) to reduce bias while keeping variance controlled. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 (the fine chain taking two steps per coarse step), and form an extrapolated estimator that cancels the leading O(γ) weak-discretization error. Variance is controlled by coupling the Gaussian noise across chains via a shared Brownian increment construction; the paper shows this coupling minimizes the asymptotic variance among a class of linear couplings. The analysis establishes consistency, a central limit theorem (CLT) with explicit minimal variance under optimal coupling, and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. With constant step size, the optimal finite-time rates for bias and MSE are O(K−2/3) and O(K−4/5), respectively; with decreasing steps γk ∝ k−α, the optimal asymptotic MSE rate is O(K−4/5) at α=1/5, improving over first-order SG-MCMC (O(K−2/3)). The method is generic and also demonstrated on SGHMC (SGRRHMC). Experiments on a Gaussian model and on large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) show improved estimation accuracy and test RMSE, and SGRRLD can exploit parallel hardware by running the two chains concurrently.

Relation to prior work
SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011] and subsequent SG-MCMC methods provide scalable sampling but suffer from bias due to discretization and stochastic gradients; non-(a)symptotic behavior has been analyzed in Teh et al. (2016) and Chen et al. (2015), with higher-order integrators yielding improved accuracy. RR extrapolation is a classical bias-reduction technique in SDE weak error analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1991) and for invariant measure estimation (Lemaire et al., 2015; Lamberton & Pagès), but its use with stochastic gradients had not been explored. This work bridges those literatures by extending RR-based bias cancellation to SG-MCMC and showing how to couple noise to control variance, thereby matching the accuracy of higher-order methods while using a first-order integrator.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are consistent with known ULA/RR analyses and SG-MCMC results. The requirement that the Poisson equation admits a sufficiently smooth solution (g ∈ C9) and standard dissipativity/Lipschitz conditions on U are strong but standard in weak error expansions. The treatment of stochastic gradients assumes unbiasedness with adequate moment control and identical law across chains; the analysis argues Gaussian noise dominates the gradient noise in the CLT, and establishes reduced bias O(γ2) and MSE O(γ4) terms. The variance-minimizing Brownian coupling (Σ=Id) is appropriate and aligns with multilevel Monte Carlo intuition.
- Empirics: Experiments corroborate the predicted scalings (bias and MSE vs γ and K) on a Gaussian model, include dimensional scaling, and show extension to SGHMC with performance comparable to a higher-order splitting integrator at larger γ. On MovieLens datasets, SGRRLD achieves lower test RMSE vs wall-clock time when two chains are run in parallel.

Concerns and possible issues:
- Assumptions and scope: The exact regularity and ergodicity conditions, and the moment assumptions on the gradient estimator, are relegated to the supplement. It would be beneficial to state them explicitly in the main text and discuss practical regimes (e.g., lack of strong convexity outside a ball, heavy-tailed posteriors).
- Gradient noise coupling: The approach requires that both chains’ stochastic gradient estimators share the same distribution L (e.g., same mini-batch size). The impact of using the same mini-batch indices across chains (to reduce additional variance) is not studied; the paper notes correlation is allowed but provides no ablation for correlated vs independent mini-batches.
- Fairness of timing: The wall-clock comparisons rely on running the two chains in parallel, which favors SGRRLD on multi-core hardware. A single-core compute-budget comparison would clarify trade-offs when parallelism is limited.
- Baselines: While SGHMC-s (higher-order) is included, comparisons to higher-order integrators for overdamped dynamics [Chen et al., 2015], preconditioned SGLD (pSGLD), or thermostat variants would strengthen empirical claims that SGRRLD “matches” higher-order methods in accuracy-cost trade-offs.
- Metrics: In the Gaussian model, posterior mean/variance estimation is appropriate. For matrix factorization, test RMSE reflects prediction quality but not posterior sampling quality (e.g., ESS, posterior uncertainty calibration). Including standard MCMC diagnostics would improve the assessment.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with clear algorithmic description and estimator definition. The role and necessity of the Brownian coupling are well motivated. Some suggestions:
- Summarize the core assumptions (on U and L) in the main text and indicate dependence of constants on dimension d and batch size B.
- Provide pseudocode in the main paper or a schematic to make the RR estimator construction immediately accessible.
- Clarify the indexing in Chain 2 updates and ensure consistent notation for noise scaling factors.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with rigorous analysis (CLT and non-asymptotic bounds) and identifying optimal Brownian coupling appears novel. The work integrates two threads (RR for invariant measures and SG-MCMC) in a simple, implementable algorithm.

Significance
The method is simple, generic, and practically useful: it offers a black-box bias reduction for SG-MCMC with provably improved rates and good parallelization properties. Given the widespread use of SGLD/SGHMC, the contribution is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., multi-level variants).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable bias-reduction technique for SG-MCMC.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: consistency, CLT with variance minimization via coupling, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds with improved rates.
- Demonstrated on both SGLD and SGHMC; empirical results align with theory.
- Parallel-friendly design; minimal implementation overhead beyond running two chains with coupled noise.

Weaknesses
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; limited discussion of applicability beyond strongly confining potentials.
- Limited ablations on gradient noise coupling and sensitivity to mini-batch choices.
- Empirical comparisons do not include several competitive SG-MCMC variants; single-core compute parity not shown.
- Lack of standard sampling diagnostics on real data.

Questions/requests for authors
- Please state the explicit assumptions on U and the gradient estimator (Lipschitz/dissipativity, moment bounds) in the main text, and comment on dependence of constants on dimension and batch size.
- What is the effect of sharing the same mini-batch indices between chains versus independent mini-batches? Can you include an ablation?
- Can you add a single-core runtime comparison to disentangle algorithmic efficiency from parallelism?
- For matrix factorization, can you report additional sampling diagnostics (e.g., ESS of selected linear functionals)?

Recommendation rationale (pros vs cons)
Pros: Novel and principled idea; strong and carefully executed analysis with improved rates; simple, generic method; convincing synthetic and large-scale experiments; practical parallelization. Cons: Assumptions and some practical details are deferred; empirical comparisons could be broader and fairer in compute; limited diagnostics for posterior quality. Overall, the contribution is technically solid and of clear interest to the NIPS community working on scalable Bayesian inference.
