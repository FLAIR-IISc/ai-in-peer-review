### **Review Summary**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias of popular Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the widely-used Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and forming a linear combination of their outputs that cancels the leading-order discretization error. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs. O(K⁻²/³)), matching the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators while using only a first-order scheme. This theoretical contribution is supported by strong empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in accuracy and efficiency. The work is a novel and practical contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, effectively connecting established numerical analysis methods with modern SG-MCMC techniques.

### **Detailed Review**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide a thorough convergence analysis, including proofs of consistency, a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1), and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results formally establish the superiority of SGRRLD's convergence rates over standard SGLD. The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step-size decay rate (α = 1/5) to balance the bias and variance terms, leading to the O(K⁻⁴/⁵) MSE rate.
*   **Completeness:** This is a complete piece of work. It presents a clear problem (bias in SG-MCMC), proposes a well-motivated solution, analyzes the solution's theoretical properties, and validates it extensively. The authors are careful in their evaluation, for instance by exploring the effect of step size and dimensionality, and by empirically verifying the theoretical convergence rates.
*   **Careful Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed. The use of a simple Gaussian model with a known posterior allows for precise quantification of bias and MSE, convincingly supporting the theoretical claims. Applying the RR idea to SGHMC as well demonstrates the generality of the approach. The large-scale matrix factorization experiment provides compelling evidence of the method's practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The technical conditions for the theorems are relegated to the supplementary material. While standard for conference papers, a brief, intuitive summary of the key assumptions (e.g., on the smoothness of the potential U and the test function f) in the main text would improve self-containment.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The organization is logical, and the arguments are easy to follow.

**Strengths:**
*   **Organization:** The paper flows logically from the background on SGLD and RR extrapolation to the proposed SGRRLD algorithm, followed by its theoretical analysis and experimental validation.
*   **Exposition:** The authors do an excellent job of explaining complex concepts intuitively. For example, the description of how RR extrapolation works to cancel the O(γ) error term is very clear. The experimental results are presented with clear figures and concise explanations.
*   **Reproducibility:** The description of the algorithm and the experimental setups is detailed enough that an expert reader could likely reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The pseudo-code for the SGRRLD algorithm is mentioned to be in the supplement. Including it in the main paper (perhaps as an appendix) would have been beneficial for immediate clarity and completeness.

#### **Originality**

The paper presents a novel and clever contribution.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Combination:** While SGLD and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation are both established techniques, their combination in the context of SG-MCMC is new. Applying a method designed for deterministic or low-noise SDEs to the high-noise stochastic gradient setting is a non-trivial step that requires novel analysis, which the authors provide.
*   **New Perspective on Bias Reduction:** This work offers a new, and arguably simpler, alternative to the prevailing approach of designing complex higher-order integrators for bias reduction in SG-MCMC. The idea of "outsourcing" the bias correction to an extrapolation scheme is elegant.
*   **Generality:** The authors convincingly argue and demonstrate (with SGHMC) that their technique is generic and can be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The contribution is a novel combination of existing ideas rather than a completely new paradigm. However, the impact and elegance of this combination make this a minor point.

#### **Significance**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a strong impact on the field.

**Strengths:**
*   **Practical Impact:** SGRRLD provides a simple, "drop-in" method to improve the performance of one of the most popular families of scalable Bayesian inference algorithms. Given that it only requires running the base algorithm twice with different step sizes, it is very easy to implement.
*   **Efficiency:** The algorithm is inherently parallelizable. As demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments (Figure 5), this allows SGRRLD to achieve higher accuracy than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time, which is the relevant metric for practitioners. This is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Advancement of State-of-the-Art:** The paper demonstrates a clear advance in the state of the art. The improved convergence rates are substantial. The ability to match the accuracy of higher-order methods without their implementation complexity is a major selling point that researchers and practitioners will likely find very appealing.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The method has a higher total computational cost (approximately 3x the floating-point operations of a single SGLD chain for the same number of effective steps K). While the authors correctly argue that parallelization mitigates this in terms of wall-clock time, this trade-off could be stated more explicitly in the main text. On a single-core machine, the method would be slower.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   **Pro:** The method is supported by strong and rigorous theoretical guarantees, demonstrating superior convergence rates.
*   **Pro:** The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly show the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm on both synthetic and large-scale real-world problems.
*   **Pro:** The paper is extremely well-written and clear, making a strong contribution easily accessible.
*   **Pro:** The work is of high significance and is likely to be influential and widely used by researchers and practitioners in scalable Bayesian inference.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**

*   **Con (minor):** The approach increases the total computational load compared to a single SGLD chain, relying on parallel hardware to be competitive in wall-clock time.
*   **Con (very minor):** The contribution, while clever and impactful, is a synthesis of existing techniques.

### **Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that presents a valuable and impactful contribution to the field of large-scale Bayesian MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is elegant, well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. It offers a practical and easy-to-implement way to achieve significant performance gains over standard methods. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
