This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aimed at reducing bias compared to SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, requiring two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error. Empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets supports the claim of improved convergence rates.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, methodology, and results. It builds directly upon the established work of Welling & Teh (2011) on SGLD and the broader field of SG-MCMC. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SDEs, referencing Talay & Tubaro (1990) and related works, is appropriately made.  The theoretical contributions, particularly the convergence analysis and error bounds, are significant. The experiments, encompassing both synthetic data for validation and a real-world matrix factorization application, strengthen the claims. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea, offering a potential pathway to reduce bias without significantly increasing variance.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including proofs of consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE. The derivations appear careful and well-motivated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm is clearly described, and the mathematical notation is standard and consistent.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD compared to SGLD and, in one experiment, SGHMC.
*   **Parallelizability:** The algorithm’s inherent parallelizability is a significant advantage for large-scale applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Step Size Tuning:** The optimal step size selection (specifically, the connection between α and K) seems sensitive and requires careful tuning, as evidenced by the experimental results. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on practical considerations for step size selection.
*   **Correlation of Gradients:** While acknowledged, the effect of correlated gradients across the two chains isn't deeply explored.  The assumption of identical mini-batch sizes is also somewhat restrictive. A discussion on the potential impact of different mini-batch sizes, or relaxed correlation assumptions, would be valuable.
*   **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The comparison to SGHMC with a higher-order splitting integrator (SGHMC-s) in the experiments is limited.  A more thorough comparison, perhaps including computational cost analysis, would strengthen the claim of achieving comparable accuracy with a first-order integrator.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus on a relatively small number of datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse real-world applications would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of SGRRLD.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm on both synthetic and real data.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian inference, particularly for large-scale applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity to step size tuning and the limited exploration of correlated gradients could hinder practical adoption.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including a wider range of datasets and a more detailed comparison with alternative methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation, makes it a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding step size tuning, correlation of gradients, and more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
