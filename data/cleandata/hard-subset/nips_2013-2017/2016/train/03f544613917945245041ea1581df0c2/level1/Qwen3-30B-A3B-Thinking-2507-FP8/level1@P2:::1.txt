

# Review for NIPS 2016

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias of standard SGLD while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the SGLD framework (Welling & Teh, 2011) by running two chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation, using correlated Brownian motions to control variance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis showing SGRRLD achieves O(γ²) bias and O(γ⁴) MSE compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²), leading to a superior convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds are well-supported with careful technical development, building on Talay & Tubaro (1990) and extending it to SG-MCMC. The authors correctly position their work against Chen et al. (2015) on higher-order integrators, demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy with simpler implementation.

2. **Practical Implementation:** The parallel nature of SGRRLD makes it well-suited for distributed systems, as the two chains can be run independently. The paper provides clear implementation details and demonstrates this advantage in the matrix factorization experiments, where SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks (ML-1M, ML-10M, ML-20M) compellingly support the theoretical claims. Figure 1(b) shows SGRRLD's MSE is approximately the square of SGLD's MSE across dimensions, and Figure 5 demonstrates consistent wall-clock time improvements across all datasets.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical results are presented logically, with clear notation and well-structured proofs (with detailed proofs in the supplement). The algorithm description is precise, and the connection between theory and experiments is well-maintained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Implementation of Correlated Brownian Motions:** While the paper justifies the use of perfectly correlated Brownian increments (equation 7) theoretically, the practical implementation details for achieving this correlation in distributed systems are somewhat vague. This could pose a challenge for practitioners seeking to implement the method.

2. **Comparison with Higher-Order Methods:** The paper mentions that SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to methods based on higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015), but doesn't provide a direct comparison. A comparison with SGHMC-s (mentioned in the experiments) would strengthen the argument for the approach's superiority.

3. **Computational Cost per Iteration:** The paper focuses on convergence rate in terms of iterations but doesn't discuss the computational cost per iteration. Since SGRRLD requires running two chains in parallel, it might have higher computational cost per iteration than SGLD, which could affect overall wall-clock time efficiency in some implementations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The idea of applying RR extrapolation to reduce bias in SG-MCMC is novel and elegant, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The practical implementation is straightforward, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages.

The paper excels in all four NIPS criteria:
- **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-supported by experimental results. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear notation and logical flow.
- **Originality:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and distinct from previous work (e.g., Chen et al., 2015).
- **Significance:** The improved convergence rate provides a practical method to enhance SG-MCMC performance without requiring complex higher-order integrators, with demonstrated benefits in both synthetic and real-world applications.

The minor weaknesses regarding implementation details and comparison with higher-order methods don't detract from the paper's overall contribution. The method is simple to implement, provides clear theoretical advantages, and delivers practical improvements in real-world applications.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS 2016. It represents a valuable advancement in scalable Bayesian inference that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
