This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, which leverages the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce bias in SGLD. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combine the results to achieve a higher order of convergence. The authors provide theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem, along with bounds on bias and MSE. Experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (matrix factorization) support the theoretical claims, showing improved performance over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in SG-MCMC, directly referencing seminal papers by Welling & Teh (2011) introducing SGLD, and subsequent extensions (e.g., SGHMC by Chen et al., 2014).  It’s well-situated within the recent trend of improving the convergence properties of SG-MCMC, particularly addressing the inherent bias of first-order methods, as highlighted by Chen et al. (2015) and Teh et al. (2016). The use of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a clever idea borrowed from numerical analysis, and applying it to SG-MCMC is novel.  Related work on higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) is also appropriately acknowledged, and the paper demonstrates how SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy with a simpler, first-order integrator. The comparison to SGRRHMC, applying RR to SGHMC, is a good demonstration of the method's generality.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including proofs for consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds. The theoretical analysis is a significant strength.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, both synthetic and real-world, convincingly demonstrate the advantages of SGRRLD over SGLD and SGHMC. The results on the large-scale matrix factorization task are particularly compelling.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The core idea of SGRRLD is clearly explained, and the paper is well-organized. The authors make a clear connection between the RR extrapolation method and its benefits in the context of SG-MCMC.
* **Generality:** The method isn't limited to SGLD and can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms (demonstrated with SGHMC).
* **Parallelism:** The inherent parallelism of the RR extrapolation lends itself well to modern hardware, which is a practical benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While theoretically elegant, implementing SGRRLD requires careful attention to correlation of Brownian motions (Equation 8) to achieve optimal performance. The authors acknowledge this, but the practical difficulty of ensuring proper correlation could be a barrier to adoption.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of step sizes (γ and γ/2) and the correlation matrix Σ impacts performance.  While the paper discusses the optimal choice for Σ (identity matrix), a more detailed exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness in the experiments would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Other Advanced Methods:** While comparisons to SGLD and SGHMC are strong, the paper would be further strengthened by a comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods.  Specifically, methods that explicitly address bias reduction, beyond higher-order integrators, would be relevant.
* **Step Size Selection Discussion:** The paper mentions an optimal step size of γ = K^(-1/3) for the bias and γ = K^(-0.2) for the MSE, but the transition from theoretical analysis to practical application of these findings could be further clarified. How are these optimal step sizes determined in practice, and how sensitive are the results to deviations from these values?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the empirical findings.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.
* The method has the potential to be widely applicable in various Bayesian inference problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity could limit the practical adoption of the method.
* The comparison to other advanced SG-MCMC methods is limited.
* The practical guidance on step size selection could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis and compelling experimental results, makes it worthy of acceptance. While the implementation complexity and limited comparison to all recent methods are minor concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the hyperparameter sensitivity and provide more detailed practical guidance for step size selection, and to include a brief discussion of related recent advancements in bias-reducing SG-MCMC techniques.
