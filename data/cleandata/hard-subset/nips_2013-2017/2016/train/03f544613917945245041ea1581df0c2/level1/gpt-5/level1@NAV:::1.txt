This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, instantiated on SGLD, to reduce bias while controlling variance. Concretely, the method runs two coupled SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated estimator; variance is controlled by correlating Gaussian increments, ideally using the same Brownian path (Σ = I). The authors prove consistency, a CLT with minimal asymptotic variance under perfect correlation, and non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ2) and MSE O(γ4). With fixed K, the optimal MSE rate becomes O(K−4/5), improving over first-order SG-MCMC’s O(K−2/3) and matching the accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS’15) without their complexity. Experiments on a Gaussian model, SGHMC variants, and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) demonstrate improved accuracy at equal wall-clock via parallel chains.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically solid, adapting RR ideas for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) to the stochastic-gradient regime. The CLT and finite-time bounds follow a Poisson equation approach with high-order Taylor expansions; conditions are reasonable but strong (smoothness up to C9, moment bounds). The empirical evaluation is convincing, though ablations on coupling choices and minibatch correlation would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. Key assumptions and the precise coupling needed for minimal variance are relegated to the supplement; surfacing the main assumptions, the pseudocode, and a short practical guide for choosing γ, K, and coupling of minibatches in the main text would improve reproducibility.

Originality: Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with explicit variance control via common random numbers is novel and well-positioned relative to prior NeurIPS work on SG-MCMC and higher-order integrators.

Significance: The method is simple, broadly applicable beyond SGLD (as shown with SGHMC), parallel-friendly, and yields improved finite-time and asymptotic rates. This is likely to influence practitioners seeking better accuracy without sophisticated integrators.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, generic idea with strong theory (consistency, CLT, finite-time bounds).
- Improved rates (O(K−4/5)) matching higher-order methods using only first-order steps.
- Practical, parallelizable; empirical gains on real datasets.

Cons against acceptance:
- Strong smoothness and moment assumptions; constants in bounds unspecified.
- Limited analysis of gradient-noise coupling; unclear best practice for minibatch sharing.
- Experiments focus on a few settings; broader tasks and sensitivity to γ/K would help.

Overall: Strong paper; I recommend acceptance.
