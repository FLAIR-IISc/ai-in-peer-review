This paper presents a novel SG-MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, based on the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce bias in stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics. The core idea—running two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining the results—is well-motivated, given the known bias of SGLD (O(γ)). The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE, positioning SGRRLD as achieving faster convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>)) than SGLD (O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>)). Empirical validation on synthetic and real (matrix factorization) datasets supports these claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution. While RR is well-established in numerical analysis, its application within the Bayesian inference/MCMC framework, particularly to mitigate SGLD's bias, hasn’t been previously explored (at least not to my knowledge, and the paper references relevant work well).
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for SGRRLD. Establishing asymptotic consistency and a CLT, along with bounds on bias and MSE, demonstrates a deep understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.  The derivation of the convergence rates is particularly valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The technical details are reasonably accessible, and the algorithm is presented in a concise manner.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results are convincing. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the bias reduction and improved convergence. The application to matrix factorization further validates the approach on a real-world problem.  The comparison to SGHMC and SGHMC with higher-order integrators is insightful.
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel nature of running the two chains in SGRRLD is a significant practical advantage. The authors correctly highlight this aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Step Size Tuning:** The theoretical analysis suggests an optimal step size dependent on K (K<sup>-1/3</sup> for bias, K<sup>-0.2</sup> for MSE). However, the experimental section primarily uses fixed step sizes, and the justification for choosing those specific values isn't fully explained. Demonstrating the performance gain with adaptive step sizes (following the theoretical guidance) would strengthen the empirical results.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** The paper acknowledges the importance of correlated Brownian increments (Eq. 8) and discusses the case of perfectly correlated increments, but the impact of *different* correlations, and the effects of not enforcing perfect correlation in practice, aren’t fully explored.  A sensitivity analysis to varying the correlation structure might be beneficial.  The statement that the correlation of stochastic gradients doesn’t matter feels a bit strong; while the theory might support it, practical implications should be discussed.
* **Computational Cost:**  While parallelization mitigates this, SGRRLD inherently requires running two chains, effectively doubling the computational cost per iteration. The paper should more explicitly address this trade-off – how much wall-clock time is saved due to the faster convergence rate compared to the doubled per-iteration cost? The experimental section touches on this, but a more direct comparison of computational effort would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the matrix factorization experiment is a good starting point, expanding the empirical evaluation to other more complex Bayesian models would further strengthen the paper's claims.
* **Connection to Existing RR Implementations:** A deeper dive into existing RR implementations in other areas (e.g., numerical integration of ODEs) might highlight common challenges and best practices that could be relevant to SGRRLD.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm.
* The empirical results are convincing and support the theoretical claims.
* The parallelizability of SGRRLD makes it well-suited for large-scale applications.
* The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field of Bayesian inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical aspects of step size tuning and correlation structure require further investigation.
* The computational cost trade-off needs to be more explicitly addressed.
* The empirical evaluation could be broadened to include more diverse Bayesian models.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong theoretical foundation and convincing empirical results, make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The weaknesses identified are not critical, but addressing them would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding step size tuning, correlation structure, computational cost, and experimental scope.  Specifically, I suggest adding a section exploring adaptive step size schemes and a more detailed analysis of the computational cost/convergence trade-off.
