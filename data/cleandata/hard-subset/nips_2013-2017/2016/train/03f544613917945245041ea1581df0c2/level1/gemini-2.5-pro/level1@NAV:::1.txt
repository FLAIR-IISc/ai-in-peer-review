This paper introduces a novel method to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The proposed approach, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), applies the classical Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to the popular SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. This work builds upon the extensive literature on SG-MCMC (e.g., Welling & Teh, 2011) and connects it with classical numerical analysis for SDEs where RR extrapolation is a known variance reduction technique (Talay & Tubaro, 1990). The key contribution is adapting this method to the stochastic gradient setting and demonstrating its efficacy as an alternative to using higher-order integrators for bias correction (Chen et al., 2015).

The paper has numerous strengths across all evaluation criteria.
**Quality & Clarity:** The work is technically sound, presenting a rigorous theoretical analysis that includes proofs for asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The claims of improved convergence rates—achieving an MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³)—are well-supported by this theory and validated through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world data. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the motivation and methodology easy to follow.
**Originality & Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and elegant contribution. While the components are not new, their combination is, and the analysis of how to control variance using correlated noise is a crucial insight. The significance of this work is high; it provides a simple, practical, and parallelizable method for improving the accuracy of a widely used class of inference algorithms. The experimental results, particularly the wall-clock time improvements on large-scale matrix factorization, convincingly demonstrate its practical value. The method's generality, shown by its application to SGHMC, further broadens its potential impact.

The paper has very few weaknesses. The primary trade-off is the doubled computational cost of running two chains. While the authors rightly argue that this can be perfectly parallelized, a more explicit discussion comparing SGRRLD to a single SGLD chain run for the same total number of gradient evaluations (i.e., twice the wall-clock time on a single core) could further strengthen the experimental section.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The strengths heavily outweigh the minor limitations, and the proposed method is likely to be adopted by both researchers and practitioners.
