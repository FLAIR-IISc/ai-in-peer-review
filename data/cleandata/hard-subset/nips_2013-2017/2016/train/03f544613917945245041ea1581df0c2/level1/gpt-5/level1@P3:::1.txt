Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC), with a focus on SGLD. The key idea is to run two coupled SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to cancel the leading weak discretization bias (O(γ)) and thus reduce bias to O(γ^2), while controlling variance via carefully correlated Gaussian increments. The estimator is a time-weighted average of 2 fine minus 1 coarse evaluations, run to the same diffusion time horizon. The authors prove a CLT for the estimator, derive non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, and show that with constant step sizes the optimal MSE rate is O(K^−4/5) (vs O(K^−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC), matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS 2015) while using a first-order integrator. Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model and on large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens datasets) demonstrate empirical improvements; an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) shows similar gains and can match SGHMC with higher-order splitting for suitable step sizes.

The work builds on classical RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro 1990) and on invariant measure estimation with duplicated diffusions and optimal Brownian correlations (Lemaire et al., 2015), as well as on recent finite-time analyses for SG-MCMC (Teh et al., JMLR 2016; Chen et al., NIPS 2015). The application of RR to SG-MCMC, together with the variance control via coupling, appears novel.

Quality:
- Theory: The CLT (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds (Theorem 2) are consistent with the RR rationale. The identification that Σ = I (i.e., coarse increments equal the average of fine-level increments) minimizes the asymptotic variance is well-motivated and aligns with duplicated diffusion results. The rate trade-offs and optimal α derivation for decreasing step sizes are careful and match the finite-time bounds.
- Assumptions: The analysis requires high regularity (solution to the Poisson equation g ∈ C^9 and smooth U) and standard ergodicity/weak error conditions. The requirement that the two stochastic gradient estimators share the same law (and ideally the same mini-batch size) is important; without it, the extrapolation introduces bias. The claim that “the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” in the asymptotics is tied to the assumed scaling of the gradient noise and may not hold when mini-batch variance is very large or state-dependent; a discussion of this regime would help.
- Practicality: The method doubles gradient computations but is trivially parallelizable. Empirically, the authors match wall-clock time by running two processes in parallel, which is reasonable for distributed/parallel settings; however, a comparison at fixed gradient-evaluation budget (single-thread) would clarify trade-offs when parallelism is limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized, with good pointers to the supplement for technical conditions and proofs. The exposition of the estimator (Eq. 6) and the coupling (Eq. 7–8) is clear.
- Potential typo: In Eq. (5), Chain 2 uses ∇Ũ evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k+1 for the update from θ(γ/2)2k to θ(γ/2)2k+1, which would be implicit; for Euler–Maruyama it should be evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k. Please verify and correct.
- It would help to include the pseudocode in the main text (not only in the supplement), and to explicitly recommend using common random numbers and common mini-batches to further reduce variance.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and showing how to control variance via coupling to achieve higher-order weak accuracy without higher-order integrators is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel contribution. The work is related in spirit to multilevel Monte Carlo and duplicated diffusions; a more explicit discussion contrasting SGRRLD with MLMC (e.g., Giles 2008) would strengthen the positioning, since SGRRLD can be seen as a two-level MLMC-style estimator for invariant measures.
- The extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) further supports generality.

Significance:
- The theoretical rates (MSE O(K^−4/5)) represent a meaningful improvement over first-order SG-MCMC and match high-order integrators while keeping implementation simple. The approach is generic and easy to layer onto existing SG-MCMC samplers.
- Empirically, improvements are clear on the synthetic model and matrix factorization benchmarks. The claim of parallel efficiency is attractive in modern multi-core/distributed environments.
- To bolster impact, comparisons to stronger baselines on real data (e.g., high-order SGHMC/SGLD from Chen et al., 2015; other variance-reduction SG-MCMC methods) would be valuable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, general method with solid theoretical support (CLT, non-asymptotic bounds).
- Improved finite-time and asymptotic rates; matches higher-order integrator accuracy without complex integrators.
- Practical and parallel-friendly; empirical gains on sizeable problems.
- Extension to SGHMC shows generality.

Cons / weaknesses:
- Assumes high smoothness and identical gradient estimator laws; practical guidance for non-ideal cases is limited.
- Empirical comparisons are mainly to SGLD; lacks head-to-head on real data against higher-order SG-MCMC or control-variate SG-MCMC.
- Fairness relies on parallel execution; single-thread costs double.
- Minor clarity issue (Eq. 5 likely typo) and lack of main-text pseudocode.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Please confirm Eq. (5) indices (explicit vs implicit Euler).
- How sensitive is SGRRLD to the variance of the stochastic gradient? Would using common mini-batches across levels further reduce variance and improve constants?
- Can you report results at fixed gradient-evaluation budgets (single-thread) to complement the wall-clock comparison?
- Could you compare against high-order SG-MCMC (e.g., SGHMC-s) on the matrix factorization task?
- Discuss the relationship to MLMC explicitly and the feasibility of multi-level (L > 2) RR in SG-MCMC in practice.

Minor comments:
- Some references lack full details; consider adding Giles (2008) for MLMC background.
- Clarify whether asymptotic “variance O(γ^4)” refers to the finite-K MSE term or the asymptotic variance parameter in the CLT.
- Typographical and notation consistency passes would help (e.g., bold vs non-bold for vectors).

Overall assessment:
A well-motivated, technically solid, and practically relevant contribution. Despite some missing empirical baselines and minor clarity issues, the paper advances SG-MCMC by importing RR extrapolation in a principled way and demonstrating both theoretical and empirical benefits. I lean toward acceptance.
