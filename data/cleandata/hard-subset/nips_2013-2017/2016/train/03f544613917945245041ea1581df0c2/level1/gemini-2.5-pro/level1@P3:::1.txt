### Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to reduce the discretization bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms like SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, a classical method from numerical analysis, to the outputs of two parallel SGLD chains. One chain is run with a step size `γ`, while the second is run with a step size `γ/2` (for twice as many steps to cover the same time horizon). A specific linear combination of the estimators from these two chains is then used to cancel the leading-order bias term. To prevent the variance from increasing, the authors propose using correlated noise for the two chains, extending a technique previously developed for SDEs without stochastic gradients.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including proofs of consistency, a central limit theorem (CLT), and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and mean squared error (MSE). A key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for standard SGLD. This improved rate matches the performance of SG-MCMC methods that use more complex higher-order integrators, but SGRRLD achieves this using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both synthetic data, where theoretical claims are verified, and on a large-scale matrix factorization task, where it outperforms SGLD in wall-clock time. The paper also shows the generality of the approach by applying it to SGHMC.

This work builds directly upon the SG-MCMC literature, notably SGLD [1], and addresses the well-known problem of discretization bias [10, 20]. The novelty lies in adapting the RR extrapolation method, previously studied for standard SDEs [21, 23], to the stochastic gradient setting, providing a practical and theoretically sound way to improve accuracy without resorting to higher-order integrators that may require Hessian computations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a highly novel and elegant contribution. It provides a conceptually simple yet powerful way to tackle the problem of discretization bias. Instead of designing more complex integrators, the authors cleverly combine outputs from a standard, easy-to-implement integrator.

2.  **Theoretical Significance:** The paper presents strong theoretical results. The improved MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a significant advance for first-order SG-MCMC methods. The analysis, which includes a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds, is thorough and provides deep insight into the behavior of the proposed algorithm. Demonstrating that SGRRLD can match the theoretical accuracy of higher-order methods [10] is a major selling point.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Performance:** The method has clear practical advantages. Its inherent parallelism allows it to effectively utilize modern multi-core architectures. The experiments on large-scale matrix factorization show that this translates into real-world performance gains, achieving better accuracy than SGLD in the same wall-clock time. The fact that it only requires first-order gradients makes it broadly applicable and easy to implement compared to methods requiring Hessians.

4.  **Clarity and Quality of Exposition:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation before presenting their method and theory. The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper emphasizes the benefits of parallelism, which allows SGRRLD to achieve better wall-clock time. However, the total computational cost (in terms of gradient evaluations) is roughly three times that of SGLD for the same number of effective steps `K`. This trade-off should be discussed more explicitly. On a single-core machine, SGRRLD would be significantly slower per iteration. It would be valuable to include a discussion on whether the improved statistical efficiency (fewer iterations `K` needed for a target accuracy) can compensate for the higher per-iteration cost in a serial setting.

2.  **Generality of the Theoretical Framework:** The authors claim the technique is "quite generic," which is demonstrated empirically by applying it to SGHMC. However, the theoretical analysis is presented for Langevin dynamics. To strengthen the paper, the authors could briefly discuss the key conditions or assumptions needed to extend their theoretical guarantees (e.g., the CLT and MSE bounds) to other SG-MCMC algorithms, such as SGHMC or methods with thermostats [7]. The phrase "rather technical weak error and ergodicity conditions" is a bit vague; a sentence or two summarizing the nature of these conditions would be helpful.

3.  **Clarity on SGRRHMC Implementation:** When applying the RR scheme to SGHMC, it is not entirely clear how the correlated noise (Eq. 7) is implemented. SGHMC involves noise injection into the momentum variable. Is the correlation applied to the momentum updates in the same way? A brief clarification in the main text or appendix would improve reproducibility and understanding.

4.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The comparison with a higher-order SGHMC variant (SGHMC-s) in Figure 4 is excellent. To make the comparison even more compelling, the authors could add a brief discussion on the computational trade-offs. SGRRLD avoids computing higher-order derivatives (like the Hessian) but requires 3x the gradient evaluations per effective step. A qualitative or quantitative comparison of these costs would further highlight the practical niche where SGRRLD is most advantageous.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Presents a novel and elegant method for bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
*   Provides significant theoretical improvements, increasing the MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵).
*   Achieves the accuracy of complex higher-order integrators using only a simple first-order scheme.
*   The method is inherently parallel, leading to demonstrated wall-clock speedups on large-scale, real-world problems.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and the claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**
*   The discussion on the trade-off between computational cost and statistical efficiency could be more nuanced, particularly for serial computation environments.
*   The claims of generality could be strengthened with more discussion of the theoretical requirements for other SG-MCMC methods.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and practically effective. It offers a new and compelling direction for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC methods that is distinct from the development of higher-order integrators. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate to clarifying certain trade-offs and the scope of the method, but they do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is of high quality and will likely be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
