**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias of existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique for accelerating the convergence of numerical approximations. The method involves running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size sequence {γ_k} and another that takes two steps of size γ_k/2 for each step of the first chain. By taking a specific linear combination of the outputs from these two chains, the leading-order error term (bias) of the discretization is canceled. The authors show that by using correlated Brownian motion for the two chains, the variance of the resulting estimator can be effectively controlled.

This work connects two well-established fields: the recent line of research on scalable Bayesian inference using SG-MCMC (e.g., Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014) and the classical numerical analysis literature on extrapolation methods for solving SDEs (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). While RR extrapolation has been analyzed for SDEs with exact gradients, its application and rigorous analysis in the stochastic gradient setting, where gradient noise introduces additional complexity, is the main contribution of this paper.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both a rigorous theoretical analysis and a comprehensive set of experiments.
*   **Theory:** The authors provide a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) that characterizes the asymptotic behavior of the SGRRLD estimator and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) (Theorem 2). These results formally establish that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence than SGLD. Specifically, the MSE convergence rate is improved from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for SGRRLD, which is a substantial theoretical improvement. The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step size decay rate and the importance of using correlated noise to minimize asymptotic variance.
*   **Experiments:** The empirical validation is strong. The experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model are particularly well-designed, systematically verifying the theoretical predictions about the behavior of bias and MSE as functions of the step size (γ) and the number of iterations (K). The experiments on large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate the practical utility of the method, showing significant improvements in RMSE over SGLD within the same wall-clock time on real-world datasets.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, building from the known limitations of SGLD. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is presented precisely, and the connection to classical RR extrapolation is well explained. The theoretical results are stated clearly, and their implications (e.g., optimal convergence rates) are discussed in an accessible manner. The organization follows a logical flow from background to method, theory, and experiments.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution—the application and analysis of RR extrapolation for SG-MCMC—is novel and significant. While the components (SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation) are not new, their combination to create a provably better sampler is a creative and impactful idea. The work is clearly distinguished from prior art. It offers an alternative path to higher-order accuracy compared to the use of higher-order integrators (Chen et al., NIPS 2015), which can be more complex to derive and implement. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by applying it to SGHMC, suggesting it is a broadly applicable "wrapper" for improving a wide class of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are important and likely to have a lasting impact on the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
*   Bias is a primary limiting factor for the accuracy of many SG-MCMC methods, and this paper provides a practical and theoretically-grounded way to mitigate it.
*   The fact that SGRRLD is inherently parallelizable makes it highly relevant for modern computational environments. It presents a clear trade-off: using twice the computational resources (in parallel) to achieve a higher convergence rate and better final accuracy.
*   Given its generality, the RR extrapolation idea is likely to be adopted and extended to other SG-MCMC variants beyond SGLD and SGHMC. It advances the state of the art by providing a new tool for constructing more accurate and efficient large-scale Bayesian inference algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Cost Discussion:** The primary weakness is the increased computational cost. SGRRLD requires approximately three times the number of gradient evaluations as SGLD to reach iteration K (K steps for the first chain, 2K for the second). The paper emphasizes that this can be parallelized to achieve similar wall-clock time, which is a fair point. However, this assumes the user has access to parallel hardware. For a user with a single computational unit (e.g., one GPU), the algorithm would be roughly 3x slower. It would be beneficial to make this trade-off more explicit. A comparison based on an equal *computational budget* (i.e., total gradient evaluations) in addition to equal wall-clock time would strengthen the experimental section. For example, comparing SGRRLD at iteration K with SGLD at iteration 3K would provide a clearer picture of the algorithmic efficiency gain.

2.  **Generality of Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on technical assumptions (detailed in the supplement) regarding the smoothness of the potential function and the properties of the gradient noise. While standard in this literature, a brief, high-level discussion of these assumptions in the main text would be helpful. For instance, do these assumptions hold for highly non-convex problems commonly found in deep learning? A comment on the potential applicability or challenges in such settings would add value.

3.  **Minor Clarification:** In Section 5.2, the statement "SGLD cannot benefit from this parallel computation architecture due to its inherently sequential nature" could be misinterpreted. While a single SGLD chain is sequential, one could run multiple independent SGLD chains in parallel. The key advantage of SGRRLD is that its parallel chains are purposefully correlated to achieve bias reduction in a principled way, which is a much stronger and more elegant use of parallelism. A slight rephrasing could make this point more precise.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Presents a novel and elegant idea for reducing bias in SG-MCMC, a central problem in the field.
*   **Pro:** Provides strong and complete theoretical support for the proposed method, demonstrating superior convergence rates.
*   **Pro:** The method is practical, parallelizable, and shown to be effective on large-scale, real-world problems.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and positions its contribution effectively within the existing literature.
*   **Pro:** The proposed technique is general and can likely be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The method requires a significant increase in computational resources (roughly 2x if parallelized, 3x if run sequentially), which might be a barrier in some applications.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful. It addresses a key limitation of widely used algorithms and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
