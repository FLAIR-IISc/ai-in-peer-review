## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to standard SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique – running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining their results – to accelerate convergence. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE, claiming improved convergence rates over SGLD. Empirical validation on both synthetic and real (matrix factorization) datasets supports these claims.

The paper builds directly on the burgeoning field of SG-MCMC, referencing key works like Welling & Teh (2011) and subsequent extensions (Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014). It correctly identifies bias as a key limitation of SGLD and appropriately leverages Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established numerical technique (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), within the MCMC context. The connection to higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) is also clearly articulated. 

**Strengths:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing valuable insights into the algorithm’s convergence properties. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating performance improvements over SGLD and, in some cases, comparable performance to higher-order integrators. The authors also highlight the algorithm’s suitability for parallel architectures, a significant advantage for large-scale applications. The inclusion of experiments on a real-world dataset (matrix factorization) strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the presentation could be more accessible. The notation is dense, and some derivations are relegated to the supplementary material, potentially hindering understanding. The discussion of the correlation between Brownian increments (equation 8) feels somewhat abrupt and could benefit from more intuitive explanation. While the authors acknowledge the importance of matching mini-batch sizes, a more detailed discussion of the implications of mismatched batch sizes would be valuable. The experiments, while convincing, could be expanded to include a wider range of datasets and model complexities.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of a well-established numerical technique to a relevant problem in Bayesian inference.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees regarding convergence and bias reduction.
*   Empirically validated performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Potential for significant speedups through parallelization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and potentially inaccessible theoretical presentation.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   The correlation aspect of the Brownian increments could be explained better.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical and empirical results are convincing, and the proposed algorithm has the potential to be a valuable tool for large-scale Bayesian inference. Despite some minor weaknesses in presentation and experimental scope, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially expand the experimental evaluation.
