

# Peer Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build on Talay and Tubaro's RR extrapolation for SDEs [21] and extend it to the SG-MCMC context, where the exact gradient is replaced with stochastic estimates. The key insight is to run two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) in parallel, then combine their outputs via RR extrapolation. The paper demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves higher convergence rates (O(K^(-4/5)) for MSE compared to SGLD's O(K^(-2/3))) while maintaining the parallelizability that makes SG-MCMC attractive for large-scale applications.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong and well-executed. The authors provide a central limit theorem for SGRRLD (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2), showing that the method achieves better convergence rates than SGLD and comparable accuracy to higher-order integrators. The proof strategy builds on previous work [23] while carefully adapting to the stochastic gradient setting. The experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization problems effectively validate the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in MSE and reduced bias. A notable strength is the careful treatment of correlated Brownian increments to control variance, which addresses a critical concern in RR extrapolation for MCMC.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the connection between RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC is clearly explained. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the pseudocode for SGRRLD. The presentation of the theoretical results is logical, with clear statements of assumptions and theorems, and the experimental design is well-motivated.

**Originality:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis for SDEs [21], its application to SG-MCMC is new to the literature. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work, particularly Chen et al. [10] which demonstrated that higher-order integrators can improve convergence rates. The authors show that SGRRLD achieves the same theoretical accuracy as higher-order methods while using only first-order integrators, which is a significant conceptual contribution. The method's generality (applicable to all SG-MCMC algorithms satisfying weak error and ergodicity conditions) further enhances its novelty.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important limitation of SG-MCMC methods (bias due to discretization) with a practical solution that leverages existing parallel computing infrastructure. The method provides a simple yet effective way to improve accuracy without the complexity of implementing higher-order integrators. The experimental results on both synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate clear practical benefits, with SGRRLD achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in approximately half the wall-clock time. This represents a meaningful advance in scalable Bayesian inference.

**Strengths:**
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear convergence rate improvements
- Practical implementation that maintains parallelizability
- Comprehensive experimental validation on synthetic and real datasets
- Clear explanation of how to control variance through correlated Brownian increments

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational cost implications beyond wall-clock time (e.g., memory requirements for two parallel chains)
- Theoretical assumptions (smoothness of f, etc.) could be better contextualized with respect to practical Bayesian models
- Comparison against more recent SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD and SGHMC would strengthen the empirical evaluation
- The paper doesn't explore the trade-off between the number of iterations and the step size choice in more depth

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, the method is both novel and practical, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its advantages. The paper satisfies all four criteria for acceptance at NIPS: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and addresses a problem of significant practical importance. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

The authors' claim that "by only using a first-order numerical integrator, the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is well-supported by both theory and experiments. This represents a meaningful advance in SG-MCMC methodology that will likely be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in Bayesian machine learning.

I recommend acceptance.
