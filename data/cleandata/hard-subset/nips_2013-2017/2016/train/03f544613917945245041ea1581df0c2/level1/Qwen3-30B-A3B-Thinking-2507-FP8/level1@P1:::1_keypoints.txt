- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to two parallel SG-MCMC chains (γ and γ/2) to reduce bias, achieving MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).
- Theoretical contribution: provides a central limit theorem (Thm 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Thm 2) establishing higher-order convergence for SGRRLD.
- Methodological grounding: builds on SGLD and numerical analysis results (Talay & Tubaro, Lemaire) and relates to higher-order SG-MCMC integrators.
- Parallelizability: inherently parallel approach suitable for distributed systems, unlike sequential higher-order integrators.
- Empirical validation: synthetic experiments and matrix factorization tasks show bias reduction, improved MSE, and better accuracy per unit time.
- Novelty: first application of RR extrapolation to the stochastic gradient setting, achieving higher-order accuracy using first-order methods.
- Practical significance: demonstrates tangible improvements for large-scale Bayesian inference tasks.
- Comprehensive experiments: systematic testing of bias, MSE, and convergence rates plus a real-world large-scale application.

- Missing implementation details: does not explain how to realize the required correlated Brownian motions in practice, especially in distributed settings.
- Limited comparisons: experiments primarily compare to SGLD and insufficiently to other advanced SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGHMC with higher-order integrators).
- Narrow experimental regime: some comparisons (Figure 4) are restricted to small step sizes (γ), limiting empirical conclusions.
- No practical heuristics: theoretical step-size schedules are given but practical guidance for selecting γ in real applications is lacking.
- Restrictive assumptions: analysis assumes smooth potentials and specific gradient estimator conditions, which may not hold for non-smooth or complex models.
- Unclear high-dimensional scaling: the paper doesn't fully address scalability to extremely high-dimensional problems.

- For acceptance: presents a novel, rigorous approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC with provable convergence-rate improvements.
- For acceptance: retains parallelizability and shows empirical gains consistent with theory.
- For acceptance: technique is generalizable to other SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD.
- Against acceptance: insufficient practical guidance for correlated Brownian motion implementation.
- Against acceptance: limited empirical benchmarking against state-of-the-art alternatives.
- Against acceptance: potential issues with scalability and restrictive theoretical assumptions.

- Quality: technically sound with rigorous analysis and supportive experiments.
- Clarity: well-organized and readable, though some technical implementation details need clarification.
- Originality: novel extension of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC, distinct from prior higher-order integrators.
- Significance: addresses a core SG-MCMC limitation (bias) with both theoretical and practical impact for scalable Bayesian inference.

- Overall: strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify implementation of correlated Brownian motions and expand empirical comparisons.
