

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon foundational SG-MCMC work like SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and recent theoretical advances on SG-MCMC convergence (Chen et al., 2015), extending numerical analysis techniques from Talay & Tubaro (1990) to the stochastic gradient setting. The key insight is running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation, achieving O(K^{-4/5}) MSE convergence compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}).

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis, including central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The theoretical claims are well-supported by comprehensive experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks, where SGRRLD consistently outperforms SGLD and SGHMC. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation of both theoretical and experimental results. The methodology is original in its application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, though the core idea of parallel chains isn't entirely novel.

The significance is high as it addresses a fundamental limitation in SG-MCMC (bias) without requiring higher-order integrators, while maintaining parallelizability. The paper adequately references related work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly Chen et al. (2015) and Talay & Tubaro (1990).

Strengths: Rigorous theoretical foundation, compelling empirical validation, clear writing, and practical significance for large-scale Bayesian inference.

Weaknesses: The technical depth of the proofs may challenge some readers, and while the method is generalizable, the paper focuses primarily on SGLD rather than demonstrating broader applicability to other SG-MCMC variants.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with practical implications, well-executed experiments, and clear presentation.
Con: Limited exploration of the method's applicability to non-Langevin SG-MCMC approaches.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, offering a theoretically grounded and practically effective solution to a persistent challenge in SG-MCMC. I recommend acceptance.
