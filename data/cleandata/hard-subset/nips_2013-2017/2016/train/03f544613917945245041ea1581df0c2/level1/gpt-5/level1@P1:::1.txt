Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a simple and general bias-reduction mechanism for SG-MCMC by applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD (and, in an experiment, to SGHMC). The method runs two SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and forms an RR estimator by a weighted difference of their time-averages. To keep variance under control, the authors correlate the Gaussian increments as in the classical RR literature for SDEs. Theoretically, they establish a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds: asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4); finite-time MSE O(K−4/5) with optimal polynomial step schedule, improving over the O(K−2/3) rate known for first-order SG-MCMC (Teh et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2015). Empirically, they verify rates on a Gaussian model, show strong reduction in bias/MSE of posterior variance, demonstrate extension to SGHMC (matching the accuracy of higher-order splitting), and show improved RMSE and wall-clock efficiency on large-scale matrix factorization, exploiting parallelism by running the two chains concurrently.

This work builds on classical RR extrapolation for weak error in SDEs and invariant measure approximation (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lamberton & Pagès, 2002; Lemaire et al., 2015), and on SG-MCMC foundations (Welling & Teh, 2011; Teh et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2015). To my knowledge, applying RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients and analyzing the coupled noise is novel.

Quality:
- Theory: The CLT for the RR estimator under decreasing step size uses a Poisson equation approach with high-order Taylor expansions, closely following the ULA literature but extended to stochastic gradients and to correlated Brownian increments; the variance-minimizing coupling Σ = Id is well-justified. The non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds are consistent with the asymptotic results, and the optimal K-dependent step-size choices are clearly derived. Assumptions (smoothness, ergodicity, existence of a sufficiently smooth Poisson solution) are standard in this line of work but strong (C9). The requirement that the two chains use gradient estimators with the same law (e.g., same minibatch size) is necessary; the discussion of potential bias otherwise is appreciated.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments validate bias/MSE scalings and step-size schedules; the SGHMC variant shows gains comparable to using higher-order integrators; the matrix factorization experiments are substantial and leverage parallelization to keep wall-clock fixed. However, comparisons to other practical SG-MCMC baselines (e.g., pSGLD, SGFS, SGNHT) are limited, and tasks beyond Gaussian/matrix factorization (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural networks) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The coupling construction and estimator are clearly specified, and the role of correlated Brownian increments is explained. Minor issues:
- Equation (5) appears to evaluate ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) when updating θ(γ/2)2k+1 from θ(γ/2)2k; this looks like a typographical error and should use θ(γ/2)2k in the first substep (Euler-Maruyama).
- It would help to explicitly state whether the same minibatch indices are reused across the two chains at a given time to further reduce estimator variance (this is hinted but not specified). Practical guidance on coupling random number generation across processes would also be useful.
- Include the full pseudocode in the main text (currently in the supplement).

Originality:
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, with analysis showing second-order weak accuracy using only first-order integrators and carefully correlated noise, appears novel and nontrivial. The idea is simple yet powerful, and the theory extends classical results to the stochastic gradient setting.

Significance:
The method is broadly applicable to SG-MCMC, gives provable improvements in bias/MSE without designing specialized higher-order integrators, and is embarrassingly parallel. For practitioners with two cores/GPUs, SGRRLD can deliver better accuracy at the same wall-clock. The analysis clarifies when and how RR helps and should stimulate further work (e.g., multilevel variants). Impact would be higher with more diverse empirical validation and comparisons to other advanced SG-MCMC variants.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, general technique with clear theoretical guarantees (CLT, non-asymptotic bounds) and improved rates.
- Careful treatment of variance via optimal coupling of Brownian increments; informative discussion of gradient-noise correlation.
- Solid empirical validation on synthetic data, and meaningful large-scale application with wall-clock comparisons exploiting parallelism.
- Bridges two literatures (RR for SDEs and SG-MCMC) in a novel way; likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., multilevel).

Cons / weaknesses:
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; unclear robustness to non-smooth or heavy-tailed posteriors.
- Empirical scope limited: lacks comparisons to pSGLD, SGFS, SGNHT/mSGNHT, and higher-order SGLD integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) in the SGLD setting.
- Practical guidance on choosing α and γ without access to ground-truth is limited; some tuning in synthetic experiments uses oracle knowledge.
- The variance-minimizing coupling assumes exact control of shared Gaussian noise across processes; implementation details could be elaborated.
- Computational cost doubles without parallel resources; the paper focuses on parallel settings but does not quantify trade-offs when parallelism is constrained.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the suspected typo in Equation (5) and include algorithm pseudocode in the main paper.
- Provide instructions on coupling: reusing the same minibatch indices across chains at matching times, RNG seeding, and handling distributed settings.
- Add benchmarks on logistic regression and one non-convex model (e.g., Bayesian neural net) and compare to pSGLD, SGFS, SGNHT, and SGLD with higher-order integrators.
- Give practitioner guidelines for step-size schedules (α, constants) and diagnostics to pick γ adaptively.
- Discuss memory/communication overhead and performance when only a single core is available.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a novel, theoretically sound, and practically attractive way to reduce SG-MCMC bias via RR extrapolation, with clear improvements and broad applicability. The empirical section could be broadened, but the contribution is strong and timely.
