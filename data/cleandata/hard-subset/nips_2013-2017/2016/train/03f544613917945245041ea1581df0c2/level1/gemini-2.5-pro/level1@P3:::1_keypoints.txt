- Introduces SGRRLD: Richardson-Romberg extrapolation applied to two parallel SGLD chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) with correlated noise to cancel leading discretization bias.  
- Theoretical results: proves consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and shows improved MSE rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).  
- Matches accuracy of higher-order SG-MCMC integrators while using only first-order Euler-Maruyama updates (no Hessians required).  
- Practical performance: inherently parallel method that outperforms SGLD in wall-clock time on large-scale matrix factorization.  
- Empirical generality: demonstrates applicability to SGHMC in experiments.  
- Originality and elegance: novel, simple, and powerful adaptation of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.  
- Strong theoretical significance: thorough analysis giving deep insight and improved convergence rates.  
- Practical relevance: easy to implement (first-order gradients) and leverages multi-core architectures.  
- Clarity and exposition: well-written, well-motivated, and experimentally convincing.  
- Computational-cost concern: SGRRLD uses ~3× gradient evaluations per effective K, which should be discussed explicitly, especially for single-core/serial settings.  
- Theory-generalization gap: theoretical analysis is for Langevin dynamics only; needs clarification on conditions required to extend CLT/MSE guarantees to SGHMC or thermostat methods.  
- Implementation clarity needed: unclear how correlated noise is applied in SGHMC (momentum updates); add brief implementation details for reproducibility.  
- Comparison with higher-order integrators: should discuss trade-offs quantitatively—avoiding Hessians vs increased gradient evaluations.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel bias-reduction method, strong theory, practical speedups, and well-supported claims—recommend accept.  
- Remaining cons: more nuanced discussion of computational vs statistical efficiency and clearer theoretical scope for other SG-MCMC variants.
