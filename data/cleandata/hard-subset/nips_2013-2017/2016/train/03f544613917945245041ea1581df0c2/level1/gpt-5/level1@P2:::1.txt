Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC sampler that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD while controlling variance via coupling of Brownian increments. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated estimator. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a CLT showing asymptotic normality of the estimator and identifying a variance-minimizing coupling (perfectly correlated Brownian increments), (ii) non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and (iii) improved rates: asymptotically O(K−2/5) for the estimator (with γk ∝ k−1/5) and, for constant step-size, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), implying optimal finite-time rates O(K−2/3) for bias and O(K−4/5) for MSE, outperforming SGLD’s O(K−1/2) and O(K−2/3) respectively. The approach is extended conceptually to other SG-MCMC methods, with an illustration on SGHMC. Experiments on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support the theory, with consistent improvements under matched wall-clock time using parallel chains.

This work is well-situated within NIPS literature on SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD [1], SGHMC [6], thermostats [7,8], high-order integrators [10]). It imports classical RR extrapolation for SDEs [21,23] into the stochastic-gradient setting, which, to my knowledge, had not been explored. The paper’s guarantees complement prior analyses of SGLD convergence and fluctuations [18–20].

Quality:
- Theory appears technically sound and carefully adapted from the RR literature to the SG-MCMC setting. The CLT (Theorem 1) under decreasing step-sizes, the role of the Brownian coupling (optimal at Σ=Id), and the non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated. The assumption that Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise in the limit is plausible and clearly stated.
- Assumptions are strong but standard for these analyses (smoothness up to high order via Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9, ergodicity/weak error conditions). The paper is honest about conditions and defers proofs and technicalities to the supplement.
- The dependence of bias on using the same law L for gradient estimators in both chains is important; the paper correctly notes that mismatched mini-batch distributions can negate the bias reduction.
- Empirical results are consistent with theory (e.g., γ-scaling of bias/MSE, best α around 1/5 for MSE). The SGHMC extrapolation experiment aligns with the claim that RR can reach the accuracy of higher-order integrators without implementing them.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition about the extrapolation and coupling. The estimator π̂R is precisely defined.
- Some details are relegated to the supplement (pseudocode, exact conditions, constants in CLT). Including the algorithm pseudocode and a brief discussion of implementation of the Brownian coupling (Eq. 7) in the main text would aid reproducibility.
- Minor typos and formatting glitches exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and proving improved rates in both asymptotic and finite-time regimes is novel and interesting. The identification of optimal Brownian coupling in the SG setting extends insights from [23]. The approach is simple yet nontrivial, and likely generalizable to other SG-MCMC schemes.

Significance:
- The improvement in rates (e.g., MSE exponent 0.8 vs 0.666) is meaningful theoretically, and the method is simple to implement and well-suited to parallel and distributed environments. This can influence practice when compute allows parallel chains.
- The empirical section demonstrates benefits on a canonical Gaussian model and a significant real task (matrix factorization). However, broader coverage (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural networks) would strengthen the case for generality.
- The cost/benefit trade-off deserves more discussion: SGRRLD doubles per-iteration work but parallelizes trivially. The wall-clock comparisons assume additional cores are available; a comparison under equal total FLOPs or single-core time would help practitioners decide.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable technique with solid theory.
- Clear improvements in bias/MSE rates without higher-order integrators.
- Careful variance control via optimal Brownian coupling; CLT provides asymptotic distribution.
- Empirical results align with theory; parallelization advantage is compelling.

Cons against acceptance:
- Strong smoothness assumptions; unclear robustness to non-smooth/non-log-concave posteriors common in modern models.
- Experimental scope limited; lacks comparisons on additional models and to alternative bias-reduction/variance-reduction SG-MCMC methods.
- Fairness of wall-clock comparison could be questioned (two chains in parallel vs one); no single-core runtime or equal-compute comparisons.
- Sensitivity to choices (e.g., sharing mini-batches across chains, correlation of gradient noise) is not explored; practical guidance on coupling mini-batch indices is missing.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include pseudocode, and clarify whether the two chains share mini-batch indices and how this affects variance.
- Add experiments on non-quadratic posteriors (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression) and a comparison to high-order integrators for overdamped dynamics where available, as well as to preconditioned/quasi-Newton SGLD variants [11,12].
- Report error bars and show performance under fixed compute budget (single-thread) alongside parallel wall-clock.
- Discuss practical tuning (γ schedules, α), and provide guidance on when recursive RR (multi-level) is beneficial.

Overall assessment:
The paper makes a solid, well-supported theoretical and algorithmic contribution by bringing RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, achieving improved convergence rates with a simple, parallelizable method. Despite some experimental limitations and assumptions, the work meets NIPS standards on quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
