- Proposes SGRRLD: an SG-MCMC algorithm using Richardson–Romberg extrapolation by running two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining results to reduce bias.
- Motivated by known SGLD bias (O(γ)) and aims to achieve faster convergence via RR extrapolation.
- Provides theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE.
- Claims improved convergence rate O(K^-4/5) for SGRRLD versus O(K^-2/3) for SGLD.
- Empirical validation on synthetic data and matrix factorization supports bias reduction and improved convergence.
- Novelty: first application of RR extrapolation within SG-MCMC/Bayesian inference context (well referenced).
- Theoretical rigor: solid derivations and convergence-rate analysis strengthen the contribution.
- Presentation: paper is generally well-written, organized, and technically accessible.
- Experiments: convincing comparisons including SGHMC and higher-order integrators; real-world matrix factorization shown.
- Parallelizability: running two chains can be parallelized, a practical advantage.
- Step-size tuning weakness: experiments use fixed step sizes without clear justification or adaptive schemes following theoretical guidance.
- Gradient correlation weakness: limited analysis of partial/imperfect correlation effects and no sensitivity study of correlation structure.
- Computational cost weakness: doubling chains increases per-iteration cost; trade-off between wall-clock time and faster convergence is not fully quantified.
- Limited experiments weakness: empirical scope is narrow—needs more diverse, complex Bayesian models.
- Missing connections: lacks deeper discussion of existing RR implementations and applicable best practices.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically supported, parallelizable, and addresses an important Bayesian inference problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: practical issues (step sizes, correlation), computational cost concerns, and limited empirical breadth.
- Overall assessment: significant contribution; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address tuning, correlation analysis, cost trade-offs, and expanded experiments.
