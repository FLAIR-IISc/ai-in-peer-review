This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a method designed to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, specifically focusing on SGLD. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, requiring running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency and a central limit theorem, alongside bounds on bias and mean squared error, claiming improved convergence rates compared to SGLD. Experiments on both synthetic and real data (matrix factorization) support these claims.

The paper is well-motivated. SG-MCMC is a crucial area for scaling Bayesian inference, and reducing bias is a key challenge. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to this domain is a novel and potentially impactful idea, building on well-established numerical analysis techniques. It clearly situates itself within the recent NIPS literature on SG-MCMC (citing Welling & Teh 2011, Chen et al. 2014, Teh et al. 2016, among others). The authors effectively highlight existing work on higher-order integrators and position SGRRLD as achieving similar accuracy with first-order methods. The theoretical results, particularly the convergence rate analysis, are a significant contribution. The experimental validation is also positive, demonstrating improvements over SGLD and, in some cases, competitive performance with more complex methods like SGHMC with higher-order integrators.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is substantial, the assumptions required for the theorems appear quite strong and are not fully discussed.  A clearer explanation of these assumptions and their practical implications would strengthen the paper.  The presentation of the theoretical results feels dense, and more intuition behind the improvements in convergence rates would be beneficial. The experimental section, while demonstrating improvement, lacks a comprehensive comparison to other SG-MCMC variants beyond SGLD and a single configuration of SGHMC.  For instance, comparing to SGLD with different step size schedules or preconditioning techniques would provide a more thorough benchmark. The computational cost of SGRRLD – running two chains – is briefly mentioned but not rigorously analyzed.  A more detailed discussion of the computational overhead and how it impacts practical scalability would be valuable.  The code is not available, hindering reproducibility.  Finally, while the paper suggests the technique is “virtually applicable” to other SG-MCMC algorithms, this claim is not fully substantiated beyond the SGHMC example.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:**  The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees for consistency, convergence, and error bounds.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Experiments demonstrate clear improvements over SGLD and competitive performance with more complex methods.
*   **Significance:** Reducing bias in SG-MCMC is a crucial problem, and this paper provides a promising solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:**  Theoretical results rely on potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   **Limited Comparison:**  The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   **Computational Cost:** Lack of detailed discussion regarding the computational overhead.
*   **Reproducibility:** Code is not available.
*   **Generality:** The claim of broad applicability needs stronger justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The idea is novel, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are promising. However, the strong assumptions, limited comparisons, and lack of code availability raise concerns. I believe the paper is currently borderline, leaning towards acceptance **with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should: (1) Provide a more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying their theorems, (2) Expand the experimental evaluation to include a broader range of SG-MCMC algorithms and step size schedules, (3) Analyze the computational overhead of running two chains, and (4) Release the code for reproducibility. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
