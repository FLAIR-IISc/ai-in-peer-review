- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) and combining outputs to reduce bias.  
- Builds on Talay–Tubaro RR for SDEs, extending it to stochastic-gradient settings where exact gradients are replaced by noisy estimates.  
- Theoretical contributions: a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds (Theorem 2) showing improved convergence.  
- Proven convergence-rate improvement: MSE O(K^(-4/5)) for SGRRLD versus O(K^(-2/3)) for SGLD.  
- Shows SGRRLD attains the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators while using only first-order updates.  
- Variance control via carefully correlated Brownian increments is analyzed and shown to be crucial for RR extrapolation in MCMC.  
- Method maintains SG-MCMC’s parallelizability and is practical for large-scale applications.  
- Empirical validation: synthetic Gaussian experiments and large-scale matrix factorization show significantly reduced bias and MSE, and roughly half the wall-clock time to match SGLD accuracy.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: well-written paper, consistent notation, clear assumptions, and supplementary pseudocode.  
- Originality: novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and broad applicability to SG-MCMC algorithms satisfying weak error and ergodicity conditions.  
- Significance: addresses discretization bias in SG-MCMC with a simple, parallel-friendly technique beneficial for scalable Bayesian inference.  
- Strengths summarized: novelty, strong theory, practical parallel implementation, comprehensive experiments, and explicit variance-control strategy.  
- Weaknesses summarized: limited discussion of additional computational costs (e.g., memory for two chains), theoretical assumptions (smoothness) not fully contextualized, limited comparisons to newer SG-MCMC methods, and insufficient exploration of iteration vs. step-size trade-offs.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, well-presented, and practically important.
