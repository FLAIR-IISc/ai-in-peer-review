

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon Welling and Teh's SGLD (2011) and extend it using RR extrapolation, which requires running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes in parallel. The paper demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K^-4/5), compared to SGLD's O(K^-2/3), and matches the theoretical accuracy of methods using higher-order integrators. The work relates to Chen et al. (2015) who showed that higher-order integrators can improve convergence rates, but SGRRLD achieves similar performance using only first-order integrators through a simple modification.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds for SGRRLD. The authors carefully address the relationship between bias reduction and variance control through correlated Brownian increments, which is well-justified by prior work on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The theoretical results are well-motivated and follow a clear logical progression.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions of key concepts (e.g., the RR extrapolation framework for SG-MCMC) and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The connection between numerical analysis techniques and MCMC is clearly explained, making the contribution accessible to the NIPS audience.

3. **Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful limitation of SG-MCMC (bias) with a simple yet effective solution. The improved convergence rate (O(K^-4/5) vs O(K^-2/3)) is theoretically significant and practically valuable for large-scale applications. The experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over SGLD and SGHMC.

4. **Generality:** The authors demonstrate that their approach can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC), showing the method's broad applicability.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty:** While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, the core technique is borrowed from numerical analysis. The paper could better emphasize what makes this application novel in the context of MCMC (beyond the simple extension to SG-MCMC).

2. **Practical Considerations:** The paper notes that SGRRLD requires running two chains in parallel, which is advantageous for distributed systems but may increase memory requirements. The paper does not thoroughly analyze the memory overhead or how this might impact very large-scale problems with limited memory resources.

3. **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-designed, they primarily focus on the Gaussian model and matrix factorization. Additional experiments on more complex models (e.g., deep Bayesian neural networks) would strengthen the claim about broad applicability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically rigorous and practically significant improvement to SG-MCMC methods
- Demonstrates clear superiority over existing methods in both theory and experiments
- Well-written with clear connections to prior work and careful theoretical justification
- The approach is simple to implement and leverages existing SG-MCMC frameworks
- Offers a practical solution to a well-known limitation in SG-MCMC

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The core technique (RR extrapolation) is not entirely new, though its application to SG-MCMC is novel
- The memory overhead of running two chains in parallel is not thoroughly analyzed
- The experimental scope could be broader to demonstrate applicability to more complex models

**Overall assessment:**
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-executed, and the experimental results convincingly validate the theoretical claims. The proposed method offers a practical solution to a significant limitation of current SG-MCMC methods, with improved convergence rates that are both theoretically interesting and practically valuable. The paper is well-written and clearly connects to prior work in numerical analysis and MCMC. While the novelty of the core technique is somewhat limited (being an application of a known numerical method), the specific application to SG-MCMC and the resulting theoretical and practical improvements constitute a significant contribution to the field.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a novel application of a known technique to improve SG-MCMC (Originality), and provides meaningful improvements to a widely used class of algorithms with significant practical implications (Significance). The theoretical results are particularly strong, demonstrating how a simple modification to existing SG-MCMC methods can achieve convergence rates previously requiring more complex higher-order integrators.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, with both theoretical and practical importance.
