The paper proposes SGRRLD: applying Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running paired chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and coupling Brownian increments to reduce discretization bias.

Key theoretical contributions: a CLT for the extrapolated estimator, identification of variance-minimizing (perfectly correlated) Brownian coupling, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds.

Improved rates: asymptotic and finite-time improvements over SGLD (e.g., MSE exponent 0.8 vs 0.666), with optimal step schedules and constant-step analyses.

Extension to other SG-MCMC methods is sketched, with an illustrative experiment on SGHMC showing RR can match higher-order integrator accuracy.

Theory appears technically sound and carefully adapted from RR literature; assumptions and limits (Gaussian noise dominance) are clearly stated.

Assumptions are strong (high-order smoothness via Poisson equation, ergodicity/weak error conditions) but standard for such analyses.

Variance reduction depends critically on using the same gradient estimator law L across chains; mismatched mini-batch distributions can negate benefits.

Empirical results (Gaussian and MovieLens matrix factorization) support theoretical claims and show wall-clock gains when parallel chains are used.

Clarity and exposition are generally good; the estimator is well-defined and intuition is clear.

Reproducibility would improve with pseudocode and explicit description of Brownian coupling implementation in the main text.

Minor typos/formatting issues exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality: novel import of RR extrapolation into the stochastic-gradient setting and identification of optimal coupling extends prior SDE results.

Significance: simple, parallelizable method with meaningful theoretical gains that can influence practice when parallel resources exist.

Experimental limitations: narrow scope (no logistic regression or Bayesian neural networks) and lack of comparisons to other bias/variance-reduction SG-MCMC methods or high-order integrators.

Cost/benefit and fairness concerns: SGRRLD doubles per-iteration work—wall-clock comparisons assume extra cores; single-core or equal-FLOP comparisons are missing.

Sensitivity and practical guidance missing: impact of sharing mini-batches, correlation of gradient noise, and tuning (γ schedules, α) are not thoroughly explored.

Suggested improvements: include pseudocode, clarify mini-batch sharing, add non-quadratic experiments and comparisons, report error bars and single-core fixed-compute results, and discuss recursive/multilevel RR and tuning advice.

Overall assessment: solid, well-supported contribution that merits acceptance despite experimental limitations and strong assumptions.
