

# Peer Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods like SGLD. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combine their outputs via RR extrapolation to achieve higher-order convergence rates. The authors theoretically show that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), while maintaining reasonable variance through correlated Brownian motions. The paper builds on the SGLD framework (Welling & Teh, 2011) and extends numerical analysis techniques from Talay & Tubaro (1990) and Lemaire et al. (2015) to the stochastic gradient setting. It also relates to recent work on higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC (Chen et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical novelty and rigor:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by establishing that RR extrapolation can achieve the convergence rates of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and technically sound, extending previous results on SG-MCMC convergence.
2. **Practical significance:** The method is inherently parallelizable (unlike sequential SG-MCMC), making it suitable for distributed systems. The experiments on both synthetic and real data (matrix factorization) demonstrate tangible improvements in accuracy per unit time.
3. **Clear theoretical foundation:** The connection between RR extrapolation in numerical analysis and SG-MCMC is well-explained, with proper citations to relevant literature (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015).
4. **Comprehensive validation:** The synthetic experiments systematically test bias, MSE, and convergence rates, while the real-world matrix factorization application demonstrates practical utility on large-scale datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Implementation details:** The paper lacks concrete guidance on implementing the correlated Brownian motions in practice. While the theory requires perfect correlation (equation 7), the paper doesn't explain how to achieve this in distributed settings without communication overhead.
2. **Comparative limitations:** The experiments compare SGRRLD primarily against SGLD, but don't fully explore how it performs against other advanced SG-MCMC methods like SGHMC with higher-order integrators (e.g., SGHMC-s from Chen et al., 2015). The comparison in Figure 4 is limited to small γ values.
3. **Practical step size selection:** While the paper derives optimal step size schedules theoretically, it doesn't provide practical heuristics for choosing γ in real applications, which could limit adoption.
4. **Theoretical scope:** The analysis assumes smooth potential functions and specific conditions on gradient estimators. The paper could better discuss how these assumptions hold for non-smooth or complex models common in practice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a novel theoretical approach to a well-known problem in SG-MCMC (bias reduction)
- The convergence rate improvement (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) is significant and rigorously proven
- The method is parallelizable, addressing a key practical limitation of existing SG-MCMC
- The experiments provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims
- The technique is generalizable to other SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The practical implementation details for correlated Brownian motions are insufficient
- The comparison against state-of-the-art methods is limited
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the method scales to extremely high-dimensional problems
- The theoretical assumptions might restrict applicability to certain model classes

**Evaluation of criteria:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by empirical validation. The theoretical bounds are well-justified, and the experiments appropriately support the claims. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for correlated Brownian motions) and provide a clear path to addressing them.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from motivation to theory to experiments. The main idea is accessible to readers familiar with SG-MCMC. However, some technical details (e.g., implementation of correlated Brownian motions) could be clarified.

**Originality:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, particularly the extension of this numerical technique to the stochastic gradient setting. The paper properly distinguishes itself from related work on higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) by showing that RR extrapolation achieves similar accuracy with first-order methods.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of SG-MCMC (bias) with a method that achieves better convergence rates while maintaining parallelizability. The practical improvements demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments make this work significant for large-scale Bayesian inference applications.

**Overall assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature with clear practical implications. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the empirical validation demonstrates tangible improvements. The main limitations relate to implementation details and comparative scope, but these do not undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify implementation details and broaden the comparison with existing methods. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing both the theory and practice of scalable Bayesian inference.
