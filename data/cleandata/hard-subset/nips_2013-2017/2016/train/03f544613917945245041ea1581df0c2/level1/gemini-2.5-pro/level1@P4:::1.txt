### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The primary goal is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in popular methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), which typically has a bias of order O(γ) where γ is the step size. The proposed method leverages Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating the convergence of a sequence.

The core idea is to run two parallel SGLD chains targeting the same posterior distribution but using different step sizes. The first chain uses a step size sequence (γ_k), while the second uses a sequence (η_k) where η_{2k-1} = η_{2k} = γ_k/2. This ensures that after K steps of the first chain, the second chain has taken 2K steps and both have evolved over the same time horizon. The final estimator for an expectation E_π[f(θ)] is constructed by a linear combination of the empirical averages from the two chains: `2 * avg(chain 2) - avg(chain 1)`. A key component of the method is the use of correlated noise terms for the two chains to control the variance of the extrapolated estimator, which might otherwise increase. The authors show that the optimal choice is to derive the noise for the coarse chain (step size γ) from the noise of the fine chain (step size γ/2).

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. It establishes that the estimator is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and provides non-asymptotic bounds for its bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The theoretical results demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This rate matches what can be achieved with more complex higher-order integrators, but SGRRLD accomplishes this using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama integrator.

The work is situated within the broader context of scalable Bayesian inference. It directly addresses the limitations of first-order SG-MCMC methods [1] and proposes an alternative to the use of higher-order integrators [10]. The use of RR extrapolation for SDEs with exact gradients is known in the numerical analysis literature [21, 23], but its application and rigorous analysis in the stochastic gradient setting is novel. The authors validate their theoretical claims with experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task using MovieLens datasets, showing significant performance improvements over SGLD.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant idea. It provides a practical and effective method for bias reduction, a key challenge in this domain. The ability to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators with a simple, first-order scheme is a significant contribution. This makes high-accuracy sampling more accessible, as first-order integrators are substantially easier to implement and tune.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derivations clearly show how SGRRLD improves upon the convergence rates of SGLD. The theoretical analysis correctly identifies the optimal correlation structure for the noise processes to minimize asymptotic variance, which is a crucial insight for the practical success of the method.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the proposed algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated formally. The connection to prior work is well-articulated, situating the contribution effectively within the field.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model provide a controlled environment to verify the theoretical claims regarding bias, MSE, and convergence rates as a function of step size and iteration count. The demonstration on SGHMC (creating SGRRHMC) effectively supports the claim of generality. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate the practical utility of the method, showing that SGRRLD achieves better accuracy than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost:** The primary drawback of SGRRLD is its increased computational cost compared to a single SGLD chain. Per "iteration" (as defined by the coarse chain), SGRRLD performs one step of the coarse chain and two steps of the fine chain, for a total of three gradient computations. The authors rightly point out that the two chains can be run in parallel, which reduces the wall-clock time overhead to approximately 1.5x that of SGLD (assuming two available cores). While the experiments show this is a worthwhile trade-off, a more explicit discussion of the computational cost (e.g., in terms of total gradient evaluations) versus accuracy improvement would be beneficial.

2.  **Generality of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper claims the method is "quite generic," but the detailed theoretical analysis is presented for SGLD. While the empirical results for SGHMC are encouraging, extending the formal theorems to a broader class of SG-MCMC algorithms would strengthen the paper's claims of generality. It is not immediately obvious if the technical conditions required for the proofs (provided in the supplement) hold for other popular samplers without modification.

3.  **Correlation of Gradient Estimators:** The paper states that the asymptotic variance is unaffected by the correlation between the stochastic gradient estimators used in the two chains. This is an interesting result. However, the practical implications could be explored further. For instance, would using the same minibatch of data for the coarse step and the corresponding two fine steps lead to any empirical variance reduction, even if it does not affect the asymptotic rate? A brief discussion or experiment on this point could provide additional practical insight.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach to reduce bias in SG-MCMC methods.
*   Provides strong and rigorous theoretical support for the proposed algorithm, demonstrating improved convergence rates.
*   The method is practically significant, offering a simple way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators.
*   Empirical results are thorough and convincing, demonstrating clear benefits on both synthetic and large-scale real-world problems.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method incurs a notable computational overhead (approx. 1.5x wall-clock time with parallelization), which is a practical trade-off.
*   The formal theoretical analysis is limited to SGLD, while broader claims of generality are made.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, novel, and impactful contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method is both theoretically well-founded and empirically demonstrated to be effective.
