## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias of SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, requiring running two Langevin dynamics chains with different step sizes in parallel. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) and show that correlated Brownian motions help control variance. They validate their approach through experiments on synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC extension) and a large-scale matrix factorization task, demonstrating improved accuracy compared to SGLD and SGHMC.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, including convergence rates and variance control. The connections to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation and its application to SDEs are clearly explained. The analysis is a key strength of the paper.
* **Improved Convergence Rate:**  The demonstrated improvement in the MSE convergence rate (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>)) compared to standard SGLD (O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>)) is significant and represents a genuine advance.
* **Practical Parallelism:** The algorithm naturally lends itself to parallel implementation due to the independent execution of the two chains, making it well-suited for modern hardware.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight the potential to apply the RR extrapolation technique to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, broadening the impact of the work.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets, and demonstrate the benefits of SGRRLD in practice. The comparison with SGHMC is also valuable.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** Implementing SGRRLD is more complex than SGLD, requiring the management of two chains and correlated Brownian motions.  While parallelism helps, the increased implementation burden could be a barrier to adoption.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of step size (γ). The authors demonstrate this in their experiments, but it's unclear how robust the method is to suboptimal step size selection in practice.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are comprehensive, they are limited to relatively standard datasets and models. More diverse and challenging applications could further demonstrate the algorithm's robustness and scalability.
* **Correlation of Gradients:**  The discussion about correlated gradients, while mentioned, feels a bit superficial.  More investigation into how to effectively correlate the gradient estimators could be beneficial.  Is the same mini-batch *required*? What happens if they are different?



**Addressing Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims. The analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results validate the theoretical findings. It is a complete piece of work, going beyond a simple position paper. The authors carefully evaluate the strengths and weaknesses.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, but the mathematical notation could benefit from more careful explanation, especially regarding the Brownian motion correlation.  A more detailed explanation of how the constants in the convergence rates scale with problem parameters would be valuable.
* **Originality:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and represents a significant contribution. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous contributions in the field.  The related work section seems thorough.
* **Significance:** The improved convergence rate and potential for parallelization make SGRRLD a potentially impactful algorithm. Researchers and practitioners working with large-scale Bayesian inference could benefit from this work. It advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with demonstrably improved convergence properties.
* The approach is well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in SG-MCMC: reducing bias.
* The experimental results provide strong evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.
* The potential for parallelization is a significant advantage.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The increased implementation complexity and parameter sensitivity might limit the practical adoption of the algorithm.
* The experiments, while solid, could be expanded to include more diverse applications.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of stochastic gradient MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithm is novel and promising, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding complexity and parameter sensitivity, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – clarifying notation and expanding on the gradient correlation discussion would be helpful).
