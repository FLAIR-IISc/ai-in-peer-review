This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aiming to improve the bias of SGLD using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea – applying RR acceleration to SGLD by running two chains with differing step sizes – is clever and potentially impactful. The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K^-4/5) MSE vs. O(K^-2/3) for SGLD) and validates the approach with experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors do a good job of relating SGRRLD to existing SG-MCMC methods, particularly SGLD and SGHMC. The inclusion of theoretical convergence analysis is commendable, and the experiments provide evidence supporting the claims. The extension to SGHMC via RR extrapolation further demonstrates the generality of the approach.  The pseudo-code is a welcome addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR extrapolation is known in numerical analysis, its application to this setting is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates and demonstrating improvements over existing methods. The connection to correlated Brownian motions for variance control is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical findings, showing improved accuracy compared to SGLD and SGHMC. The parallelizability aspect is highlighted which is particularly appealing for large-scale applications.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as being applicable to various SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, enhancing its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions needing to tune the step size γ. While the theory provides guidance, a more detailed discussion of practical considerations and sensitivity to hyperparameter choices would be valuable. Specifically, how sensitive is performance to deviations from the optimal α (1/5) found theoretically?
* **Computational Cost:** While the parallelizability is a strength, the need to run two chains introduces a computational overhead. A more thorough discussion of the trade-off between improved accuracy and increased computational cost is needed.  A more detailed benchmark comparing wall-clock time for equivalent accuracy would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse datasets and model types would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions about the potential function *U* and the gradient estimators. A clearer discussion of the limitations imposed by these assumptions is needed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the claims made in the paper.
* The experimental results provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of SGRRLD.
* The potential for parallelization makes the algorithm attractive for large-scale applications.
* The generality of the approach to other SG-MCMC algorithms is promising.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational overhead of running two chains might limit practical applicability.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis could pose challenges in real-world scenarios.
* The experimental evaluation could be broadened to encompass a more diverse set of problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and solid theoretical foundation. While there are some practical considerations and limitations, the potential benefits of SGRRLD, particularly its improved convergence rates and parallelizability, are significant.  The paper prioritizes a novel idea over absolute methodological polish, which aligns with my reviewing philosophy. I believe the benefits outweigh the drawbacks, and I **recommend acceptance**. The authors should be encouraged to address the limitations in future work, such as providing more guidance on hyperparameter tuning and exploring methods to mitigate the computational overhead.
