

# Peer Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The authors propose running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes in parallel, then combining them via RR extrapolation to reduce bias while controlling variance. The key theoretical claim is that SGRRLD achieves MSE convergence at O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. The paper provides theoretical analysis and experiments on synthetic and real datasets.

## Strengths

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and well-motivated, extending previous work on RR for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro [21], Lemaire et al. [23]) to the stochastic gradient setting. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with Theorems 1 and 2 establishing the asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. The experiments convincingly support the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in bias and MSE over SGLD on both synthetic and real-world matrix factorization tasks. The parallel nature of the algorithm is well-exploited in the implementation, and the paper demonstrates practical computational advantages.

## Weaknesses and Critical Concerns

1. **Theoretical gaps and assumptions**: The paper relies heavily on technical conditions that are relegated to the supplementary document. The assumption that the Poisson equation has a solution in C⁹(Rᵈ) (for the CLT proof) is quite strong and not well-justified for practical Bayesian models. The dependence of constant C in Theorem 2 on gradient estimator moments is not analyzed, which could significantly impact practical performance.

2. **Lack of comparison with higher-order methods**: The paper claims SGRRLD "can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" (Section 1), but fails to provide a direct comparison with actual higher-order methods like those in Chen et al. [10]. Without such comparisons, the claim remains unsubstantiated.

3. **Practical implementation details**: The paper states SGRRLD "is well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures" but doesn't clarify how the parallelism is actually implemented in practice. The experiments show similar wall-clock times for SGRRLD and SGLD, but this is because SGRRLD runs for half as many iterations. A more informative comparison would show accuracy at the same wall-clock time (not just half the iterations).

4. **Sensitivity to step size choices**: The paper claims optimal convergence rates for specific step size sequences (e.g., α=1/5 for MSE), but doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to deviations from these optimal choices in practice. The experiments in Figure 2 show performance drops for small γ, but the paper doesn't explain how to select γ in practice.

5. **Overstated significance of convergence rates**: While the O(K⁻⁴/⁵) rate is theoretically superior to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), the paper doesn't quantify how much this translates to practical benefits. For instance, at K=10⁵, the improvement is only about 1.5× in the convergence rate, which may not be substantial in practical applications.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to SG-MCMC methods with promising experimental results. However, several key weaknesses limit its impact. The theoretical claims are not fully substantiated by the presented analysis, and the practical significance of the improved convergence rate is not clearly demonstrated. The paper's claim about achieving "theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators" is particularly problematic without direct comparisons.

## Recommendations

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions for revision:
1. Add a direct comparison with actual higher-order SG-MCMC methods (e.g., from Chen et al. [10]) to validate the key theoretical claim.
2. Provide more detailed practical implementation guidance, including how the two chains are synchronized in practice.
3. Include a comparison of accuracy at the same wall-clock time (not just half the iterations) to better demonstrate the practical benefits of the parallelism.
4. Discuss the practical sensitivity to step size choices and how to select them in real applications.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the SG-MCMC literature, but needs to address these gaps to fully justify its claims and significance. With these revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real data
- Practical parallel implementation that leverages distributed architectures

**Con:**
- Key theoretical claims not fully substantiated by analysis or experiments
- Overstated significance of convergence rate improvements
- Lack of comparison with actual higher-order methods
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation details and sensitivity

The paper has merit but requires substantial clarification and additional experiments to fully support its claims.
