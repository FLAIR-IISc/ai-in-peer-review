Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a new SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce discretization bias without resorting to higher-order integrators. SGRRLD runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 and constructs an extrapolated estimator that cancels the leading-order bias. To control variance inflation, the method uses correlated Gaussian increments across the two chains; the theory shows that perfect correlation (Σ = I) minimizes asymptotic variance.

The authors establish a CLT for the extrapolated estimator, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and finite-time convergence rates. Under appropriate assumptions and with a first-order integrator, SGRRLD attains O(γ^2) bias and O(γ^4) variance, and achieves an MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) with optimal constant step size γ ∝ K^{-1/5}, improving over O(K^{-2/3}) for SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. They also discuss decreasing step sizes, with optimal α = 1/5. Empirically, they demonstrate gains on a Gaussian model (closed-form posterior) and a large-scale probabilistic matrix factorization task on MovieLens datasets, and show similar benefits when applying RR to SGHMC (termed SGRRHMC).

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on SGLD [Welling & Teh 2011], its non/asymptotic analyses [Teh et al. 2016], and higher-order SG-MCMC integrators [Chen et al. 2015].
- Leverages RR extrapolation for SDE discretization bias reduction [Talay & Tubaro 1990; Lemaire et al. 2015], extending it to the SG-MCMC setting with stochastic gradients.
- Related in spirit to multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) for SDEs but distinct in focus; the authors note potential connections/future work.

Strengths
- Clear, principled idea: cancel leading discretization bias of first-order integrators using RR, with theory tailored to stochastic gradients.
- Solid theory: CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and rate optimization for both constant and decreasing step sizes. The O(K^{-4/5}) MSE rate with a first-order integrator is compelling.
- Insightful variance control via correlated noise; the result that Σ = I is optimal is useful and actionable.
- Generic framework potentially applicable to other SG-MCMC schemes (e.g., SGHMC), with preliminary evidence (SGRRHMC) supporting this.
- Practical angle: the two chains can be run in parallel, turning extra per-iteration work into wall-clock gains on multicore machines.
- Experiments are consistent with theory on synthetic data; real data (MovieLens) shows better test RMSE over time at matched wall-clock.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Assumptions: The analysis relies on strong smoothness and ergodicity conditions (e.g., solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C^9, polynomial growth, dissipativity), and on the gradient estimator properties. It would help to discuss how sensitive results are to violations (e.g., non-smooth posteriors, heavy tails, non-convex deep models).
- Gradient noise and coupling: The theory emphasizes correlating Gaussian increments across chains. In practice, how important is aligning the stochastic gradient noise (e.g., using the same mini-batch across chains at a given time)? The paper states same distribution is required; an ablation showing the impact of sharing vs. not sharing mini-batches would be informative.
- Experimental scope: Core experiments use a Gaussian posterior and matrix factorization with Gaussian likelihoods. More challenging/non-Gaussian posteriors (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural networks) would better demonstrate robustness beyond strongly log-concave settings.
- Baselines: For SGLD, consider comparing against higher-order or variance-reduced SG-MCMC baselines cited (e.g., methods from Ma et al. 2015; pSGLD), and include SGHMC-s baselines on the large-scale task. The SGHMC comparison is only on the Gaussian model.
- Fairness and compute: The wall-clock parity assumes perfect parallelism for two chains; many users may lack such resources. A complementary comparison under a fixed total number of stochastic gradients (single core) would clarify trade-offs. Also discuss memory/communication overheads of running coupled chains.
- Step-size tuning: The experiments mainly use constant step sizes; the theory dedicates space to decreasing schedules. Including results with decreasing schedules would round out the empirical story.
- Practical guidance: Provide more guidance for choosing γ and B in practice (e.g., how to tune toward γ ∝ K^{-1/5} when K is unknown, or adaptively). Also include pseudo-code in the main text if space permits.
- Minor issues: Some typos/formatting glitches; ensure clarity and consistent notation (e.g., reiterate the noise scaling √(2γ) vs. √(γ) for half-steps, as done correctly, and emphasize the correlation construction).

Quality
Technically sound with careful proofs sketched and full details deferred to the supplement. The main claims are supported by both theory and experiments consistent with the predicted trends. Assumptions are strong but standard in this literature; better discussion of their practical implications would improve the paper.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. The method and its motivation are clear; the theorems are interpretable with helpful rate discussions. Some dependence on the supplement for conditions/proofs is expected at NIPS but adding pseudo-code and a brief ablation on correlation choices would help.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC to achieve higher-order accuracy with first-order integrators is novel to my knowledge at the time of submission. The variance control via correlated Brownian increments in the SG setting is a nice extension of prior RR work for SDEs.

Significance
Improving first-order SG-MCMC to match higher-order accuracy without developing complex integrators is impactful. The method is generic, parallelizable, and simple to implement. If broadly adopted, it could meaningfully improve SG-MCMC performance.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, novel, and broadly applicable idea with solid theory and practical gains.
- Demonstrated improved finite-time MSE rates and better wall-clock performance on a large-scale task.
- Bridges a mature numerical technique (RR) into SG-MCMC.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental diversity and baselines; unclear performance on non-Gaussian/non-convex posteriors.
- Strong assumptions with limited discussion of robustness.
- Practical costs when parallelism is unavailable are not explored.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is SGRRLD to using different mini-batches across the two chains? Any guidance on coupling gradient noise?
- Have you tried SGRRLD on logistic regression or non-convex models (e.g., BNNs)? Any failure modes?
- Can the RR idea be stacked (multi-step RR) in practice without excessive variance or cost?
- Under single-threaded compute budgets, how does SGRRLD compare to SGLD with twice as many iterations?

Recommendation
Weak accept (7/10). Strong theoretical contribution with a simple, general, and implementable idea that yields practical improvements. Expanding empirical validation and baselines would strengthen the paper further. Confidence: high.
