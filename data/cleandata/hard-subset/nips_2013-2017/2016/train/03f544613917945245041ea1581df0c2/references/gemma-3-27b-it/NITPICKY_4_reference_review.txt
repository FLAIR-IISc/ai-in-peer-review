This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD by employing Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea—running two chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs—is a valid and potentially impactful technique, drawing inspiration from numerical analysis for SDEs. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates and validate their approach with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

However, while the idea is sound and the presentation generally clear, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a degree of overclaiming that, collectively, significantly diminish its impact. These are not necessarily dealbreakers individually, but their sheer number raises concerns about the thoroughness and rigor of the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide theoretical guarantees for convergence and bias reduction.
* **Experimental Validation:** Experiments demonstrate improvements over SGLD and SGHMC, lending credibility to the proposed method.
* **Parallelizability:** The algorithm’s suitability for parallel architectures is a valuable attribute.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Formatting & Presentation:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., uneven spacing, awkward phrasing, inconsistent notation). While seemingly trivial, these detract from readability and suggest a lack of meticulousness. The introduction is also somewhat verbose and repetitive.
* **Technical Details & Clarity:** Several technical details lack sufficient clarity. For example, the justification for the correlation structure in Equation (8) feels underdeveloped. The relationship between the chosen step size sequences and the theoretical convergence rates isn't always explicitly stated.
* **Overclaiming:** The claim that SGRRLD achieves “the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators” is overstated. While convergence rates improve, the algorithm fundamentally relies on a first-order integrator; achieving equivalent accuracy to higher-order methods is a strong claim needing more precise justification.
* **Experimental Setup & Reporting:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail. The specific parameter settings for the matrix factorization experiment (beyond step size and minibatch size) are missing. Reporting RMSE alone for the matrix factorization task is insufficient; metrics like precision/recall or NDCG would be more informative. The justification for choosing the step sizes in the synthetic experiments is also weak.
* **Related Work:** The discussion of related work, while present, feels incomplete. For instance, the connection to multi-level Monte Carlo methods (mentioned briefly in the conclusion) deserves more extensive exploration as a related technique for variance reduction.
* **Minor Errors:** A few minor errors were spotted (e.g., typos in equations). These don't invalidate the work but further contribute to the impression of insufficient polish.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The impact of the correlation structure introduced in Equation (8) is not thoroughly investigated. An ablation study comparing performance with and without correlated Brownian motions would be beneficial.
* **Inconsistent Notation:** The usage of subscripts is often confusing. Distinguishing between different variables indexed by 'k' requires more careful consideration and consistent notation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The core idea is novel and potentially valuable for scaling Bayesian inference.
* The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* The experimental results demonstrate tangible improvements over existing approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies raises concerns about the overall rigor of the work.
* The overclaiming regarding accuracy compared to higher-order integrators is misleading.
* The experimental section lacks sufficient detail and uses inadequate evaluation metrics for the matrix factorization task.
* The incomplete discussion of related work and missing ablation studies limit the paper’s impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its potential, this paper falls short of NIPS quality due to a significant number of minor flaws and a tendency towards overstatement. While the underlying idea is promising, the lack of meticulousness and thoroughness detract from the overall contribution. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the points raised in this review and resubmit in the future. A more polished presentation, more detailed experimental analysis, and a more nuanced discussion of the algorithm’s limitations would significantly improve the paper's chances of acceptance.
