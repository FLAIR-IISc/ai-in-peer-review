## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias in Bayesian inference. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, typically used in numerical analysis for accelerating convergence, to SGLD. This involves running two Langevin dynamics chains in parallel with different step sizes and extrapolating their results to achieve a higher order of accuracy. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (MSE of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) and show that SGRRLD is well-suited for parallel computation.  The efficacy of SGRRLD is validated through experiments on both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC) and a real-world application (matrix factorization for movie recommendation).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the burgeoning field of SG-MCMC, initiated by Welling and Teh (2011) with SGLD.  It acknowledges and correctly positions itself within the landscape of subsequent improvements to SGLD, citing key works like SGHMC and higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015). The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SDEs, drawing from the work of Talay and Tubaro (1990) and Lemaire & Pagès (2015), is a clever and novel contribution. The paper effectively contrasts its approach with alternatives like higher-order integrators, highlighting that SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy with a simpler first-order base integrator.  The connection to multilevel Monte Carlo (Lemaire & Pagès, 2014) is acknowledged as a potential direction for future work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea that offers a potentially significant improvement in efficiency.  The authors are to be commended for bridging concepts from numerical analysis and Bayesian inference.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including convergence theorems and bounds on bias and MSE. The derivation of the O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) convergence rate is a key result. The detailed analysis of the correlation structure of Brownian motions is also commendable.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results, on both synthetic and real data, convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD. The performance gains on the matrix factorization task, particularly leveraging parallel processing, are encouraging.
* **Generality:** The authors correctly note the potential for applying RR extrapolation to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, broadening the impact of their work.
* **Clarity (Generally Good):** The paper is mostly clearly written and well-organized. The equations are well-defined and the core ideas are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations & Scalability:** While the theoretical gains are appealing, the practical overhead of running two chains in parallel needs further discussion.  The authors mention parallelism, but do not detail the communication costs associated with maintaining correlated Brownian motions across processes, particularly in a highly distributed setting. How does this overhead scale with the problem size and number of processors?
* **Sensitivity to Step Size:**  The performance of RR extrapolation is known to be sensitive to the choice of step sizes (γ and γ/2). The paper discusses this, but the practical guidelines for selecting optimal step sizes in complex, high-dimensional Bayesian models are lacking.  A more thorough sensitivity analysis of step size selection across different problem settings would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions & Conditions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several technical assumptions (weak error and ergodicity conditions). While stated, the implications of violating these assumptions are not thoroughly explored.  A discussion of the robustness of the method to deviations from these assumptions would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to SGLD and SGHMC is well done, the authors could strengthen their evaluation by comparing SGRRLD to more recent, highly optimized SG-MCMC variants and potentially to variational inference methods.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Equation (8) defining the correlated Brownian increments is slightly opaque and could benefit from further explanation.  



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for improving SG-MCMC.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a clear performance gain.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets.
* The potential for broader applicability beyond SGLD is promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical overhead associated with the parallel chain execution needs more detailed consideration.
* The sensitivity of the method to step size selection warrants further investigation.
*  A more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art alternatives would strengthen the evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are compelling, and the authors clearly articulate the benefits of their approach.  While there are some practical considerations and potential limitations that could be further explored, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this work is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – address the scalability concerns and provide more guidance on step size selection).
