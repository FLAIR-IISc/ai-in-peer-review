

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors build on Welling and Teh's (2011) SGLD and Chen et al.'s (2015) work on higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC. While Chen et al. showed that higher-order integrators improve convergence rates, SGRRLD achieves similar theoretical accuracy using only first-order integrators by running two chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining them via RR extrapolation. The paper claims SGRRLD reduces bias to O(γ²) and variance to O(γ⁴), leading to an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The authors demonstrate that their approach can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms and provide theoretical guarantees along with experimental validation on synthetic and real datasets.

The paper's strengths include strong theoretical contributions, with rigorous analysis of convergence properties (Theorems 1 and 2) that build on existing work in SDE discretization and RR extrapolation. The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, as the authors correctly note this technique has not been previously applied in this context. The method's parallelizability (two chains run independently) is well-suited for distributed computing environments, and the experimental validation on Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks supports the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in bias and MSE.

Several weaknesses merit consideration. The paper lacks comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD and SGHMC. For instance, it doesn't compare with more recent approaches like Stochastic Quasi-Newton Langevin Monte Carlo (Simsekli et al., ICML 2016) or other methods proposed after Chen et al. (2015). The claim that the technique can be applied to "all current SG-MCMC algorithms" requires more detailed discussion of the technical conditions necessary for this generality. While the paper mentions that the two chains can be run in parallel, it doesn't adequately address memory requirements compared to SGLD, which could be a practical limitation for very large-scale problems. The experimental section could provide more details about computational resources and implementation choices.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation. It properly contextualizes the work within NIPS proceedings (Welling and Teh 2011, Chen et al. 2015) while introducing a new technique that addresses a meaningful limitation in SG-MCMC. The improved convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) is significant for large-scale Bayesian inference, and the parallel nature of the algorithm enhances its practical utility.

For acceptance considerations:
- Pro: Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with strong theoretical guarantees, addressing a meaningful problem in scalable Bayesian inference.
- Pro: Demonstrated practical improvements in both synthetic and real-world experiments.
- Con: Limited comparison with the most recent SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD and SGHMC.
- Con: Insufficient discussion of practical limitations like memory requirements.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel technique that improves convergence rates for SG-MCMC while maintaining parallelizability. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is appropriate, and the work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS publications. While the paper could be strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons with recent methods and deeper exploration of practical limitations, the core contribution is novel, technically sound, and impactful for scalable Bayesian inference. The paper meets all four review criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original in its application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, and significant for improving convergence rates in large-scale Bayesian applications.

I recommend acceptance as the paper presents a strong theoretical contribution that is well-supported by empirical results and addresses an important problem in the field.
