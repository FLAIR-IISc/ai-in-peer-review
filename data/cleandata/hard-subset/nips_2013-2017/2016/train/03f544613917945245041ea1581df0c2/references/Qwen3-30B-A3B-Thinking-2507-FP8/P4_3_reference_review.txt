

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon SGLD [1] and the recent work by Chen et al. [10] on the convergence of SG-MCMC methods. While Chen et al. showed that higher-order integrators can improve convergence rates, SGRRLD demonstrates that a first-order integrator combined with RR extrapolation can achieve similar theoretical accuracy without the complexity of higher-order methods.

The main contribution is the theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}), compared to O(K^{-2/3}) for SGLD and similar first-order methods. The authors provide a central limit theorem for the estimator and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. The paper also demonstrates that SGRRLD can be applied to other SG-MCMC methods like SGHMC, resulting in SGRRHMC, which achieves accuracy comparable to higher-order integrator methods.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, establishing a central limit theorem and deriving precise convergence rates. The paper carefully addresses the variance control through correlated Brownian motions, justifying the choice of correlation structure.
2. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization for recommendation systems) consistently support the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in accuracy with comparable computation time.
3. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient mathematical detail. The connection between the theoretical analysis and experimental validation is strong.
4. The method is inherently parallelizable, which is a significant practical advantage for large-scale applications, as demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not clearly establish why the specific RR extrapolation approach (using two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2) is superior to other potential bias reduction techniques for SG-MCMC. While the authors cite previous work on RR for SDEs, they do not adequately contrast their approach with other bias reduction methods in the SG-MCMC literature.
2. The theoretical analysis relies on several technical assumptions (e.g., on the potential energy function U and the gradient estimator distribution L) that are not thoroughly discussed or validated in the experiments. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion of how restrictive these assumptions are in practice.
3. The experiments on the matrix factorization task are limited to three datasets with fixed hyperparameters. More comprehensive experiments exploring the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (particularly the step size schedule) would better demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs (referenced to supplementary materials) and experimental validation. The analysis of the bias and MSE convergence rates is thorough and well-supported.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and precise mathematical notation. The connection between the theoretical claims and experimental results is well-established.

**Originality:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, as evidenced by the lack of prior NIPS papers addressing this specific combination. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from Chen et al.'s [10] work on higher-order integrators by showing that RR extrapolation can achieve similar accuracy without higher-order discretization.

**Significance:** The method provides a practical and theoretically grounded approach to improving the convergence rates of SG-MCMC methods, which is crucial for large-scale Bayesian inference. The demonstrated improvements in accuracy with comparable computational cost make this contribution significant for practitioners.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with strong theoretical justification
- Improved convergence rate (O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3}))
- Theoretically sound with rigorous analysis
- Practical benefits demonstrated on real-world datasets
- Inherently parallelizable, matching NIPS focus on scalable methods

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Some technical assumptions for the theoretical results may be restrictive in practice
- Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity in experiments
- The paper could better differentiate from other bias reduction approaches in SG-MCMC

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference by introducing a theoretically grounded and practically effective method for improving SG-MCMC convergence rates. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental results convincingly support the claims. While the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying the theoretical results and more comprehensive parameter sensitivity analysis, these do not substantially undermine the main contribution. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and provides clear improvements over existing approaches. Given the importance of scalable Bayesian inference in modern machine learning, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
