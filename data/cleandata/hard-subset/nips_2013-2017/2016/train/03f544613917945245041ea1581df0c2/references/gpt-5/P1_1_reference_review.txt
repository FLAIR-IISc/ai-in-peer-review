Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a stochastic-gradient MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD. The idea is to run two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and form a bias-canceling estimator by a linear combination of the two discretizations. To prevent variance inflation, the authors correlate the Gaussian noises across the two chains using common random numbers (a la multilevel Monte Carlo), and analyze the asymptotic optimality of full correlation. Theoretically, they establish a CLT for the estimator and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, showing improved orders compared to vanilla SGLD: asymptotic bias O(γ^2) (vs. O(γ)) and MSE O(γ^4) (vs. O(γ^2)); for K iterations and constant step size, the optimal MSE rate is O(K^{-4/5}) (vs. O(K^{-2/3})). The approach is claimed to be applicable to other SG-MCMC methods; an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) is illustrated. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) show lower bias/MSE and better RMSE-time tradeoffs than SGLD, and competitive accuracy to higher-order SGHMC integrators.

Relation to prior work
RR extrapolation for SDE weak error reduction has a long history (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire et al.). Its use for invariant measures and CLTs in the deterministic-gradient setting is known. Applying RR to SG-MCMC, where gradients are noisy and bias/variance tradeoffs involve both discretization and stochastic gradient noise, appears novel. The work complements analyses of SG-MCMC convergence and higher-order integrators (e.g., Ma et al. 2015; Chen et al. 2015) by showing that a first-order integrator with extrapolation can match higher-order accuracy, and relates to multilevel ideas (acknowledged as future work).

Quality
- Theory: The main claims (CLT; bias/MSE non-asymptotic bounds; optimal correlation of Gaussian noises) are plausible and consistent with MLMC theory. The step-size optimization yielding O(K^{-4/5}) is correct given the bound O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)). However, many technical conditions are deferred to the supplement and look strong (e.g., solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C^9), which may limit generality. It would be helpful to state the core assumptions on U and on the stochastic gradient noise L in the main text (Lipschitzness, dissipativity, moment bounds).
- Correctness: There appears to be a likely typo in the Chain 2 update (eq. (5)): the first half-step uses ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) on the RHS, which makes it an implicit update; SGLD should use θ(γ/2)2k on the RHS. Please clarify/correct.
- Variance control: The analysis that Σ=Id minimizes the asymptotic variance aligns with MLMC/common random numbers intuition; good to see this formalized for SG-MCMC.
- Practical aspects: The requirement that both chains use gradient estimators with the same distribution L is necessary; in practice, sharing the same mini-batch indices across levels (in addition to shared Gaussian noise) would likely further reduce variance—this should be discussed/ablation-tested.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The introduction and preliminaries set the stage well. Some notation is heavy, and several important conditions and the algorithm pseudocode are in the supplement; including a concise algorithm box in the main paper would help. Please fix the suspected typo in eq. (5) and unify notation (e.g., Σ vs Id, and consistent use of √2 in noise terms). The discussion contrasting asymptotic and finite-time rates is helpful.

Originality
The use of RR extrapolation in the SG-MCMC setting, with correlated Brownian increments to control variance and a detailed convergence analysis, appears novel. While RR itself is classical, its adaptation to noisy-gradient sampling and the derived finite-time rates constitute an original contribution. The extension to SGHMC is natural but useful.

Significance
Reducing bias/MSE of SG-MCMC without resorting to higher-order integrators, and doing so in a way that is parallelizable, is valuable. The theoretical finite-time rates and practical gains on real datasets suggest that SGRRLD could be adopted by practitioners. The approach may inspire broader MLMC-style designs for SG-MCMC.

Empirical evaluation
The synthetic experiments convincingly validate the predicted scalings (bias/MSE vs γ and K) and show clear gains over SGLD. The matrix factorization experiments demonstrate wall-clock advantages at equal parallel resources. Two caveats:
- Fairness: SGRRLD uses two chains run in parallel; SGLD is inherently sequential in the implementation. For compute-parity, it would be informative to also compare against SGLD that uses twice the iterations or minibatch size (or two independent SGLD chains averaged), to isolate the effect of extrapolation vs more compute.
- Ablations: Show the impact of (i) independent vs correlated Gaussian noises; (ii) sharing vs not sharing minibatches across levels; (iii) comparison to preconditioned SGLD (pSGLD) and other SG-MCMC baselines cited.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, generic technique with solid theoretical backing.
- Clear improvement in bias and MSE rates; finite-time bounds and CLT provided.
- Parallel-friendly algorithm; practical gains on large-scale matrix factorization.
- Extension to SGHMC and comparison to higher-order integrators.

Cons
- Some theoretical assumptions are strong and relegated to the supplement.
- Possible typo/ambiguity in the two-level update equation.
- Fairness of empirical comparisons could be strengthened (compute parity, additional baselines).
- Limited discussion on how to couple/structure the minibatch sampling across levels.

Questions for authors
1) Can you confirm/correct the Chain 2 first half-step (eq. (5))? Is it explicit Euler?
2) Do you share mini-batch indices between the γ and γ/2 chains in experiments? If not, what is the impact?
3) How sensitive are the gains to the mini-batch size and to using decreasing step-size schedules in practice?
4) Can SGRRLD be combined with preconditioning or control variates, and do the rates persist?

Minor comments/typos
- Several minor typos (e.g., “varice,” “Futher,” “the theorems”).
- Be consistent with factors of 2 in noise scaling across levels.
- Provide the algorithm pseudocode in the main text.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a well-motivated, technically solid paper that brings a classical extrapolation idea to SG-MCMC with nontrivial analysis and practical benefits. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation issues would strengthen the submission further.
