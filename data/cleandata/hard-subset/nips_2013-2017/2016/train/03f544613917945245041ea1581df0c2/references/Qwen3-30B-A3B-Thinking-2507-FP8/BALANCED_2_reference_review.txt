

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon existing SG-MCMC methods like SGLD [1] and extend the RR extrapolation technique from numerical analysis [21] to stochastic gradient MCMC settings. The key insight is that running two chains with different step sizes in parallel, followed by RR extrapolation, can yield an estimator with improved convergence rates compared to standard first-order SG-MCMC methods.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}) compared to O(K^{-2/3}) for SGLD. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and correctly derived from the existing literature on SDE discretizations and RR extrapolation.
2. **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the algorithm, its theoretical properties, and experimental results. The connection to prior work is properly established, particularly regarding the relationship to the recent work by Chen et al. [10] on higher-order integrators.
3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization for movie recommendations) convincingly support the theoretical claims. The results demonstrate significant improvements in accuracy with comparable computational time.
4. **Practical Insight:** The authors correctly note that SGRRLD's parallel nature makes it well-suited for modern distributed architectures, which is a significant practical advantage over sequential SG-MCMC methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Computational Overhead:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel. While the authors note that the two chains can be run independently, they don't quantify whether the wall-clock time is approximately doubled or whether parallelization can mitigate this cost. This is critical for assessing practical utility.
2. **Implementation Details:** The requirement that both chains use the same mini-batch size (and presumably same mini-batch sampling strategy) is mentioned but not thoroughly discussed. In practice, maintaining identical mini-batch distributions across two parallel chains might require additional synchronization overhead, which could offset the theoretical gains.
3. **Comparison with Higher-Order Methods:** The paper claims SGRRLD achieves "the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" [10], but the experimental comparison in Section 5.1 only includes SGHMC (with Euler discretization) and SGHMC-s (with higher-order integrator), without showing direct comparison of convergence rates. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the claim.
4. **Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper doesn't explore how sensitive the performance is to the choice of step size sequences or the correlation structure in Equation (8). Understanding this would be valuable for practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature with a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The results are well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the method has clear practical advantages for parallel computing environments. The main weaknesses relate to the lack of detailed discussion about computational overhead and implementation challenges, which are crucial for assessing the method's real-world applicability.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, and the experimental results support the claims. The authors should address the computational overhead question more thoroughly, perhaps by adding a brief discussion of the practical wall-clock time implications of running two chains in parallel. They should also provide a more detailed comparison with higher-order methods in the experiments to fully validate their claim about achieving "the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators."

The paper is well-written, makes a clear contribution to the field, and meets all the criteria for NeurIPS publication. The minor weaknesses identified do not undermine the paper's overall quality, but addressing them would strengthen the work further.
