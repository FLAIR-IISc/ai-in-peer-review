### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, a classical method for numerical sequence acceleration, to the SGLD algorithm to reduce its discretization bias. The method involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard first-order SG-MCMC methods (O(K⁻²/³)). This improved rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex higher-order integrators. The paper's claims are supported by experiments on both a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and significant contribution. While RR extrapolation is a well-known technique for deterministic and stochastic differential equations (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), its adaptation and analysis in the context of *stochastic gradient* noise is non-trivial and new. The resulting improvement in the theoretical convergence rate for MSE from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is substantial and advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference.

2.  **High Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a thorough theoretical analysis.
    *   The derivation of both a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds (Theorem 2) provides a comprehensive picture of the algorithm's behavior. The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step-size decay rate (α = 1/5 for MSE) and highlights the critical role of using correlated noise across the two chains to control variance, a subtle but important detail.
    *   The theoretical claim that a first-order integrator can achieve the accuracy of higher-order ones (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) is compelling, as it offers a conceptually simpler path to higher accuracy.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The empirical results convincingly support the theoretical claims.
    *   The linear Gaussian model experiment is well-designed, allowing for precise quantification of bias and MSE against a known ground truth. The results in Figures 1, 2, and 3 clearly demonstrate the superior performance of SGRRLD over SGLD in terms of bias and MSE reduction and confirm the derived optimal convergence rates with respect to K and γ.
    *   The demonstration of the method's generality by applying it to SGHMC (creating SGRRHMC) is a strong point, showing the technique is not just a one-off trick for SGLD. The comparison with a higher-order SGHMC integrator (SGHMC-s) in Figure 4 provides evidence for the claim of matching higher-order accuracy.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiment on MovieLens datasets successfully translates the theoretical gains into practical benefits, showing faster convergence in wall-clock time to a lower RMSE. This is a crucial demonstration of the algorithm's real-world utility.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is presented precisely, and the theoretical results are stated without ambiguity. The connection to prior work is well-established, making the paper's contribution easy to understand and appreciate.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational and Memory Overhead:** The most significant weakness is the inherent computational trade-off. SGRRLD requires approximately three times the computation of a single SGLD run (one step for the γ chain, two steps for the γ/2 chain). The paper argues this is offset by parallelism, leading to similar wall-clock times. However, this relies on the availability of at least two parallel processing units. On a single core, SGRRLD would be significantly slower per "effective" iteration. The total computational cost (e.g., total gradient evaluations) is tripled, a fact that should be more explicitly stated and discussed. Similarly, the memory footprint is doubled as two separate chains must be maintained, which could be a concern for extremely large models.

2.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** While the paper claims to match the accuracy of higher-order integrators and provides some evidence in Figure 4, the comparison could be more direct. Higher-order methods also have increased computational cost per step. A detailed comparison of SGRRLD against a method like SGHMC-s based on a fixed *total computational budget* (not just wall-clock time on a parallel machine) would provide a fairer and more insightful evaluation of the practical trade-offs.

3.  **Scope of Generality:** The paper claims the technique can be "virtually applied to all the current SG-MCMC algorithms." While this is demonstrated for SGHMC, the theoretical analysis relies on specific weak error expansion properties of the underlying integrator. A brief discussion of which SG-MCMC methods satisfy these properties would strengthen this claim of generality. For example, would it apply to methods with thermostats or Riemannian geometry?

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   In Section 3, the choice of correlating the Brownian noise (`Z`) is well-justified by theory to minimize variance. However, the paper is silent on the correlation of the stochastic gradients (`∇Ũ`). Since the same mini-batch could be used for the γ and γ/2 updates within an iteration, this would induce correlation. Does the theory or practice suggest an optimal strategy here (e.g., using the same mini-batch vs. independent mini-batches)? The assumption that the estimators are i.i.d. seems to imply independent mini-batches are used.
*   The results in Figure 2 show that for very small `γ`, the performance of SGRRLD degrades and becomes worse than SGLD. This is correctly attributed to the `1/(Kγ)` term dominating the MSE. This highlights that SGRRLD is most beneficial when the discretization error (related to `γ`) is the dominant error source, which is a key practical consideration for users of this method. This trade-off is important and well-illustrated.

### **Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful algorithm. The idea of using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation in the SG-MCMC context is both elegant and effective. The authors provide a strong combination of rigorous theory and convincing experiments that demonstrate a significant improvement in convergence rates. The weaknesses identified, primarily concerning the computational trade-off, are not fundamental flaws but rather practical considerations that could be discussed more explicitly. The paper represents a clear and important contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference and is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
