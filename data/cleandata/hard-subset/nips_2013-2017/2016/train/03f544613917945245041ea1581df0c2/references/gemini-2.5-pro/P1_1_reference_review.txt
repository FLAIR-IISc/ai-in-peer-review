### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed method applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2, where the γ/2 chain takes two steps for every one step of the γ chain) and combining their outputs in a specific way to cancel the leading-order error term. A key technical aspect is the use of correlated Brownian motion between the two chains to control the variance of the resulting estimator.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They show that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD. This allows a first-order method to achieve the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. The paper supports these theoretical claims with extensive experiments on both a synthetic Gaussian model, where exact error can be measured, and a large-scale matrix factorization task, demonstrating practical performance gains in terms of wall-clock time.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elegant and Novel Idea:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a simple, elegant, and novel contribution. It provides a new and effective way to tackle the problem of integrator bias, which is a fundamental limitation of many scalable MCMC methods. This approach is a clever alternative to designing more complex, higher-order integrators.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is technically sound and provides rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors derive both asymptotic (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic (Theorem 2) results that clearly quantify the improvement in convergence rates for both bias and MSE. The analysis of the optimal step size schedule (α=1/5 for MSE) is insightful and well-justified.

3.  **Excellent Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is thorough and convincing.
    *   The linear Gaussian model provides a perfect testbed for verifying the theoretical claims, as the true posterior is known. The experiments clearly demonstrate the predicted reduction in bias and MSE (Figs 1, 2) and validate the optimal step-size decay rate (Fig 3).
    *   The extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) effectively demonstrates the generality of the proposed technique.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiment on MovieLens datasets shows that the theoretical improvements translate into real-world performance gains, with SGRRLD achieving better accuracy faster than SGLD in terms of wall-clock time.

4.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** The algorithm is designed for modern computing architectures. The authors correctly identify that the two chains required for RR extrapolation can be run in parallel. Their experiments leverage this, showing a direct advantage in wall-clock time, which is the most relevant metric for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational and Memory Overhead:** The primary drawback of SGRRLD is that it requires twice the computation (two gradient estimates per effective step) and twice the memory (to store two parameter chains) compared to standard SGLD. While the paper addresses the computational cost via parallelization, this is a critical trade-off that should be discussed more explicitly. On a single-core system, the method would be roughly twice as slow per iteration. A brief discussion on how SGRRLD compares to simply running SGLD for twice as many iterations (or with a smaller step size) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Discussion on Gradient Correlation:** The theory shows that correlating the Brownian motion is crucial for variance reduction, but the impact of correlating the mini-batch draws for the gradient estimators is not deeply explored. The paper assumes they can be correlated but does not analyze the potential benefits or prescribe a specific strategy. In practice, using the same mini-batch for the corresponding steps in the two chains seems like a natural choice, and its effect could be worth discussing.

3.  **Complexity of the Estimator:** The final estimator in Eq. (6) is a linear combination of function evaluations from three points at each step (`θ(γ)k`, `θ(γ/2)2k-1`, `θ(γ/2)2k`). This is slightly more complex to implement and track than the standard ergodic average in SGLD, which could be a minor barrier to adoption.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

- **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the method is explained well, and the connection between the theory and experiments is strong. The sketch proofs in the main text are helpful, with full details appropriately deferred to the supplement.

- **Quality:** The technical quality is high. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors are careful in their analysis and experimental design. The comparison against SGHMC with a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s) is a nice touch that substantiates their claim of achieving higher-order accuracy with a first-order method.

- **Originality:** The work is highly original. While SG-MCMC and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation are known techniques, their combination in this context is novel and impactful. It clearly differs from previous work that has focused on designing new integrators or adding correction terms.

- **Significance:** The results are highly significant for the field of scalable Bayesian inference. SGRRLD offers a practical, general-purpose "wrapper" that can be applied to improve the accuracy of many existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Given the demonstrated performance gains, this method is likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners. It advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  Introduces a novel and elegant method for bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
2.  Provides strong and comprehensive theoretical guarantees for improved convergence rates.
3.  Demonstrates significant practical performance gains on a large-scale, real-world problem, leveraging parallel computation.
4.  The proposed technique is general and can be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  The method inherently doubles the computational cost per effective iteration, a trade-off that could be discussed more explicitly.
2.  Memory requirements are also doubled, which could be a concern for extremely large models.

**Recommendation:** **Accept.**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound algorithm. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths and represent inherent trade-offs rather than fundamental flaws. The comprehensive experimental validation provides compelling evidence of the method's practical utility. This work is a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian machine learning and is an excellent fit for NIPS.
