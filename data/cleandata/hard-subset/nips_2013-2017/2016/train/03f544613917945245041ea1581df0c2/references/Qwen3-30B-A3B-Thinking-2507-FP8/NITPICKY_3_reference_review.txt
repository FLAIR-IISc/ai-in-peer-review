

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes SGRRLD, an extension of SGLD that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The main contribution is the theoretical analysis showing SGRRLD achieves MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), with supporting experiments on synthetic and real datasets.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the SG-MCMC literature, building on previous work by [10] and [21]. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with careful derivation of bias and MSE bounds. The experiments are well-designed, showing clear improvements over SGLD and SGHMC on both synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization problems. The parallel implementation advantage is well-explained.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Errors
The paper contains multiple formatting and technical inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism:

1. Inconsistent mathematical notation: "O(γ2)" appears throughout instead of "O(γ^2)" (e.g., abstract, Section 1, Theorem 2). This is a critical formatting error that should be corrected.

2. Equation (6) has multiple notational issues: "K+1∑ k=2" should be "∑_{k=2}^{K+1}" and "K∑ k=1" should be "∑_{k=1}^K". The summation notation is confusing and unprofessional.

3. Inconsistent use of matrix transpose: "Σ >" (with space) appears multiple times instead of "Σ^T" or "Σ^>".

4. Equation (8) contains "Σ >" and "(Id−Σ>Σ)1/2" - these should be consistently formatted as "Σ^T" and "(Id−Σ^TΣ)^{1/2}".

5. The phrase "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice more time than the other one" is grammatically incorrect and confusing. It should state "the chain with the smallest step size is run for twice as many iterations" or "the chain with the smallest step size requires twice as many steps to reach the same time horizon."

### Theoretical Presentation
The paper claims "the bias and the MSE of a general class of SG-MCMC methods can be bounded as O(γ) and O(γ2), respectively" (Section 1). This is incorrect - it should be O(γ) and O(γ^2), not O(γ2). This error appears multiple times throughout the paper, creating confusion about the theoretical contributions.

### Reference Issues
The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies:
- "Non-)asymptotic properties" (should be "Non-asymptotic")
- Extra commas in multiple references (e.g., "ICML, 2008, pp. 880–887., ,")
- "th Conference" should be "The Conference" (NIPS 2016)

### Experimental Details
The paper states "We fix the step size to γ = 10−3" (Section 5.1), but the notation should be "γ = 10^{-3}". The inconsistent use of mathematical notation throughout the paper (e.g., "10−3" vs "10^{-3}") is unprofessional.

### Theoretical Claims
The paper claims "SGRRLD is well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures" because "the two chains required by the RR extrapolation can be generated independently." While this is true, the paper fails to acknowledge that other SG-MCMC methods can also be parallelized, diminishing the significance of this claim.

## Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to SG-MCMC with promising experimental results. However, the numerous formatting and technical errors throughout the paper significantly undermine its credibility and professionalism. The consistent use of incorrect mathematical notation ("O(γ2)" instead of "O(γ^2)") is particularly concerning, as it suggests a lack of attention to detail that should be expected in a high-quality NIPS submission.

While the theoretical contribution is novel and the experiments support the claims, the paper's presentation is marred by numerous avoidable errors. A perfect paper would have been accepted, but the accumulation of minor but pervasive formatting and notational errors makes it difficult to trust the technical content.

I recommend rejection due to the excessive number of formatting and notational inconsistencies that detract from the paper's scientific contribution. These are not trivial errors but rather reflect a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Theoretical contribution to SG-MCMC literature is novel and significant
- Comprehensive convergence analysis with well-supported theorems
- Well-designed experiments showing clear improvements over baselines
- Practical implementation details for parallelization

**Cons:**
- Multiple instances of inconsistent mathematical notation throughout the paper
- Critical formatting errors in key equations and theorems
- Grammatical errors and confusing phrasing
- Reference list contains formatting inconsistencies
- Failure to properly distinguish between O(γ) and O(γ^2) in multiple places

The paper's scientific contribution is strong, but the numerous technical errors in presentation make it difficult to assess the work with confidence. For a paper at this level of technical sophistication, the quality of presentation should be equally high.
