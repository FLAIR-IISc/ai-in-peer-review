## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), aiming to reduce bias in estimation by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is to run two Langevin dynamics chains with different step sizes and combine their results to achieve a higher-order accuracy than standard SGLD with a first-order integrator. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the bias and variance of SGRRLD, demonstrating a faster convergence rate (O(K^-4/5) for MSE) compared to SGLD (O(K^-2/3)). The paper also highlights the algorithm’s parallelizability and generic applicability to other SG-MCMC methods.  Experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (matrix factorization) support the theoretical findings, showcasing improved accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper builds directly on the burgeoning field of SG-MCMC, acknowledging seminal work by Welling & Teh (SGLD) and subsequent extensions. It appropriately positions itself within the broader context of numerical acceleration techniques for SDEs, particularly highlighting the relevance of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation as pioneered by Talay & Tubaro. The authors correctly identify the bias-variance tradeoff inherent in SG-MCMC and motivate their approach as a means of striking a better balance.  The connection to higher-order integrators, as discussed in Chen et al. (2015), is well drawn, and the paper’s value lies in achieving comparable accuracy with a simpler, first-order base integrator.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel contribution. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to the stochastic setting and its specific benefits within the SG-MCMC framework are new and interesting.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis. The derivation of the convergence rates and the bounds on bias and variance are clearly presented (though details are delegated to the supplement, as appropriate). The central limit theorem established for the estimator is valuable.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The authors explicitly address the parallelizability of SGRRLD, a critical feature for large-scale applications.  The discussion of correlated Brownian motions to control variance is insightful and strengthens the method.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results, on both synthetic data and a real-world matrix factorization problem, provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The comparison with SGLD and SGHMC is well-executed and demonstrates clear performance improvements.
* **Generality:** The claim that SGRRLD can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms (beyond SGLD) is an important observation, broadening the potential impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Assumptions:** The paper relies on several technical assumptions (regarding smoothness of U, ergodicity, and weak error conditions). While standard in this field, the precise nature and implications of these assumptions could be discussed more thoroughly in the main text.  The reader is often referred to the supplement for details.
* **Practical Tuning:** While the paper establishes theoretical convergence rates, it doesn’t offer extensive guidance on *how* to optimally tune the step sizes (γ) in practice.  The experimental section hints at this, but a more systematic exploration of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial. Determining optimal α for step size decay based on problem specifics may require additional investigation.
* **Computational Cost:** The authors note the parallelizability, but don’t provide a detailed analysis of the *overall* computational cost. Running two chains, even in parallel, introduces overhead. A more comprehensive comparison of wall-clock time and accuracy gains relative to SGLD would be insightful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the matrix factorization experiment is substantial, the performance of SGRRLD on other complex, high-dimensional models beyond those already explored would be valuable.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*  Significant theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis.
*  Novel approach with clear advantages over existing methods.
*  Strong empirical evidence supporting the claims.
*  Addresses a relevant and important problem in Bayesian machine learning.

**Against:**

*  Reliance on technical assumptions that might limit applicability.
*  Limited discussion of practical tuning and computational overhead.
*  Relatively narrow scope of experimental evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is sound, the proposed algorithm is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding practical considerations and the scope of experiments, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Rating:** Strong Accept (8/10)

**Recommendations:**

*   Briefly discuss the implications of the technical assumptions in the main text.
*   Provide more guidance on parameter tuning (step size selection).
*   Include a discussion of the computational overhead associated with running two chains.
*   Consider adding experiments on a broader range of models.
