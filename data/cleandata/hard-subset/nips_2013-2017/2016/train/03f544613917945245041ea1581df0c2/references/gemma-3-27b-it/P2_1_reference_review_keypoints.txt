- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two Langevin chains with different step sizes to reduce bias.  
- Theoretical claim: improved MSE convergence rate O(K^-4/5) vs SGLD’s O(K^-2/3), with correlated Brownian motions used to control variance.  
- Empirical validation: experiments on synthetic models, SGHMC extension, and a large-scale matrix factorization task show improved accuracy over SGLD/SGHMC.  
- Strength — Strong theoretical foundation: rigorous analysis and clear connection of RR extrapolation to SDEs.  
- Strength — Improved convergence rate: significant theoretical MSE improvement demonstrated.  
- Strength — Practical parallelism: two-chain structure naturally admits parallel implementation.  
- Strength — Generality: RR extrapolation potentially applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.  
- Strength — Comprehensive experiments: well-designed synthetic and real-world tests, including SGHMC comparison.  
- Strength — Clear writing: paper is generally well-organized and accessible.  
- Weakness — Increased complexity: implementation requires managing two chains and correlated Brownian motions, raising adoption barriers.  
- Weakness — Parameter sensitivity: performance depends on step-size choice, unclear robustness to suboptimal tuning.  
- Weakness — Limited experimental scope: needs more diverse and challenging applications to validate robustness and scalability.  
- Weakness — Superficial treatment of gradient correlation: insufficient investigation of how to effectively correlate gradient estimators or effects of using different mini-batches.  
- Quality: technically sound with rigorous analysis and validated experimental claims.  
- Clarity: mostly clear but mathematical notation and Brownian correlation treatment need clearer explanation; constants’ dependence on problem parameters not fully detailed.  
- Originality: novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC and well-differentiated from prior work.  
- Significance: improved rates and parallelism make it a potentially impactful advancement for large-scale Bayesian inference.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound algorithm that reduces bias with convincing experimental support and parallelization benefits.  
- Against acceptance: practical concerns about implementation complexity, parameter sensitivity, and limited experimental diversity.  
- Overall: strong contribution with rigorous theory and compelling experiments; minor revisions suggested on notation and gradient correlation discussion.  
- Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions).
