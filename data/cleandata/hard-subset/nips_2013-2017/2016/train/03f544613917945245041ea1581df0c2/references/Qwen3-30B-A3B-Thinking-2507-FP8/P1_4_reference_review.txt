

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The authors propose running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel (one with step size γ and the other with step size γ/2) and combining their outputs through RR extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The theoretical analysis shows that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods without their complexity. The approach is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms, and experiments on synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical claims.

The paper builds upon the growing literature of SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGLD [1], SGHMC [6]) and RR extrapolation for SDEs [21, 23]. It extends previous work by [10] which showed that higher-order integrators can achieve better convergence rates, but the authors demonstrate that RR extrapolation can achieve similar rates with simpler first-order integrators.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis with clear theorems showing improved bias and MSE bounds. The derivation of the O(K⁻⁴/⁵) convergence rate is a significant theoretical contribution, particularly since it achieves this rate without requiring higher-order integrators.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a detailed algorithm with proper handling of correlated Brownian motions to control variance, which addresses a key challenge in applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks effectively validate the theoretical claims. The results consistently show SGRRLD outperforming SGLD and SGHMC in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.

4. **Parallelization Advantage**: The paper correctly notes that the two chains required for RR extrapolation can be run independently, making SGRRLD well-suited for distributed architectures—a key advantage for large-scale applications.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-structured with clear definitions, logical progression from theory to experiments, and sufficient technical detail in the supplementary material.

## Weaknesses

1. **Novelty Clarification**: While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, the paper could more clearly distinguish this from previous work. Specifically, it should better explain why the RR approach achieves similar rates to higher-order integrators without their complexity, which is a key insight.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that "the mini-batch size has to be the same for the two chains" but doesn't provide a thorough justification for this constraint. This could be a limitation for some applications where varying mini-batch sizes might be beneficial.

3. **Computational Cost**: While the authors note that SGRRLD is parallelizable, they don't quantitatively compare the computational cost per iteration between SGRRLD and SGLD. Since SGRRLD requires two chains, it might require approximately twice the computational resources per iteration, though the parallelism could offset this. A more detailed analysis of the computational tradeoffs would strengthen the paper.

4. **Convergence Rate Explanation**: The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" but doesn't explicitly compare the constant factors in the convergence rates, which could be important in practice.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The convergence analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results consistently support the theoretical findings. The authors are careful to address the variance issue through correlated Brownian motions, which is an important technical detail. The paper is a complete contribution that addresses both theoretical and practical aspects of the problem.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation. The presentation of the algorithm and theoretical results is logical. The supplementary material appears to contain sufficient detail for reproducibility. The only minor improvement would be to provide a more intuitive explanation of why RR extrapolation works well with SG-MCMC, particularly the connection between the correlation of Brownian motions and variance control.

## Originality Assessment

The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel. While RR extrapolation has been used for SDEs in [21, 23], the authors successfully extend it to the stochastic gradient setting. The paper clearly differentiates their approach from previous work, particularly noting that their method achieves the same convergence rate as higher-order integrator methods without their complexity.

## Significance Assessment

The improvement in convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is significant for large-scale Bayesian inference. The method's parallelizability makes it particularly valuable for modern distributed computing architectures. The experiments demonstrate clear practical advantages on both synthetic and real-world datasets, suggesting the method could be widely adopted in practice.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference with significant practical implications. The theoretical results are well-supported, the experiments are comprehensive, and the method is clearly presented. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.

I recommend **acceptance** for NIPS. The paper advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC methods and provides a theoretically sound and practically useful algorithm that could have broad impact in Bayesian machine learning.

## Final Note

The paper is well-positioned to make a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors have successfully bridged a gap between theoretical analysis and practical implementation in SG-MCMC, which is valuable for both researchers and practitioners. The proposed method is both theoretically elegant and practically applicable, making it a strong candidate for publication at NIPS.
