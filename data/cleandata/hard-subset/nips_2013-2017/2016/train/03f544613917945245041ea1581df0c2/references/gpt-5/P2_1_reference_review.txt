Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to form an extrapolated estimator that cancels the O(γ) weak bias of Euler–Maruyama, while using correlated Gaussian noise across chains to control variance. Under technical conditions, the authors prove: (i) a CLT with variance minimized when the Brownian increments are perfectly correlated as Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding an optimal K−4/5 MSE rate (vs K−2/3 for first-order SG-MCMC). Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization show consistent improvements over SGLD; a Hamiltonian variant (SGRRHMC) performs comparably to a higher-order SGHMC integrator at larger steps.

Relation to prior work
RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures is classical (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès et al.). High-order integrators for SG-MCMC (e.g., Chen et al. 2015) improve bias/MSE but change the integrator. This paper’s novelty is to bring RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting, showing that with only Euler steps one can match the theoretical accuracy of some higher-order approaches, with tight non-asymptotic analysis. The work also connects to multilevel ideas and duplicated diffusions with coupled noise.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with strong theory: a minimal change (two Euler chains + extrapolation) gives a one-order improvement in weak bias and improved finite-time MSE rate, with clean optimization of step schedules (α = 1/5).
- Careful asymptotic and non-asymptotic analysis: CLT under decreasing steps; explicit finite-time bias/MSE bounds; clear optimization of γ for fixed K and for polynomially decaying schedules. The argument that correlated Gaussian increments minimize asymptotic variance is well motivated and aligns with SDE RR literature.
- Generality: the approach extends to other SG-MCMC schemes (demonstrated for SGHMC) and is amenable to parallelism since the two chains can run independently.
- Empirical support: synthetic experiments verify scaling of bias/MSE with γ and K and confirm the predicted exponents; matrix factorization on large MovieLens datasets shows improved RMSE at matched wall-clock time; SGHMC extension compares favorably with a higher-order integrator.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions and practicality: The CLT relies on strong smoothness/ergodicity (e.g., Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9). It would help to state these assumptions in the main text and discuss how they map to typical Bayesian posteriors (e.g., non-log-concave, mildly heavy tails).
- Gradient noise modeling: The theory assumes unbiased stochastic gradients with bounded moments and argues Gaussian noise dominates. Please clarify how sensitive the results are to state-dependent gradient noise and whether bounds degrade with large gradient variance or poorly conditioned models.
- Coupling of stochastic gradients: For unbiasedness, the two chains must use gradient estimators with the same law L; for variance reduction, sharing mini-batches across chains could be beneficial. The current text does not specify whether mini-batches are shared; an ablation comparing independent vs shared mini-batches would be useful.
- Empirical fairness and baselines: SGRRLD leverages parallelism by running two chains; SGLD gradients could also be parallelized across cores/GPUs. Please report results under a fixed gradient-evaluation budget in addition to wall-clock. For SGLD, a comparison to a higher-order integrator (as done for SGHMC) would strengthen the empirical case. Reporting the effect of correlated vs independent Gaussian noise empirically would directly validate Theorem 1’s guidance.
- Scope of experiments: Beyond linear-Gaussian and matrix factorization, a demonstration on a non-linear, higher-dimensional posterior (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression or a small Bayesian neural network) would increase confidence in robustness.
- Implementation details and reproducibility: Hyperparameter selection (γ, burn-in, batch sizes) appears tuned via preliminary experimentation; more detail and releasing code would aid reproducibility.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: High. The theoretical claims are well motivated and mostly well supported. Some technical assumptions are heavy; more discussion in the main text would improve transparency. Experiments align with theory, though broader benchmarks and fairness checks would help.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, with helpful intuition. However, key assumptions and the precise statement of the CLT/bounds are deferred to the supplement; summarizing them more concretely in the main text would improve readability. Including pseudocode in the paper (not only the supplement) would help practitioners.
- Originality: Strong. To my knowledge, RR extrapolation has not been systematically applied to SG-MCMC for posterior sampling, nor has the coupling strategy for variance control been analyzed in this context.
- Significance: Solid. The method is simple, general, and practically attractive for parallel settings. The improved finite-time rate and bias are of broad interest to the SG-MCMC community and may spur follow-on work (e.g., multilevel variants).

Pros and cons
Pros:
- Simple, general idea with rigorous analysis and practical benefits
- Improves finite-time bias/MSE rates without higher-order integrators
- Parallel-friendly; extends to SGHMC
- Empirical results match theory on synthetic and large real data

Cons:
- Strong technical assumptions; limited discussion of their practical reach
- Limited diversity of applications; fairness of timing comparisons could be strengthened
- Missing ablations on coupling choices (Gaussian and mini-batch)
- Reproducibility details could be expanded

Recommendation
Score: Accept (weak to clear accept). The paper presents a novel and well-founded idea with demonstrable gains and minimal implementation burden. Addressing the empirical fairness, adding ablations on coupling, and clarifying assumptions in the main text would further strengthen it, but the contribution is, in my view, above the bar.
