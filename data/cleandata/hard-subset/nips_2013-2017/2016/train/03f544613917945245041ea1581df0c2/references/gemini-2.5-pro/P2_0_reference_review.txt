**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core problem it addresses is the discretization bias inherent in SG-MCMC methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), which arises from the use of numerical integrators (e.g., Euler-Maruyama) to approximate a continuous-time Langevin diffusion. The authors' central idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the SGLD estimator. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2). A new estimator is then constructed by a linear combination of the outputs of these two chains, designed to cancel the leading-order O(γ) bias term. To manage the variance of this combined estimator, the authors propose using perfectly correlated Brownian motions for the two chains. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD reduces the asymptotic bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) and improves the Mean Squared Error (MSE) convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, showing significant improvements over standard SGLD.

**Review**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a clever and effective method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms. The application of a classical numerical technique to a modern machine learning problem is elegant, and the work is well-supported by both strong theory and compelling experiments.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.

*   **Theory:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength. The authors provide both a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) describing the asymptotic behavior and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derivation of an improved MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a significant theoretical result, placing the method on par with algorithms using more complex higher-order integrators, but using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The analysis of variance control via correlated noise is crucial and well-justified, connecting to prior work in the SDE literature. The provided proof sketches are clear and follow established methodologies.

*   **Experiments:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The use of a linear Gaussian model is an excellent choice, as the analytical posterior allows for the precise measurement of bias and MSE, directly verifying the theoretical claims. The experiments systematically explore the effect of step size (γ), number of iterations (K), and the step-size decay rate (α), with results that align perfectly with the theory. The application of the RR-extrapolation idea to SGHMC demonstrates the generality of the approach. Crucially, the large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show that the theoretical improvements translate into tangible performance gains on a real, challenging problem, with SGRRLD achieving lower RMSE faster than SGLD in terms of wall-clock time.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated in the introduction. The background on SGLD and RR extrapolation is presented concisely, providing the necessary context. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described formally and intuitively. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and their implications (e.g., optimal convergence rates) are discussed. The experimental setup and results are easy to understand. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to grasp the method and its implications.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While SGLD and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation are both known techniques, their combination in the context of *stochastic gradient* MCMC is novel and not straightforward. Previous work applied RR to SDEs with exact gradients, but extending it to the SG-MCMC setting, where noise comes from both discretization and mini-batching, is a new contribution. The analysis of how to control the variance from the combined chains using correlated noise is a key insight that makes the method practical. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art, such as methods using higher-order integrators, by offering an alternative, parallelizable path to achieving similar accuracy improvements. Related work is adequately cited.

**Significance:** The paper's results are significant and likely to be influential.

*   It addresses a fundamental limitation (discretization bias) of a widely used class of algorithms (SG-MCMC).
*   The proposed method offers a demonstrable improvement in statistical efficiency (faster convergence to a more accurate posterior approximation).
*   The approach is generic and, as shown with SGHMC, can likely be applied to a wide range of other SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   A key practical advantage is its amenability to parallelization. Since the two chains can be run on separate cores, the wall-clock time overhead is minimal compared to the computational overhead, making it very attractive for modern hardware. This is a strong practical argument over inherently sequential methods for bias reduction.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Trade-off:** The primary weakness is the increased computational cost. To get one SGRRLD sample, one must compute one step of the γ-chain and two steps of the γ/2-chain, resulting in roughly 3x the gradient computations of a single SGLD step. The authors cleverly mitigate this in wall-clock time by running the chains in parallel, but the total computational budget is still higher. The paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this computation vs. statistical efficiency trade-off. For a fixed *computational budget* (e.g., a fixed number of gradient evaluations), is SGRRLD always superior to running a single SGLD chain for more iterations with a smaller step size? The experiments strongly suggest the answer is yes, but explicitly framing the results in this way would make the practical benefits even clearer.

2.  **Memory Overhead:** The method requires storing two separate chains, doubling the memory requirement for the parameters. While this is unlikely to be an issue for most models, it could be a consideration for extremely large models where memory is a bottleneck. A brief mention of this would be appropriate.

3.  **Clarity on SGHMC-s comparison:** In Figure 4, SGRRHMC is compared to SGHMC-s (a higher-order integrator). The plot shows SGRRHMC with large γ achieves similar MSE to SGHMC-s with small γ. To make the comparison fairer and more impactful, it would be helpful to comment on the relative computational cost *per iteration* of SGHMC-s versus the combined cost of the two chains in SGRRHMC. It is likely that the SGRRHMC iterations are cheaper, which would further strengthen the paper's claims.

**Final Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method is elegant, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are strong and convincing. It presents a new, practical, and parallelizable tool for reducing bias in SG-MCMC methods. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
