- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains with different step sizes and combining them to reduce bias.  
- Key theoretical result: SGRRLD attains bias O(γ²), MSE O(γ⁴), and convergence rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵), improving over SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).  
- Demonstrates empirical gains on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks, showing improved accuracy and practical benefits.  
- Connects prior work on SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation for SDEs, making a novel cross-field contribution.  
- Technical analysis is rigorous with detailed theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) and proofs provided in the supplement.  
- Experiments are thorough and well-designed, including parameter sensitivity studies and baseline comparisons.  
- Minor weakness: lacks detailed comparisons to other bias-reduction techniques or specific higher-order integrators beyond limited baselines.  
- Clarity is high: well-organized, consistent notation, and clear explanation of RR extrapolation applied to SG-MCMC.  
- Suggestion: summarize technical assumptions for the theorems in the main text for reader accessibility.  
- Originality: first to apply RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and notable insight that first-order integrators can match higher-order accuracy via extrapolation.  
- Generality: approach extends beyond SGLD (e.g., SGRRHMC), increasing potential impact.  
- Significance: addresses scalable Bayesian inference, offering better convergence rates and suitability for parallel/distributed architectures.  
- Strength: combines novel idea, strong theory, practical demonstrations, parallelizability, and method generality.  
- Weakness: limited main-text summary of technical conditions and missing discussion of communication overhead in distributed settings.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant contribution with rigorous support and convincing experiments.  
- Recommendation: accept (NeurIPS) — strong theoretical and practical advance in SG-MCMC.
