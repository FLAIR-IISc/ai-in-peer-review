Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. The algorithm runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to a matched time horizon, and constructs an extrapolated estimator of expectations. The authors prove (under smoothness/ergodicity assumptions) a CLT for the extrapolated estimator, show that the asymptotic bias and variance scale as O(γ2) and O(γ4), and derive non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. Optimizing step-size schedules yields an MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) for SGRRLD versus O(K^{-2/3}) for SGLD-class methods with first-order integrators. They also analyze the optimal correlation structure of the Gaussian noise and argue that the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise asymptotically. Empirically, they validate on a linear-Gaussian model, extend to SGHMC (SGRRHMC), and demonstrate gains on large-scale probabilistic matrix factorization.

This work builds on: (i) SGLD and SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh 2011; Ma et al. 2015; Chen et al. 2015); (ii) RR extrapolation and weak error expansions for SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro 1990; Lemaire et al. 2015; Pagès 2007); and (iii) recent SG-MCMC convergence analyses (Teh et al. 2016; Vollmer et al. 2015). To my knowledge, applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and establishing corresponding CLTs/non-asymptotic bounds is novel and timely.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, general technique that can wrap around first-order SG-MCMC schemes and leverage parallel hardware.
- Theoretical contributions: CLT with explicit asymptotics; non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds; identification of optimal step-size schedules; analysis of optimal Brownian coupling. The improved rates (bias O(γ2), MSE O(K^{-4/5})) are compelling for first-order integrators.
- Practical relevance: requires only running two chains and correlating noise; no need for sophisticated high-order splitting schemes.
- Empirical evidence shows consistent improvements on synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization; extension to SGHMC aligns with theory.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and scope: The CLT and rates rely on strong smoothness (e.g., solution to the Poisson equation with high regularity) and “appropriate” conditions on the gradient estimator L. It would help to make these assumptions explicit in the main text and discuss their realism for common models (e.g., logistic regression, non-convex posteriors).
- Role of stochastic gradient noise: The claim that Gaussian noise dominates gradient noise is important. More intuition/conditions are needed (e.g., bounded variance uniformly in θ, moment bounds) and what happens when gradient noise is heavy-tailed or θ-dependent.
- Variance control via coupling: While the analysis suggests Σ = I (perfect coupling) is optimal, practical guidance on correlating stochastic gradients (e.g., sharing mini-batches) is missing. Using the same L (same batch size) prevents additional bias, but sharing the same mini-batch across the two chains could further reduce variance—this is not explored empirically.
- Fairness of empirical comparisons: In the matrix factorization experiments, SGRRLD uses two cores in parallel while SGLD is run sequentially. Although SGLD’s updates are inherently sequential, one can trivially parallelize across multiple independent chains and average estimates, or use batched likelihood/gradient evaluations. A stronger baseline would: (a) run two independent SGLD chains on two cores and average; (b) compare at fixed single-core wall-clock; and (c) match the total number of gradient evaluations.
- Baseline coverage: For SGHMC, the comparison to a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s) is suggestive but limited; broader comparisons to the “complete recipe” SG-MCMC family [Ma et al. 2015] and high-order schemes [Chen et al. 2015] under matched compute would strengthen claims.
- Practical guidance: Tuning rules (e.g., α = 1/5 for MSE) are theoretically motivated, but some concrete recipes (burn-in, γ constants, effect of K, batch size selection, when SGRRLD helps vs hurts) would aid practitioners.
- Clarity and completeness: Key algorithmic details (pseudo-code) and assumptions are deferred to the supplement. Bringing minimal pseudo-code and a concise assumption summary into the main text would improve readability.

Quality
Technically solid overall, with non-trivial extensions of RR analysis to the SG-MCMC setting. Some proofs are understandably deferred, but the main conditions and their implications should be more explicit. Experimental methodology could be more rigorous in terms of fairness and baselines.

Clarity
Generally clear, with good motivation and connection to prior work. A few notational/typographical issues and some results rely on the supplement. Consider adding pseudo-code, a brief assumption box, and a practical “how to use” paragraph.

Originality
High. Applying RR extrapolation with variance-optimal Brownian coupling to SG-MCMC and deriving CLT and finite-time bounds appears novel.

Significance
Moderate to high. If broadly applicable, SGRRLD provides a simple, parallelizable tool to improve accuracy of SG-MCMC without resorting to complex integrators. The improved rates are meaningful, and the method could influence future multilevel/coupled SG-MCMC research.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please state the precise assumptions on U and L in the main text (e.g., Lipschitz gradients, dissipativity, bounded moments of gradient estimators) and discuss their applicability to non-convex and non-Gaussian models.
- Have you tried sharing the same mini-batch across the two chains (in addition to Brownian coupling)? How does this affect variance and bias empirically?
- Provide a comparison where both SGRRLD and SGLD are constrained to a single core (or matched total gradient evaluations), and a baseline with two independent SGLD chains averaged on two cores.
- For SGHMC, can you compare under matched compute against high-order integrators across a range of step sizes? Any results on logistic regression or deep models?
- Any guidance on adaptively choosing α and γ in practice? Does the optimal α = 1/5 for MSE hold robustly across models?
- Discuss limitations: when Kγ is small (small γ or small K), the 1/(Kγ) term dominates; can you provide heuristics to avoid this regime?

Pros
- Simple, general idea; strong theoretical guarantees and improved rates.
- Parallelization-friendly; clear variance reduction via optimal coupling.
- Consistent empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale tasks.

Cons
- Strong assumptions; limited empirical breadth and baseline fairness.
- Practical details (mini-batch coupling, tuning, single-core budget) underexplored.
- Reliance on supplemental material for key algorithmic and assumption details.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a novel and theoretically well-founded method with promising empirical results. Addressing the fairness of comparisons, clarifying assumptions, and adding a couple of stronger baselines would further solidify the contribution.
