

# Peer Review for "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel MCMC algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The key insight is that by running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining them via RR extrapolation, the method can achieve higher-order convergence rates without requiring higher-order numerical integrators.

The paper builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and extends the recent theoretical work by Chen et al. (2015) on SG-MCMC convergence rates. The authors show that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias of O(γ²) and variance of O(γ⁴), leading to a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). This is significant because it achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator-based methods using only first-order integrators. The paper also demonstrates that the algorithm is naturally parallelizable, making it well-suited for modern distributed computing architectures.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Contribution**: The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in the field. This is a clean, theoretically grounded approach to improving convergence rates without requiring more complex integrators.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with proofs for central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The results are well-supported by careful mathematical development, with appropriate citations to related work in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and MCMC theory.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The authors clearly explain how to implement the method using correlated Brownian increments to control variance, and provide a pseudo-code in the supplementary material. The theoretical results are directly tied to practical implementation considerations.

4. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The paper includes thorough experimental validation on both synthetic data (Gaussian model) and a large-scale matrix factorization task (MovieLens datasets). The results consistently support the theoretical claims, with clear evidence of improved accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

5. **Parallel Architecture Advantage**: The paper correctly emphasizes that SGRRLD's parallel nature (two chains running independently) makes it well-suited for modern distributed computing environments, which is increasingly important for large-scale Bayesian inference.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Assumptions Not Fully Explained**: The theoretical results depend on several technical assumptions about the potential energy function U, the gradient estimator distribution L, and the smoothness of test functions. These assumptions are mentioned in the supplementary material but are not clearly stated or justified in the main paper, making it difficult for readers to assess the applicability of the results.

2. **Limited Discussion of Practical Trade-offs**: While the paper demonstrates improved convergence rates, it doesn't thoroughly discuss the practical trade-offs. For instance, the computational cost per iteration is higher (two chains instead of one), and the paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of the actual wall-clock time improvements in different computational settings.

3. **Missing Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper doesn't explore how sensitive the method is to the choice of step size ratio (γ and γ/2). The results assume a specific ratio (γ/2), but it's unclear whether this is optimal or how performance varies with different ratios.

4. **Limited Generalization Discussion**: The claim that "our technique is quite generic and can be virtually applied to all the current SG-MCMC algorithms" is made but not thoroughly demonstrated. The paper only briefly applies the method to SGHMC (in Figure 4), without providing a systematic analysis of its applicability to other SG-MCMC variants.

5. **Missing Comparison with Other Acceleration Methods**: The paper doesn't compare SGRRLD with other bias-reduction techniques for SG-MCMC, such as the methods proposed in Chen et al. (2015) or the stochastic gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo with higher-order integrators (SGHMC-s).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by experimental validation. The proofs are well-structured, and the theoretical claims are carefully developed. The authors are honest about the technical assumptions required for their results, though these could be made more explicit in the main paper. The experimental methodology is appropriate, with sufficient replication (100 runs for some experiments) and clear metrics (bias, MSE, RMSE). The paper is a complete contribution rather than a position paper.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The main contributions are clearly articulated early in the paper. The notation is consistent throughout, and the figures effectively illustrate the key results. Some technical details are deferred to the supplementary material, which is appropriate for a conference paper. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the main results, though the supplementary material could be more detailed about the implementation specifics.

## Originality

The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), its application to SG-MCMC is new. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by showing that SGRRLD achieves higher-order convergence rates with first-order integrators, which is a significant conceptual advance over the recent work by Chen et al. (2015). The authors adequately reference related work and clearly explain how their contribution differs.

## Significance

The results are highly significant for the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The improved convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) represents a meaningful theoretical advance. The practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments (improved accuracy in the same wall-clock time) make this work directly applicable to real-world large-scale Bayesian inference problems. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of current SG-MCMC methods (bias vs. variance trade-off) in a way that advances the state of the art. The natural parallelism of the method also makes it particularly relevant to modern distributed computing environments.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Explicitly state key assumptions**: Clearly list the technical assumptions required for Theorems 1 and 2 in the main paper (e.g., smoothness conditions on U, moment conditions on gradient estimators).

2. **Expand experimental comparisons**: Include a comparison with SGHMC-s (the higher-order integrator method) in the MovieLens experiments to directly demonstrate the equivalence in accuracy claimed in the paper.

3. **Add sensitivity analysis**: Provide a section or figure showing how performance varies with different step size ratios (not just γ/2), to help practitioners understand the practical implementation details.

4. **Clarify mini-batch size requirements**: Explain more thoroughly why the mini-batch size must be the same for both chains (the paper states this but doesn't justify it).

5. **Quantify parallel speedup**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the actual wall-clock time improvements (not just "nearly exactly the same") to help readers understand the practical benefits.

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The improved convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) is a meaningful advance that addresses a fundamental limitation of current SG-MCMC methods.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. The theoretical contribution is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are significant for both theory and practice. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed with minor revisions, but the paper is already strong as is.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, contributing to the growing body of work on scalable Bayesian inference. The method has clear potential for adoption by practitioners working on large-scale Bayesian models, and the theoretical insights advance the field in a meaningful way.
