Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC (primarily SGLD) to reduce discretization bias without increasing variance unduly. The method runs two SGLD chains with stepsizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated estimator; correlated Brownian increments across the two chains are used to control variance, following insights from RR for SDEs and multilevel Monte Carlo. The authors prove a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds: for constant step γ, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), implying an optimal MSE rate O(K−4/5) versus O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC. They also show optimal asymptotic schedule α = 1/5 for γk ∝ k−α. Experiments on a Gaussian model, SGHMC variants, and large-scale matrix factorization show consistent improvements over SGLD (and SGHMC), with the two chains run in parallel for fair wall-clock comparisons.

This builds directly on: (i) SGLD and SG-MCMC literature [Welling & Teh; Chen et al.; Teh et al.; Ma et al.], (ii) RR extrapolation for SDE weak error [Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire, Pagès, Panloup], and (iii) variance control via correlated Brownian increments in multilevel methods. To my knowledge, applying RR extrapolation systematically to SG-MCMC with unbiased stochastic gradients had not been explored at the time; the paper positions itself well with appropriate citations.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and simplicity: A clean, generic bias-reduction mechanism for SG-MCMC that leverages well-established RR ideas; applicable beyond SGLD (demonstrated on SGHMC).
- Theoretical support: A CLT and finite-time bias/MSE bounds with explicit rates, including guidance on optimal step schedules. The variance-minimizing coupling for Brownian increments is carefully justified.
- Practical relevance: Running two chains is embarrassingly parallel; the method achieves accuracy similar to higher-order integrators while using first-order updates.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic Gaussian model validates predicted scaling of bias/MSE w.r.t. γ and K; RR improves SGHMC; large-scale matrix factorization shows improved RMSE per wall-clock.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and generality: The theorems rely on strong smoothness and ergodicity/Poisson equation conditions (e.g., solution g ∈ C9), which exclude many practical posteriors (nonsmooth priors, heavy tails). While standard in weak error analyses, the paper should better discuss how fragile the rates are when assumptions are relaxed.
- Algorithmic clarity: Equation (5) for Chain 2 appears to use ∇Ũ(θ2k+1) in the first half-step, which would make it an implicit update; this is likely a typo and should be ∇Ũ(θ2k). Please clarify. Also, there is occasional ambiguity in notation for √(2γ) versus √2 γ.
- Gradient coupling: The theory emphasizes coupling of Gaussian noise; the role of coupling the stochastic gradients (e.g., reusing the same minibatch indices across the two half-steps and the coarse step) is not fully explored. Even if asymptotically the Brownian term dominates, finite-K variance could benefit from stronger gradient coupling; a brief empirical ablation would strengthen the paper.
- Baselines: Beyond SGLD/SGHMC, comparisons to other practical SG-MCMC improvements (e.g., preconditioned SGLD, SGFS, thermostat methods) would help establish broader significance. The paper compares to a higher-order SGHMC integrator but not to other common SGLD variants.
- Practical guidance: While the theory identifies optimal α and constant-step tradeoffs, the paper could offer more concrete guidance on tuning γ and K in practice, and discuss sensitivity. The parallelism assumption is reasonable, but scenarios without parallel resources imply 2× cost; a brief discussion of the speed-accuracy tradeoff in that setting would be useful.

Quality
- The technical claims are consistent with the literature on RR extrapolation and weak error expansions. The CLT and finite-time bounds are plausible under the stated conditions, and the variance-minimizing coupling is well motivated. However, many details are deferred to the supplement; checking conditions for typical ML posteriors (e.g., log-concavity/nonconvexity, Lipschitz constants, moment bounds for ∇Ũ) should be summarized in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation supports the theory: bias/MSE vs γ and K behave as predicted; wall-clock comparisons are fair through parallel execution. Still, broader benchmarks and sensitivity analyses would improve robustness of the claims.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with a clear motivation and results. Some notation typos/ambiguities should be corrected (noted above). Including pseudocode in the main text would improve readability. The estimator definition could be explained more intuitively (time-averaging over matched horizons, why weights are γk+1).

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation with Brownian coupling to SG-MCMC is a novel and natural idea that, to my knowledge, had not been formalized. The extension to SGHMC is straightforward but useful. The work differentiates itself clearly from prior higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC.

Significance
- Reducing discretization bias with simple, parallelizable modifications is impactful for practitioners using SG-MCMC at scale. The improved rates (K−4/5) and empirical gains are compelling. I expect the idea to be picked up in practice and extended (e.g., multilevel variants, recursive RR).

Pros
- Simple, general method with strong theoretical backing.
- Clear improvements in bias/MSE and wall-clock performance.
- Insightful use of Brownian coupling; guidance on optimal schedules.
- Applicability beyond SGLD (shown for SGHMC).

Cons
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; limited discussion of robustness.
- Minor but important notation/algorithm typos; missing pseudocode in main.
- Limited baselines and ablations (gradient coupling, sensitivity).
- Parallelism assumed; single-core tradeoffs not discussed.

Questions for authors
- Please confirm the explicit Euler updates in Chain 2 (Eq. 5) and fix the gradient evaluation point.
- Did you try sharing minibatches across the two half-steps and the coarse step? Does it improve finite-K variance?
- How sensitive are results to γ and to the coupling choice in practice? Any heuristic tuning guidance?
- Can you comment on extending to recursive RR or multilevel schemes under stochastic gradients?

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a neat, well-supported idea that advances SG-MCMC both theoretically and practically, with clear relevance to the NeurIPS community. My confidence is high, contingent on clarifying the update equations and notation.
