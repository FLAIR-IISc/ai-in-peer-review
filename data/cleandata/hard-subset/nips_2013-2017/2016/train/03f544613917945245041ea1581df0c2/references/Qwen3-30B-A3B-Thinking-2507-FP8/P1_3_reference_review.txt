

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to improve its convergence properties. The authors propose running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and then combining their outputs via RR extrapolation to reduce bias while controlling variance. The paper shows theoretically that SGRRLD achieves an asymptotic bias of O(γ²) and variance of O(γ⁴), compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²), respectively. Consequently, SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly better than SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The authors demonstrate the approach on synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task, showing consistent improvements over SGLD and other SG-MCMC methods.

The paper builds on recent advances in SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD, SGHMC) and extends the RR extrapolation technique, previously used in numerical analysis for SDEs, to the stochastic gradient MCMC context. The work relates to Chen et al. (NIPS 2015) which established bias and MSE bounds for SG-MCMC methods, and to Talay & Tubaro (1990) who first applied RR extrapolation to SDEs.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing how RR extrapolation can improve the convergence rate of SG-MCMC methods. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and properly supported.

2. **Novel Application**: Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea that hasn't been explored before, as the authors note. This is particularly significant because it shows how a first-order integrator can achieve the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The paper demonstrates that the two chains required by RR extrapolation can be generated independently, making SGRRLD well-suited for parallel and distributed architectures. The experimental results on large-scale matrix factorization show clear computational advantages.

4. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on both synthetic data (with analytical posterior) and real-world datasets, showing consistent improvements over SGLD and SGHMC. The results align well with the theoretical predictions.

5. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (with supplementary material for detailed proofs). The experimental setup is well-described and reproducible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: While the experiments are well-designed, they are limited to specific models (Gaussian and matrix factorization). It would be valuable to see results on more complex Bayesian models (e.g., neural networks) to better demonstrate the generality of the approach.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't fully address how to choose optimal step sizes in practice. While the theoretical analysis provides guidance (e.g., γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵), the paper lacks a clear practical protocol for selecting these parameters in real applications.

3. **Computational Cost**: While the paper notes that the two chains can run in parallel, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the actual computational cost (wall-clock time) versus SGLD, especially for very large datasets. The claim that "wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" needs more justification.

4. **Connection to Other Methods**: The paper mentions that the technique is "quite generic" and can be applied to "all current SG-MCMC algorithms," but doesn't provide a concrete example beyond SGHMC. A brief discussion of how the method would apply to other SG-MCMC variants (e.g., SGLD with preconditioning) would strengthen the argument.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The theorems are properly motivated, and the proofs (in supplementary material) appear rigorous. The experimental results support the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to note limitations (e.g., the requirement for correlated Brownian increments).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The main contributions are clearly stated, and the theoretical results are presented in a way that's accessible to experts in the field. Some of the more technical details (e.g., the correlation structure for Brownian increments) might benefit from a more intuitive explanation for non-specialists.

**Originality**: The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by showing how this technique can achieve the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important problem in scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method improves the accuracy of existing SG-MCMC methods without significantly increasing computational cost (since the two chains can run in parallel). This could have practical implications for large-scale Bayesian machine learning applications.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to improving the convergence properties of SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. The method is well-suited for parallel and distributed computation, which is increasingly important for large-scale applications.

While the paper has some limitations in experimental scope and practical implementation details, these are relatively minor given the strength of the theoretical contribution and the clear demonstration of improvements over existing methods. The work represents a significant advancement in the SG-MCMC field and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference with well-justified theoretical and empirical results.

## Pro and Con Arguments

**Pro (for acceptance)**:
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Rigorous theoretical analysis showing improved convergence rates
- Demonstrated improvements over SGLD in both synthetic and real-world experiments
- Parallel-friendly design that maintains computational efficiency
- Clear connection to existing SG-MCMC literature

**Con (against acceptance)**:
- Limited experimental scope (only Gaussian models and matrix factorization)
- Some practical implementation details (step size selection) could be more thoroughly addressed
- Limited discussion of how the method extends to more complex models

Overall, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
