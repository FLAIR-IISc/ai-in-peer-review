

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors propose running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes in parallel, then combining their results via RR extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. Theoretical analysis shows that SGRRLD achieves an asymptotic bias of O(γ²) and variance of O(γ⁴), leading to a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which improves upon SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. The paper demonstrates that this approach can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods using only a first-order integrator, while being well-suited for parallel computing architectures. The authors validate their claims through experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks.

This work builds upon prior SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGLD [1], SGHMC [6]) and extends the application of RR extrapolation, previously used for SDE discretizations [21, 23], to the stochastic gradient context. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference, particularly for large-scale applications.

## Evaluation

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and makes well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) is rigorous and builds upon established results in SDE discretizations and SG-MCMC convergence. The authors properly cite prior work [10, 21, 23] and clearly state how their contribution differs from previous research. The proof sketches provided are sufficient for understanding the main arguments, and the supplementary material is appropriately referenced for full proofs.

The experimental results effectively validate the theoretical claims. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model demonstrate the expected improvements in bias and MSE, while the real-world matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show practical improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency. The experimental setup is well-described, with appropriate baselines (SGLD, SGHMC) and clear metrics (RMSE, bias, MSE).

The authors are careful about evaluating both strengths and weaknesses, noting that for very small step sizes (γ → 0), the MSE may not improve due to the 1/(Kγ) term dominating the bound. They also acknowledge the technical conditions required for their method to work (e.g., same mini-batch size for both chains).

*Strengths:* Strong theoretical foundation, clear experimental validation, careful evaluation of limitations.

*Weaknesses:* The paper could have provided more discussion on how restrictive the technical conditions (on U and L) are in practice. While the method is claimed to be applicable to "all current SG-MCMC algorithms," the experiments only cover SGLD and SGHMC.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem, motivation, and contribution. The preliminary sections (2.1 and 2.2) provide sufficient background without being overly verbose. The proposed method (Section 3) is clearly described with precise mathematical formulations, and the theoretical results (Section 4) are presented logically.

The figures (Figures 1-5) are well-designed and effectively communicate the key results. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the supplementary material is appropriately referenced for more technical details.

*Strengths:* Excellent organization, clear writing, well-designed figures, sufficient detail for reproducibility.

*Weaknesses:* None significant.

### Originality

The paper's main contribution is the novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC methods. While RR extrapolation has been used for SDEs [21, 23], its application to SG-MCMC is new. The paper clearly articulates this novelty in the introduction and provides a compelling theoretical justification for why this approach should work.

The claim that a first-order integrator with RR extrapolation can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods (which is the main contribution of [10]) is particularly novel and significant. The paper also correctly notes that this approach can be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms, not just SGLD.

*Strengths:* Clear novelty in applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, significant theoretical insight about the equivalence between first-order with RR and higher-order integrators.

*Weaknesses:* The RR extrapolation technique itself is not new, but its application to SG-MCMC is.

### Significance

This work addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian machine learning: scaling MCMC to large datasets. The improved convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD) are substantial and directly translate to better accuracy or faster convergence in practice. The parallelizable nature of SGRRLD makes it particularly well-suited for modern distributed computing architectures, which is a practical advantage over sequential SG-MCMC methods.

The experiments on real-world matrix factorization tasks demonstrate that SGRRLD can achieve the same accuracy as SGLD in approximately half the time, which is a significant practical improvement. This could have substantial impact on practitioners working with large-scale Bayesian models.

*Strengths:* Addresses a significant practical problem in Bayesian inference, provides demonstrable improvements over state-of-the-art methods, well-suited for modern computing architectures.

*Weaknesses:* The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations in distributed settings (e.g., communication overhead between parallel chains), though this is a minor point.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful extension to SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results convincingly validate the claims. The work addresses a real-world problem with significant practical impact, and the proposed method has clear advantages over existing approaches.

The paper meets all four review criteria at a high level:
- **Quality**: 9/10 - Technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical support.
- **Clarity**: 9/10 - Well-written, well-organized, and sufficiently detailed.
- **Originality**: 8/10 - Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC (the technique itself is not new, but its application is).
- **Significance**: 9/10 - Addresses a significant problem with demonstrable improvements.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and MCMC methods. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical implications are significant. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to the NIPS community.

The main weakness is that the paper could have provided more discussion on the practical implementation details of running two chains in parallel (e.g., memory requirements, communication overhead), but this does not detract from the overall quality of the work. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong theoretical and empirical results, makes this a valuable contribution to the field.
