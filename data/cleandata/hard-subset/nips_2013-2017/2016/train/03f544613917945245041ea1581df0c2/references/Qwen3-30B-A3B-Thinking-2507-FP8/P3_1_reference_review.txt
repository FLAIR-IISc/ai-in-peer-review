

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods like SGLD. The authors propose running two parallel chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs using RR extrapolation to reduce bias while controlling variance through correlated Brownian increments. The theoretical analysis shows that SGRRLD achieves an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), and experimental results on synthetic and real datasets support these theoretical claims.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Welling and Teh [1] (SGLD), the theoretical analysis of SG-MCMC by Chen et al. [10], and the numerical analysis of RR extrapolation by Talay and Tubaro [21]. It extends the RR extrapolation technique from standard SDEs to the stochastic gradient setting, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both asymptotic (central limit theorem) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) results, which is crucial for understanding the convergence properties of the proposed method. The theoretical results are well-supported with detailed proofs in the supplementary material.

*Strengths:*
- The theoretical analysis is thorough, addressing both bias and variance components
- The connection between RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC is well-motivated
- The paper carefully considers the impact of correlated Brownian increments to control variance, which is a key insight
- The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical claims

*Weaknesses:*
- The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of running two chains in parallel. While the authors note that the two chains can be run independently, they don't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity compared to standard SG-MCMC methods. For large-scale problems, this could be a significant drawback.
- The assumption that both chains must use the same mini-batch size (Section 3) seems restrictive. The paper doesn't explore how the mini-batch size affects performance when the two chains have different step sizes.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly compare against other methods that also aim to reduce bias in SG-MCMC, such as higher-order integrators (e.g., the method in [10] mentioned in the paper) or methods that use multiple steps.
- The theoretical analysis assumes certain conditions on U, f, and L, but doesn't provide detailed discussion about how restrictive these conditions are in practice.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly states the motivation and contributions, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The key concepts are explained with sufficient detail for experts in the field.

*Strengths:*
- The paper has a clear structure with well-defined sections
- The main contributions are clearly stated
- The theoretical results are presented with sufficient mathematical rigor
- The experimental section provides a good balance between synthetic and real-world validation

*Weaknesses:*
- Some parts of the theoretical sections (particularly the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2) are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations or examples.
- The connection between the RR extrapolation and the specific implementation of SGRRLD could be clearer, especially regarding how the two chains interact at each iteration.
- The experimental section would benefit from more detailed descriptions of parameter choices, particularly for the real-world datasets (e.g., why the step sizes were chosen as γ = 2 × 10⁻⁶ for ML-1M and γ = 2 × 10⁻⁵ for larger datasets).
- The paper frequently references the supplementary material but doesn't provide it, making it harder to fully evaluate the theoretical claims.

## Originality

The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution to the field. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis and for SDEs with exact gradients, its application to SG-MCMC (where gradients are estimated) is new and represents a meaningful extension of existing techniques.

*Strengths:*
- The paper identifies a clear gap in the literature: the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
- The proposed method is a clever extension of existing techniques to a new setting.
- The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work, particularly from the higher-order integrators mentioned in [10].
- The extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) demonstrates the generality of the approach.

*Weaknesses:*
- The paper doesn't adequately discuss whether similar ideas have been explored in the context of stochastic optimization or other areas of numerical analysis. For example, multi-level Monte Carlo methods (referenced in the conclusion as [27]) share some conceptual similarities.
- The paper could have better positioned itself against related work, such as the multi-level Monte Carlo methods mentioned in the conclusion.
- The paper doesn't explore the potential limitations of the RR extrapolation approach for SG-MCMC, such as whether it can be extended to other types of SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD and SGHMC.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in SG-MCMC: reducing bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The theoretical improvements are meaningful, as achieving an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) instead of O(K⁻²/³) represents a substantial improvement for large-scale Bayesian inference.

*Strengths:*
- The theoretical improvements are significant and well-justified
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing improved performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- The method is generalizable to other SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD, which increases its potential impact
- The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on large-scale matrix factorization problems, which is relevant to real-world applications

*Weaknesses:*
- The computational cost of running two chains in parallel might offset the theoretical gains in practice, especially for very large-scale problems. The paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of this trade-off.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear roadmap for how this method could be integrated into existing machine learning pipelines or how it compares to other state-of-the-art methods for large-scale Bayesian inference.
- The significance of the theoretical improvements might be limited by practical considerations like the need for careful tuning of the step sizes and the potential increase in memory requirements for storing two chains.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a novel contribution to the field of SG-MCMC by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the computational cost of the proposed method, providing more detailed comparisons with related work, and exploring the practical limitations of the approach.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but the practical significance is somewhat limited by the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel. If the authors can provide a more thorough analysis of this trade-off and demonstrate that the theoretical gains are not offset by computational costs in practice, the paper would be significantly stronger.

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper has significant strengths in theoretical contribution and experimental validation, but needs to address the weaknesses mentioned above before it can be accepted. The authors should:

1. Provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of SGRRLD compared to SGLD and other SG-MCMC methods, including wall-clock time and memory requirements.
2. Explore the impact of different mini-batch sizes for the two chains and discuss whether the assumption of equal mini-batch sizes is restrictive.
3. Conduct more thorough comparisons with other bias-reduction methods in SG-MCMC (e.g., higher-order integrators from [10]).
4. Provide more detailed explanations of the experimental setup and parameter choices, especially for the large-scale matrix factorization experiments.

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and MCMC methods.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper mentions that "our technique is quite generic and can be virtually applied to all the current SG-MCMC algorithms besides SGLD" (Section 1). It would be helpful to provide a concrete example of how this technique could be applied to another SG-MCMC method beyond SGHMC.

2. In Section 3, the authors assume "the mini-batch size has to be the same" for the two chains. This is a significant restriction that should be discussed in more detail. Is there a way to relax this assumption while maintaining the theoretical guarantees?

3. The experimental results in Figure 5 show that SGRRLD achieves better accuracy than SGLD in less time, but the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. The theoretical analysis suggests the method should be more efficient, but the authors should explicitly connect the theoretical gains to the empirical results.

4. The paper references multi-level Monte Carlo methods in the conclusion but doesn't discuss them in the main text. It would be helpful to briefly compare the proposed method with multi-level Monte Carlo approaches, as they share some conceptual similarities.

5. In Section 4, the authors state "the constant C which appears in Theorem 2 depends on moments of the estimator of the gradient." It would be helpful to provide more detail about how this constant behaves with respect to the dimension d and the mini-batch size B, as these are important practical considerations.

## Final Recommendation

The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of SG-MCMC with a theoretically sound approach. With revisions addressing the computational cost analysis and practical limitations, it would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS. As it stands, it needs to be revised before acceptance.
