## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce bias compared to standard SGLD. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation – a numerical acceleration technique – by running two Langevin dynamics chains in parallel with different step sizes and then combining their outputs. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (MSE of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) and show adaptability to parallel architectures. They validate their approach on both synthetic (Gaussian model, SGHMC) and real-world (matrix factorization) datasets, observing performance improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the foundational work of Welling & Teh (SGLD) and the recent advances in analyzing SG-MCMC convergence rates (e.g., [10]). It’s situated within a growing body of research exploring variance reduction techniques for SG-MCMC, including higher-order integrators and preconditioning methods. The application of RR extrapolation to SDEs is credited to Talay & Tubaro, and the authors successfully bridge this numerical analysis technique with the burgeoning field of scalable Bayesian inference. They appropriately position their work as applicable *beyond* SGLD, extending to algorithms like SGHMC.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to the stochastic gradient setting is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem for the estimator and bounds on bias and MSE. The derived convergence rate of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) is a compelling theoretical advantage.
* **Practical Adaptability:** The inherent parallelism of SGRRLD is a key strength, making it well-suited for modern computing infrastructure. The authors acknowledge and address the need for correlated Brownian motions to control variance effectively.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD. The results consistently show improved performance over SGLD and, in some cases, SGHMC. The ablation studies (varying step sizes and comparing to SGHMC) are particularly insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The presentation of the algorithm and the theoretical analysis are clear and accessible. The inclusion of a pseudo-code is very helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Overhead:** While parallelism is a strength, the paper doesn’t fully delve into the *practical* overhead associated with running two chains concurrently.  While wall clock time may be similar, memory usage will be doubled.  A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity to Step Size:** The theoretical analysis and experiments highlight the sensitivity of performance to the step size. Choosing the optimal step size requires careful tuning and understanding of the problem. This could be a barrier to adoption for practitioners. More guidance on how to select appropriate step sizes in different scenarios would be helpful.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** While acknowledging the importance of correlated Brownian increments, the discussion of correlated stochastic gradients is somewhat limited.  The authors state that non-correlated gradients can introduce bias. Exploring different strategies for correlated gradient estimation (beyond simply using the same mini-batch) might be worthwhile.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to SGHMC with higher-order integrators is somewhat brief. A more comprehensive comparison, perhaps using established benchmark datasets and comparing to more sophisticated SGHMC variants, would strengthen the claims.
* **Condition on Mini-batch Size:** The requirement for equal mini-batch size across the two chains is a potential limitation, especially in scenarios where resource constraints or data heterogeneity might necessitate different batch sizes.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to improving the efficiency of SG-MCMC.
* The convergence rate analysis is strong and provides a clear theoretical advantage over existing methods.
*  The empirical results demonstrate consistent performance improvements on diverse datasets.
*  The inherent parallelism makes the algorithm well-suited for modern computing environments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The practical overhead associated with running two chains is not fully addressed.
*  The sensitivity to step size selection and the potential need for careful tuning could limit usability.
*  The comparison to competing methods is somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC while leveraging the benefits of parallel computation. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation are compelling.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the points regarding practical overhead, step size sensitivity, and potential for more comprehensive comparisons to other SGHMC variants. Adding a brief discussion of the potential benefits of exploring more sophisticated gradient correlation strategies would also be valuable.
