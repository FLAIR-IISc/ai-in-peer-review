This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, requiring two parallel chains with different step sizes, while carefully managing variance through correlated Brownian motions. The authors provide theoretical analysis, bounding the asymptotic bias and variance, and demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K^-4/5) vs. O(K^-2/3) for SGLD).  Experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization) supports the theoretical findings, showcasing improved accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in SG-MCMC, citing foundational papers like Welling & Teh (2011) and subsequent extensions (Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014; etc.). It correctly positions itself within this context, acknowledging the bias-variance trade-off inherent in these methods and proposing RR extrapolation as a means to address the bias. The connection to numerical analysis, specifically Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2003), is clearly established. The use of correlated Brownian motions, inspired by Pages (2007) and Lemaire et al. (2015), is also well motivated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel contribution. It offers a potentially significant improvement in bias reduction without substantially increasing computational cost due to the parallel nature of the required chains.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for SGRRLD. The convergence analysis, including bounds on bias, variance, and the MSE, is well-developed and presented with clear assumptions.  The derivation of the O(K^-4/5) convergence rate is compelling.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets convincingly demonstrate the advantages of SGRRLD over SGLD and SGHMC.  The experiments are well-designed and the results are clearly presented. The comparison against SGHMC with higher-order integrators is particularly insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise yet informative.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight that the technique can be applied to various SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, broadening its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:**  While the theoretical analysis is strong, it relies on several technical assumptions (regarding smoothness of U, properties of L, etc.). A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of these assumptions would be beneficial. How sensitive are the results to violations of these assumptions?
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper mentions the parallelizability of SGRRLD as an advantage. However, it does not provide detailed empirical evidence of the speedup achieved in a truly parallel environment. More in-depth benchmarking on different parallel architectures would strengthen the claims.
* **Step Size Tuning:** The optimal choice of step sizes (γ) appears to be crucial for performance. The paper provides some guidance based on the theoretical analysis, but a more systematic investigation of step size tuning strategies would be valuable.  How robust is the algorithm to suboptimal step size selection?
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** While SGLD and SGHMC are used for comparison, a brief discussion of other recent advances in SG-MCMC (e.g., methods focusing on variance reduction or adaptive step sizes) would help contextualize the contribution of SGRRLD.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are convincing, exploring more diverse real-world applications beyond matrix factorization would further demonstrate the algorithm’s versatility.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
* Strong experimental validation supporting the theoretical results.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potential for significant impact in large-scale Bayesian inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on several technical assumptions that may not always hold in practice.
* Limited exploration of practical considerations (parallel scaling, step size tuning).
* Comparison could be broadened to include more recent SG-MCMC methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is novel, theoretically well-motivated, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses related to the discussion of assumptions and practical considerations, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*  Elaborate on the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions.
*  Provide more detailed empirical results on parallel scaling.
*  Discuss step size tuning strategies and their impact on performance.
*  Include a brief comparison to other recent advances in SG-MCMC.
* Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse real-world applications.
