

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD to reduce bias while controlling variance. The authors build on foundational SG-MCMC work (Welling & Teh, 2011; SGLD) and extend the RR extrapolation framework from Talay & Tubaro (1990) to stochastic gradient settings. They theoretically show that SGRRLD achieves MSE convergence rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods (Chen et al., 2015) while using only first-order integration. The paper demonstrates the approach's generality by applying it to SGHMC as well.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis with both central limit theorems (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2). The proof strategy carefully extends existing RR extrapolation results to SG-MCMC settings, addressing the critical challenge of controlling variance through correlated Brownian motions (equation 8). The theoretical results are well-motivated and properly contextualized against prior work.

2. **Practical Significance**: The parallelizability of SGRRLD is a major practical advantage over sequential SG-MCMC methods. The authors demonstrate this effectively in the large-scale matrix factorization experiments where SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to SGLD in half the wall-clock time.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The synthetic experiments thoroughly validate the theoretical claims, showing the bias reduction (from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴) and MSE improvement (order of magnitude reduction) with varying dimensions and step sizes. The real-world matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets provide strong evidence of practical utility.

4. **Generality Claim**: The paper correctly notes that the RR extrapolation technique is applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms, with a compelling demonstration using SGHMC (as SGRRHMC).

## Weaknesses

1. **Novelty Clarification**: While the application of RR to SG-MCMC is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish between theoretical novelty (extension of RR to SG-MCMC) and practical novelty (parallel implementation). The theoretical framework relies heavily on existing RR results (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), and the paper would benefit from a clearer statement of what's truly new.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper lacks practical implementation details critical for reproducibility. How exactly are the two chains coordinated in distributed settings? What is the communication overhead? The claim that "wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" needs justification - running two chains in parallel should theoretically require double the computational resources, though the paper claims comparable wall-clock time. This requires clarification.

3. **Step Size Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't address the sensitivity to the choice of step size ratio (γ and γ/2). While they use γ/2, is this optimal? The experiments show good performance with this ratio, but a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.

4. **Comparison with Higher-Order Methods**: The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" but doesn't directly compare computational cost. Higher-order methods (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) may have higher per-iteration cost, so SGRRLD's advantage needs to be evaluated in terms of total computational resources.

5. **Correlation Strategy**: While equation (8) justifies the optimal Σ=Id, the paper doesn't provide empirical evidence that this correlation strategy is indeed optimal in practice. The experiments don't test alternative correlation schemes.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical contributions that advance the state of the art in SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly support the claims. The method's parallelizability is a significant practical advantage for large-scale applications.

The main weaknesses are related to insufficient clarification of the novelty and lack of implementation details. The paper could be strengthened by:
1. More precise statement of what's novel (theoretical framework vs. application)
2. Additional details on parallel implementation and communication overhead
3. Sensitivity analysis for step size ratio
4. Direct comparison of total computational cost with higher-order methods

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a significant contribution to scalable Bayesian inference. The improved convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs. O(K⁻²/³)) is demonstrably better, and the parallel implementation is a practical advantage over sequential methods. The work is well-positioned to influence future SG-MCMC research and practice.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. The paper makes a valuable contribution with solid theoretical foundation and strong experimental validation. The improvements in convergence rate and parallelizability are significant for the field. While the implementation details could be more thorough, the core contribution is substantial and well-supported. The paper advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference and provides a promising direction for future work.

The main issues are minor and could be addressed in the camera-ready version. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
