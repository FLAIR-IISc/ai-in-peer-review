

# Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel MCMC algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The key insight is that by running two SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) in parallel and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation, the authors demonstrate improved convergence rates. The paper shows that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The method is theoretically sound, parallelizable, and applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms. The authors validate their claims through experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

This work builds upon previous contributions in SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), but makes the novel contribution of applying RR to SG-MCMC, which has not been explored before.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds (Theorem 2) for SGRRLD, with careful attention to the conditions under which these results hold. The proofs are referenced to the supplementary material, which appears to contain the necessary technical details. The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate baselines (SGLD, SGHMC) and multiple datasets (synthetic Gaussian models and MovieLens datasets). The experiments support the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in accuracy over SGLD with comparable computation time.

One minor concern is that the paper assumes certain technical conditions on U, f, and L that might be challenging to verify in practice. However, the authors appropriately reference these conditions and acknowledge them as technical requirements for the theoretical results. The authors also acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for the same mini-batch size for both chains), demonstrating honesty about the work's scope.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly states the problem, motivation, and contributions. The preliminaries section provides a solid foundation for readers unfamiliar with SG-MCMC or RR extrapolation. The presentation of the SGRRLD algorithm (Section 3) is clear, with well-defined notation and pseudocode. The theoretical results are presented logically, with the key theorems followed by detailed explanations of their implications. The experimental section is well-structured, with appropriate visualizations and clear descriptions of the experimental setup.

The paper could be even clearer about the computational cost of the parallel chains. While the authors note that the two chains can be run independently, they don't explicitly state how the computational cost compares to SGLD (e.g., whether it's exactly double or slightly more due to synchronization overhead). This is a minor point, as the results show comparable wall-clock times in the experiments.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to this domain, which has not been explored before. The key novelty lies in using RR extrapolation to achieve higher convergence rates with first-order integrators, effectively matching the performance of higher-order integrator methods. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior contributions, particularly from Chen et al. (2015), which showed that higher-order integrators can improve convergence rates.

The paper also demonstrates the generality of the approach by applying it to SGHMC as well as SGLD, showing that the technique can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms. The connection between RR extrapolation for SDEs and SG-MCMC is novel and well-motivated, with the authors properly citing relevant prior work in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015).

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The improved convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) represents a meaningful theoretical advance in SG-MCMC, especially since it's achieved using only first-order integrators. This is particularly valuable because higher-order integrators often introduce significant computational complexity. The method's parallelizability is a major practical advantage for large-scale applications, as demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments where SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to SGLD in half the time.

The experimental results provide strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims, with clear improvements across multiple datasets and metrics. The method's ability to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods without their computational overhead represents a tangible practical benefit for practitioners. This work advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference and provides a useful tool for practitioners working with large-scale MCMC problems.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical implications are clear. The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian machine learning (scaling MCMC to large datasets) with a novel and effective approach.

**Strengths:**
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rate improvements
- Clear experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets
- Practical parallelizability that matches the theoretical advantages
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational cost beyond noting parallelizability
- Technical conditions for theoretical results could be more thoroughly discussed
- Limited exploration of extremely high-dimensional problems beyond matrix factorization

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of scalable MCMC methods. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is strong, and the practical implications are important for the Bayesian machine learning community. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with both theoretical and practical significance. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
