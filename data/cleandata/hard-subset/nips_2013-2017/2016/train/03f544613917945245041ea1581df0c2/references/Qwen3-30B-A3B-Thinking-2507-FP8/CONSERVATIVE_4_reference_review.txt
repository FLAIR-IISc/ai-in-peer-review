

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes SGRRLD, a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of SGLD while maintaining reasonable variance. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine their results via RR extrapolation, claiming improved convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) MSE vs. SGLD's O(K⁻²/³)).

**Strengths:** The paper provides a theoretically sound framework for applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The convergence analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) is technically rigorous and extends previous work on RR extrapolation for SDEs [23] to the stochastic gradient setting. The experimental validation on synthetic data and matrix factorization shows consistent improvements over SGLD in terms of bias and MSE. The paper also demonstrates the generality of the approach by extending it to SGHMC.

**Weaknesses:** As a skeptical reviewer, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and validation:

1. **Theoretical claims vs. practical relevance:** While the theoretical convergence rate improvement is mathematically sound, the paper fails to demonstrate that this translates to meaningful practical improvements. The claimed rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) requires a specific step size schedule (γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵), but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this schedule is optimal in practice or that it's better than using higher-order integrators directly. The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators," but doesn't compare directly to such methods (e.g., [10]) which might be more efficient than running two parallel chains.

2. **Inadequate experimental validation:** The experimental section is limited to two scenarios: a simple Gaussian model and matrix factorization on MovieLens datasets. There's no validation on more challenging problems (e.g., deep Bayesian neural networks) or comparison to more recent SG-MCMC methods (e.g., [10], [13]). The paper claims generality across "all current SG-MCMC algorithms," but only demonstrates this for SGHMC, not for the broader family of methods.

3. **Computational cost concerns:** The paper claims SGRRLD is "well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures" since the two chains can run independently. However, it doesn't quantify the actual computational overhead. Running two chains in parallel doubles the computational cost (assuming no communication overhead), which may negate the claimed convergence rate advantage. The paper shows SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to SGLD in half the time, but this is only because it runs for half the iterations (K=5250 vs K=10500), not because it's more efficient per iteration.

4. **Lack of comparison to baseline methods:** The paper doesn't compare to more recent SG-MCMC methods that might achieve similar or better rates without the additional computational cost. For example, [10] shows that higher-order integrators can achieve similar rates without requiring parallel chains. The paper should demonstrate why SGRRLD is preferable to these established methods.

5. **Sensitivity analysis:** The paper shows that for very small γ, bias and MSE increase, but doesn't provide guidance on selecting the optimal step size in practice. The experiments use fixed step sizes, but the paper's theoretical results depend on carefully chosen decreasing step size schedules, which aren't properly validated.

**Overall assessment:** While the theoretical contribution is non-trivial and the paper is well-written, the practical significance of the proposed method is questionable. The claimed improvement in convergence rate doesn't translate to meaningful efficiency gains in practice, as demonstrated by the limited experimental validation. The paper makes strong claims about the method's advantages but fails to provide sufficient evidence that these advantages hold in real-world applications with practical computational constraints.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, but the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and failure to demonstrate clear practical advantages over established methods make it difficult to justify its acceptance. The authors should provide more extensive experiments on diverse datasets and compare against higher-order integrator methods before claiming significant improvement over the state of the art.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:* 
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and extends previous work on RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
- The method is conceptually simple and could be applied to various SG-MCMC algorithms.
- The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over SGLD in the experiments provided.

*Con:*
- The claimed convergence rate improvement doesn't translate to meaningful practical efficiency gains.
- The paper lacks comprehensive experimental validation across diverse problems and against recent baselines.
- The computational cost of running two parallel chains is not adequately addressed.
- The paper doesn't demonstrate why this approach is preferable to using higher-order integrators directly.
- The practical sensitivity to step size choices is not adequately explored.
