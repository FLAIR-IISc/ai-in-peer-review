Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The method runs two coupled SGLD chains with stepsizes γ and γ/2 to cancel the leading O(γ) discretization bias, yielding an estimator with O(γ^2) bias and O(γ^4) variance (as functions of γ), while controlling variance by correlating Brownian increments. The authors prove a CLT for the RR estimator, provide non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and show that with appropriate stepsize schedules the MSE rate improves from O(K^−2/3) (first-order SG-MCMC) to O(K^−4/5). They argue the approach is generic and demonstrate extensions to SGHMC. Empirical results on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization show clear accuracy/time improvements over SGLD, and parity with higher-order SGHMC variants at larger stepsizes.

Relation to prior work
RR extrapolation for SDEs and duplicated diffusions is classical (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup). SG-MCMC analyses (Teh–Vollmer–Zygalakis; Chen–Ding–Carin) have emphasized discretization and higher-order integrators. To my knowledge, RR has not been brought to SG-MCMC before; the coupling of Brownian increments to minimize variance is known in the exact-gradient setting and is adapted here to stochastic gradients. The connection to multilevel Monte Carlo is acknowledged.

Strengths
- Conceptually novel and simple: importing RR extrapolation into SG-MCMC is a clean idea with broad applicability.
- Theoretical advances: CLT with optimal Brownian coupling; non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds; clear identification of optimal stepsize schedules (α=1/5 for MSE), improving known rates for first-order SG-MCMC without requiring higher-order integrators.
- Practicality: easy to implement; parallelizable (two chains) so wall-clock can match a single chain on multi-core hardware.
- Generality: applies beyond SGLD; preliminary SGHMC results support this.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory on both synthetic and large real datasets, showing substantial accuracy/time gains.

Weaknesses and questions
- Possible typo in Chain 2 update (Eq. 5): the first fine step’s gradient is written at θ(γ/2)2k+1 although the state being updated is θ(γ/2)2k. This would make the step implicit; likely it should be ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k). Please clarify/correct.
- Computational budget fairness: results assume two chains run in parallel. Many users cannot double cores; a single-core comparison (same gradient-eval budget) would be informative.
- Coupling of stochastic gradients: theory allows arbitrary correlation and requires same mini-batch size distributions across chains, but RR cancellation is purely in the discretization error. In practice, sharing mini-batches (or using matched seeds/antithetic sampling) across levels could further reduce variance; this is not explored. An ablation showing the effect of: (i) independent vs shared mini-batches, and (ii) correlated vs independent Brownian increments, would strengthen the practical guidance.
- Assumptions: the CLT proof requires high smoothness (solution g∈C9) and standard dissipativity/ergodicity conditions. This is typical but should be discussed more explicitly in main text (currently deferred to supplement), including examples where they hold (e.g., log-concave posteriors) and where they may fail.
- Scope of experiments: beyond Gaussian/posterior-conjugate models and MF, it would be valuable to show a non-convex, non-Gaussian posterior (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression on real data; a simple Bayesian NN), and a stronger comparison with higher-order integrators (e.g., more SGHMC-s settings).
- Tuning: practical guidance on choosing γ and α is scattered; a brief recipe in the main text would help practitioners.

Quality
Overall technically solid. The RR idea is correctly adapted, and the analyses (CLT, non-asymptotic bounds, optimal coupling Σ=Id) are consistent with prior theory in the exact-gradient case and appear carefully extended to stochastic gradients. The suspected typo in Eq. (5) should be fixed. More discussion of the impact of stochastic gradient noise coupling would improve completeness.

Clarity
Well-organized and readable, though notation is dense. The algorithmic description could be clearer (pseudo-code in the main text would help). Explicitly state in the main paper the conditions on U and L, and the requirement to keep mini-batch distributions identical across chains. Minor edits: unify transpose notation (Σ> vs Σ^T), and check indices in Eq. (6) denominator.

Originality
High. RR extrapolation is classic in SDE numerics, but its application to SG-MCMC with a principled variance control via Brownian coupling, and the demonstration that first-order schemes can match higher-order accuracy in this framework, is a fresh and compelling direction.

Significance
High. SG-MCMC is widely used; a simple, parallelizable technique that improves bias/MSE rates and is compatible with existing methods is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., multilevel SG-MCMC). The theoretical rates and empirical gains are meaningful.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and elegant idea with broad applicability
- Strong theory with improved rates and clear optimal coupling
- Simple to implement; parallel-friendly
- Convincing empirical improvements

Cons against acceptance
- Minor but important clarity/typo issues in algorithm description
- Limited exploration of gradient coupling and single-core budget fairness
- Experimental scope could be broader

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a new and promising direction for SG-MCMC with both theoretical and practical impact. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation points would further strengthen the contribution. Confidence: high.
