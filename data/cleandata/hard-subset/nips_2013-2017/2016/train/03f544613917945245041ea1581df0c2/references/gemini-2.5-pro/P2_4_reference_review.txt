### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical method for accelerating convergence, to the widely-used Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The motivation is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in SG-MCMC methods, which arises from the Euler-Maruyama scheme used to approximate the continuous-time Langevin diffusion.

The proposed method runs two parallel SGLD chains on the same problem: one with a step size sequence `γk` and another that takes two steps of size `γk/2` for each single step of the first chain. By taking a specific linear combination of the estimators from these two chains, the leading O(γ) bias term is cancelled out. To control the variance of the resulting estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motion for the two chains.

The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for standard SGLD. This improved rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex higher-order integrators, but SGRRLD achieves this using only the first-order Euler integrator. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task on the MovieLens datasets, showing clear improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and clever contribution. It elegantly bridges classical numerical analysis with modern large-scale Bayesian inference, providing a new direction for improving SG-MCMC methods that is distinct from the common approaches of variance reduction or higher-order integrator design.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper is theoretically rigorous. It provides both asymptotic analysis (a central limit theorem) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. These results formally establish the faster convergence rates and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm.
3.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The theoretical gains translate into substantial practical benefits. The MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a state-of-the-art result for this class of methods. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves lower bias and MSE than SGLD and can match the accuracy of more complex higher-order methods like SGHMC-s.
4.  **Practicality and Generality:** The method is practical and well-suited for modern computing architectures. The requirement of running two chains is a perfect use case for parallel processing, as highlighted by the authors. Furthermore, the technique is presented as a general "wrapper" that can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms, a claim supported by the successful application to SGHMC.
5.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive. The synthetic experiments meticulously verify the theoretical claims regarding the dependency of bias/MSE on the step size (γ) and the number of iterations (K). The large-scale matrix factorization experiment demonstrates the method's real-world utility, showing faster convergence in wall-clock time on benchmark datasets.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection/revision):**

1.  **Increased Computational Cost:** The most significant drawback is the computational overhead. SGRRLD requires approximately twice the computational effort of SGLD because it effectively runs two chains (one for K steps and one for 2K half-steps). While the authors correctly point out this is amenable to parallelization, in a resource-constrained or non-parallel environment, this represents a direct doubling of cost. A more explicit discussion of the trade-off between this cost and the convergence speedup would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Increased Memory Footprint:** The algorithm requires maintaining two separate chains, meaning it doubles the memory required to store the model parameters. For very large models, such as deep neural networks, this could be a significant limitation, but this aspect is not discussed.
3.  **Clarity on Assumptions:** The theoretical results are presented under "appropriate conditions" which are deferred to the supplementary material. While standard for conference papers, a brief, intuitive summary of these assumptions (e.g., strong convexity of the potential U, smoothness of the test function f) in the main text would help the reader gauge the practical applicability and limitations of the theory without having to consult the supplement.

### **Detailed Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound and builds upon established literature in both SDE discretization and SG-MCMC. The claims are well-supported by both the theorems and the extensive experimental results. The experiments are carefully constructed to isolate and validate the specific theoretical predictions, such as the optimal exponents for the step-size decay schedule. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, the algorithm is described precisely, and the main theoretical results are stated cleanly. The progression from the problem (SGLD bias), to the proposed solution (RR extrapolation), to the theoretical and empirical validation is logical and easy to follow. The figures are informative and effectively illustrate the key performance differences between the methods.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first paper to successfully apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation in the context of *stochastic gradient* MCMC. While RR has been used for SDEs with exact gradients, its application here is non-trivial due to the presence of stochastic gradient noise. The paper correctly identifies and addresses the key challenges, such as controlling the variance via correlated noise. This work clearly advances the field beyond existing SGLD variants and provides a novel alternative to the development of higher-order integrators.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. SGRRLD offers a simple, general, and parallelizable method to substantially improve the accuracy of a widely used class of algorithms for large-scale Bayesian inference. The ability to achieve the accuracy of complex higher-order methods using only a first-order integrator is a powerful result. This work is likely to be influential and could inspire further research into applying other numerical acceleration techniques to machine learning algorithms. Practitioners using SG-MCMC methods would find this technique to be a compelling and relatively easy-to-implement upgrade for improving their models' accuracy. It demonstrably advances the state of the art in scalable sampling.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective algorithm. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to the inherent computational trade-offs of the method. It is a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
