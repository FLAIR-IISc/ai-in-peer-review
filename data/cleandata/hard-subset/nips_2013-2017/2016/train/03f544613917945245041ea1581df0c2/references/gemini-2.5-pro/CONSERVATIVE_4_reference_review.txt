**Summary:**
This paper proposes a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation method, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to the well-established Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The goal is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the Euler-Maruyama scheme used in SGLD. The proposed method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2 for a given time horizon) and combines their estimators to cancel out the leading-order bias term. To prevent the variance from increasing, the method uses correlated Brownian motion for the two chains. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves a mean-squared-error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD. This improved rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex higher-order integrators. The method is evaluated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, where it is shown to outperform standard SGLD.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound in its theoretical analysis.
*   **Strengths:** The derivation of a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2) provides a solid formal foundation for the proposed algorithm. The theoretical results clearly demonstrate the advantage of the RR scheme, predicting a faster convergence rate than standard SGLD. The experimental validation on the linear Gaussian model is well-designed and provides convincing empirical support for the theoretical claims, particularly regarding the scaling of bias and MSE with the step size γ.
*   **Weaknesses:** The primary weakness lies in the empirical validation, which feels incomplete for a NIPS paper.
    1.  The experiments are conducted on a simple convex Gaussian model and a matrix factorization problem, which is also relatively well-behaved. The performance of SG-MCMC methods is often most challenged by complex, high-dimensional, and non-convex posterior landscapes, such as those arising in Bayesian neural networks. The absence of such an evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of SGRRLD in more demanding modern machine learning settings.
    2.  The theoretical analysis (as hinted by the proof sketch mentioning a 7th-order Taylor expansion) likely relies on strong smoothness assumptions for the potential U. These assumptions are often violated in practice (e.g., models with ReLU activations). The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged and discussed these limitations.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for reducing bias in SGLD is well-established, and the authors do a good job of explaining how the classical RR method can be adapted for this purpose. The algorithm is described precisely, and the main theoretical results are stated clearly. The connection to prior work in both SG-MCMC and numerical methods for SDEs is adequately explained.

**Originality:**
The originality of the work is moderate. The core components—SGLD and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation—are both well-known, established methods in their respective fields. The main contribution is the synthesis of these two ideas and the accompanying theoretical analysis that rigorously characterizes the behavior of the RR estimator in the presence of stochastic gradient noise. While applying a known numerical trick to an existing algorithm might be seen as incremental, the formal analysis of the resulting algorithm in the SG-MCMC context is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The idea of using correlated noise to control variance, while also inspired by prior work on SDEs, is correctly identified as a critical component for the method to be effective.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is substantial, but the current evidence does not fully realize it.
*   **Strengths:** The paper offers a conceptually simple and easily parallelizable method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC. The ability to achieve the convergence rates of higher-order integrators without the hassle of computing higher-order derivatives of the potential or implementing complex symplectic schemes is very appealing. If the empirical gains hold more generally, this could become a standard technique for practitioners using SG-MCMC.
*   **Weaknesses:** The significance is currently limited by the experimental evaluation. The paper claims SGRRLD is more efficient and accurate, but it only demonstrates this against the original SGLD algorithm. SGLD is an important baseline, but the field has advanced since its introduction. A comparison against more recent and powerful SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGHMC, preconditioned methods, or the higher-order integrators mentioned in [10]) on the real-world matrix factorization task would be necessary to establish that SGRRLD is truly state-of-the-art. The claim that SGRRLD matches the accuracy of higher-order methods is only supported by an experiment on the toy Gaussian model.

Furthermore, a critical baseline is missing. The method's main practical advantage is its parallel nature. A natural alternative use of two cores would be to run two independent SGLD chains and average their results. While this would not reduce bias, it would reduce variance. A comparison against this "ensemble SGLD" baseline is essential to demonstrate that the RR combination is a more effective use of parallel resources than simple ensembling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel combination of established techniques to address a known limitation (discretization bias) in SG-MCMC.
*   Provides a rigorous and complete theoretical analysis with improved convergence rates.
*   The method is simple to implement and inherently parallel.
*   The experiments, though limited, successfully validate the core theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough. It lacks experiments on challenging non-convex problems and comparisons against stronger, more modern SG-MCMC baselines on the main real-world task.
*   The paper's claim of matching the performance of higher-order methods is not sufficiently substantiated on a practical, large-scale problem.
*   A crucial parallel baseline (running and averaging two independent SGLD chains) is missing, making it hard to assess the efficiency of the proposed parallelization scheme.

In summary, this is a good paper with a solid theoretical contribution and a promising idea. However, the experimental evidence is not yet strong enough to support the claims of general applicability and superiority in the competitive landscape of SG-MCMC methods. The work would be much more compelling with a more thorough experimental section that addresses the weaknesses outlined above.
