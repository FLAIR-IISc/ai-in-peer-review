Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and combines their time-averaged estimators using the RR recipe. To keep variance under control, it uses correlated Gaussian noise across chains, with the optimal choice being perfectly correlated Brownian increments. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a CLT for the extrapolated estimator with minimal asymptotic variance when the Brownian noises are perfectly correlated; (ii) finite-time bounds showing bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant γ, yielding optimal MSE rate O(K−4/5), improving over SGLD’s O(K−2/3); and (iii) rates for decreasing step sizes and identification of optimal exponents. Experiments on a Gaussian model, SGHMC (as SGRRHMC), and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support the theory and demonstrate wall-clock gains by parallelizing the two chains.

Relation to prior work
The idea of RR extrapolation for SDE discretizations is classical (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire, Pagès, Panloup), and its use for invariant measure estimation has a known asymptotic theory. SG-MCMC theory (Teh, Vollmer, Zygalakis; Chen et al.) has established O(γ), O(γ2) bias/MSE for first-order integrators and improvements via higher-order integrators. To my knowledge, applying RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients and analyzing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic behavior is new and well positioned relative to prior work. The link to correlated Brownian increments and its effect on variance mirrors results in the SDE literature and appropriately extends them.

Quality
- Theory: The results are technically sound in spirit and align with known weak-error expansions for Euler schemes, adapted to the SG-MCMC setting. The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are plausible; the optimal rates (α = 1/5 for MSE, α = 1/3 for bias with decreasing steps) are consistent with the expansions. That said, the analysis relies on strong assumptions (existence of a smooth solution g to the Poisson equation with high regularity, bounded moments of gradient estimators, ergodicity, etc.), and many details are deferred to the supplement. The claim that asymptotically “the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” is important and should be made precise in the main text (which scaling and conditions on L are needed).
- Variance control: The result that Σ = Id minimizes asymptotic variance is in line with multi-level/RR insights and is a useful, actionable implementation detail.
- Non-asymptotic bounds: The finite-time bias/MSE bounds for constant γ are valuable for practice; the γ–K tradeoffs are clearly discussed.
- Experiments: The synthetic experiments convincingly show bias/MSE reductions and match predicted rates; the MovieLens experiments show consistent wall-clock improvements by exploiting parallelism. The SGHMC extension (SGRRHMC) is encouraging, though theoretical support for Hamiltonian dynamics is not provided here.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear description of the estimator and its intuition. However:
- Some key assumptions are only stated as “appropriate conditions” with details in the supplement. At least the main ones (regularity, moment conditions on stochastic gradients, ergodicity) should be summarized in the main text so readers can judge applicability.
- Possible typo in Chain 2 updates (Eq. (5)): the gradient appears to be evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k+1, which would make it semi-implicit; Euler–Maruyama should use θ(γ/2)2k (and θ(γ/2)2k+1 for the second substep). Please clarify/correct.
- The estimator requires the two chains to have the same minibatch size and gradient estimator law L. Clarify whether you reuse the same minibatch across substeps/chains (which could reduce variance further) and whether this affects unbiasedness.

Originality
The core idea—using RR extrapolation to upgrade first-order SG-MCMC without higher-order integrators—and its variance-controlled implementation via correlated Brownian increments are novel in the SG-MCMC context. The theoretical rates matching higher-order integrators with only first-order updates is an elegant contribution.

Significance
- Methodological impact: The approach is simple, generic, and orthogonal to many SG-MCMC variants; it should be easy to adopt and combine with existing methods (e.g., pSGLD, thermostats), potentially broadening impact.
- Practical relevance: Gains are most compelling when parallel resources are available; serially, the method doubles gradient work per time horizon (two chains) and thus the tradeoff is less favorable. Still, the improved bias/MSE rates are meaningful.
- Empirical scope: The large-scale matrix factorization application is relevant; additional comparisons to other strong SG-MCMC baselines (e.g., pSGLD, SGNHT) would strengthen claims but are not strictly necessary.

Strengths
- Clear, principled use of RR extrapolation tailored to SG-MCMC.
- Both asymptotic CLT and finite-time bias/MSE bounds with improved rates.
- Practical guidance on correlating Brownian increments.
- Consistent empirical improvements, including wall-clock when parallelism is used.
- Extension to SGHMC demonstrates generality.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Assumptions are strong and mostly hidden in the supplement; summarize them in the main text and discuss applicability (e.g., non-smooth likelihoods/priors).
- Clarify the Chain 2 update equations (potential semi-implicit typo).
- Discuss the effect of gradient noise magnitude versus injected Gaussian noise; when minibatches are very small, does the benefit persist?
- Fairness of comparisons: SGRRLD uses two parallel processes; include a serial runtime comparison or explicitly position the method as leveraging extra cores.
- Provide ablations: effect of Σ (vary correlation), effect of sharing vs not sharing minibatches across chains/substeps.
- SGHMC extension lacks theory; at least briefly discuss expected changes and assumptions.

Questions for the authors
- Do you share minibatches across the two substeps of the γ/2 chain and/or across chains? Does sharing further reduce variance without introducing bias?
- How sensitive are gains to batch size (i.e., when stochastic gradient noise dominates)?
- Can you comment on memory overhead and communication if parameters are large and chains run on separate devices?
- Could RR be applied recursively in practice (as suggested) without destabilizing variance in SG-MCMC?

Minor issues
- Fix the potential typo in Eq. (5).
- A brief pseudocode in the main text (not only supplement) would aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution: theoretically sound under standard (albeit strong) assumptions, simple to implement, and empirically beneficial. It advances SG-MCMC by importing a powerful extrapolation idea, with clear guidance on variance control. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions and fixing the Chain 2 update.
