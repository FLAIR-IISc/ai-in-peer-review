This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The primary motivation is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order SG-MCMC methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), which arises from the Euler-Maruyama discretization of the underlying Langevin SDE. SGLD, introduced by Welling and Teh (2011), is a foundational method for scalable Bayesian inference, but its accuracy is limited by a bias of order O(γ), where γ is the step size. While subsequent work (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) has shown that higher-order integrators can reduce this bias, they can be more complex to implement.

The proposed SGRRLD method leverages Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. The core idea is to run two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size sequence (γk) and another that takes two steps of size γk/2 for each step of the first chain. A new estimator is then formed by a linear combination of the outputs of these two chains, which is designed to cancel the leading O(γ) error term. This technique has been studied for SDEs with exact gradients (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), but its application and analysis in the SG-MCMC setting, which includes noise from data subsampling, is novel. To control the variance of the combined estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motions for the two chains, showing that perfect correlation is optimal.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. They prove a central limit theorem for the estimator and derive non-asymptotic bounds on its bias and mean-squared error (MSE). The key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²) and an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for K iterations, which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem using MovieLens datasets.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE of the proposed SGRRLD estimator. The proofs, sketched in the main text and detailed in the supplement, appear to correctly adapt established techniques from the analysis of SDEs to the more complex SG-MCMC setting. The claims of improved convergence rates are well-supported by this theory. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed; it not only validates the theoretical rates on a synthetic problem where the ground truth is known but also demonstrates practical performance gains on a large-scale real-world application.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for reducing the discretization bias in SGLD is clearly articulated. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the connection to classical Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-explained. The main theoretical results are stated clearly in Theorems 1 and 2, and their implications for choosing optimal step-size schedules are discussed. The experimental sections are easy to follow, and the figures effectively illustrate the performance improvements of SGRRLD over baseline methods.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework—is novel. While the RR technique itself is not new, its adaptation and analysis in the context of stochastic gradient methods, where there are two sources of noise (discretization and subsampling), represent a significant and original contribution. This work provides a new perspective on improving SG-MCMC methods, offering an alternative to the development of more complex, higher-order integrators. The idea of using correlated noise to control the variance of the RR estimator is also a key insight that makes the approach practical.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are likely to be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian machine learning. SGRRLD offers a practical and simple way to achieve higher-order accuracy while only requiring the implementation of a first-order integrator (Euler-Maruyama). Given the widespread use of SGLD and its variants, a method that improves its accuracy with minimal implementation overhead is highly valuable. Furthermore, the algorithm is inherently parallel, which is a crucial feature for scalability on modern hardware. The demonstration that the technique can be generalized to other SG-MCMC methods like SGHMC further enhances its potential impact.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The SGRRLD algorithm requires performing three gradient computations per effective time step γ (one for the coarse chain, two for the fine chain), compared to two for a standard SGLD implementation running on the same time scale (i.e., with two steps of size γ/2). This represents a 50% increase in the total number of gradient evaluations. While the authors correctly argue that this can be perfectly parallelized to yield similar wall-clock times, this advantage is contingent on the availability of at least two parallel processing units. On a single-core system, SGRRLD would be slower. A more explicit discussion of this computational trade-off would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scope of Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper states that the technique is "quite generic" and can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms. While this is demonstrated empirically for SGHMC, the theoretical analysis is presented for Langevin dynamics. It is not immediately clear what precise conditions an arbitrary SG-MCMC algorithm must satisfy for the RR extrapolation to be effective and for similar theoretical improvements to hold. Expanding on this would make the claim of generality more concrete.

3.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Methods:** The paper positions SGRRLD as an alternative to methods using explicit higher-order integrators. The empirical comparison to SGHMC-s in Figure 4 is a good first step, but it is limited. A more extensive comparison against such methods, perhaps on the matrix factorization task as well, would provide a clearer picture of the relative trade-offs in terms of implementation complexity, computational cost, and statistical efficiency.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the widely used SG-MCMC framework.
*   Provides strong and rigorous theoretical results, demonstrating a superior rate of convergence (MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD.
*   The proposed method achieves higher-order accuracy using only a simple first-order integrator, making it easy to implement.
*   The algorithm is inherently parallel, making it highly suitable for scalable Bayesian inference.
*   Empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data convincingly validate the theoretical claims and show significant practical benefits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method incurs a 50% higher cost in terms of gradient computations, the mitigation of which depends on parallel hardware.
*   The comparison to existing higher-order integrators is somewhat limited, making it difficult to fully assess its competitiveness against that line of work.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful algorithm that advances the state of the art in scalable MCMC. The contribution is clear, significant, and of high relevance to the NIPS community.
