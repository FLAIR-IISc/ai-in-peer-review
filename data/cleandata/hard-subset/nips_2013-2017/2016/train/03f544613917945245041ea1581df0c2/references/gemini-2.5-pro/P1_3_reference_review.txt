### Summary
This paper proposes a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The method adapts the classical Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique from numerical analysis. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order bias term. A key technical contribution is the use of correlated Brownian motions for the two chains to control the variance of the resulting estimator, an idea previously explored for standard SDEs but new to the SG-MCMC context.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean-Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD. This effectively provides the accuracy of a higher-order integrator while only using the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The method's effectiveness and theoretical claims are validated through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task using MovieLens datasets.

### Strengths
1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and clever idea. It addresses a fundamental limitation—discretization bias—of many popular scalable MCMC algorithms. The resulting improvement in convergence rates from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is substantial and advances the state of the art in this area. The ability to achieve higher-order accuracy with a simple, first-order integrator is a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is technically sound and provides rigorous convergence analysis. The authors derive a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) that characterizes the asymptotic behavior and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results clearly quantify the improvement over SGLD and correctly identify the optimal step-size decay rate to balance bias and variance, which is a non-trivial result.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is excellent.
    *   The synthetic Gaussian model experiments are well-designed to isolate and verify the theoretical claims. They clearly demonstrate the reduction in bias (Figure 1a) and MSE (Figure 1b, 2) compared to SGLD. The experiment validating the optimal step-size decay rate (Figure 3) is particularly strong evidence supporting the theory.
    *   The demonstration of applying the RR technique to another algorithm (SGHMC) supports the claim of generality.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world datasets (MovieLens) show that the theoretical gains translate into practical performance improvements in terms of wall-clock time, which is the most relevant metric for practitioners.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established, the method is described precisely, and the connection to prior work in numerical analysis for SDEs is well explained. The results are presented effectively, making the paper easy to follow and understand.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The primary weakness is the understated discussion of the computational trade-off. SGRRLD requires running two chains. For each "effective" step of the combined estimator, one step of the γ-chain and two steps of the γ/2-chain are performed. This amounts to roughly 3x the gradient computations per effective step compared to SGLD. While the authors state that the two chains can be run in parallel (and do so in their experiments), this still implies a 2x increase in resource utilization (e.g., two CPU cores instead of one). For a user without access to parallel hardware, the wall-clock time per effective step would be ~3x that of SGLD. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this cost-benefit analysis. While the experiments show SGRRLD is faster in wall-clock time even with this overhead, this point deserves more prominence.

2.  **Memory Overhead:** The method requires storing two separate parameter chains (θ(γ) and θ(γ/2)), which doubles the memory footprint. For extremely large models where parameter storage is a bottleneck (e.g., massive neural networks), this could be a practical limitation. This is a minor point but worth acknowledging.

3.  **Practical Guidance on Gradient Noise:** The paper states that the stochastic gradient sequences can be correlated but does not assume a specific form. For a practitioner, it would be useful to have more guidance here. For instance, should one use the same mini-batch of data for the three gradient calculations within one effective step (one for the γ-chain, two for the γ/2-chain)? This would reduce the variance from the data subsampling but would also mean the computational cost is only 1x gradient evaluation per effective step (since the gradient is computed at different θ values). The current implementation appears to use independent mini-batches (3x cost). Clarifying and justifying this choice would be beneficial.

### Questions/Suggestions for Authors
*   Could you add a paragraph in the main paper explicitly discussing the computational cost (3x gradient evaluations per effective step) and how it is mitigated by parallelism? It would be helpful to frame the results as "SGRRLD achieves better accuracy in less wall-clock time, provided at least two parallel compute units are available."
*   In Figure 5, the time axis is "wall-clock time." Could you clarify if SGRRLD was run on two cores and SGLD on one? This is implied but should be stated explicitly in the experiment setup to make the comparison completely transparent.
*   Regarding the application to SGHMC: Is the analysis in Section 4 directly applicable, or would it require significant modification to account for the auxiliary momentum variables and the Hamiltonian dynamics?

### Overall Recommendation
This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by strong theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical validation. It successfully bridges classical numerical analysis with modern machine learning, leading to a practical algorithm with demonstrably better performance. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to the need for a more explicit discussion of computational trade-offs. The paper is of high quality and will likely be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
