### NIPS Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation method, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The method works by running two parallel SGLD chains on the same target distribution, one with a step size `γ` and the other with `γ/2` (run for twice as many steps to cover the same time horizon). The outputs of these two chains are then linearly combined to cancel the leading-order term in the discretization bias. A key element of the proposal is the use of correlated Brownian motions for the two chains, which is shown to control the variance of the resulting estimator.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They claim that their method reduces the asymptotic bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) and improves the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵). This effectively achieves the accuracy of methods using complex second-order integrators, but by using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of SGRRLD on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, showing significant improvements over standard SGLD.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and conceptual elegance. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a bold, insightful, and, to my knowledge, entirely new idea in this context. It makes a clever connection between a well-established technique in numerical analysis for SDEs and the modern challenges of large-scale Bayesian inference. This is precisely the kind of creative, cross-disciplinary work that pushes the field forward.

The theoretical contribution is significant. The authors don't just propose an algorithm but back it up with rigorous analysis. Deriving a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds that clearly demonstrate a higher rate of convergence (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) is a substantial achievement. This improved rate is not merely an incremental gain; it represents a fundamental speedup over existing first-order methods. The idea that one can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators without the hassle of computing their complex update steps is highly appealing and practically important.

The experimental validation is convincing and well-executed. The experiments on the linear Gaussian model serve as an excellent "proof of concept," clearly demonstrating the predicted reduction in bias and MSE as a function of the step size. The comparison to SGHMC and a higher-order SGHMC variant further strengthens the claim of generality and performance. Most importantly, the large-scale matrix factorization experiments on the MovieLens datasets show that this is not just a theoretical curiosity. The demonstrated improvement in RMSE as a function of wall-clock time is a compelling argument for the practical utility of SGRRLD. The authors make a strong case for the method's suitability for parallel architectures, which is a crucial consideration for modern machine learning.

The paper is also very well-written and clear. The motivation is well-established, the proposed method is described precisely, and the theoretical and empirical results are presented in a coherent manner.

The weaknesses of the work are relatively minor and largely represent inherent trade-offs rather than fundamental flaws.

1.  **Computational Cost:** The most obvious drawback is that SGRRLD requires running two MCMC chains, effectively doubling the computational operations compared to running a single SGLD chain for the same number of effective iterations. The authors correctly argue that this cost can be perfectly amortized by running the chains in parallel, leading to no increase in wall-clock time. While true on multi-core hardware, it does imply a doubling of resource utilization (e.g., two CPUs/GPUs instead of one). This trade-off—more resources for faster convergence/higher accuracy—is likely acceptable in many scenarios but should be acknowledged.

2.  **Increased Complexity:** While the method avoids the complexity of higher-order integrators, it introduces its own complexity in managing two chains, ensuring the noise terms are properly correlated, and implementing the final extrapolation step. This complexity is modest, but it makes the algorithm slightly less "drop-in" than vanilla SGLD.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The core idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is highly original and opens up a new avenue for improving these algorithms.
*   **Significant Impact:** The work provides a general recipe for improving the accuracy of a wide class of first-order SG-MCMC methods. The promise of higher-order accuracy from a first-order integrator is a significant practical and theoretical contribution.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, establishing a faster rate of convergence that is a clear improvement over the state of the art for first-order methods.
*   **Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments robustly support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical benefits on a large-scale real-world problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The approach requires double the computational resources (though not necessarily wall-clock time) of its baseline competitor, which could be a limitation in resource-constrained environments.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend **acceptance** for this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly effective idea that bridges a gap between classical numerical analysis and modern scalable MCMC. The work is technically sound, theoretically deep, and empirically validated. While it comes with the trade-off of increased computational resource usage, the significant gains in accuracy and convergence speed, combined with its amenability to parallelism, make it a compelling and important contribution. This is an excellent paper that is likely to inspire follow-up work on variance reduction and multi-level methods within the SG-MCMC framework.
