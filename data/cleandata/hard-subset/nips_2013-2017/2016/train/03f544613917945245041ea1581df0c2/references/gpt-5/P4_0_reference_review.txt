Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD-like samplers while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. The approach runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and constructs a time-averaged estimator using RR weights. The paper extends RR extrapolation ideas previously used for SDE weak error reduction (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and for invariant measure estimation with ULA (Lemaire et al., 2015) to the stochastic-gradient setting. Theoretical contributions include a CLT for the RR estimator with conditions on step-size schedules, a demonstration that optimal correlation of Gaussian noise (Σ = Id) minimizes asymptotic variance, and non-asymptotic finite-time bounds showing improved rates versus SGLD. Empirically, the method reduces bias and MSE on a linear Gaussian model and improves wall-clock RMSE on large-scale matrix factorization; the RR idea is also shown to benefit SGHMC.

This work sits in the SG-MCMC literature initiated by SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011), with subsequent methods such as SGHMC (Chen et al., 2014), thermostat-based samplers, and higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015). Relative to these, SGRRLD achieves second-order bias reduction while retaining a first-order integrator, by leveraging step-size extrapolation. The paper also connects to multilevel methods and duplicated diffusions used for variance control in RR extrapolation.

Quality
- Theory: The paper states asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and MSE O(γ^4) for the invariant-measure bias/variance under constant step size, and finite-time bounds of O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) for the bias and O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) for the MSE. Optimizing γ yields K−2/3 bias and K−4/5 MSE rates, improving upon SGLD’s K−1/2 and K−2/3. For decreasing γk = γ1 k−α, the CLT and non-asymptotic results imply optimal α = 1/5 for MSE convergence O(K−2/5), improving upon SGLD’s O(K−1/3). These results appear consistent with prior ULA RR analyses and with SG-MCMC bounds in Ma et al. (2015) and Chen et al. (2015).
- Assumptions: The CLT requires a Poisson equation solution g ∈ C^9 and “appropriate” conditions on U, f, and the gradient noise distribution L. The paper assumes i.i.d. unbiased gradient estimators with a common distribution across iterations. While standard in SG-MCMC analyses, the smoothness and independence assumptions are strong; a concise statement of the main assumptions (e.g., dissipativity/convexity-at-infinity, Lipschitz ∇U, bounded moments of gradient noise conditional on θ) in the main text would improve transparency.
- Variance control: The correlation structure for Brownian increments is well motivated and aligns with RR theory; showing Σ = Id minimizes variance is a useful result. The claim that gradient noise correlation is asymptotically negligible is plausible under the given scaling but could be discussed further for finite-K behavior.
- Potential issue in updates: Equation (5) appears to use ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) when computing θ(γ/2)2k+1 from θ(γ/2)2k, which would be an implicit step. This is likely a typo and should be θ(γ/2)2k on the right-hand side for the first half-step; please correct for clarity and to avoid suggesting an implicit scheme.
- Computation: The method costs two small-step updates per one large-step update. The paper fairly notes parallelization can match SGLD wall-clock on multi-core hardware, but a brief discussion of single-core settings (3× per-iteration cost) and memory footprint would be useful.

Clarity
The exposition is generally clear, with good motivation and connection to prior work. Several notational/indexing glitches hinder readability:
- Fix the likely typo in Eq. (5) as noted above.
- In Eq. (6), clarify indexing and normalization (sum limits K vs K+1, starting at 2 vs 1) to avoid confusion.
- Summarize key assumptions from the supplement in the main text.
- Provide pseudo-code in the main paper or a figure summarizing the extrapolation schedule and correlation.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a rigorous analysis (CLT, finite-time bias/MSE bounds) appears novel relative to prior SG-MCMC work. The use of correlated Brownian increments to control variance in the SG setting is a careful extension of duplicated diffusion ideas. The method is distinct from using higher-order integrators (e.g., splitting schemes) and from preconditioning/thermostat variants.

Significance
The work advances the accuracy-efficiency frontier for SG-MCMC by turning a first-order integrator into an effectively second-order scheme via extrapolation, without requiring specialized integrators. The analysis offers improved rates and guidance on step-size schedules. The empirical results on synthetic data are diagnostic and align with theory; the matrix factorization experiments demonstrate practical benefits and parallelizability on large datasets. Broader impact will depend on ease of implementation and availability of parallel resources; still, the approach is simple enough to be adopted in existing SG-MCMC codebases.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, general technique that can wrap around many SG-MCMC methods.
- Solid theoretical contributions: CLT, variance-minimizing correlation, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, improved convergence rates.
- Empirical validation on both controlled and large-scale tasks; demonstration on SGHMC as well.
- Parallelization advantage is practical and well supported in experiments.

Cons and suggestions
- Clarify and correct the update equations and estimator indexing; include key assumptions in the main text.
- Discuss computational trade-offs for single-core settings and memory use.
- Provide more detail on the stochastic gradient noise assumptions (e.g., conditional independence given θ, bounded variance) and whether sharing the same minibatch across chains affects finite-time variance.
- Include additional benchmarks beyond Gaussian/matrix factorization (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) and sensitivity to minibatch size.
- Report variability (confidence intervals) in empirical plots.
- Consider releasing code to aid reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
The paper is technically sound, clearly motivated, and offers a novel and useful contribution to SG-MCMC with both theoretical and practical benefits. With minor clarity fixes and expanded discussion of assumptions and compute trade-offs, it would be a strong addition to NeurIPS.
