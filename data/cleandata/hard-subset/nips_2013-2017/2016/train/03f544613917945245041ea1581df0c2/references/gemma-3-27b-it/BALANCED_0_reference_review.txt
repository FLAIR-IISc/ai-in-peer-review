This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD by employing Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two Langevin dynamics chains with different step sizes and combine their outputs to achieve a higher-order accuracy. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K^-4/5) MSE vs. O(K^-2/3) for SGLD) and demonstrate empirical improvements on both synthetic and real datasets, including a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel contribution. While RR extrapolation is well-known in numerical analysis, adapting it to the stochastic gradient setting and demonstrating its benefits is significant.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including convergence proofs and bounds on bias and MSE. The authors clearly state the assumptions required for their results.
* **Improved Convergence Rates:** The theoretical results showing faster convergence rates (O(K^-4/5)) compared to SGLD and its extensions are compelling.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC with RR) and a real-world dataset (matrix factorization) support the theoretical findings, demonstrating improved accuracy and efficiency. The comparisons with SGHMC and SGHMC-s are particularly insightful.
* **Parallelizability:** The algorithm's inherent parallelizability, stemming from the independent execution of the two chains, is a practical advantage, particularly for large-scale problems.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight the applicability of their technique to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Step Size Sensitivity:**  The experiments show significant sensitivity to the step size (γ).  While the theory provides guidance, practical tuning could be challenging. More discussion on robust step size selection strategies would be beneficial.  How does this sensitivity compare to SGLD?
* **Correlation of Gradients:** The paper mentions the importance of correlated gradients for optimal performance, but the discussion is somewhat brief.  A more in-depth analysis of the impact of gradient correlation, and potential strategies for explicitly controlling it, would strengthen the work. Is there a computational cost to maintaining these correlations?
* **Computational Cost:** While the algorithm is parallelizable, it inherently requires running two chains simultaneously, which doubles the computational cost per iteration. The paper needs a more comprehensive discussion of the trade-off between the increased computational cost and the improved convergence rate.  How does the total wall-clock time compare to SGLD for achieving a specific accuracy?  The ML-20M results are helpful, but more detailed comparisons across datasets would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:**  The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions (smoothness of U, ergodicity, etc.). A clearer discussion of the practical implications of these assumptions, and their potential limitations, would be useful.
* **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons with SGLD and SGHMC are present, it would be beneficial to compare SGRRLD against more recent and advanced SG-MCMC methods, if applicable.
* **Notation:** Some of the notation (e.g., use of subscript 'k' for both step size and iteration number) could be slightly ambiguous.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm that addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian inference – reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical guarantees and empirical validation provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The parallelizable nature of the algorithm makes it well-suited for large-scale applications.
* The work is a valuable contribution to the growing body of research on scalable Bayesian inference methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity to the step size and the increased computational cost (running two chains) could limit the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The analysis of gradient correlation could be more thorough.
* The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is novel, theoretically well-motivated, and empirically validated. While some practical considerations (step size sensitivity, computational cost) need to be addressed, the potential benefits of reduced bias and improved convergence rates are significant. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  A more detailed discussion of robust step size selection strategies.
2.  A deeper analysis of the impact of gradient correlation, potentially including practical strategies for controlling it.
3.  A more comprehensive comparison of the computational cost and wall-clock time of SGRRLD versus SGLD.
4.  A clearer discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions.
5. A comparison with more recent SG-MCMC methods, if applicable.
