Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, specifically SGLD, to reduce discretization bias from O(γ) to O(γ^2) while controlling variance via correlated Gaussian increments across two coupled chains with stepsizes γ and γ/2. The authors establish a CLT for the extrapolated estimator, along with non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds; they derive optimal step-size schedules (e.g., MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) with γ ∝ K^{-1/5}) improving over SGLD’s O(K^{-2/3}). They argue the method matches accuracy of higher-order integrators while using first-order Euler. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) suggest improved accuracy at comparable wall-clock time, and an extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) is illustrated.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and well-motivated use of RR extrapolation in the SG-MCMC setting; to my knowledge, RR hasn’t been explicitly applied to SG-MCMC previously.
- Clean theoretical results (CLT; non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds) with explicit rate improvements over first-order SG-MCMC.
- Sensible guidance on optimal step-size schedules for both fixed and decreasing step-size regimes.
- The role of correlation between Gaussian noises is clearly identified, with a principled optimal coupling (Σ = I).
- Empirical improvements demonstrated on synthetic and a real large-scale problem; an extension to SGHMC is shown.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Eq. (5) appears to use an implicit update: θ(γ/2)_{2k+1} depends on ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)_{2k+1}). This contradicts SGLD’s explicit Euler–Maruyama step and is almost surely a typo (should be ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)_{2k})). The second half-step also shows gradient evaluated at θ(γ/2)_{2k+1}, which is fine. Please correct; as written, the algorithm is ill-defined or materially different.
- The analysis hinges on very strong smoothness: existence of a Poisson solution g ∈ C^9, and “mild assumptions” on U and the gradient noise distribution L. These are not stated in the main text; only “see supplement.” The success of the RR expansion (Talay–Tubaro-type) in SG-MCMC requires precise dissipativity, growth, and moment conditions on the stochastic gradient noise; without these, claims like “Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” are not justified.
- The coupling of Brownian increments is essential: variance is minimized at Σ = I with Z^(γ) = (Z^(γ/2)_{2k-1}+Z^(γ/2)_{2k})/√2. However, the experimental section states the two chains “run in parallel as two independent processes.” It is unclear whether the required coupling (shared RNG ensuring (7)) is enforced. If not, the theory does not apply. This must be stated and demonstrated (e.g., ablation across Σ, including Σ = 0).
- Notation/indexing is awkward and error-prone: e.g., π̂_RK uses ∑_{k=2}^{K+1} γ_k in the denominator but weights numerator by γ_{k+1}; Γ^{(n)}_K is defined using γ_{k+1}. This off-by-one convention obscures proofs and reproducibility.
- Claim “Gaussian noise dominates stochastic gradient noise” is asserted after Theorem 1 but not quantitatively supported (e.g., explicit conditions on moments/tails of ∇Ũ−∇U and its interaction with step sizes). Many SG-MCMC settings have gradient noise that is not sub-Gaussian; how do results change under heavy-tailed or state-dependent noise?
- The complexity discussion is underdeveloped. SGRRLD requires three gradient evaluations per “macro-iteration” (1 coarse + 2 fine). Comparisons using “same wall-clock time” while leveraging parallelism are hardware-dependent and potentially unfair; one should also compare under equal gradient-evaluation budgets. What happens if SGLD is given 3× more iterations?

Relation to prior work and novelty
- The paper positions itself against higher-order integrators (e.g., [10]) but does not provide a thorough complexity-accuracy comparison. Higher-order methods can also be parallelized (e.g., across mini-batches); direct wall-clock comparisons may be misleading.
- RR/MLMC for SDE approximations (e.g., Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup 2015; multilevel RR [27]) should be discussed more deeply; connections to multilevel SG-MCMC (or variance reduction/control variates in SG-MCMC) are only mentioned briefly and not explored experimentally.
- Related works on bias reduction via control variates for SG-MCMC and variance-reduced stochastic gradients are not cited/compared.

Experiments
- Synthetic experiments focus primarily on posterior variance in a linear Gaussian model; this is a forgiving setting. Additional nonlinear/posterior-multimodal tests (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) would strengthen claims.
- No assessment of sampling quality beyond MSE of moments: no ESS, autocorrelation, or potential scale reduction; no diagnostics on mixing.
- SGHMC comparison: only a simple setting; no detailed tuning parity or robustness; no multivariate metrics.
- In matrix factorization, mini-batch sizes differ across datasets, but it’s not specified whether both chains use identical batch sizes and whether batch indices are coupled across chains (which could reduce variance). Please clarify.
- Lack of ablation on the coupling parameter Σ and on the effect of coupling minibatches across chains.
- No error bars or statistical significance reporting; figures appear to show means only.

Presentation and minor issues
- Numerous typos/grammar: “variace,” “Futher,” “twice more time,” “sence,” etc. Reference list has many inconsistencies (duplicate years, missing venues/pages, punctuation). The “th Conference…” header fragment remains in the body. These detract from clarity.
- The algorithm pseudocode is only in the supplement; key implementation details (coupling of Gaussians; mini-batch synchronization) should be in the main paper for reproducibility.

Questions for authors
1) Did you implement the exact coupling (7) in all experiments? If running as “independent processes,” how did you ensure Z^(γ) = (Z^(γ/2)_{2k-1}+Z^(γ/2)_{2k})/√2 across processes?
2) Are mini-batches synchronized across the two chains at each macro-iteration? Does synchronizing them affect variance or bias?
3) Please correct Eq. (5) and any other implicit-step typos; if intentional, explain why an implicit step is used and how it is computed.
4) Can you provide comparisons under equal gradient-evaluation budgets (e.g., SGLD run 3× longer)?
5) What are the precise assumptions on U and L (growth/dissipativity, Lipschitz, moments) that guarantee the CLT and non-asymptotic bounds? Are there results under heavy-tailed gradient noise?
6) Provide ablations varying Σ and empirical confirmation that Σ = I minimizes asymptotic variance in practice.

Pros
- Simple, potentially broadly applicable idea; theoretical improvements; promising initial empirical results.

Cons
- Critical ambiguity about the required coupling and an apparent algorithmic typo; heavy unstated assumptions; limited and potentially unfair empirical evaluation; presentation issues.

Recommendation: Reject (score: 5/10). The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs careful correction of algorithmic details, stronger and fairer experiments (including proper coupling and ablations), and clearer, self-contained statements of assumptions to meet NIPS standards. Confidence: 0.7.
