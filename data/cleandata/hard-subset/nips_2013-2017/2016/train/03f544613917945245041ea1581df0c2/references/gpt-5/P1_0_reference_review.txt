Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a new SG-MCMC method that reduces the discretization bias of SGLD by applying the Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The algorithm runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 in parallel up to a matched time horizon, and combines them with an RR-style estimator. To control variance, the method uses correlated Brownian increments between the two chains; the authors show the optimal choice is to reuse the same Brownian motion in a specific way. The main theoretical results are: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator under decreasing stepsizes with conditions on ΓK and Γ(3)K, showing asymptotic variance is minimized by full Brownian correlation; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) under constant stepsizes; (iii) resulting MSE convergence O(K−4/5), improving over SGLD’s O(K−2/3) with first-order integrators, and equal to the rates of some higher-order-integrator SG-MCMC schemes. Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models (including SGHMC) and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens 1M/10M/20M) demonstrate lower bias/MSE and better wall-clock RMSE when the two chains are run in parallel.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011] and subsequent theory on SG-MCMC bias/MSE (e.g., Teh, Vollmer, Zygalakis; Chen, Ding, Carin). RR extrapolation for SDEs is classical (Talay–Tubaro) and has been analyzed for invariant measures and duplicated diffusions (Lemaire, Pagès, Panloup). The novelty here is to apply RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, prove a CLT and finite-time bounds, and show that Brownian correlation remains the key to variance control in the SG setting. The paper also relates to higher-order/splitting integrators for SGHMC and demonstrates that RR with first-order integrators can match their theoretical accuracy.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are plausible and in line with known ULA/RR analyses, extended to the SG setting. The optimal α = 1/5 tradeoff and K−4/5 MSE rate are clearly derived. The variance-minimizing correlation structure for Brownian increments is well motivated and consistent with prior results. However, many technical assumptions are deferred to the supplement and appear strong (e.g., existence of a C9 solution to the Poisson equation), and it would help to state the main assumptions in the paper body and comment on their scope (e.g., strongly log-concave vs. general posteriors).
- Experiments: Synthetic Gaussian experiments convincingly validate the exponents and bias/MSE improvements, including a variant for SGHMC vs. higher-order SGHMC-s. The matrix factorization results show better wall-clock RMSE when exploiting parallelism. Some additional baselines and ablations would strengthen the case (see below).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear algorithmic idea and results. A few points need clarification/correction:
- Eq. (5) Chain 2 appears to have a typo: the first update uses θ(γ/2)2k+1 on both sides. It should update from θ(γ/2)2k to θ(γ/2)2k+1 with the gradient evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k.
- The RR estimator (6): a brief derivation explaining how the weighting corresponds to 2πγ/2(f) − πγ(f) would help avoid confusion.
- Please state in the main text the core assumptions used in Theorems 1–2 (smoothness, dissipativity, moment conditions on the gradient noise), and discuss how/when they hold for common Bayesian posteriors.
- Implementation details of Brownian correlation (Eq. 7–8) in practice should be briefly explained (e.g., reuse of random seeds, combining normals).

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC to achieve higher-order bias cancellation with first-order integrators appears novel and timely (relative to 2015–2016 work on high-order SG-MCMC). The identification and proof that Brownian correlation remains optimal in the SG setting is also a useful contribution. The extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) further supports generality.

Significance
- Theoretically, the paper shows that RR can upgrade first-order SG-MCMC to match the convergence rates of higher-order integrators, with clean finite-time bounds.
- Practically, it offers a simple lever—run two coupled stepsize chains and correlate Gaussian noise—that is easy to parallelize and can reduce error at the same wall-clock in multi-core settings.
- If adopted more broadly (e.g., with SGNHT, preconditioned SGLD), this could impact large-scale Bayesian sampling.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumptions: The regularity assumptions (e.g., C9, conditions ensuring Poisson equation solutions) are heavy; please clarify applicability beyond strongly log-concave settings and discuss nonconvex posteriors.
- Gradient noise: Bounds depend on moments of the stochastic gradient; it would be helpful to discuss how batch size B affects constants and whether coupling the mini-batches across the two chains can further reduce variance (you currently only require a common distribution L).
- Fairness of timing: The claimed equal wall-clock uses two parallel processes. For completeness, include a single-core comparison at matched gradient computations (SGRRLD does ≈2× gradient work) and show error vs. total gradient evaluations. This will help practitioners without parallel resources.
- Baselines: On real data, compare also against preconditioned SGLD (pSGLD), SGNHT/thermostats, and higher-order integrators for SGLD (not only SGHMC-s on synthetic). For matrix factorization, consider adding an optimization baseline (e.g., SGD/ALS) for RMSE context.
- Non-Gaussian tasks: All synthetic tasks are Gaussian; adding a logistic regression or a simple nonconjugate posterior would test robustness.
- Minor: Provide pseudo-code in the main text (not only supplement), and add guidance for choosing γ/α in practice from Theorem 2. Include confidence intervals in plots.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea with solid theory (CLT and finite-time bounds) and improved rates.
- Novel and broadly applicable technique; minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory; practical parallelism advantage.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong/deferred assumptions; limited discussion of applicability beyond well-behaved posteriors.
- Experimental baselines could be broader; fairness under single-core compute not shown.
- A few presentation issues/typos that may confuse readers.

Typos and nits
- Eq. (5) first line likely a typo (see above).
- Various minor typos: “variace”, “Futher”, “wheareas”, “th Conference…”, etc. Please proofread.
- Clarify that mini-batch sizes must match across chains (as you note) and recommend whether to reuse the same mini-batches.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a principled, general method to improve SG-MCMC with compelling theory and promising empirical results. Addressing the clarity/assumption issues and strengthening experimental comparisons would make it even stronger.

Reviewer confidence: High.
