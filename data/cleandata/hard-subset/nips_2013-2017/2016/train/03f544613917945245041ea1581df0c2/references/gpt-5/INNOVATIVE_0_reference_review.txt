Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a simple but bold idea: apply Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC (in particular SGLD) to cancel first-order discretization bias while keeping variance controlled via carefully correlated Brownian increments across two coupled chains run with step sizes γ and γ/2. The estimator is a time-average of 2× fine minus 1× coarse evaluations. The authors provide (i) a CLT with minimal variance attained when Brownian increments are perfectly coupled, (ii) non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds showing O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) bias and O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) MSE (constant step-size), yielding optimal finite-time rates O(K−2/3) for bias and O(K−4/5) for MSE, and (iii) guidance for decreasing step-sizes with optimal α = 1/5. Empirically, on a linear Gaussian model, SGRRLD reduces bias and MSE substantially vs SGLD; the idea transfers to SGHMC; and on large MovieLens matrix factorization benchmarks, SGRRLD attains better RMSE at the same wall-clock time by running the two chains in parallel.

Relation to prior work
RR extrapolation for SDEs is classical (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) and has been used to reduce bias in ULA and for invariant measures. High-order integrators for SG-MCMC have been proposed (e.g., Ma et al., Chen et al.). To my knowledge, applying RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, analyzing the interplay with gradient noise, and showing that with only first-order integrators one can recover the accuracy/rates associated with higher-order schemes, is new. The tie to correlated Brownian increments (and proof that identity coupling minimizes asymptotic variance) usefully adapts ideas from the diffusion literature to the stochastic-gradient setting.

Strengths
- Novel, elegant idea: RR extrapolation plus Brownian coupling for SG-MCMC. Conceptually simple and widely applicable beyond SGLD (demonstrated on SGHMC).
- Solid theory: CLT under standard Poisson-equation assumptions; non-asymptotic, finite-time bias/MSE bounds; explicit rate comparisons to SGLD; clear identification of optimal step-size schedules (constant and decaying).
- Practicality: parallel-friendly (two chains can run independently and then be combined), aligning with modern hardware.
- Empirical evidence matches theory on synthetic data (bias/MSE scaling) and shows tangible wall-clock gains on large-scale matrix factorization; comparison to a higher-order SGHMC variant supports the claim that SGRRLD reaches similar accuracy with first-order updates.

Weaknesses and questions
- Fairness/cost model: SGRRLD uses two chains (effectively 3 gradient evaluations per “iteration”) and leverages parallelism to match wall-clock time with SGLD. This is compelling for multi-core settings, but a clearer accounting of total gradient calls and equivalently parallelized SGLD baselines (e.g., parallel mini-batch computation) would strengthen the claims. A single-GPU/CPU budget comparison would be informative.
- Gradient noise coupling: The theory emphasizes Brownian coupling; the paper notes that the gradient estimators “can also be correlated,” but the practical implementation is not detailed. In practice, sharing mini-batches across levels can further reduce variance; conversely, using different subsamples may degrade the RR benefit. Please clarify whether you used common random mini-batches across the two chains and report sensitivity.
- Assumptions and scope: The CLT requires high smoothness (g ∈ C9) and standard ergodicity/Poisson assumptions; the paper defers most details to the supplement. It would help to state the main conditions (dissipativity, Lipschitz, growth) in the main text and discuss if common SG-MCMC settings (e.g., nonconvex posteriors, neural nets) satisfy them.
- Empirical breadth: The synthetic experiments are careful, but the real-data evaluation is limited to matrix factorization. Including a Bayesian neural network or logistic regression with large N would broaden applicability, and adding MCMC diagnostics (effective sample size per second for posterior means/variances) would better connect to sampling quality.
- Coefficient choice: The RR weights are implicitly implemented via two fine steps and one coarse step per γk; a short derivation in the main text to show that the estimator corresponds to 2πγ/2 − πγ would aid clarity.
- Comparison to multilevel methods: The paper mentions MLMC as future work. A brief discussion of how SGRRLD relates to MLMC estimators (and Rhee–Glynn style debiasing) would be valuable.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and credible, with the right dependence on γ and K, and the variance-minimizing coupling is well motivated. Proofs are deferred, but the main statements and conditions are appropriately referenced. Experiments are positive and align with theory, though broader tasks and stronger baselines would help.

Clarity
Overall well written. Some notation is dense and a few typos exist. Include the pseudocode in the main text if space permits. Explicitly list the key assumptions in Section 4. Clarify the implementation details for gradient subsampling and coupling.

Originality
High. While RR for SDEs is known, its adaptation to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, the variance analysis under Brownian coupling, and the demonstration that first-order integrators can match the accuracy of higher-order SG-MCMC via extrapolation constitute a fresh and impactful contribution.

Significance
High. This is a practical, general technique that can be dropped into many SG-MCMC samplers to reduce bias with minimal algorithmic complexity and natural parallelization. The improved finite-time rates and empirical gains suggest real utility.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable idea with solid theory and gains.
- Bridges classical numerical analysis (RR) and modern SG-MCMC.
- Parallel-friendly, aligning with current computing trends.

Cons against acceptance
- Experimental scope limited; fairness under fixed compute budget could be better quantified.
- Practical guidance on gradient noise coupling and implementation could be clearer.

Recommendation
Accept (strong novelty with promising theory and practical impact; minor experimental limitations are outweighed by the conceptual contribution).

Confidence
High.
