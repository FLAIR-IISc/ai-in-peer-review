

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel extension to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to the SGLD algorithm, running two chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to reduce bias while controlling variance. The authors claim that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴) respectively, compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²), and achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The paper provides theoretical analysis with central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bounds, and validates the approach with experiments on synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization.

The work builds on existing SG-MCMC literature (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and extends the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) to the stochastic gradient setting. The paper makes a compelling theoretical claim that a first-order method can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with detailed proofs (in the supplement) establishing the improved convergence rates. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are technically sound and well-motivated.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The approach is parallelizable, which is a significant practical advantage for modern distributed computing architectures. The paper correctly identifies that the two chains can be run independently, making it well-suited for parallel hardware.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets convincingly support the theoretical claims. The matrix factorization results on MovieLens datasets clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of SGRRLD over SGLD in terms of accuracy per wall-clock time.

4. **Generality**: The authors correctly note that the technique can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC), and they provide evidence for this with SGRRHMC.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Computational Cost Mischaracterization**: The paper claims "their wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" (Section 5.2), which is confusing. Running two chains in parallel should approximately double the computational cost per iteration, not keep wall-clock time the same. The paper needs to clarify this point. If the two chains are run in parallel on two processors, the wall-clock time should be roughly the same as SGLD running on one processor, but the total computational cost (e.g., CPU time) would be doubled. The paper should explicitly address this and clarify the actual computational trade-off.

2. **Limited Comparison with Higher-Order Methods**: The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" but doesn't provide direct comparisons with those methods. For example, Chen et al. (2015) proposed higher-order SG-MCMC methods that achieve similar rates without needing two chains. The paper should compare SGRRLD directly with these methods to demonstrate its practical advantages.

3. **Memory Requirements**: The paper doesn't address the memory overhead of running two chains in parallel. For large-scale problems, maintaining two sets of parameters could significantly increase memory requirements, which might be prohibitive for very large models. This is a practical limitation that should be discussed.

4. **Assumptions in Theoretical Analysis**: The theoretical results depend on several technical assumptions (e.g., smoothness of the potential function, existence of a solution to the Poisson equation). The paper doesn't discuss how realistic these assumptions are for practical applications, especially with complex models like deep neural networks.

5. **Step Size Selection Guidance**: While the paper discusses optimal step size choices theoretically, it doesn't provide clear practical guidance for practitioners. For example, the paper suggests γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵ for MSE but doesn't discuss how to choose γ in practice for a given problem.

## Technical Concerns

1. **Equation (5) and Chain Running**: The description of how the two chains are run (Equation 5) is confusing. The paper states "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice more time than the other one," but the equations show that for each iteration of the first chain (with step size γ), the second chain (with step size γ/2) takes two steps. This means that for K iterations of the first chain, the second chain takes 2K iterations, not "twice more time" as stated. The paper should clarify this point.

2. **Correlation of Brownian Motions**: The paper's justification for using perfectly correlated Brownian increments (Equation 7) is based on [23], but the connection to SG-MCMC isn't fully explained. The paper should better justify why this correlation choice is optimal in the stochastic gradient setting, as opposed to the deterministic SDE setting.

3. **Comparison with Multi-Level Monte Carlo**: The paper mentions future work on multi-level Monte Carlo approaches [27], but doesn't discuss whether these would be more efficient than the proposed RR approach. This is a missed opportunity to position the work more clearly in the broader context of variance reduction techniques.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature with clear practical implications. The paper makes a significant claim that a first-order method can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods, which is valuable for practitioners who want to avoid the complexity of implementing higher-order integrators.

However, the paper's claims about computational efficiency need to be clarified. The statement that "wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" is misleading without context about the computational resources used. If two chains are run in parallel on two processors, the wall-clock time might be similar to SGLD on one processor, but the total computational cost would be higher. The paper should explicitly address this trade-off.

The experimental results are convincing, but the paper could strengthen its case by directly comparing against higher-order SG-MCMC methods from Chen et al. (2015) and others. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether SGRRLD provides a net advantage over existing methods.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC field with rigorous analysis and convincing experimental validation. The main concerns are about clarifying the computational trade-offs and providing more direct comparisons with existing higher-order methods. These can be addressed in a revision.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Clarify the computational cost discussion in Section 5.2, explicitly stating that while wall-clock time may be similar (with parallel implementation), the total computational cost is approximately doubled.

2. Add a comparison with higher-order SG-MCMC methods (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) in the experiments to directly validate the claim that SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to higher-order methods.

3. Discuss the memory requirements for running two chains in parallel and how this might impact scalability.

4. Provide more practical guidance for step size selection in real-world applications.

5. Clarify the relationship between the RR extrapolation for SDEs (Section 2.2) and the specific implementation in SGRRLD (Section 3), to help readers understand the connection.

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and would be a strong addition to NIPS if these minor clarifications are made. The theoretical results are sound, the practical implications are significant, and the experimental validation is thorough.
