### Summary

This paper introduces a novel algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), for reducing the discretization bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs linearly to cancel out the leading-order error term. A key technical contribution is the use of correlated noise between the two chains to control the variance of the resulting estimator, which would otherwise be problematic. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD achieves a mean-squared-error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This higher-order accuracy is achieved using only first-order integrators, offering an elegant alternative to the development of more complex, higher-order SDE solvers for SG-MCMC. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold Conceptual Contribution:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is, to my knowledge, entirely new. It represents a significant conceptual leap. Instead of making incremental improvements to gradient estimators or integrator dynamics, the authors have imported a powerful idea from classical numerical analysis and cleverly adapted it to the challenges of stochastic gradients and MCMC. This is a bold and creative approach to bias reduction that opens up a new direction for research in this area.

2.  **Strong and Significant Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical justification for the proposed method. The main result—improving the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for SGRRLD—is a substantial theoretical advance. The analysis correctly identifies the need to correlate the noise terms to manage variance and provides a Central Limit Theorem that characterizes the asymptotic behavior of the estimator. This is precisely the kind of foundational work that can inspire follow-up research.

3.  **Generality and Elegance:** The proposed RR scheme is not just a one-off trick for SGLD. The authors convincingly argue, and demonstrate with an experiment on SGHMC, that the technique is generic and can be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms. The ability to achieve higher-order accuracy using only simple, first-order Euler-Maruyama updates is particularly elegant. It sidesteps the complexities of deriving and implementing bespoke higher-order integrators, which can be cumbersome, especially for constrained or non-Euclidean parameter spaces.

4.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** The algorithm is inherently parallel. The two chains required for extrapolation can be run on separate cores with minimal communication overhead. The authors make a fair comparison in their experiments by measuring performance against wall-clock time, showing that on modern hardware, SGRRLD can be significantly more efficient than SGLD. The positive results on the large-scale MovieLens datasets demonstrate that this is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical method with real-world benefits.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Increased Computational Cost:** While parallelizable, the method's total computational cost is roughly three times that of a single SGLD run (one step with size γ and two steps with size γ/2). In scenarios where parallel resources are unavailable, this could be a significant drawback. While the wall-clock time comparisons are fair, the paper could be more explicit about this trade-off in total FLOPS. However, given the novelty and performance gains, this feels more like a known cost than a fundamental flaw.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The experiments convincingly show that SGRRLD outperforms SGLD. The comparison with a higher-order integrator method (SGHMC-s) is also insightful but confined to the synthetic Gaussian model. To fully establish SGRRLD as a competitive alternative to higher-order integrators, a comparison on the more complex large-scale matrix factorization task or another challenging problem (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) would have strengthened the paper's claims.

3.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The method introduces no new hyperparameters, which is a strength. However, the theoretical results suggest that the optimal step-size decay rate is different from that of SGLD (α=1/5 for MSE vs. α=1/3). This implies that practitioners might need to re-tune existing schedules, though the experiments show that even with a fixed step size, the benefits are substantial.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that introduces a truly novel and powerful idea to the scalable MCMC literature. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to the scope of the evaluation than to the core contribution. The conceptual elegance of achieving higher-order convergence with first-order methods, the strong theoretical backing, and the demonstrated practical utility make this a standout submission. It is exactly the kind of bold, creative, and significant work that NIPS should showcase.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly original idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Provides a significant, theoretically-backed improvement in the convergence rate.
*   The method is generic, elegant, and applicable to a broad class of algorithms.
*   It is naturally parallel and shows strong empirical performance on a large-scale problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The total computational cost is higher than standard SGLD.
*   The experimental comparison against state-of-the-art higher-order integrators could be more extensive.

I weigh the arguments for acceptance far more heavily. The introduction of a fundamentally new technique for error reduction in this field is much more valuable than an incremental improvement. I strongly recommend acceptance.
