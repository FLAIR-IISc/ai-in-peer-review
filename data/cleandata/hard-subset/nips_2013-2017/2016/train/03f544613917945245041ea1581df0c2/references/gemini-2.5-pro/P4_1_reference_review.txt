This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to improve the convergence rate of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators, like the one used in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). SGLD, a foundational SG-MCMC method, suffers from a bias of order O(γ), where γ is the step size, limiting its mean-squared-error (MSE) convergence rate to O(K⁻²/³). While prior work, such as Chen et al. (NIPS 2015), has shown that higher-order integrators can improve this rate, they often introduce additional computational complexity.

The proposed approach is based on Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis. The authors apply this technique by running two parallel SGLD chains: one with step size γ and another with step size γ/2 (run for twice the number of steps to match the time horizon). A new estimator is then constructed by taking a specific linear combination of the outputs of these two chains. This procedure is designed to cancel the leading O(γ) term in the bias. To manage the variance of the combined estimator, the method employs correlated Gaussian noise for the two chains, an idea previously explored for SDEs with exact gradients (Lemaire et al., 2015).

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. It establishes a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) and derives non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²) and an MSE of O(γ⁴), leading to an improved MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵). This rate matches that of methods using second-order integrators but is achieved using only the simpler first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The authors validate their theoretical claims with experiments on both synthetic and real-world data, showing significant performance gains over SGLD and demonstrating the generality of the approach by applying it to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC).

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are strong and well-supported by a formal analysis that includes both a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic error bounds. The connection to the existing literature on numerical methods for SDEs provides a solid foundation for the approach. The experimental methodology is sound, using a synthetic Gaussian model with a known posterior to precisely measure bias and MSE, which directly corroborates the theoretical rates. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments demonstrate the practical utility of the method in a realistic setting. The authors are careful to evaluate their method under fair conditions, comparing wall-clock time to account for the parallel nature of SGRRLD.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework—is novel. While RR extrapolation is a well-known technique, its adaptation and analysis in the context of stochastic gradients, where noise arises from both the discretization and the data subsampling, is a new and valuable contribution. This work presents an elegant alternative to using higher-order integrators for bias reduction. The demonstration that the technique can be generalized to other SG-MCMC algorithms like SGHMC further underscores its originality and potential impact.

**Significance:** The results are significant for the SG-MCMC community. Improving the convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a substantial theoretical and practical advance. The method is particularly appealing because of its simplicity and amenability to parallelization. Since the two chains can be run completely independently, the method can achieve wall-clock time speedups on modern multi-core hardware, as demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments. This makes SGRRLD a practical and readily implementable tool for practitioners seeking to improve the accuracy and efficiency of large-scale Bayesian inference without resorting to more complex numerical integrators.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides clear motivation and context. The preliminaries section efficiently covers the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely in Section 3. The main theoretical results are stated clearly, and the experimental sections are easy to follow. The figures effectively illustrate the key performance improvements of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Assumptions and Proofs in Supplement:** The technical conditions required for the theorems to hold (on the potential U, test function f, and gradient estimator law L) are not stated in the main paper and are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice due to space constraints, it prevents a full assessment of the theory's generality from the main text alone.

2.  **Computational Overhead:** The method inherently requires roughly double the computational resources of a standard SGLD run (one step of size γ and two of γ/2). The authors correctly argue this is mitigated by parallelization, leading to similar wall-clock times. However, for users on single-core systems or in environments where parallel processing is not available, the method would be twice as slow. This trade-off could be stated more explicitly.

3.  **Clarity of Estimator Formulation:** While the paper is generally clear, the pseudo-code for the algorithm is in the supplement. Including a concise algorithm box in the main paper would enhance reproducibility and make the implementation details immediately accessible.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and elegant application of a classical numerical method to a modern machine learning problem.
*   **Significance:** Provides a significant improvement in the theoretical convergence rate of SG-MCMC methods, supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   **Practicality:** The method is simple to implement on top of existing SG-MCMC algorithms and is naturally suited for parallel execution, offering real-world performance gains.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The work is theoretically rigorous, with formal proofs of consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic error bounds.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The computational cost is doubled, which is a key trade-off that relies on the availability of parallel hardware to be effective in terms of wall-clock time.
*   The main paper omits the technical assumptions underlying the theorems, which are crucial for judging the scope of the results.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel, significant, and practical method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms, backed by solid theory and compelling experiments.
