This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aiming to improve the bias of SGLD through Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea—running two chains with different step sizes and combining their estimates—is clever and builds upon established numerical analysis techniques applied to SDEs. It’s a well-motivated approach, leveraging the known benefits of higher-order integration methods while aiming to retain computational efficiency. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, providing convergence guarantees and demonstrating improved rates compared to standard SGLD. The experimental evaluation, encompassing both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task, provides empirical support for the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel contribution. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to the stochastic gradient setting and MCMC algorithms hasn’t been previously explored (to the best of my knowledge). This is a significant departure from the incremental improvements often seen in submissions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a reasonably thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. The convergence analysis provides a strong foundation for understanding the algorithm's behavior.
* **Potential for Efficiency:** The parallelizable nature of SGRRLD (running the two chains independently) is a valuable asset, particularly for large-scale problems.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not exhaustive, demonstrate improvements over SGLD and, importantly, show competitive performance with higher-order integrators like SGHMC-s, suggesting that the proposed method effectively captures some of the advantages of more complex schemes.
* **Generality:** The authors correctly point out the technique’s applicability beyond SGLD to other SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** The theoretical benefits are contingent on careful tuning of step sizes and the correlation matrix Σ. The sensitivity of performance to these parameters isn't fully explored in the experiments. A more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of Σ and its optimal setting would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus primarily on Gaussian models and matrix factorization. Expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse and challenging Bayesian inference problems (e.g., deep learning models) would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Overhead:** While parallelizable, the method inherently requires running two chains, which doubles the computational cost per iteration. The paper briefly acknowledges this but doesn't provide a detailed cost-benefit analysis comparing it to using a higher-order integrator directly.
* **Step Size Selection:** The method's efficiency appears dependent on accurately setting the step size, based on the theoretical results. A more practical guide for step size selection in non-ideal scenarios would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to SG-MCMC with a strong theoretical foundation.
* The empirical results provide preliminary evidence supporting the theoretical claims.
* The potential for parallelization and improved efficiency is significant, particularly for large-scale applications.
* The work opens up new avenues for research in combining numerical analysis techniques with stochastic gradient-based inference.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods across a broad range of problems.
* The practical considerations surrounding parameter tuning and computational overhead aren't fully addressed.
* A more detailed comparison with alternative high-order integrators is needed to justify the trade-offs involved.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong submission. The novelty and theoretical contributions are substantial. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the paper demonstrates a clear potential for advancing the field of scalable Bayesian inference. I believe the benefits of this novel approach outweigh its current limitations. Despite the weaknesses, the boldness of the idea and the clear theoretical grounding make it a valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validations given the innovative nature of the work and the potential impact it could have. A minor revision addressing the suggestions regarding step-size sensitivity and a slightly broader experimental scope could further strengthen the paper.
