Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, specifically SGLD, by simultaneously running two discretizations with steps γ and γ/2 and combining their time-averaged estimators. The key idea is to cancel the O(γ) discretization bias using RR while keeping variance controlled via a carefully correlated Gaussian noise construction across the two chains (Brownian increments sharing). The authors provide a CLT for the estimator, non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, and identify optimal step-size schedules. They also demonstrate the approach on synthetic Gaussian models, compare to SGHMC and a higher-order SGHMC integrator, and evaluate on three MovieLens datasets for matrix factorization, exploiting parallel execution of the two chains.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on classical RR extrapolation for SDE weak error (Talay–Tubaro) and its use for invariant measures (Lamberton–Pagès; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup), and adapts the correlated increment construction argued to minimize variance. Within SG-MCMC, it complements analyses of SGLD and higher-order integrators (Teh et al., Chen et al., Ma et al.), and relates to ideas in multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) and variance reduction. To my knowledge, applying RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients is novel and well-motivated.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, generic idea (applicable beyond SGLD; a SGHMC variant is shown).
- Theoretical contributions: CLT with variance minimization via correlated Brownian increments, non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds showing O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) bias and O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) MSE, and rate-optimal step-size schedules (α=1/5 for MSE, α=1/3 for bias).
- Clear performance improvements over SGLD on synthetic tasks and in a large-scale matrix factorization application; competitive to higher-order SGHMC for suitable γ.
- Parallel/distributed amenability by design.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Potentially serious typo in Chain 2 update (Eq. (5)): the first half-step uses ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) though θ(γ/2)2k+1 is the updated variable; Euler’s method should evaluate the gradient at θ(γ/2)2k. If this is not a typo and an implicit step is intended, the algorithm and proofs need modification and a solver is required. Please clarify and correct.
- Off-by-one/time-averaging indices: the estimator denominator is ∑k=2 K+1 γk while the numerator uses ∑k=1 K γk+1. This is confusing and appears inconsistent with the stated time horizon ∑k=1 K γk. A clear derivation is needed, or just use ∑k=1 K γk consistently.
- Theorems rely on heavy regularity (e.g., Poisson solution g ∈ C9 and 7th-order Taylor expansions) with “appropriate conditions” deferred to the supplement. The main text should clearly state key assumptions on U (dissipativity, smoothness), the gradient noise distribution L (moment, independence, conditional structure), and the mixing/ergodicity conditions to make the results interpretable and checkable.
- The statement “the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” is strong; the conditions under which this holds (e.g., bounded variance of the gradient estimator uniformly in θ; how errors propagate through the RR combination) should be spelled out. In practice, with large gradient noise (small B), this may fail.
- The result “asymptotic variance O(γ4)” is stated in the introduction but the finite-K MSE also contains the 1/(Kγ) term that will dominate for small γ or finite K; the text should be consistent about which “variance” is being referred to (discretization variance vs Monte Carlo variance).
- The role of correlated stochastic gradients is underexplored. You note the minibatch sizes must match but claim correlation does not impact asymptotics. Given RR cancellation involves matching leading-order weak error terms, using identical minibatches across coupled steps could substantially reduce variance; either analyze or empirically study this choice.

Algorithmic and experimental issues
- Parallelism fairness: SGRRLD is run as two processes in parallel on a quad-core CPU; SGLD is reported as “inherently sequential,” which is not entirely fair. SGLD iterations are sequential but per-iteration computations can leverage parallel BLAS/OMP and data parallelism; also one could run two independent SGLD chains in parallel for comparable resource usage. Please report thread/process counts and BLAS threading for both methods, and compare per-gradient runtime and total floating-point work to isolate algorithmic gains from parallelism.
- Mini-batch sizes across datasets are inconsistent ratios (N/10, N/50, N/500); this changes the stochastic gradient noise level and may favor SGRRLD differently across datasets. Justify these choices, or sweep B.
- Tuning: Some synthetic experiments infer constants (γ?b, γ?M) from prior runs; this risks cherry-picking. A more systematic procedure or cross-validation should be used, or hyperparameters fixed a priori.
- Baselines: For overdamped Langevin, a comparison to the higher-order integrators for SGLD (e.g., symmetric splitting or BAOAB-like variants as in Chen et al. 2015; Ma et al. 2015) is missing. Only SGHMC-s is considered. This is important given the claim “achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators.”
- Metrics: Synthetic experiments focus mainly on posterior variance MSE; including means and other functionals f(θ) would better test the generality of the extrapolation benefit.
- Practical guidance: The paper does not discuss the wall-clock trade-off when parallelism is unavailable (SGRRLD costs roughly 3 gradient evaluations per macro-step). A fair single-thread speed vs accuracy plot would be informative.

Presentation and formatting
- The stray line “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” should be removed.
- Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted (missing years/pages; inconsistent punctuation and capitalization).
- Minor notational inconsistencies: e.g., the use of √2γ vs √γ in (4)–(5) is correct but could be explicitly tied to the η=γ/2 choice; explain the rationale once to avoid confusion.
- Typo: “variace” → “variance”; “Futher” → “Further”; “mean squarederror” → “mean-squared error”.
- Clarify the dependence of constants C in Theorem 2 on moments of ∇Ũ and state explicit moment assumptions (order needed).

Questions for the authors
- Please confirm and correct the Chain 2 first half-step gradient argument.
- Do you share the same minibatch between the two half-steps at level γ/2 and with the γ step (i.e., a coupled subsampling scheme)? If not, what is the impact?
- Can you provide (even informally) the expression for σR^2 and its dependence on Σ in the main text?
- How sensitive are results to B and to using decreasing step sizes in practice?
- Can you include an overdamped higher-order SGLD baseline?

Pros
- Simple, generic idea with clear theoretical and practical appeal.
- Solid theoretical results (CLT; non-asymptotics) extending RR to SG-MCMC.
- Demonstrated gains on synthetic and large-scale applications; parallel-friendly.

Cons
- A likely error in the algorithm update and indexing ambiguities.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary assumptions; unclear scope of validity.
- Experimental fairness and baseline coverage need improvement.
- Presentation/formatting issues and several minor typos.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely impactful, but the algorithmic typo, lack of clarity on assumptions and coupling of stochastic gradients, and experimental concerns (parallelism fairness; missing baselines) need to be addressed. With these fixes and a clearer empirical protocol, I would be inclined to accept.
