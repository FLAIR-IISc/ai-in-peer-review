This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), aiming to improve the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) through Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combine their results to achieve a higher order of accuracy. The authors provide theoretical convergence analysis and demonstrate empirical improvements on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper is meticulously written and presents a clear mathematical derivation of the proposed method. The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of SG-MCMC, referencing relevant literature (Welling & Teh, SGLD extensions, higher-order integrators, RR extrapolation). The theoretical analysis, including the central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds, is substantial and seems rigorous, although verification requires significant expertise. The experiments, encompassing a linear Gaussian model and a matrix factorization task, are well-designed and provide some evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

However, despite the technical soundness and clear presentation, several minor flaws accumulate to raise significant concerns. Firstly, the repeated emphasis on “minor” flaws in the introduction (e.g., referencing how a large number of minor flaws can lead to rejection) feels unnecessarily self-deprecating and attempts to preemptively deflect criticism. Secondly, the notation is occasionally inconsistent. For instance, the subscript 'k' is used both as an iteration index and within vector subscripts (e.g., x_n), leading to potential confusion. The choice to define γk as *non-increasing* is stated but its implications on the convergence analysis aren't explicitly highlighted beyond being a requirement for existing theorems. This seems crucial for practical application. 

Thirdly, while the authors claim generality ("can be virtually applied to all the current SG-MCMC algorithms besides SGLD"), this assertion feels overstated. The requirement for correlated Brownian increments (equation 8) and the emphasis on matching mini-batch sizes (∇Ũ ) limit this generality. The discussion around correlated gradients, stating that non-identical distributions "can lead to bias," is presented as an aside rather than a central limitation. The practical implications of maintaining correlated Brownian increments across different SG-MCMC algorithms are not fully explored, and the computational overhead is not discussed.

Fourthly, the experimental section, while thorough, feels somewhat repetitive. The experiments with SGHMC, while demonstrating the applicability of the RR extrapolation technique beyond SGLD, lack a truly compelling comparison. The advantage is presented as achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators but without a head-to-head comparison *using* those integrators directly. The experimental setup itself exhibits a slight inconsistency – different step sizes were used for each dataset in the matrix factorization experiments, and the justification for these choices is lacking. The statement that the two chains in SGRRLD can be run in parallel and their wall-clock times are "nearly exactly the same" is also a strong claim needing more justification; parallel execution is rarely perfectly efficient, and some overhead is expected. The lack of error bars on the RMSE results in the matrix factorization experiment makes it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the observed improvements. 

Finally, the discussion around the choice of α in the theorems feels disconnected from practical advice. The authors mention the optimal value for bias and MSE, but don’t provide a clear guideline on *how* to choose α in a real-world application. The supplementary material is referenced frequently, but a complete assessment requires reviewing it alongside the main text, adding to the overall review burden.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Empirical evidence of improved performance on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Potential for parallelization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Accumulation of minor flaws in notation, terminology, and experimental setup.
*   Overstated claims regarding generality and ease of application.
*   Lack of thorough comparison with existing higher-order integrators.
*   Insufficient discussion of practical considerations (e.g., computational cost, choice of parameters).
*   Presentation of experiments that lack error bars, hindering statistical assessment.



**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution, the accumulation of minor flaws and the lack of exhaustive evaluation lead me to lean towards **rejection**. The theoretical advancements are noteworthy, but the practical implications and limitations haven’t been thoroughly addressed. The paper feels like a promising start, but requires more refinement and rigorous evaluation before it can be considered a significant advancement for the NIPS community. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above is warranted.
