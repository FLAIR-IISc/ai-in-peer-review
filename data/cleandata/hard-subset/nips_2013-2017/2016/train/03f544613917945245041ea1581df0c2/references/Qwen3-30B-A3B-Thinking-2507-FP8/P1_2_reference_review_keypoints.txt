- Summary: Proposes SGRRLD, applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) to reduce bias and achieve higher-order convergence without higher-order integrators.

- Strength 1 (Novelty): Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a clean conceptual advance.

- Strength 2 (Theory): Rigorous theoretical analysis with proofs, CLTs, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, well-grounded in prior numerical analysis literature.

- Strength 3 (Practicality): Clear implementation recipe (correlated Brownian increments) and pseudo-code linking theory to practice.

- Strength 4 (Experiments): Comprehensive experiments on synthetic and large-scale MovieLens tasks that support theoretical claims and show improved accuracy over SGLD/SGHMC.

- Strength 5 (Parallelism): Naturally parallel (two independent chains), making it suitable for distributed computing.

- Weakness 1 (Assumptions): Technical assumptions on U, gradient estimator L, and test-function smoothness are buried in supplement and not clearly justified in the main text.

- Weakness 2 (Practical trade-offs): Limited discussion of practical trade-offs, especially higher per-iteration cost (two chains) and lack of wall-clock time analysis across settings.

- Weakness 3 (Sensitivity): No sensitivity analysis for the step-size ratio (γ vs γ/2) to assess optimality or robustness.

- Weakness 4 (Generality): Claim of broad applicability to other SG-MCMC variants is not thoroughly demonstrated beyond a brief SGHMC example.

- Weakness 5 (Comparisons): Missing direct comparisons with other bias-reduction/acceleration methods (e.g., Chen et al. 2015, SGHMC-s).

- Quality: Technically sound, complete contribution with well-structured proofs and appropriate experimental methodology.

- Clarity: Well-organized and clearly written; some technical details appropriately deferred to supplement but could include more implementation specifics.

- Originality: Genuinely novel in applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, distinguishing it from prior work and achieving higher-order rates with first-order integrators.

- Significance: Significant theoretical and practical advance (MSE rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs SGLD O(K⁻²/³)) with relevance to large-scale Bayesian inference and distributed computation.

- Suggestion 1: Explicitly state key technical assumptions for main theorems in the paper body.

- Suggestion 2: Expand experiments to compare with higher-order integrator methods (e.g., SGHMC-s) on MovieLens.

- Suggestion 3: Add sensitivity analysis for different step-size ratios beyond γ/2.

- Suggestion 4: Clarify and justify why mini-batch sizes must match across both chains.

- Suggestion 5: Quantify actual wall-clock parallel speedup rather than qualitative claims.

- Final recommendation: Accept — significant, well-supported contribution with minor issues addressable by revision.
