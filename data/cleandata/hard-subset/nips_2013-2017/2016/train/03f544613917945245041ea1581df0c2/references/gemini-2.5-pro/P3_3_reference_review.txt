This paper introduces the Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm, a novel method for improving the accuracy of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) samplers. The core idea is to apply the classical Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, widely used in numerical analysis for solving differential equations, to the SGLD algorithm. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their estimators to cancel the leading-order O(γ) bias term inherent in the Euler-Maruyama discretization. A key insight is the use of perfectly correlated Brownian increments for the two chains, which effectively controls the variance of the combined estimator. The authors provide a strong theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds, showing that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This brings the accuracy of a first-order integrator on par with more complex higher-order integrators. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Contribution:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and clever idea. It elegantly solves the problem of discretization bias, a major limitation of SGLD and related methods, by leveraging a well-established numerical technique. Achieving higher-order accuracy using only a simple first-order integrator is a significant conceptual and practical advantage.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper is supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) detailing the asymptotic behavior and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derivation of the improved MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) and the identification of the optimal step-size decay schedule (α=1/5) are key theoretical results that clearly establish the superiority of SGRRLD over SGLD.

3.  **Practicality and Parallelizability:** A major strength of the proposed method is its inherent suitability for parallel computation. Since the two chains required for RR extrapolation can be run independently, SGRRLD can achieve its improved accuracy in roughly the same wall-clock time as a single SGLD chain, provided two cores are available. This is a crucial feature for modern large-scale machine learning and was effectively demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is well-structured and convincing.
    *   The experiments on the linear Gaussian model, where the ground truth is known, provide clear and direct validation of the theoretical claims, showing a dramatic reduction in both bias (O(γ) to O(γ²)) and MSE (O(γ²) to O(γ⁴)).
    *   The experiment verifying the optimal convergence rate by varying the step-size decay exponent α is an excellent piece of analysis that directly supports the theory.
    *   The application of the RR technique to SGHMC demonstrates the generality of the approach beyond just SGLD.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show that the theoretical improvements translate into tangible real-world benefits, with SGRRLD achieving lower RMSE faster than SGLD in terms of wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Areas for improvement):**

1.  **Computational Overhead:** While parallelization reduces the wall-clock time, the total computational cost of SGRRLD is higher than SGLD. To advance the simulation to the same effective time horizon, SGRRLD requires one step of the γ-chain and two steps of the γ/2-chain, resulting in three times the number of gradient computations compared to one step of SGLD. The paper emphasizes the wall-clock time benefit of parallelization but could be more explicit about this trade-off in total compute resources. A comparison against SGLD run for 3x the iterations (equivalent total compute) would provide a more complete picture of the efficiency gains.

2.  **Correlation of Stochastic Gradients:** The analysis elegantly handles the correlation of the Brownian noise but is less clear on the gradient noise. The authors state that the sequences of gradient estimators can be correlated but do not explore this. It seems plausible that correlating the mini-batches (e.g., using the same data subsample for the γ step and the first γ/2 step) could further reduce variance. A discussion of this possibility would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Clarity on Assumptions:** The theoretical results are presented under "appropriate conditions" on the potential U, test function f, and gradient estimator distribution L, with details deferred to the supplement. While this is standard practice, a brief, high-level description of these assumptions in the main text (e.g., smoothness of U, finite moments of the gradient noise) and how they compare to standard assumptions for SGLD would improve the paper's self-containedness.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The originality lies in the clever adaptation of a classic numerical method to a modern machine learning problem. The work is technically sound, with rigorous theory and comprehensive experiments that validate the claims. The resulting algorithm, SGRRLD, offers a demonstrable improvement in accuracy over standard methods and is well-suited for parallel implementation, making it highly significant for practitioners.

The main weakness is the increased total computational cost, though this is mitigated by parallelization. This trade-off could be discussed more explicitly. Despite this minor point, the strengths of the paper in terms of novelty, theoretical depth, and practical relevance are overwhelming. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and advances the state of the art. I strongly recommend acceptance.
