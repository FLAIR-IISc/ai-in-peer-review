This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aiming to reduce bias by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD. The core idea is to run two Langevin chains with differing step sizes and combine their results, leveraging correlated Brownian motions for variance control. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to standard SGLD and present experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets supporting their claims.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for reducing bias in SG-MCMC is sound, and the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a natural extension of existing numerical techniques. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears rigorous and attempts to justify the proposed algorithm’s improvements. The experimental setup is reasonable, covering both a simple Gaussian model for validation and a more complex matrix factorization task for practical demonstration.

However, despite the clarity of presentation, I find myself unconvinced of the overall significance of this work. While the theoretical improvements – achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators with a first-order method – are appealing, the practical benefits demonstrated are marginal and come with increased complexity. The authors state the algorithm is “well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures,” but this adaptation appears inherent in running the two chains independently and doesn’t represent a unique contribution. 

My main concern is the relatively modest performance gains observed in the experiments. In the synthetic Gaussian model, improvements are shown, but the setup feels contrived and may not generalize well. The matrix factorization experiments demonstrate improvement, but the difference in RMSE is not substantial enough to warrant a significant shift in methodology, especially considering the added overhead of managing two chains. The authors acknowledge needing to tune the step sizes and mini-batch sizes and this tuning appears crucial for achieving the claimed improvements. This sensitivity reduces the practical appeal of the method.

Furthermore, the paper builds upon a considerable body of work in SG-MCMC. While it references relevant papers, it doesn't fully articulate *why* this approach hasn’t been explored previously. Is the increased computational cost a deterrent? Are there inherent instability issues with the correlated Brownian motions that are not fully addressed? The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of these considerations. The comparison against SGHMC with a higher-order integrator is helpful, but a more extensive comparison with other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods would strengthen the argument for SGRRLD’s superiority.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound and offers a rigorous analysis of the proposed method.
*   Demonstrates improved convergence rates compared to standard SGLD.
*   Provides experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical improvements are modest and may not justify the increased complexity.
*   The sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning (step sizes, mini-batch sizes) is a concern.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of why this approach hasn’t been previously explored.
*   The experiments are limited in scope and a more extensive comparison with other SG-MCMC methods is needed.
*   The paper doesn't clearly establish a compelling advantage over existing methods that might prompt adoption by researchers or practitioners.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and presents a novel idea, the limited practical benefits and lack of compelling advantages over existing methods do not justify acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a significant contribution that will likely impact the field. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above, with more substantial empirical evidence demonstrating clear and consistent advantages, would be necessary for reconsideration.
