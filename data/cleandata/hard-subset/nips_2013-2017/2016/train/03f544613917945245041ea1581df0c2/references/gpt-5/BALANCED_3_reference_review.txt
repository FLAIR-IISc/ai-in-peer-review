Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a new SG-MCMC scheme that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD-like samplers while controlling variance via correlated Gaussian increments. The algorithm runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon, and forms an extrapolated estimator 2π̂γ/2(f) − π̂γ(f). Building on weak error expansions for SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and RR extrapolation for invariant measures of diffusions (Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup, 2015), the paper extends these ideas to the stochastic-gradient setting. The authors prove a CLT for the extrapolated estimator and derive non-asymptotic bounds: with constant step-size γ, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), leading to an optimal MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) (vs O(K^{-2/3}) for first-order SG-MCMC). They show that using perfectly correlated Gaussian increments across levels minimizes asymptotic variance. Experiments on a Gaussian model, on SGHMC (with a higher-order integrator baseline), and on large-scale Bayesian matrix factorization (MovieLens 1M/10M/20M) indicate improved accuracy at similar wall-clock time by running the two chains in parallel.

The work is positioned relative to (i) SGLD and SG-MCMC variants [Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014; Ma et al., 2015; Ding et al., 2014; Li et al., 2016; Şimşekli et al., 2016], (ii) high-order SG-MCMC integrators [Chen, Ding & Carin, 2015], and (iii) RR/MLMC for SDEs [Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015; Pagès, 2007]. To my knowledge, applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC (with stochastic gradients) is new.

Quality
- Theory: The asymptotic CLT and the non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds are technically nontrivial. The derivations rely on weak error expansions and a Poisson equation approach, with smoothness and ergodicity assumptions. The conclusion that perfectly correlated Gaussian increments (Σ=Id) minimize variance matches diffusion results and is well-justified. The conditions on the stochastic gradient noise are clearly discussed (e.g., same common distribution across levels).
- Caveats: The smoothness requirements (g ∈ C9, U sufficiently regular) and stability/ergodicity assumptions are strong and may exclude many practical non-convex posteriors. The analysis abstracts away the impact of data-dependent minibatch noise beyond moment and unbiasedness assumptions; it would help to more clearly delineate when the RR cancellation term remains valid in the presence of gradient noise. Also, the consequences of using different minibatch schemes across the two levels (which can break the cancellation) deserve more explicit guidance for practitioners.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments convincingly illustrate bias/MSE scalings and the benefit of correlated Gaussians. The matrix factorization experiments are large-scale and demonstrate practical gains; the SGHMC comparisons include a higher-order integrator baseline. However, broader coverage (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, deeper non-convex models) would strengthen claims of generality, and reporting confidence intervals would help assess statistical significance.

Clarity
- Overall exposition is clear and well-organized, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The algorithmic description is precise and the extrapolated estimator is clearly defined.
- Potential typo in Eq. (5): the first update for Chain 2 evaluates ∇Û at θ(γ/2)2k+1 on the right-hand side; this seems implicit and likely should be at θ(γ/2)2k (Euler step). Please correct/clarify.
- Practical guidance could be expanded: how to implement the correlated Gaussians; recommendations on coupling minibatches across levels; sensitivity to batch size; discussion of constant vs. decreasing step-size policies in practice.

Originality
- The central idea—bringing RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with explicit variance control via correlated Brownian increments—is, to my knowledge, novel for stochastic-gradient samplers. It is a natural but nontrivial extension of known RR/MLMC ideas for SDEs to the SG-MCMC setting.
- The work also shows that a first-order integrator augmented with RR achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators, which is an interesting and practically appealing point.

Significance
- The improved finite-time and asymptotic rates (MSE O(K^{-4/5})) over first-order SG-MCMC are theoretically meaningful.
- The method is attractive when parallel resources are available, as it trades two coupled chains for improved accuracy at near-equal wall time; the empirical matrix factorization results support this.
- Adoption may be tempered by (i) the need to run and coordinate two chains, (ii) sensitivity to assumptions (smoothness, ergodicity), and (iii) unclear behavior on highly non-convex posteriors common in deep learning (not tested here).

Pros
- Clear, principled algorithm grounded in RR extrapolation; simple to implement in SGLD/SGHMC frameworks.
- Solid theory: CLT and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds; variance-minimizing correlation result.
- Parallel-friendly; practical gains demonstrated on large-scale matrix factorization.
- Extends to other SG-MCMC variants (shown for SGHMC).

Cons
- Strong regularity assumptions; unclear applicability to non-convex/posterior landscapes often seen in practice.
- Experimental scope could be broader; lack of uncertainty bars; limited ablations (e.g., effect of Σ, minibatch coupling).
- Fairness of time comparisons depends on parallel resources. A single-core setting would roughly double compute for SGRRLD; a baseline with two independent SGLD chains averaged could be included for fairness.
- Implementation detail: guidance on coupling minibatches across levels to avoid degradation of RR cancellation is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Chain 2 update typo and clarify whether updates are explicit Euler.
- Provide practical recipes: (a) exact construction of correlated Gaussian increments; (b) whether and how to couple minibatch indices across levels (shared seeds) to reduce variance and preserve cancellation; (c) recommended step-size schedules (constant vs. decreasing) and tuning.
- Add experiments on additional models (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, a moderately deep Bayesian neural net) and report confidence intervals.
- Include ablations: effect of Σ (including independent vs. perfectly correlated), effect of using the same vs. independent minibatches across levels, and sensitivity to batch size.
- Report results under single-threaded execution to complement the parallel wall-clock results; optionally include a baseline averaging two independent SGLD chains with matched compute.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a neat, well-justified, and practically relevant idea with solid theoretical backing and promising empirical results. While assumptions are strong and experimental scope could be expanded, the contribution is sufficiently novel and valuable for the SG-MCMC community. With minor fixes and added practical guidance, the paper would make a good NeurIPS contribution.
