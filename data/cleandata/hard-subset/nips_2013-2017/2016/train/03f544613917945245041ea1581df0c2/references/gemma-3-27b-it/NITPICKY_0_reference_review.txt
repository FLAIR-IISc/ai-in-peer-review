This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD by employing Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea—running two chains with different step sizes and combining the results—is a reasonable approach to bias reduction, drawing inspiration from numerical analysis techniques applied to SDEs. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates and validate their method with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

However, despite the potentially interesting idea and demonstrable empirical gains, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor (and some not-so-minor) flaws that ultimately detract from its overall quality and reproducibility. My assessment is leaning towards rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea, albeit building upon established techniques in numerical analysis.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a convergence analysis, establishing theoretical guarantees for bias and MSE reduction.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both Gaussian models and matrix factorization demonstrate improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelism of the two chains is a strong practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing is often dense and difficult to follow. Equations are introduced without sufficient context or explanation. For example, equation (8) introduces Σ without prior motivation; a reader unfamiliar with the context would struggle to understand its purpose. The jump between theoretical sections and experimental setups is abrupt.
*   **Technical Details & Justifications:** The choice of correlated Brownian increments using equation (8) is justified by referencing [23], but the connection isn't adequately explained *within* the paper. The reader is expected to consult an external source to understand the reasoning. Similarly, the claim that the mini-batch size *must* be the same for both chains lacks a clear explanation – why is this critical for avoiding bias?
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** There's a lack of consistency in notation. For example, γk is used both as a step size *and* in defining Γ(n)K. This ambiguity makes the equations harder to parse.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments, while showing improvements, lack sufficient detail for reproducibility. The specific parameter settings for SGHMC-s (used for comparison in the synthetic experiment) are not clearly stated, making it difficult to verify the results. The choice of step sizes for the matrix factorization experiments seems arbitrary; a more systematic tuning procedure would strengthen the conclusions. The wall-clock time comparisons in the matrix factorization experiment are not presented with error bars, and the computational resources (CPU, memory) are not specified.
*   **Minor Errors/Omissions:** The statement before Theorem 2 mentioning "appropriate conditions" on U, f, and L feels incomplete. The conditions themselves should be briefly summarized or referenced explicitly. There are minor grammatical errors throughout the text.
*   **Overclaiming:** The claim of achieving the "theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" feels overstated, especially given the use of a first-order integrator. The RR extrapolation *improves* accuracy, but doesn't magically equate it to higher-order methods.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is potentially impactful.
*   Theoretical guarantees and empirical results suggest the method can improve sampling efficiency.
*   The parallelizability of the algorithm is a significant practical advantage.

**Con:**

*   The paper lacks clarity and rigor in presentation.
*   Numerous technical details are glossed over or insufficiently explained.
*   Experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   The claims, while potentially true, are often overstated and lack nuance.
*   The multitude of minor flaws suggests a lack of careful attention to detail.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the central idea of SGRRLD is promising, the paper’s numerous shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of clarity, the insufficient detail, and the somewhat overstated claims undermine the contribution. A major revision, addressing these concerns with a stronger emphasis on clarity, completeness, and reproducibility, would be necessary for reconsideration. The current state of the paper is too rough for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
