### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The primary motivation is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order SG-MCMC methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs linearly to cancel out the leading-order bias term. To manage the variance of the combined estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motions for the two chains.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed SGRRLD algorithm. The authors establish a Central Limit Theorem and derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). A key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This improved rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex higher-order integrators, yet SGRRLD achieves this using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task using real-world datasets, showing significant improvements over SGLD in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency (wall-clock time).

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is a novel and clever contribution. While RR extrapolation is a known technique in numerical analysis for SDEs, its adaptation and analysis in the context of SG-MCMC, where noise comes from both the Langevin dynamics and the gradient subsampling, is a new and valuable contribution. The approach is elegant in its simplicity, providing a path to higher-order accuracy without resorting to more complex numerical integrators.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is technically sound and provides a thorough convergence analysis. Theorem 1 (CLT) and Theorem 2 (non-asymptotic bounds) are significant results that formally characterize the behavior of SGRRLD. The derivation of the improved MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a key strength, as it quantifies the advantage of the proposed method and places it on par with more complex state-of-the-art methods.

3.  **Significant Practical Implications:** The proposed method has clear practical benefits. By reducing bias, it can achieve higher accuracy for a given computational budget or reach a target accuracy faster. The inherent parallelism of running two chains makes SGRRLD well-suited for modern multi-core architectures. The experiments on large-scale matrix factorization compellingly demonstrate this, showing that SGRRLD achieves the same or better RMSE than SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time.

4.  **Generality and Thorough Evaluation:** The authors rightly claim that the RR extrapolation idea is generic and can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms. They support this by successfully applying it to SGHMC in their experiments, broadening the impact of their work. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, starting with a controlled synthetic setting that allows for precise verification of the theoretical claims (e.g., bias/MSE vs. step size γ, and convergence rates) and moving to a challenging real-world application that showcases its practical utility.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Discussion on Computational Cost:** The primary drawback of SGRRLD is the increased computational cost. To advance the simulation by one "coarse" step of size γ, the method requires one gradient evaluation for the γ-chain and two evaluations for the γ/2-chain, totaling three gradient evaluations. This is a 3x increase in gradient computations per time step compared to SGLD. While the authors highlight the benefit of parallelization, which reduces the wall-clock time overhead to approximately 2x (the time of the two fine steps), a more explicit discussion of this trade-off between computational cost and statistical efficiency would strengthen the paper. For instance, would running a single SGLD chain for 3x longer with a smaller step size yield comparable results? The empirical results suggest SGRRLD is superior, but a direct discussion of this alternative would be beneficial.

2.  **Clarity on Gradient Estimator Correlation:** The paper notes that the two sequences of stochastic gradients can be correlated. In practice, for the RR extrapolation to work as intended, the distributions of the gradient estimators for both chains must be the same (as stated in Section 3, requiring the same mini-batch size). A point that could be clarified is the strategy for drawing mini-batches. Are the mini-batches for the three gradient evaluations within one SGRRLD step (one for the coarse chain, two for the fine chain) independent, or could they be shared? Using the same mini-batch might reduce the variance from the stochastic gradients but could introduce complex dependencies not covered by the current theory. A brief comment on the choice made in the experiments and its potential implications would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding.

3.  **Assumptions for Theoretical Results:** The paper defers the technical assumptions for the main theorems to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, a brief, high-level summary of the key assumptions in the main text (e.g., smoothness of the potential U, growth conditions, moment conditions on the gradient noise) would improve the paper's self-containedness and help readers gauge the applicability of the theory to other problems, such as non-convex deep learning models.

### Decision and Justification

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper.

The paper presents a novel, simple, and effective method for improving the accuracy of a widely used class of algorithms in Bayesian machine learning. The contribution is well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis and convincing empirical evidence on both synthetic and large-scale real-world problems. The strengths—in particular, the originality of the idea, the significant improvement in convergence rates, and the demonstrated practical performance gains—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work addresses the important problem of discretization bias in SG-MCMC and provides a practical solution that is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners. It represents a solid and significant contribution to the NeurIPS community.
