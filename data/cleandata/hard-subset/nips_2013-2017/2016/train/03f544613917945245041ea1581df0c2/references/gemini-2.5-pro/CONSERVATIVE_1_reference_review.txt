This paper proposes a new stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, which applies the classical Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The goal is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in SGLD. The method involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds, showing that their method improves the MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵). This work builds upon the extensive literature on SG-MCMC methods, particularly SGLD, and connects it with established techniques for variance reduction in the numerical solution of SDEs. The authors position their contribution as a way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators without their implementation complexity.

**Strengths:**
*   The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting is a clever and well-motivated idea. It provides an alternative path to bias reduction that is different from the more common approach of designing higher-order integrators.
*   The paper is theoretically sound. The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis, deriving a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE. These results clearly formalize the intuition that RR extrapolation should improve the convergence rate.
*   The experiments on the synthetic Gaussian model are well-designed and provide clear empirical evidence for the theoretical claims. Specifically, Figures 2 and 3 effectively demonstrate the predicted reduction in bias and MSE and confirm the derived optimal scaling of the step size with the number of iterations.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary concerns with this work relate to the evaluation of its practical utility and the rigor of its comparison against established methods.

1.  **Computational Cost and Efficiency Claims:** The paper's central claim of improved efficiency is not convincingly supported. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm requires running two chains. The authors state that "Each iteration of SGRRLD will consist of one step of the first SGLD chain... and two steps of the second SGLD chain." This means that for each effective "step" of the combined algorithm, three gradient evaluations are performed (one for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain). The experimental comparisons are made against an SGLD baseline that is run for twice the number of iterations to match wall-clock time, which implies SGLD performs two gradient evaluations in the same time period. Therefore, SGRRLD uses a total of 3K gradient evaluations while the baseline SGLD uses 2K. This 50% increase in the total computational budget (in terms of gradient calls, which is the dominant cost) is a critical detail that is not explicitly addressed. The observed performance gains may simply be a result of this increased computational effort rather than a fundamental improvement in efficiency. A more principled comparison would normalize performance by the total number of gradient evaluations.

2.  **Reliance on Parallelism:** The argument that the method is efficient relies entirely on the availability of parallel hardware to run the two chains. While this is a reasonable assumption in many settings, it makes the benefits conditional. The paper should discuss the performance in a sequential setting or, more importantly, compare against an SGLD baseline that is allowed the same total computational budget (e.g., SGLD with a 50% larger batch size, or run for 50% more iterations). It is not clear that the proposed two-chain approach is superior to these simpler strategies for utilizing additional compute.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper claims to achieve the accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators, which is a key motivation. However, the empirical comparison to support this claim is limited. Figure 4 compares SGRRHMC to SGHMC with a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s), but the comparison is not comprehensive. It shows that for a sufficiently large step size γ, SGRRHMC outperforms the first-order SGHMC, but it only achieves an MSE comparable to SGHMC-s at a very small step size. A thorough comparison against SGHMC-s across a range of step sizes and for an equivalent computational budget is necessary to substantiate the claim that SGRRLD is a competitive alternative. Without this, the proposed method appears to be an overly complex solution to a problem for which more direct methods may already exist.

4.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper introduces a more complex algorithm involving the management of two correlated chains. While theoretically elegant, the practical challenges of implementation and tuning are not discussed. It is plausible that tuning the step size γ for this coupled system is more difficult than for a single SGLD chain. The benefits of a new method must be significant enough to justify the adoption of this additional complexity over a simple, well-understood baseline like SGLD.

In summary, while the theoretical contribution of the paper is sound and the core idea is interesting, the work falls short in demonstrating its practical significance and rigorously validating its efficiency claims. The experimental evaluation does not control for the total number of gradient computations, which is a major flaw in the comparison. The benefits are conditional on a specific parallel hardware model, and the comparison with other advanced methods like higher-order integrators is not thorough enough. The work in its current form presents an interesting but not yet proven approach. Substantial revisions to the experimental section would be required to provide convincing evidence of the method's value over established techniques.
