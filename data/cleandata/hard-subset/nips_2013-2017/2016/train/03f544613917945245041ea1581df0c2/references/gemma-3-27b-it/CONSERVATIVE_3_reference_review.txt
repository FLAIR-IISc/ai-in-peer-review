This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), aiming to reduce bias through Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea—running two chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs—is presented as a way to achieve higher accuracy without resorting to higher-order integrators. The authors provide theoretical analysis bounding bias and variance, and demonstrate improvements over SGLD and SGHMC on synthetic and real datasets.

While the idea is conceptually sound and builds upon established numerical methods (Richardson-Romberg extrapolation), the presentation feels somewhat incremental and the justification for the complexity introduced isn't fully convincing given the presented evidence. The paper relies heavily on theoretical analysis, which, while rigorous, feels disconnected from the practical benefits demonstrated.  The claimed asymptotic improvements are interesting, but the practical impact isn’t sufficiently highlighted. 

A major concern is the lack of a truly compelling comparison to existing, well-tuned baselines. SGLD is the primary comparator, but the parameter tuning for SGLD (step size) is not described in detail.  Moreover, the claim of achieving “the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators” without demonstrating a clear advantage *over* those higher-order methods in terms of computational cost (which is implied to be a benefit of this approach) feels unsubstantiated. The experiments with SGHMC and its RR extrapolation are a positive addition, but still lack a strong head-to-head comparison against a well-implemented higher-order SGHMC variant. 

The analysis of the convergence rate is thorough, but the practical translation into faster convergence in realistic scenarios is not fully shown. The experiments show improvements, but the magnitude of those improvements (e.g., the time saving in the matrix factorization experiment) appears modest.  The sensitivity analysis with respect to step size, while present, could be more extensive.  The choice of step size γ seems somewhat arbitrary and a more systematic exploration of its impact on performance would be beneficial. The explanation about correlated Brownian increments is complex and doesn't clearly demonstrate a significant benefit in the presented experiments.

The clarity of the paper is reasonable, though the numerous equations and theoretical derivations make it dense.  The organization is logical, but the connection between theoretical results and experimental validation could be strengthened.  Related work is adequately referenced, although a more thorough discussion of existing variance reduction techniques in SG-MCMC would be valuable.

The originality lies in applying the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting. However, the extrapolation technique itself is well-known. The novelty is in its adaptation, not invention.

The significance is questionable. While the theoretical improvements are noted, the practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments are not substantial enough to warrant widespread adoption. The algorithm adds complexity (running two chains) for a relatively modest gain in accuracy. The potential for parallelization is a positive aspect, but the overhead of managing two chains needs to be carefully considered. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis provides bounds on bias and variance.
*   Demonstrated improvements over SGLD on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modest practical improvements over SGLD and other baselines.
*   Lack of a compelling comparison to well-tuned higher-order methods.
*   Complexity added without clear demonstration of sufficient benefit.
*   Theoretical results don't strongly translate to practical gains.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

This paper presents a technically sound contribution but falls short of the significance threshold for NIPS. The improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity.  A more thorough experimental evaluation, comparing against stronger baselines and demonstrating a clear computational advantage, would be required for reconsideration. The paper feels like a solid, but ultimately incremental, step in the field of SG-MCMC, rather than a breakthrough contribution.
