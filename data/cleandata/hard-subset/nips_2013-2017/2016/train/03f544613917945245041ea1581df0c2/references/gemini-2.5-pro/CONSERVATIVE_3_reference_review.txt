**Summary**

This paper proposes a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), aimed at reducing the discretization bias inherent in first-order methods like SGLD. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. To manage the variance of the combined estimator, the method employs correlated noise processes for the two chains, a technique previously explored for standard SDEs. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which improves upon SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) and matches the rate of more complex higher-order integrators. The method's effectiveness is validated through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, correctly adapting the well-established Richardson-Romberg extrapolation method to the SG-MCMC context. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing both asymptotic convergence results (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derived convergence rates are a significant improvement over standard first-order SG-MCMC methods.

2.  **A Simple and General Approach to Bias Reduction:** The proposed method offers a conceptually simple alternative to using higher-order numerical integrators (e.g., splitting schemes), which can be complex to derive and implement. The RR extrapolation principle is straightforward and, as demonstrated by its application to SGHMC, appears to be a general "wrapper" that could be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the theoretical claims. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model clearly illustrate the reduction in bias and MSE compared to SGLD and validate the predicted optimal convergence rates with respect to the step size schedule. The comparison with a higher-order SGHMC integrator provides evidence for the claim that SGRRLD can achieve similar accuracy with only a first-order scheme. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments demonstrate the practical utility of the method, showing significant improvements in wall-clock time to reach a target accuracy on real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** The primary contribution is the application of a well-known, decades-old numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg extrapolation) to the SG-MCMC framework. While the theoretical analysis for the stochastic gradient setting is non-trivial and valuable, the core concept has been extensively studied for SDEs and standard Langevin algorithms (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the unique challenges posed by the stochastic gradient noise in the context of RR extrapolation and how the analysis addresses them, to better highlight its novel contributions over this prior work.

2.  **Computational Overhead:** The method's main drawback is its computational cost. SGRRLD requires running two chains, effectively doubling the number of gradient computations and updates compared to SGLD for the same number of effective steps. The authors mitigate this by leveraging parallelization, leading to favorable wall-clock time comparisons. However, this assumes the availability of parallel computing resources. A discussion on the total computational budget (e.g., total CPU hours) is absent. On a single processor, SGRRLD would be approximately twice as slow. This trade-off between parallel resources and statistical efficiency should be made more explicit.

3.  **Comparison to Higher-Order Integrators:** The paper claims to match the accuracy of higher-order integrators. While Figure 4 supports this, the comparison is limited. Higher-order methods, while potentially more complex to implement, do not require doubling the computational resources. A more detailed comparison of the trade-offs—including implementation effort, memory usage, and total computational cost (not just wall-clock time)—is needed to firmly establish SGRRLD as a preferable alternative. It is not immediately clear if running two simple first-order chains is always better than running one more complex second-order chain.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed piece of work. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a logical and effective way to reduce discretization bias. The theoretical results are strong, and the empirical evidence is convincing. The main reservation is the degree of originality, as the work consists of applying an established numerical method to a now well-understood class of algorithms. Furthermore, the practical advantage hinges on the availability of parallel hardware to offset the doubled computational cost.

Despite these reservations, the paper makes a solid contribution by providing a simple, general, and parallel-friendly tool for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC methods. It provides a clear alternative to implementing more complex higher-order integrators. The work is of interest to the MCMC and Bayesian inference communities. While not a groundbreaking conceptual leap, it is a valuable and rigorous methodological contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Strong theoretical analysis with improved convergence rates over SGLD.
*   The proposed method is conceptually simple and generalizable.
*   Convincing empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world problems.
*   Provides a practical, parallelizable alternative to complex higher-order integrators.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core idea (RR extrapolation) is a classical technique, limiting the conceptual novelty.
*   The method requires double the computational resources of its base algorithm, and its practical speed-up is contingent on parallel hardware.
*   The comparison against state-of-the-art higher-order integrators could be more comprehensive.
