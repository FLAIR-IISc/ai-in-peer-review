This paper introduces a novel approach for improving the accuracy of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis. The resulting algorithm, termed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combines their outputs to cancel out the leading-order bias term originating from the Euler-Maruyama discretization. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate that SGRRLD improves the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵), effectively achieving the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators using only a simple first-order scheme. This work builds upon the foundational SGLD algorithm [1] and connects the SG-MCMC literature with classical work on variance reduction for SDEs [21, 23], a connection that, to my knowledge, has not been previously explored in this context.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and elegance. The idea of using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established but somewhat forgotten tool in the ML community, to accelerate modern, large-scale Bayesian inference methods is both insightful and powerful. It represents a bold and creative direction for improving SG-MCMC algorithms, moving beyond the common path of designing more complex, model-specific integrators. This is precisely the kind of conceptual contribution that can inspire new avenues of research.

The theoretical contributions are significant and appear technically sound. The authors go beyond simply proposing a heuristic and provide a rigorous analysis of their method's convergence properties. The key result—that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)—is a substantial improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD and its first-order variants. This is not an incremental improvement but a fundamental leap in theoretical efficiency. The analysis correctly identifies the optimal strategy for correlating the noise terms in the two chains to control the variance, which is a crucial and non-trivial aspect of making the extrapolation effective.

From a practical standpoint, the proposed method has several compelling advantages.
1.  **Simplicity and Generality:** SGRRLD is remarkably simple to implement. It acts as a "wrapper" around any existing SGLD-like sampler, requiring only the execution of a second chain and a final linear combination of the results. This plug-and-play nature, which the authors demonstrate by also applying it to SGHMC, makes the method broadly applicable without requiring bespoke derivations for new samplers.
2.  **Parallelism:** The algorithm is inherently parallel. The two chains can be run on separate cores or machines with minimal communication overhead. The experiments effectively demonstrate that this parallelism translates into superior performance in terms of wall-clock time, making the method highly suitable for modern computational environments.

The experimental validation is solid. The experiments on the synthetic Gaussian model are particularly well-designed, providing clear empirical confirmation of the theoretical claims regarding the improved rates for bias and MSE as functions of both the step size γ and the number of iterations K. The comparison against SGHMC with a higher-order integrator [10] convincingly shows that SGRRLD can match the performance of more complex methods, supporting the paper's central thesis. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets successfully demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, showing significant gains in accuracy for a fixed computational time budget.

### Weaknesses

The paper’s weaknesses are minor and do not detract significantly from its core contributions.

1.  **Computational Cost:** The most direct drawback is the doubling of computational resources required to run two chains. The authors correctly argue that this cost can be mitigated by parallelization, which leads to no increase in wall-clock time. However, in resource-constrained settings where parallel cores are not available, the method incurs a 2x slowdown. While this is a fair trade-off for the significant accuracy gains, it is a point worth acknowledging more explicitly.

2.  **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are strong, they could have been even more impactful by including a more challenging, non-convex problem, such as Bayesian neural network training. The matrix factorization problem, while large-scale, has a relatively well-behaved posterior landscape. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness in a highly multi-modal or complex geometry would have provided stronger evidence for its robustness and general applicability.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a highly novel and significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. It bridges a gap between classical numerical analysis and modern machine learning with an idea that is simple, elegant, and effective. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are convincing. While the method doubles the computational cost, its perfect parallelizability makes it a highly practical tool for improving the accuracy of a wide range of SG-MCMC methods at no cost to wall-clock time. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to potential extensions than to fundamental flaws in the presented work. This paper introduces a genuinely new and powerful concept that other researchers are likely to build upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and original idea.
*   Significant theoretical improvement in convergence rates.
*   Simple, general, and easy to implement.
*   Inherently parallel, making it practical for large-scale problems.
*   Strong empirical validation that supports the theory.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Doubles the computational resource requirement (though not wall-clock time).
*   Experiments do not include highly non-convex models like BNNs.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold new directions, the arguments for acceptance are overwhelmingly stronger. This is a clear accept.
