**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation method, a classical numerical analysis technique for sequence acceleration, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs in a specific way to cancel the leading-order bias term. To manage the variance of the resulting estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motions for the two chains. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean-Squared-Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard first-order SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, where it outperforms SGLD in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a simple yet clever idea. It provides a way to achieve the benefits of higher-order methods (i.e., reduced bias) using only a first-order integrator, which is an elegant and appealing approach.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both a CLT (Theorem 1) establishing asymptotic properties and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derived optimal convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD and its variants.

3.  **Practical Significance:** The proposed algorithm is inherently parallelizable since the two chains can be run independently. This is a major practical advantage in modern computing environments and is well-demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiment, where wall-clock time is used as a metric.

4.  **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide clear evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The Gaussian model experiments meticulously verify the predicted behavior of bias and MSE with respect to step size γ and iteration count K. The large-scale matrix factorization results on MovieLens datasets show that the theoretical gains translate into substantial real-world performance improvements, with SGRRLD achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, despite its strong core contribution, suffers from a notable lack of meticulousness in its presentation, with several errors and ambiguities that detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Errors in Algorithm Definition:**
    *   There appears to be a critical typo in the update rule for Chain 2 in Equation (5). The first line states: `θ(γ/2)_(2k+1) = ... - (γ_{k+1}/2) ∇Ũ(γ/2)_(2k+1)(θ(γ/2)_(2k+1)) + ...`. The gradient should be evaluated at the state from the previous step, `θ(γ/2)_(2k)`, not the state being computed, `θ(γ/2)_(2k+1)`. This is a fundamental error in the description of the iterative process.
    *   In the definition of the RR estimator, Equation (6), the normalization term is `(∑_{k=2}^{K+1} γ_k)⁻¹` while the main summation runs from `k=1` to `K`. This inconsistency in indices is confusing and likely incorrect. The text suggests the denominator should be `∑_{k=1}^{K} γ_{k+1}`.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Crucial Details:**
    *   The paper correctly emphasizes the importance of using correlated Brownian motions to control variance. However, it is entirely ambiguous about the handling of the stochastic gradients. It states that the gradient estimator sequences can be correlated but provides no details. For reproducibility, it is essential to clarify if the same mini-batch of data is used for the `θ(γ)` update and the corresponding two `θ(γ/2)` updates. This choice has significant implications for the variance of the final estimator, and its omission is a major oversight.

3.  **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The manuscript is replete with minor but distracting errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The footnote on the first page reads "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems", which is improperly formatted.
    *   In the references, [21] lists two different years: "(1991), 1990.". Reference [16] has a double comma "Paris-Est,,". The author name in [1] is "Y. W Teh", where "W" should be lowercase.
    *   In Theorem 1(c), the term `Γ(3)k` should almost certainly be `Γ(3)K`.
    *   In Section 5.2, the latent factor matrix H is defined as `H ∈ R^{D×P}`, but the context of the `I×J` data matrix implies it should be `H ∈ R^{J×P}`. `D` is undefined and appears to be a typo for `J`.

4.  **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the method "can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is not fully substantiated. Figure 4 shows that SGRRHMC with a large γ can match the MSE of a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s) with a small γ. While this demonstrates a significant improvement over the baseline SGHMC, it does not show equivalence to higher-order methods across the board and is a somewhat misleading comparison.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and impactful idea for improving SG-MCMC methods.
*   The theoretical results are significant, demonstrating a clear improvement in convergence rates.
*   The algorithm has a key practical advantage due to its parallel nature.
*   Experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over SGLD.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper contains a substantial number of errors, including a critical one in the algorithm's mathematical definition (Eq. 5).
*   Key implementation details necessary for reproducibility (e.g., handling of mini-batches for the two chains) are missing.
*   The overall lack of polish and attention to detail undermines confidence in the work's rigor.
*   The volume of minor flaws is too high for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

While the core idea of this paper is strong and the results are promising, the execution of the manuscript is flawed. The presence of errors in fundamental equations, coupled with a lack of clarity on key methodological details and a general sloppiness in presentation, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper has the potential to be an excellent contribution, but it requires a thorough revision to correct the errors and clarify the ambiguities. I would encourage the authors to perform these revisions and resubmit to a future conference.
