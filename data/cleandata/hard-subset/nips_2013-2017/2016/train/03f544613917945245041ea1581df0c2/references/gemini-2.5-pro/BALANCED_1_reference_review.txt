### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The key idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed method leverages Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating convergence. SGRRLD runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combines their outputs to cancel the leading-order bias term, reducing the bias from O(γ) to O(γ^2). A crucial part of the method is the use of correlated noise between the two chains to control the variance of the combined estimator, which would otherwise be inflated by the extrapolation.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). Their analysis shows that SGRRLD achieves an optimal MSE convergence rate of O(K^(-4/5)) after K iterations, a significant improvement over the O(K^(-2/3)) rate for standard SGLD. This improved rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex higher-order integrators, but SGRRLD achieves this using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The paper's claims are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model, where the theoretical improvements are clearly demonstrated, and on a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendation, where SGRRLD shows superior performance in terms of wall-clock time compared to SGLD.

The work is well-situated within the literature on scalable Bayesian inference. It directly addresses the bias-variance trade-off in SG-MCMC methods, a central topic since the original SGLD paper [1]. It presents an alternative to the line of work that uses higher-order integrators to reduce bias [10], drawing instead from classical numerical analysis on SDEs [21, 23] and adapting these ideas to the stochastic gradient setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is both novel and elegant. It provides a new and practical approach to mitigating discretization bias, a fundamental challenge in this area. The ability to achieve the accuracy of a higher-order method using only a first-order integrator is a significant contribution. This makes the method easier to implement and more broadly applicable than methods requiring complex, model-specific derivations for higher-order updates.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. These results rigorously establish the improved convergence rates and provide theoretical justification for the design choices, such as using correlated noise. The analysis successfully extends results from the numerical SDE literature [23] to the more challenging SG-MCMC setting where noise comes from both discretization and data subsampling.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical contributions are stated with clarity. The progression from the background on SGLD and RR to the proposed method and its analysis is logical and effective.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The experiments on the synthetic Gaussian model are well-designed to isolate and verify the theoretical claims about bias and MSE as functions of step size (γ) and iteration count (K). These results provide strong evidence for the correctness of the theory.
    *   The demonstration of applying the RR idea to SGHMC effectively supports the claim of the method's generality.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiment on real-world datasets (MovieLens up to 20M) demonstrates the practical utility of SGRRLD. Showing improved RMSE vs. wall-clock time is a compelling result, especially given the method's inherent parallelism.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Discussion of Computational Trade-offs:** The primary drawback of SGRRLD is its computational cost. To produce one SGRRLD sample, the algorithm performs one step of the γ-chain and two steps of the γ/2-chain, totaling three gradient evaluations. The authors state that the two chains can be run in parallel, which is a key advantage. However, even with perfect parallelism, the wall-clock time per effective iteration would be roughly twice that of a single SGLD chain (dominated by the two steps of the γ/2-chain). The paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this trade-off. For instance, a direct comparison against running a single SGLD chain for 3x the number of iterations with an optimally tuned smaller step size would be insightful. While the improved convergence rate suggests SGRRLD should win, explicitly showing this would make the practical benefit even clearer.

2.  **Memory Overhead:** Running two parallel chains also doubles the memory requirement for storing the parameters and any associated states. While this may not be an issue for the models in the paper, it could be a limiting factor for extremely large models (e.g., in deep learning) where memory is a bottleneck. A brief mention of this limitation would provide a more complete picture.

3.  **Generality of the Theory:** The authors claim the method is "quite generic" and can be applied to other SG-MCMC methods, which they demonstrate empirically with SGHMC. However, the formal theoretical analysis is presented for SGLD. It would be beneficial to briefly state the general conditions under which the theoretical results (e.g., the O(γ^2) bias reduction) would hold for a broader class of SG-MCMC algorithms. This would help researchers apply the technique to other samplers with more confidence.

4.  **Comparison to Higher-Order Integrators:** The comparison with SGHMC-s [10] is excellent and supports a key claim of the paper. This comparison could be slightly enhanced by also reporting the number of gradient/potential energy evaluations. This would allow for a more direct comparison of the computational efficiency of achieving a certain accuracy level between SGRRLD and integrator-based approaches.

### Decision

**Accept.**

This paper presents a significant and high-quality contribution to the field of scalable MCMC. The idea of using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC methods is novel, well-motivated, and powerful. The work is supported by a solid theoretical foundation and a convincing set of experiments that demonstrate clear performance benefits on both synthetic and real-world problems. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to areas where the discussion could be expanded for completeness; they do not detract from the core contribution of the paper. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is a practical and impactful tool that is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by researchers in Bayesian machine learning.
