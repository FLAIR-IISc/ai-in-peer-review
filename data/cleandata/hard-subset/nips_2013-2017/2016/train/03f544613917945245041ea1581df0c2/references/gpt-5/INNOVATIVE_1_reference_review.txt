Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC. The key idea is to run two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon, and combine their ergodic averages to cancel the O(γ) bias, while controlling variance by using correlated Brownian increments. The authors prove a CLT for the estimator, derive non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and show improved finite-time rates over standard SGLD: with fixed γ, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding an optimal MSE rate O(K−4/5) vs O(K−2/3) for SGLD. They argue the approach is generic and also demonstrate it for SGHMC. Experiments on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) indicate improved accuracy at matched wall-clock through parallel execution of the two chains.

This work connects classical RR extrapolation for SDE weak error (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) with SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh; Teh–Vollmer–Zygalakis; Chen et al. on high-order integrators). To my knowledge, this is the first explicit application of RR to SG-MCMC, offering a new way to obtain higher-order accuracy without using higher-order integrators.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Bringing RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a simple but powerful idea. The design—two coupled discretizations and pathwise extrapolation—is elegant and broadly applicable beyond SGLD (e.g., to SGHMC).
- Theoretical contributions: The paper provides both asymptotic (CLT with optimal coupling choice Σ=Id) and non-asymptotic bounds. The improved rate O(K−4/5) with first-order integrators is compelling and theoretically matches higher-order integrator methods, but with a simpler implementation.
- Variance control via coupling: The analysis that correlated Brownian increments minimize the asymptotic variance, and the explicit coupling construction, are well-motivated and grounded in prior SDE theory.
- Practicality and parallelism: The two chains can run independently in parallel; the wall-clock comparisons exploit this and show tangible benefits.
- Empirical support: Synthetic Gaussian experiments align with theory (bias/MSE scalings and optimal step-size schedules), and the large-scale matrix factorization results show improved RMSE at similar time budgets. The extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) and comparison to a higher-order integrator variant further support the claims.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and scope: The theoretical results rely on strong smoothness (e.g., solution to Poisson equation, high-order derivatives) and technical conditions on the gradient noise. While standard in the literature, the breadth of models that truly satisfy these in SG-MCMC practice (non-convex posteriors, heavy tails) is not discussed.
- Clarity/derivation details: Some crucial pieces are relegated to the supplement (pseudo-code, conditions, exact forms of σR2 and µR). Equation (6) would benefit from a clearer justification showing explicitly how it implements 2πγ/2(f) − πγ(f) in ergodic averages; the weighting by γk and the “two small steps vs one big step” construction is intuitive, but the mapping to the classical RR coefficients could be made more explicit in the main text.
- Computational trade-offs: The speedup hinges on running two chains in parallel. On single-core/GPUs or memory-limited settings, the method doubles gradient and noise generation cost; an explicit complexity comparison (wall-clock and FLOPs) under different hardware constraints would be useful.
- Limited empirical breadth: Beyond the linear Gaussian model and matrix factorization, additional standard Bayesian benchmarks (logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) would strengthen the case. For SGHMC, only synthetic results are shown; including large-scale SGHMC comparisons would be informative.
- Comparisons: While the paper contrasts to higher-order integrators theoretically and to SGHMC-s empirically on synthetic data, broader head-to-head wall-clock comparisons against practical higher-order SG-MCMC and variance-reduced SG-MCMC variants would help position the method.
- Gradient noise correlation: The theory notes that correlation of stochastic gradients does not impact asymptotics, but practical choices (e.g., sharing minibatches across chains) could reduce variance. An ablation studying independent vs shared minibatches and different Σ would be valuable.
- Tuning: The method requires specific step-size schedules (e.g., α=1/5 for optimal asymptotics) and equal minibatch sizes across chains. Guidance on robust tuning and sensitivity to these choices in practice is limited.

Questions for the authors
- Could you provide more intuition in the main text for why (6) implements RR (i.e., show the mapping to 2πγ/2 − πγ at the level of ergodic averages)?
- How sensitive is performance to sharing the same minibatch across chains at each “macro” iteration versus drawing them independently? Any empirical ablation?
- Can you include an experiment where parallelism is not available (single core/GPU) to assess the cost–benefit trade-off?
- For SGHMC, can you show results on at least one large dataset and compare to high-order SGHMC in wall-clock?
- Do you foresee or have preliminary results on recursive/multistep RR (as hinted) within SG-MCMC?

Minor comments
- Some typos and minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., occasionally √2γ vs √γ in different contexts) could be harmonized.
- Please bring a concise version of the algorithm and key assumptions from the supplement into the main text.

Overall evaluation
This is a bold and original contribution that introduces a new dimension—RR extrapolation—to the SG-MCMC toolbox, with solid theoretical backing and encouraging empirical results. The idea is simple, general, and likely to inspire follow-up work (e.g., multilevel variants, broader samplers). Despite some practical caveats and limited empirical breadth, the novelty and potential impact outweigh the weaknesses.
