### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed method achieves this by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. The algorithm runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combines their outputs to cancel out the leading-order bias term. A key technical contribution is the use of correlated Brownian motion between the two chains to control the variance of the resulting estimator, a technique inspired by prior work on RR for SDEs [23].

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean-Squared-Error (MSE). They show that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD. This effectively provides the accuracy of a higher-order integrator while only using the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated empirically on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, where it outperforms SGLD and is shown to be competitive with higher-order methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and appears sound. The authors provide both a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) to characterize the asymptotic behavior and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results rigorously support the central claims of the paper regarding improved convergence rates. The experimental evaluation is also strong. The synthetic experiments are well-designed to verify the theoretical predictions, such as the dependence of bias/MSE on the step size γ and the optimal decay rate of the step size schedule. The authors are careful in their evaluation, for instance by explicitly testing the derived optimal step-size schedule exponent. The application to a large-scale real-world problem demonstrates clear practical benefits.
*   **Weaknesses:** The main paper defers all technical assumptions to the supplementary material. While standard for space constraints, a brief, high-level description of the required conditions (e.g., smoothness/convexity of the potential U, moments of the gradient noise) in the main text would have made the theoretical section more self-contained.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation is clearly established in the introduction. The background on SGLD and RR extrapolation is concise and effective, providing the necessary context. The proposed algorithm is described precisely, and the intuition behind using correlated noise is well-explained. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, with figures that are easy to interpret and directly support the paper's claims. The connection between the theoretical rates and the experimental validation is made explicit, which is a significant strength.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** While SG-MCMC and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation are both established techniques, their combination in this context is novel and clever. Applying this classical numerical analysis idea to modern large-scale Bayesian inference is an important contribution. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from previous work on improving SG-MCMC, such as using higher-order integrators [10]. The proposed method offers a conceptually different and practically appealing alternative: achieving higher-order accuracy through extrapolation of a simple first-order method, rather than through the implementation of more complex integrators. The extension to SGHMC further underscores the originality and generality of the core idea.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a strong impact.

*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in SG-MCMC: the trade-off between computational cost (larger step size) and statistical accuracy (bias). SGRRLD offers a compelling solution that demonstrably improves this trade-off. The method's key advantages are its simplicity and parallelism. Practitioners can achieve higher accuracy without implementing complex and potentially problem-specific higher-order symplectic integrators. Since SGRRLD is built on two parallel runs of a standard algorithm (SGLD), it is easy to implement and can immediately leverage parallel hardware. The significant wall-clock time improvements shown in the matrix factorization experiments suggest that this method will be highly valuable for practitioners working on large-scale Bayesian models. The general framework is likely to inspire follow-up work applying similar extrapolation techniques to other stochastic approximation algorithms.
*   **Weaknesses:** The primary weakness, which the paper acknowledges via its emphasis on parallelization, is the computational cost. SGRRLD requires approximately three times the gradient computations per effective iteration compared to SGLD (one step for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain). On a single-core machine, this could be a disadvantage. The authors show compelling wall-clock time results on parallel hardware, but a more explicit discussion of this trade-off and a comparison against, for example, SGLD run for 3x the iterations, would be beneficial. Additionally, the method doubles the memory requirement to store the parameters for the two chains, which could be a consideration for extremely large models.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a highly novel and insightful contribution.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating significantly improved convergence rates for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)).
3.  **Excellent Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, directly verifying the theoretical claims on synthetic data and demonstrating substantial practical gains in wall-clock time on a large-scale, real-world task.
4.  **High Practical Impact:** The method is simple to implement, naturally parallelizable, and offers a straightforward way to boost the performance of a widely used class of algorithms without requiring complex higher-order integrators.
5.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Increased Computational Cost:** The method requires roughly 3x the computational work per effective step compared to the baseline SGLD. While the parallel nature mitigates this in terms of wall-clock time, the total computational budget is higher. This trade-off could be discussed in more detail.
2.  **Increased Memory Footprint:** Running two chains in parallel doubles the memory required to store the model parameters.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is a clever and elegant fusion of ideas from numerical analysis and machine learning. The authors provide strong evidence, both theoretical and empirical, that their method achieves higher-order accuracy using only a first-order integrator, leading to substantial improvements over standard SGLD. The weaknesses related to computational and memory overhead are minor compared to the demonstrated benefits, especially given the method's amenability to parallelization. This work clearly advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC and is likely to be highly influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
