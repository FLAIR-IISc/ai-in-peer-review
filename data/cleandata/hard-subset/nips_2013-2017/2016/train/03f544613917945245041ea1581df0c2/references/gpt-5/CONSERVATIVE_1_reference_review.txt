Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC variant that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce discretization bias while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and forms a weighted combination of their ergodic averages. Theoretically, the authors claim: (i) asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4); (ii) a CLT with minimal asymptotic variance when Brownian increments are perfectly correlated across levels; (iii) non-asymptotic bounds yielding an MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) under optimal tuning, improving over first-order SG-MCMC methods’ O(K^{-2/3}). They also describe extension to SGHMC. Experiments on a Gaussian model and matrix factorization (MovieLens) suggest lower bias/MSE and improved wall-clock predictive RMSE versus SGLD, and competitive accuracy with SGHMC using higher-order splitting.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on well-established weak error expansions and RR extrapolation for SDE discretizations (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) and recent theory for SG-MCMC (Teh et al.; Chen et al.). Applying RR to SG-MCMC appears novel. The linkage to duplicated diffusions and variance reduction via common random numbers is appropriate. However, the paper relies heavily on supplemental material for the nontrivial extension from exact-gradient ULA to stochastic-gradient dynamics.

Quality
- Theory: The main claims are plausible by analogy with RR for ULA, but many results hinge on strong and somewhat opaque assumptions deferred to the supplement (e.g., existence of a sufficiently smooth Poisson solution g ∈ C^9, “appropriate” conditions on L). In SG-MCMC, the extra stochastic gradient noise fundamentally changes the Markov kernel; it is not obvious that the weak error expansion and bias cancellation proceed unchanged. The statement that “the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” needs clear, checkable conditions (e.g., bounded second and higher moments of the gradient estimator, how these interact with γ). It is also critical whether the two chains use shared minibatches; using only the same distribution L may not suffice to preserve the RR cancellation in finite K.
- Variance control: The minimal variance for Σ = I (perfect Brownian correlation) is consistent with duplicated diffusions, but the analysis ignores the potentially dominant effect of minibatch noise unless additional coupling is used. This should be formalized.
- Non-asymptotic bounds: Useful, but constants depend on moments of the gradient estimator and may be large in practice. The optimal tuning (γ ∝ K^{-1/5} for MSE) assumes those constants are benign.
- Empirics: The synthetic experiments are clean but limited (Gaussian posterior). The matrix factorization evaluation uses predictive RMSE, which reflects optimization quality at least as much as posterior fidelity. There are no mixing diagnostics (ESS/time), posterior calibration, or comparisons to strong SG baselines (e.g., preconditioned SGLD, thermostat variants, control variate SG-MCMC). Hyperparameter tuning fairness across methods is unclear. The claimed wall-clock parity relies on parallel hardware; SGLD could also run two parallel chains and average, narrowing the gap.

Clarity
The exposition is generally clear at a high level, but many crucial details are deferred, making it difficult to verify soundness. Assumptions are repeatedly termed “mild,” yet the needed smoothness (high-order derivatives, Poisson equation solution) and ergodicity conditions are strong and arguably fail in common ML posteriors (nonsmooth priors, heavy tails). The role of coupling minibatches between chains is under-specified. Some notation is heavy; a concise pseudocode in the main text would help.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC appears new and conceptually interesting. The Brownian correlation idea is known in the RR/SDE literature but its explicit use in SG-MCMC is a valuable contribution.

Significance
If the theoretical guarantees indeed extend robustly to practical SG-MCMC settings, SGRRLD could be broadly useful. However, the current empirical validation is modest and focused on settings where ground truth or predictive RMSE may not reflect posterior quality. Without stronger evidence against established baselines and under more realistic conditions (e.g., non-Gaussian, high-dimensional, nonsmooth models), it is hard to judge impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled idea grounded in classical numerical analysis.
- Provides asymptotic and non-asymptotic analyses; CLT with variance minimization via coupling is insightful.
- Simple to implement on top of SGLD/SGHMC; parallelizable.
- Synthetic results match theory; some wall-clock gains on matrix factorization.

Cons
- Strong, partly hidden assumptions; unclear robustness to typical SG-MCMC conditions (high gradient noise, nonsmooth posteriors).
- No ablation on minibatch coupling; unclear whether RR benefits persist without sharing minibatches or with high-variance gradients.
- Empirical evaluation limited; metrics prioritize prediction over sampling quality; missing comparisons to strong SG baselines and to parallel multi-chain SGLD.
- Benefits rely on having parallel resources; compute fairness to baselines is debatable.
- Practical tuning (γ, batch size) sensitivity not systematically studied.

Questions/clarifications for authors
1. Do you reuse the same minibatch indices across the two levels (and across the two half-steps) at each iteration? If not, how does stochastic gradient noise affect the RR cancellation and variance?
2. Precisely which assumptions on L (moment bounds, independence, correlation allowed) are needed for Theorem 1? Under fixed batch size, can gradient noise dominate the Brownian term?
3. Can you report ESS/time or autocorrelation and posterior calibration (e.g., coverage) on a non-Gaussian model with known ground truth?
4. How does SGRRLD compare to: (a) SGLD with two parallel chains averaged (same hardware), (b) preconditioned SGLD, (c) SG thermostats, (d) variance-reduced SG-MCMC?
5. How sensitive are the gains to step size and batch size? Please include tuning details for all methods.

Minor comments
- Please move key assumptions and algorithm pseudocode to the main paper.
- Discuss computational cost vs higher-order integrators per gradient evaluation.
- “Mild assumptions” should be replaced with explicit, verifiable conditions.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is elegant and potentially impactful, but the current paper leans heavily on strong assumptions and limited experiments. Strengthening the empirical section with rigorous sampling diagnostics on challenging, non-Gaussian posteriors, clarifying and relaxing theoretical assumptions (especially around stochastic gradients and minibatch coupling), and fair compute comparisons to established SG-MCMC baselines would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
