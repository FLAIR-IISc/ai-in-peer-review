This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in SGLD. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the SG-MCMC context. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size γ and another that takes two steps of size γ/2 for each step of the first chain. The final estimator is a linear combination of the outputs from these two chains, designed to cancel out the leading-order bias term. A key technical contribution is the use of correlated Brownian motions between the two chains to control the variance of the resulting estimator, preventing the benefits of bias reduction from being negated by increased variance.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. This improved rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex, higher-order integrators. The paper's claims are supported by extensive experiments on both a synthetic Gaussian model, where the theoretical predictions are precisely verified, and a large-scale matrix factorization task, which demonstrates practical utility and computational speed-ups in terms of wall-clock time.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant idea. While RR is well-known for deterministic and stochastic differential equations with exact gradients, its adaptation and analysis in the presence of stochastic gradient noise is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The resulting improvement in the theoretical convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is significant and advances the state-of-the-art in scalable Bayesian inference. The ability to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only a simple first-order (Euler-Maruyama) scheme is a powerful and practical result.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing both asymptotic (CLT) and non-asymptotic (Bias/MSE) results that precisely characterize the algorithm's performance. The identification and mitigation of the potential variance explosion through the use of correlated noise is a crucial insight that makes the method viable. The proof sketches are clear, and the theoretical results align perfectly with the empirical findings in the synthetic experiments.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The use of a linear Gaussian model provides a clean setting to meticulously verify the theoretical claims regarding the dependence of bias and MSE on the step size (γ) and the number of iterations (K). The experiments confirming the optimal step-size decay rates are particularly strong. The application to SGHMC successfully demonstrates the generality of the proposed technique. The large-scale matrix factorization experiment on MovieLens datasets shows that the theoretical gains translate into practical benefits (faster convergence in wall-clock time), especially by leveraging the inherent parallelism of the dual-chain approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem of discretization bias in SG-MCMC, introduces the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation, and presents the proposed SGRRLD algorithm and its analysis in a logical, easy-to-follow manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The primary weakness is the increased computational cost. SGRRLD requires approximately three times the gradient computations per effective step compared to standard SGLD (one step for the γ chain, two steps for the γ/2 chain). While the authors correctly argue that this can be parallelized to achieve wall-clock time improvements, this benefit disappears on single-core architectures. A more direct comparison of total computational effort (e.g., number of gradient evaluations) versus error would provide a more complete picture. For serial execution, SGLD might still be preferable for a fixed computational budget.

2.  **Memory Cost:** The method requires maintaining two separate chains, doubling the memory footprint for the model parameters. While this is not an issue for the models in the paper, it could become a limiting factor for extremely large models (e.g., large language models) where memory is a critical constraint.

3.  **Scope of Generality:** The paper claims the technique is "quite generic" and can be applied to "all the current SG-MCMC algorithms." While demonstrated for SGLD and SGHMC, the claim would be stronger if it were discussed in the context of more recent or complex samplers, such as those with adaptive preconditioning (e.g., [11]) or thermostats (e.g., [7]). The interaction between the RR scheme and adaptive step sizes or momentum terms could be complex.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of scalable MCMC. It introduces a clever and effective method for reducing the bias of widely used SG-MCMC algorithms. The combination of novel methodology, rigorous theoretical backing, and convincing empirical results is impressive. The weaknesses, primarily related to computational and memory overhead, are inherent trade-offs of the approach and are reasonably addressed by the authors' focus on parallel execution. The paper is well-written, significant, and likely to inspire follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
