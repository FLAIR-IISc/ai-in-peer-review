

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a novel SG-MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in SGLD. The main contribution appears to be the theoretical analysis showing SGRRLD achieves MSE rate O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), along with empirical validation.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) is generally sound, and the experimental results on synthetic and real data provide empirical support for the theoretical claims. The parallelism aspect of the algorithm is well-motivated and correctly identified as a potential advantage.

## Critical Weaknesses

1. **Technical Errors in Key Claims**: The most serious issue is the consistent confusion between MSE and variance. In the introduction, the authors claim "the asymptotic bias and variance of SGRRLD can be bounded as O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively." This is incorrect - they are bounding the MSE (which combines bias and variance), not the variance alone. This fundamental error appears in multiple places, including the abstract and section 4. The paper should have explicitly stated that they're bounding the MSE, not the variance.

2. **Equation and Notation Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous notational errors that undermine its technical credibility:
   - In equation (5), the right-hand side should be θ^(γ/2)_2k, not θ^(γ/2)_2k+1
   - In equation (6), the summation notation is incorrectly written as "K+1∑k=2 γk" instead of "∑_{k=2}^{K+1} γ_k"
   - In equation (8), the transpose symbol is written as ">" instead of "^T"
   - The paper inconsistently uses "γ" and "γ_k" in the same context
   - The proof sketch of Theorem 1 incorrectly references g(θ^(γ)_2k+1) when it should be g(θ^(γ/2)_2k+1)

3. **Ambiguous Rate Claims**: The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves a rate of convergence for the MSE of order O(K^{-4/5})" but fails to specify whether this is for fixed step size or decreasing step size. The actual rate depends on the choice of step size sequence, and the paper doesn't clearly distinguish between these cases. The claim that "by only using a first-order numerical integrator, the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is misleading without comparing to specific methods from [10].

4. **Overstated Parallelism Claim**: The paper claims "the two chains required by the RR extrapolation can be generated independently" and "the SGRRLD algorithm is well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures." However, in the experiments, they state "their wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" when running SGRRLD with half the iterations as SGLD. This is contradictory - if it's truly parallel, SGRRLD should run in approximately half the time for the same number of iterations, not the same time for half the iterations.

5. **Missing Technical Details**: The paper references "appropriate conditions on U, f and L" throughout but never explicitly states these conditions. For a theoretical paper, this is a serious omission. The claim that "g ∈ C^9(R^d)" for the Poisson equation solution is an extremely strong assumption that should be justified or discussed in more depth.

6. **Formatting and Typographical Errors**: The paper contains multiple formatting issues including missing "The" in "th Conference" (should be "The Conference"), inconsistent spacing in "mean squared-error" (sometimes with space, sometimes without), and extra commas in references.

## Overall Assessment

While the core idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is potentially interesting, the paper suffers from significant technical errors, inconsistent notation, and ambiguous claims that undermine its credibility. The confusion between MSE and variance is particularly egregious for a theoretical paper. The numerous mathematical errors in equations and notation suggest the paper was not carefully proofread or reviewed internally before submission.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to correct these technical issues, clarify the theoretical claims, and properly distinguish between MSE and variance. Without these corrections, the paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains too many fundamental technical errors and inconsistencies that prevent it from being a credible contribution to the field. The theoretical claims are undermined by the confusion between MSE and variance, and the numerous notational errors suggest a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a NIPS submission.
