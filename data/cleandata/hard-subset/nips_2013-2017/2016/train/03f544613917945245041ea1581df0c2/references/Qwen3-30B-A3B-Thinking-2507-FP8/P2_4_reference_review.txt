

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods like SGLD. The authors propose running two parallel chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation. The theoretical analysis shows that SGRRLD achieves bias O(γ²), variance O(γ⁴), and MSE convergence rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly better than SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The paper demonstrates the approach on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks, showing practical improvements in accuracy and computational efficiency.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide clear theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) with well-justified proofs (referenced to supplementary materials) that establish the convergence properties of SGRRLD. The theoretical claims about bias, variance, and MSE rates are well-supported by the analysis. The experimental design is thorough, with both synthetic and real-world datasets showing consistent improvements over baselines.

The authors demonstrate appropriate caution by acknowledging limitations: for very small step sizes, the bias and MSE increase due to the 1/(Kγ) term dominating the error bounds. This balanced evaluation of strengths and weaknesses strengthens the paper's credibility.

The main technical weakness is that some details of the proofs are deferred to supplementary materials, which could make it harder for readers to fully assess the theoretical claims without consulting those materials. The paper could also have provided more detailed analysis of the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel (beyond just stating it's parallelizable).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and appropriate for the topic. The experimental results are presented with clear figures showing the performance improvements of SGRRLD over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of how the RR extrapolation interacts with the two chains, particularly regarding the correlation structure of the Brownian increments (Equation 8). While the paper references previous work on RR extrapolation for SDEs, a more explicit connection to how this differs from standard applications would help readers understand the novelty.

## Originality
The core idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel. The authors correctly note that while RR extrapolation has been used for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), it has not been applied to SG-MCMC before. This is a significant conceptual contribution that bridges numerical analysis and MCMC methods.

The paper demonstrates how this approach can achieve the convergence rates of higher-order integrators using only first-order numerical schemes (as shown in Theorem 2), which is a particularly valuable insight. The generalizability to other SG-MCMC methods (like SGHMC, as demonstrated in Section 5.1) further enhances the originality.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. SG-MCMC methods are crucial for large-scale Bayesian inference, and the improved convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) represents a meaningful theoretical advance. The experimental results confirm these theoretical advantages: SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in approximately half the time on matrix factorization tasks (Figure 5), which is highly valuable for practical applications.

The paper opens promising research directions, such as recursive RR extrapolation and combination with multi-level Monte Carlo methods. The ability to improve convergence rates without changing the fundamental numerical integrator (using only first-order schemes) is particularly significant for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical significance is evident. The paper successfully bridges numerical analysis (RR extrapolation) with MCMC methodology, resulting in a novel and valuable algorithm.

The main areas for improvement would be to provide more detailed discussion of the computational overhead of the parallel chains and to include a more comprehensive comparison with other recent SG-MCMC methods. However, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's overall quality.

## Recommendation
I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work is technically sound, novel, and significant, with clear practical implications for large-scale Bayesian inference. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the experimental results convincingly support the theoretical claims. This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Strengths**: Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, rigorous theoretical analysis, strong experimental validation, practical significance for large-scale applications.

**Weaknesses**: Some technical details deferred to supplementary materials, limited discussion of computational overhead of parallel chains, could include more comprehensive comparison with recent SG-MCMC methods.

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference that aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning and statistical methods.
