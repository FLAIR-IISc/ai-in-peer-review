Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC (specifically SGLD) by running two coupled discretizations with stepsizes γ and γ/2 and combining their ergodic averages to cancel the O(γ) weak bias. The method uses correlated Brownian increments across the two chains to control the variance inflation induced by extrapolation, with the optimal choice being a perfectly coupled Brownian motion (coarse increment equals normalized sum of two fine increments). Theoretical contributions include: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator under decreasing stepsizes, showing minimal asymptotic variance when Brownian increments are maximally correlated; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE under constant and decreasing stepsizes, yielding bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)); (iii) optimized convergence rates in K for decreasing stepsizes, with an MSE rate O(K−4/5), improving upon the O(K−2/3) rate for first-order SG-MCMC. Experiments on linear Gaussian models, an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC), and large-scale probabilistic matrix factorization show improved finite-time bias/MSE and test RMSE under matched wall-clock budgets via parallel execution.

This work connects classical weak-error expansion for SDEs and RR extrapolation (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) with SG-MCMC (Welling–Teh; Teh–Vollmer–Zygalakis; Chen–Ding–Carin). To my knowledge, applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and analyzing variance control via correlated Brownian increments in the presence of stochastic gradients is novel and bridges two previously separate lines.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable idea: RR extrapolation can be layered on top of existing SG-MCMC schemes without higher-order integrators, and maps naturally to parallel hardware.
- Rigorous theory: CLT with explicit scaling regimes; non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds; clear optimization of stepsize schedules leading to improved rates (K−4/5 vs K−2/3).
- Variance control via Brownian coupling is well-motivated and theoretically justified; extends results known for exact-gradient SDEs to SG-MCMC.
- Empirical validation aligns with theory on synthetic problems and demonstrates wall-clock efficiency on large datasets; extension to SGHMC shows generality.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions are strong: the CLT proof requires high smoothness (solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C9) and fairly technical conditions on U and on the gradient noise L with high-order bounded moments. While standard in weak-error analyses, the paper calls them “mild”; a more candid discussion of practicality and the class of posteriors covered (e.g., dissipativity, local Lipschitzness, tails) would help.
- Gradient estimator coupling: the theory assumes the two chains’ stochastic gradients share the same law L; the text notes that different mini-batch sizes reintroduce O(γ) bias. In practice, further variance reduction likely requires coupling the mini-batch indices as well (common random mini-batches across levels), but this is not analyzed or explored empirically. Clarify whether mini-batches were shared across chains in experiments.
- Parallelism fairness: empirical comparisons equalize wall-clock by running two chains in parallel, but this presumes access to additional cores. On a fixed-core budget, SGRRLD doubles gradient computations; comparisons at equal gradient evaluations (or single-threaded time) would be informative.
- Limited breadth of baselines: comparisons omit variance-reduced SG-MCMC (e.g., control variates, SVRG/SAGA-based samplers), preconditioned SGLD/SGHMC thermostats, or higher-order SGLD variants; the SGHMC comparison includes a splitting integrator but only in a narrow setting.
- Practical metrics: the paper focuses on bias and MSE of posterior variance or RMSE in matrix factorization; effective sample size, autocorrelation/mixing, and sensitivity to stepsize and batch size would strengthen the case.
- Notational/typing issues: Eq. (5) indexing appears off; some minor typos and clarity issues in defining the estimator and the coupling could be improved. Pseudo-code is relegated to the supplement.

Quality
The analysis appears technically sound and is consistent with known results when specialized. The finite-time bounds are interpretable and match the empirical trends. Some constants depend on high-order moments of gradient noise and dimension; making those dependencies explicit (even qualitatively) would improve interpretability.

Clarity
Overall clear, though dense. The construction of the RR estimator, time-averaging weights, and the precise coupling used in code would benefit from a concise algorithm box in the main text. A clearer statement of assumptions (U’s dissipativity, smoothness, gradient noise moment conditions) in the main body would help readers assess applicability.

Originality
The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with an analysis that handles stochastic gradients and correlated Brownian increments is, to my knowledge, new. The idea is a neat synthesis rather than an incremental tweak.

Significance
If adopted, this could provide a generally applicable accuracy–cost tradeoff improvement for SG-MCMC on parallel hardware, achieving higher-order bias cancellation without higher-order integrators. The K−4/5 rate improvement is theoretically meaningful and practically visible in the experiments.

Questions for the authors
- Do you couple the mini-batches across the coarse and fine chains at each level? If not, how sensitive are results to such coupling? Could you analyze the benefit/harm of sharing mini-batches (common random numbers for gradient noise)?
- How do the non-asymptotic constants scale with dimension d and mini-batch size B? Any empirical evidence on high-dimensional posteriors (e.g., d > 100)?
- What is the communication/synchronization cost to enforce the Brownian coupling (Eq. 7) in parallel? Is it negligible in your setup?
- Can you report ESS/time for a few observables to quantify mixing?
- How does SGRRLD compare to control-variate SG-MCMC (e.g., SAGA-LD, SVRG-LD) under equal compute?

Pros
- Simple, general method with solid theory and practical gains
- Parallel-friendly design
- Extends to SGHMC and likely other SG-MCMC schemes

Cons
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; unclear practical scope
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons depends on extra cores
- Limited baselines and diagnostics; coupling of gradient noise not explored
