Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD to reduce discretization bias while controlling variance. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and forms a control-variate-style estimator: a weighted difference of empirical averages that cancels the O(γ) bias term under a Talay–Tubaro expansion, aiming for O(γ^2) bias. To prevent a variance blow-up, the Gaussian noises in the two chains are correlated so that Z(γ) ≈ (Z(γ/2)1+Z(γ/2)2)/√2, aligning with theoretical results on variance-optimal duplication for SDEs.

The paper provides: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator with asymptotic variance minimized by perfect correlation of the Gaussian increments; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ^2+1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4+1/(Kγ)) for constant step size, yielding optimal MSE O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3}) for first-order SG-MCMC without RR; and (iii) convergence rates under decreasing step sizes γk ∝ k^{-α}, with optimal α = 1/5 for the RR estimator (rate O(K^{-2/5}) vs O(K^{-1/3}) for SGLD). The approach is extended to SGHMC (SGRRHMC), with empirical performance comparable to higher-order splitting methods [10] at larger γ. Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate reduced bias and improved RMSE–time tradeoffs.

This work builds on: SGLD [1], analyses of SG-MCMC (e.g., [19,20]), RR extrapolation for SDEs and duplicated diffusions [21,23], and higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC [10]. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, RR extrapolation has not been applied to SG-MCMC prior to this.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are plausible and consistent with classical RR arguments for SDEs [21,23]. The requirement of high smoothness (solution g to the Poisson equation in C^9 and seventh-order Taylor expansions) is standard in RR analyses but strong; the paper defers full conditions and proofs to the supplement, which is acceptable. The result that Brownian noise dominates stochastic gradient noise under decreasing step sizes is reasonable: gradient noise contributes at O(γ) per step in the drift, whereas the injected Gaussian noise is O(√γ). The dependence of constants on gradient estimator moments is noted.
- Estimator construction: Bias reduction relies on unbiased stochastic gradients with the same distribution for the two chains; the paper correctly cautions that mismatched mini-batch distributions reintroduce O(γ) bias. Correlated Gaussian increments are used to control variance as in [23]. This is implementable because the injected noise is under algorithmic control.
- Rates: The non-asymptotic bounds support the MSE improvement O(K^{-4/5}) under optimal constant-γ tuning. Under decreasing γk, the estimator convergence rate O(K^{-2/5}) aligns with the CLT regimes discussed. The paper could more clearly reconcile the different settings (constant vs decreasing step sizes) when reporting rates in the introduction to avoid confusion.
- Generality: The extension to SGHMC is conceptually straightforward (apply RR to the integrator). The empirical evidence supports that RR can bring first-order SGHMC close to higher-order schemes.
- Potential issues: (i) There is a likely typographical error in Chain 2’s first update, where θ(γ/2)2k+1 appears on both sides of the equation; it should update from θ(γ/2)2k. (ii) While the theory emphasizes Brownian correlation, the effect of correlating mini-batches across chains is not explored; although not required for bias, it could reduce variance in finite K. (iii) The claim that SGLD “cannot benefit” from parallelism is too strong; SGLD can parallelize gradient computations and run multiple chains. A fair parallel baseline would be two independent SGLD chains averaged under the same wall-clock budget.

Clarity
The presentation is generally clear, with a precise description of the estimator and assumptions at a high level. Theorems are stated crisply. Notation is dense; some reminders (e.g., which rates refer to constant vs decreasing step size) would improve readability. Including the explicit forms of σR^2 and μR in the main text (or at least a brief discussion of their dependence) would aid intuition. Ensure the Chain 2 update typo is corrected. Providing pseudo-code in the main paper would help reproducibility.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with rigorous analysis (CLT and finite-time bounds) appears novel. Prior RR work focuses on exact-gradient SDE discretizations; this paper adapts and analyzes RR in the presence of stochastic gradients. The variance-control via correlated Gaussian increments mirrors duplicated diffusion ideas but is new in SG-MCMC.

Significance
The method is simple, generic, and parallelizable, improving finite-time and asymptotic rates over first-order SG-MCMC without requiring higher-order integrators. This can be valuable in large-scale Bayesian inference. The empirical studies, including a real large-scale recommendation task, indicate practical gains.

Strengths
- Clear and principled method with minimal implementation overhead.
- Theoretical advances: CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and optimal correlation result.
- Demonstrated generality (SGLD, SGHMC).
- Strong empirical support on synthetic and large-scale data; improved RMSE–time performance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Fairness of parallel comparisons: include a baseline with two independent SGLD chains averaged under equal wall-clock time, and/or report single-threaded overhead.
- Assumptions: discuss the practical scope of smoothness/Poisson regularity conditions and whether results extend to non-smooth posteriors.
- Clarify rate statements across regimes (constant vs decreasing step sizes) and emphasize which are MSE vs estimator error.
- Typographic correctness in Chain 2 update; add pseudo-code in main text.
- Ablations: study the impact of mini-batch correlation across chains; test regimes where gradient noise is large (very small B) to validate the “Gaussian noise dominates” claim empirically.
- Broader baselines: compare to higher-order SG-MCMC (e.g., [10]) in the matrix factorization task and to control-variate SG-MCMC variants.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-founded application of RR to SG-MCMC with rigorous analysis.
- Improved theoretical rates and practical performance.
- Generic, easy to adopt, and compatible with parallel hardware.

Arguments against acceptance
- Parallel fairness baseline missing; unclear how much gain remains if SGLD also uses parallel resources similarly.
- Strong smoothness assumptions; limited discussion of robustness to non-ideal conditions.
- Minor clarity issues (typo, rate regimes).

Overall, the paper offers a technically sound and practically useful contribution to scalable Bayesian sampling, with room for strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying assumptions.
