## Peer Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, requiring two parallel chains with differing step sizes, while maintaining reasonable variance through correlated Brownian motions. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (MSE of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs. SGLD’s O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>)) and show generality, claiming applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms.  Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task support the theoretical results, indicating improved accuracy and efficiency.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing field of SG-MCMC, referencing key works like Welling & Teh (2011) on SGLD and recent advancements in bias reduction and higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015).  The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) is well-established, although its application to SG-MCMC is novel. The authors also appropriately highlight related work in Hamiltonian Monte Carlo variations.  This context clearly demonstrates the paper's contribution as an extension to existing techniques rather than a radically new paradigm.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a strong theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE.  The convergence rate analysis (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>)) is a significant theoretical result, especially relative to standard SGLD.
* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. Leveraging correlated Brownian motions to control variance is also a thoughtful addition.
* **Generality:** The claim that SGRRLD can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms (beyond SGLD) broadens its potential impact. The demonstration with SGHMC is a positive step towards establishing this generality.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while limited in scope, provide initial evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world datasets are encouraging.
* **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The core idea of the algorithm is presented clearly.  The mathematical notation is generally understandable, and the algorithm is formally defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Complexity:** The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, is dense and relies on numerous assumptions.  The full implications of these assumptions (and their practicality) are not always clearly discussed.  Readers lacking a strong background in stochastic differential equations might struggle with the details.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited.  The matrix factorization example is good, but more extensive evaluations across diverse datasets and model architectures would strengthen the claims of generality and practical utility. The ablation study could be improved by testing the impact of correlation between the two chains.
* **Computational Overhead:**  The paper acknowledges the parallelizability of SGRRLD but doesn’t comprehensively analyze the overhead introduced by running two chains and maintaining correlated Brownian motions.  A more detailed computational cost analysis would be helpful. While wall-clock time is comparable in the experiments presented, it’s not clear how this scales with problem size or model complexity.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions finding optimal step size parameters, but doesn't thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of performance to these choices or provide guidance for practitioners on how to tune them effectively.
* **Clarity in certain sections**: The discussion about the impact of correlated gradients on SGRRLD’s efficiency could be clarified. The condition that mini-batch size has to be the same for both chains, while stated, isn't fully motivated or explained.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to the field of SG-MCMC.
* The improved convergence rate is a significant theoretical result.
* The empirical results, though limited, support the theoretical claims.
* The potential for broad applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms is promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is dense and may be inaccessible to a broad audience.
* The experimental evaluation lacks comprehensive testing across diverse scenarios.
* The computational overhead of running two chains is not fully analyzed.
* The parameter sensitivity and tuning guidance are insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field.  The theoretical work is strong, and the experimental results are encouraging. While the technical complexity and limited scope of the experiments are weaknesses, they do not fundamentally invalidate the contribution. The novelty of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and the potential for improved performance make this paper worthy of acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions).

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Include more diverse datasets and model architectures. Consider experiments demonstrating the scalability of the algorithm.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by running two chains.
* **Parameter Tuning Guidance:** Include a more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity and provide guidance for practitioners on how to tune the algorithm effectively.
* **Clarify Assumptions:** Explicitly state and discuss the implications of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis.
* **Improve Clarity:** Refine the explanation of correlated gradients.
