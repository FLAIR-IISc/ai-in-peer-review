### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new scalable MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the discretization bias of existing Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the SGLD algorithm. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. To prevent the variance from increasing excessively due to the extrapolation, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motion increments for the two chains.

The paper provides a formal theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. It establishes a Central Limit Theorem (CLT) for the proposed estimator and derives non-asymptotic bounds for its bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. This improved rate matches the theoretical accuracy of methods based on more complex higher-order integrators, while only requiring the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme.

The work is situated in the context of scalable Bayesian inference, building directly upon SGLD [1] and its extensions. It presents an alternative approach to bias reduction compared to methods using higher-order integrators [10]. While RR extrapolation is a known technique for standard SDEs with exact gradients [21, 23], its application and rigorous analysis in the stochastic gradient setting is the main contribution of this paper. The authors validate their theoretical claims through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, showing that SGRRLD achieves higher accuracy than SGLD in a given amount of wall-clock time.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous analysis, including a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The proofs, sketched in the main text and detailed in the supplement, appear to follow established techniques from the analysis of SDEs and their discretizations, correctly adapted to the SG-MCMC setting. The experiments are carefully designed to verify the theoretical predictions. For instance, the experiments directly test the dependence of bias and MSE on the step size γ and the number of iterations K, confirming the derived convergence rates. The evaluation on a large-scale real-world problem demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method.

A minor weakness is that the main paper relies heavily on the supplementary material for the precise technical assumptions under which the theorems hold, as well as for the full proofs. While this is common practice, it makes a complete assessment of the technical soundness from the main text alone difficult.

#### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for reducing discretization bias in SG-MCMC is clearly articulated. The background on SGLD and RR extrapolation is presented concisely, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with the area. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the implications of the main theoretical results are explained well. The figures in the experimental section are informative and effectively illustrate the performance gains of SGRRLD over the baseline methods. A small addition of an algorithm box in the main paper would have further improved its self-containedness.

#### Originality

The originality of this work lies in the novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting. While neither SG-MCMC nor RR extrapolation are new concepts, their combination is novel and addresses the important problem of bias reduction in a new way. Previous work on reducing this bias focused primarily on developing higher-order integrators [10], which can be more complex to implement. This paper offers an elegant and conceptually simple alternative. The analysis of how to control the variance of the RR estimator using correlated noise in the SG-MCMC context is also a key original contribution, extending results from the exact gradient setting [23]. The demonstration that the idea is generalizable (e.g., to SGHMC) further strengthens its novelty.

#### Significance

The work is significant for both researchers and practitioners in scalable Bayesian inference. It provides a practical and effective method for improving the accuracy of a widely used class of algorithms. The theoretical improvement in the MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is substantial. From a practical standpoint, the method is simple to implement, as it only requires running two instances of a standard first-order algorithm. The inherent parallelism of the approach is a major advantage, allowing it to achieve higher accuracy in the same wall-clock time as SGLD, provided parallel resources are available. These properties make it likely that the proposed technique will be adopted by users of SG-MCMC methods and could inspire further research into variance reduction and acceleration techniques for stochastic samplers.

The primary trade-off is the requirement of double the computational cost per effective iteration. While the experiments show this is beneficial when parallelized, it is a consideration for resource-constrained environments.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution to the important problem of bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
*   The proposed method is supported by strong theoretical guarantees, demonstrating a significant improvement in the convergence rate of the MSE.
*   The approach is conceptually simple, easy to implement, and generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The inherent parallelism makes the method computationally efficient in practice, as demonstrated by wall-clock time comparisons on a large-scale problem.
*   The paper is very well-written and the experiments convincingly validate the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core components of the method (SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation) are themselves not new; the novelty is in their combination and analysis.
*   The method requires twice the computational resources of a standard SGLD chain, which could be a limitation in some settings.
*   A full verification of the technical claims requires a deep dive into the supplementary material, as the main paper omits the precise assumptions for the theorems.
