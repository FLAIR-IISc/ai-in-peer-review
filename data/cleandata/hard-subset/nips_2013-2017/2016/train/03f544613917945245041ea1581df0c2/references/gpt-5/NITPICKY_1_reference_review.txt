Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which combines SG-MCMC with Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce discretization bias. The method runs two SGLD chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon, and forms a time-weighted combination 2π̂γ/2(f) − π̂γ(f). To control the variance blow-up that typically accompanies RR, the authors use correlated Brownian increments, showing (under technical conditions) that variance is minimized when the increments are perfectly correlated. They prove a CLT for the RR estimator with decreasing steps, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE with general step schedules, yielding improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC: for constant steps, MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) leading to optimal O(K^−4/5), versus O(K^−2/3) for SGLD. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and matrix factorization demonstrate empirical gains; an SGHMC variant is also explored.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing and natural application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC; the coupling of Brownian increments is well-motivated and aligns with known results in the diffusion literature.
- Theoretical contributions include a CLT and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds that recover improved rates, matching higher-order integrators without using them explicitly.
- The estimator is generic and, in principle, applicable to various SG-MCMC algorithms.
- Experiments on synthetic and large-scale recommendation tasks show consistent empirical improvements at matched wall-clock time.
- Parallelizability is an attractive practical feature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Potentially serious typo/issue in the algorithm definition (Eq. (5)): the stochastic gradient for the first half step is evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k+1, i.e., the future iterate, making the update implicit; likewise, the second line also uses θ(γ/2)2k+1. This seems unintended and would fundamentally change the algorithm (and computational cost). Please clarify; if this is a typo, correct it and confirm that all theory/experiments used the explicit scheme.
- Inconsistency in the noise scaling between ULA and SGLD: Eq. (1) uses √2 dB, so Euler–Maruyama should be θk+1 = θk − γ∇U(θk) + √(2γ)Z. The ULA line in Sec. 2.1 instead uses √γ Z (missing √2), while Eq. (2) uses √(2γ) Z. This matters for constants in the expansions and could affect both theory and practice.
- Heavy reliance on unshown technical conditions (“see Supplement”), including existence of a C^9 solution to the Poisson equation and high-order Taylor expansions. These assumptions are strong and not verified for typical ML posteriors (e.g., non-smooth likelihoods, heavy tails). The paper should clearly state the assumptions in the main text and discuss their realism.
- Ambiguity in the “asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4)” claim: bias/variance of what (time-averaged estimator, invariant measure error)? The manuscript should precisely define the object and norm/metric used.
- The claim that “the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” is asserted, but the precise scaling regime is not made explicit in the main text (constant step sizes in experiments vs. decreasing steps in theory; variance of gradient estimator depending on θ and minibatch size). A clear, quantitative comparison is needed to justify the conclusion and to delineate when SGRRLD provides net gains.
- Correlated Brownian increments are essential to variance control, but it is unclear if they were actually enforced in experiments (“two independent processes” is not sufficient to guarantee the coupling in Eq. (7)/(8)). The paper should specify how the correlation was implemented across processes and whether minibatch selections were also coupled.
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons: SGRRLD uses two chains in parallel, whereas the paper asserts SGLD “cannot benefit” from parallelism. This is debatable: SGLD can run multiple chains in parallel or parallelize minibatch computations. A more equitable comparison would either (a) give SGLD twice the iterations or (b) report results at equal total gradient evaluations, and also include a parallel multi-chain SGLD baseline.
- Experimental evaluation lacks ablations isolating key design choices (coupled vs. independent Gaussians and minibatches; effect of mini-batch size; sensitivity to step size schedules). The synthetic validations of K-exponent optimality are informative but limited; more dimensions and different likelihoods would strengthen the case.
- Limited baselines. Beyond SGLD and one SGHMC variant with a higher-order integrator, there is no comparison to other variance-reduction SG-MCMC methods or control-variate approaches (even within the 2015–2016 literature).
- Clarity/notation issues throughout (see below) impede reproducibility.

Clarity and presentation issues (partial list)
- Multiple typographical errors and grammatical issues: “variace,” “Futher,” “twice more time,” etc.
- Notation inconsistency: “Id” for identity vs. I, J as dimensions; “an” used ambiguously; spacing around superscripts and indices (e.g., θ (γ/2) 2k+1).
- Reference formatting is poor (repeated commas, inconsistent styles, missing venues/pages, stray text like “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” inside the main text).
- Important algorithmic details relegated to the supplement (pseudocode, conditions), yet central for understanding and reproducing results.
- The derivations are only sketched, with key constants (e.g., in Theorem 2) left opaque.

Originality and significance
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a natural and interesting idea; the variance-control via coupling is known in SDE contexts but less explored for SG-MCMC. The theoretical rates are appealing and, if correct under practical assumptions, could have impact.
- However, the novelty is somewhat incremental (RR + coupling is classic), and the practical significance hinges on careful implementation (couplings), fair comparisons, and robustness to realistic model/pathologies. The current version underspecifies these aspects and overstates parallelization advantages.

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct/clarify Eq. (5): are the gradients evaluated at θ2k or θ2k+1? Was an implicit scheme actually used anywhere?
2. Were Brownian increments explicitly coupled across processes as in Eq. (7)/(8)? Were minibatches also coupled? Please describe the exact procedure.
3. Which precise conditions on U, f, and L are required for Theorems 1–2? Any results under less stringent smoothness?
4. Can you report comparisons at equal total gradient evaluations and with parallel multi-chain SGLD?
5. How sensitive are gains to B and to gradient noise variance? Any cases where RR harms performance?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, appealing idea with solid theoretical framing.
- Potentially useful in practice, with encouraging empirical results.
- Generic and extensible to other SG-MCMC methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Algorithmic typo/ambiguity (Eq. (5)) and noise scaling inconsistency undermine confidence.
- Heavy, unstated assumptions; proofs deferred; limited verification under realistic conditions.
- Questionable fairness in experimental comparisons; missing ablations on crucial couplings.
- Numerous clarity/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current manuscript has too many technical ambiguities, missing details, and presentation issues to recommend acceptance. A revised version addressing the algorithmic correctness, experimental fairness, and clarity could be much stronger.
