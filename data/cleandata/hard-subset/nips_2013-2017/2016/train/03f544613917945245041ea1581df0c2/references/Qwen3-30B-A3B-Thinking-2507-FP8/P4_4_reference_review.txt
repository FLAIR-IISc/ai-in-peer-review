

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. Building on SGLD [1] and extending the RR extrapolation technique for SDEs [21, 23], the authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The paper establishes theoretical guarantees including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds, and validates the approach through experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks. The work relates to recent NIPS papers on SG-MCMC [6, 10] and extends them by showing how RR extrapolation can achieve higher-order accuracy with first-order integrators.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-structured theorems (Theorem 1 and 2) establishing the convergence properties of SGRRLD. The authors carefully derive bias and MSE bounds, showing the improved rates compared to existing SG-MCMC methods. The proofs follow established techniques for SDE discretizations while introducing the novel RR extrapolation framework.

2. **Novelty**: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrator-based methods using only first-order numerical integrators, which is theoretically significant and practically valuable for large-scale applications.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on synthetic data (Gaussian model) and real-world matrix factorization tasks (MovieLens datasets) consistently support the theoretical claims. The results show significant improvements in MSE and bias reduction over SGLD, with clear evidence of the predicted convergence rates.

4. **Practical Considerations**: The paper correctly identifies that SGRRLD's parallelizable nature (two independent chains) makes it well-suited for distributed architectures, addressing a key limitation of sequential SG-MCMC methods. The implementation details for the large-scale experiments are well-described.

## Weaknesses
1. **Assumption Limitations**: While the paper establishes the theoretical guarantees under "appropriate conditions," it doesn't thoroughly discuss how restrictive these conditions are in practice. For instance, the requirement for specific correlation structures between Brownian motions (Equation 8) and the technical conditions for the central limit theorem could limit applicability in some settings.

2. **Limited Method Comparison**: Although the paper applies the RR technique to SGHMC (SGRRHMC), it only provides limited experimental validation for this extension. A more comprehensive comparison with higher-order integrator-based methods across multiple SG-MCMC variants would strengthen the claim that SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper mentions the parallel nature of SGRRLD but lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis. While it notes that SGRRLD achieves similar wall-clock time as SGLD, it doesn't quantify the computational overhead of running two chains versus one, which is important for practical adoption.

4. **Failure Case Analysis**: The paper doesn't explore scenarios where the RR extrapolation might fail or where the assumptions for the theoretical guarantees are violated. A discussion of such limitations would provide a more balanced assessment of the method's applicability.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of SG-MCMC by introducing a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical implications for large-scale Bayesian inference are significant. The method achieves a clear improvement in convergence rates over existing first-order SG-MCMC methods while maintaining the parallelizability that makes SG-MCMC attractive for large-scale applications.

The paper advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by demonstrating that higher-order accuracy can be achieved with simpler first-order integrators, which is both theoretically interesting and practically valuable. The connection to existing NIPS work on SG-MCMC [1, 6, 10] is well-established, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from previous contributions.

The main limitations are the lack of detailed discussion about the practical restrictions of the theoretical assumptions and the incomplete exploration of method comparisons beyond SGLD. However, these do not significantly undermine the paper's core contributions.

**Pro Acceptance**: Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis, clear improvements in convergence rates, practical parallelization benefits, and thorough experimental validation.

**Con Acceptance**: Limited discussion of practical implementation constraints and failure cases; more comprehensive comparison with higher-order methods would be beneficial.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality: it is technically sound with well-supported claims; clearly written with sufficient detail; novel in its application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC; and significant for advancing the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference. The work is well-suited for NIPS and would be of interest to the machine learning community working on scalable Bayesian methods.
