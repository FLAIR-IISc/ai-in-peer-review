Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC estimators (primarily SGLD) to cancel the O(γ) time-discretization bias using two coupled chains with step sizes γ and γ/2. The estimator forms 2π̂γ/2(f) − π̂γ(f) by running the γ/2 chain twice per γ step and using correlated Brownian increments to control the variance. The authors establish a CLT for the extrapolated estimator under decreasing step sizes and provide non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. For constant step size, optimizing γ yields MSE O(K−4/5) (vs. O(K−2/3) for SGLD). Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization suggest reductions in bias/MSE and improved wall-clock RMSE when the two chains are run in parallel.

Relation to prior work
RR extrapolation for SDE weak error is classical (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup). Duplicated diffusions and variance control via common Brownian increments for RR are also known. In SG-MCMC, high-order integrators to reduce bias were studied (Chen et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2015). This work appears to be the first to adapt RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, show a CLT and finite-time bounds for the extrapolated time-averaged estimator, and demonstrate empirical benefits relative to SGLD/SGHMC.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: the RR idea is cleanly ported to SG-MCMC and should be applicable beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC).
- Theory: a CLT for the extrapolated estimator and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds that match the intuition (optimal α = 1/5 for decreasing steps; improved K−4/5 MSE rate).
- Insight on variance control: shows that the Brownian coupling choice is crucial and optimal at Σ = Id (perfect correlation).
- Empirics: Linear-Gaussian experiments corroborate predicted scalings; SGHMC variant compared to higher-order splitting shows similar accuracy at larger steps; large-scale MF experiments indicate practical gains.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Inconsistent discretization noise scaling. The ULA update (Sec. 2.1) uses +√γ Zk+1, while the SDE is dθ = −∇U dt + √2 dB; the correct Euler step should be +√(2γ) Z. Later SGLD uses “√ 2γ” (spacing ambiguous). This inconsistency should be corrected throughout.
- Chain 2 update (Eq. (5)) appears to evaluate the gradient at θ(γ/2)2k+1 in the first substep; it should be at θ(γ/2)2k. This is more than a typo—it changes the algorithm.
- Correlated Gaussians: Eq. (8) defines Z(γ) via Σ but also claims optimality at Σ = Id. With the mis-scaled noise noted above, it is hard to verify the covariance matching; please provide exact covariance checks in the main text.
- Assumptions are heavy and mostly deferred. The CLT requires a Poisson equation solution g ∈ C^9 and multiple terms of Taylor expansion; the non-asymptotic bounds hide constants depending on moments of the gradient estimator. The paper does not spell out key conditions on L (e.g., bounded moments, tail behavior, dependence on θ), nor stability/ergodicity conditions for the coupled scheme in the presence of gradient noise. The statement “Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” is asserted but not quantified vis-à-vis Var(∇Û).
- Bias from stochastic gradients: While RR cancels the O(γ) weak error from Euler discretization, SG-MCMC introduces additional state-dependent noise via minibatch gradients. The paper requires the same distribution L in both chains but does not analyze how correlation of gradient estimators across chains affects the residual bias/variance or whether common minibatches are beneficial/necessary.

Methodology and fairness of experiments
- Parallelization claims are overstated. The paper claims “SGLD cannot benefit from this parallel computation architecture due to its inherently sequential nature.” SGLD can parallelize minibatch gradient computation, exploit vectorization/GPU, or run multiple chains. A fair wall-clock comparison should control for total compute resources (e.g., same number of cores/GPUs) and report throughput-normalized performance. As written, SGRRLD appears to use twice the compute (two chains) at the same wall-clock time.
- SGHMC comparison lacks tuning detail (friction, mass, injected noise calibration). Without careful, matched tuning this comparison is hard to interpret.
- Reproducibility: No code or pseudocode in the main text. Key implementation choices (mini-batch coupling, random seed reuse, how Z coupling is realized with variable step sizes) are missing.

Positioning and novelty
- The primary novelty is adapting RR to SG-MCMC and carrying over the variance-control coupling; most theoretical arguments mirror known RR analyses with additional technicalities due to stochastic gradients. This is a reasonable but incremental contribution; significance rests on practical impact, which is weakened by the fairness concerns and missing details.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typographical issues: “RichardsonRomberg” (missing hyphen), “variace,” “Futher,” “time more time,” inconsistent notation for ΓK, π̂R, and the SGLD noise term, missing parentheses, and several minor LaTeX spacing artifacts.
- Several references are incomplete or malformed (e.g., repeated commas, missing years/pages, inconsistent journal formatting).

Questions for the authors
- Please correct Eq. (5) (gradient evaluation point) and the noise scaling everywhere. Provide a compact pseudocode in the main paper.
- What precise assumptions on L (unbiasedness, bounded moments, Lipschitz-in-θ, independence across steps and chains) are required for Theorems 1–2? Can you quantify when “Gaussian noise dominates” in terms of Var(∇Û) and γ?
- How should one best correlate the stochastic gradients across chains (same minibatch indices vs independent) for variance reduction? Is there a theoretical statement?
- Can you provide resource-normalized comparisons (e.g., same total FLOPs or cores) and/or GPU results? How does SGRRLD fare when SGLD parallelizes gradient computation?
- How sensitive are results to mis-specified Σ, or to not coupling the Brownian increments?

Pros
- Clear idea; theoretically grounded; improved rates; simple to implement in principle.
- Demonstrates generic applicability (SGLD, SGHMC).
- Useful practical guidance on Brownian coupling.

Cons
- Multiple technical/notation errors and heavy reliance on supplement for key assumptions.
- Questionable fairness in experimental comparisons and missing implementation details.
- Incremental novelty relative to established RR for SDEs and duplicated diffusions.
- Overstated claims about parallelization advantages.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (weak reject). The idea is promising and likely publishable with corrections, stronger experimental methodology (resource-controlled comparisons), and clearer, self-contained assumptions and algorithmic specification. However, the current version contains several technical inaccuracies and presentation issues that, taken together, outweigh the contributions for a NeurIPS acceptance.

Confidence
High on technical/notation concerns; moderate on empirical significance pending fair comparisons.
