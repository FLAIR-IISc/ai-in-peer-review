This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to improve the accuracy of the popular SGLD algorithm. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation method, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to reduce the discretization bias inherent in SGLD's Euler-Maruyama integrator. The method involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs. To control the variance of the resulting estimator, the method employs correlated noise between the two chains. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD can achieve a faster convergence rate for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The approach is evaluated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, showing empirical improvements over SGLD.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The primary strength of this work is that it is grounded in a well-established and theoretically understood numerical method. Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a standard tool for improving the accuracy of numerical integrators for SDEs. Applying this established technique to the SG-MCMC setting is a logical and well-motivated contribution, providing a principled way to reduce integrator bias.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a commendable theoretical treatment of the proposed algorithm. The authors derive a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE. The key result—that SGRRLD achieves an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) with an optimal step-size schedule—is a significant theoretical improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD and represents a solid contribution to the SG-MCMC literature.

3.  **Generality of the Approach:** The authors correctly point out that the RR extrapolation technique is not limited to SGLD. They demonstrate this by applying it to SGHMC in an experiment, suggesting that this bias-reduction strategy could be a valuable tool for a wide range of existing and future SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper's central claim is that it can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) using only a first-order integrator. While this is an appealing proposition, the experimental validation of this claim is insufficient. The comparison against a higher-order method (SGHMC-s) is confined to a single synthetic experiment (Figure 4). To substantiate this significant claim, a direct comparison against a higher-order SGLD integrator on the more compelling large-scale matrix factorization problem would be necessary. As it stands, the primary baseline is standard SGLD, which, while essential, does not fully contextualize the performance of the proposed method against other bias-reduction techniques.

2.  **Understated Computational Cost and Trade-offs:** The SGRRLD algorithm requires three gradient evaluations for each effective "step" (one for the γ-chain and two for the γ/2-chain). The authors address this by running the two chains in parallel and comparing wall-clock time, which is a fair evaluation given the availability of parallel resources. However, this relies on the assumption that at least two cores are available. In a resource-constrained or single-core setting, SGRRLD would be approximately three times slower per iteration than SGLD. This trade-off between computational cost and statistical efficiency is not discussed sufficiently. A plot of RMSE versus the total number of gradient computations, in addition to wall-clock time, would provide a more complete and hardware-agnostic picture of the algorithm's efficiency.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The experiments are conducted on a linear Gaussian model and a probabilistic matrix factorization model. While these are reasonable choices, both models have convex or relatively well-behaved posterior landscapes. The performance of SG-MCMC methods can differ significantly on the highly non-convex problems frequently encountered in modern machine learning, such as Bayesian neural networks. While the provided theory does not necessarily preclude such applications, the strong technical assumptions required for the proofs may not hold in those settings. The lack of experiments on more challenging, non-convex models limits the assessment of the method's practical robustness.

**Summary of evaluation:**

The paper presents a methodologically sound and theoretically rigorous approach to improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC methods. The application of a classic numerical technique to this modern machine learning context is elegant. The theoretical results demonstrating improved convergence rates are a clear contribution. However, the experimental validation, while positive, is not as comprehensive as one would hope. The comparison against state-of-the-art baselines is limited, and the discussion of the inherent computational trade-offs could be more transparent.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel application of a well-established numerical method (RR extrapolation) to the SG-MCMC framework.
*   Strong and comprehensive theoretical analysis showing improved convergence rates over SGLD.
*   The method is conceptually simple, general, and supported by positive experimental results on large-scale data.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The claim of achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators is not sufficiently validated experimentally against such methods on the main, large-scale task.
*   The computational overhead is significant, and the practical efficiency trade-offs are not fully explored.
*   Experiments are limited to models with relatively simple posterior geometries.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly its theoretical contribution and sound methodological basis, outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference. However, the authors should be encouraged to address the weaknesses in the final version, especially by adding a more direct comparison to a higher-order integrator on the matrix factorization task and by providing a clearer discussion of the computational costs.
