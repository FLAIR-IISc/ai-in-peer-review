This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), aiming to reduce bias by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combine their outputs to achieve a higher-order convergence rate. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed method. The theoretical analysis appears sound, building upon existing work on SGLD and RR extrapolation for SDEs. The inclusion of convergence analysis, both asymptotic (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic (Theorem 2), is commendable. The experiments, while not extensive, provide some evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The connection to existing SG-MCMC methods, particularly SGHMC, and the demonstration of SGRRLD's generality are positive aspects.

However, despite a technically sound approach, I have significant reservations about the overall significance and originality of the work as presented. My primary concern is the limited practical advantage demonstrated, especially considering the increased complexity. While theoretically showing faster convergence (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>)), the experimental results, even on the larger matrix factorization task, show only a modest improvement in performance that could plausibly be attributed to careful parameter tuning rather than a fundamental algorithmic advance. The claim of achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators with a first-order method is somewhat overstated; the increased computational cost of running two chains somewhat diminishes this advantage. Furthermore, the experiments are performed with relatively small batch sizes and step sizes, potentially obscuring the benefits of the proposed approach in more realistic, large-scale scenarios. 

The reliance on correlated Brownian motions, while theoretically motivated, adds complexity and requires careful implementation. The discussion on the impact of correlated gradients is somewhat superficial. The experimental setup, specifically the choice of step sizes and the number of iterations, feels arbitrarily chosen, lacking a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis. The performance gains on the matrix factorization task, while present, aren’t quantified sufficiently to establish a clear advantage over existing optimized SGLD implementations.  The authors state that running the chains in parallel mitigates the increased computation, but don't quantify this speedup, and implicitly assume access to parallel hardware.

The related work section is adequate but could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in variance reduction techniques for SGMCMC. The claim of originality is also weakened by the fact that RR extrapolation is a well-established numerical technique; the novelty lies in its application to SGMCMC, which, while valuable, is not groundbreaking.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and theoretically well-motivated.
*   Clear presentation of the method and analysis.
*   Demonstrates potential for improving convergence rates.
*   General applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modest experimental improvements, potentially not statistically significant.
*   Increased complexity without a commensurate practical benefit.
*   Limited sensitivity analysis and parameter tuning exploration.
*   Incremental originality – application of an existing technique.
*   The benefits are not clearly demonstrated on truly large-scale problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the technical work is solid and the paper is well-written, the practical significance of the proposed method is not convincingly demonstrated. The improvements observed in the experiments are too modest to justify the added complexity. The paper lacks a compelling argument for why practitioners should adopt SGRRLD over existing, well-optimized SGLD implementations or higher-order methods. I encourage the authors to address the limitations outlined above, conduct more extensive experiments on larger datasets, and provide a more thorough analysis of the computational trade-offs before resubmitting.
