## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, dubbed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a numerical acceleration technique, to SGLD. This is achieved by running two Langevin dynamics chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their outputs to reduce bias and improve the rate of convergence. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE) compared to standard SGLD (O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>)), and showcase empirical validation on both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC) and a real-world application (matrix factorization for movie recommendation).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the growing body of work on SG-MCMC, starting with Welling & Teh (2011) and SGLD. It acknowledges and positions itself relative to subsequent extensions of SGLD (e.g., SGHMC, covariance-controlled methods) and recent theoretical analyses bounding the bias and MSE of SG-MCMC algorithms (Chen et al., 2015). The application of RR extrapolation to SDEs, originating in the work of Talay and Tubaro (1990), is a key contribution, bridging numerical analysis techniques with Bayesian inference.  The paper correctly places itself within the context of efforts to improve the efficiency of MCMC for large-scale problems.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to the stochastic gradient setting and MCMC is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees and bounds on bias and MSE. Deriving these bounds, especially for the correlated Brownian motion scenario, is a valuable addition to the field. The asymptotic analysis and the finite-time bounds are clearly presented.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, covering both a simple synthetic case (allowing for analytical verification) and a more realistic large-scale application (matrix factorization). The results consistently demonstrate the performance benefits of SGRRLD.  The comparison with SGHMC and the demonstration of improved accuracy are particularly compelling.
* **Parallelizability:**  The inherent parallel nature of running the two chains required by RR extrapolation is highlighted, which is crucial for scaling to large datasets.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the potential for applying RR extrapolation to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, broadening the impact of their work.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the motivation for each step is well explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Overhead:** While parallelizability is a strength, the paper doesn’t deeply investigate the practical overhead associated with managing two chains.  The experiments show similar wall-clock time, but further investigation into communication costs and synchronization issues in a truly distributed setting would be valuable.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** The discussion regarding correlated gradients feels slightly underdeveloped.  The authors state that the two gradient sequences shouldn’t have different distributions, but don't offer specific guidance on how to achieve this in practice (beyond using the same mini-batch size). More practical considerations about gradient correlation would be useful.
* **Choice of Sigma:** The choice of correlation matrix Σ in equation (8) seems to heavily rely on the case where Σ = Id. While justified, the sensitivity to deviations from this ideal scenario isn’t fully explored.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with SGHMC is insightful, but expanding the comparison to other modern SG-MCMC methods (e.g., those employing adaptive step sizes) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't detail the specific hyperparameter tuning process used for the experiments. More transparency in this area would enhance reproducibility.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to improving SG-MCMC efficiency.
* Strong empirical evidence supporting the claimed benefits.
* Potential for significant impact in scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Con:**

* Practical overhead of running two chains requires further investigation.
* Limited discussion of gradient correlation issues.
*  Comparison could be broadened to more recent SG-MCMC methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  The potential benefits of SGRRLD, particularly for large-scale Bayesian inference, are substantial.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor points:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical overhead associated with running two chains, including potential communication costs in a distributed setting.
2.  Expand on the implications of gradient correlation and offer practical guidance for ensuring a similar distribution between the two gradient sequences.
3.  Consider adding a brief discussion of the sensitivity of performance to deviations from the ideal Σ = Id correlation matrix.
4. Briefly mention how the proposed approach compares to more recent adaptive step size SG-MCMC methods.
