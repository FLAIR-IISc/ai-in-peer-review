Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD to cancel the leading O(γ) weak bias while controlling variance via correlated Gaussian noise across two coupled discretizations with step-sizes γ and γ/2. The estimator time-averages 2 fine-step evaluations minus 1 coarse-step evaluation over matched time horizons. The analysis establishes: (i) a CLT for the extrapolated estimator with minimal asymptotic variance when Brownian increments are maximally correlated (Σ = I), (ii) non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), implying optimal finite-time rates O(K−2/3) for bias and O(K−4/5) for MSE under constant step-size tuning, and (iii) for decreasing steps γk ∝ k−α, an optimal asymptotic rate O(K−2/5) at α = 1/5. Experiments on a Gaussian model (including a dimension sweep and step-size scaling tests), SGHMC (extension termed SGRRHMC), and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support reduced bias/MSE and improved wall-clock efficiency when two chains are run in parallel.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: RR extrapolation has a long history for SDE weak error acceleration, but its application to SG-MCMC appears novel and well-motivated. The idea is simple and broadly applicable: any SG-MCMC with a known weak error expansion could benefit.
- Solid theory: The CLT and finite-time bias/MSE bounds are in line with classical RR for Euler–Maruyama and extend it to stochastic gradient settings. The improved rates O(K−4/5) vs O(K−2/3) for SGLD are attractive and carefully derived. The role of Gaussian noise correlation is analyzed and the optimal choice Σ = I is clearly justified.
- Practical implications: The method is embarrassingly parallel (two chains at different step-sizes), a compelling match to modern compute. The empirical results are consistent with theory, including rate-confirmation experiments. The matrix factorization study shows meaningful wall-clock improvements at scale.
- Generality: The extension to SGHMC is natural and suggests broader applicability (e.g., thermostats, preconditioned variants).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions/regularity: The proofs require high smoothness (Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9 and 7th-order Taylor expansions) and “mild” conditions on U and L are deferred to the supplement. Many Bayesian posteriors (non-smooth priors, piecewise likelihoods) violate such regularity. Please clarify the precise assumptions, how they compare to those in Teh et al. (JMLR’16), and which popular models are covered.
- Gradient noise: The analysis essentially treats the Gaussian noise as dominating the stochastic gradient noise and requires moment bounds. In high-variance minibatch regimes, how do the constants behave? Is there a regime where gradient noise undermines the RR bias cancellation? Empirical ablations varying batch size (and showing when SGRRLD ceases to help) would be valuable.
- Correlations in practice: While Brownian increments can be coupled exactly, the two chains use independent minibatches by default. Would sharing the same minibatch indices across fine and coarse steps further reduce variance? The paper claims gradient correlation does not affect the asymptotic variance, but finite-time performance might benefit; an ablation would strengthen the empirical case.
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons: With two workers, SGRRLD exploits parallelism; however, a fair baseline is “two independent SGLD replicas averaged” or “SGLD with doubled batch size/throughput.” This would separate bias reduction from mere parallel resource utilization. Please include such baselines in the matrix factorization experiments.
- SGHMC theory: The theoretical results are developed for overdamped Langevin. The SGHMC extension is only empirical; the higher-order weak error structure and RR cancellation for second-order dynamics merit at least a sketch of theory or discussion of conditions under which the same O(γ2)/O(γ4) behavior holds.
- Notational/typo issues: In eq. (5), the first fine update appears to use ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) on the right-hand side; presumably it should be evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k. Please correct. Also, be explicit that the estimator in (6) corresponds to 2πγ/2(f) − πγ(f) over matched horizons.
- Related work: Given the clear link to multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC), the classical MLMC reference (Giles, 2008) should be cited. There is also related work on debiasing via coupling for MCMC/SG-MCMC (some appears post-2016), but discussing the conceptual connections would help position the contribution.

Quality
Technically sound and complete with respect to the main claims, but the strength of the results hinges on nontrivial regularity and moment assumptions that should be made explicit in the main text. The experimental section is thoughtful, with rate diagnostics, but lacks a parallelism-matched SGLD baseline and ablations on gradient correlation.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The estimator, coupling, and schedules are explained succinctly. Please fix the SGHMC update typo and move key assumptions (or a compact summary) from the supplement into the main paper. Provide guidance for choosing α (1/5 for MSE, 1/3 for bias) and practical step-size tuning.

Originality
High within SG-MCMC: RR extrapolation is classical for SDEs, but its translation to the stochastic gradient setting with variance control via correlated noise and finite-time bounds is novel and impactful.

Significance
The improved rates with a simple, parallelizable modification are compelling and likely to influence practice and further research (e.g., recursive RR, MLMC for SG-MCMC).

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Include results comparing against: (a) two-replica SGLD averaged (same parallel budget), (b) SGLD with doubled batch size, and (c) SGLD with tuned decreasing steps α = 1/5.
- Show the effect of using independent vs correlated Gaussian noises between levels in practice.
- Investigate sharing minibatches across levels; discuss theory and practicality.
- Clarify applicability beyond smooth posteriors; comment on robustness to non-smooth models.
- Provide brief theoretical intuition (or references) for the SGHMC extension.
- Cite Giles (2008) MLMC and discuss how your approach relates/extends to multilevel SG-MCMC.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a simple, general, and well-justified technique that improves both theory and practice for SG-MCMC. Addressing the fairness of comparisons and clarifying assumptions would further strengthen the submission.
