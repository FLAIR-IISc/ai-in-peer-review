This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aiming to reduce bias by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD. The core idea – leveraging RR extrapolation, traditionally used for numerical integration of ODEs, to accelerate convergence in SG-MCMC – is genuinely interesting and represents a potentially significant contribution. The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) MSE vs. O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD) and supports these findings with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (Gaussian model and matrix factorization). The work builds upon the established foundations of SGLD and higher-order integrators, offering a clever and relatively simple modification to achieve comparable accuracy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to stochastic gradient settings and MCMC is a clear contribution.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, including convergence rate analysis and a central limit theorem, bolstering the claims made. The derivation of the MSE rate is particularly compelling.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical findings, demonstrating improved accuracy and efficiency compared to SGLD and SGHMC. The matrix factorization experiments provide a practical demonstration of the algorithm’s utility.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelizability of SGRRLD, stemming from the independent execution of the two chains used in the RR extrapolation, is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Generality:** The authors explicitly state the method's applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, increasing its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Comparison:** While the comparisons to SGLD and SGHMC are valuable, the paper could benefit from a more extensive comparison to other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods, particularly those employing variance reduction techniques.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The analysis doesn't deeply explore the sensitivity of the algorithm to hyperparameter choices (step size, correlation matrix Σ) and the difficulty in tuning these parameters in practice.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion on correlated Brownian increments, while theoretically sound, could be expanded to address practical implementation challenges and potential overheads. Specifically, the correlation matrix Σ and its effect on performance could be further investigated.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The presentation of the theoretical results, while generally clear, could be streamlined for better readability. Certain equations could benefit from more descriptive explanations.
*  **Limited ablation study:** The results demonstrate performance gains, but a more thorough ablation study examining the individual contributions of RR extrapolation and correlated Brownian motion would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel idea with strong theoretical justification and empirical support.
*   The proposed algorithm offers a promising approach to improve the efficiency of SG-MCMC, a crucial technique for large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   The parallelizability of the algorithm makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions, despite some minor clarity issues.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The comparison to other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods is limited.
*   The practical considerations regarding hyperparameter tuning and implementation overhead are not fully addressed.
*   The paper, while strong, doesn’t revolutionize the field. It’s an incremental but valuable improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the strong theoretical and empirical results, outweighs the minor weaknesses. While further investigation into practical implementation details and comparisons to a broader range of methods would be beneficial, the current work provides a solid foundation for future research. This is a paper that introduces a new idea and offers a clear path forward. I recommend **acceptance**.
