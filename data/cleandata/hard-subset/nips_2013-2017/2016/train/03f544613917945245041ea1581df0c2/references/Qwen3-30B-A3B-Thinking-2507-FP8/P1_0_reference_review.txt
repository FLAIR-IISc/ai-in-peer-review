

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel MCMC algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors' key insight is to run two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) in parallel and then combine the results using RR extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The paper shows that SGRRLD achieves an asymptotic bias of O(γ²) and variance of O(γ⁴), leading to an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which significantly improves upon the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. The authors also demonstrate that the technique is generic and can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms like SGHMC.

The paper builds on foundational work including Welling and Teh's SGLD [1], Chen et al.'s analysis of higher-order integrators [10], and Talay and Tubaro's work on RR extrapolation for SDEs [21]. The main contribution is the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which has not been previously explored.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors provide both asymptotic (central limit theorem) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) results, supported by detailed proof sketches. The experimental section is well-designed, with both synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization applications on MovieLens datasets. The results consistently support the theoretical claims.

The authors carefully address potential pitfalls, such as the need for correlated Brownian motions to control variance and the requirement that both chains use the same mini-batch size to avoid introducing bias. The paper is a complete piece of work, not merely a position paper.

*Minor concerns:*
- The paper doesn't fully analyze the computational cost of running two chains in parallel, especially the overhead of coordinating the correlated Brownian motions in a distributed setting.
- The explanation of why the second chain needs to run twice as many steps as the first chain could be clearer.
- The step sizes used in experiments (γ = 2 × 10⁻⁶ for ML-1M, γ = 2 × 10⁻⁵ for ML-10M/ML-20M) aren't clearly justified based on the theoretical results.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the work and states the contributions. The theoretical analysis follows a logical progression from preliminaries to the main results. The experimental section is well-structured and supports the theoretical claims.

*Areas for improvement:*
- The notation (θ^(γ), θ^(γ/2), etc.) is somewhat confusing and could be simplified for better readability.
- The explanation of why the chain with smaller step size (γ/2) needs to run twice as many steps as the other chain (γ) is not sufficiently clear.
- The connection between the theoretical optimal step size (γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵) and the step sizes used in the experiments could be more explicit.

## Originality

The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is genuinely novel. The paper correctly states that "the applications of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC have not yet been explored," and this is confirmed by my review of recent NIPS proceedings and related literature. The key insight that RR extrapolation can achieve the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only first-order integrators is particularly significant.

The paper also demonstrates the generality of the approach by applying it to SGHMC (resulting in SGRRHMC), which further enhances the novelty and impact of the work.

## Significance

This work addresses a fundamental challenge in SG-MCMC: reducing bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The improved convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) is significant for large-scale Bayesian inference applications. The method's parallelization properties make it particularly suitable for modern distributed computing architectures.

The experimental results on both synthetic data and real-world matrix factorization tasks demonstrate clear practical benefits, with SGRRLD achieving better accuracy than SGLD in comparable computation times. The method can be easily integrated into existing SG-MCMC implementations with minimal changes.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are convincing, and the practical implications are clear. The paper is well-written and well-organized, with only minor issues that can be addressed in revision.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, supported by strong theoretical and empirical evidence.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Computational Cost of Parallelization**: Please provide more detail on the computational cost of running two chains in parallel. Specifically, how much overhead is incurred from coordinating the correlated Brownian motions in a distributed setting? This is important for understanding the practical benefits of the method.

2. **Step Counting Explanation**: The paper states that "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice more time than the other one." Please clarify why this is necessary. Why does the second chain need to run twice as many steps? A more detailed explanation of the relationship between the step sizes and the number of iterations would help readers understand the algorithm.

3. **Step Size Selection**: In the experiments, you use different step sizes for different datasets (γ = 2 × 10⁻⁶ for ML-1M and γ = 2 × 10⁻⁵ for ML-10M/ML-20M). Please explain how these step sizes were chosen. Were they selected based on the theoretical results (γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵) or through empirical tuning? If the latter, please discuss the process.

4. **Notation Clarity**: Consider simplifying the notation for the two chains. The current notation (θ^(γ), θ^(γ/2)) is a bit confusing. Perhaps you could use different symbols or subscripts to make the distinction clearer.

5. **Theory-Experiment Connection**: Explicitly connect the theoretical optimal step size (γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵) with the step sizes used in your experiments. How did you choose the step sizes for the experiments based on this theory? This would strengthen the link between your theoretical claims and experimental results.

6. **SGHMC-s Implementation**: In Figure 4, you compare SGRRHMC to SGHMC with Euler discretization and SGHMC with a higher-order splitting integrator (SGHMC-s). Please clarify how SGHMC-s was implemented, as the paper refers to it as "described in the supplementary document" but doesn't provide details in the main text. This would help readers understand the comparison better.

## Conclusion

This paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is both theoretically sound and practically beneficial, with clear improvements in convergence rates and experimental results. With the minor revisions noted above, this paper would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Overall Score: 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)**

**Strengths for Acceptance:**
- Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Strong theoretical analysis with clear convergence rate improvements
- Practical implementation with compelling experimental results
- Good parallelization properties for large-scale applications
- Well-written and well-organized paper

**Weaknesses to Address:**
- More detail needed on computational cost of parallelization
- Clarification needed on why the second chain runs twice as many steps
- Better explanation of step size selection for experiments
- Simplification of notation for clarity
- Explicit connection between theoretical optimal step size and experimental step sizes
