

# Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

## Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The key insight is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine their outputs using RR extrapolation. The authors theoretically demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves an MSE rate of O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), representing a significant improvement. The paper provides formal convergence analysis, proves central limit theorems, and validates results on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, building upon established work on SGLD [1, 10] and RR extrapolation for SDEs [21, 23]. The authors provide clear theorems with conditions, and while full proofs are relegated to supplementary material (standard practice), the sketch proofs are convincing. The convergence rate analysis (Theorem 2) properly accounts for both bias and variance terms, showing how SGRRLD improves upon SGLD by reducing bias without substantially increasing variance.

The experimental validation is thorough, with well-designed synthetic experiments on Gaussian models and real-world applications on movie recommendation datasets. The results consistently support the theoretical claims, showing significantly reduced bias and MSE for SGRRLD compared to SGLD. The authors also demonstrate the method's generality by applying it to SGHMC (as SGRRHMC), further validating their approach.

A minor concern is the lack of explicit discussion about the computational cost of running two chains in parallel. While the paper notes the parallel nature of the algorithm, it doesn't quantify the actual speedup (e.g., whether it's a 1.5x or 2x speedup) or discuss how this impacts practical implementation on distributed systems.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem, previous work, and the proposed solution. The preliminaries section effectively establishes the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation. The proposed method is explained with precise mathematical notation, and the pseudo-code reference to supplementary materials is appropriate.

The connection between the theoretical results and experimental findings is well-maintained. Figures 1-4 clearly illustrate the bias and MSE improvements, and the experiments are well-motivated and properly designed. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for identical mini-batch sizes for both chains), demonstrating intellectual honesty.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution by applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC. As the authors correctly note, "the applications of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC have not yet been explored." This is a novel combination of techniques that directly addresses a key limitation of current SG-MCMC methods (bias scaling with step size).

The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly from Chen et al. [10] who showed that higher-order integrators can improve rates, while SGRRLD achieves similar rates using only first-order integrators. The proposed method is also generic enough to apply to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, as demonstrated with SGHMC.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. The improved convergence rate (O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3})) represents a meaningful theoretical advance for large-scale Bayesian inference. For practitioners dealing with massive datasets, this could translate to significant computational savings while maintaining or improving accuracy.

The parallel nature of SGRRLD makes it particularly well-suited for modern distributed computing architectures, which is a major practical advantage. The experimental results on large-scale matrix factorization problems (MovieLens datasets) provide concrete evidence of the method's practical benefits, showing that SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in half the time.

The paper advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by providing a simple yet effective technique to improve convergence rates without requiring complex higher-order integrators. This makes it more accessible for practitioners than methods based on higher-order schemes [10].

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of SG-MCMC with strong practical implications. The combination of rigorous theory and compelling experimental results demonstrates the value of the proposed approach. While the computational cost of running two chains could be more explicitly discussed, this does not substantially undermine the paper's contribution.

**Strengths:**
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Strong theoretical analysis with clear convergence rate improvements
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real data
- Practical significance for large-scale Bayesian inference
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational cost for parallel implementation
- Theoretical analysis could briefly address sensitivity to step size choices

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian machine learning, with clear theoretical advances and practical implications. It addresses an important limitation of current SG-MCMC methods and provides a simple, effective solution that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The work is clearly distinguishable from prior NIPS publications and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.
