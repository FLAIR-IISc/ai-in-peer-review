Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a stochastic-gradient MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD-like samplers while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. The method runs two coupled SG-MCMC chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated ergodic average combining two fine steps and one coarse step. Under standard assumptions (existence of Poisson equation solution, smoothness, ergodicity), the authors prove a CLT for the estimator, showing asymptotic variance is minimized when the Brownian increments are perfectly coupled (Σ = Id), and derive non-asymptotic bounds: with constant γ, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal-in-K rates K−2/3 (bias) and K−4/5 (MSE). With decreasing steps γk = γ1k−α, they obtain an asymptotic rate O(K−((1−α)/2)∧(2α)), optimized at α = 1/5, i.e., O(K−2/5), improving over SGLD’s O(K−1/3). They argue this matches the accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS 2015) while using first-order Euler but two levels. They also extend the idea to SGHMC (SGRRHMC).

The theoretical framing builds on weak error expansions for SDE discretizations and RR extrapolation (Talay–Tubaro; Lamberton–Pagès et al.) and adapts variance-minimizing couplings akin to MLMC couplings. In SG-MCMC, the work relates to SGLD (Welling & Teh), SGHMC (Chen et al., ICML 2014), thermostat variants, and high-order integrators (Chen et al., NIPS 2015). Applying RR to SG-MCMC and establishing rates under stochastic gradients appears to be new.

Strengths
- Clear methodological idea grounded in established numerical analysis (RR) and MLMC-style coupling; well-motivated for SG-MCMC.
- Theoretical contributions: CLT with optimal coupling, explicit bias and MSE non-asymptotic bounds, and optimized rates; results align with intuition and prior ULA analyses extended to stochastic gradients.
- Practical relevance: trivial parallelization via two independent chains; empirically demonstrates reduced bias/MSE on a tractable Gaussian model and improved predictive RMSE on large-scale matrix factorization without increasing wall-clock time, plus extension to SGHMC with favorable comparisons to a higher-order integrator.
- Discussion of necessary conditions (same gradient estimator distribution L; effect of correlated Brownian noise) is careful and informative.

Weaknesses
- Notational and potential typographical issues in the algorithm definition (Eq. (5) appears to evaluate gradients at θ(γ/2)2k+1 rather than the previous state; this should be corrected for clarity and correctness).
- Assumptions for the CLT (e.g., g ∈ C9 and 7th-order expansions) are strong; while standard in weak-error analyses, the main text defers almost all conditions to the supplement, limiting accessibility.
- The experimental section does not explicitly state whether the optimal Brownian coupling (Σ = Id) was implemented, nor whether minibatches were synchronized across levels; clarifying these is important for reproducing gains and matching theory.
- Computational cost trade-off is discussed via parallelism, but a more explicit accounting (e.g., FLOPs, memory, potential synchronization for coupling) and sensitivity to hardware availability would strengthen claims of practical efficiency.
- Limited baselines: for SGHMC, comparison includes a higher-order integrator, but for SGLD variants (e.g., preconditioned SGLD, control variate schemes), additional comparisons could contextualize gains.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theoretical development is coherent and consistent with the RR/MLMC literature and recent SG-MCMC analyses. The coupling argument and rate derivations are plausible. The O(γ2)/O(γ4) bias/variance with constant steps and the optimized K−4/5 MSE rate appear correctly derived. The necessity that both chains use gradient estimators from the same distribution is appropriately highlighted.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments corroborate predicted rates and bias reductions; large-scale matrix factorization shows time-to-accuracy improvements. However, details about coupling implementation and minibatch sharing should be made explicit.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with a clear exposition of preliminaries, method, theory, and experiments. However:
  - Fix Eq. (5) to evaluate the gradient at the current state before the update (explicit Euler) and check for any other typographical inconsistencies.
  - Provide the exact algorithm pseudocode in the main text (not only supplement), including coupling choices for Z and minibatch sampling policies across levels.
  - Summarize the key assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 in the main paper for readability.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and proving improved rates under stochastic gradients and coupled Brownian increments is, to the best of my knowledge, novel at the time relative to NeurIPS/ICML work. The connection to higher-order accuracy using first-order integrators via extrapolation is conceptually interesting and practically useful.

Significance
- The method offers a generic, easy-to-implement bias reduction technique for SG-MCMC with demonstrable gains, particularly compelling in parallel/distributed settings. The analysis may stimulate further work on multilevel/extrapolation techniques for scalable Bayesian sampling. The impact is moderate-to-high given the ubiquity of SG-MCMC.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contributions with improved rates and clear coupling guidance.
- Simple, generic technique applicable beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC), with empirical benefits.
- Good fit to NeurIPS topics on scalable Bayesian inference and stochastic algorithms.

Arguments against acceptance
- Minor but critical clarity/typo issues in the algorithm equations.
- Assumptions are strong and mostly deferred to supplementary material.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader, and implementation details about coupling/minibatch sharing need to be explicit for reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct Eq. (5) and include full pseudocode with coupling and minibatch policies; state explicitly whether Σ=Id and whether matching minibatches were used across levels.
- Briefly list the main regularity and ergodicity assumptions in the main text and discuss their practical scope (e.g., non-convex potentials).
- Add comparisons to additional SG-MCMC baselines (e.g., preconditioned SGLD, control variate SGMCMC) or ablations on coupling/minibatch synchronization.
- Discuss practical considerations for parallel implementations, including synchronization-free ways to share common random numbers and potential memory trade-offs.
- If feasible, include preliminary results or discussion on recursive/multi-step RR and its cost-accuracy trade-offs.
