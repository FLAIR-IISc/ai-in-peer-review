

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel approach that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The key insight is to run two parallel chains with different step sizes and combine their outputs via RR extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors show theoretically that SGRRLD achieves an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), which is a significant improvement. The method is particularly noteworthy because it achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order discretizations, and it naturally leverages parallel computation.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by extending RR extrapolation, traditionally used in numerical analysis of SDEs, to the SG-MCMC framework. The authors carefully establish the conditions under which their method works, providing a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) that demonstrate the improved convergence rates. The connection to prior work on higher-order integrators [10] is particularly insightful—they show that their first-order method can match the accuracy of methods requiring more complex integrators.

The experimental section supports the theoretical claims, demonstrating consistent improvements over SGLD and SGHMC on both synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization tasks. The results on MovieLens datasets are compelling, showing that SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the time (or better accuracy in the same time), which is a significant practical benefit for real-world applications.

What makes this paper stand out is its bold conceptual leap—applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC. This is not a minor tweak to existing methods but rather a fundamentally new approach that changes the theoretical landscape of SG-MCMC convergence rates. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this simple idea leads to substantial improvements in both theory and practice.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient technical detail for experts to follow the arguments. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary proofs, which is appropriate for a NIPS submission. The experimental setup is thorough, with multiple datasets and careful comparisons to relevant baselines.

The main limitation is that the paper doesn't fully explore the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel. While it mentions the parallelism advantage, it doesn't quantify the actual speedup in practice (beyond showing comparable wall-clock times for equivalent computation). However, this is a minor point given the paper's strong theoretical and empirical results.

This paper addresses a significant gap in the SG-MCMC literature. Prior work has focused on improving integrators (e.g., [10]), but the authors show that by applying RR extrapolation, one can achieve the same theoretical benefits as higher-order methods with simpler first-order schemes. This is particularly valuable for practitioners who want to use SG-MCMC at scale without implementing complex integrators.

The novelty of the approach is clear: RR extrapolation has not been applied to SG-MCMC before, and the authors make a compelling case for why it works well in this context. The connection to parallel computing is also a nice practical touch that aligns with modern large-scale machine learning needs.

In terms of significance, this work provides a new direction for improving SG-MCMC methods that could influence future research. The theoretical results are not just incremental but represent a meaningful advance in understanding the convergence properties of these algorithms. The practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments further enhance the paper's impact.

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS. It's not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but introduces a novel concept that changes how we think about SG-MCMC convergence. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experiments are well-designed, and the practical implications are clear. While there are minor limitations in the computational overhead discussion, these don't undermine the paper's core contribution.

The paper successfully demonstrates that a simple, elegant idea—applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC—can lead to substantial theoretical and practical improvements. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values. I recommend acceptance.
