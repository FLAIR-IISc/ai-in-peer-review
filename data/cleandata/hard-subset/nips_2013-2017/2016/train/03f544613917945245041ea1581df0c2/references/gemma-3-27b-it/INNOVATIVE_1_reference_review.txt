This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm leveraging the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce bias in SGLD. The core idea—applying RR to Langevin dynamics via parallel chains with differing step sizes—is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for MSE) and support this with experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization). The generality of the approach, extending beyond SGLD to other SG-MCMC methods like SGHMC, is also highlighted.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to this context represents a significant conceptual leap.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees and rate analysis. The connection to existing theory on SGLD and higher-order integrators is clearly articulated.
*   **Potential for Improved Efficiency:** The results suggest a pathway to achieving higher accuracy with the same computational cost, or comparable accuracy with reduced cost, which is crucial for large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   **Generality:**  The applicability to various SG-MCMC algorithms expands the potential impact.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, though relatively simple, demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD. The matrix factorization results are particularly encouraging.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel nature of the RR extrapolation (running chains independently) is a significant advantage in modern computing environments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, while solid, could be more extensive. The matrix factorization experiment is a good start, but exploring more complex models and datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Practical Considerations of Step Size Tuning:** The theoretical analysis suggests an optimal step size scaling, but the paper doesn't deeply address the practical challenges of selecting step sizes in real-world applications, particularly in non-stationary settings. The dependence on tuning a hyperparameter (step size) is not thoroughly explored in the experimental setup.
*   **Correlation of Gradients:** The paper acknowledges the importance of correlated gradients but doesn't explore this aspect extensively.  The impact of different correlation structures on performance isn't fully investigated.
*    **Missing Details in Experiments:** Some experimental details were lacking (e.g. details on model selection for matrix factorization, computational resources used, more thorough hyperparameter search).
*   **Clarity of Notation:** The notation, particularly regarding the correlated Brownian motion increments, could be more clear and consistently used.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel idea with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The potential impact on the scalability of Bayesian inference is significant.
*   The results, while not exhaustive, provide compelling evidence of the benefits of SGRRLD.
*   The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature and related work.
*   The inherent parallelizability of the algorithm makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is somewhat limited and doesn’t fully demonstrate the algorithm's robustness across a wide range of problems.
*   The practical challenges of step size tuning and gradient correlation are not fully addressed.
*   Minor clarity issues in the presentation could hinder understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant and promising contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the theoretical results and encouraging empirical evidence, outweighs the relatively minor weaknesses. While more extensive experimentation and a deeper discussion of practical considerations would strengthen the paper, the core idea is strong enough to warrant acceptance. I believe this work has the potential to influence future research in scalable Bayesian inference. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.
