Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC), primarily SGLD, to reduce discretization bias. Two SGLD chains are run with step sizes γ and γ/2 (the latter at double frequency to match time horizons), and their outputs are combined in a standard RR fashion to cancel the O(γ) weak error, aiming for O(γ^2) bias. To control variance inflation due to extrapolation, the method uses coupled Gaussian noises across the two chains and shows that the asymptotic variance is minimized when the Brownian increments are perfectly correlated via Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2, extending insights from RR for SDEs without stochastic gradients (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) to the SG-MCMC setting.

The paper establishes: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator under decreasing steps, with expressions for asymptotic bias and variance and proof that the Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise; (ii) finite-time bounds for bias and MSE under constant or decreasing steps, showing bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant γ; optimizing yields an MSE rate O(K^−4/5), compared to O(K^−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC (Teh, Vollmer, Zygalakis; Chen et al.); and (iii) that the extrapolation can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order updates. Empirically, the method improves over SGLD on a Gaussian model (bias and MSE of variance), extends to SGHMC (matching a splitting integrator in accuracy for suitable budgets), and improves test RMSE/time in large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) using parallel execution of the two chains.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC appears new and is simple to implement as a wrapper around existing samplers.
- Theory: CLT with correlated Brownian increments, finite-time bias/MSE bounds, and clear rates. The identification that Σ = Id minimizes asymptotic variance is useful and practically actionable.
- Generality: The approach is applicable to other SG-MCMC schemes (demonstrated with SGHMC).
- Practicality: Parallel/distributed suitability is inherent because the two chains can run independently; empirical results on real large-scale data demonstrate improvements in wall-clock efficiency.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Resource accounting in experiments: SGRRLD requires three gradient evaluations per “pair” (one for γ-chain, two for γ/2-chain). The matrix factorization comparison equalizes wall-clock time by running the two chains as independent processes, but this uses more total gradient computations (≈1.5×) and more cores than SGLD. A normalization by total gradient evaluations or total core-time would provide a fairer computational comparison. SGLD can also exploit parallelism (e.g., parallelized minibatch gradients or multiple chains).
- Assumptions and clarity of conditions: The main results rely on strong smoothness/Poisson equation assumptions (g ∈ C^9) and technical conditions on the gradient-noise law L; the paper calls them “mild,” which understates their strength. A clearer statement in the main text of the precise assumptions (moment bounds, Lipschitz/ergodicity, growth conditions, independence/correlation structure of stochastic gradients) would improve transparency.
- Coupling of stochastic gradients: The theory emphasizes coupling of Brownian increments and notes that the two gradient estimators should have the same distribution L; practical guidance on whether to share minibatches across chains (to further reduce variance) is not provided. In practice, gradient noise can dominate for some regimes; empirical or theoretical analysis of the impact of minibatch coupling would strengthen the work.
- Scope of baselines: For SGLD, there is no empirical comparison against higher-order or variance-reduced SG-MCMC methods; the SGHMC comparison includes a splitting integrator but only on synthetic data. Including higher-order SGLD-type integrators (e.g., Chen et al. 2015) or thermostat methods on real tasks would better substantiate the claim of comparable accuracy using first-order steps.
- Estimation target: The RR estimator directly targets π(f) via weighted averages; it does not yield bias-corrected samples. This is fine for expectation estimation (common in SG-MCMC), but tasks requiring posterior draws are not directly addressed.
- Minor clarity issues: In equation (5), the gradient arguments appear to use θ(γ/2)2k+1 in both updates; likely a typo (the first should be θ(γ/2)2k). A brief pseudo-code in the main text would aid reproducibility.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The extension of RR extrapolation with correlated Brownian increments to SG-MCMC is carefully argued, with CLT and finite-time analyses consistent with prior SDE literature. The assumptions are strong but standard for such expansions. The dependence of rates on step-size schedules is clearly derived.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic and real experiments match the predicted trends. However, compute fairness and broader baselines could be improved.

Clarity
- Generally clear exposition with good positioning relative to SGLD/SGHMC and RR for SDEs. Some derivations and all proofs are deferred to the supplement; more explicit assumptions and corrected typos would help.

Originality
- The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with variance control via correlated Brownian increments appears novel. Theoretical results bridge a gap between SDE weak error analysis and SG-MCMC.

Significance
- The method offers a simple, general mechanism to improve bias/MSE rates for SG-MCMC while leveraging parallel hardware, with potential practical impact. The improvement from O(K^−2/3) to O(K^−4/5) for MSE is meaningful. Broader empirical validation would enhance significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and simple idea with solid theory connecting RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC.
- Improved finite-time and asymptotic rates; practical variance-control prescription.
- Demonstrated gains on synthetic and large-scale real data; parallel-friendly.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental comparisons do not normalize for total compute; advantages partly due to increased gradient evaluations/parallel cores.
- Strong assumptions are under-specified in the main text; minor clarity issues/typos.
- Limited baselines on real tasks and limited analysis of minibatch coupling effects.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add compute-normalized comparisons (e.g., equal total gradient calls or core-time) and ablations on minibatch coupling (shared vs independent indices across chains).
- Include higher-order SG-MCMC baselines on real tasks.
- State full assumptions in the main text; correct equation (5) and provide pseudo-code.
- Discuss applicability to producing approximate samples vs expectations and any practical heuristics for step-size tuning in SGRRLD.
