### Paper Summary

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in SGLD. Instead of developing complex higher-order integrators, the authors propose a clever application of a classical numerical technique: Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The method involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading O(γ) error term. To manage the variance of the resulting estimator, the authors advocate for using correlated noise across the two chains. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This theoretical improvement is validated through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, where SGRRLD shows significant gains in accuracy and efficiency over SGLD.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and elegance. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is, to my knowledge, entirely new and represents a significant conceptual contribution. It connects the field of large-scale Bayesian inference with a powerful and well-established tool from numerical analysis. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a genuinely new and bold approach to tackling the long-standing problem of discretization bias in SG-MCMC.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a new "meta-algorithm" for improving existing SG-MCMC methods. The idea is simple, powerful, and generalizable (as demonstrated by its application to SGHMC). It opens up a new research direction for designing more accurate samplers by leveraging classical error-cancellation techniques, rather than focusing solely on constructing more complex integrators. The demonstrated ability to achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods using only a first-order integrator is a very important result.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis that goes beyond simple heuristics. The derivation of a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE rigorously establishes the advantages of the proposed method. The analysis correctly identifies the improved convergence rates and provides valuable insights, such as the importance of using correlated noise to control variance.

3.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** The proposed method is practically appealing. It avoids the implementation complexity of higher-order symplectic or splitting integrators. Furthermore, the two chains required for the extrapolation can be run in parallel, making the approach a natural fit for modern multi-core architectures. The experiments effectively demonstrate that this parallelism translates into wall-clock time improvements on a real-world, large-scale problem.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated cleanly. The connection to related work in both SG-MCMC and numerical SDEs is well-articulated, making the paper accessible and placing the contribution in its proper context.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Minor Weaknesses):**

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The most apparent drawback is the need to run two MCMC chains, effectively doubling the computational cost in terms of total operations or required parallel resources. While the authors rightly point out the benefits of parallelization, in a resource-constrained or non-parallel environment, this represents a significant overhead. A more direct discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-executed and support the paper's claims. However, the validation is limited to a convex Gaussian model and matrix factorization. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on highly non-convex problems, such as training Bayesian Neural Networks, would have made the empirical case for the algorithm's broad utility even more compelling. The comparison is also mainly limited to the baseline SGLD/SGHMC, and a broader comparison to other state-of-the-art samplers would be beneficial.

### Conclusion

Despite the minor weaknesses regarding computational overhead and the scope of experiments, I am very enthusiastic about this paper. The central idea is highly innovative, theoretically sound, and practically relevant. The paper presents a bold new direction for improving SG-MCMC algorithms that prioritizes conceptual elegance and simplicity over complex, bespoke integrators. This is precisely the kind of creative and foundational work that NIPS should champion. The potential impact is high, as the technique is general enough to be applied across the entire family of SG-MCMC methods. I believe this work will inspire follow-up research exploring similar error-cancellation schemes in stochastic settings. I strongly recommend acceptance.
