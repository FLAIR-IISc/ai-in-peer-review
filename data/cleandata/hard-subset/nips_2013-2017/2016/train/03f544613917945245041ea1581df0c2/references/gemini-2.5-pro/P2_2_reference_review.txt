### Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), aimed at improving the accuracy of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating sequence convergence, to the widely-used Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs in a specific way to cancel out the leading-order error term in the bias. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to standard first-order SG-MCMC methods like SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This is significant as it allows a first-order integrator to match the theoretical accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

The work is well-situated within the context of scalable Bayesian inference. It directly addresses the fundamental trade-off in SG-MCMC between computational cost and the bias introduced by the discretization step size. While prior work has focused on developing higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) to reduce this bias, this paper proposes a conceptually different and elegant alternative rooted in classical numerical analysis, which has been explored for SDEs with exact gradients but not in the stochastic gradient setting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and significant contribution. It provides a new and practical tool for bias reduction that is orthogonal to the development of higher-order integrators. The demonstrated improvement in convergence rate is substantial and addresses a key limitation of existing first-order SG-MCMC methods. The idea is simple, elegant, and has the potential to be widely adopted and extended.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.
    *   **Theory:** The authors provide a comprehensive convergence analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) that characterizes the asymptotic distribution of the estimator and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results formally establish the improved convergence rates and provide theoretical justification for the method's superiority over SGLD. The analysis of using correlated noise to control the variance of the RR estimator is particularly sophisticated and crucial for the method's practical success.
    *   **Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The use of a simple Gaussian model with a known posterior is an excellent choice for precisely validating the theoretical claims regarding bias and MSE as functions of step size and iteration count (Figures 1-3). The extension to SGHMC (Figure 4) successfully supports the claim of generality. Most importantly, the large-scale matrix factorization experiment on real-world datasets (MovieLens) demonstrates that the theoretical gains translate into practical benefits (improved RMSE over wall-clock time), which is a critical test for any scalable algorithm.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, the method is presented formally and logically, and the theoretical and experimental sections directly support the main claims. The authors do a good job of connecting their work to the relevant literature in both machine learning and numerical analysis.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper highlights that the two chains in SGRRLD can be run in parallel, making the wall-clock time comparable to SGLD (for a reduced number of total iterations). However, the total computational cost (number of gradient evaluations) is 3x that of an SGLD run of the same length (one step for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain per iteration). While the experiments show SGRRLD is superior in wall-clock time, a more direct comparison based on total gradient evaluations would strengthen the claims. For example, does SGRRLD still outperform an SGLD chain run for 3x the number of iterations? The results suggest it would, but explicitly addressing this comparison would make the paper's evaluation of efficiency more complete.

2.  **Clarity on Technical Assumptions:** The main paper defers all technical conditions for the theorems to the supplementary material. While this is common practice due to space constraints, adding a brief, high-level summary of the key assumptions in the main text (e.g., smoothness and growth conditions on the potential `U`, moment conditions on the gradient estimator `L`) would improve the paper's self-containedness and allow readers to better appreciate the scope of the theoretical results.

3.  **Generality of the Approach:** The paper claims the technique is "quite generic" and can be applied to "virtually all" SG-MCMC algorithms. While this is demonstrated for SGLD and SGHMC, a short discussion on potential requirements or challenges for applying it to other classes of samplers (e.g., those with adaptive preconditioning or thermostats like SGNHT or Adam-MCMC) would make this claim more concrete and provide valuable insights for future work.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and impactful contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm provides a powerful new way to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC methods, backed by strong theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical evidence. The work is of high quality, original, clearly presented, and significant. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to expanding on certain details of the presentation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
