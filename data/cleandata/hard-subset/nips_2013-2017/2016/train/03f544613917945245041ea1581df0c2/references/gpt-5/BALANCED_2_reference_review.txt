Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a variance-reduced, bias-reduced SG-MCMC estimator that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD using two coupled discretizations with steps γ and γ/2. The key idea is to form an extrapolated estimator that cancels the O(γ) weak discretization bias of Euler–Maruyama, yielding O(γ^2) asymptotic bias while controlling variance by correlating the Brownian increments across the two chains. The authors establish a CLT for the estimator, identify the optimal correlation (Σ = Id, i.e., Zγ = (Zγ/2,odd + Zγ/2,even)/√2), and provide non-asymptotic bounds: with constant step size, bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)). Optimizing step size yields MSE O(K^−4/5), improving over the O(K^−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD). They also analyze decreasing step schedules, showing an optimal α = 1/5 asymptotic rate O(K^−2/5) under the CLT scaling.

Empirically, they validate on a linear-Gaussian model (analytic posterior), show improved bias/MSE versus SGLD and, for SGHMC, show that the RR version (SGRRHMC) matches the accuracy of a higher-order integrator. On large-scale probabilistic matrix factorization (MovieLens), they report better test RMSE at equal wall-clock by running the two chains in parallel.

This work connects numerical weak-error analysis for SDEs and RR extrapolation (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) with the SG-MCMC literature (Welling & Teh; Teh–Thiéry–Vollmer; Ma–Chen–Fox; Chen–Ding–Carin), bringing a classical bias-cancellation technique to the stochastic-gradient regime and analyzing its behavior under gradient noise.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT (three regimes depending on Γ(3)/√Γ), variance minimization via correlated Brownian motion, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds are well-motivated and technically sound within the stated assumptions. The improvement in non-asymptotic rates (bias O(K^−2/3), MSE O(K^−4/5)) is significant for first-order integrators.
- Assumptions: The analysis requires smoothness and a Poisson equation solution g ∈ C^9, as well as stability/ergodicity conditions and control of stochastic gradient moments. These are standard in SG-MCMC analyses but can be restrictive in non-convex/high-dimensional settings; it would help to highlight which parts truly require high-order smoothness.
- Variance control: The identification and use of optimal Brownian coupling is a nice contribution; it generalizes RR variance control to SG-MCMC and is supported by the CLT.
- Experiments: The synthetic Gaussian example is appropriate to validate bias/MSE scaling. The SGHMC experiment supports claims that RR can match higher-order integrators. The matrix factorization experiments demonstrate practical gains in wall-clock.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-related to prior work. However, there are a few issues:
  - Possible typo in Eq. (5): the first half-step for the fine chain appears to evaluate ∇Ũ at θ(γ/2)2k+1 (implicit), whereas SGLD uses an explicit Euler step and should evaluate at θ(γ/2)2k. Please verify/correct.
  - The main text alludes to “mild assumptions”; listing the key assumptions (on U, L, and f) in the main paper would improve readability.
  - It would help to include pseudo-code for SGRRLD in the main text (not only in the supplement) and explicitly state how correlated Gaussians are realized in parallel implementations.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a careful variance coupling and proving improved finite-time and asymptotic rates is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior SG-MCMC work. The connection to duplicated diffusions and invariant measures is timely and original in this context. The extension to SGHMC suggests generality across SG-MCMC methods.

Significance
- The theoretical rates improve over standard first-order SG-MCMC without resorting to complex higher-order integrators, and the method leverages parallelism naturally. This is likely to influence practitioners seeking higher accuracy at similar wall-clock cost on multi-core/cluster settings. The method is generic and could be applied to other SG-MCMC variants.

Concerns and suggestions
- Compute fairness: Experimental comparisons use equal wall-clock time by running two chains in parallel. This is advantageous for SGRRLD but implicitly uses more cores. For completeness, please add:
  - A comparison under equal total gradient evaluations (or FLOPs) on a single core.
  - A baseline with two independent SGLD chains run in parallel whose estimates are averaged (to reduce variance but not bias) to isolate the benefit of RR extrapolation versus parallelism.
- Gradient estimator coupling: The theory requires the two chains’ gradient estimators to share the same distribution L and notes that differing L induces bias. In practice, would sharing the same mini-batch indices across the two chains (or reusing the same subsamples across the paired fine steps) further reduce variance? An ablation on same vs independent subsamples would be illuminating.
- Practical guidance: Provide recommendations on choosing γ and α in practice (e.g., start with α = 1/5 for decreasing steps; for constant γ, tune via pilot runs using the derived K^−1/5 and K^−1/3 scalings for MSE/bias).
- Robustness: Report results on a non-Gaussian posterior (e.g., logistic regression) to demonstrate robustness beyond conjugate models and matrix factorization.
- Multi-level variants: Since you mention recursive RR/multilevel, a brief empirical demonstration of two levels (or a discussion of complexity/variance trade-offs) would strengthen the claim of extensibility.

Pros
- Clear, principled use of RR extrapolation with optimal coupling; improves both non-asymptotic and asymptotic rates for first-order SG-MCMC.
- Solid theoretical development (CLT, bias/MSE bounds) and practical insights on step-size schedules.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization; extension beyond SGLD to SGHMC.
- Naturally parallelizable and easy to implement on multi-core machines.

Cons
- Some clarity/typo issues in the algorithmic description; key assumptions relegated to the supplement.
- Experimental comparisons conflate algorithmic gains with increased parallel resources; no single-core, equal-compute baseline.
- Strong smoothness assumptions may limit direct applicability to highly non-smooth/non-convex posteriors; discussion could be expanded.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a novel and well-supported improvement to SG-MCMC with both theoretical and practical impact. Addressing compute fairness, clarifying the algorithmic details (Eq. 5), and adding a couple of robustness/ablation experiments would further strengthen the submission.
