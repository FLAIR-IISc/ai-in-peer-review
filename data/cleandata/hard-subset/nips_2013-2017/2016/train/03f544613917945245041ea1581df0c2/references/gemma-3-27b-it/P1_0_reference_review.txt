## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm designed to reduce the bias of standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, requiring the parallel execution of two chains with different step sizes and correlated Brownian motions.  The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) and show the method is applicable to a broad class of SG-MCMC algorithms. They validate their theoretical findings through experiments on synthetic Gaussian models, matrix factorization, and comparisons with SGHMC, demonstrating improved accuracy.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the burgeoning field of SG-MCMC, stemming from the work of Welling & Teh (2011) introducing SGLD. It acknowledges and references key extensions to SGLD (e.g., SGHMC) and recent theoretical analyses bounding bias and MSE (Chen et al., 2015). The incorporation of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a novel contribution, drawing on numerical analysis techniques previously applied to SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2015) but not, to the authors’ knowledge, to SG-MCMC. The paper positions itself as a method to achieve similar accuracy to higher-order integrators without the computational cost.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, including a central limit theorem and bounds on bias and MSE. The derivation of the O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) convergence rate is a significant result.
* **Generality:**  The authors convincingly argue that SGRRLD is applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms, offering a broadly useful improvement technique.  The demonstration with SGHMC further supports this claim.
* **Practical Implementation & Parallelism:** The algorithm naturally lends itself to parallelization due to the independent running of the two chains, aligning well with modern computational resources.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets consistently demonstrate the improved performance of SGRRLD over SGLD and, in some cases, SGHMC.  The scaling experiments and ablation studies in the supplementary material are helpful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and mathematical derivations. The inclusion of a pseudo-code is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Correlation of Brownian Motions:** While the theoretical justification for correlated Brownian motions is provided, the practical implications and sensitivity to the choice of the correlation matrix Σ are not fully explored.  The selection of Σ = Id seems somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from further investigation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal step size selection (and potentially the correlation matrix) isn't discussed in detail in the main paper, and the experiments demonstrate dependence on the dataset. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity is needed.
* **Computational Overhead:** While the algorithm is parallelizable, the overhead of maintaining and synchronizing two chains and managing the correlated Brownian motions could impact performance in certain scenarios. A more detailed comparison of computational cost (including wall-clock time) for different problem sizes is warranted.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with SGHMC-s (higher-order splitting integrator) is brief. A more comprehensive benchmark against other recent advancements in SG-MCMC would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound SG-MCMC algorithm with demonstrable performance improvements.
* The generality of the approach is a significant strength.
* The work addresses a crucial challenge in scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The discussion of the correlation of Brownian motions and hyperparameter sensitivity could be more in-depth.
* A more thorough benchmarking against the latest SG-MCMC techniques would enhance the impact of the paper.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The theoretical contributions are solid, the experimental results are convincing, and the algorithm addresses an important problem in Bayesian machine learning. While the weaknesses I’ve identified could benefit from further investigation, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  The paper's originality and potential impact on the field make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the correlation matrix, hyperparameter sensitivity, and benchmarking.



**Confidence:** 8/10
