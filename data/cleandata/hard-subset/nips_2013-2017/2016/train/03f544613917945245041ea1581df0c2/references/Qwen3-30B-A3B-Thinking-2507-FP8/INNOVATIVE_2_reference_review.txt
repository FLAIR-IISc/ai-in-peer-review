

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel approach to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The proposed algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combines them using RR extrapolation. The authors demonstrate theoretically that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly better than SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).

The novelty of this work is substantial. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis for SDEs, its application to SG-MCMC represents a bold new direction. The authors cleverly adapt RR to the stochastic gradient context by using correlated Brownian motions to control variance, a key insight that addresses a critical concern with extrapolation methods. This approach achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods using only first-order discretization, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications where higher-order methods become computationally prohibitive.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution, with clear theorems establishing the convergence properties of SGRRLD. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and rigorously derived. The authors successfully bridge the gap between numerical analysis (RR extrapolation) and MCMC methods, creating a novel framework that could inspire future work in scalable Bayesian inference.

The experimental validation is appropriate and supports the theoretical claims. The synthetic Gaussian model experiments demonstrate clear improvements in bias and MSE, with SGRRLD reducing variance estimation error by two orders of magnitude compared to SGLD. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show consistent efficiency gains across multiple scales, with SGRRLD achieving comparable accuracy to SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time. The parallel nature of SGRRLD is highlighted as a practical advantage, which aligns well with the authors' emphasis on scalability.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition of the method and thorough comparison to prior work. The connections to related work (SGLD, SGHMC, RR extrapolation in numerical analysis) are properly contextualized. The authors are careful to note limitations, particularly regarding the optimal step size selection and the need for larger K when γ is small.

While the experimental section could potentially include more diverse datasets or comparative analysis with additional SG-MCMC methods, these are minor limitations that don't undermine the core novelty and theoretical contribution. The paper's strength lies in its conceptual innovation rather than comprehensive empirical validation.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. It introduces a genuinely novel technique that addresses a fundamental limitation in SG-MCMC methods while maintaining parallelizability. The theoretical results are impressive, demonstrating that a first-order integrator can achieve the convergence rates of higher-order methods through RR extrapolation. This is a bold and impactful idea that could influence future work in scalable MCMC.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it introduces a novel approach to an important problem in machine learning, with clear theoretical and practical significance. The authors have successfully bridged concepts from numerical analysis to MCMC, creating a new direction for research in scalable Bayesian methods.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the RR extrapolation approach to SG-MCMC is compelling and the theoretical results are strong. While the experimental validation is good, it's the conceptual innovation that makes this paper stand out, and this is precisely the type of bold contribution that NIPS should prioritize. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, offering a new technique that could be broadly applicable to SG-MCMC methods.
