## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, termed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to leverage the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce the bias inherent in standard SGLD, by running two Langevin chains with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) and show that their approach can achieve accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators with only a first-order integrator. They support these findings with experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea, extending a well-established numerical analysis technique to a modern Bayesian inference setting.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including convergence analysis and bounds for bias and MSE. The derived convergence rate of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) is a significant improvement over standard SGLD.
* **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm is well-suited for parallel computation, making it attractive for large-scale Bayesian inference problems. The experiments on matrix factorization demonstrate practical benefits.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight the potential for applying the RR extrapolation technique to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, broadening the impact of their work.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The mathematical derivations are presented with reasonable clarity, though some details are relegated to the supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:**  While the core idea is elegant, the implementation introduces additional complexity with two chains and correlated Brownian increments. The benefit needs to clearly outweigh this added complexity in practice.
* **Sensitivity to Step Size:** The theoretical analysis shows optimal performance with specific step size choices.  Determining these choices in practice might require careful tuning, which is not fully discussed in the experimental section.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are encouraging, the performance gains on the real-world matrix factorization problem are not overwhelmingly large. More extensive evaluations on a wider range of datasets and models would strengthen the claims.
* **Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on several assumptions regarding the smoothness of the potential energy function and the properties of the gradient estimators. The limitations of these assumptions and their potential impact on the practical performance are not thoroughly discussed.
* **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with SGHMC-s (higher-order integrator) is positive, but more extensive benchmarking against other advanced SG-MCMC methods would be valuable.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. However, the assumptions and sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning are potential areas for concern.  It feels like a complete piece of work, though expanding the experimental evaluation would be beneficial.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but the correlation structure introduced by the RR extrapolation could be explained more intuitively. The notation is reasonably consistent.
* **Originality:**  The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution. The paper properly cites and builds upon related work in both numerical analysis and Bayesian inference.
* **Significance:** The improved convergence rate and potential for parallelization are significant contributions.  However, the practical impact depends on the overhead of the added complexity and the effectiveness of parameter tuning.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Novel and theoretically sound approach to improving SG-MCMC.
*  Demonstrated improvements in convergence rate and empirical performance.
*  Potential for parallelization, making it applicable to large-scale problems.
*  Clear and generally well-written presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Added complexity may limit practical adoption.
*  Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*  Sensitivity to step size and the impact of assumptions require further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The application of RR extrapolation is a clever and theoretically justified approach to reducing bias. While the added complexity and the need for careful parameter tuning are legitimate concerns, the potential benefits in terms of convergence rate and parallelization are significant. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges associated with choosing appropriate step sizes.
2.  Expand the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods.
3.  Discuss the limitations of the theoretical assumptions and their potential impact on performance in real-world scenarios.
4.  Clarify the intuition behind the correlated Brownian increments and provide guidance on how to implement this efficiently.
