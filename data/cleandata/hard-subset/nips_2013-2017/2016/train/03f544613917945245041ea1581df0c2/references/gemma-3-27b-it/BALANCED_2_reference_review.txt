This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD by leveraging the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique. The core idea is to run two Langevin dynamics chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine their results to achieve a higher order of accuracy. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates—O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE compared to O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD—and support these findings with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (Gaussian model and matrix factorization).

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in scaling Bayesian inference—reducing bias in SG-MCMC. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established numerical analysis technique, is insightful and represents a novel application within the SG-MCMC context. The theoretical analysis appears thorough, with clear statements of theorems and supporting arguments. The experimental validation is comprehensive, showcasing improvements in both synthetic and realistic scenarios, including a compelling demonstration on a large-scale matrix factorization problem. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times (see "Clarity" below).  The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature on SG-MCMC and highlights the advantages of SGRRLD over existing methods. The inclusion of experiments on both SGLD *and* SGHMC with RR extrapolation further demonstrates the generality of the proposed technique.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including convergence analysis and rate of convergence results.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD.
* **Generality:** The method is shown to be applicable to different SG-MCMC algorithms (SGLD and SGHMC).
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel structure of the RR extrapolation is well-recognized and exploited.
* **Clear Comparison to Existing Methods:** The authors provide a solid comparison with other approaches like SGLD and SGHMC, clearly demonstrating the benefits of SGRRLD.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Mathematical Notation):**  The mathematical notation, particularly in sections 3 and 4, is quite dense and could benefit from more explanation. For instance, the explanation of the correlated Brownian increments (Equation 8) feels somewhat abrupt. Expanding on the intuition behind the correlation matrix Σ would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:** While the algorithm is parallelizable, running two chains inherently doubles the computational cost per iteration compared to SGLD.  A more detailed discussion of the trade-off between improved accuracy and increased computational cost would be valuable.  The experiments show wall-clock time improvements, but the paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the overhead of managing two chains.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments present results with specific step sizes (γ). The sensitivity of the performance to the choice of step size is not extensively investigated. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Limited Exploration of Σ:**  The authors mention that the variance is minimized when Σ = Id. However, they don’t present any results exploring the impact of different choices of Σ.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper cites related work, there is a limited discussion of how SGRRLD compares to other bias-reduction techniques in SG-MCMC, such as variance reduction methods or control variates.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods.
* The method is generally applicable and can be combined with other SG-MCMC algorithms.
* The paper is well-written (despite some density in the mathematical notation) and clearly explains the proposed method and its advantages.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The increased computational cost associated with running two chains is a significant drawback that is not fully addressed.
* The lack of detailed sensitivity analysis regarding parameter choices (e.g., step size, correlation matrix Σ) raises concerns about the robustness of the method.
* The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its relationship to other bias-reduction techniques in the SG-MCMC literature.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis and experimental validation are both well-executed. However, the increased computational cost and limited exploration of parameter sensitivity are legitimate concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand on the explanation of the mathematical notation,** especially regarding the correlated Brownian increments.
2.  **Provide a more detailed discussion of the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost.** Quantify the overhead of managing two chains.
3.  **Include a sensitivity analysis of the step size (γ)** to demonstrate the robustness of the method.
4.  **Briefly explore the impact of different choices for the correlation matrix Σ.**
5.  **Discuss how SGRRLD compares to other bias-reduction techniques in SG-MCMC.**

Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
