## Review of Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias of standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) by employing the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combine their results to achieve a higher order of accuracy. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and explore the method's applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms like SGHMC. They validate their approach through experiments on synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task, demonstrating improved performance and efficiency.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to the stochastic setting of MCMC, and particularly SG-MCMC, is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for SGRRLD, including convergence analysis and bounds on bias and MSE. The proof sketches are present and point to detailed proofs in the supplement, which is a good practice. Demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for MSE) is a compelling result.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The algorithm is well-suited for parallel computation as the two chains can be run independently.  The experiments on the large-scale matrix factorization problem demonstrate practical benefits in terms of both accuracy and computation time.
* **Generality:** The authors correctly point out that the RR extrapolation technique can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms besides SGLD, broadening its potential impact.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly explains the core ideas. The connection to established methods like SGLD and RR extrapolation is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Implementation:** While theoretically sound, the implementation details and practical considerations for choosing optimal step sizes and correlation parameters (Σ in equation 8) are not sufficiently addressed.  The discussion on the choice of Σ is somewhat brief.
* **Experimental Detail:**  The experimental setup, while providing results, lacks sufficient detail. For example, the justification for the specific step sizes chosen in the matrix factorization experiments isn’t clearly explained, beyond mentioning computation time.  A more thorough ablation study varying parameters would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Dependence on Technical Conditions:** The theoretical results rely on several technical conditions, and it's not entirely clear how easily these conditions would be met in practical applications beyond the specific examples presented.
* **Limited Comparison:** While SGHMC is included in experiments, comparison to other recent and relevant SG-MCMC algorithms (e.g., those building on SGLD with variance reduction techniques) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.




**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and provides a well-supported analysis. The theoretical results are compelling. However, the lack of extensive sensitivity analysis in the experiments and the reliance on potentially restrictive technical conditions slightly reduce the score.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally clear, but the explanation of certain implementation details (e.g., choice of Σ) could be more thorough. The notation is mostly standard but can be dense at times.
* **Originality (9/10):** The core idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is highly original and represents a significant contribution.
* **Significance (8/10):** The potential for improving the efficiency and accuracy of Bayesian inference in large-scale applications is significant. The demonstrated performance gains in the matrix factorization experiment further support this.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to improving SG-MCMC.
* Demonstrated performance gains in both synthetic and real-world experiments.
* Potential for broad applicability to various SG-MCMC algorithms.
* Well-written and generally clear presentation.

**Against:**

* Implementation complexity and sensitivity to parameter choices are not fully addressed.
* Limited scope of experimental comparison.
* Reliance on technical conditions that may be difficult to verify in practice.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. 

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical foundation, and the promising experimental results outweigh the minor weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions that address the points mentioned above, specifically:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of practical considerations for implementation, including strategies for choosing optimal step sizes and the correlation parameter Σ.
2.  Expand the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with a wider range of state-of-the-art SG-MCMC algorithms.
3.  Clarify the conditions under which the theoretical results hold and discuss their implications for practical applications.
