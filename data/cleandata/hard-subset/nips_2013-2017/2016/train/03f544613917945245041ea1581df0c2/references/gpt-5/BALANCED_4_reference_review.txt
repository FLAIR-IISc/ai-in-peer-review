Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of SGLD-type estimators. The algorithm runs two coupled discretizations of the same SDE, with step sizes γ and γ/2, and forms an extrapolated ergodic average akin to 2πγ/2(f) − πγ(f). To control the variance increase that may accompany bias reduction, the authors use correlated Gaussian increments across the two chains, with the optimal choice corresponding to sharing Brownian increments so that Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2.

Theoretically, the paper (i) proves a CLT for the RR-based estimator under decreasing step sizes, identifying regimes where the fluctuation or bias dominates and showing the variance is minimized when the Brownian increments are perfectly correlated, (ii) derives non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. For constant step size γ, the bias and MSE are O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal finite-time rates O(K^−2/3) and O(K^−4/5), respectively; with decaying steps γk ∝ k^−α, the optimal asymptotic rate for MSE occurs at α = 1/5 (O(K^−2/5)). These results mirror classical RR for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) but extend them to the stochastic-gradient setting, and compare favorably to first-order SG-MCMC results (e.g., Teh–Vollmer–Zygalakis; Chen–Ding–Carin for higher-order integrators).

Empirically, the paper validates the theory on a linear Gaussian model (bias/MSE scaling, including an SGHMC variant) and a large-scale matrix factorization task (MovieLens), showing improved RMSE vs. wall-clock time when running the two RR chains in parallel.

Quality
- Theory: The extension of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a careful variance control via correlated Brownian increments is technically sound and well-motivated. The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds align with known results for ULA and provide new rates in the SG setting. Assumptions (smoothness, existence of Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9, dissipativity/ergodicity conditions, bounded moments of stochastic gradients) are standard but fairly strong; a clear statement of the exact assumptions in the main text (not just supplement) would help.
- A potential issue: Eq. (5) appears to use θ(γ/2)2k+1 inside its own update gradient argument in the first half-step, which would be an implicit scheme; likely a typo that should read ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k) in the first line and ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) in the second.
- Experiments: The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate bias/MSE scaling and the benefit of RR in SGHMC. The matrix factorization results are positive and exploit parallel execution of the two chains. However, ablations are limited: there is no comparison to “run two independent SGLD chains in parallel and average” (which controls variance without bias reduction), nor to widely used strong baselines like pSGLD/SGNHT or the high-order SGHMC variants beyond a single SGHMC-s baseline.

Clarity
The exposition of the main idea is clear and well-grounded in prior literature, and the estimator is precisely defined. Some notation is heavy, and several key assumptions and the forms of σR^2 and μR are relegated to the supplement. The aforementioned likely typo in Eq. (5) needs correction. More implementation guidance on how to realize the Brownian coupling and mini-batch choices in practice would be beneficial.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and establishing both CLT and finite-time bounds in this noisy-gradient setting is novel and non-trivial. The idea of optimal variance reduction via correlated Brownian increments in the SG context is also a useful contribution. The work is well-differentiated from higher-order integrator approaches (e.g., Chen et al. 2015) and from RR in the exact-gradient ULA literature.

Significance
The approach is simple to implement, parallel-friendly, and demonstrably improves bias/MSE rates, matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order methods while using a first-order integrator. This can be impactful for large-scale Bayesian inference where parallel resources are available. The empirical section supports the claims but could be broader to strengthen practical significance across more models and baselines.

Pros
- Clear and well-motivated extension of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with rigorous analysis (CLT and finite-time bounds).
- Practical guidance on optimal Brownian coupling; variance minimal at Σ = I.
- Improved theoretical rates (bias/MSE) over first-order SG-MCMC; matches higher-order integrator accuracy while keeping simplicity.
- Demonstrated on synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization with parallel execution.

Cons
- Possible typo in the SG updates for the half-steps; needs correction.
- Strong/technical assumptions are not all stated in the main text; limited discussion of their practicality.
- Empirical comparisons could be stronger: missing ablation vs. two independent SGLD chains averaged; limited baselines (e.g., pSGLD, SGNHT); no ESS/mixing diagnostics.
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons hinges on parallelism; SGLD can also benefit from parallel gradient computations—some discussion/measurement would be useful.
- No experiments on recursive RR or multilevel variants, despite being discussed as future work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix Eq. (5) and provide a concise algorithm box in the main text with the exact coupling for Brownian increments and recommended mini-batch handling (same size, optional coupling).
- State key assumptions in the main paper and discuss their realism for common models; clarify dependence of constants on gradient noise moments.
- Add ablation: (i) two independent SGLD chains averaged under the same wall-clock; (ii) effect of Brownian coupling Σ (uncorrelated vs. optimal); (iii) coupling/anti-coupling of mini-batches across chains.
- Broaden baselines to include pSGLD, SGNHT, and high-order SGHMC under matched compute.
- Report ESS, autocorrelation, and sensitivity to step-size scheduling; provide guidance for choosing α and constants in practice.
- Consider a brief experiment on a non-Gaussian posterior (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression) to demonstrate generality.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a neat, theoretically solid idea that is simple, general, and shows promising empirical gains. With minor fixes and stronger empirical comparisons, it would be a strong addition to the SG-MCMC literature.
