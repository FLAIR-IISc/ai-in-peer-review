Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. The algorithm runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 in parallel and forms an extrapolated estimator that cancels the O(γ) weak error, yielding O(γ^2) bias and, with appropriate correlation, controlled variance. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a CLT (Theorem 1) for the extrapolated estimator under decreasing step sizes, showing minimal asymptotic variance when Brownian noise is perfectly correlated across the two chains; and (ii) non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) showing bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant steps, achieving optimal MSE rate O(K^−4/5) with γ ∝ K^−1/5. The method is generic and can be applied to other SG-MCMC (e.g., SGHMC). Experiments on a Gaussian model validate rates, an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) competes with higher-order SGHMC-s, and large-scale matrix factorization on MovieLens datasets shows improved RMSE at the same wall-clock time by exploiting parallelism.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and subsequent SG-MCMC developments, including analyses of bias/MSE for first- and higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016).
- Adapts classical RR extrapolation for SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) to the stochastic gradient setting, which to my knowledge has not been explored in SG-MCMC.
- Connects to ideas in multilevel Monte Carlo, though not fully developed here.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable: RR extrapolation atop standard SG-MCMC with a clear prescription for noise correlation.
- Solid theory: CLT with identification of optimal correlation, finite-time bias/MSE bounds, and step-size guidance (α=1/5 optimal for MSE; α=1/3 for bias).
- Practical relevance: parallelizable two-chain implementation preserves wall-clock time on multi-core settings; empirical improvements demonstrated on synthetic and large-scale real data.
- Extensibility: shows application to SGHMC and suggests potential for recursive RR or multilevel variants.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions are strong: CLT requires high smoothness (solution of Poisson equation g ∈ C9) and technical ergodicity/weak error conditions. The scope of models where these hold (notably nonconvex posteriors common in deep learning) is not clarified.
- Algorithm specification: In Eq. (5), gradients appear evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k+1 for both substeps; this seems like a typo (standard Euler uses θ(γ/2)2k for the first substep). Please clarify.
- Gradient noise handling: The theory emphasizes Brownian correlation; the role of correlating minibatches across chains is not explored empirically. In finite-time regimes, correlated minibatches may further reduce variance; guidance would help.
- Computational cost: Absent parallel hardware, SGRRLD incurs roughly 3 gradient estimates per iteration vs 1 for SGLD. The wall-clock comparisons assume two processes/cores; a discussion of scalability and fairness (e.g., same total FLOPs) would strengthen the case.
- Empirical scope: Real-data results are only for matrix factorization vs SGLD; comparisons to strong SG-MCMC baselines (pSGLD, SGNHT/SGRLD thermostats, preconditioning) are missing. Posterior quality diagnostics beyond RMSE would be informative.
- Clarity: Many key conditions and proofs are deferred to the supplement; a compact statement of assumptions (on U and the gradient estimator law L) in the main text would improve readability.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and rigorous in spirit, with clear finite-time and asymptotic statements and consistent rates. The need for perfectly correlated Brownian increments is well-motivated, and the non-asymptotic bounds align with the CLT. Empirics corroborate predicted scalings on a controlled Gaussian model and show encouraging results on large-scale MF. However, broader empirical validation and comparisons to more advanced SG-MCMC baselines would strengthen the evidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic idea is easy to grasp. Some equations need correction/clarification (Eq. (5) update points). Explicitly stating the main assumptions in the body would help. Implementation details for inducing Brownian correlation (shared random seeds and the exact linear combination) could be made more explicit.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a variance-control mechanism via correlated Brownian motion is, to my knowledge, novel. The work connects two established areas (SG-MCMC and RR/MLMC for SDEs) in a useful way.

Significance
If adopted, RR extrapolation could systematically improve SG-MCMC with modest engineering effort, especially in parallel environments. The theoretical rates match or surpass those of higher-order integrators while keeping a first-order scheme, which is practically attractive. The idea is likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., multilevel SG-MCMC, recursive RR).

Questions for authors
- Please confirm/correct the chain-2 updates in Eq. (5) (implicit vs explicit Euler).
- Do you reuse the same minibatch indices across the two half-steps and/or across chains? Any empirical ablation on minibatch correlation?
- How sensitive are results to imperfect Brownian correlation (e.g., due to parallel RNG or GPU kernels)?
- Can you comment on applicability when U is non-smooth or heavy-tailed, and whether your conditions hold for typical deep models?
- Could you report comparisons to pSGLD/SGNHT or Riemannian variants on at least one dataset?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with clear theoretical benefits
- Rigorous analysis with improved bias/MSE rates
- Practical parallelization and demonstrated gains on large-scale task
- Novel connection likely to influence future SG-MCMC work

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions; limited discussion of applicability beyond smooth settings
- Minor ambiguity/typo in algorithm equations
- Limited empirical comparisons to stronger baselines; reliance on parallelism for fairness

Overall recommendation: Accept (weak). The idea is elegant and well-supported theoretically, with promising empirical results; addressing clarity and broader comparisons would further strengthen the paper.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
