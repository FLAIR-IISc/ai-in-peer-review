## Review of Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, termed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to leverage the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce the bias of the standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm.  SGRRLD achieves this by running two Langevin chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide theoretical convergence analysis, demonstrating improved convergence rates (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) and present experimental validation on both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC) and a large-scale real-world problem (matrix factorization).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the foundation laid by Welling & Teh (SGLD) and subsequent advancements in SG-MCMC. It acknowledges the work on higher-order integrators for bias reduction (Chen et al., 2015) and demonstrates a way to achieve similar accuracy gains using a first-order integrator and the RR extrapolation.  The use of RR extrapolation itself is not novel in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) but its application to SG-MCMC is a significant contribution. The paper also connects to recent work on variance control in RR extrapolation (Lemaire et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely new idea, providing a practical approach to reducing bias without resorting to complex higher-order integrators.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates and demonstrating the benefits of the proposed approach. The proof sketch is included, and more details are promised in the supplementary material.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:**  The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical claims.  The matrix factorization experiment is particularly compelling, showcasing the practical benefits of SGRRLD in a large-scale setting. The experiments effectively demonstrate performance gains over SGLD and SGHMC.
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelism of running two independent chains makes the algorithm well-suited for modern computing architectures.
* **Generality:**  The authors highlight that the technique can be applied to various SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD (demonstrated with SGHMC), expanding its potential impact.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas, although some sections (like the detailed convergence analysis) could benefit from further elaboration.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Depth:** The theoretical analysis, while present, is somewhat condensed in the main paper.  A full understanding requires delving into the supplementary material.  The conditions on U, f, and L are mentioned but not thoroughly discussed.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** The assumption that correlated Brownian increments are crucial for variance control, while theoretically sound, might be challenging to implement effectively in practice, especially when dealing with very large datasets and complex gradient estimators. The impact of *not* perfectly correlating the gradients is acknowledged, but a more detailed discussion of practical considerations would be beneficial.
* **Step Size Tuning:** The optimal step size selection, while theoretically derived, might require careful tuning in real-world applications.  More guidance on practical step size selection strategies would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons to SGLD and SGHMC are provided, a comparison to other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods (e.g., those utilizing preconditioning or adaptive step sizes) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to improving the efficiency of SG-MCMC.
* The experimental results are compelling and support the theoretical claims.
* The algorithm is well-suited for parallel implementation, addressing a key challenge in large-scale Bayesian inference.
* The generality of the approach extends its potential impact beyond SGLD.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is somewhat condensed and requires careful study of the supplementary material.
* The practical implementation of correlated Brownian increments might pose challenges.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is a novel and effective way to reduce bias and improve convergence rates, and the theoretical analysis provides strong justification for its effectiveness.  The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach. While there are some areas for improvement (primarily related to the depth of theoretical discussion and practical implementation details), these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016.  The authors should consider:

* Providing more detailed explanations of the theoretical conditions in the main paper.
* Discussing the practical considerations and potential challenges of implementing correlated Brownian increments.
* Expanding the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods.
* Improving the clarity of the explanation of step size selection.



**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
