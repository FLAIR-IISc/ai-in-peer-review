- Proposes SGRRLD: Richardson–Romberg extrapolation applied to SGLD by coupling two chains (stepsizes γ and γ/2) to cancel O(γ) weak bias.  
- Uses correlated Brownian increments (optimal = perfect coupling) to control variance inflation from extrapolation.  
- Theoretical contributions: CLT for RR estimator under decreasing stepsizes showing minimal asymptotic variance with maximal Brownian correlation.  
- Provides non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds: bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant stepsizes.  
- Shows optimized decreasing stepsize yields MSE rate O(K^−4/5), improving on first-order SG-MCMC O(K^−2/3).  
- Empirical validation on linear Gaussian models, SGHMC variant, and large-scale matrix factorization shows reduced bias/MSE and improved test RMSE with parallel execution.  
- Novelty: first application and analysis of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with stochastic-gradient variance control via Brownian coupling.  
- Strength: conceptually simple, broadly applicable approach that layers on existing SG-MCMC without higher-order integrators.  
- Strength: rigorous theory including CLT, non-asymptotic bounds, and stepsize optimization aligning with experiments.  
- Strength: variance control via Brownian coupling is well-motivated and theoretically justified, extending exact-gradient results to stochastic gradients.  
- Strength: empirical wall-clock efficiency and extension to SGHMC indicate practical generality.  
- Concern: strong and technical assumptions (e.g., solution to Poisson equation in C^9, high-order gradient-noise moments) may limit practical applicability.  
- Concern: theory assumes gradient estimators for the two chains share the same law; coupling of mini-batches is not analyzed or clarified in experiments.  
- Concern: wall-clock comparisons assume extra cores—on fixed-core budgets SGRRLD doubles gradient work; comparisons at equal gradient evaluations needed.  
- Concern: limited baseline comparisons—omits variance-reduced SG-MCMC, preconditioned thermostats, and broader higher-order integrators.  
- Concern: practical diagnostics missing—ESS/time, autocorrelation, and sensitivity to stepsize/batch size are not reported.  
- Minor issues: some notational/indexing typos, clarity issues, and algorithm pseudo-code placed in supplement rather than main text.  
- Quality: analysis appears technically sound and consistent with known results, but constants depend on high-order moments and dimension which should be clarified.  
- Clarity: overall clear but dense; would benefit from a concise algorithm box and clearer statement of assumptions in the main text.  
- Significance: could offer a practical accuracy–cost improvement for SG-MCMC on parallel hardware and a meaningful theoretical rate boost.  
- Key questions: were mini-batches coupled across chains; how do constants scale with dimension and batch size; communication cost of Brownian coupling; ESS/time metrics; comparisons to control-variate SG-MCMC.  
- Pros summary: simple, general, parallel-friendly method with solid theory and practical gains; extends to other SG-MCMC schemes.  
- Cons summary: strong assumptions, fairness of wall-clock comparisons depends on extra cores, limited baselines and diagnostics, and gradient-noise coupling not explored.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (reviewer confidence: high).
