

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel approach to improving Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods through Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The authors propose SGRRLD, which runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combines their outputs through RR extrapolation to reduce bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) while keeping variance manageable. The key theoretical contribution is showing that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly better than the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. The paper also demonstrates that this approach can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators without requiring complex higher-order discretizations.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis for SDEs, its application to SG-MCMC represents a fresh perspective that bridges two distinct fields. The authors successfully extend RR extrapolation to the stochastic gradient setting, showing how to properly correlate the Brownian increments to control variance. This is a bold conceptual leap that could inspire new directions in scalable Bayesian inference. The paper's theoretical results are rigorous, with careful analysis of both asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties, and the experimental validation on both synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization tasks convincingly supports the theoretical claims.

The paper's significance is high. It addresses a fundamental limitation of current SG-MCMC methods—bias due to discretization—and provides a theoretically grounded solution that is also practical for parallel architectures. The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods while using only first-order integrators, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications where higher-order integrators might be computationally prohibitive. The parallel nature of the approach also aligns well with modern distributed computing paradigms.

The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear connections to prior work on SGLD and RR extrapolation. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental section provides strong evidence for the claimed improvements. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach and discuss how it relates to existing work, including the potential for extension to other SG-MCMC methods.

The main weakness is that the paper builds upon established techniques (SGLD and RR extrapolation) rather than introducing entirely new mathematical tools. However, the novel combination and application to SG-MCMC is sufficiently significant to outweigh this concern. The paper does not overclaim its contributions, and the experimental results are convincing.

What makes this paper particularly compelling is how it rethinks the trade-off between bias and variance in SG-MCMC. The authors show that by using RR extrapolation with appropriately correlated Brownian increments, they can simultaneously reduce bias and control variance—addressing a key challenge in the field. This is not just an incremental improvement but a conceptual advance that could influence future work on scalable Bayesian inference.

The paper's theoretical results are particularly strong: Theorem 2 provides clear non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and the analysis of the optimal step size sequences (γ = O(K⁻¹/⁵) for MSE) is both theoretically sound and practically relevant. The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets consistently demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

In the context of NIPS, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. It introduces a novel method that is theoretically grounded, practically implementable, and significantly outperforms existing approaches. The work is not merely an incremental improvement but a new direction that could influence future research on SG-MCMC methods.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty and significance of the approach outweigh any minor methodological concerns. The paper presents a bold and well-supported idea that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The authors have provided a complete and rigorous treatment of their method, with both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. This work has the potential to be highly influential in the Bayesian machine learning community, particularly for large-scale applications where computational efficiency is critical.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a significant problem in scalable Bayesian inference with a novel and impactful solution. It represents a meaningful advance over previous work and opens up new research directions in the application of numerical analysis techniques to MCMC. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their approach through both theory and experiments.
