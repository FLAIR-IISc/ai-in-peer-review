

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel approach to scaling Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for Bayesian inference by combining Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) with Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The proposed algorithm, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), applies RR extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with different step sizes in parallel, then combining their outputs. The key theoretical contribution is showing that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, resulting in a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly better than SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).

The paper makes a bold and novel contribution by applying RR extrapolation—a technique traditionally used in numerical analysis—to the SG-MCMC framework. This represents a significant conceptual leap from previous work on SG-MCMC, which has focused primarily on higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS 2015) to improve convergence rates. The authors cleverly demonstrate that RR extrapolation can achieve the same theoretical accuracy as higher-order integrators while using only first-order numerical methods, with the added advantage of parallelizability.

The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful derivation of central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The paper successfully shows that SGRRLD's convergence rate surpasses all known SG-MCMC methods using first-order integrators. The experimental results on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks consistently support the theoretical claims, with SGRRLD demonstrating significantly improved accuracy compared to SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper's greatest strength lies in its conceptual novelty. While previous work has explored higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC (Chen et al., 2015), this paper introduces a fundamentally different approach to improving convergence rates that doesn't require complex numerical schemes. The method is also remarkably general—it can be applied to virtually any SG-MCMC algorithm, as the authors demonstrate by extending it to SGHMC as SGRRHMC.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with appropriate technical depth. The experiments are comprehensive and directly support the theoretical claims. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior art, particularly distinguishing their approach from higher-order integrator methods.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The experiments, while supportive, could have included more diverse datasets or model complexities to further validate the generality of the approach.
2. The discussion of computational overhead could have been more detailed—while the paper notes the parallelizability, it doesn't quantify the exact speedup compared to sequential implementations.
3. The paper assumes the two chains can be run with correlated Brownian increments, which requires careful implementation details not fully explored.

However, these minor points do not detract from the paper's significant contribution. The authors have presented a genuinely novel approach that fundamentally changes how we might think about improving SG-MCMC convergence rates. The paper's theoretical results are compelling, the experimental validation is solid, and the practical implications for large-scale Bayesian inference are substantial.

This work stands out from the typical incremental improvements in the SG-MCMC literature. While previous papers have focused on refining existing integrators or adding momentum, this paper introduces a completely new conceptual framework for improving convergence rates. The authors have successfully bridged numerical analysis (RR extrapolation) with machine learning (SG-MCMC), creating a novel direction for future research.

Given my review criteria prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The conceptual contribution is significant, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results validate the claims. The paper represents a meaningful advancement in scalable Bayesian inference, not just an incremental improvement on existing methods.

The authors have provided a complete and well-executed piece of work that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. Their approach to using RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC is bold, novel, and promising for future research in scalable Bayesian methods.

**Recommendation: Accept**
