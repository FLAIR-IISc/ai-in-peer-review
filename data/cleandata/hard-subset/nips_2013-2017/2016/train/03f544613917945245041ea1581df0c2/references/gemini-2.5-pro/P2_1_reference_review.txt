**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators used by methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed method leverages Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating convergence. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order bias term. A key element for practical success is the use of correlated Brownian motion between the chains to control the variance of the resulting estimator.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean-Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD. This allows a first-order method to match the theoretical accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated empirically on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem for movie recommendations, showing substantial improvements over SGLD in terms of accuracy and wall-clock time.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant idea. It addresses a fundamental and well-known limitation of popular methods like SGLD—the bias introduced by the Euler-Maruyama discretization. The demonstrated improvement in the theoretical convergence rate for the MSE (from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) is a significant contribution to the field. This work provides a new, principled way to improve the accuracy of a wide class of sampling algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality and Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is technically very strong. The authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis, including a CLT (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step-size decay rate (α=1/5) to balance bias and variance, which is a non-trivial result. The theoretical claims are well-supported and build upon established literature in SDEs and stochastic approximation. The insight to use correlated noise (Eq. 7) to minimize the asymptotic variance is crucial and well-justified by the theory.

3.  **Practicality and Empirical Validation:** The proposed method is not just a theoretical curiosity; it is highly practical. The key advantage is its inherent parallelism. The two required chains can be run on separate cores or machines with minimal communication, making it a perfect fit for modern computational architectures. The experiments convincingly support the theory.
    *   The synthetic Gaussian model experiments are well-designed, clearly illustrating the reduction in bias and MSE (Figures 1, 2) and empirically verifying the derived optimal convergence rates (Figure 3).
    *   The application to SGHMC (Figure 4) effectively demonstrates the generality of the proposed RR framework beyond just SGLD.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments (Figure 5) show a clear and compelling real-world benefit, with SGRRLD achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, provides the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation, and then systematically presents the new algorithm, its theoretical properties, and experimental results. The logic is easy to follow, and the claims are stated precisely.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Cost:** The most direct drawback is that SGRRLD requires approximately double the computational resources (or three gradient evaluations per "meta-iteration" compared to SGLD's one). While the authors correctly argue that parallelism can make the wall-clock time competitive or even superior, this is contingent on the availability of parallel hardware. In a strictly serial environment, the method would be slower per iteration. This trade-off between per-iteration cost and total number of iterations for a given accuracy should be discussed more explicitly.

2.  **Memory Overhead:** The method requires maintaining two separate MCMC chains, which doubles the memory required to store the state of the samplers. For models with an extremely large number of parameters (e.g., massive neural networks), this could become a practical concern.

3.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The paper claims to achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. While the experiment with SGHMC-s provides some evidence, a more direct empirical comparison between SGRRLD and a second-order SG-MCMC method (like those in Chen et al., 2015) in the matrix factorization task would have made this claim even more compelling. It would be interesting to see if the simpler implementation of SGRRLD offers advantages over the potentially more complex implementation of a higher-order integrator in practice.

**Justification for Rating**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The idea of using RR extrapolation is both simple and powerful. The strengths—strong theory, significant improvement in convergence rates, compelling empirical results, and high practicality due to parallelism—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to computational overhead. The work clearly advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC and is likely to be influential, inspiring researchers to apply this technique to other stochastic algorithms. It perfectly fits the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
