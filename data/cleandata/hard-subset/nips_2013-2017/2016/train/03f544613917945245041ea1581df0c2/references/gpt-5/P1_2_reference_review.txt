Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a simple but insightful application of Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC), primarily SGLD. The method runs two coupled SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and extrapolates their ergodic averages to cancel the leading-order discretization bias, while using correlated Gaussian noise across chains to control variance. The authors prove: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator with minimal asymptotic variance achieved by a specific coupling of Brownian increments (Σ = I), (ii) asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4), and (iii) non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, showing an optimal MSE rate O(K−4/5) versus O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC without RR. They argue their approach matches the accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen–Ding–Carin 2015) while retaining a first-order integrator and fitting distributed settings since the two chains can be run in parallel. Experiments on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support improved accuracy and wall-clock efficiency; an SGHMC version (SGRRHMC) similarly improves over SGHMC and approaches SGHMC with higher-order integrators.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and SGHMC (Chen et al., 2014), and the general SG-MCMC framework (Ma et al., 2015).
- Uses RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup, 2015; Lamberton–Pagès) to reduce bias; extends ideas to the noisy-gradient setting and emphasizes optimal coupling of Gaussian increments (as in duplicated diffusions).
- Compares favorably to high-order SG-MCMC integrators (Chen–Ding–Carin, 2015), claiming similar rates without higher-order discretization.

Quality
- Theory appears sound and follows known strategy via Poisson equation/Taylor expansions. The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are consistent with intuition: RR halves bias order while appropriate coupling keeps variance in check. The optimal step-size exponents and rates are clearly derived.
- Assumptions are strong (existence of smooth solution to the Poisson equation g ∈ C9, technical conditions on U and L), common in this literature but limiting for non-convex/posterior landscapes typical in deep models.
- Some implementation details are under-specified: while Brownian coupling is explicit, the treatment of stochastic gradient coupling is left open; the paper notes both chains must share the same L and mini-batch size to avoid reintroducing O(γ) bias, but practical guidance on minibatch coupling (e.g., shared minibatches across chains/half-steps) is not provided.
- Minor technical clarity issues: there seems to be a likely typo in Eq. (5) where the first half-step uses ∇Ũ at θ(γ/2)2k+1 (should be at θ(γ/2)2k); also Section 2.1’s ULA noise term appears inconsistent (√γ vs √(2γ)) with the SDE dθ = −∇U dt + √2 dB.

Clarity
- The narrative is generally clear, with precise statements of main theorems and interpretation (optimal α, asymptotic vs finite-time regimes). Deferring proofs to the supplement is reasonable; however, given the simplicity of the algorithm, including the pseudocode in the main text would help reproducibility.
- Some notation is heavy; a brief “recipe” box summarizing the estimator and the correlation choices would benefit readers.
- Experimental descriptions are clear; plots show the expected rates and bias/MSE behavior.

Originality
- RR extrapolation is classical in SDE numerics, but its use in SG-MCMC with explicit variance control via correlated Brownian increments is, to my knowledge, novel in the NeurIPS literature at the time. The insight that one can match high-order integrator accuracy using two first-order chains and coupling is elegant and practically relevant.
- The extension to SGHMC further supports generality.

Significance
- The method is simple to implement, generic, and parallelizable. The improved finite-time rates (O(K−4/5)) are meaningful, and the empirical results indicate practical benefits under a fixed wall-clock when parallelism is available.
- Could impact practitioners who want higher accuracy without implementing higher-order integrators or second derivatives.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical coupling of stochastic gradients: The analysis treats Gaussian noise coupling carefully, but offers no concrete scheme for mini-batch coupling. Using identical minibatches across the two chains and across the two half-steps might reduce additional variance; please clarify recommended practice and its effect on constants.
- Resource fairness: The wall-clock comparison exploits two parallel processes for SGRRLD. In many settings, SGLD can also exploit parallelism for gradient computation and data loading. A comparison under matched compute resources (e.g., same total gradient evaluations per second, or multi-core for both methods) would be fairer.
- Assumptions and robustness: The smoothness/ergodicity conditions are strong and may fail for heavy-tailed or multi-modal posteriors common in deep learning. Discussion of robustness or diagnostics would strengthen the claims.
- Constants matter: While rates improve, constant factors can dominate at practical K. The experiments are supportive, but broader benchmarks (including more challenging posteriors) and sensitivity to step size and batch size would be valuable.
- Typos/minor inconsistencies as noted above.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit guidance on gradient-noise coupling: whether to use shared minibatches across chains/half-steps, and empirical impact.
- Clarify Eq. (5) and the ULA noise scaling consistency in Section 2.1.
- Include the algorithm pseudocode in the main text; add a short paragraph on implementation tips (e.g., reuse RNG streams to implement the Brownian coupling).
- Add a compute-normalized comparison (e.g., same total number of stochastic gradients per second) and possibly a decreasing-step-size experiment on the large datasets.
- Discuss applicability to other SG-MCMC methods (SGRLD, thermostats) and any pitfalls.

Pros
- Simple, general idea with solid theory and clear improvement in rates.
- Variance control via optimal Brownian coupling is well-justified.
- Parallel-friendly; practical gains shown on large datasets.
- Extends to SGHMC and matches higher-order integrator accuracy.

Cons
- Strong assumptions; limited discussion of non-ideal settings.
- Practical guidance on minibatch coupling missing.
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons could be improved.
- Minor notational/typo issues.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong and timely contribution that combines classical numerical analysis with modern SG-MCMC to yield a practically useful and theoretically grounded improvement. I lean toward acceptance after addressing clarity and experimental fairness concerns.
