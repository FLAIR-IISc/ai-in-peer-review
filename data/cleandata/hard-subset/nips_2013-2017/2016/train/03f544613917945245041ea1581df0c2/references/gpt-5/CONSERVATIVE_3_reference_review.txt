Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC variant that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of SGLD without using higher-order integrators. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and combines their estimates with an RR estimator, using correlated Gaussian noises across chains to control variance. Theoretical results include a CLT for the RR estimator, non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and rates suggesting improved error decay over SGLD (e.g., MSE O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3}) for first-order integrators). Experiments on a Gaussian model, SGHMC, and large-scale matrix factorization show empirical improvements, with a focus on wall-clock time when parallelizing the two chains.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and subsequent SG-MCMC results (e.g., CLT and bounds in Teh et al., 2016; high-order integrators in Chen et al., 2015). It leverages RR extrapolation ideas originally developed for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, Lemaire et al.), and multilevel/extrapolation literature (Pagès). The novelty is adapting RR to SG-MCMC and establishing error bounds in that setting, plus the use of correlated Gaussian increments to minimize asymptotic variance.

Strengths
- Clear and technically careful extension of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with nontrivial analysis (CLT, bias/MSE bounds).
- Insight that correlated Brownian increments minimize variance; explicit guidance (Σ = I).
- Non-asymptotic fixed-γ bounds are practically relevant and compare favorably to known SGLD bounds.
- The method is simple to implement on top of SGLD and easily parallelizable.
- Empirical results broadly consistent with theory on synthetic and a real matrix factorization task; extension to SGHMC is demonstrated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions for the theory are strong (solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C9, smoothness, weak-error expansion); many practical posteriors in ML violate high-order smoothness (nonsmooth priors, piecewise models). It is unclear how robust the guarantees are in realistic settings.
- The claim that “Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” critically depends on conditions that may not hold when minibatch noise is substantial; this deserves empirical ablations (e.g., varying batch sizes) and clearer assumptions.
- Experimental validation is limited in scope:
  - Comparisons focus mainly on SGLD and SGHMC. Missing baselines include other first-order SGMCMC improvements (e.g., preconditioned SGLD, thermostats, control variates/variance-reduced SG-MCMC) or higher-order SGLD-type integrators.
  - Fairness concerns: SGRRLD uses two parallel chains and is compared by wall-clock time, but SGLD could also leverage parallel replicas or vectorization. A comparison under equal gradient-evaluation budgets (or showing both) is needed.
  - Limited diagnostics of posterior accuracy (ESS, calibration, comparisons to full-batch ULA/MALA or exact posteriors on small problems). RMSE in matrix factorization is not sufficient to establish posterior fidelity.
  - The benefit on complex, non-Gaussian posteriors is not thoroughly explored.
- The method relies on using the same minibatch size for both chains; whether minibatches are shared to correlate gradient noise is unclear. Not sharing could inflate variance; sharing could reintroduce bias depending on details. This is an important practical detail to specify and test.
- Constants in the bounds may be significant; the empirical improvements, while present, appear modest in some settings, and the practical trade-off of two chains vs one under fixed computational resources is not fully quantified.

Quality
Technical development is solid and well connected to SDE/RR literature. However, strength of assumptions and the gap between theory and common ML settings reduce confidence in practical guarantees. Experimental methodology needs stronger baselines, fairer budgeting, and more diagnostics to convincingly support broad claims.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, but many essential assumptions and proofs are deferred to the supplement. Implementation details for the correlated noises and gradient estimators should be made explicit. Minor notational density could be eased.

Originality
Moderate. RR extrapolation for SDEs is known; the application to SG-MCMC with correlated noise and proofs of improved rates is a reasonable and nontrivial adaptation, yet conceptually incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners who can parallelize and want lower-bias first-order SG-MCMC without implementing high-order integrators. However, practical impact is uncertain given compute overhead, assumptions, and limited experimental breadth.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please clarify whether the two chains share the same minibatch indices at each synchronized time or use independent minibatches. Provide an ablation on this choice and its impact on variance/bias.
- Report results under equal total gradient evaluations in addition to equal wall-clock time. Consider multi-replica SGLD as a parallel baseline.
- Add baselines: preconditioned SGLD, thermostat methods, and variance-reduced SG-MCMC. For SGLD, a higher-order or splitting integrator baseline analogous to SGHMC-s would strengthen the case.
- Provide posterior diagnostics (ESS, R-hat, KL to a high-quality reference posterior on small problems).
- Discuss applicability when U is not C9 or has nonsmooth components; if possible, include an experiment with a nonsmooth model.
- Quantify constants: show how the empirical error scales with K and γ across a wider range, and discuss the regime where the predicted O(K^{-4/5}) rate is observable.

Pros
- Simple idea with solid theory and practical implementation.
- Clear guidance on correlated noise to control variance.
- Empirical gains consistent with predicted trends.

Cons
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; unclear robustness.
- Limited and arguably unfair experimental comparisons.
- Missing baselines; limited posterior diagnostics.
- Practical gains may depend on parallel resources; unclear trade-offs under fixed compute.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (weak reject). Interesting and technically competent, but the empirical validation and fairness of comparisons are insufficient for NIPS. Strengthening the experiments, clarifying practical details (minibatch correlation), and adding broader baselines would make a much stronger case.

Reviewer confidence: 4/5
