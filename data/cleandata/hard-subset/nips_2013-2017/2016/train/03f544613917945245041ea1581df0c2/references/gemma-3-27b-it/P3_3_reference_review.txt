## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm. The core idea is to leverage the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, commonly used in numerical analysis, to reduce the bias of the standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two Langevin chains in parallel with different step sizes and then extrapolating their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (MSE of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) compared to SGLD’s O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>)) and show that SGRRLD can achieve accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators with only a first-order numerical integrator. They validate their theoretical findings through experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in SG-MCMC, starting with the foundational SGLD by Welling & Teh.  It correctly positions itself within the line of research aiming to scale Bayesian inference via stochastic optimization techniques.  The authors appropriately cite relevant extensions to SGLD and the recent work by Chen et al. (2015) on convergence bounds. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation from numerical analysis is also well-established, and the authors correctly highlight its application to SDEs in prior work (Talay & Tubaro, Lemaire et al.).  The discussion of SGHMC and its variants, along with the application of RR to it, further demonstrates awareness of the landscape of existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR extrapolation is well-known, its application to this context is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE.  The analysis appears careful and well-motivated. The derivation of the improved convergence rate is a key strength.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and the key concepts (SGLD, RR extrapolation, SGRRLD) are explained clearly. The algorithm itself is presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical claims. The matrix factorization experiments, in particular, demonstrate the practical benefit of SGRRLD in a large-scale setting.
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelizability of the algorithm, due to the independent nature of the two chains, is a significant advantage, particularly for modern computational infrastructure.
* **Generality:** The authors convincingly argue for the generality of the approach, noting its applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Overhead:** While theoretically promising, the overhead of running two chains (even in parallel) compared to a single chain in SGLD isn't explicitly discussed in detail in terms of constant factors. The experiments demonstrate improved *wall-clock* time, but a more detailed breakdown of computational costs would be beneficial.
* **Step Size Tuning:**  The theoretical analysis suggests an optimal step size decay rate. However, tuning these hyperparameters in practice can be challenging, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical considerations for step size selection.  The limited range of step sizes explored in the synthetic experiments is also a minor concern.
* **Correlation of Gradients:**  The discussion regarding correlated stochastic gradients feels a little hand-wavy. While the authors acknowledge it’s important for optimal performance, the implications of *not* achieving perfect correlation are not fully explored.  A more detailed investigation of different strategies for correlating the gradients could be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  While comparisons to SGLD and SGHMC are provided, the paper could benefit from a comparison to other recent SG-MCMC algorithms that employ variance reduction techniques (e.g., SAGA, SVRG).
* **Minor Presentation Issues:** The notation is generally clear, but occasional inconsistencies (e.g., differing symbols for step sizes) could be smoothed out.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides clear convergence guarantees.
* The experimental results are compelling and support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.
* The parallelizability of the algorithm is a significant advantage.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical overhead of running two chains, and the implications for constant factors in runtime, are not fully explored.
* The discussion on correlated gradients could be more detailed and informative.
* The comparison to other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC algorithms is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, coupled with its strong theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results, makes it a worthy addition to NIPS.  While there are some minor weaknesses, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical overhead of running two chains and the implications for computational cost.
2.  Elaborate on the impact of gradient correlation on performance and explore different correlation strategies.
3.  Include a comparison to other relevant state-of-the-art SG-MCMC algorithms.
4. Address any minor notational inconsistencies.
