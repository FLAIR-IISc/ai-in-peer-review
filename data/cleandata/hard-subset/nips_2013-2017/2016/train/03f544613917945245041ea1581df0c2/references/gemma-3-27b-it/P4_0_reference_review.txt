This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aimed at reducing the bias of SGLD by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine their outputs to achieve a higher-order convergence rate. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and showcase empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets supporting their claims.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, algorithm, theoretical analysis, and experimental validation. The connection to existing work in SG-MCMC and numerical analysis (Richardson-Romberg extrapolation) is effectively established in the introduction and preliminaries. The theoretical results, particularly the convergence analysis, are a significant contribution. The authors rigorously demonstrate the bias and variance reduction achieved by SGRRLD and derive convergence rates for both the bias and MSE. The experimental section provides a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm, including comparisons with SGLD and SGHMC on both Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task. The experiments effectively showcase the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC algorithms is a novel contribution. This extends a well-established technique from numerical analysis to the realm of Bayesian inference.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, including convergence guarantees and bounds on bias and MSE. The derivation of improved convergence rates is a significant accomplishment.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.
*   **Completeness:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets, which reinforces the validity of the theoretical results.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelism of SGRRLD, stemming from running two chains independently, is highlighted, making it well-suited for modern computing architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address the practical challenges of tuning the step sizes (γ and γ/2) in SGRRLD, particularly in high-dimensional problems. While the theory guides optimal choices, real-world applications may require more robust adaptation techniques.
*   **Correlation of Gradients:** The assumption of correlated Brownian increments and its impact on performance is briefly touched upon. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between correlation and computational cost could be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:** Although parallelism is a benefit, running two chains inherently doubles the computational cost per iteration. A more detailed discussion of the computational overhead and the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons with SGLD and SGHMC are present, a comparison against other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods (e.g., those utilizing preconditioning or adaptive step sizes) would provide a more complete benchmark.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound algorithm that addresses a significant challenge in SG-MCMC (bias reduction).
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the convergence behavior of the proposed approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian inference, particularly in large-scale applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical considerations of step size tuning and computational overhead are not fully addressed.
*   The comparison with other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods is limited.
*   The assumption about correlated Brownian motions needs further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While some practical considerations could be further explored, the contributions of this work are substantial and warrant acceptance to NIPS. The paper addresses a relevant problem in a creative and rigorous manner, and the results are likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the machine learning community.
