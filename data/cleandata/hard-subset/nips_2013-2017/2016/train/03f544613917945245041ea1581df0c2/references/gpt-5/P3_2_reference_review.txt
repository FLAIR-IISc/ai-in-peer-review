Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the O(γ) weak error of SGLD to O(γ^2) using two coupled discretizations with steps γ and γ/2. The key idea mirrors Talay–Tubaro expansions and RR extrapolation for SDEs, but adapts them to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients. The method runs two SGLD chains to the same time horizon, uses a coupling of their Gaussian noises so that Zγ ≈ (Zfine,1 + Zfine,2)/√2, and forms a time-weighted estimator that corresponds to 2π̂γ/2(f) − π̂γ(f). The authors establish a CLT for the estimator under decreasing step-sizes with optimal variance attained by the exact Brownian coupling, and derive non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds for constant and decreasing step-sizes. They claim bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding an optimal finite-time MSE rate O(K^-4/5) (vs. O(K^-2/3) for SGLD and first-order integrators). Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support improved accuracy at matched wall-clock time by running the two chains in parallel. They also show an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) competitive with higher-order integrators (SGHMC-s).

The work builds on: SGLD and SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh 2011; Teh, Vollmer & Zygalakis 2016; Ma et al. 2015; Chen et al. 2015), classical RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures (Talay & Tubaro; Lamberton & Pagès; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup 2015), and variance control for duplicated diffusions via correlated Brownian paths. To my knowledge, applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and analyzing its bias/MSE trade-offs is novel and timely.

Quality:
- Theory: The asymptotic CLT (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are carefully argued via Poisson equation techniques and high-order Taylor expansions, following the literature on weak error expansions. The variance minimization via exact Brownian coupling is well motivated and consistent with duplicated diffusion results. The finite-time trade-offs (γ ∝ K^-1/5 for MSE) are clear and mirror known analyses for SG-MCMC with higher-order integrators.
- Assumptions: The required regularity is strong: existence of a C^9 solution to the Poisson equation, high-order smoothness of U, polynomial growth bounds, and conditions ensuring geometric ergodicity. For stochastic gradients, the results depend on moment conditions and controlled variance, and the CLT conclusion that “Gaussian noise dominates stochastic gradient noise” hinges on these conditions and the step-size regime; this should be stated more explicitly. The necessity of using the same mini-batch size across levels to avoid first-order mismatch is noted; however, whether sharing the same mini-batches across levels is beneficial for variance reduction is not explored (see below).
- Experiments: The synthetic Gaussian model is an appropriate sanity check where the posterior is known; the observed bias/MSE scalings are consistent with theory. The dimension-scaling study is informative. The matrix factorization experiments are meaningful and demonstrate practical gains at matched wall-clock time by exploiting parallelism. The SGHMC comparison suggests that RR can match higher-order integrators’ accuracy using first-order steps.

Clarity:
Overall well written and organized. The construction of the estimator (Eq. 6) and the coupling (Eqs. 7–8) are clear. Some notational slips exist in Eq. (5) (the argument of ∇Ũ for the first fine step seems misstated) and could use careful proofreading. Many technical assumptions and proofs are deferred to the supplement; while standard for NeurIPS, it would help to make the key conditions (e.g., assumptions on the stochastic gradient noise: unbiasedness, bounded variance, state dependence) explicit in the main text.

Originality:
High. RR extrapolation has a long history in SDE numerics, but adapting it to SG-MCMC, identifying appropriate couplings for variance control, and deriving finite-time rates in the presence of gradient noise appear novel. The extension to SGHMC indicates generality beyond overdamped Langevin.

Significance:
Moderate to high. The method is simple to implement, generic (applicable to SGLD/SGHMC and others satisfying weak error/ergodicity conditions), and parallelizable. The improved bias/MSE rates are theoretically appealing and practically relevant. The idea is likely to inspire further work (e.g., multilevel SG-MCMC, unbiased estimators) and may be useful to practitioners when parallel resources are available.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Strength of assumptions and scope: The smoothness/Poisson equation assumptions limit immediate applicability to posteriors arising in nonsmooth or heavy-tailed models. Please clarify how the theory adapts if gradients are only Lipschitz and dissipative.
- Role of stochastic gradient noise: The analysis treats stochastic gradient noise as a perturbation and asserts that Gaussian noise dominates. In practice with small batches and state-dependent variance, gradient noise can dominate. Can the authors formalize conditions under which the RR cancellation is not destroyed by the gradient noise term? Is there a bias expansion constant C that depends on L, and does it cancel at order γ when L differs across levels?
- Coupling of gradient estimators: The paper only correlates Brownian noise. Sharing mini-batches (or using common random numbers) across levels would likely reduce variance further and may be necessary in small-batch regimes. Please discuss and, if possible, provide an experiment showing the effect of using common mini-batches across the two levels versus independent ones.
- Computational budget: SGRRLD doubles gradient computations per time horizon. The paper achieves matched wall-clock time via two processes on a multicore CPU. This may not hold on single-GPU or resource-limited settings. A discussion or experiment varying the parallel resources would strengthen claims about practical efficiency.
- Empirical breadth: Experiments focus on Gaussian linear models and matrix factorization. Including a standard Bayesian logistic regression or a neural network posterior would demonstrate robustness. Also, the main text promises decreasing step-size experiments; these are relegated to the supplement.
- Constants and dimension dependence: The non-asymptotic bounds hide constants depending on moments of the gradient estimator and potentially on d. Any discussion or empirical evidence on dimension dependence would be helpful.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, elegant application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Solid theoretical analysis (CLT and finite-time bias/MSE bounds) with improved rates
- Variance control via optimal Brownian coupling is principled
- Parallel-friendly and easy to implement
- Empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization; extension to SGHMC

Cons:
- Strong regularity assumptions; limited discussion of state-dependent SG noise
- No coupling of stochastic gradients across levels; potential missed variance reduction
- Practical efficiency depends on parallel resources; not always free
- Experimental scope could be broader; some details deferred to supplement

Questions for the authors:
1) Can you formalize the conditions on the stochastic gradient noise (e.g., bounded state-dependent variance, martingale difference) under which the O(γ) bias cancellation holds? What fails if the mini-batch sizes differ across levels?
2) Have you tried sharing mini-batches across the two levels to couple the gradient noise? How does it affect variance/MSE?
3) Can you report an experiment with decreasing step-sizes in the main text to confirm the α = 1/5 optimality for MSE?
4) What is the empirical impact of imperfect Brownian coupling (Σ ≠ Id) and of using independent Brownian streams?
5) Could you comment on dimension dependence of the constants in Theorem 2?

Overall assessment:
This is a strong, technically sound, and original contribution that advances SG-MCMC by importing RR extrapolation with appropriate couplings, with both theoretical and empirical support. Addressing the concerns about gradient noise coupling and clarifying assumptions would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
