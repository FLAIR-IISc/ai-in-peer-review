Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC (in particular SGLD) to cancel first-order discretization bias while keeping variance controlled via correlated Brownian increments. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and combines their time-averaged estimates with the RR weights. The authors analyze both asymptotic and finite-time behavior: they prove a CLT for the extrapolated estimator under decreasing step sizes, non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and show optimal rates—MSE O(K^-4/5) vs O(K^-2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC—while only using a first-order integrator. They also show that correlating the Brownian increments as in Zγ = (Zγ/2,1 + Zγ/2,2)/√2 minimizes the asymptotic variance. Empirically, they demonstrate improvements over SGLD on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization, and show that the same idea improves SGHMC (SGRRHMC), matching the accuracy of a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s) at larger steps.

Relation to prior work:
- Builds on RR extrapolation and Talay–Tubaro expansions for SDEs, and duplication/coupling arguments for invariant distributions (Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup).
- Extends consistency and fluctuation analyses of SGLD (Teh et al.; Vollmer et al.) by incorporating an RR estimator with correlated Brownian increments.
- Complements high-order integrator approaches for SG-MCMC (Chen et al., 2015) by achieving higher-order accuracy using only first-order discretization plus extrapolation.
- Related to multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) ideas; the authors mention potential extensions.

Quality and correctness:
- The theoretical development appears sound and nontrivial. The CLT (Theorem 1) with conditions on ΓK and Γ(3)K, the identification of α = 1/5 as optimal, and the non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are consistent with classical RR/MLMC intuition adapted to the SG setting. The observation that Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise when γ → 0 is well-justified.
- The variance-minimization over the correlation parameter Σ is in line with duplicated-diffusion results and is practically actionable.
- Finite-K bounds for bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) are useful and correctly imply optimal choices γ ∝ K^-1/3 (bias) and γ ∝ K^-1/5 (MSE).
- Assumptions are strong (solution to Poisson equation with g ∈ C^9, smooth f with polynomial growth, dissipativity/ergodicity), but typical for this line of analysis. It would be helpful to state them explicitly in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful intuition about bias–variance trade-offs. However:
  - Equation (5) seems to have a likely typo: the first half-step’s gradient is evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k+1 instead of θ(γ/2)2k. Please correct if this is not an implicit scheme.
  - Important technical assumptions are deferred to the supplement; summarizing the key ones (e.g., dissipativity, Lipschitz, bounded moments of the gradient estimator) would improve readability.
  - The dependence on the mini-batch distribution L is important; highlighting when differing mini-batch sizes introduce first-order bias would help practitioners.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with an explicit variance-minimizing coupling and deriving SG-specific CLT and non-asymptotic bounds is, to my knowledge, novel for 2016. The extension to SGHMC is also natural and useful. The connection drawn to higher-order integrators and MLMC is insightful.

Significance and impact:
- The work provides a principled, generic variance/bias reduction technique for SG-MCMC with potentially broad applicability (SGLD, SGHMC, thermostats). The ability to exploit parallelism by running the two chains concurrently is practically attractive.
- The improvement in finite-time rates and empirical gains on large datasets make this relevant for practitioners.

Empirical evaluation:
- Synthetic Gaussian experiments clearly corroborate the predicted bias/MSE scalings and the optimal exponent choices (α = 1/3 for bias, α = 1/5 for MSE).
- The SGHMC experiment suggests the approach can match a higher-order integrator’s accuracy without implementing it.
- The matrix factorization experiments are large-scale and run both chains in parallel, showing consistent RMSE improvements at equal wall-clock time.
- Some caveats:
  - The fairness of the wall-clock comparison relies on perfect parallelism. Many users may not have spare cores/GPUs; reporting results at equal total FLOPs/gradient evaluations (or a serial version with doubled K for SGLD) would strengthen the case.
  - It would be informative to report whether using the same mini-batch indices across the two chains (i.e., correlating the stochastic gradient noise) further reduces variance in practice, and to include an ablation varying B and correlation choices.
  - Experiments are limited to Gaussian likelihoods; including a non-Gaussian posterior (e.g., logistic regression) would increase generality.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can the authors clarify whether gradient estimators are coupled across the two chains (e.g., same Rk) in experiments? The theory suggests Brownian correlation dominates asymptotically, but finite-K performance might benefit from gradient coupling.
- What practical guidance do you recommend for choosing between constant and decreasing step sizes? For practitioners with a fixed budget, should one use γ ∝ K^-1/5 and time-averaging?
- How sensitive are the gains to model misspecification and non-smooth priors/likelihoods?
- For SGHMC, please detail friction/mass choices in the main text and any stability constraints of SGRRHMC.

Minor issues:
- Several typographical errors; please proofread equations and subscripts closely (noted above).
- Clarify the notation in Eq. (4) for the Brownian term as √(2γk+1) Z(γ)k+1 to match Eq. (2).

Pros:
- Clear, principled idea; theoretically grounded with SG-specific CLT and finite-time bounds
- Simple to implement; generic across SG-MCMC variants; exploits parallelism
- Empirical results support theory; improvements on large-scale tasks

Cons:
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; not fully discussed in main text
- Experimental comparisons could be more comprehensive/fair to non-parallel settings
- Some implementation details and ablations missing (gradient coupling, effect of B)

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept). The paper offers a neat, general technique with solid theory and promising empirical results, and should be of interest to the SG-MCMC community. My confidence is high.
