Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a new SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce discretization bias while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. Concretely, the method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2; at each “coarse” step it performs two “fine” steps and forms an extrapolated estimator proportional to 2πγ/2(f) − πγ(f). Theoretical contributions include a CLT for the extrapolated estimator (with minimal asymptotic variance achieved by perfectly correlated Brownian noises across levels), non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds showing O(γ2) bias and O(γ4) variance (vs. O(γ) and O(γ2) for first-order SG-MCMC), and finite-time rates that improve from O(K−2/3) (SGLD) to O(K−4/5) under optimal step schedules. The approach is generic and can be applied to other SG-MCMC schemes; a SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) is also demonstrated. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) show improved bias/MSE and better wall-clock RMSE at equal computation time via parallel execution of the two chains.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on SGLD and a body of SG-MCMC analysis (Teh et al., Chen et al.) showing first-order integrator limitations and improvements with higher-order integrators.
- Transposes classical RR extrapolation for SDE invariant measures (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup) to the SG-MCMC setting with stochastic gradients, establishing coupled-noise CLTs and finite-time bounds.
- Compared to high-order integrators (e.g., SGHMC with splitting), SGRRLD attains similar asymptotic accuracy using only first-order integrators, with a different computational profile (two coupled chains, favorable to parallel/distributed settings).

Strengths
- Novelty/significance: Bringing RR extrapolation into SG-MCMC is a simple, elegant, and impactful idea. The theoretical and empirical results suggest a clear pathway to reduce bias without resorting to more complex high-order integrators, and the method is naturally parallelizable.
- Theory: The CLT with optimized coupling, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and rate analysis are solid and informative. The identification of α = 1/5 as optimal for MSE rate O(K−4/5) is a useful prescription. The minimal-variance coupling (Σ = Id) is well-motivated and extends prior ULA results to the stochastic gradient setting.
- Generality: The technique applies beyond SGLD; the SGHMC case and discussion of extensions are convincing.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments verify predicted bias/MSE scalings. The matrix factorization results show practical wall-clock benefits when parallelism is available. The SGHMC comparison is particularly informative: SGRRHMC with RR can match high-order integrators’ accuracy with larger γ.

Weaknesses and questions
- Possible typo/ambiguity in update (Eq. (5)): The first fine step uses ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) rather than ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k). This looks like an implicit update; the rest of the paper and SGLD are explicit Euler. Please clarify/correct.
- Assumptions: Some are strong (solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C9, geometric ergodicity, smoothness of U and f). These are standard in theory but far from satisfied in many practical posteriors (e.g., deep models). A brief discussion on robustness when assumptions fail would help.
- Gradient noise coupling: The analysis focuses on coupling the Brownian increments; the two stochastic gradient sequences only share the same law L. In practice, further coupling (e.g., using identical minibatches across the two fine steps and the corresponding coarse step, or antithetic/correlated mini-batches) may reduce variance. Did you explore such couplings empirically? An ablation comparing independent vs. coupled mini-batches would strengthen the case.
- Fairness and compute budget: The claimed wall-clock parity hinges on parallelism. On a single worker (or when GPU is the bottleneck), SGRRLD doubles gradient computations per wall-clock unit. It would be useful to include a single-threaded comparison or, alternatively, SGLD with doubled iterations as a baseline to separate bias reduction from parallelization benefits.
- Constants and stability: The bounds hide constants depending on gradient noise moments. Are there regimes where these constants make SGRRLD unattractive? Any observed stability issues at large γ compared to SGLD?
- Scope of experiments: The synthetic example is 1D/low-d; the matrix factorization task is convincing but structured. Even a small nonlinear Bayesian benchmark (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural net on UCI) would bolster generality.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; theoretical contributions and intuition are communicated clearly.
- Please fix Eq. (5) if it is indeed a typo; also ensure consistent notation around indices in Eq. (6).
- It would help to include the pseudo-code in the main text or briefly summarize key implementation details (how Brownian correlation is realized; whether mini-batches are shared across levels).

Reproducibility
- The method is simple to implement atop SGLD/SGHMC.
- Important details (correlation construction, step-size schedules, batch sizes, seeds) appear to be provided, though some are deferred to the supplement. Public code would be beneficial.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and bold adaptation of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
- Improves theoretical rates using only first-order integrators; clear, provable gains.
- Natural parallelism and demonstrated practical benefits.
- Generic technique likely to spur follow-up work (multi-level extensions, other SG-MCMC variants).

Cons against acceptance
- Strong assumptions and some missing implementation details/ablations.
- Limited experimental breadth; parallelism-dependent wall-clock advantages.
- Minor clarity issues (potential typo) that need correction.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a fresh, impactful direction for SG-MCMC: a simple, parallelizable mechanism that reduces bias and improves finite-time rates without high-order integrators. Despite some assumptions and experimental limitations, the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to influence subsequent work.
