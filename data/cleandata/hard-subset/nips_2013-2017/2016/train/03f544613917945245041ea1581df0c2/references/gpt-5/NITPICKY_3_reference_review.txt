Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, specifically SGLD. Two discretizations of the Langevin SDE with step sizes γ and γ/2 are run in parallel to cancel O(γ) bias. The estimator uses a time-weighted combination that corresponds to 2πγ/2 − πγ, and the authors advocate coupling the Gaussian noises (Brownian increments) across the two chains to control variance. They prove a CLT for the RR estimator under decreasing step sizes, provide non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, and claim improved finite-time and asymptotic rates over first-order SG-MCMC: bias O(γ2) and MSE O(γ4) asymptotically, and with K steps the optimal MSE rate O(K−4/5) (vs O(K−2/3) for SGLD). Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization suggest empirical gains, and they also show an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) competitive with a higher-order integrator.

Quality
- Theory: The use of RR for SG-MCMC is sound in spirit, and the scaling arguments (optimal α = 1/5 for γk ∝ k−α giving O(K−2/5) asymptotic rate and O(K−4/5) finite-time MSE with tuned γ) are consistent with the derived conditions involving ΓK, Γ(3)K. The variance-minimization argument via correlated Brownian increments is consistent with multilevel methods.
- However, the mathematical assumptions appear unusually strong for SG-MCMC: the CLT proof sketch requires a Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9 and 7th-order expansions; the paper defers all conditions to the supplement. It is unclear whether common Bayesian models with non-smooth likelihoods or heavy-tailed posteriors satisfy the required conditions. The statement “Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” needs explicit assumptions on the gradient estimator variance scaling; otherwise, in practical subsampling regimes, gradient noise can dominate and may break the claimed variance behavior.
- A likely serious typo appears in Eq. (5): the first fine-step uses ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) instead of ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k), making the update implicit rather than Euler–Maruyama. This affects implementability and correctness.
- The coupling of stochastic gradients across levels is not addressed. For variance reduction in multilevel methods, one typically shares random numbers/minibatches across coarse/fine levels. The paper states only that minibatch sizes must match; it neither analyzes nor experiments with common minibatches or other couplings, missing an important lever.
- Experiments: Gains are shown, but they are limited in scope and tuning is favorable to the proposed method. The 1D Gaussian example is too simple; higher-dimensional posterior structure (e.g., correlated/multi-modal) and non-Gaussian models (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression) are absent. In the large-scale MF, only RMSE is reported; no posterior metrics, no sensitivity to step-size schedules, no comparison to higher-order SGLD variants [Ma et al. 2015; Chen et al. 2015] beyond a small SGHMC study. There is also no ablation on coupling choices (Σ, shared minibatches).

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are many typos and inconsistencies that hinder precision:
  - Eq. (5) likely wrong (implicit gradient). Please fix to ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k) in the first fine step.
  - Notational inconsistencies: γk+1 vs γk; θ(γ/2)2k+1 appearing on both sides; “variace”; “twice more time” should be “twice as many steps”; “RichardsonRomberg” missing hyphen; “th Conference...” leftover header line; inconsistent use of ∑K k=1 γk vs ∑K k=1 γk+1 in the normalizer of (6).
  - Reference formatting is inconsistent and contains errors (e.g., [21] has “(1991), 1990.”; missing years/pages in several entries; capitalization).
  - Many technical conditions and the pseudocode are missing from the main paper; at least the SGRRLD algorithm (with coupling details) should be included.
- The fairness of runtime comparisons is insufficiently discussed. SGRRLD uses two gradient computations per “coarse” unit time; matching wall-clock via two processes presumes parallel resources. The claim that SGLD “cannot benefit from this parallel computation architecture due to its inherently sequential nature” is misleading; per-iteration gradients and data parallelism are standard for SGLD.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with an analysis of bias/MSE and a CLT is, to my knowledge, novel. There is related work on RR for SDEs and multilevel Monte Carlo; bridging to the stochastic-gradient setting is interesting and nontrivial.
- However, the idea of coupling coarse/fine discretizations and using correlated Brownian increments is standard in multilevel literature; the specific estimator and analysis are an adaptation rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic framework.

Significance
- If the theoretical guarantees hold under realistic SG-MCMC conditions, the result is significant: improving rates using only first-order integrators via a simple, parallelizable modification is valuable.
- That said, the empirical evidence is not broad enough, and the practical overhead/benefit trade-off (especially without abundant parallel resources) is not convincingly quantified.

Pros
- Conceptually simple method; leverages parallelism.
- Theoretical bias/MSE bounds and CLT, with clear rate improvements under the stated model.
- Empirical results show promising gains on synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task.
- Extension to SGHMC suggests generality.

Cons
- Critical typo in the update equations (Eq. 5), and multiple clarity/formatting issues.
- Strong/opaque assumptions for theory; limited discussion on when they hold with stochastic gradients.
- No analysis or experiments on coupling of stochastic gradients across levels (missed variance reduction opportunity).
- Limited experimental diversity and baselines; fairness of runtime claims is questionable.
- Heavy reliance on supplemental material for essential algorithmic and theoretical details.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological/clarity issues and insufficient empirical validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix Eq. (5) and clean up all notation/typos; include full pseudocode with the exact coupling (Σ=Id) and any minibatch coupling used.
- State explicit, checkable assumptions on U, f, and the gradient estimator (moments, Lipschitzness, dissipativity), and discuss applicability to common Bayesian models.
- Analyze and test shared-minibatch coupling across levels (and across the two fine steps) to reduce variance; relate to multilevel Monte Carlo coupling strategies.
- Expand experiments: higher-dimensional posteriors, non-Gaussian models (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression), decreasing step-size schedules, sensitivity analyses, credible intervals, and more baselines (e.g., higher-order SGLD/SGHMC from [10]).
- Provide fair resource accounting: single-core vs multi-core; per-iteration cost; wall-clock under fixed hardware budgets; show whether gains persist when parallel resources are constrained.
- Improve and correct the references.
