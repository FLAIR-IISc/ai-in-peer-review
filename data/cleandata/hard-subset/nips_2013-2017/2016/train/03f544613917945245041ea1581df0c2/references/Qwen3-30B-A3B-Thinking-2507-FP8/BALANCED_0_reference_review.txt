

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The method runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combines their outputs via RR extrapolation. The authors theoretically analyze the method, showing it achieves bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD. They demonstrate the approach on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks, showing improved accuracy over SGLD.

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis. The authors establish a central limit theorem for the SGRRLD estimator and provide non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, showing clear improvements over SGLD. The proofs are well-structured and follow the established approach in [23] for ULA. The experimental results generally support the theoretical claims, particularly in the Gaussian model where the posterior is analytically available.

However, the paper could better address the computational trade-offs. While the authors note that SGRRLD maintains parallelizability, they don't explicitly compare the computational cost of SGRRLD to higher-order methods (like those in [10]) that achieve similar accuracy. It's unclear whether the two-chain approach with RR extrapolation requires significantly more computation than a single higher-order integrator, which would impact its practical utility.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate notation and logical flow. The theoretical contributions are explained thoroughly, and the connection to prior work (particularly [21], [23], and [10]) is well-motivated. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, some technical details in the convergence analysis (e.g., the exact conditions for the central limit theorem) are relegated to the supplement, which may make the paper less accessible to readers unfamiliar with MCMC theory.

## Originality
The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by showing that SGRRLD can achieve the same theoretical accuracy as higher-order integrator methods but with a first-order numerical integrator, while maintaining parallelizability. The authors properly acknowledge related work, particularly [10] and [23], and clearly explain how their method differs from these approaches.

## Significance
The proposed method addresses a meaningful limitation of existing SG-MCMC methods (bias reduction) while maintaining scalability. The theoretical improvement in convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) is significant, and the experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support this claim. The method's parallelizability is particularly valuable for large-scale applications, and the authors correctly note that the technique can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.

## Strengths
1. Clear theoretical contribution showing improved convergence rates over existing SG-MCMC methods.
2. Well-structured theoretical analysis with both asymptotic and non-asymptotic results.
3. Practical demonstration of the method on real-world matrix factorization tasks.
4. Maintains parallelizability, which is crucial for large-scale applications.
5. Properly contextualizes the work within the broader literature on SG-MCMC.

## Weaknesses
1. Limited discussion of computational trade-offs compared to higher-order methods.
2. Experiments are primarily on simple Gaussian models and matrix factorization; more complex applications (e.g., deep Bayesian models) would strengthen the significance claim.
3. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore potential limitations or failure cases of the method.
4. The connection between RR extrapolation and existing higher-order methods (from [10]) could be more explicitly addressed.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature with a novel application of RR extrapolation. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the experimental results generally support the claims. While the method shows promise for improving convergence rates in large-scale Bayesian inference, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational costs compared to higher-order methods and additional experiments on more complex models.

The paper is technically sound and addresses an important problem in scalable Bayesian inference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational trade-off discussion and to provide more comprehensive experimental validation.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a detailed comparison of computational costs between SGRRLD and higher-order methods (e.g., [10]).
2. Include experiments on a more complex Bayesian model (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) to demonstrate broader applicability.
3. Expand the discussion of potential limitations (e.g., when the potential function is not smooth).
4. Clarify the relationship between the RR approach and higher-order integrator methods in the introduction and conclusion.
5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method might perform with adaptive step sizes or in non-convex settings.
