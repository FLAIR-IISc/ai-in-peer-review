- Summary: Proposes SGRRLD, which runs two Langevin chains with different step sizes and combines them via Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias, claiming MSE O(K^-4/5) vs SGLD’s O(K^-2/3) and showing empirical gains.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on SGLD and SG-MCMC convergence analyses and adapts Talay & Tubaro’s RR extrapolation to stochastic-gradient SDEs, extensible beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC).

- Novelty: First adaptation of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic-gradient MCMC setting.

- Theoretical rigor: Provides CLT, bias and MSE bounds, and derives improved convergence rate O(K^-4/5).

- Practical adaptability: Algorithm is inherently parallel and the paper addresses correlated Brownian increments to control variance.

- Empirical validation: Synthetic and real-data experiments (including ablations) show consistent improvements over SGLD and some SGHMC comparisons.

- Clarity: Paper is well-written with clear presentation and helpful pseudo-code.

- Practical overhead: Running two chains doubles memory and practical costs; overhead analysis is insufficient.

- Step-size sensitivity: Performance is sensitive to step-size choice and requires careful tuning; more guidance is needed.

- Gradient correlation discussion: Treatment of correlated stochastic gradients is limited beyond using the same mini-batch.

- Limited comparisons: Comparison to SGHMC and higher-order integrators is brief and would benefit from broader benchmarks.

- Mini-batch size constraint: Requiring equal mini-batch sizes across chains may limit applicability under resource/data heterogeneity.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically strong, empirically validated, and well-suited to parallel compute environments.

- Arguments against acceptance: Unaddressed practical overhead, step-size tuning challenges, and limited comparisons to competing methods.

- Overall assessment: Strong contribution with significant novelty and rigor despite some practical and comparative limitations.

- Recommendation: Strong accept with minor revisions to address overhead, step-size guidance, expanded comparisons, and more discussion of gradient-correlation strategies.
