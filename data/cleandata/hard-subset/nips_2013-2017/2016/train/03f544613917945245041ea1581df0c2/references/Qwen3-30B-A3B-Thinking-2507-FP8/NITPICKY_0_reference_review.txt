

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a novel MCMC algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias. The authors claim SGRRLD achieves bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, with a convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE, improving upon SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).

## Major Concerns

### 1. Technical Inconsistencies and Undefined Terms
The paper contains multiple critical technical inconsistencies that undermine its theoretical claims:

- The paper claims "the asymptotic bias and variance of SGRRLD can be bounded as O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively," but Theorem 2 states the bias bound is O(Γ(3)K/ΓK), which is not equivalent to O(γ²). The paper fails to explain this disconnect.

- Γ(3)K is never defined in the main text (only Γ(1)K = ∑γk+1 is defined). In Theorem 2, the bound for bias is written as O(Γ(3)K/ΓK), but Γ(3)K is undefined, making the bound meaningless.

- The paper states "the optimal choice for the exponent α for obtaining the fastest convergence turns out to be α = 1/5, which implies a rate of convergence of order O(K⁻²/⁵)" but then claims "the optimal choice of the step size is γ = O(K⁻¹/⁵), leading to a bound of order O(K⁻⁴/⁵)." These are inconsistent and contradict each other.

- The notation for the two chains in Section 3 is confusing and inconsistent. The paper states "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice more time than the other one" but the mathematical formulation (equations 4-5) doesn't clearly explain how this time horizon matches.

### 2. Critical Omissions in Mathematical Formulation
- The key claim about correlated Brownian increments (equation 7) is cited as [23], but the paper doesn't provide a proof or clear justification for why this correlation minimizes variance. The reference [23] is a paper on invariant measures, not specifically about variance control in this context.

- The paper states "the mini-batch size has to be the same for the two chains" but provides no justification for this requirement. Why would different mini-batch sizes introduce bias of the same order as vanilla SGLD?

- The paper claims that "if the two sequences (∇Ũ(γ/2)k)k≥1 and (∇Ũ(γ)k)k≥1 do not have the same common distribution, then the SGRRLD estimator can have a bias, which would have the same order as of vanilla SGLD." This claim lacks justification and is not explained.

### 3. Formatting and Typographical Errors
- The paper inconsistently writes "RichardsonRomberg" (missing hyphen) in the title and "Richardson-Romberg" later in the text.
- The term "SGRRLD" is sometimes written as "SGRRLD" and sometimes as "SGRRLD" (inconsistent capitalization).
- In equation (6), the summation notation is incorrect: "K+1∑k=2 γk" should be Σ_{k=2}^{K+1} γ_k.
- The reference list contains formatting errors (e.g., "Bayesian learning via Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics, M. Welling, Y. W Teh, ICML, 2011, pp. 681–688., , " with two commas at the end).

### 4. Experimental Details
- The paper states "we run SGLD for K = 21000 iterations where we discard the first 1000 samples as burn-in, and we run SGRRLD for K = 10500 iterations accordingly, where we discard the samples generated in the first 500 iterations as burn-in." This is inconsistent because SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel, so it should be doing more work per iteration than SGLD. The paper fails to justify why they're using half as many iterations for SGRRLD to match wall-clock time.

- For the matrix factorization experiment, the paper uses different step sizes (γ = 2 × 10⁻⁶ for ML-1M, γ = 2 × 10⁻⁵ for ML-10M and ML-20M) but provides no explanation for these choices, which could significantly impact the results.

## Minor Strengths

The paper has a clear motivation for reducing bias in SG-MCMC methods, and the idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel. The experiments on synthetic and real data generally support the theoretical claims, though the experimental details are insufficient.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting idea that could potentially improve SG-MCMC methods, it suffers from multiple serious technical inconsistencies that undermine its theoretical claims. The undefined terms, inconsistent notation, and lack of justification for key claims make it difficult to evaluate the validity of the results. The paper is not technically sound as written, and the inconsistencies are substantial enough to prevent the results from being credible.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address the technical inconsistencies, clearly define all terms, provide justification for key claims, and fix the numerous formatting errors. As it stands, the paper fails to provide a complete and rigorous theoretical foundation for the proposed method, which is a critical requirement for NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial technical flaws that prevent the paper from being properly evaluated. The paper's main contribution is interesting, but the execution is flawed to the point where it cannot be considered a complete piece of work.
