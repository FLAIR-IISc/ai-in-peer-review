This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a modification of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) aimed at reducing bias through Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combine their results to achieve a higher-order convergence rate. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved bias and MSE bounds, alongside experimental validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the SGRRLD algorithm. The motivation – addressing the bias inherent in first-order SGLD methods – is sound. The theoretical analysis, while detailed (and largely relegated to the supplementary material, which is appropriate), attempts to rigorously justify the improved convergence rates. The experiments, employing both Gaussian models and matrix factorization, provide some evidence supporting the claims.

However, despite these strengths, I have significant reservations about accepting this paper. My primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution. While the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, its application to SG-MCMC, while not entirely trivial, feels like a relatively straightforward extension. The theoretical gains—demonstrating an improvement from O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) to O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) convergence for the MSE—are modest, and the practical impact, as shown in the experiments, doesn’t appear overwhelmingly significant. The experiments, while reasonably thorough, don’t convincingly demonstrate a transformative improvement over existing methods like SGLD or SGHMC, particularly given the increased computational cost (running two chains). The authors mention running the chains in parallel, but the overhead of managing two chains, even in parallel, is not explicitly addressed, and the discussion of computational complexity is lacking. 

Furthermore, the experimental setup feels somewhat limited. The parameter settings (e.g., step sizes) seem tuned for specific datasets, and a broader exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial. The claim that SGRRHMC achieves performance comparable to SGHMC-s (using a higher-order integrator) *with smaller step sizes* is interesting, but the relative cost of the reduced step size isn’t discussed. The focus on demonstrating improved accuracy with comparable wall-clock time is good, but the scaling behavior with problem size isn't sufficiently explored. The connection to existing work is adequate, but could benefit from a more critical discussion of the trade-offs compared to other variance reduction techniques used in SG-MCMC.

A major weakness is the reliance on strong assumptions (e.g., correlation structure in the Brownian motions) to achieve the theoretical guarantees. While the authors attempt to justify these assumptions, their practicality in more complex models isn't fully explored. The conditions required for applicability (“virtually applied to all current SG-MCMC algorithms…provided that they satisfy rather technical weak error and ergodicity conditions”) are vague and potentially limit the algorithm’s broad utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides a rigorous justification for the proposed method.
*   The experiments show consistent, though modest, improvements over SGLD.
*   The paper presents a novel application of a well-established numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg) to SG-MCMC.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The incremental nature of the contribution; the improvement in convergence rate is modest.
*   The increased computational cost (running two chains) isn’t adequately justified.
*   The experiments lack a comprehensive hyperparameter search and scaling analysis.
*   The strong assumptions required for theoretical guarantees may limit practical applicability.
*   The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from existing variance reduction techniques in SG-MCMC.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper is technically sound and presents a valid extension to SGLD, the incremental nature of the contribution, coupled with the increased computational cost and the strong assumptions required, do not warrant acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels more suited for a workshop or a more specialized conference focused on numerical methods for Bayesian inference. A major revision, including more extensive experiments, a detailed complexity analysis, and a stronger justification for the practical relevance of the assumptions, would be needed for reconsideration.
