This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC algorithm aiming to reduce bias compared to SGLD by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two Langevin dynamics chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine their results to achieve a higher order accuracy. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates (MSE of O(K−4/5) for SGRRLD vs. O(K−2/3) for SGLD) and validate their approach through experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution. While RR extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to stochastic gradient settings, especially within the MCMC framework, represents a meaningful extension.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper includes a substantial theoretical analysis, providing convergence guarantees and bounding the bias and MSE of the proposed algorithm. The derivation of the O(K−4/5) MSE convergence rate is a significant result.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data support the theoretical claims. The performance gains demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiment, particularly the reduced wall-clock time to achieve comparable accuracy, are compelling.
* **Generality:** The authors highlight the potential for applying the RR extrapolation technique to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC), increasing the impact of their work.
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelism of the two chains in SGRRLD is explicitly noted and leveraged in the experiments, making the algorithm well-suited for modern computing architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity - Technical Details:** The presentation of the theoretical analysis is dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with stochastic differential equations and numerical analysis. More intuitive explanations of key steps and assumptions would improve accessibility. Equations (7) and (8) introducing the correlated Brownian increments, while mathematically correct, lack sufficient justification regarding practical implementation. How is the matrix Σ determined or estimated?
* **Clarity - Notation:** The use of multiple subscripts (e.g., γk, η2k−1) can make the equations difficult to follow. A more consistent and descriptive notation could enhance readability.
* **Related Work - Limited Discussion of RR in SDE Context:** While the authors cite relevant work on RR extrapolation, they could benefit from a more detailed discussion of existing research applying RR (or similar variance reduction techniques) specifically to SDEs *before* this work. A deeper contextualization within the existing literature would strengthen the paper.
* **Experimental Details - Mini-Batch Size:** The choice of the mini-batch size (B) and its relation to the dataset size (N) is stated, but the rationale for these specific choices isn’t provided. A sensitivity analysis or justification for these parameters would be valuable.
* **Experimental Evaluation - Comparison to Other Variance Reduction Techniques:** The paper primarily compares against SGLD and SGHMC. Including a comparison against other variance reduction techniques commonly used in SG-MCMC (e.g., SAGA, SVRG) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of SGRRLD's performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the convergence rate of the proposed algorithm.
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The inherent parallelizability of the algorithm makes it attractive for large-scale applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The technical density of the theoretical analysis may limit accessibility for some readers.
* The lack of detailed justification for certain implementation choices (e.g., mini-batch size, correlation matrix Σ) and a more thorough comparison with existing variance reduction techniques weaken the overall impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach for improving the accuracy and efficiency of Bayesian inference in large-scale settings. While the paper has some weaknesses related to clarity and completeness of experimental details, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and experimental validation strongly support its acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper, with a request for minor revisions to address the clarity concerns and provide more detailed explanations of implementation choices.
