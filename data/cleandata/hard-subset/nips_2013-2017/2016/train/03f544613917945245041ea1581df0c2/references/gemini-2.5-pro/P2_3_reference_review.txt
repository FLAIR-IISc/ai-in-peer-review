### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the bias inherent in first-order Euler-Maruyama integrators, which are commonly used in algorithms like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed method achieves this by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size `γ` and another taking two steps of size `γ/2` for each step of the first chain. The final estimator is a linear combination of the outputs of these two chains. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD achieves a mean-squared-error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. The paper also demonstrates the practical benefits of this approach through experiments on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, showing improved accuracy and faster convergence in terms of wall-clock time.

### Review

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method is an elegant and effective way to improve the accuracy of SG-MCMC methods.

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical contributions are the main strength of the paper. The authors provide both a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derivation of an improved MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a significant result. This demonstrates a fundamental improvement over existing first-order methods. The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step size decay rate (`α=1/5`) to balance the bias and variance terms, showcasing a deep understanding of the underlying stochastic processes. The discussion on using correlated noise to control the variance of the RR estimator is crucial and builds correctly on prior work in the SDE literature.

*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    1.  The use of a linear Gaussian model with an analytical posterior is an excellent choice for validating the theoretical claims. The experiments clearly demonstrate the predicted reduction in bias and MSE compared to SGLD (Figures 1 & 2).
    2.  The experiment that varies the step size decay exponent `α` to empirically find the optimal rate (Figure 3) provides strong evidence for the correctness of the theoretical analysis. This is a very well-designed experiment.
    3.  The application of the RR technique to SGHMC demonstrates the generality of the proposed framework, showing it is not just a specific trick for SGLD.
    4.  The large-scale matrix factorization experiment on MovieLens datasets confirms the practical utility of the method. The comparison using wall-clock time is the correct way to evaluate the method's efficiency, as it directly addresses the trade-off of running two chains versus one. The results convincingly show that SGRRLD can achieve better accuracy in less time due to its parallel nature and faster convergence.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   The structure is logical, starting with a clear motivation and background, followed by a precise description of the algorithm, its theoretical properties, and finally, empirical validation.
*   The core idea of applying RR extrapolation is explained intuitively before the formal algorithm is presented. The equations defining the two chains and the final estimator (Eq. 4-6) are unambiguous.
*   The connection to prior work on RR for SDEs and the specific challenges and contributions in the stochastic gradient setting are clearly articulated.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   While both SG-MCMC and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation are known techniques, their combination in this context is novel and insightful. Previous work on improving SG-MCMC accuracy has largely focused on developing higher-order integrators (e.g., [10]), which can be complex to derive and implement.
*   This paper presents a conceptually different and elegant alternative: using two simple first-order integrators in parallel to achieve the accuracy of a higher-order method. This is a clever and powerful idea.
*   The analysis extends existing results for RR on SDEs with exact gradients [23] to the more challenging SG-MCMC setting, where noise arises from both the Brownian motion and the data subsampling.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   It addresses a fundamental limitation of a very popular class of algorithms (SG-MCMC): the bias introduced by the numerical integrator.
*   The proposed method offers a practical "black-box" way to improve almost any existing first-order SG-MCMC algorithm. The demonstration on SGHMC supports this claim. Researchers and practitioners can readily apply this technique to their existing samplers to gain accuracy.
*   The algorithm is perfectly suited for modern parallel computing architectures. As single-core performance has plateaued, methods that can effectively leverage parallelism are increasingly important. SGRRLD provides a clear path to improved performance on multi-core CPUs or distributed systems.
*   The improved convergence rate is not just a theoretical curiosity; the experiments show it translates to real-world gains, either achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the time or providing significantly better results for the same computational budget.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Novelty:** Proposes a new and clever combination of existing ideas (SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation) to solve an important problem.
*   **Strong Theory:** Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with a significantly improved convergence rate for MSE.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough, validating the theory on synthetic data and demonstrating practical utility on a large-scale real-world task.
*   **Practicality and Generality:** The method is simple to implement, inherently parallel, and applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   The computational cost per "effective iteration" is roughly doubled since two chains are run. While the paper effectively argues that parallelization offsets this in terms of wall-clock time, it does require twice the computational resources. This trade-off could have been discussed slightly more explicitly, but the wall-clock time experiments make the benefits clear. This is a minor point and does not detract from the paper's overall quality.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is an excellent paper that should be accepted. It presents a clear, novel, and significant contribution with strong theoretical backing and compelling empirical results. The work is likely to be influential and widely adopted by the Bayesian machine learning community.
