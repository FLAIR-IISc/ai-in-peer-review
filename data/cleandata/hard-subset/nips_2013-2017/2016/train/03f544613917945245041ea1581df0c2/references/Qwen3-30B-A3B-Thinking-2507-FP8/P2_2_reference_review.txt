

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods like SGLD. The key insight is to run two chains with different step sizes in parallel, then combine their results through RR extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors theoretically show that SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²) and MSE of O(γ⁴), leading to an overall convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is better than SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The paper demonstrates the approach on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks, showing significant improvements in accuracy.

The paper builds on previous work on SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), but makes the novel connection between these two fields for scalable Bayesian inference.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors provide rigorous convergence analysis with theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the improved rates of SGRRLD compared to SGLD. The proofs are sufficiently detailed in the supplementary material, and the authors carefully discuss the technical conditions required for their results (e.g., smoothness of the potential function, properties of the gradient estimators).

The experimental section is thorough, with both synthetic experiments on a Gaussian model (showing the bias reduction) and real-world experiments on large-scale matrix factorization (demonstrating practical benefits). The experiments are well-designed to test the theoretical claims, including parameter sensitivity studies and comparisons to baseline methods.

A minor weakness is that the paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison with other bias-reduction techniques in SG-MCMC (e.g., higher-order integrators like those in Chen et al., 2015). While the authors claim that SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of higher-order methods, they don't directly compare against specific higher-order methods beyond SGHMC-s in Figure 4. This would strengthen the argument for the relative significance of their contribution.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and motivation, and the theoretical development follows a logical progression. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the key concepts (like the RR extrapolation applied to SG-MCMC) are explained clearly.

The paper could be slightly improved by summarizing the technical conditions required for the theorems in the main text (rather than only in the supplementary material), which would help readers quickly grasp the scope of the results. However, the paper is generally well-written and accessible to readers familiar with MCMC and stochastic optimization.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC. While RR extrapolation has been used for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), the authors are the first to apply it to SG-MCMC, which is a novel and important connection. The key insight that "by only using a first-order numerical integrator, the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is particularly noteworthy.

The paper also demonstrates the generality of the approach by applying it to SGHMC (creating SGRRHMC), showing that the technique is not limited to SGLD. This generality adds to the originality and potential impact of the work.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian machine learning: scaling MCMC to large datasets while maintaining accuracy. The improved convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)) is meaningful for practical applications, especially in large-scale settings where computational efficiency is crucial.

The experimental results on matrix factorization demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves better accuracy than SGLD in the same wall-clock time, which is a significant practical contribution. The paper also highlights that SGRRLD is well-suited for parallel and distributed architectures, which aligns with the growing need for scalable Bayesian inference.

The paper advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by providing a method that achieves higher accuracy with first-order integrators, potentially avoiding the complexity of higher-order methods while still achieving comparable performance.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. Strong theoretical analysis with clear convergence rate improvements.
3. Practical demonstration of the approach on both synthetic and real datasets.
4. Demonstrated parallelizability, which is important for large-scale applications.
5. Generality of the technique (applicable to other SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD).

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited comparison with other bias-reduction techniques in SG-MCMC (beyond higher-order integrators).
2. Technical conditions for the theorems are not fully summarized in the main paper.
3. The paper doesn't fully discuss the communication overhead in distributed settings, which could be a practical concern.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel solution that advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC.

The main weakness is the limited comparison with other bias-reduction techniques, but this doesn't significantly detract from the paper's overall contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear advancement over previous work.
