- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two Langevin chains with different step sizes and combining outputs to reduce bias.  
- Theoretical result: shows improved MSE convergence rate O(K^-4/5) compared to SGLD’s O(K^-2/3).  
- Claims SGRRLD achieves accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators while retaining first-order parallelizability.  
- Builds on SG-MCMC literature (SGLD, SGHMC) and novelly brings RR extrapolation from numerical analysis to SG-MCMC.  
- Properly cites and differentiates from related higher-order integrator methods.  
- Novelty: applying RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC is a genuinely new idea with clear potential.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides detailed convergence analysis, bias and variance bounds, and solid derivations.  
- Practicality: algorithm is well-suited to parallel architectures and includes helpful pseudo-code.  
- Empirical validation: experiments on synthetic and matrix factorization datasets support the theoretical claims and show improvements over SGLD/SGHMC.  
- Comparison with higher-order SGHMC is insightful and supports the method’s competitiveness.  
- Generality: approach could be extended to other SG-MCMC algorithms.  
- Writing: paper is generally clear and well organized despite theoretical complexity.  
- Weakness — step-size sensitivity: experiments reveal practical sensitivity and the paper needs more guidance on choosing step sizes.  
- Weakness — gradient estimator correlation: assuming equal mini-batch sizes is restrictive; the impact of different correlation structures is underexplored.  
- Weakness — computational overhead: running two chains doubles per-iteration cost and the paper lacks a detailed trade-off or practical speedup analysis.  
- Weakness — limited experiments: empirical scope is narrow; more datasets and models would strengthen validation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically supported, and potentially impactful for large-scale Bayesian inference.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical issues with step-size tuning, increased computational cost, and limited experimental validation.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with valuable contribution; minor weaknesses do not outweigh its strengths.  
- Rating: Strong Accept (8/10).  
- Recommendations: expand discussion on step-size selection (heuristics/adaptive methods), analyze cost vs. convergence trade-offs, explore different gradient correlation structures, and add more experiments.
