This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) aiming to reduce bias via Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with differing step sizes and combine their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis bounding the bias and variance, claiming improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and other first-order methods. Empirical validation is presented on a Gaussian model and a matrix factorization task.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the SGRRLD algorithm. The mathematical derivations appear reasonably sound, building on established results in numerical analysis and stochastic gradient MCMC. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is clearly explained, and the extension to SGHMC is a welcome addition demonstrating the method’s generality. However, despite the technical correctness, I find myself unconvinced of the practical significance of this work, and harbor concerns about the thoroughness of the experimental validation.

My primary concern lies with the incremental nature of the contribution. While reducing bias is always desirable, the complexity added by running two chains and carefully correlating Brownian increments (equation 8) seems disproportionate to the claimed benefits. The theoretical improvement in convergence rate (O(K^-4/5) vs O(K^-2/3)) is asymptotic and may not translate to substantial gains in practical settings, particularly given the overhead of the doubled computation. The paper does not adequately address this trade-off.

The experimental evaluation, while present, is not convincing enough to alleviate these concerns. The synthetic Gaussian model experiment, while demonstrating bias reduction, lacks detailed sensitivity analysis. The impact of step size selection and the correlation parameter Σ are not thoroughly investigated. The matrix factorization experiments are more compelling, but the reported speedups are modest and could potentially be attributed to better parallelization rather than algorithmic efficiency. Furthermore, the comparison to SGHMC-s, a higher-order method, feels somewhat unfair—the authors highlight SGRRLD achieving comparable accuracy, but fail to acknowledge the computational cost difference between a first-order method with extrapolation and a true higher-order method.  The authors state that SGRRLD can achieve the *theoretical* accuracy of higher-order methods, but this does not necessarily translate into equivalent *practical* performance.

The paper also relies heavily on theoretical results without fully exploring their practical implications. For instance, the optimality of Σ = Id is stated, but the consequences of deviating from this ideal setting are not discussed. The impact of mini-batch size on the correlation of gradient estimators, crucial for the theoretical guarantees, is briefly mentioned but not experimentally assessed.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear presentation of the algorithm and theoretical analysis.
*   Demonstrated generality by applying the technique to both SGLD and SGHMC.
*   Theoretical justification for improved convergence rates.
*   Parallelizable implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental contribution with a potentially high computational overhead.
*   Insufficient experimental validation, lacking detailed sensitivity analysis and comparisons to optimized baselines.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications of theoretical results.
*   Comparison to SGHMC-s is not entirely fair, focusing on accuracy without considering computational cost.
*   Dependence on correlated Brownian increments adds complexity without clear practical benefits.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper is technically sound and presents a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGMCMC.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of SGRRLD.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical significance of the contribution is questionable, given the added complexity and modest reported gains.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of SGRRLD over existing methods.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy.




**Overall:** I lean towards rejection. While the paper is technically correct, the contribution feels incremental and the lack of compelling experimental evidence raises doubts about its practical value. The authors need to provide more convincing evidence that the added complexity of SGRRLD justifies its benefits in real-world applications. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including sensitivity analysis and comparisons to optimized baselines, is essential.
