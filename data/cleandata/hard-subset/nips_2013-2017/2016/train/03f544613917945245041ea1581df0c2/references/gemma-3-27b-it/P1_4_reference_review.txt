## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm. The core idea is to accelerate the convergence of standard SGLD by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, which involves running two Langevin dynamics chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their outputs. The authors theoretically demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves improved convergence rates – O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for the mean squared error compared to O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD – and provide conditions for controlling variance through correlated Brownian motions.  They validate their approach through experiments on both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC extrapolation) and a real-world large-scale matrix factorization problem, showing improved accuracy and efficiency.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper builds directly upon the increasingly popular line of work in SG-MCMC, starting with Welling & Teh (2011) and branching into numerous extensions aiming to scale Bayesian inference. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, referencing key contributions like SGLD, SGHMC, and more recent advancements focusing on higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015). The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SDEs, rooted in the work of Talay and Tubaro (1990), is a clever adaptation not previously explored in the context of SG-MCMC.  The connection to multilevel Monte Carlo (Lemaire & Pages, 2014) is also mentioned as a potential future direction.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. It represents a creative attempt to improve convergence without resorting to more complex, higher-order integrators.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough theoretical analysis, including convergence rate analysis, central limit theorems, and discussion of bias and variance control.  The proofs are deferred to supplementary material, which is standard practice.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets are well-designed and support the theoretical claims. The results consistently demonstrate the improved performance of SGRRLD compared to SGLD and SGHMC, especially on larger datasets. The matrix factorization experiment convincingly shows the practical benefit of the proposed algorithm.
* **Parallelizability:**  The algorithm’s inherent parallel nature (running two chains independently) is a significant advantage, particularly for large-scale applications. This is explicitly highlighted and leveraged in the experiments.
* **Generality:** The authors point out the potential to apply the RR extrapolation technique to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, demonstrating the broader applicability of their approach.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is, for the most part, well-written and clearly organized. The notation is mostly consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While theoretically sound, the implementation and tuning of SGRRLD appear more complex than vanilla SGLD. The need to manage two chains with correlated Brownian motions and carefully choose step sizes adds overhead.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations and sensitivity to hyperparameter choices.
* **Practical Improvement Magnitude:** While statistically significant, the practical speedup demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiment (e.g., ~2x faster) might not be dramatic enough to warrant the added complexity for all applications. A more in-depth analysis of the computational cost of the correlation between Brownian motions would be valuable.
* **Correlation Implementation:** The implementation details of correlating the Brownian motions (Equation 8) aren't fully fleshed out. A discussion of the computational cost and numerical stability of implementing this correlation would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to SGHMC-s (higher-order SGHMC) is relatively brief. A more thorough comparison, exploring different parameter settings and problem sizes, would strengthen the argument that SGRRLD can achieve comparable accuracy with a simpler first-order integrator.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically well-supported algorithm for improving SG-MCMC convergence.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
* The potential for parallelization is a significant advantage.
* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in Bayesian machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The added complexity of the algorithm may limit its widespread adoption.
* The practical speedup may not be substantial enough for all applications.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of implementation details and practical considerations.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While the added complexity is a concern, the novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical analysis, and the convincing empirical results outweigh the drawbacks.  The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more detail on implementation considerations, the computational cost of correlation, and a more comprehensive comparison to higher-order SGHMC variants.



**Confidence:** 8/10
