

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes SGRRLD, an extension of SGLD that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The theoretical analysis claims improved convergence rates (O(K^{-4/5}) vs. SGLD's O(K^{-2/3})), with experimental validation on synthetic and real datasets. While the idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is interesting and the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, I identify several significant technical inconsistencies and formatting issues that undermine the paper's credibility.

The most severe issue is a fundamental contradiction between the paper's claims and its technical implementation. The authors claim "the two chains required by the RR extrapolation can be generated independently" (Section 1), but then require them to be correlated via equations (7) and (8). Specifically, equation (7) states Z^(γ)_k = (Z^(γ/2)_{2k-1} + Z^(γ/2)_{2k})/√2, while equation (8) (which is supposed to justify this choice) gives Z^(γ)_{k+1} = Σ^>(Z^(γ/2)_{2k+1} + Z^(γ/2)_{2(k+1)})/√2 + ..., which implies a different indexing relationship. This inconsistency in the Brownian motion correlation is not resolved in the paper and directly contradicts the "independently generated" claim.

There are also serious mathematical inconsistencies in the theoretical results. In Theorem 1, the notation Γ^(n)_K = ∑_{k=1}^K γ_{k+1}^n is inconsistent with standard mathematical notation (Γ^(n) would typically denote the gamma function, not a sum of powers). More critically, in Theorem 2, the bias bound |E[π̂RK(f) - π(f)]| ≤ (C/ΓK){Γ^(3)_K + 1} is mathematically problematic because it implies the bias could exceed 1 for small ΓK, which is impossible for a well-behaved estimator of a probability distribution.

The paper contains several formatting and notational inconsistencies:
1. The title uses "Richardson-Romberg" but the text alternates between "Richardson-Romberg" and "Richardson-Romberg" without consistency.
2. The reference to "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" contains a clear typo ("th" instead of "The").
3. Equation (6) has inconsistent summation notation: it writes "K+1∑k=2 γk" instead of the standard "∑_{k=2}^{K+1} γ_k".
4. The paper cites "Multi-step Richardson-Romberg extrapolation" [13] but the reference is actually to "Multilevel Richardson-Romberg extrapolation" (Lemaire and Pages, 2014), creating confusion about the cited work.

In the experiments, the paper claims "SGRRLD requires only ≈ 200 seconds for achieving the accuracy that SGLD provides after ≈ 400 seconds" (Section 5.2). However, they ran SGRRLD for K=5250 iterations (with two parallel chains) versus SGLD for K=10500 iterations. If the two chains in SGRRLD run in parallel, the wall-clock time should be approximately half of SGLD's time for the same number of iterations, which is exactly what they observed. The claim that SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy in half the time is not a meaningful performance gain but rather a consequence of their implementation choice, not an inherent property of the algorithm.

The paper also fails to explain why step sizes differ across datasets (γ = 2×10^{-6} for ML-1M vs. 2×10^{-5} for ML-10M/ML-20M) despite using the same algorithm. This lack of explanation undermines reproducibility.

While the paper makes a theoretically interesting proposal and provides some experimental validation, the technical inconsistencies and formatting errors are too severe to overlook. The contradictions in the methodology, particularly regarding the correlation of the two chains, significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The minor technical flaws in the theoretical analysis (notably the problematic bias bound in Theorem 2) further erode confidence in the results.

Given the paper's focus on precision and theoretical rigor, these issues are particularly concerning. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the paper to resolve these inconsistencies before it could be considered for publication.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*Strengths:*
- Interesting application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Theoretical analysis provides improved convergence rates compared to SGLD
- Experiments support the theoretical claims on both synthetic and real datasets

*Weaknesses:*
- Fundamental contradiction between claims of independent chains and required correlation
- Mathematical inconsistencies in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2
- Serious notational and formatting errors throughout
- Unexplained differences in step sizes across datasets
- The claimed performance gain is not a meaningful improvement but a consequence of parallel implementation

**Recommendation: Reject** - The technical inconsistencies in the methodology and theoretical analysis are too severe to overlook, particularly for a paper that claims to provide rigorous theoretical guarantees. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to resolve these issues before reconsideration.
