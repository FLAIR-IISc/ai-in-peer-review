- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains with different step sizes and combining results to reduce bias.  
- Theoretical guarantees: asymptotic bias O(γ²), variance O(γ⁴), yielding MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵), improving over SGLD’s O(K⁻²/³).  
- Achieves higher-order integrator accuracy using only a first-order integrator and is well-suited to parallel architectures.  
- Builds on prior SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation literature and clearly positions the contribution relative to prior work.  
- Proofs and theoretical analysis are rigorous, with sufficient sketches and full proofs in supplementary material.  
- Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models confirm predicted bias and MSE improvements.  
- Real-world matrix factorization experiments (MovieLens) show practical RMSE improvements and roughly halved runtime to match SGLD accuracy.  
- Experimental setup is well-described, reproducible, and uses appropriate baselines (SGLD, SGHMC) and metrics (RMSE, bias, MSE).  
- Authors acknowledge limitations: for very small γ the 1/(Kγ) term may dominate and same mini-batch size is required for both chains.  
- Weakness: limited discussion on how restrictive technical conditions on U and L are in practice.  
- Weakness: experiments only cover SGLD and SGHMC despite claims of applicability to all SG-MCMC algorithms.  
- Minor omission: lack of discussion on distributed implementation details (memory and communication overhead for parallel chains).  
- Paper clarity: exceptionally clear, well-organized, with effective figures and sufficient experimental detail.  
- Originality: novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC (technique not new, but application is).  
- Significance: addresses scalable Bayesian inference, offers substantial practical and theoretical improvements, and is highly relevant to modern distributed computing.  
- Recommendation and scores: strongly recommend acceptance (NIPS); Quality 9/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 9/10.
