This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the estimates produced by the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order bias term. To manage the variance of the combined estimator, the method employs correlated Brownian motions for the two chains. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean-Squared-Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is a novel and clever contribution. It elegantly addresses the well-known issue of discretization bias in Langevin-based samplers by leveraging a powerful, well-established technique from numerical analysis.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method. The derivation of a Central Limit Theorem and, more importantly, the non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE are significant contributions. The improved convergence rates are a clear theoretical advantage over existing first-order SG-MCMC methods.
3.  **Generality:** The authors demonstrate that the RR extrapolation principle is not limited to SGLD by also applying it to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC). This suggests the proposed framework could be a general-purpose "wrapper" for improving a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.
4.  **Promising Empirical Performance:** The experimental results, particularly on the large-scale matrix factorization task, show a clear performance improvement in terms of RMSE versus wall-clock time compared to SGLD, supporting the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor to moderate flaws in its presentation, methodology, and experimental evaluation that, in aggregate, undermine the work's quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Clarity and Correctness of Algorithm Definition:** The presentation of the core algorithm contains a critical error. In Equation (5), the update for the second chain is given as:
    `θ(γ/2)_(2k+1) = θ(γ/2)_(2k) - (γ_(k+1)/2) * ∇Ũ(γ/2)_(2k+1)(θ(γ/2)_(2k+1)) + ...`
    The gradient is evaluated at the *next* state, `θ(γ/2)_(2k+1)`, which defines an implicit numerical scheme. This is fundamentally different from the explicit Euler-Maruyama scheme used in SGLD and described elsewhere in the paper. This is almost certainly a typo and should be `...∇Ũ(γ/2)_(2k+1)(θ(γ/2)_(2k))`. However, such a significant error in the definition of the proposed method is a major concern. There are also several other minor typos, such as "variace" (Section 3) and "mean squarederror" (Section 6).

2.  **Incomplete Theoretical Justification:** The paper repeatedly defers crucial information to the supplementary material. The technical conditions and assumptions on the potential `U`, the test function `f`, and the gradient noise distribution `L` are not stated in the main paper. Without these, it is impossible for a reviewer to assess the validity and generality of the theoretical claims in Theorems 1 and 2. A self-contained paper should at least summarize these key assumptions. Similarly, the explicit forms of the asymptotic variance `σ²_R` and bias `μ_R` are omitted.

3.  **Flawed Experimental Comparison:** The claims regarding computational cost are not rigorously justified. The SGRRLD method performs three gradient evaluations per effective iteration (one for the γ-chain, two for the γ/2-chain). In the experiments, SGRRLD is run for half the number of iterations as SGLD (e.g., 5250 vs 10500 in Section 5.2). This means SGRRLD performs `5250 * 3 = 15750` gradient evaluations, while SGLD performs `10500`. This is a 50% increase in total computation. The authors argue that wall-clock time is equivalent due to parallelization on a quad-core CPU. However, this claim is not substantiated. The two steps for the γ/2-chain are sequential, so perfect 3x parallelization is not possible. A more detailed breakdown of computation time is required to make this comparison credible. A fairer comparison would be against an SGLD baseline run with an equal budget of gradient evaluations (e.g., 15750 steps). Without this, the practical efficiency advantage of SGRRLD is questionable.

4.  **Formatting and Presentation:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness in formatting. The reference list is inconsistent: some entries include page numbers while others do not; the formatting of conference vs. journal papers varies; Reference [21] has a confusing year entry "(1991), 1990"; Reference [27] has a stray number "9" at the end. The footnote on the first page mentioning the conference location is also non-standard. While minor, these numerous inconsistencies detract from the overall professional quality of the manuscript.

5.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper claims that the correlation of stochastic gradients does not have an important impact on the asymptotic convergence. This seems counter-intuitive. Using the same mini-batch of data for the corresponding steps of the two chains would seem to be a variance reduction technique, analogous to using correlated Brownian motions. The lack of analysis or even empirical investigation of this aspect is a missed opportunity.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

While the paper introduces an interesting and theoretically well-motivated idea, the execution is flawed. The critical typo in the algorithm's definition, the questionable claims about computational efficiency in the experiments, the heavy and inappropriate reliance on supplementary material for core assumptions, and the general lack of polish in the presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address these significant issues. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
