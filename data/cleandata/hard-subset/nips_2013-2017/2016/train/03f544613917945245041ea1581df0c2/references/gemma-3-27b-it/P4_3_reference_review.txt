This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) designed to reduce bias. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, running two Langevin chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their outputs. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and demonstrate empirical improvements on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, proposed method, theoretical analysis, and experimental results. It builds upon the recent advancements in SG-MCMC, specifically acknowledging the work of Welling and Teh (2011) and Chen et al. (2015), and appropriately cites relevant literature. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of existing SG-MCMC methods and positions SGRRLD as a potential solution. The derivation of the theoretical results, particularly the convergence analysis, is a significant contribution. The experiments are well-designed, comparing SGRRLD with SGLD and SGHMC on relevant tasks. The inclusion of a large-scale matrix factorization experiment adds practical relevance.

Strengths:

* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea and offers a promising approach for reducing bias.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and a central limit theorem for the estimator. The derivation of the theoretical results is a key strength.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the ideas are explained clearly. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are presented in a logical manner.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD on both synthetic and real-world datasets, supporting the theoretical findings. The comparison against SGHMC, and the demonstration of achieving similar accuracy as higher-order integrators, are compelling.
* **Parallelizability:**  The algorithm’s inherent parallel structure is highlighted, making it suitable for modern computing architectures.

Weaknesses:

* **Technical Conditions:** The paper relies on a number of technical conditions for the theoretical results to hold. While these conditions are mentioned, a more detailed discussion of their practical implications would be beneficial. It is not immediately clear how easily these conditions will be satisfied in real-world applications.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** The paper mentions the need for correlated gradients between the two chains for optimal performance, but the discussion on this aspect is somewhat limited.  Exploring different strategies for achieving this correlation could be valuable.
* **Choice of Step Size:** Determining the optimal step sizes (γ) for SGRRLD in practice could be challenging. More guidance on how to choose appropriate step sizes, perhaps through adaptive methods, would be useful. While the analysis provides theoretical guidance, the practical implementation details are somewhat lacking.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with SGLD and SGHMC is valuable, a comparison with other advanced SG-MCMC methods, such as those utilizing preconditioning or adaptive step sizes, could further strengthen the paper.




Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithm.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The parallelizability of the algorithm makes it well-suited for large-scale applications.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The technical conditions required for the theoretical results to hold may be difficult to verify in practice.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the correlation between gradients and strategies for choosing appropriate step sizes.
* The comparison with other advanced SG-MCMC methods is limited.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the compelling experimental results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, particularly regarding the practical implications of the technical conditions and the discussion of gradient correlation.
