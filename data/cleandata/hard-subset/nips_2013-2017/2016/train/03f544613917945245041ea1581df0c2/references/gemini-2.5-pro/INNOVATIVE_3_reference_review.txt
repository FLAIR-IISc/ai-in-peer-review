### Summary
This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), aimed at reducing the discretization bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to the outputs of SG-MCMC. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and then linearly combining their estimators to cancel out the leading-order bias term. A key technical contribution is the use of correlated noise between the two chains to control the variance of the resulting RR estimator, which could otherwise negate the benefits of bias reduction. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than standard first-order methods (O(K⁻²/³)). The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated on a linear Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, where SGRRLD shows significant improvements over SGLD and achieves performance comparable to methods using more complex higher-order integrators.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing both asymptotic (CLT) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE) results that formally establish the advantage of the proposed method. The proofs, sketched in the main paper and detailed in the supplement, appear sound and follow from established techniques in the analysis of SDEs, adapted to the stochastic gradient setting. The experimental validation is well-designed. The use of a simple Gaussian model with a known posterior allows for a precise evaluation of bias and MSE, directly supporting the theoretical claims. The large-scale matrix factorization experiment demonstrates the practical utility and scalability of the method on a real-world task.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides a clear motivation for the problem of discretization bias in SG-MCMC and situates the work within the relevant literature. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the intuition behind using RR extrapolation and correlated noise is communicated effectively. The experimental results are presented clearly and convincingly support the paper's claims.

**Originality:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is, to my knowledge, a completely novel idea. While bias reduction in SG-MCMC has been previously addressed, the dominant approach has been the development of bespoke higher-order numerical integrators. This paper introduces a fundamentally different and more general paradigm: a "meta-algorithm" that can wrap around existing first-order methods to improve their accuracy. This is a bold, creative, and elegant fusion of ideas from classical numerical analysis and modern scalable Bayesian inference. The insight to use correlated noise to make the extrapolation practical is also a crucial and clever contribution.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial.
1.  **Generality:** The RR approach is not specific to SGLD. As the authors demonstrate with SGHMC, it can likely be applied to a wide family of SG-MCMC algorithms, offering an immediate, "plug-and-play" path to improving their performance. This is far more impactful than a single, improved algorithm.
2.  **Practicality:** The method achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators while only requiring the implementation of a simple first-order method (SGLD). This lowers the barrier to obtaining higher-accuracy samples.
3.  **Parallelism:** The algorithm is naturally suited for parallel architectures, as the two required chains can be run independently. In an era of multi-core processors, this is a significant practical advantage, turning the method's primary cost (running two chains) into a non-issue in many settings. This is well demonstrated in the experiments by comparing wall-clock time.
4.  **New Research Direction:** This work opens the door to exploring other variance reduction and sequence acceleration techniques from the numerical analysis literature (e.g., multilevel Monte Carlo) in the context of SG-MCMC, potentially spurring a new line of fruitful research.

The main weakness is the inherent computational overhead; the method requires roughly twice the computation of a single SGLD chain. However, the authors convincingly argue that this cost is justified by the much faster convergence in terms of wall-clock time, especially when parallel hardware is available. While the experimental validation is strong, it could be further bolstered by testing on more complex models, such as Bayesian neural networks, which are a major application area for SG-MCMC. This, however, is a minor point and can be left for future work; the current experiments are more than sufficient to demonstrate the power of the core idea.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **High Novelty:** Proposes a genuinely new and elegant approach for bias reduction in SG-MCMC by importing a powerful idea from classical numerical analysis.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** Provides rigorous proofs of improved convergence rates.
*   **Significant Practical Implications:** The method is simple, general, parallelizable, and empirically shown to outperform its baseline significantly in terms of wall-clock time efficiency.
*   **Potential for High Impact:** Opens up a new and promising research direction for improving scalable MCMC methods.

### Arguments for Rejection
*   The method doubles the computational cost per effective iteration compared to SGLD. While this is mitigated by parallelism and faster convergence, it is a non-trivial overhead.

### Recommendation
This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, powerful, and theoretically sound idea. The fusion of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation with stochastic gradient MCMC is a significant conceptual contribution that is both elegant and practically useful. The paper is well-executed, with strong theory and convincing experiments. Its potential to influence future work in scalable Bayesian inference is high. The minor weakness of computational overhead is well-addressed and is a small price to pay for the substantial gains in accuracy and convergence speed. This is precisely the kind of bold and creative work that NIPS should showcase. I strongly recommend acceptance.
