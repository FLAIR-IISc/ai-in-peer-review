

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors propose running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and then combining their outputs using RR extrapolation. The paper claims SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²), variance of O(γ⁴), and MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly improving upon SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. The approach is shown to be parallelizable and applicable to other SG-MCMC methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The analysis extends previous work on SG-MCMC (e.g., [10]) by showing how RR extrapolation can achieve higher convergence rates without requiring higher-order integrators.

2. **Novel Application:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is genuinely novel. While RR extrapolation has been used in deterministic numerical analysis and for standard SDEs ([21], [23]), this is the first application to stochastic gradient MCMC methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks clearly support the theoretical claims. The results show consistent improvements in bias and MSE across different settings, including comparisons to SGHMC and higher-order integrators.

4. **Practical Parallelism:** The paper effectively demonstrates how SGRRLD leverages parallel architecture, with wall-clock time comparisons showing significant improvements over SGLD on real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clear Motivation for RR in SG-MCMC:** The paper cites [23] as having justified RR for diffusion sampling, but doesn't clearly explain why RR hasn't been previously applied to SG-MCMC. A more detailed discussion of this gap would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Assumption Details:** The theoretical analysis relies on several technical assumptions (e.g., smoothness conditions on f, specific decay rates for step sizes). While these are stated in the supplementary material, the main paper could better explain how restrictive these assumptions are in practice, particularly for non-smooth objectives common in machine learning.

3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper mentions that Σ = I is optimal for variance control but doesn't provide guidance on how to implement this correlation in practice. The experimental section uses a specific correlation structure but doesn't explore how sensitive the results are to different correlation choices.

4. **Comparison to Higher-Order Methods:** While the paper claims SGRRLD achieves "the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators," the comparison to SGHMC-s in Figure 4 is limited to MSE at a single step size. A more thorough comparison across step sizes would better support this claim.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. Clarify the motivation for applying RR to SG-MCMC by explicitly discussing why previous SG-MCMC works haven't used this approach.

2. In the main paper, provide a more accessible summary of the key technical assumptions and their practical implications, perhaps with a table comparing assumptions to prior work.

3. Add a subsection discussing the practical implementation of the correlated Brownian motion (i.e., how to implement the correlation structure in code) and sensitivity analysis to different correlation choices.

4. Extend the experiments to include a more comprehensive comparison between SGRRLD and higher-order methods across a range of step sizes and problem dimensions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature by introducing a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results support the claims. While the paper has some weaknesses in explaining the motivation and practical implementation details, these are relatively minor compared to the strength of the core contribution.

The work is highly relevant to NeurIPS as it addresses a fundamental challenge in scalable Bayesian inference. The improved convergence rates could have significant practical implications for large-scale Bayesian machine learning applications. The paper also demonstrates the ability to achieve higher accuracy with first-order methods that would otherwise require more complex higher-order integrators.

The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field. While the theoretical analysis is somewhat dense, the experimental validation provides strong support for the claims. The parallel nature of the algorithm is particularly valuable for modern distributed computing architectures.

**Decision:** Accept

The paper presents a strong theoretical contribution with clear experimental validation. The improvements in convergence rates are significant and the parallel nature of the algorithm makes it practically relevant for large-scale applications. While some implementation details could be clarified, these do not undermine the core contribution. The work advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC methods and should be of interest to the NeurIPS community.
