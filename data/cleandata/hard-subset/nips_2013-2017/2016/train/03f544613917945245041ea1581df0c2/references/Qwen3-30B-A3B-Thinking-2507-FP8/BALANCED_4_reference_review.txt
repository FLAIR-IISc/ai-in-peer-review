

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors build upon the SGLD framework (Welling & Teh, 2011) and previous work on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) to develop a method that achieves higher convergence rates than existing SG-MCMC approaches. The key insight is that by running two chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation, the bias can be reduced from O(γ) to O(γ²) while controlling variance, leading to a MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).

## Strengths

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed, with clear theorems establishing the convergence properties of SGRRLD, including asymptotic behavior and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The authors successfully demonstrate how their method achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator-based methods using only first-order integrators, which is a significant theoretical contribution. 

Second, the experimental section is well-designed and effectively supports the theoretical claims. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model clearly demonstrate the bias reduction and MSE improvements of SGRRLD over SGLD, while the large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show practical benefits in real-world applications. The authors also appropriately extend their method to SGHMC, showing its generality.

Third, the paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The connection to previous work is well-articulated, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from existing SG-MCMC methods.

## Weaknesses

While the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution, there are several weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, the computational cost analysis is insufficient. The authors note that the two chains can be run in parallel, but they don't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or memory requirements compared to SGLD. For instance, how much additional memory is required for the second chain, and what is the actual speedup in wall-clock time (beyond just "nearly the same" as stated in the experiments)? This is particularly important for large-scale applications where memory constraints are critical.

Second, the paper doesn't thoroughly compare SGRRLD to other bias-reduction techniques for SG-MCMC. While it mentions that their method achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrator-based methods (Chen et al., 2015), it doesn't provide direct comparisons with these methods in experiments, which would strengthen the empirical claims.

Third, the paper could better address the practical limitations of the approach. For example, the authors don't discuss how the method performs when the two chains become misaligned due to different step sizes, or how sensitive the method is to the choice of step size sequences. The paper also doesn't discuss potential scenarios where the RR extrapolation might not be beneficial.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a detailed computational complexity analysis comparing SGRRLD to SGLD in terms of both time and memory requirements. This should include a breakdown of the additional cost for the second chain and the actual speedup achieved in practice.

2. Include direct comparisons with higher-order integrator-based methods (like those in Chen et al., 2015) in the experimental section. This would provide stronger empirical evidence for the claim that SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as these more complex methods.

3. Expand the discussion of practical limitations. For instance, analyze how the method performs when the step size sequences aren't perfectly aligned, or when the two chains diverge significantly. This would help practitioners understand the conditions under which SGRRLD is most effective.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how SGRRLD might be applied to other SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD and SGHMC, providing more concrete examples of implementation challenges.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising extension to SG-MCMC methods. The core idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and well-executed, and the theoretical results are significant. The experimental validation is strong and supports the main claims.

However, the paper's practical impact would be significantly enhanced by addressing the computational cost analysis and providing more direct comparisons with existing bias-reduction techniques. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution, while theoretically interesting, may not fully demonstrate its practical value for large-scale Bayesian inference.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of SG-MCMC, and the proposed method has clear potential for practical applications in large-scale Bayesian inference. The main weaknesses are related to the practical implementation details and comparative analysis, which can be addressed with the suggested revisions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality research in machine learning.
