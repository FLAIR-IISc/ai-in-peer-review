### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating convergence, to the widely used Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The goal is to reduce the discretization bias introduced by the first-order Euler-Maruyama integrator used in SGLD. The proposed method runs two SGLD chains in parallel: one with a step size sequence (γk) and another that takes two steps for each step of the first, using a halved step size (γk/2). The final estimator is a linear combination of the outputs of these two chains, designed to cancel the leading O(γ) error term. A key aspect of the proposed method is the use of correlated Gaussian noise for the two chains to control the variance of the final estimator.

This work builds directly upon the foundational SGLD algorithm [1] and the broader SG-MCMC literature [6, 10]. The novelty lies in connecting this line of research with established work on RR extrapolation for stochastic differential equations (SDEs) [21, 23]. While RR extrapolation has been analyzed for SDEs with exact gradients (e.g., ULA), its application and analysis in the *stochastic gradient* setting is new. The authors position their contribution as an alternative to using higher-order numerical integrators [10] to reduce bias, arguing that SGRRLD achieves similar theoretical accuracy improvements while only requiring simple, first-order updates.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and clever idea. It provides a principled way to improve the accuracy of a foundational algorithm by borrowing a powerful tool from classical numerical analysis. The approach is elegant and intuitive.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution (Quality & Significance):** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and the non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2) are significant contributions. The key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a substantial improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This demonstrates a clear and quantifiable advantage, advancing the state of the art in the theory of SG-MCMC methods.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Generality:** The method is highly practical.
    *   It achieves the accuracy of higher-order methods using only first-order updates, which are simpler to implement and reason about.
    *   The two chains required for extrapolation can be run in parallel, making the algorithm well-suited for modern multi-core architectures. The wall-clock time can be comparable to a single SGLD chain, despite the increased total computation.
    *   The technique is general and, as the authors note and demonstrate with SGHMC, can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms, increasing its potential impact.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation (Quality):** The experiments are well-designed and convincing.
    *   The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model provide clear, controlled evidence supporting the theoretical claims regarding bias and MSE reduction as a function of step size and dimensionality. The experiments verifying the optimal convergence rates (Fig. 3) are particularly strong.
    *   The application to SGHMC successfully demonstrates the generality of the proposed extrapolation scheme.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world datasets (MovieLens) effectively showcase the practical benefits, demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves better predictive accuracy than SGLD within the same wall-clock time.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Computational Cost Trade-offs:** The primary drawback of SGRRLD is the increased computational cost. Each "meta-iteration" of SGRRLD involves one step of the γ-chain and two steps of the γ/2-chain, totaling three gradient evaluations. The paper rightly points out that this can be parallelized. However, the discussion could be more nuanced.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would strengthen the paper to include a more direct comparison based on the total number of gradient evaluations. For example, how does SGRRLD for K iterations compare against standard SGLD run for 3K iterations? While the wall-clock time experiments are very valuable, this additional comparison would clarify the trade-off between statistical efficiency and raw computational budget, which is important for users in non-parallel or resource-constrained environments.

2.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The comparison with SGHMC-s (a higher-order method) in Figure 4 is a crucial piece of evidence for the paper's claims. However, the comparison is plotted against step size γ, which can be misleading as different methods may have different optimal γ values and different computational costs per step.
    *   **Suggestion:** This comparison would be much more compelling if performance (MSE) were plotted against wall-clock time or the total number of gradient evaluations. This would provide a direct, fair comparison of the practical efficiency of SGRRLD versus higher-order integrators in achieving a target accuracy.

3.  **Practical Guidance on Hyperparameters:** The theory suggests a different optimal step size decay rate for SGRRLD (α=1/5 for MSE) compared to SGLD (α=1/3). This is an interesting and important finding.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would benefit from a short discussion on the practical implications of this. For instance, does this make the method more or less sensitive to the choice of the initial step size γ₁? A brief paragraph offering practical guidance to users on tuning the step size schedule for SGRRLD would be a valuable addition.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically demonstrated to be effective. The theoretical analysis provides new insights into the convergence rates of SG-MCMC methods, and the method itself is a practical tool for improving accuracy. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate primarily to the framing of the experimental comparisons and the depth of the practical discussion; they do not detract from the core value of the contribution. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported, and the work is of high originality and significance. I recommend acceptance.
