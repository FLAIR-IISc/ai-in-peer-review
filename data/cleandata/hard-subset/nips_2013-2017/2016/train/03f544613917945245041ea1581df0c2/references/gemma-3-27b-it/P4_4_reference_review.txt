This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) aiming to reduce bias and improve convergence rates in Bayesian inference. The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique by running two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved bias and MSE bounds compared to SGLD and experimentally validate these findings on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a large-scale matrix factorization task.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in SG-MCMC, referencing key contributions like SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011] and extensions thereof [Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014; Li et al., 2016]. It appropriately situates itself within this context and acknowledges the existing bounds on bias and MSE [Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016]. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SDEs is also properly referenced [Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2015].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence analysis and bounds on bias and MSE. The derivation of the O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) convergence rate is a noteworthy result.
*   **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel approach.  While RR extrapolation is established for SDEs, its application in the context of stochastic gradients and the adaptation for parallelization are significant contributions.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD over SGLD and SGHMC, both on synthetic and real datasets. The results align with the theoretical predictions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent. The algorithm is well-defined and a pseudocode is provided in the supplementary material.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelizability of SGRRLD, stemming from the independent execution of the two chains, is a practical advantage, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions regarding the smoothness of the potential energy function and the properties of the gradient estimators.  A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of these assumptions would be valuable.
*   **Correlation of Gradients:** The paper acknowledges the importance of correlated Brownian increments but the discussion regarding the correlation of the stochastic gradient estimators is less thorough. It mentions that the estimators need to have the same distribution, but a more in-depth exploration of how different correlations affect performance would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of step sizes (γ) is crucial for performance. While the paper provides some guidance, a more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters is warranted.
*   **Limited Scope of Comparison:**  The comparison primarily focuses on SGLD and SGHMC. Including comparisons with other recent advancements in SG-MCMC, such as those utilizing adaptive step sizes or preconditioning techniques, would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Repetitive Theoretical Results:** Some of the theoretical derivations and discussion feel repetitive between the main body and the supplementary material. Tightening the presentation in the main text could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with demonstrably improved performance over existing methods.
*   The technical contributions are significant and address a key challenge in SG-MCMC: bias reduction.
*   The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing.
*   The parallel nature of the algorithm makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may limit the applicability of the algorithm in certain scenarios.
*   The discussion of gradient correlation could be more detailed.
*   A broader comparison against recent advancements in SG-MCMC would further strengthen the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach to reducing bias and improving convergence rates, and the theoretical analysis and experimental validation are strong. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance.
