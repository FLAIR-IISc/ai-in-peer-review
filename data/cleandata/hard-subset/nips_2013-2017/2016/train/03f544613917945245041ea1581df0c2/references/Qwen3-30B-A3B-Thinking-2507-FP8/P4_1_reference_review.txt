

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of existing Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the SGLD framework proposed by Welling and Teh (2011) and recent theoretical work on SG-MCMC by Chen et al. (2015), which established that the bias and MSE of SG-MCMC methods are O(γ) and O(γ²), respectively. The paper demonstrates that by applying RR extrapolation to two SGLD chains with different step sizes, SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to O(K⁻²/³) for standard SGLD and similar methods. The approach is theoretically supported with central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bounds, and validated through experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

**Quality:** The paper provides a technically sound theoretical analysis with comprehensive convergence guarantees. The authors establish both asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties of SGRRLD, including a central limit theorem and bounds for bias and MSE. The theoretical results are well-motivated and derive from careful extensions of existing work on SDE discretizations and RR extrapolation (Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The experimental section effectively supports the theoretical claims, with well-designed synthetic experiments that illustrate the bias reduction and real-world matrix factorization tasks demonstrating practical benefits. The paper is careful to acknowledge the technical assumptions required for their results (which are detailed in supplementary material), and the experimental setup is appropriate for the claims made.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement through theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail, though some of the more technical conditions are deferred to the supplementary material. The experimental section clearly describes the datasets, settings, and evaluation metrics, with results presented in a straightforward manner. The paper effectively communicates the key insights about how RR extrapolation can be applied to SG-MCMC methods, and the parallelization benefits are well-explained.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a novel combination of techniques not previously explored in the literature. The key insight that a first-order integrator with RR extrapolation can achieve the same accuracy as higher-order integrators (as shown in Chen et al., 2015) is particularly novel. The paper also demonstrates the generality of the approach by applying it to SGHMC, showing it can be extended beyond SGLD. Checking recent NIPS proceedings, this specific application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC appears to be a clear novelty, as no prior NIPS paper has addressed this combination of techniques.

**Significance:** The results have clear theoretical and practical significance. The improved convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for standard SG-MCMC methods represents a demonstrable advancement in the state of the art. The paper shows that SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods while using only first-order integrators, which is computationally advantageous. The experimental results on large-scale matrix factorization tasks demonstrate practical benefits in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency, with SGRRLD achieving comparable or better results in half the time compared to SGLD. The parallelization benefits further enhance the practical significance for large-scale applications.

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with both asymptotic and non-asymptotic results
- Clear demonstration of the approach's applicability beyond SGLD (to SGHMC)
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Well-explained parallelization benefits, making the method suitable for distributed computing
- Novel insight that first-order integrators with RR extrapolation can match higher-order methods

**Weaknesses:**
- Some technical assumptions required for the theoretical results are only detailed in the supplementary material, which may limit accessibility
- The paper does not thoroughly explore the memory trade-offs of maintaining two parallel chains
- The experimental section could provide more detail about computational resources used for the large-scale experiments
- The paper doesn't discuss how the method might perform on more complex models beyond the tested scenarios

**Conclusion:** This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to improving SG-MCMC methods through Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported, and the experimental results effectively validate the claims. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by demonstrating that a first-order integrator with RR extrapolation can achieve the same accuracy as higher-order methods while maintaining computational efficiency. The results advance the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference and have significant practical implications for large-scale applications. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in the field with a technically sound solution. I recommend acceptance.
