

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a novel MCMC algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The authors claim that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), with the method also being well-suited for parallel computation.

## Strengths
The paper demonstrates technical rigor in its theoretical analysis, providing clear theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) with appropriate mathematical justification for the convergence properties of the proposed method. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation. The experiments on synthetic data provide some evidence supporting the theoretical claims, particularly in the Gaussian model where the bias reduction is observed.

## Weaknesses
Despite the theoretical soundness, I have significant concerns about the paper's practical significance and the evidence supporting its claims:

1. **Lack of practical justification for theoretical improvements**: The claimed convergence rate improvement (O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3})) is mathematically interesting but the paper fails to translate this into meaningful practical benefits. The authors don't provide any analysis showing how much faster SGRRLD would achieve a target accuracy compared to SGLD. For instance, to achieve a specific MSE level, how much more computational time would SGRRLD require? The paper doesn't address this critical question.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The experiments only compare against SGLD (a specific method) and don't include comparisons against other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods. The matrix factorization results (Figure 5) show marginal improvements in RMSE, with SGRRLD achieving "better accuracy" but the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of how this translates to practical benefits. The claimed "significant performance improvements" are not convincingly demonstrated.

3. **Misleading claims about parallelization**: The paper states that "the two chains required by the RR extrapolation can be generated independently, the SGRRLD algorithm is well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures," but the experimental results contradict this. The paper claims "their wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" for SGRRLD and SGLD, which is misleading. Running two chains in parallel should roughly double the computational cost (if using a single processor), but the paper doesn't provide a proper wall-clock time comparison that accounts for this. The paper fails to demonstrate any actual speedup or computational efficiency gain.

4. **Lack of comparison to higher-order integrators**: The paper cites [10] which states that "these bounds can be improved by making use of higher-order integrators." The authors claim their method "can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators," but don't provide a meaningful comparison to these methods. Without such comparison, it's unclear what the specific advantage of their approach is.

5. **Marginal practical improvements**: The experimental results show that the bias of the estimated variance is reduced from ≈10^{-2} (SGLD) to ≈10^{-4} (SGRRLD) in the Gaussian model. While mathematically significant, this is a 100x improvement in bias but the paper doesn't explain how this translates to meaningful improvements in practical applications. The matrix factorization results don't show dramatic improvements in RMSE.

## Overall Assessment
While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant practical improvements. The theoretical rate improvement (O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3})) is not convincingly demonstrated to translate into meaningful practical benefits given the doubled computational cost (running two chains in parallel). The paper would need to provide more comprehensive experimental results showing how the method performs compared to state-of-the-art methods on larger datasets, with detailed wall-clock time comparisons, to justify the proposed approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims of "significant performance improvements" not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The theoretical contribution is interesting but lacks the practical validation required for a NIPS paper. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical trade-offs, including a detailed analysis of the computational cost versus accuracy gain.

I recommend rejection. The authors would need to substantially improve the experimental validation to demonstrate clear, practical advantages over existing methods before this work would be suitable for publication at NIPS.
