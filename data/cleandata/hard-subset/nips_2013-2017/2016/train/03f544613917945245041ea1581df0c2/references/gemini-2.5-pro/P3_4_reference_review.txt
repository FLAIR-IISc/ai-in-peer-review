### Summary

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the outputs of two parallel SGLD chains run with different step sizes (γ and γ/2). A key component of the method is the use of correlated noise between the two chains to control the variance of the final extrapolated estimator.

The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²) and an optimal MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(γ) bias and O(K⁻²/³) MSE rate of standard SGLD. This effectively allows a first-order integrator to achieve the accuracy of more complex second-order integrators, a notable contribution. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model, where theoretical claims are precisely verified, and on a large-scale matrix factorization task, where it shows superior performance in terms of wall-clock time compared to SGLD.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting is a novel and clever idea. It elegantly tackles the well-known problem of discretization bias by leveraging a powerful, well-understood technique from a different domain. While RR has been used for SDEs, its application and analysis in the stochastic gradient context is a new and valuable contribution.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is theoretically rigorous. It provides both asymptotic analysis (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic finite-time bounds (Theorem 2). The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step-size decay rate (α=1/5 for MSE) and clearly articulates the resulting improved convergence rates. The justification for using correlated noise to minimize the asymptotic variance is well-grounded in prior work on SDEs and is a crucial element for the method's practical success.

3.  **Practical Significance and Simplicity:** SGRRLD offers a compelling alternative to using higher-order integrators (e.g., those proposed by Chen et al., 2015) for bias reduction. Higher-order integrators can be more complex to derive and implement. In contrast, SGRRLD only requires running two instances of the standard, easy-to-implement Euler-Maruyama integrator. This simplicity is a major advantage. Furthermore, the two chains can be run in parallel, making the method well-suited for modern multi-core architectures, as convincingly demonstrated in the experiments.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The synthetic Gaussian model provides a controlled environment to meticulously verify the theoretical claims regarding bias and MSE as functions of step size (γ) and iteration count (K). The matrix factorization experiment on large MovieLens datasets demonstrates the practical utility of the algorithm, showing that the theoretical gains in convergence translate to real-world improvements in wall-clock time for a fixed accuracy. The inclusion of an experiment applying the RR scheme to SGHMC also effectively supports the claim of the method's generality.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational and Memory Overhead:** The primary weakness is the increased computational cost. To achieve the same time horizon, SGRRLD performs three SGLD steps (one with step γ, two with step γ/2) for every one step of a standard SGLD chain. While the authors leverage parallelization to keep wall-clock time comparable, the total computational budget (FLOPS) is roughly tripled. Similarly, the memory footprint is doubled as two chains must be maintained. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this trade-off. For instance, is it more efficient to run one SGLD chain for 3K iterations or SGRRLD for K iterations? The current results suggest SGRRLD is better, but a direct comparison and discussion of the cost-benefit analysis would be valuable.

2.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The paper claims to achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods. The experiment comparing SGRRHMC with a splitting-based SGHMC (SGHMC-s) provides some evidence. However, a more direct comparison of SGRRLD against a higher-order SGLD variant in the main experiments would have been more powerful. This would help to position the work more clearly, evaluating not just accuracy but also the trade-offs in implementation complexity and computational cost against state-of-the-art bias-reduction techniques.

3.  **Generality of Theoretical Claims:** The authors state that the technique is "quite generic" and can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms, but the provided theorems are specific to the Langevin dynamics case. While the SGHMC experiment offers empirical support, a brief theoretical discussion on what modifications or assumptions would be needed to extend the convergence proofs to other diffusions (e.g., SGHMC, SGNHT) would make the claim of generality more concrete.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for a significant problem in scalable Bayesian inference (discretization bias).
*   The theoretical results are strong, providing improved asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence rates.
*   The method is inherently parallelizable, making it highly relevant for modern computing environments.
*   The experimental results are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of SGRRLD over SGLD in practice.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   The increased computational and memory cost is a notable drawback that could be discussed more transparently.
*   The experimental comparison to other state-of-the-art bias-reduction methods (i.e., higher-order integrators) could be more direct and comprehensive.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by both strong theory and compelling experiments. It offers a practical and powerful tool for practitioners looking to improve the accuracy of scalable Bayesian inference. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the positioning and discussion of trade-offs rather than any fundamental flaws in the method itself. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem. It is very likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
