Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD while controlling variance through correlated Brownian increments. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and forms a debiased estimator π̂R(f) by a time-weighted combination of expectations from the fine and coarse chains. The authors show that, under standard dissipativity/smoothness conditions and with properly correlated Gaussian noise across chains, SGRRLD attains asymptotic bias O(γ2) and variance O(γ4), improving over SGLD’s O(γ) and O(γ2) with first-order integrators. Optimizing the finite-time MSE bound γ4 + 1/(Kγ) yields a rate O(K−4/5), improving upon SGLD’s O(K−2/3). A CLT is established for decreasing step sizes, along with non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate lower bias/MSE and improved predictive RMSE at comparable wall-clock time using parallel chains.

This work connects three lines of research:
- SG-MCMC methods, including SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011], theoretical analyses of SGLD/SG-MCMC [Teh et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2015], and higher-order/splitting integrators [Chen et al., 2015].
- Classical RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures [Talay & Tubaro, 1990/91; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup, 2015], which improve weak-error bias via multi-step discretizations and careful coupling.
- Extensions to other SG-MCMC algorithms (e.g., SGHMC [Chen et al., 2014]) by applying the same RR strategy.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Adapts RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting with stochastic gradients and establishes both asymptotic (CLT with optimal coupling Σ = I, bias/variance orders) and finite-time bounds. The improved K−4/5 MSE rate with only first-order integrators is theoretically meaningful and aligns with the RR intuition.
- Variance control via coupling: The analysis identifies the optimal Gaussian coupling across the two levels (Σ = I), extending known results for exact-gradient ULA to stochastic-gradient settings and showing that Gaussian noise dominates gradient noise asymptotically under the stated conditions.
- Generality: The method is conceptually simple, generic, and applicable to other SG-MCMC schemes (e.g., SGHMC), not tied to a specific integrator.
- Practicality and scalability: The two chains are embarrassingly parallel, yielding similar wall-clock time to SGLD when two cores are available. Experiments corroborate theory (bias ~ γ2, MSE ~ γ4; optimal schedules) and demonstrate benefits on large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumptions and scope: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds rely on strong smoothness (e.g., existence of C9 solution to the Poisson equation) and standard ergodicity/dissipativity conditions, which may exclude important nonconvex/posterior models common in deep learning. It would help to explicitly list these conditions in the main text and discuss how restrictive they are in typical Bayesian ML models.
- Role of stochastic gradient noise: The theory claims Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise and that gradient-noise correlation does not affect asymptotic variance. This deserves more intuition and, ideally, an ablation showing the effect of: (i) using the same versus independent mini-batches across levels; (ii) varying mini-batch sizes. Since the RR cancellation targets weak discretization error, mismatched gradient-noise distributions between levels can reintroduce O(γ) bias (as noted); experiments quantifying this effect would strengthen the case.
- Experimental fairness and metrics: The main practical gain assumes parallel resources. A comparison at equal total gradient evaluations (single-core budget) would clarify trade-offs when parallelism is limited. Additionally, reporting posterior diagnostics (e.g., ESS per gradient, bias on known posteriors beyond variance, or errors on multiple test functions f) would better assess sampling quality beyond predictive RMSE.
- Extension breadth: While SGHMC results are promising, a comparison to higher-order SG-Langevin integrators (e.g., from [Chen et al., 2015]) on the same tasks would illustrate whether RR achieves comparable accuracy at similar computational cost in the Langevin case, as shown for SGHMC-s.
- Practical guidance: The method benefits from specific step-size schedules (e.g., α = 1/5 for MSE). Providing a short “recipe” for practitioners (how to pick γ, K, coupling, and whether to share mini-batches) would improve usability.
- Notational and minor clarity issues: Some typos and dense notation (e.g., equation (5) indexing, “accordingly to (2)”) could be streamlined. The estimator definition and time-averaging weights are correct but could be motivated more directly by weak-error expansions.

Quality
- The theoretical development is coherent and appears technically sound, following established RR/Poisson-equation techniques adapted to SG-MCMC. Proof sketches are appropriate for the main text, with details deferred to the supplement.
- The finite-time bounds and their optimization match the asymptotic CLT insights. The empirical results are consistent with the predicted scaling.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but dense. Moving key assumptions from the supplement into the main text, adding a brief algorithm box, and clarifying the coupling/mini-batch usage would improve readability.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a rigorous analysis is, to the best of current knowledge, novel. The coupling analysis and extension to SGHMC add to the contribution.

Significance
- The method offers a simple, parallelizable way to improve bias/MSE rates without higher-order integrators, potentially impacting a range of SG-MCMC applications. The demonstrated gains on large-scale matrix factorization suggest practical utility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include ablations on mini-batch coupling across levels and on Gaussian coupling (Σ = I vs partial/independent).
- Add a single-core budget comparison to quantify cost-accuracy trade-offs without parallelism.
- Compare against higher-order SG-Langevin integrators where available.
- Provide a practitioner's guide for step-size schedules and resource allocation.
- Summarize key technical assumptions in the main paper.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advance with improved rates using a simple, general technique.
- Solid analysis (CLT and finite-time bounds) and consistent empirical validation.
- Practical relevance with parallelization and applicability beyond SGLD.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong assumptions may limit applicability; practical guidance on when they hold is limited.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader and fairer under single-core budgets.
- Limited analysis of the impact of stochastic gradient noise coupling.

Overall assessment
A technically solid and novel contribution with promising empirical evidence and practical relevance. With clarifications and broader experiments, this work would be a strong addition to NIPS.
