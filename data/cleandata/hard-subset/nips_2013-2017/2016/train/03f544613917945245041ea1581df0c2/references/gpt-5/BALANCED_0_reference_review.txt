Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC, primarily SGLD. The method runs two SGLD chains with steps γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated estimator to cancel first-order discretization bias. To control variance, the authors use correlated Brownian increments, with a theoretically optimal coupling Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. Theoretically, the paper establishes a CLT for the extrapolated estimator under standard Poisson equation assumptions, derives non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, and shows improved rates relative to standard first-order SG-MCMC: for fixed step size, bias and MSE improve from O(γ + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) (SGLD) to O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) (SGRRLD); with γk ∝ k^−α, the optimal non-asymptotic MSE rate improves from O(K^−2/3) to O(K^−4/5). Empirically, on synthetic Gaussian posterior and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens 1M/10M/20M), SGRRLD yields lower bias/MSE and better test RMSE per wall-clock time than SGLD. The authors also illustrate generality by applying RR to SGHMC, showing parity with a higher-order integrator.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and subsequent SG-MCMC developments (SGHMC, SGNHT, preconditioning, higher-order integrators). RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures is classical (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup), including duplicated diffusions and multilevel ideas, but to my knowledge has not been applied to SG-MCMC. The use of correlated Brownian increments follows the variance reduction principles in RR/MLMC for SDEs and stationary sampling; the paper correctly cites [23] and related works.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are technically sound in spirit and consistent with known expansions for Euler–Maruyama. The optimal coupling result is standard and well-motivated. However, the assumptions are strong: high smoothness (up to C^9), existence/regularity of the Poisson solution, and “appropriate” conditions on U and L are deferred to the supplement. The treatment largely assumes the stochastic gradient noise is unbiased with bounded moments and that its effect is dominated by Gaussian noise under the chosen scaling; this is plausible but the regime where gradient noise could dominate (small γ, small batch) is not deeply dissected. The requirement that both chains use the same minibatch size and distribution is crucial; otherwise the estimator can inherit first-order bias.
- Empirics: Experiments are clean and match the theory (bias/MSE vs γ, rates vs K), including dimensional scaling for a Gaussian model. The matrix factorization experiments are large-scale and demonstrate practical improvements with parallel chains. Missing are ablations on the effect of the Brownian correlation (independent vs correlated), the effect of sharing vs not sharing minibatches across chains, and sensitivity to batch size and step-size choices. Comparisons to other variance-reduction SG-MCMC variants (e.g., preconditioned SGLD, thermostats) are limited; SGHMC comparisons are welcome but could be broader.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with crisp statements of the algorithm and theorems. However, key algorithmic details (pseudo-code), assumptions, and proofs are deferred to the supplement, which hinders readability. Some notation is heavy (two chains, three time indices). Practical guidance on tuning (γ schedules, batch size, burn-in, coupling implementation across processes) could be expanded. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation systematically to SG-MCMC with careful variance control via Brownian coupling is novel and nontrivial. The idea is simple yet insightful, and the theoretical and empirical developments are a meaningful advance beyond prior SG-MCMC work that improved accuracy via higher-order integrators.

Significance
The contribution is significant for practitioners of SG-MCMC: it provides a generic, parallelizable way to reduce bias and improve MSE without resorting to complex integrators. The demonstrated wall-clock parity by running chains in parallel is compelling. The technique should be applicable to a broad class of SG-MCMC samplers. The main caveats are the strong assumptions and limited empirical breadth; nonetheless, the potential impact is clear.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general bias-reduction approach for SG-MCMC based on RR extrapolation with principled variance control.
- Solid theory: CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, optimal asymptotic rates and coupling.
- Parallel-friendly design; empirical improvements in both synthetic and large-scale real tasks.
- Extension beyond SGLD (SGHMC) and parity with higher-order integrators.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy smoothness/Poisson assumptions; unclear robustness when gradient noise dominates or under weaker regularity.
- Practical constraints: requires same minibatch law across chains; unclear best practice for coupling gradient noise.
- Missing ablations (independent vs correlated Brownian, shared vs independent minibatches), and limited comparisons to other advanced SG-MCMC baselines (e.g., pSGLD, SGNHT variants, control variate SG-MCMC).
- Limited diversity of models (Gaussian, matrix factorization); no classification/logistic regression, deep models, or multimodal posteriors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include ablations: (i) independent vs correlated Brownian increments; (ii) shared vs independent minibatches across chains; quantify their impact on variance and bias empirically.
- Provide practical guidance for coupling when chains run on separate devices: reproducible PRNG seeding and communication costs.
- Compare against additional strong baselines (e.g., pSGLD, SGNHT/CCAdL) and higher-order SG-Langevin integrators on the same tasks.
- Explore multi-step RR (recursive extrapolation) empirically to validate the claim of further accuracy gains and study the variance–cost trade-off.
- Test on additional posterior families (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) and report both predictive metrics and posterior diagnostics (ESS, calibration).
- Clarify assumptions in the main text (at least a concise list) and give intuition on when gradient noise may invalidate the asymptotic dominance of Gaussian noise.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a conceptually elegant and practically relevant method with solid theoretical backing and promising empirical results. With stronger empirical validation and clearer guidance on implementation and assumptions, it would be a strong accept.
