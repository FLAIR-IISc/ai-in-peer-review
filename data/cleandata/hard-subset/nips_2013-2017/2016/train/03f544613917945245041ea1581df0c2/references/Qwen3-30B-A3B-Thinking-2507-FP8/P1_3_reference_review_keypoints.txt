SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with different step sizes and combining outputs to reduce bias.  
Theoretical gains: achieves asymptotic bias O(γ²), variance O(γ⁴), and MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's bias O(γ), variance O(γ²), and MSE O(K⁻²/³).  
Empirical results: demonstrates consistent improvements on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization, matching theoretical predictions.  
Novelty: first application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, enabling first-order integrators to reach higher-order accuracy.  
Theoretical rigor: provides a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) with supporting proofs.  
Parallelizability: the two required chains can be generated independently, making SGRRLD suitable for parallel and distributed computation.  
Comprehensive experiments: includes both analytical-posteriors synthetic tests and real-world datasets, comparing to SGLD and SGHMC.  
Clarity: paper is well-organized with clear definitions, theorems, and reproducible experimental setup; supplementary material contains detailed proofs.  
Limited experimental scope: evaluations are restricted to Gaussian models and matrix factorization, lacking complex Bayesian models (e.g., neural networks).  
Step-size selection: paper lacks a clear practical protocol for choosing optimal step sizes despite theoretical scaling guidance.  
Computational cost analysis: insufficient wall-clock time comparisons versus SGLD for very large datasets; claim of similar wall-clock times needs more evidence.  
Generality claims: states method is generic for SG-MCMC but provides limited concrete examples beyond SGHMC.  
Quality: technically sound and rigorous, with sensible assumptions noted (e.g., correlated Brownian increments).  
Clarity caveat: some technical details (e.g., Brownian increment correlation) could use more intuitive explanation for non-specialists.  
Originality: novel and significant contribution to SG-MCMC methodology.  
Significance: improves scalable Bayesian inference accuracy without materially increasing computational burden, relevant to large-scale ML.  
Recommendation: accept — strong theoretical contribution and empirical support justify publication.  
Pros summarized: novel RR application to SG-MCMC, rigorous analysis, empirical improvements, parallel-friendly, well-connected to literature.  
Cons summarized: limited experiments, incomplete practical guidance on step sizes, limited discussion of extensions to more complex models.
