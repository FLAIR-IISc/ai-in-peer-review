Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), applying classical Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2, and forms a bias-reducing estimator by combining time-averages from the fine and coarse chains. To control variance, the authors correlate the Gaussian noise across chains using a coupling where the coarse increment is the normalized sum of the two fine increments. They prove a CLT for the estimator, and non-asymptotic bounds: under their assumptions, the asymptotic bias and variance scale as O(γ2) and O(γ4), and with K iterations the MSE scales as O(K^−4/5), improving on the O(K^−2/3) rate for first-order SG-MCMC. Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model, SGHMC on a toy problem, and large-scale Gaussian matrix factorization (MovieLens) show gains over SGLD/SGHMC at comparable wall-clock time when the two chains are run in parallel.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and grounded in well-established numerical analysis (RR extrapolation for SDEs). The extension to SG-MCMC and the correlated-increment construction are sensible.
- Non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds and a CLT, with an explicit optimal decay schedule (α=1/5) and variance-minimizing coupling, are technically nontrivial.
- Simple to implement atop existing SG-MCMC; parallel-friendly.
- Experiments demonstrate reductions in bias/MSE on a model with known posterior and improved test RMSE in a large-scale matrix factorization task.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on strong, deferred “mild assumptions”: solvability of the Poisson equation with g∈C^9, geometric ergodicity, and conditions on the gradient noise. These are not verified for the models used, and may be unrealistic in nonconvex/posterior settings common in practice.
- The claim that “Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” is central to the variance control argument but is not empirically validated; no ablation contrasting correlated vs independent Gaussian increments, or the effect of correlating mini-batch gradients, is provided.
- Empirical scope is limited to Gaussian likelihood models (linear-Gaussian, Gaussian MF). There are no experiments on standard SG-MCMC benchmarks (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) with non-Gaussian/posterior structure.
- Fairness and resource accounting: SGRRLD requires two chains and more gradient evaluations per unit time; the reported wall-clock parity relies on parallelism with multiple cores. SGLD can also leverage parallelism (e.g., for gradient computation), so equal wall-clock on unequal core usage is not a like-for-like comparison. No core-hour or gradient-evaluation parity plots are shown.
- Comparisons with higher-order integrators are limited. For SGHMC, the paper includes a comparison to a splitting integrator on a toy model, but for SGLD-like samplers there is no direct comparison to recent higher-order SG-MCMC variants on realistic tasks. Constants may overwhelm asymptotic advantages; this is not discussed.
- Sensitivity to design choices (fixed vs decreasing step sizes, batch size, coupling strength Σ, reuse of mini-batches across chains) is not systematically studied.
- Practical guidance (how to set γ without knowing K; impact on ESS or mixing; memory/compute overhead) is missing.

Quality
- The theoretical development follows established RR extrapolation techniques and appears technically careful, but hinges on strong smoothness and ergodicity assumptions not verified for the presented applications. The empirical section is suggestive but not comprehensive and lacks ablations/testing against robust baselines under matched compute budgets. As such, the claims about broad practical superiority are not yet fully supported.

Clarity
- The presentation is generally clear; the main idea and estimator are understandable. However, many crucial assumptions and proofs are deferred to the supplement; including a brief pseudo-code and a concise list of assumptions in the main text would improve readability. The discussion of computational cost and resource parity should be made explicit.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation and correlated-increment coupling to SG-MCMC is a novel and natural extension of prior RR work for SDEs and invariant measure estimation. The specific analysis for SG-MCMC under stochastic gradients is a useful contribution.

Significance
- If robustly validated, the idea could be broadly useful as a generic bias-reduction wrapper for SG-MCMC with minimal code changes. However, given the limited empirical evidence, strong assumptions, and unclear compute fairness, the practical impact is uncertain at this stage.

Questions for the authors
1) How often does the “Gaussian noise dominates gradient noise” regime hold in practice? Can you provide ablations varying batch size and showing the impact of correlated vs independent Brownian increments, and of correlating mini-batch indices across the two chains?
2) Can you report results under matched compute (same gradient evaluations or core-hours), not just wall-clock with extra cores for SGRRLD? How do constants compare to higher-order integrators?
3) How sensitive is SGRRLD to violating identical mini-batch distributions across chains? What if batch sizes differ slightly?
4) Can you add benchmarks on non-Gaussian posteriors (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural networks)?
5) Provide practical guidance for choosing step size without prior K, and report ESS/auto-correlation diagnostics.

Pros
- Solid, well-motivated theory extending RR to SG-MCMC
- Simple, generic mechanism; parallelizable
- Demonstrated gains on synthetic and large-scale MF

Cons
- Strong, unverified assumptions underpinning theory
- Limited, homogeneous empirical validation; no ablations
- Compute fairness concerns; reliance on extra parallel resources
- Sparse comparison to competing high-order or variance-reduced SG-MCMC methods

Recommendation: Weak reject. Interesting idea with promising theory, but empirical evidence and practical validation against established baselines under fair compute budgets are insufficient for NeurIPS at this time.

Confidence: High.
