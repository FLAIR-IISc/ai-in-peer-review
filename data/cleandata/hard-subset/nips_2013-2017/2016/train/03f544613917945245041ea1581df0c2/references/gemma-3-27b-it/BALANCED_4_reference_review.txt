This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, dubbed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), aiming to reduce the bias of the standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) method. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, which requires running two chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates and validate their approach with experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, showcasing improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper builds directly on a growing body of work in SG-MCMC, citing key papers from Welling & Teh (2011), Chen et al. (2014), and others. It correctly identifies the bias of SGLD as a limiting factor and intelligently leverages a well-known numerical analysis technique (RR extrapolation) to address it. The connection to diffusion processes and the relevant literature on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) is also well established. The extension of RR extrapolation to SGHMC is a nice addition, demonstrating the generality of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea and offers a potentially significant improvement over existing methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. The derivation of convergence rates is convincing.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real data support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the improved performance of SGRRLD.  The large-scale matrix factorization experiment is particularly compelling.
* **Generality:** The authors demonstrate the applicability of SGRRLD beyond SGLD to other SG-MCMC methods like SGHMC.
* **Parallelizability:**  The inherent parallel structure of the RR extrapolation is explicitly highlighted, making the algorithm well-suited for modern computing architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Conditions:** The assumptions required for the theoretical results are numerous and not always clearly stated or justified.  A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** While acknowledging the possibility of correlated gradients, the paper doesn't fully explore the impact of different correlation structures on performance.  The assumption that correlations don't substantially affect the results seems potentially strong.
* **Step Size Tuning:** Choosing the optimal step sizes (γk) is crucial for performance. While the paper provides some guidance based on the theoretical analysis, a more detailed discussion of practical step size tuning strategies, particularly for non-stationary problems, would be helpful.  Could adaptive step size methods be easily incorporated?
* **Computational Overhead:** The paper acknowledges the need to run two chains in parallel.  A more detailed analysis of the computational overhead associated with maintaining and synchronizing these chains would be useful, especially in the context of very large-scale problems.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods (e.g., those utilizing preconditioning techniques) is limited.  A more comprehensive benchmark would strengthen the claims of superiority.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-supported algorithm that addresses a significant challenge in SG-MCMC (bias reduction).
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD.
* The work is likely to have a significant impact on the field of Bayesian inference and scalable MCMC.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The numerous technical conditions and potential sensitivity to step size tuning might limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The limited comparison with other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods leaves some questions about the overall performance advantage of SGRRLD.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some weaknesses, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Clarify Technical Assumptions:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the technical conditions required for the theoretical results, including their practical implications and potential limitations.
* **Explore Gradient Correlation:** Investigate the impact of different correlation structures on the performance of SGRRLD.
* **Step Size Tuning Strategies:**  Discuss practical step size tuning strategies and consider the potential for incorporating adaptive step size methods.
* **Computational Overhead Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead associated with running the two chains in parallel.
* **Expanded Benchmarking:**  Include a comparison with a wider range of state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods.
* **Code Release:**  Making the code publicly available would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the research.
