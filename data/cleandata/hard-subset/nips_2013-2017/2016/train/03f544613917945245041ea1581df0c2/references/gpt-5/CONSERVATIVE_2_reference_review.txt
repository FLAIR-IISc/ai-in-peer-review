Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. Concretely, it runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and combines their trajectory-based ergodic averages to cancel leading-order discretization bias. To contain variance inflation from extrapolation, the authors use correlated Gaussian noise between the two chains, following ideas from RR for SDEs. They prove: (i) a CLT with optimal variance achieved by perfect correlation of Brownian increments; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant step size, yielding a K−4/5 MSE rate under optimal tuning (vs K−2/3 for first-order SG-MCMC). They present experiments on a Gaussian linear model (including an SGHMC variant) and on large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens), claiming improved accuracy at similar wall-clock due to parallelization of the two chains.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in classical RR extrapolation for SDE weak error (Talay–Tubaro) and invariant measure estimation for discretized diffusions (Lemaire–Pagès et al.), and builds on SG-MCMC analyses (Teh et al.; Ma et al.; Chen et al.). The idea of reducing bias via extrapolation is known for SDE simulation but, to my knowledge, has not been systematically explored in SG-MCMC; this is a plausible extension.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable: RR extrapolation can, in principle, wrap around many SG-MCMC schemes without higher-order integrators.
- Theoretical development: CLT with an explicit variance-minimizing coupling of Gaussian increments; non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds with improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC under the stated assumptions and tuning.
- Practical insight: using correlated Brownian increments is important to control variance; the proposed coupling is implementable.
- Experiments show consistent gains on a controlled Gaussian model and a real large-scale matrix factorization task, with wall-clock matched via parallel execution of the two chains.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions vs practice: Theorems rely on strong smoothness and ergodicity assumptions (e.g., solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C9, polynomial growth, dissipativity). These are satisfied in the Gaussian examples but are far from typical deep/posterior models (e.g., nonsmooth ReLU networks, heavy-tailed priors). The paper claims broad applicability “to virtually all SG-MCMC,” but the analysis seems to require conditions rarely met in modern applications; no empirical evidence is provided beyond smooth log-concave/quadratic models.
- Limited and selective baselines: On real data, comparisons are only to vanilla SGLD. Given the claims, one would expect comparisons to (i) higher-order SG-MCMC (e.g., SGHMC with splitting integrators, as done in synthetic but not in matrix factorization), (ii) more competitive SG-MCMC variants (preconditioned SGLD, thermostats), and (iii) variance-reduction or control-variate SG-MCMC. Without these, it is hard to assess practical significance.
- Fairness of “same wall-clock” claim: SGRRLD performs two gradient evaluations per coarse step. The paper argues wall-clock parity by running the two chains in parallel, but in many realistic settings the bottlenecks are memory bandwidth, data IO, GPU utilization, or gradient computation that could also be parallelized for SGLD (e.g., larger minibatches, multi-worker gradient computation). The comparison omits these considerations and may overstate the real-world advantage.
- Sensitivity and constants: The improved K−4/5 rate is asymptotic and depends on optimal step-size tuning; constants in the bounds are not quantified and may be large due to extrapolation and gradient noise. Empirical robustness to step-size, minibatch size, and coupling choices is not systematically explored (no ablation of correlated vs independent noise; no study of sharing minibatches across chains).
- Correlation of gradient noise: Theory asserts Gaussian noise dominates, and variance optimization focuses on Brownian coupling Σ. In practice, stochastic gradient noise can dominate, especially with small minibatches. The method requires the two gradient estimators to have the same distribution; the impact of reusing the same minibatch indices across the coupled steps (which could further reduce variance) is not explored.
- Metrics: As a sampling method, quality should be assessed beyond point RMSE—e.g., posterior calibration, effective sample size per second, autocorrelation, or discrepancy to ground-truth posterior (available in Gaussian setting). These diagnostics are missing.
- Clarity/typos: Eq. (5) seems to evaluate ∇Ũ(·) at θ(γ/2)2k+1 in the first half-step, which is circular; likely it should be θ(γ/2)2k. Please correct. Some conditions relegated to the supplement are critical to understand applicability.

Questions for authors
1. Can you quantify constants in Theorem 2 (or at least discuss their dependence on gradient noise variance), and provide empirical evidence that SGRRLD’s constants do not negate the improved rates at practical K?
2. How does SGRRLD perform with independent vs correlated Brownian increments, and with shared vs independent minibatch indices across the coupled updates?
3. On MovieLens, why not compare to SGHMC with splitting integrators [10], preconditioned SGLD, or other strong SG-MCMC baselines? Can you report ESS/sec or posterior predictive calibration?
4. How sensitive are wall-clock gains to hardware and data pipelines? Could SGLD close the gap by parallelizing minibatch gradient computations?
5. Do you envision any extension of the theory to nonsmooth or non-log-concave posteriors?

Pros
- Clear, principled idea with solid grounding in numerical analysis.
- Non-asymptotic analysis yielding improved rates under explicit tuning.
- Simple to implement; potentially useful wrapper for SG-MCMC.

Cons
- Strong assumptions; limited empirical scope restricted to smooth/quadratic models.
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines on real data.
- Questionable fairness of wall-clock comparisons; lack of sampling diagnostics.
- Some clarity issues and untested design choices (noise/gradient coupling).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and the analysis is careful within its scope, but the empirical validation is too narrow and does not convincingly demonstrate practical superiority over established, stronger SG-MCMC baselines under fair computational budgets. Addressing the missing comparisons, adding sampling diagnostics, and clarifying the algorithmic details/assumptions would substantially strengthen the paper.
