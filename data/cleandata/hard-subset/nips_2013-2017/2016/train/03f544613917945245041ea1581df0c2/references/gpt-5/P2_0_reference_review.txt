Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, specifically to SGLD, to cancel leading-order discretization bias without resorting to higher-order integrators. The method runs two coupled SGLD chains with stepsizes γ and γ/2 and forms an extrapolated time-averaged estimator 2π̂γ/2 − π̂γ. The authors show that with properly correlated Gaussian noise across the two chains (a standard MLMC-style coupling), SGRRLD achieves bias O(γ2) and variance O(γ4) (versus O(γ), O(γ2) for SGLD), and derive both asymptotic CLTs and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. Optimizing the finite-time bounds yields an MSE rate O(K−4/5) with constant stepsize, improving over the O(K−2/3) rate of first-order SG-MCMC. Empirically, SGRRLD improves over SGLD on a Gaussian model and on large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens datasets). The paper also briefly applies RR to SGHMC and shows behavior comparable to a higher-order splitting integrator at larger stepsizes. The work builds on classic RR/MLMC ideas for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) and on theoretical analyses of SG-MCMC (Teh–Thiéry–Vollmer; Chen–Ding–Carin).

Quality
- Theory: The main claims are supported by a clear adaptation of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, with (i) a CLT for the extrapolated estimator under decreasing stepsizes; (ii) bias/MSE non-asymptotic bounds for constant/decreasing stepsizes; and (iii) an argument that optimal variance coupling is achieved by sharing the Brownian path so that Z(γ) = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. The resulting rates (bias O(γ2), MSE O(γ4) asymptotically; finite-time MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) and optimized O(K−4/5)) are consistent with RR intuition and with prior results on first- vs higher-order schemes.
- Assumptions: The analysis relies on standard smoothness/ergodicity assumptions, existence of a smooth solution to a Poisson equation, and moments of the stochastic gradient estimator. These are strong but typical for the literature. The paper correctly notes that both chains must use gradient estimators with the same law (e.g., same minibatch size) to avoid reintroducing bias.
- Correctness/rigor: Sketches are provided; full proofs are deferred to the supplement. One apparent typo in Eq. (5): the first fine update uses ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k+1) rather than ∇Ũ(θ(γ/2)2k); likewise the second line reuses θ(γ/2)2k+1 on both sides. This looks like an indexing error (implicit update) and should be corrected. Beyond this, the derivations are standard and plausible.
- Experiments: The synthetic Gaussian posterior cleanly demonstrates the predicted bias and MSE scalings and dimensional trends. The matrix factorization experiments are large-scale and show clear wall-clock gains by exploiting parallelism (two chains). The SGHMC comparison is useful, though limited. It would strengthen the case to include comparisons to other variance/bias reduction ideas in SG-MCMC (e.g., control variates) and to higher-order SGLD-like integrators, not only SGHMC-s.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The role of correlated Brownian increments and why they are optimal is nicely motivated. The estimator definition (Eq. 6) corresponds to 2π̂γ/2 − π̂γ via time averaging and is clear.
- Minor issues: fix Eq. (5) indexing; explicitly state whether minibatch indices are shared or independent across the two fine steps in a coarse step (this can impact variance). Provide the pseudocode in the main text or a clearer pointer.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC estimators and the first to show that simple first-order integrators coupled via RR can reach the accuracy/rates of higher-order schemes in this setting. The variance coupling analysis extends known MLMC couplings to the stochastic-gradient setting and clarifies when gradient noise is subdominant. Related work is well referenced.

Significance
- The contribution is conceptually simple, broadly applicable (can wrap around many SG-MCMC schemes), and practically attractive when parallel resources are available: two chains can be run independently and coupled with negligible overhead. The improved rates (O(K−4/5) MSE) and bias reduction are meaningful; the empirical improvements on MovieLens are convincing. Limitations are that the method doubles per-iteration computation on a single device and requires more careful bookkeeping (two chains, coupling, same gradient law), and the theory assumes smoothness that may not hold in all modern ML posteriors.

Pros
- Simple, general idea with strong theoretical backing; clear improvement in bias/MSE rates.
- Sound variance coupling argument; asymptotic CLT and non-asymptotic bounds.
- Empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real data; practical parallel implementation.
- Extends to SGHMC and likely to other SG-MCMC algorithms.

Cons
- Some theoretical assumptions are strong (smoothness, Poisson equation solution).
- Empirical scope could be broader (e.g., compare to alternative bias/variance reduction, higher-order SGLD).
- Practicality depends on available parallelism; on a single worker, cost doubles.
- Minor clarity issues/typos (Eq. 5), and some implementation details relegated to supplement.

Questions/suggestions
- Clarify whether the two fine steps within a coarse step use the same minibatch indices or independent ones, and whether sharing improves variance in practice.
- Include a direct comparison to a higher-order SGLD discretization, not only SGHMC-s.
- Discuss robustness when U is not globally smooth (e.g., non-differentiable likelihoods).
- Provide pseudocode in the main paper and release code if possible.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a principled, novel extrapolation-based variance/bias reduction technique for SG-MCMC with solid theory and meaningful empirical gains. With minor clarifications and corrections, it would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS program.
