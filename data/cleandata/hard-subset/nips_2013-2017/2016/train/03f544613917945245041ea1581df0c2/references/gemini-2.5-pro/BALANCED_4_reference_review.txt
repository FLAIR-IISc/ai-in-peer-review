### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the well-known Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two SGLD chains in parallel: one with a step size sequence {γ_k} and another with a step size sequence that evolves twice as fast but with half the step size {γ_k/2}. The outputs of these two chains are then combined linearly to cancel the leading-order bias term of the Euler-Maruyama discretization. To manage the variance of the combined estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motions for the two chains, extending prior work on RR for SDEs (e.g., Lemaire et al., 2015) to the stochastic gradient setting.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). A key theoretical result is that SGRRLD can achieve an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This new rate is comparable to that of methods using more complex, higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015), but is achieved using only the first-order Euler integrator. The authors empirically validate their claims on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, demonstrating superior performance over SGLD and SGHMC.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and elegant contribution. It cleverly bridges classical numerical analysis with modern large-scale Bayesian inference. The resulting improvement in the theoretical convergence rate for the MSE (from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) is substantial and highly significant for the field. Achieving higher-order accuracy with a simple first-order integrator is a compelling practical advantage.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous convergence analysis. It offers both an asymptotic Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step size decay rate (α=1/5) to balance bias and variance, and it insightfully shows that correlating the driving Brownian motions is crucial for controlling the variance of the final estimator.

3.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** A major strength of SGRRLD is its inherent parallelism. The two required chains can be run independently on separate cores or machines. The experiments on large-scale matrix factorization convincingly demonstrate that this parallelism allows SGRRLD to achieve better accuracy than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time, making it a practical tool for modern computing environments.

4.  **Generality:** The authors demonstrate the generality of their approach by applying it not only to SGLD but also to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC). The experimental results for SGRRHMC are promising and suggest that the RR extrapolation technique could be a general-purpose "wrapper" for improving a wide range of first-order SG-MCMC methods.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Cost Trade-off:** The most significant drawback of SGRRLD is its computational cost. For each "effective" step, SGRRLD requires three gradient evaluations (one for the γ-chain, two for the γ/2-chain) compared to one for SGLD. The paper's main argument for efficiency relies on perfect parallelization. While this is a valid and important use case, the authors should be more explicit about the trade-offs.
    *   **Suggestion:** I would recommend adding a discussion on the performance in a non-parallel (single-core) setting, where SGRRLD would be roughly 3x slower per effective iteration. Is the improved convergence rate still sufficient to make SGRRLD competitive in terms of total gradient computations? A plot of RMSE vs. the number of gradient evaluations (in addition to wall-clock time) would make the comparison fairer and more complete.

2.  **Clarity on Assumptions:** The theoretical results are presented without a summary of the underlying assumptions in the main text, with the authors referring to the supplement. While this is common due to space constraints, it makes it difficult to assess the scope and limitations of the theory.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief, informal summary of the key assumptions (e.g., smoothness of the potential U, boundedness of moments of the gradient estimator noise) in Section 4 would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and clarity.

3.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The performance of SGLD and SGRRLD is highly sensitive to the choice of the step size schedule. The experiments use a fixed step size γ, but it is not entirely clear how this γ was selected. Was it tuned optimally and independently for each algorithm? The optimal γ for SGRRLD may be different from that of SGLD.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should clarify the procedure for selecting γ in the large-scale experiments. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of SGRRLD to this choice compared to SGLD would also be a valuable addition for practitioners.

4.  **Experiment Details:** The claim in Section 5.2 that "their wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" due to parallelization is plausible but would be stronger if supported by data.
    *   **Suggestion:** Reporting the actual mean wall-clock time for one full run of SGLD and SGRRLD in the matrix factorization experiments would add quantitative evidence to this claim and strengthen the empirical results.

### **Decision**

This is a high-quality paper with a novel idea, strong theoretical backing, and convincing empirical results. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a significant and practical improvement to the state-of-the-art in scalable MCMC. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate mostly to clarifying the computational trade-offs and experimental details, which can be addressed in a revision. The work is original, technically sound, and its potential impact on the field is high. I recommend acceptance.
