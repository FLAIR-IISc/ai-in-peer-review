Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, primarily SGLD, to reduce discretization bias without resorting to higher-order integrators. The method runs two coupled SGLD chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and combines their time-averaged estimators in the RR fashion, while using correlated Brownian increments (ideally perfectly correlated per the duplication construction) to control variance. The authors provide a CLT and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds showing improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC: with constant stepsizes, bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal finite-time rates K^{-2/3} for bias and K^{-4/5} for MSE (vs K^{-1/2} and K^{-2/3} for SGLD). With decreasing stepsizes γk ∝ k^{-α}, the optimal asymptotic MSE rate is O(K^{-2/5}) (α=1/5), again improving over SGLD’s O(K^{-1/3}). Empirically, the method improves estimation accuracy on a Gaussian model and on large-scale matrix factorization, and extends to SGHMC, matching high-order integrators’ accuracy.

This draws on classical RR for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) and recent SG-MCMC analyses (Teh et al., Chen et al.). To my knowledge, RR has not been applied to SG-MCMC before; the variance control via correlated Brownian increments builds directly on the duplicated diffusion framework.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are technically plausible and well-aligned with the SDE RR literature. The requirement to solve the Poisson equation and perform a 7th-order Taylor expansion (g ∈ C9) is strong but standard in this line of work. The paper clearly identifies the optimal coupling (Σ = I) and shows that, asymptotically, Brownian correlation drives the variance term, whereas correlation of stochastic gradients is less critical for the CLT.
- Assumptions: Some important conditions are deferred to the supplement (ergodicity, smoothness, growth conditions, moment bounds for the stochastic gradients, and the exact meaning of “i.i.d. unbiased gradient estimators with common distribution L”). In practice, ∇Ũ(θ) is unbiased conditional on θ but its distribution depends on θ; clarifying that the analysis uses conditional unbiasedness with bounded moments would improve rigor and readability in the main text. The dependence of constants on gradient-noise moments is noted, which is good.
- Algorithmic details: The estimator and coupling are described clearly, but the pseudocode is in the supplement. It would help to state explicitly whether using the same minibatch indices across chains affects variance (it likely helps in finite samples, even if asymptotically the Brownian term dominates).
- Experiments: The synthetic Gaussian setup convincingly validates rate predictions (bias vs γ, MSE vs γ, and optimal α). The SGHMC extension is a useful sanity check. The matrix factorization results show consistent RMSE/time improvements with parallel execution. A caveat is that fairness relies on running two chains in parallel; without parallelism, SGRRLD incurs ≈2× compute per unit time horizon. Reporting results both at equal wall-clock and equal total gradient evaluations would strengthen claims. More diverse models (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) would help demonstrate robustness.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with clear motivation and positioning relative to higher-order SG-MCMC. However:
- Several key assumptions and the algorithm pseudocode are only in the supplement; summarizing the most important conditions (ergodicity, smoothness, conditional unbiasedness) in the main text would aid the reader.
- The notation “i.i.d. unbiased estimators with common distribution L” is potentially misleading; it would be better to emphasize conditional unbiasedness given θ and independence of minibatch selection across iterations.
- A short discussion on whether one should reuse the same minibatch across the two chains (and any empirical ablation) would be valuable.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is, to my knowledge, novel, and the analysis that shows how to couple the Brownian increments to minimize asymptotic variance in the presence of stochastic gradient noise is a nontrivial contribution. The connection to multi-level Monte Carlo and duplicated diffusions is apt and appropriately cited. The extension to SGHMC is straightforward but useful.

Significance
The contribution is potentially impactful:
- It offers a simple wrapper that can improve the finite-time and asymptotic accuracy of a broad family of SG-MCMC methods while retaining first-order integrators.
- It leverages parallelism naturally: the two chains can be run concurrently with minimal synchronization overhead.
- The theoretical rates are improved and the empirical results are promising on sizeable datasets.
Limitations include reliance on strong smoothness assumptions for the theory, and the fact that benefits hinge on parallel resources; otherwise, total gradient cost increases. Demonstrations on more modern Bayesian models would further enhance significance.

Pros
- Clear, principled idea with strong theoretical backing (CLT and non-asymptotic bounds).
- Novel adaptation of RR to SG-MCMC with optimal Brownian coupling.
- Generic and parallel-friendly; extends beyond SGLD (shown for SGHMC).
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory; improved RMSE/time on large datasets.

Cons
- Key assumptions and algorithm details are deferred to the supplement; “i.i.d. gradient estimators” phrasing is confusing.
- Empirical comparisons depend on parallelization; missing a compute-equal baseline (e.g., equal total gradient evaluations).
- Limited diversity of applications; no classification/logistic regression or deep models.
- No ablation on minibatch sharing/correlation across the two chains.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please clarify in the main text the precise conditions on the gradient estimators (conditional unbiasedness, bounded conditional variance/moments) and summarize the ergodicity/smoothness assumptions.
- Report an additional comparison at fixed total gradient computations to complement the wall-clock results.
- Discuss and, if possible, empirically test whether sharing the same minibatch indices across the two chains further reduces variance.
- Provide pseudocode in the main paper; specify SGHMC-s hyperparameters.
- Comment on applicability when U is not highly smooth (e.g., piecewise-linear networks).

Overall assessment
This is a solid, technically careful paper with a novel and useful idea that improves SG-MCMC accuracy through a simple, parallelizable extrapolation. Despite some presentation gaps and limited empirical breadth, the contribution meets NeurIPS standards on quality, originality, clarity (with minor fixes), and significance. I recommend acceptance (weak accept to accept), contingent on clarifying assumptions and adding a compute-equal comparison.
