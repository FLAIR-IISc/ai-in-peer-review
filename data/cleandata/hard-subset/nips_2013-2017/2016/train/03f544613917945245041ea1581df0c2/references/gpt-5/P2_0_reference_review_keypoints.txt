- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two coupled chains (γ and γ/2) and forming estimator 2π̂γ/2 − π̂γ to cancel leading discretization bias.  
- With MLMC-style coupling of Gaussian noise, SGRRLD achieves bias O(γ2) and variance O(γ4) versus SGLD’s O(γ) and O(γ2), and yields CLTs plus non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds.  
- Optimizing finite-time bounds gives an MSE rate O(K−4/5) with constant stepsize, improving on first-order SG-MCMC’s O(K−2/3).  
- Empirically improves over SGLD on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens), and shows SGHMC+RR comparable to higher-order integrators at larger stepsizes.  
- Theory is well-adapted: provides CLT, non-asymptotic bounds, and shows optimal variance coupling by sharing the Brownian path across chains.  
- Analysis relies on standard but strong smoothness/ergodicity/Poisson-equation and gradient-moment assumptions; both chains must use gradient estimators with the same law.  
- Sketches and derivations are plausible; full proofs are in the supplement.  
- There is a likely indexing typo in Eq. (5) (fine update uses wrong θ index) that should be corrected.  
- Synthetic experiments validate predicted bias/MSE scalings; large-scale experiments demonstrate wall-clock gains via parallelism.  
- Experimental comparisons could be broadened to include control variates and higher-order SGLD integrators beyond SGHMC-s.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; the role and optimality of correlated Brownian increments are well motivated.  
- Minor clarity fixes: correct Eq. (5) indexing, state whether minibatch indices are shared in the two fine steps, and include pseudocode in main text.  
- Novelty: first application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC and first to show first-order integrators can match higher-order accuracy via RR coupling.  
- Significance: conceptually simple, broadly applicable, and practically attractive with parallel resources; improves rates and shows convincing empirical gains.  
- Practical limitations: doubles per-iteration computation on a single device, requires bookkeeping/coupling and same-gradient-law constraint, and theory assumes smoothness that may not hold universally.  
- Pros: simple general idea, strong theoretical backing, solid variance coupling argument, CLT and non-asymptotic bounds, and scalable empirical validation.  
- Cons: strong theoretical assumptions, narrower empirical comparisons, doubled cost without parallelism, and minor typos/implementation details hidden in supplement.  
- Suggestions: clarify minibatch-sharing, compare directly to higher-order SGLD, discuss robustness to non-globally-smooth U, provide main-paper pseudocode and release code.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — principled, novel, theoretically sound, and empirically promising with minor clarifications and corrections needed.
