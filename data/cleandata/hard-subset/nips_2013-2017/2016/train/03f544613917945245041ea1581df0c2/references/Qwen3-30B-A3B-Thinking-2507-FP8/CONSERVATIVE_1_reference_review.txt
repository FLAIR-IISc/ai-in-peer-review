

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a novel SG-MCMC algorithm called SGRRLD that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors claim their method achieves a MSE convergence rate of O(K^-4/5) compared to SGLD's O(K^-2/3), which they argue is superior to methods using higher-order integrators.

The paper builds on established work in SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (Talay & Tubaro, 1990). The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with the authors providing central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bounds for their estimator. The derivation of the O(K^-4/5) convergence rate appears mathematically correct under the stated assumptions.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's practical relevance and the strength of its validation. While the theoretical claims are interesting, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that this method offers meaningful advantages in real-world applications. The experiments are limited to a synthetic Gaussian model and one matrix factorization task, which is insufficient to establish the method's broad utility.

The most concerning omission is the lack of thorough comparison with established methods on diverse datasets. The paper claims "significant performance improvements" on matrix factorization, but the reported speedups (e.g., SGRRLD requiring "≈200 seconds for achieving the accuracy that SGLD provides after ≈400 seconds") are modest. For large-scale applications where SG-MCMC is typically used, such a 2x speedup may not be sufficient to justify the additional complexity of implementing and maintaining two parallel chains.

The paper also fails to address a critical practical concern: the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel. While the authors claim "wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" due to parallelization, in practice, this assumes perfect parallelization efficiency with no communication overhead. For many distributed systems, achieving this ideal parallelization is challenging, and the actual speedup might be substantially less than claimed. The paper provides no empirical evidence of parallelization efficiency or scalability to larger distributed systems.

Furthermore, the paper's claim that "the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is potentially misleading. The authors' theoretical analysis assumes that the two chains use the same mini-batch size and correlated Brownian motion, which may not be straightforward to implement in practice. The paper does not provide evidence that this implementation detail is trivial or that it can be easily applied to other SG-MCMC methods as claimed.

The experiments on the matrix factorization task are particularly weak. The authors use only three datasets (ML-1M, ML-10M, ML-20M) and a fixed rank of P=10. For real-world recommendation systems, the optimal rank is often determined through hyperparameter tuning, which the paper does not address. The claimed improvements in RMSE appear marginal (e.g., Figure 5 shows only a small reduction in RMSE), and without showing results for different ranks or more complex models, it's difficult to assess the method's true utility.

The paper also lacks a clear discussion of when SGRRLD would be preferable to existing methods. The theoretical advantage of O(K^-4/5) versus O(K^-2/3) is asymptotic, but the practical benefits depend heavily on the specific problem, dataset size, and implementation details. The authors do not provide a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy improvements.

While the theoretical contribution is novel and mathematically sound, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that this method offers substantial practical advantages over existing SG-MCMC approaches. The experimental validation is insufficiently extensive, the claimed benefits are modest in practical terms, and the paper does not adequately address implementation challenges or computational overhead.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches without thorough validation, I believe this paper falls short of the rigorous standards expected at NIPS. The theoretical novelty is interesting but not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence demonstrating significant practical improvements over existing methods. The paper would benefit from more extensive experiments on diverse datasets, a thorough analysis of computational overhead, and a clearer discussion of when SGRRLD would be preferable to simpler alternatives.

Strengths: Solid theoretical foundation, clear mathematical presentation, good discussion of related work.

Weaknesses: Insufficient experimental validation, lack of practical significance demonstration, failure to address implementation challenges, modest claimed improvements.

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the claimed advantages do not appear sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance at NIPS given the current state of the field.
