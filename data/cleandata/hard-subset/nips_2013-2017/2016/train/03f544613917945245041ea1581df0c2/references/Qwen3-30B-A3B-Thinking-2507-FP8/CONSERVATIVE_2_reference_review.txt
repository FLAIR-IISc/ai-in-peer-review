

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a novel MCMC method called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine their outputs via RR extrapolation. The authors claim theoretical improvements in bias (O(γ²) vs SGLD's O(γ)) and MSE convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs SGLD's O(K⁻²/³)), while maintaining parallelizability.

**Strengths:**
The paper demonstrates solid theoretical foundations, with rigorous proofs of central limit theorems and convergence rates (Theorems 1 and 2). The connection between RR extrapolation from numerical analysis and MCMC is conceptually interesting and well-motivated. The experimental validation on synthetic Gaussian models and matrix factorization tasks provides some empirical support for the theoretical claims. The paper also clearly articulates the method's parallel nature, which is a practical advantage for distributed systems.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the strong theoretical claims, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in experimental validation and practical assessment:

1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The experiments only compare SGRRLD against SGLD and SGHMC, but not against methods that explicitly use higher-order integrators (e.g., the "SGHMC-s" method mentioned in Section 5.1 but not properly evaluated). The claim that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators" is not adequately supported by experiments. The authors only compare against SGHMC with Euler discretization, not against the higher-order integrator methods that are theoretically supposed to match SGRRLD's performance.

2. **Lack of Comprehensive Real-World Validation:** The matrix factorization experiments are limited to three standard movie recommendation datasets, with no comparison against state-of-the-art Bayesian matrix factorization methods or other scalable MCMC approaches. The paper doesn't demonstrate whether the claimed improvements translate to more challenging, high-dimensional applications.

3. **Practical Cost Analysis:** The paper claims SGRRLD achieves better performance "in shorter time" but provides no detailed analysis of the actual computational overhead. Running two chains in parallel (with the same mini-batch size) doubles the memory requirements and may introduce communication overhead in distributed settings. The paper doesn't quantify whether the theoretical gains outweigh these practical costs.

4. **Theoretical-Experimental Disconnect:** The theoretical analysis assumes "mild assumptions" that may not hold in practice, but the experiments don't validate whether these assumptions are reasonable for the real-world applications tested. For example, the Gaussian model experiments use a simple posterior with known analytical form, which doesn't represent the complex posteriors typically encountered in real Bayesian machine learning.

5. **Overstated Claims:** The paper states that "by only using a first-order numerical integrator, the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators." This is a strong claim that isn't sufficiently substantiated by the experiments, which only show improvements over SGLD (a first-order method), not against higher-order methods.

**Methodological Concerns:**
As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without thorough validation, I'm concerned that the paper overemphasizes theoretical improvements while underestimating practical implementation challenges. The paper makes significant claims about performance gains but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these gains are meaningful in real-world scenarios. The experimental design lacks comparisons with relevant baselines that would properly test the core claim about matching higher-order methods' performance.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper fails to provide the thorough empirical validation required to support its strong claims. The experimental section is insufficiently comprehensive to demonstrate that the method provides practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper makes a novel theoretical contribution but doesn't adequately establish its significance for real-world Bayesian inference problems. The authors would need to conduct more extensive experiments comparing against both first-order and higher-order MCMC methods across multiple challenging benchmarks before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS. The current evidence doesn't justify the theoretical claims with respect to practical impact.
