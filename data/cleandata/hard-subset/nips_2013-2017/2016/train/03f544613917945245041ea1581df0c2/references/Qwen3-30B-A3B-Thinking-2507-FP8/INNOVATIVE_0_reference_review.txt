

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel approach to improving Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The key innovation is SGRRLD (Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics), which runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combines them using RR extrapolation. The authors show theoretically that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), representing a significant theoretical improvement. The paper also demonstrates that this approach can achieve accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators without their computational complexity, while maintaining parallelizability.

The paper makes a bold and novel contribution by applying RR extrapolation, a technique from numerical analysis, to the SG-MCMC framework. This is a creative cross-disciplinary idea that hasn't been explored in previous SG-MCMC literature. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with both asymptotic (central limit theorem) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) results. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGLD, SGHMC) and from standard RR applications to SDEs. The experimental results on synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization for movie recommendations) support the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in accuracy with comparable computation time.

The paper's strengths lie in its bold approach to a fundamental limitation of current SG-MCMC methods (bias). The RR extrapolation technique is elegantly applied to the SG-MCMC framework, and the authors successfully demonstrate its generality by applying it to SGHMC as well (SGRRHMC). The theoretical results are significant, as they show that a first-order method can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods with a simple modification. The paper also acknowledges the practical advantage of parallelizability, which is crucial for large-scale applications.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The paper doesn't fully address the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel (though it does note they can run independently)
2. The experiments, while well-designed, are somewhat limited to one real-world application (matrix factorization)
3. The optimal choice of step size parameters is theoretically derived but not thoroughly tested across a broader range of settings

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over minor methodological imperfections, I find these weaknesses negligible compared to the paper's significant contribution. The core idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is both novel and impactful, addressing a key limitation in the field with a relatively simple yet powerful technique.

The paper significantly advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference. It's rare to see such a clean theoretical improvement that also translates to practical gains. The authors successfully demonstrate how a technique from numerical analysis can be fruitfully applied to a major machine learning problem, creating a new direction for future research. The approach is general enough to apply to other SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD, which greatly increases its significance.

In comparison to recent NIPS papers on SG-MCMC (e.g., Chen et al. 2015 on higher-order integrators), this work is substantially more novel. While Chen et al. developed higher-order integrators, this paper achieves similar accuracy with a first-order method through a clever extrapolation technique. This represents a significant conceptual leap rather than just an incremental improvement.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a strong case for its contribution. The theoretical results are rigorously derived, and the experiments effectively validate the claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge both the strengths and limitations of their approach.

This is a highly novel contribution that advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference. The core idea is bold, the theoretical results are significant, and the experimental validation is solid. The paper's approach to reducing bias while maintaining parallelizability addresses a critical challenge in large-scale Bayesian methods.

I recommend acceptance. The novelty and significance of this work outweigh the minor methodological concerns, and it represents a meaningful advance in the field that will likely influence future research on scalable MCMC methods.
