Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, specifically to SGLD (and briefly to SGHMC). The method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combines their ergodic averages via RR to cancel the O(γ) weak-discretization bias, while using correlated Gaussian noise to control variance. The authors prove a CLT under decreasing steps, derive non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds for fixed/decreasing steps, and argue that with first-order integrators SGRRLD attains bias O(γ^2), variance O(γ^4), and finite-time MSE rate O(K^−4/5), improving over standard SGLD’s O(K^−2/3). Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model, a brief SGHMC variant, and large-scale Gaussian matrix factorization show reduced bias/MSE and better test RMSE at matched wall-clock.

The idea is a natural extension of RR/multilevel techniques for SDEs and invariant measures (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) to SG-MCMC. It relates to high-order SG-MCMC integrators (e.g., Chen et al. 2015) but aims to achieve comparable accuracy with coupled first-order discretizations.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, generic, and easy to implement given two chains; parallel-friendly design is attractive in distributed settings.
- Nontrivial theory: CLT with discussion of optimal noise coupling, and finite-time bias/MSE bounds that match and improve known SGLD rates. The K^−4/5 rate with optimal decay seems consistent with the RR cancellation idea.
- Empirical results broadly align with claims on synthetic Gaussian and (Gaussian) matrix factorization; the SGHMC variant suggests the approach may transfer beyond Langevin.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions are strong and may limit practical relevance. The CLT relies on high smoothness (solution of a Poisson equation with g ∈ C^9) and rather technical weak error/ergodicity conditions; many modern Bayesian posteriors (non-smooth priors/likelihoods, heavy tails, nonconvex landscapes) do not satisfy these. It is unclear how robust the results are outside these regimes.
- Variance control hinges on perfectly correlated Brownian increments across levels. In SG-MCMC, gradient noise from subsampling is typically non-negligible; the claim that Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise is asserted but not convincingly demonstrated empirically. There is no ablation on the effect of noise coupling or mini-batch coupling on variance.
- Computational cost and practicality. The method doubles gradient computations; the argument that wall-clock remains unchanged presumes ideal parallel resources and negligible synchronization/IO overhead. Many users operate on a single GPU/CPU or are memory/IO bound; the paper does not quantify resource usage or throughput differences, nor discuss scenarios where parallelism is constrained.
- Experimental scope is narrow. All real-data experiments use Gaussian models (linear Gaussian, Gaussian MF), which align well with the theory and are relatively benign. There are no results on common non-Gaussian models (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, Poisson factorization) or more challenging posteriors (e.g., hierarchical models, BNNs). The SGHMC comparison to higher-order integrators is limited to one synthetic setting; stronger baselines (full-batch MALA/HMC for small N, or recent SG-MCMC variants with control variates/preconditioning) are missing.
- Evaluation lacks diagnostic depth. There are no mixing diagnostics, effective sample size, or posterior calibration checks; performance is often summarized via test RMSE, which does not disentangle bias vs variance of the sampler. While 100-run averages are reported, error bars are not shown.
- Clarity/notation issues. Equation (5) appears to use ∇Ũ at θ(γ/2)2k+1 on the right-hand side of the first update; this seems like a typo (gradient should be evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k). The estimator in (6) implicitly implements 2πγ/2 − πγ via two evaluations at γ/2; this is fine but could be explained more explicitly. Details on how mini-batches are chosen/correlated across levels (needed so both chains share the same L) are not sufficiently specified; theory warns mismatch can reintroduce O(γ) bias, but practical guidance is thin.
- Hyperparameter guidance is fragile. The practical recipe requires selecting γ and decay exponents; several experiments tune constants using prior runs. It is unclear how robust the method is under misspecification, or how to set γ, decay, and burn-in without oracle knowledge.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please correct Eq. (5) and clarify the gradient evaluation points. Provide a pseudocode in the main text or ensure the supplement is precise.
- Provide an ablation on:
  - Noise coupling: Σ = I vs Σ = 0 (independent noise), and impact of sharing vs not sharing mini-batches across levels.
  - Resource usage: wall-clock vs FLOPs vs memory on single-device and multi-device setups.
- Add experiments on non-Gaussian models (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression on UCI datasets) and a small-N setting with ground-truth MCMC (e.g., full HMC/MALA) to validate posterior accuracy.
- Compare against stronger baselines: SGHMC with higher-order integrators (beyond a single plot), preconditioned SGLD/thermostats, and control-variate SG-MCMC.
- Discuss robustness when the assumptions (smoothness, Lipschitzness) are relaxed, and the relative magnitude of subsampling noise vs Brownian noise in practice.

Pros
- Solid and relevant idea (RR extrapolation) adapted to SG-MCMC, with theoretical analysis and some empirical gains.
- Potentially useful in parallel/distributed settings.
- Improves finite-time rates without designing bespoke high-order integrators.

Cons
- Heavy assumptions and limited empirical validation beyond Gaussian settings.
- Practical overhead and sensitivity not fully addressed; benefits may vanish without ideal parallelism and careful coupling.
- Missing comparisons and diagnostics that would convince practitioners of robustness and utility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The contribution is promising and technically interesting, but the current paper does not provide sufficient empirical breadth, practical guidance, or robustness evidence to justify publication at NIPS given the added complexity and assumptions. Strengthening the experiments (broader models, stronger baselines, ablations) and clarifying implementation details/typos would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: High.
