**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The primary motivation is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators used by popular methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to the SGLD algorithm. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order bias term. To manage the variance that this extrapolation could introduce, the authors propose using correlated noise between the two chains. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the Mean Squared Error (MSE), O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD. The method is validated on a linear Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem, showing empirical improvements over SGLD.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical contributions, including a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2), are the main strength of the work. The derivations appear rigorous and clearly demonstrate the theoretical advantage of the proposed method in terms of convergence rates. The analysis of variance reduction through correlated noise is a methodologically important and well-handled aspect.

However, the experimental validation, while supportive, is not as comprehensive as one might hope. The linear Gaussian model is a useful sanity check but is an overly simplistic setting (convex, unimodal posterior) that may not reflect the challenges of many real-world Bayesian inference problems. The matrix factorization experiment is a more compelling large-scale application. Nevertheless, the robustness of the method on problems with more complex posterior geometries (e.g., multi-modal or highly non-convex, as found in Bayesian neural networks) remains unproven. The technical assumptions required for the theorems are deferred to the supplementary material, which makes it difficult to assess their restrictiveness compared to standard assumptions for SGLD.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation for reducing bias is well-established, and the explanation of how Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is applied in the SG-MCMC context is easy to follow. The algorithm itself is presented unambiguously.

**Originality:** The work's originality lies in the novel combination of two established techniques: SG-MCMC and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. While neither component is new, their application together in this domain, along with the specific analysis of variance control for the stochastic gradient setting, constitutes a novel contribution. This work presents an alternative to using higher-order integrators for bias reduction, another established approach in the literature. The paper acknowledges this and makes a comparison, which is good practice.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant. Bias reduction is a critical issue in SG-MCMC, and achieving a higher convergence rate is a valuable theoretical and practical goal. The fact that the algorithm is inherently parallelizable is a major practical advantage, as it can leverage modern multi-core architectures effectively. The general framework also seems applicable to other SG-MCMC variants, as demonstrated with SGHMC, which increases its potential impact.

A significant weakness, however, is the discussion of computational cost. The SGRRLD algorithm requires approximately three times the number of gradient evaluations per effective time step compared to a single SGLD chain (one for the γ-chain and two for the γ/2-chain). The experiments compare the methods based on wall-clock time, arguing that parallelization makes them comparable. While this is a valid perspective for users with available parallel resources, it obscures the fundamental increase in computational effort. A more standard and transparent comparison in the stochastic optimization literature would be against a baseline given the same total budget of gradient computations. For instance, comparing SGRRLD at K iterations against SGLD at 3K iterations would provide a clearer picture of the trade-off between statistical efficiency and computational cost. The current experiments show SGRRLD is faster in wall-clock time, but it's unclear if it's more efficient in terms of total computation. This makes it difficult to assess whether the improved accuracy is worth the substantial increase in required calculations, especially in resource-constrained environments (e.g., single-core systems).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and well-founded method for bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
*   The theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating a superior rate of convergence for the MSE compared to standard first-order methods.
*   The proposed algorithm is inherently parallel, making it well-suited for modern hardware and potentially leading to faster wall-clock times, as demonstrated in experiments.
*   The approach is general and can be extended to other SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method introduces a substantial computational overhead (~3x gradient evaluations), and the experimental comparison does not adequately control for this. The reliance on parallelization to justify the cost may not be universally applicable.
*   The experimental validation is limited to relatively well-behaved models. The performance on more challenging, non-convex posterior distributions is not explored, leaving the method's general applicability in question.
*   The contribution, while solid, can be viewed as an incremental improvement that combines existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach to sampling. The practical advantage over other established bias-reduction techniques, like higher-order integrators, is not conclusively demonstrated.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a solid and interesting contribution. The theoretical analysis is the high point, clearly showing the potential of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for improving SG-MCMC. However, I am skeptical about the practical trade-offs. The high computational cost is a major concern, and the experimental evaluation does not fully convince me of the method's superiority once this cost is properly accounted for. While the parallel nature is a strong selling point, the field would benefit from a more direct comparison based on total gradient evaluations to understand if the statistical gains truly outweigh the computational burden. The work is promising, but its claims of improved efficiency need to be presented with more nuance regarding the definition of "cost."
