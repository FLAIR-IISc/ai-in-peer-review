

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. Building on the SGLD framework [1] and RR extrapolation for SDEs [21, 23], the authors theoretically analyze SGRRLD, showing it achieves bias O(γ²) and variance O(γ⁴) compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²). The paper demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), with empirical validation on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

## Strengths
1. **Strong theoretical contribution**: The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis with central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) that clearly demonstrate improved rates over existing SG-MCMC methods. The analysis is technically sound and well-motivated.

2. **Novel application of RR extrapolation**: The paper successfully bridges numerical analysis (RR extrapolation) and MCMC, creating a meaningful new approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC. The authors convincingly show that RR extrapolation can achieve the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators with first-order methods.

3. **Thorough empirical validation**: The experiments on both synthetic data (to verify theoretical claims) and real-world matrix factorization tasks (to demonstrate practical utility) are well-designed. The results consistently show SGRRLD outperforming SGLD in terms of MSE and computation time, with clear evidence of the claimed improvements.

4. **Practical relevance**: The paper highlights SGRRLD's parallel nature (two chains run independently), making it well-suited for distributed architectures. The implementation details for large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate practical scalability.

## Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical assumptions require more detail**: The paper states results hold "under appropriate conditions" without specifying what these conditions are. For a NIPS paper, providing concrete assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz conditions on gradients, smoothness of potential) would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Limited comparison with related bias-reduction techniques**: The paper doesn't sufficiently contrast with other bias-reduction approaches in SG-MCMC. For instance, how does SGRRLD compare to methods like the higher-order integrators in [10] that also aim to reduce bias? This would better contextualize the novelty.

3. **Insufficient detail on parallel implementation**: While the paper mentions parallel execution, it lacks concrete details about parallel overhead, synchronization mechanisms, and scalability with more processors. For a paper claiming parallel advantages, this is a significant omission.

4. **Empirical analysis could be deeper**: The matrix factorization experiments show improved performance but don't provide sufficient analysis of how the improvement scales with dataset size or model complexity. More detailed computational complexity analysis would strengthen the practical claims.

## Recommendation
This is a technically strong paper with a meaningful contribution to SG-MCMC methodology. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the empirical results support the claims. The paper makes a clear contribution by showing how RR extrapolation can achieve higher-order convergence rates with first-order methods, which is both theoretically interesting and practically valuable.

The main weaknesses relate to insufficient detail on theoretical assumptions and parallel implementation, as well as limited comparison with alternative bias-reduction techniques. These could be addressed with minor revisions.

**Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions)**

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Providing concrete assumptions for the theoretical results
2. Adding a brief comparison with other bias-reduction techniques in SG-MCMC
3. Including more details about the parallel implementation and overhead
4. Expanding the empirical analysis of computational complexity

These revisions would make the paper even more valuable to the NIPS community while maintaining its strong theoretical and empirical contributions. The work represents a significant advance in understanding how to improve SG-MCMC methods through numerical techniques, and the proposed approach is both theoretically sound and practically implementable.
