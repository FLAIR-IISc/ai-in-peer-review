This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the discretization bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the SGLD algorithm. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes, γ and γ/2, where the second chain takes two steps for each single step of the first. A new estimator is formed by a linear combination of the outputs of these two chains, which is designed to cancel the leading O(γ) bias term. This work is related to the literature on SG-MCMC methods that seek to improve upon the original SGLD, particularly those that address the discretization bias. While previous work, such as Chen et al. (2015), has focused on using higher-order integrators to reduce bias, this paper presents an alternative approach that achieves similar theoretical accuracy improvements while relying only on the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama integrator. The application of RR extrapolation to SDEs is known in the numerical analysis literature (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), but its application and rigorous analysis in the SG-MCMC context, which includes an additional source of noise from data subsampling, is novel.

**Strengths**

1.  **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous analysis, including both a Central Limit Theorem for the asymptotic regime and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean-Squared-Error (MSE). The analysis shows that SGRRLD reduces the bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) and improves the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵), where K is the number of iterations. This rate matches that of methods using more complex second-order integrators. The authors carefully consider the practical issue of variance inflation from combining two chains and address it by using correlated noise, proving that full correlation is optimal for minimizing asymptotic variance. The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong evidence for the theoretical claims. The synthetic Gaussian model experiments allow for precise quantification of bias and MSE, directly verifying the predicted dependencies on the step size (γ) and iteration count (K). The large-scale matrix factorization experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the method, showing improved performance in terms of wall-clock time.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, and the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation is concisely explained. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is presented formally and unambiguously. The main theoretical results are stated clearly in Theorems 1 and 2, and their implications for optimal convergence rates are discussed effectively. The experimental sections are well-organized, with the setup and results described in sufficient detail. The figures are informative and support the paper's conclusions.

3.  **Originality:** The primary contribution—applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting—is novel. While the constituent components (SGLD, RR extrapolation) are established, their combination and the corresponding theoretical analysis for a setting with both discretization and gradient noise is a new and insightful contribution. This work provides a new path to achieving higher-order accuracy in SG-MCMC that is conceptually different from, and arguably simpler to implement than, deriving and using higher-order numerical integrators. The demonstration that the technique can be generalized to other SG-MCMC methods, such as SGHMC, further underscores the originality and breadth of the core idea.

4.  **Significance:** The paper addresses the important problem of improving the accuracy and efficiency of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a practical and impactful solution. Its main advantage is its inherent parallelism; the two required chains can be run on separate cores, leading to significant improvements in wall-clock time versus accuracy, as demonstrated on the MovieLens datasets. This makes the method highly attractive for practitioners working with large-scale models. By achieving the accuracy of second-order methods with a simple first-order integrator, it lowers the implementation barrier for obtaining higher-accuracy SG-MCMC samplers. The generality of the RR approach suggests it could become a standard technique for improving a wide range of existing and future SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The method's primary trade-off is its computational cost. SGRRLD requires approximately double the computational resources (e.g., two processor cores, or twice the sequential runtime) of a standard SGLD sampler. While the experiments effectively show that this cost is justified by the faster convergence in wall-clock time when parallelized, this requirement might be a limitation in resource-constrained environments where parallel execution is not an option.

2.  The full technical conditions for the theorems and some implementation details (e.g., pseudo-code) are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice for conference papers, including a concise algorithm box in the main text would have improved its self-containment and readability for readers without immediate access to the supplement.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a novel and elegant method for bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
    *   The theoretical analysis is sound and demonstrates superior convergence rates compared to standard first-order methods.
    *   The proposed method is simple to implement and inherently parallelizable, offering significant practical benefits in terms of wall-clock time efficiency.
    *   The claims are convincingly validated through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data.
    *   The work is of high quality, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem in the field.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The novelty can be viewed as a clever combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new concept.
    *   The method has a higher computational footprint than its baseline competitor (SGLD), requiring double the resources.

Overall, the strengths of this paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
