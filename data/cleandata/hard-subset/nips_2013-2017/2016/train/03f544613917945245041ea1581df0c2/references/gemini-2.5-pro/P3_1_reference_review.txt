### Summary

This paper proposes a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the discretization bias of existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. A key technical contribution is the use of correlated Brownian motions for the two chains, which is shown to be crucial for controlling the variance of the resulting estimator.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. They prove a central limit theorem and derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). These results show that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of first-order methods like SGLD. The paper demonstrates that this approach allows a first-order integrator to achieve the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. The method's effectiveness is validated through experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model, where theoretical predictions are confirmed, and on a large-scale matrix factorization task, where SGRRLD outperforms SGLD in wall-clock time.

### Review

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. It cleverly combines a classic numerical analysis technique with modern scalable Bayesian inference methods, supported by solid theory and compelling experiments.

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon established proof techniques in the SDE and SG-MCMC literature (e.g., using Poisson equations and Taylor expansions). The main claims are well-supported by this analysis.

*   **Theorem 1 (CLT):** This is a strong asymptotic result. The characterization of the limiting distribution and the finding that perfect correlation (Σ = Id) minimizes the asymptotic variance is a key insight that justifies the algorithmic design.
*   **Theorem 2 (Non-asymptotic Bounds):** These bounds provide a clear picture of the trade-off between discretization error (O(γ⁴)) and Monte Carlo error (O(1/Kγ)). The derivation of the O(K⁻⁴/⁵) optimal MSE rate is a major theoretical result of the paper and represents a substantial improvement.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The use of a linear Gaussian model with a known posterior is an ideal setting to precisely verify the theoretical claims about bias and MSE reduction, and the results (e.g., Figure 2) align perfectly with the theory. The matrix factorization experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the method on a real-world, large-scale problem.

**Clarity: High**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation is presented concisely. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described formally and unambiguously. The theoretical results are stated clearly, and while the full proofs are in the supplement, the proof sketches provide good intuition. The experimental setup and results are easy to follow and effectively illustrate the benefits of the proposed method.

**Originality: High**
The core idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and significant. While RR extrapolation is a known technique for deterministic and stochastic differential equations (as the authors correctly cite, e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), its application and analysis in the *stochastic gradient* setting is new and non-trivial. The key challenge in the SG-MCMC setting is managing the two sources of error: discretization bias and stochastic gradient noise. The paper’s main original contributions are:
1.  The formulation of the RR scheme for SG-MCMC.
2.  The crucial insight to use correlated noise to control the variance, which might otherwise overwhelm the gains from bias reduction.
3.  The complete theoretical analysis (CLT and non-asymptotic bounds) that quantifies the improvements in this new setting.

This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on reducing SGLD bias, which has primarily focused on developing bespoke higher-order integrators [10]. This paper presents a more general, "black-box" approach to bias reduction that is arguably simpler to implement.

**Significance: High**
The results presented are important and likely to be impactful.
1.  **Improved Accuracy:** The improved convergence rate is a significant theoretical and practical advance. It means that for a given number of iterations, SGRRLD can achieve much higher accuracy than SGLD.
2.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** The algorithm's main cost is a doubling of computation. However, the authors correctly point out that this is perfectly suited for parallel execution. On modern multi-core architectures, SGRRLD can achieve its superior accuracy in roughly the same wall-clock time as SGLD, as convincingly demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiment. This makes it a very practical algorithm.
3.  **Generality:** The authors demonstrate the method's generality by applying it to SGHMC. The principle is indeed applicable to any SG-MCMC method based on a time-discretized SDE, provided the necessary regularity conditions for the error expansion hold. This opens the door for improving a wide range of existing and future SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**
While this is a strong paper, there are a few points worth considering:
1.  **Computational Cost:** The primary weakness is the doubled computational cost (effectively three gradient evaluations are needed to advance the combined system by one γ step). The authors' argument about parallelization is strong and valid, but it is worth explicitly discussing limitations. In scenarios where computation is limited to a single thread or a single GPU stream, SGRRLD would be slower in wall-clock time. How would the method compare to SGLD if both were given the same *computational budget* (i.e., SGLD runs for 3x more iterations) on a single core?
2.  **Memory Cost:** The method requires storing two separate parameter chains, doubling the memory requirement. For very large models where parameters barely fit in memory, this could be a practical issue. This is a minor point but could be acknowledged.
3.  **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on smoothness and other regularity conditions on the potential U and the test function f, which are deferred to the supplement. It would be helpful to briefly state in the main text how restrictive these assumptions are for typical machine learning models, such as deep neural networks with ReLU activations or models with non-smooth priors.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant method for bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
*   Provides strong theoretical results demonstrating significantly improved convergence rates.
*   The use of correlated noise to manage variance is a key technical insight.
*   The algorithm is simple to implement and inherently parallel, making it highly practical.
*   Experiments are thorough, convincing, and validate both the theory and the practical utility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method doubles the computational and memory cost, which could be a limitation in non-parallel or memory-constrained environments.

**Conclusion:**
The arguments for acceptance are overwhelming. This paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. It is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem with an effective and practical solution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
