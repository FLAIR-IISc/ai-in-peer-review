This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) aimed at reducing bias via Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with differing step sizes and combine their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and show empirical improvements on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is meticulously written and attempts a rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed method. The presentation of the mathematical derivations is detailed, and the authors make efforts to connect their work to existing literature on SGLD, Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, and stochastic differential equations. The inclusion of pseudo-code is helpful. The experiments are well-structured and demonstrate improvements over baseline methods on multiple datasets.

However, despite the technical sophistication and apparent improvements, several issues lead me to recommend rejection. My primary concern is a concerning level of detail-oriented flaws coupled with a somewhat incremental nature of the contribution, given the complexity of the presentation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a formal analysis of the proposed algorithm, including convergence rate analysis and a central limit theorem.
*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that SGRRLD outperforms SGLD and SGHMC on several datasets.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel nature of the RR extrapolation is explicitly highlighted and leveraged in the experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Detailed and Dense Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally dense and detailed, making it difficult to follow the key ideas. Many equations and derivations could be simplified or moved to the supplementary material. The constant references to specific theorem numbers and equation numbers within the text feel unnecessarily intrusive.
*   **Incremental Improvement:** While the theoretical and empirical results show improvement over existing methods, the magnitude of the improvement doesn't appear substantial enough to justify the added complexity. The gains appear modest, especially given the increased computational cost of running two chains.
*   **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The experimental setup in the large-scale matrix factorization experiment is not entirely convincing. Specifically, the claim of running chains in "nearly exactly the same" wall-clock time requires justification. While SGRRLD *can* be parallelized, achieving perfect synchronization and identical runtimes is unlikely. A more detailed breakdown of the computational cost is needed.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** Several minor technical issues detract from the overall quality. For instance, the justification for using the same mini-batch size for both chains, while noted as important, feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more rigorous explanation of the consequences of differing mini-batch sizes would be beneficial. The use of Σ in equation (8) is introduced without adequate motivation; a clearer explanation of its role in controlling variance is needed. There are also inconsistencies in notation (e.g., different uses of 'K' for number of iterations and related parameters).
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of practical considerations. For example, how sensitive is the performance of SGRRLD to the choice of step sizes? How does the performance scale with the dimensionality of the problem? How does the choice of the correlation matrix Σ impact performance?
*   **Repetitive Claims:** The statement that SGRRLD achieves the "theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is repeated multiple times but lacks sufficient justification. While the convergence rate is comparable, this doesn’t automatically equate to equivalent accuracy in practice.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Empirical improvements over baseline methods.

**Con:**

*   Incremental improvement that doesn’t justify the added complexity.
*   Overly detailed and dense presentation.
*   Minor technical inconsistencies and lack of practical consideration.
*   Experimental setup requires further justification.



**Overall:**

While the paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution, the combination of the incremental nature of the improvement, the overly dense presentation, and the minor technical flaws lead me to recommend rejection. The paper feels like a polished but ultimately unsatisfying increment to the existing literature. A more focused presentation, a more substantial demonstration of the benefits, and a more thorough discussion of practical considerations would significantly strengthen the work. The paper currently prioritizes meticulous detail over clarity and impact.
