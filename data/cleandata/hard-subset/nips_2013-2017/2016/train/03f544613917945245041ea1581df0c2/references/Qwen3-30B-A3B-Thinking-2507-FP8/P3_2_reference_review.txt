

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors claim that by running two parallel chains with different step sizes and applying RR extrapolation, SGRRLD achieves higher-order convergence rates than standard SG-MCMC methods like SGLD. Specifically, they show that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. The paper builds on previous work by Chen et al. (2015) on higher-order integrators and Talay and Tubaro (1991) on RR extrapolation for SDEs, but extends these ideas specifically to the SG-MCMC setting where gradient estimates are noisy.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for SGRRLD, with Theorems 1 and 2 establishing the asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. The authors correctly identify that the correlation between Brownian increments is crucial for controlling variance, and they provide a clear justification for using perfectly correlated Brownian increments (equation 7).

2. **Practical Parallelism**: The paper correctly identifies that the RR approach naturally lends itself to parallel computation, which is a significant advantage for large-scale applications. The matrix factorization experiments clearly demonstrate this parallel advantage with comparable wall-clock times for SGRRLD versus SGLD.

3. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets support the theoretical claims. The Gaussian model experiments provide clear evidence of bias reduction, and the matrix factorization experiments demonstrate practical efficiency gains.

4. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear sections for introduction, preliminaries, proposed method, convergence analysis, and experiments. The notation is mostly consistent throughout.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to Higher-Order Methods**: The paper's main claim is that "by only using a first-order numerical integrator, the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" (e.g., Chen et al. 2015). However, the paper fails to provide a direct comparison with these higher-order methods in the main experiments. The SGHMC-s comparison in Figure 4 is insufficient, as it doesn't show SGRRLD achieving comparable performance to the higher-order method across different γ values. This is a critical weakness that undermines the main claim of the paper.

2. **Insufficient Connection to Previous RR Work**: While the paper references Lemaire et al. (2015) on RR extrapolation for MCMC, it doesn't adequately explain how the SG-MCMC setting differs from standard MCMC in terms of the RR application. The extension from standard MCMC to SG-MCMC is non-trivial due to the stochastic gradient noise, and the paper should more clearly articulate this distinction.

3. **Algorithm Description Clarity**: The description of the algorithm in equations (4)-(6) is confusing. The relationship between the two chains and their step sizes needs clarification. Specifically, it's unclear why Chain 2 is run twice as often as Chain 1, and why the step sizes for Chain 2 are defined as η₂ₖ₋₁ = η₂ₖ = γₖ/2. A simple example or diagram would greatly improve clarity.

4. **Assumptions and Practical Applicability**: The theoretical results depend on strong assumptions (smoothness of f, U, and L), but the paper doesn't discuss how restrictive these are in practice, especially for complex Bayesian models. The paper also doesn't address how the mini-batch size affects the practical implementation.

5. **Lack of Computational Overhead Analysis**: While the paper claims that the wall-clock times for SGRRLD and SGLD are "nearly exactly the same," it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the parallel efficiency (e.g., speedup, scalability) or how the computational overhead of running two chains compares to running one chain with half the step size.

## Specific Comments

1. **Section 3 (Proposed Method)**: The algorithm description needs significant clarification. For instance, the statement "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice more time than the other one" is ambiguous. A concrete example with specific step size sequences would help readers understand how the two chains are synchronized.

2. **Section 4 (Convergence Analysis)**: The proof sketch for Theorem 1 is quite brief. For a NIPS paper, it would be helpful to include more details on the key steps, particularly regarding how the stochastic gradient noise is handled in the Taylor expansion.

3. **Section 5.1 (Experiments)**: The comparison with SGHMC-s in Figure 4 is promising but insufficient. The paper should directly compare SGRRLD with SGHMC-s across multiple γ values and show that SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy to SGHMC-s without requiring the higher-order integrator.

4. **Figure 2**: The paper states that "for very small γ, the bias and MSE increase" due to the 1/(Kγ) term dominating. However, it doesn't explain why this term is larger for smaller γ, which would help readers understand this trade-off.

5. **Section 6 (Conclusion)**: The claim that "SGRRLD appears to be more efficient than SGLD in terms of computation time" needs quantification. How much faster is SGRRLD? A speedup metric would strengthen the practical significance of the results.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The main novelty is applying RR extrapolation specifically to SG-MCMC, which is a non-trivial extension from standard MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims.

However, the paper has a critical weakness in failing to provide a direct comparison with higher-order integrator methods (e.g., Chen et al. 2015), which is central to the main claim of the paper. Without this comparison, the claim that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" lacks empirical support.

The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. A direct comparison with higher-order methods like SGHMC-s across multiple step sizes
2. A clearer explanation of the algorithm's implementation details
3. A more detailed analysis of the practical computational overhead

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors will address the weaknesses mentioned above in a revision. The paper has significant potential but needs to better substantiate its main claim with direct comparisons to higher-order methods.

## Detailed Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and well-motivated
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported
- The parallel nature of the algorithm is a significant practical advantage
- The experimental results on real-world datasets demonstrate practical efficiency gains

**Con:**
- The paper lacks direct comparison with higher-order integrator methods, which is central to the main claim
- The connection to previous work on RR extrapolation in MCMC is not sufficiently established
- The algorithm description is confusing in places
- The practical overhead of running two chains is not adequately analyzed

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, but it needs to be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above. With revisions to include a direct comparison with higher-order methods and clarify the algorithm description, this would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, it's a good paper but not yet at the level of a top-tier NIPS paper. I recommend weak acceptance.
