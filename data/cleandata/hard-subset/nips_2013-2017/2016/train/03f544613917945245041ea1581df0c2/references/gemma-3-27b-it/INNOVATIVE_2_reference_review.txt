This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) method leveraging the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce bias. The core idea – applying RR to SGLD by running two chains with different step sizes and correlating Brownian motions – is clever and conceptually sound, building upon existing work in numerical SDEs and SG-MCMC. It’s positioned well within the recent surge of interest in scaling MCMC to large datasets. The theoretical analysis, providing convergence guarantees and MSE bounds, is a significant strength. The authors correctly highlight the potential for parallelization and the generality of the approach beyond SGLD, applying it to SGHMC as well.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. While RR is well-established in numerical analysis, its adaptation to stochastic gradient settings hasn’t been explored before, representing a potentially important direction.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates and bounds on bias and MSE. The claim of achieving accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators using only a first-order integrator is compelling.
*   **Potential for Parallelization:** The inherent parallelism of running the two chains in RR is a practical advantage, especially for large-scale applications.
*   **Generality:** The framework extends beyond SGLD to other SG-MCMC algorithms like SGHMC, broadening its applicability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical findings, showing improved accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-executed, they remain relatively limited in scope. The matrix factorization experiment, though valuable, could benefit from comparison with more recent and sophisticated baselines. A more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity (especially the choice of step sizes and correlation matrix Σ) would be beneficial.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The theoretical advantages hinge on carefully choosing step sizes and ensuring correlated Brownian motions. The practical difficulty of tuning these parameters in high-dimensional settings isn't fully addressed. The impact of imperfect correlation between gradient estimators is also not deeply explored.
*   **Clarity on Correlation:** The explanation of the Brownian motion correlation (equation 8) could be more intuitive. While mathematically defined, the implications for practical implementation and performance are not completely clear.
*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper doesn’t explicitly quantify the computational overhead of running two chains. While parallelization mitigates this, it’s important to consider the increased memory requirements and communication costs in a distributed setting.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel idea with strong theoretical underpinnings.
*   The empirical results provide encouraging evidence of its effectiveness.
*   The potential for improving the efficiency of SG-MCMC, a crucial area for Bayesian inference, is significant.
*   The generality of the approach and its inherent parallelism are compelling features.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is somewhat limited.
*   The practical challenges of implementing and tuning the algorithm aren’t fully addressed.
*   The computational overhead isn't thoroughly analyzed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the experiments could be more extensive and the practical considerations could be discussed in greater detail, the theoretical soundness and originality of the idea are compelling.  I believe the benefits of this approach—reduced bias and improved accuracy—outweigh the current limitations.  I lean towards **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should address the reviewer's comments regarding experimental scope and practical implementation details in a future version. The boldness of the approach and the potential for significant impact warrant overlooking minor flaws.
