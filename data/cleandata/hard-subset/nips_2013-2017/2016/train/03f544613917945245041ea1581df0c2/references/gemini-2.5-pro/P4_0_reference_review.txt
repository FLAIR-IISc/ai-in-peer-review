**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The primary goal is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order SG-MCMC methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size sequence {γ_k} and another with a step size sequence {γ_k/2}, where the second chain performs two steps for each single step of the first. The outputs of these two chains are then linearly combined to cancel the leading-order O(γ) bias term of the estimator.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, establishing a central limit theorem and deriving non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). A key theoretical finding is that SGRRLD can achieve an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for standard SGLD. This brings the accuracy of the first-order based SGRRLD in line with methods using more complex higher-order integrators. The analysis also highlights the importance of using correlated noise between the two chains to control the variance of the combined estimator. The paper's claims are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem for movie recommendations, demonstrating empirical improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.

This work builds upon the well-established SG-MCMC framework [1] and incorporates ideas from the numerical analysis literature on SDEs, where RR extrapolation has been successfully applied to algorithms with exact gradients [21, 23]. The main contribution is the adaptation and analysis of this technique for the stochastic gradient setting, offering a new path to high-accuracy scalable Bayesian inference.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is of high technical quality.
*   The theoretical analysis appears sound, providing both asymptotic (Central Limit Theorem) and non-asymptotic (Bias/MSE bounds) results. The derivation of the improved O(K⁻⁴/⁵) MSE rate is a strong theoretical contribution and provides a solid justification for the proposed method.
*   The insight to use correlated Brownian motions to control the variance of the RR estimator is crucial and correctly extends prior work from the ULA setting to the SG-MCMC context.
*   The experiments are well-designed to validate the theoretical claims. The synthetic experiments meticulously verify the derived convergence rates with respect to step size (Fig. 2) and number of iterations (Fig. 3). The large-scale matrix factorization experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the method in a realistic setting.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   The motivation for reducing bias in SG-MCMC is well-established in the introduction.
*   The algorithm is formally presented in Section 3, and the connection to classical RR extrapolation is explained well.
*   The theoretical results in Section 4 are stated precisely, with proof sketches provided in the main text to give intuition.
*   Figures are clear and effectively support the paper's conclusions. The comparison of wall-clock time in the large-scale experiment is particularly effective at demonstrating practical advantage.

**Originality:**
The paper demonstrates a high degree of originality.
*   While RR extrapolation is a known technique, its application, analysis, and empirical validation within the SG-MCMC framework is novel.
*   This work presents a compelling alternative to using higher-order integrators [10] for bias reduction. The proposed approach is arguably simpler to implement, as it only requires running a standard first-order integrator twice, rather than deriving and implementing more complex update rules.
*   The extension of the method to SGHMC, creating SGRRHMC, effectively demonstrates the generality of the core idea.

**Significance:**
The potential impact of this work is significant.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental limitation—discretization bias—of a widely used class of algorithms (SG-MCMC).
*   The substantial improvement in the theoretical convergence rate is of high interest to both researchers and practitioners. For a fixed computational budget, SGRRLD promises more accurate posterior estimates.
*   The inherent parallelism of the algorithm makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures, which is a key consideration for scalability. The results on the MovieLens datasets suggest that this can translate to real-world gains in efficiency.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Computational Cost Trade-off:** The SGRRLD algorithm requires three gradient evaluations per effective "step" (one for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain). While the authors highlight the benefit of parallelization, this requires twice the hardware resources. The current experiments compare performance against wall-clock time, which is favorable for a parallelizable algorithm. A comparison against the total number of gradient computations would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's efficiency. It would be valuable to clarify whether the method is more efficient in terms of total compute, or primarily in wall-clock time when parallel resources are available.
*   **Clarity on Assumptions:** The theoretical results are presented under "appropriate conditions" which are deferred to the supplementary material. For the benefit of the reader, it would be helpful to briefly state the nature of these key assumptions (e.g., strong convexity of the potential, smoothness of the test function, bounded moments of the gradient estimator) in the main paper.
*   **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The experimental comparison with the higher-order integrator SGHMC-s in Figure 4 is useful but could be expanded. The results suggest SGRRHMC is competitive, but a more direct discussion of the trade-offs in terms of implementation complexity, computational cost per step, and stability for larger step sizes would further strengthen the paper's positioning.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and elegant method for reducing bias in SG-MCMC by cleverly applying a classical numerical technique.
2.  Provides strong and significant theoretical results, demonstrating a superior convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs. O(K⁻²/³)) over standard first-order methods.
3.  The method is general, practical, and well-suited for parallel implementation.
4.  Empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data convincingly support the theoretical claims and show practical benefits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The computational overhead (3x gradient evaluations per effective step) is a notable drawback that could be discussed more transparently with respect to total compute vs. wall-clock time.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a clear and impactful contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
