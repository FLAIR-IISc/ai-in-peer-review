**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The key idea is to reduce the discretization bias inherent in first-order integrators like the one used in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The method achieves this by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique. In practice, this involves running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size γ and another that takes two steps of size γ/2 for each step of the first. The final estimator is a linear combination of the estimators from these two chains. To control the variance of the combined estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motion for the two chains.

The main contributions are both theoretical and empirical. Theoretically, the authors prove that SGRRLD reduces the bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) and the MSE from O(γ²) to O(γ⁴) for a constant step size γ. This leads to an improved non-asymptotic MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is significantly better than the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD. Notably, this matches the rate of more complex higher-order integrators while only using the simple Euler-Maruyama scheme. The work is well-situated in the literature, building upon SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and the analysis of its bias (Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016), while cleverly adapting classical results on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) to the stochastic gradient setting. Empirically, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task using MovieLens datasets, showing clear improvements in accuracy and efficiency over SGLD and SGHMC.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant idea. It addresses a fundamental limitation (discretization bias) of widely used algorithms like SGLD. The resulting improvement in convergence rates is significant and provides a practical alternative to developing and implementing more complex higher-order integrators. This work has the potential to be highly influential, as the technique is general and can be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors derive a Central Limit Theorem for the SGRRLD estimator and provide non-asymptotic bounds for both bias and MSE. The key result—improving the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵)—is a substantial theoretical advance and clearly demonstrates the power of the proposed method. The analysis of variance control via correlated noise is also a key technical contribution.

3.  **Excellent Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated cleanly. The structure logically flows from background and motivation to the proposed method, its theoretical analysis, and finally its empirical validation.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is strong.
    *   The experiments on the synthetic linear Gaussian model are well-designed, allowing for a direct and quantitative comparison with the ground truth posterior. These experiments convincingly validate the theoretical claims regarding the reduction in bias and MSE as a function of the step size γ and the number of iterations K.
    *   The application to large-scale matrix factorization demonstrates the practical utility of SGRRLD. By comparing performance against wall-clock time on large, real-world datasets, the authors show that the method provides tangible efficiency gains. The fact that SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the time is a compelling result.
    *   Demonstrating the generality of the method by applying it to SGHMC and comparing it to a higher-order SGHMC integrator further strengthens the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Cost Trade-off:** The primary weakness is the increased computational cost. Each "iteration" of SGRRLD requires three gradient evaluations (one for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain). The authors rightly argue that the two chains can be run in parallel, making the wall-clock time per iteration roughly equivalent to two sequential SGLD steps. The experiments fairly compare against wall-clock time. However, in a resource-constrained (e.g., single-core) environment, SGRRLD is 3x slower per iteration than SGLD. A brief discussion on whether running SGLD for 3x as many iterations would be competitive in a non-parallel setting would have made the analysis of this trade-off more complete.

2.  **Memory Requirements:** The method requires storing states for two parallel chains, effectively doubling the memory footprint compared to SGLD. While this is unlikely to be a bottleneck in many applications, it could become a consideration for extremely large models where memory is a limiting factor. This is a minor point but worth acknowledging.

3.  **Practicality of Step Size Schedule:** The theoretical analysis derives an optimal exponent α=1/5 for a decreasing step size schedule of the form γk ∝ k⁻ᵃ. While theoretically sound, tuning such schedules in practice remains a challenging "dark art" for SG-MCMC methods. Most experiments presented use a constant step size, which is common but sidesteps the practical difficulties of tuning a decreasing schedule. The experiment in Figure 3 confirms the theory for a fixed K, which is good, but a more extensive discussion on the practical aspects of tuning would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and clear contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method is simple, clever, and effective. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the empirical results are convincing. The work successfully demonstrates how a classical numerical method can be adapted to solve a modern machine learning problem, leading to significant improvements in both theory and practice. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, original, and significant. It will be of great interest to researchers and practitioners working on MCMC and large-scale Bayesian methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
