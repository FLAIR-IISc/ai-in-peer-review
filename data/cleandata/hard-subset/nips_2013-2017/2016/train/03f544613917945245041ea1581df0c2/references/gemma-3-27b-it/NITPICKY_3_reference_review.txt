This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) aimed at reducing bias via Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea – running two chains with different step sizes and combining their results – is a well-established numerical technique applied to stochastic differential equations, and the authors appropriately connect their work to prior art in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and SGMCMC. The theoretical contributions include bounding the bias and variance of SGRRLD as O(γ2) and O(γ4), respectively, and demonstrating a convergence rate of O(K−4/5) for the MSE, an improvement over SGLD’s O(K−2/3). The experimental evaluation uses both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC extrapolation) and a real-world matrix factorization task, providing empirical support for the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel contribution. While RR extrapolation is known, its adaptation to this stochastic setting and the resulting theoretical analysis represent new work.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including convergence analysis and bounds on bias and variance. The derivations appear careful, although a deeper dive would be necessary to definitively confirm their correctness.
*   **Improved Convergence Rate:** The theoretical improvement in the MSE convergence rate (O(K−4/5) vs. O(K−2/3)) is significant, and if realized in practice, could make SGRRLD a valuable tool for large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel nature of running the two chains is a practical benefit, fitting well with the distributed computing environments common in large-scale machine learning.
*   **Generality:** The authors correctly note the potential applicability of RR extrapolation to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are reasonably comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real datasets and incorporating comparisons to SGLD and SGHMC.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Excessive Technical Detail & Notation:** While thoroughness is appreciated, the paper suffers from a density of notation and technical detail that occasionally obscures the core ideas. For instance, the relationship between γ and η is stated but not intuitively explained. The notation in Theorem 1 (Γ(n)K) is unnecessarily cumbersome.
*   **Lack of Justification for Parameter Choices:** The experiments provide results, but offer little justification for the chosen step sizes (γ) or mini-batch sizes (B). A more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity would be valuable. For example, why was γ = 2 × 10−6 chosen for ML-1M, and how does performance vary with different settings?
*   **Limited Exploration of Correlation Matrix Σ:** The discussion of the correlated Brownian motion introduces the matrix Σ, but the experimental results do not explore its impact. The assumption that Σ=Id provides minimal variance is stated but not demonstrated empirically. This feels like a missed opportunity to explore a potentially impactful aspect of the algorithm.
*   **Minor Formatting Inconsistencies:** Several minor formatting issues are present. For instance, the reference list uses inconsistent punctuation. The use of subscripts for variables (e.g., γk+1) is often visually distracting and could be simplified.
*   **Clarity of the Bias Reduction Mechanism:** While the authors state SGRRLD reduces bias, the underlying mechanism isn't intuitively explained. A more visual or conceptual explanation of how the RR extrapolation achieves bias reduction would be helpful.
*   **Overly Optimistic Conclusion:** The concluding statement that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is somewhat overstated. It achieves *similar* accuracy, but the cost is increased computation (running two chains).
*   **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** While parallelization is noted, a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead of running two chains (versus a single chain in SGLD) would be valuable. Specifically, how does the increased cost compare to the gains in accuracy?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of SGLD.
*   The theoretical results are promising, demonstrating improved convergence rates.
*   The experimental results provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims.
*   The algorithm’s parallelizability is a practical advantage.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and potentially difficult to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the field.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks a thorough investigation of parameter sensitivity and the impact of the correlation matrix Σ.
*   Several minor formatting and clarity issues detract from the overall presentation.
*   The claimed "accuracy of higher-order integrators" is a slight overstatement.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the strengths of this work, I lean towards **rejection**. While the core idea is novel and the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper suffers from a number of minor flaws – excessive technicality, lack of parameter sensitivity analysis, a superficial exploration of the correlation matrix, and minor formatting issues – that collectively detract from its overall quality. The improvements offered by SGRRLD, while theoretically significant, aren’t convincingly demonstrated to outweigh the added computational cost in the presented experiments. The paper feels *almost* ready for NIPS, but requires significant refinement and clarification before it reaches the necessary standard. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, including more thorough experimentation and a more accessible presentation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
