

# Peer Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

## Summary

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm that aims to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) framework, which requires running two chains with different step sizes in parallel. The key insight is that by combining these two chains using RR extrapolation, the bias can be reduced to O(γ²) and the MSE to O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to SGLD's O(γ) bias and O(K⁻²/³) MSE. The paper also demonstrates that this approach can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only first-order numerical integrators. The authors provide theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds, and validate their approach on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks for movie recommendations.

The work builds on the foundational SGLD algorithm by Welling and Teh [1] and extends the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique for SDEs (Talay and Tubaro [21]) to the SG-MCMC setting. The paper also relates to recent work on higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC [10] by showing that their approach can achieve similar accuracy without requiring complex higher-order schemes.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis of the SGRRLD algorithm. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and carefully derived, with appropriate technical conditions specified. The convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) is a significant improvement over existing first-order SG-MCMC methods.

2. **Novel Application of RR Extrapolation**: The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is genuinely novel. While RR extrapolation has been used for SDEs in the past [21, 23], its adaptation to SG-MCMC is a meaningful contribution that addresses a key limitation of current SG-MCMC methods (bias).

3. **Practical Parallelization**: The paper correctly identifies that the two chains required by RR extrapolation can be generated independently, making SGRRLD well-suited for parallel and distributed architectures. This is a significant practical advantage for large-scale applications.

4. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation**: The experiments on both synthetic data (Gaussian models) and real-world datasets (MovieLens) provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims. The results consistently show SGRRLD outperforming SGLD and SGHMC in terms of bias and MSE.

5. **Clear Writing and Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Computational Cost**: While the paper mentions that SGRRLD is parallelizable, it doesn't thoroughly analyze the practical computational cost. Running two chains requires approximately twice the computation (though parallelized), so the actual speedup compared to SGLD depends on the specific hardware and implementation. The paper should clarify whether the improved convergence rate translates to better wall-clock time performance (which the experiments suggest it does, but the analysis is not explicit).

2. **Technical Conditions May Be Restrictive**: The theoretical results depend on several technical conditions (e.g., smoothness of the potential function, moment conditions on the gradient estimator). While these are standard in the field, the paper doesn't discuss how restrictive these conditions are for practical applications, especially in high-dimensional settings common in machine learning.

3. **Limited Comparison to Other Methods**: While the paper compares SGRRLD to SGLD and SGHMC, it could have included more recent SG-MCMC methods. For example, methods like Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo with adaptive step sizes [6] or other variance reduction techniques might provide more meaningful comparisons.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper shows results for fixed step sizes and fixed K, but doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the performance is to the choice of step size schedule, mini-batch size, or other hyperparameters. This would be valuable for practitioners.

5. **Limited Exploration of Recursive RR**: The paper mentions that "the RR extrapolation can be applied multiple times in a recursive manner" but doesn't explore this in detail or show the benefits. A brief discussion of how recursive RR might further improve the convergence rate would strengthen the paper.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors carefully address the conditions under which their results hold and provide detailed proofs in the supplementary materials. The experiments are well-designed and support the theoretical claims. The paper is a complete piece of work, not just a position paper. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths (improved convergence rate) and limitations (computational cost of two chains). **(Excellent)**

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The main contributions are stated early, and the technical details are presented in a logical flow. The notation is consistent throughout, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors could have made the experimental section slightly more detailed (e.g., specific implementation choices for the MovieLens experiments), but overall the paper is highly readable. **(Excellent)**

**Originality**: The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel. While RR extrapolation has been used for SDEs before [21, 23], its application to SG-MCMC is a fresh perspective that addresses a key limitation of current SG-MCMC methods. The paper properly cites related work and clearly distinguishes their contribution. The combination of RR with SG-MCMC represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches. **(Strong)**

**Significance**: The improved convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD is significant, especially since it achieves this without requiring higher-order integrators. The method's parallel nature makes it particularly valuable for large-scale applications, and the experimental results confirm the theoretical claims. This work advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC and provides a practical approach that could be adopted by practitioners working on large-scale Bayesian inference. **(Strong)**

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to improving the convergence properties of SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical implications are clear. The main weakness is the limited discussion of the practical computational cost, but this is a minor concern given the strong theoretical and empirical results.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, and the SGRRLD algorithm has clear potential for practical applications in large-scale machine learning. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept** (Strong Accept)

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief discussion of the practical computational cost of SGRRLD compared to SGLD, including how the wall-clock time benefits from the parallelization.

2. Provide more detail on the hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., step size, mini-batch size) in the experiments section.

3. Consider adding a short discussion of recursive RR extrapolation and its potential benefits for further improving convergence rates.

4. Include a brief comparison to more recent SG-MCMC methods (e.g., those with adaptive step sizes or other variance reduction techniques) in the experimental section.

5. In the theoretical analysis, explicitly state how restrictive the technical conditions are for practical machine learning applications, especially in high dimensions.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear theoretical and practical contributions to the field of scalable MCMC methods. The authors have made a significant advancement in the SG-MCMC literature that is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community.
