

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors claim their method achieves a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}), significantly better than SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}). The paper contains theoretical analysis and experiments on synthetic and real datasets supporting these claims.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, with well-structured theorems about the bias and MSE convergence rates. The experimental results on Gaussian models and matrix factorization tasks provide reasonable empirical support for the theoretical claims. The idea of using RR extrapolation with correlated Brownian increments to control variance is novel and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

### Critical Technical Errors
The paper contains several critical mathematical errors that undermine its credibility. In Equation (2), the update rule for SGLD should be θ_{k+1} = θ_k - γ_{k+1}∇Ũ_{k+1}(θ_k) + √(2γ_{k+1})Z_{k+1}, but the paper writes "√ γ_{k+1}Z_{k+1}" without the factor of 2. This is a fundamental error in the SGLD algorithm description. Similarly, in Equation (5), the term "γ_{k+1} 2" should be "γ_{k+1}/2" and "√ γ_{k+1}" should be "√γ_{k+1}" with proper spacing. These are not just typographical errors but would completely change the algorithm's behavior.

### Theoretical Inconsistencies
The paper claims that the bias of SGLD is O(γ) but cites [10] for this bound without specifying that this is for constant step sizes. The paper's theoretical analysis assumes constant step sizes for Theorem 2 but later uses decreasing step sizes (γ_k = γ_1k^{-α}), creating a disconnect between the theoretical assumptions and experimental setup. The proof sketch of Theorem 1 mentions a "7th order Taylor expansion" without justification for why this specific order is necessary.

### Ambiguous Formulation
The description of the two chains is confusing: "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice more time than the other one" should be "the chain with the smallest step size is run twice as many iterations as the other chain" or "the chain with the smallest step size runs two steps for every one step of the other chain." The paper also states "the mini-batch size has to be the same for the two chains" but doesn't explain why this is necessary for the theoretical results, creating confusion between implementation requirements and theoretical assumptions.

### Missing Experimental Details
The paper states "the MSE of SGRRLD is in the order of the square of the MSE of SGLD" but doesn't provide actual numerical values or statistical significance for this claim. The experimental section lacks standard deviations for the reported results, making it impossible to assess whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. The paper also doesn't report the actual computational time per iteration for the two chains, which is crucial for understanding the practical efficiency claims.

### Formatting and Citation Issues
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies: inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "e−U(θ)/ ∫" should be "e^{-U(θ)}/∫"), missing "The" before "Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)", and inconsistent notation for the step size sequence (γ_k vs. γ_k+1). The reference to [23] for the correlated Brownian increments is misleading because [23] deals with standard SDEs, not SG-MCMC, and the connection to the stochastic gradient case isn't properly established.

### Theoretical Overreach
The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" without providing a clear comparison of computational complexity. While the convergence rate is better, the paper doesn't quantify the computational cost of running two chains in parallel versus a single chain with a higher-order integrator. This is a significant omission since the primary motivation for SG-MCMC is scalability to large datasets.

### Lack of Critical Analysis
The paper fails to address limitations of the proposed method. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the method scales to high-dimensional problems beyond the d=1 case in the synthetic experiments. It also doesn't compare against other bias-reduction techniques for SG-MCMC, such as those using higher-order integrators directly.

## Conclusion
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, it is marred by critical mathematical errors, theoretical inconsistencies, and insufficient experimental reporting. The fundamental errors in the SGLD algorithm description (missing the factor of 2 in the stochastic term) are particularly concerning. The paper also overstates its contributions without proper comparison to alternative methods and fails to address key implementation details.

The theoretical results appear plausible given the correct algorithm, but the paper's numerous technical errors make it difficult to evaluate the validity of the theoretical claims. The experimental section lacks sufficient statistical detail to support the reported improvements. Given the paper's position as a NIPS submission, it must adhere to the highest standards of technical accuracy and completeness.

I recommend rejection due to the presence of critical mathematical errors and insufficient theoretical and experimental rigor. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
