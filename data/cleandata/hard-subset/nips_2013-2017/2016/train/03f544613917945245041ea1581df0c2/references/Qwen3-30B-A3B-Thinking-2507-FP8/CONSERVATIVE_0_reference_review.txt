

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes a novel extension to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The key idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 in parallel, then combining their results to achieve improved convergence rates. The authors claim SGRRLD achieves an MSE rate of O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), with asymptotic bias and variance bounded by O(γ^2) and O(γ^4), respectively.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its theoretical analysis. The authors provide a central limit theorem for their estimator (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2), with proofs sketched and detailed in the supplement. The methodology is well-motivated by previous work on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), and the connection to existing SG-MCMC methods is properly established.

However, as a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I have significant concerns about the paper's practical significance and the evidence supporting its claims. While the theoretical improvements are mathematically sound, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that these translate to meaningful practical benefits.

The most critical issue is the lack of thorough comparison with existing higher-order SG-MCMC methods. The authors claim SGRRLD achieves "the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" (Section 1), but they only compare with SGLD and SGHMC (with Euler discretization), not with methods like those in Chen et al. (2015) that already use higher-order integrators. The paper's Figure 4 shows SGRRHMC outperforming SGHMC but not SGHMC-s (which uses a higher-order integrator), undermining the claim that SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods without their computational complexity.

Furthermore, the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel is not adequately justified. While the authors claim the method is "well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures," they don't provide a clear analysis of the wall-clock time trade-off. For instance, in their large-scale experiments (Section 5.2), SGRRLD runs half as many iterations as SGLD (K=5250 vs K=10500) to maintain similar wall-clock time. However, the MSE improvement shown in Figure 5 is marginal (e.g., for ML-1M, RMSE decreases from ~0.92 to ~0.91), which may not justify the theoretical complexity of the method.

The synthetic data experiments (Section 5.1) show more promising results, but the improvements are limited to specific settings (e.g., small dimension d=1, fixed step size). The paper fails to demonstrate that the benefits scale to more complex, real-world problems where SG-MCMC is typically applied.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of limitations. For instance, the theoretical guarantees require specific technical conditions on U, f, and L that may not hold in practice, and the paper doesn't address how sensitive the method is to the choice of step size sequences or mini-batch sizes.

While the paper is well-written and the theoretical contributions are technically sound, it falls short of demonstrating that this novel approach provides meaningful practical advantages over existing methods. The claimed theoretical improvements do not necessarily translate to significant practical benefits, especially when considering the added complexity of running two parallel chains. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with higher-order SG-MCMC methods and a more detailed analysis of the practical trade-offs involved.

I recommend rejection for the following reasons:

**Pro:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-justified theorems
- Clear writing and good organization
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC

**Con:**
- Insufficient evidence that theoretical improvements translate to practical benefits
- Inadequate comparison with existing higher-order SG-MCMC methods
- Lack of thorough analysis of computational trade-offs
- Limited experimental validation on complex, real-world problems
- Overstated claims about achieving "the accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators"

The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to SG-MCMC, but as a scientific contribution to the field, it lacks the compelling practical evidence needed to justify its novelty and potential impact. The community would benefit more from a thorough comparison of this approach with existing higher-order methods rather than a new method that makes strong claims without sufficient empirical validation.
