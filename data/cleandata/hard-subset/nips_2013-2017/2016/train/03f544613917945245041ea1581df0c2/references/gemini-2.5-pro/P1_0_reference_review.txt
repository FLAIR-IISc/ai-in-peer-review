### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique for accelerating convergence, to the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains on the same target distribution but with different step sizes (γ and γ/2). The outputs of these two chains are then combined in a specific way to cancel the leading-order bias term inherent in the Euler-Maruyama discretization used by SGLD. A key component of the proposed method is the use of correlated Brownian motions for the two chains, which is shown to be crucial for controlling the variance of the final estimator.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. They prove a central limit theorem and derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean-Squared Error (MSE). These results show that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence than standard SGLD. Specifically, the MSE of SGRRLD is shown to converge at a rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. This improved accuracy is comparable to that of methods using more complex, higher-order numerical integrators, but is achieved using only the simple first-order Euler integrator.

The theoretical findings are supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. On a synthetic Gaussian model where the ground truth is known, the authors demonstrate that SGRRLD significantly reduces both bias and MSE compared to SGLD. They also show the generality of their approach by applying it to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC). Finally, on a large-scale matrix factorization task using the MovieLens datasets, SGRRLD is shown to achieve better predictive accuracy (lower RMSE) in less wall-clock time than SGLD, demonstrating its practical utility.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and significant contribution. While RR extrapolation is a known technique for SDEs, its adaptation to the stochastic gradient setting, with the additional noise source from data subsampling, is non-trivial. The paper presents a compelling solution that directly addresses the well-known issue of discretization bias in SG-MCMC algorithms. The resulting improvement in convergence rates is a substantial advance for the field.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper is theoretically very strong. The authors provide both asymptotic (Central Limit Theorem) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) analyses. The derived convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for the MSE is a state-of-the-art result for SG-MCMC methods based on first-order integrators and clearly demonstrates the power of the proposed technique. The analysis also correctly identifies the optimal choice for correlating the noise processes to minimize asymptotic variance, which is a crucial practical insight.

3.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** A major strength of SGRRLD is its suitability for modern parallel computing architectures. The two chains required for the extrapolation can be run completely in parallel. The authors rightly emphasize this and use wall-clock time as a key metric in their large-scale experiments, which shows that the theoretical gains translate into real-world performance improvements. This makes the method highly practical and appealing compared to potentially more complex and serial higher-order integration schemes.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is excellent. The use of a synthetic Gaussian model allows for precise verification of the theoretical claims regarding bias, MSE, and the effect of the step size. The experiment demonstrating the generality of the method by applying it to SGHMC is a strong addition. Most importantly, the large-scale matrix factorization experiment on MovieLens datasets confirms that the method is not just a theoretical curiosity but offers tangible benefits on a challenging, real-world problem.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The method requires running two chains and performing three gradient updates (one for the coarse chain, two for the fine chain) for each effective "step" of the algorithm. While the authors correctly argue that parallelism mitigates the wall-clock time increase, the total computational cost (in terms of gradient evaluations) is roughly 3x that of a single SGLD chain. On a system with limited parallel resources (e.g., a single GPU or CPU), this could be a drawback. A brief discussion of this trade-off would make the paper even more balanced.

2.  **Clarity of the Estimator Formula:** The form of the estimator in Equation (6) is correct but could be made more intuitive for readers familiar with standard RR extrapolation. A short sentence explaining how this ergodic average formulation relates to the more common point estimate combination `2 * (fine estimate) - (coarse estimate)` would enhance clarity. This is a very minor point, however.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   The paper claims the technique can be applied to "virtually all the current SG-MCMC algorithms." While this is demonstrated for SGLD and SGHMC, are there any known SG-MCMC variants where the required "weak error and ergodicity conditions" might not hold, or where this approach would be problematic?
-   The analysis of the non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) shows that the optimal step size `γ` depends on the total number of iterations `K` (e.g., `γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵` for MSE). In practice, `K` is often not known in advance. How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to a suboptimal choice of the step size schedule? The experiments use a constant step size, which works well, but some discussion on choosing decreasing step size schedules in practice would be helpful.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful algorithm. It makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theory and extensive experiments. The writing is clear and the work is of high quality. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Originality:** Novel application of RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework.
*   **Significance:** Achieves higher-order convergence rates using only a first-order integrator, advancing the state of the art. The method is practical, parallelizable, and general.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The computational cost in terms of total gradient evaluations is higher than SGLD, which could be a limitation in non-parallel settings. (This is a minor point, well-addressed by the focus on wall-clock time).

Overall, this paper is a clear accept for NIPS. It will likely be influential and inspire follow-up work, such as the multi-level extensions mentioned by the authors.
