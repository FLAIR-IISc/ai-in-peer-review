- Proposes SGRRLD: applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two Langevin chains with different step sizes and combining results to reduce bias.  
- Theoretical claim: improved MSE convergence rate O(K^-4/5) versus SGLD’s O(K^-2/3), with proofs sketched and full details in supplement.  
- Experimental validation on synthetic models and large-scale matrix factorization shows empirical gains over SGLD and SGHMC.  
- Builds on SGLD and higher-order integrator literature; RR extrapolation is borrowed from numerical analysis but new in SG-MCMC context.  
- Connects to prior work on variance control in RR extrapolation (e.g., Lemaire et al.).  
- Novelty: first practical application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC without using complex higher-order integrators.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees, albeit condensed in the main text.  
- Demonstrates strong experimental results, including a compelling large-scale matrix factorization task.  
- Algorithm is naturally parallelizable by running two independent chains.  
- Technique is general and applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms (shown for SGHMC).  
- Writing is generally clear, though some theoretical sections need more elaboration.  
- Weakness: main-paper theoretical analysis is condensed and relies on supplementary material for full understanding.  
- Weakness: implementing correlated Brownian increments for variance control may be practically challenging on large datasets and complex estimators.  
- Weakness: optimal step-size theory may require careful tuning in practice; more practical guidance is needed.  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparisons to other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods (e.g., preconditioning, adaptive steps).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, empirically validated, parallel-friendly, and broadly applicable.  
- Arguments against acceptance: condensed theoretical exposition and potential implementation difficulties with correlated increments.  
- Recommended revisions: expand theoretical conditions in main paper, discuss practical issues for correlated increments, add broader empirical comparisons, and clarify step-size selection.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution deserving acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation & rating: Accept with Minor Revisions; score 8/10 (Strong Accept).
