Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC scheme that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SGLD. Two SGLD chains are run at step sizes γ and γ/2 (the fine chain takes two half-steps per coarse step), and a time-averaged estimator combines them in the RR form (2×fine − coarse). To control variance, the Brownian increments are coupled so that the coarse noise is the appropriately scaled sum of the two fine noises. Theoretical results include: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator under decreasing steps, with asymptotic variance minimized by perfect Brownian coupling; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE for constant and decreasing step sizes, yielding improved rates over SGLD with first-order integrators (e.g., MSE O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3})). Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model, an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC), and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) demonstrate reduced bias/MSE and improved wall-clock accuracy when the two chains are run in parallel.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and subsequent SG-MCMC analyses (e.g., Teh et al., Ma et al., Chen et al.), and on RR extrapolation for SDE weak error and invariant measures (Talay–Tubaro; Lamberton–Pages; Lemaire–Pages–Panloup). The key idea of multilevel coupling (correlated Brownian increments) mirrors established MLMC principles; the authors extend the variance-minimizing correlation argument to the SG-MCMC setting and to time-averaged estimators. The connection to higher-order integrators (e.g., SGHMC-splitting) is discussed and empirically contrasted.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and novel combination: RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC, with principled coupling to control variance. The approach is broadly applicable beyond SGLD (SGHMC demonstration).
- Solid theory: a CLT showing Gaussian noise dominates gradient noise under their scaling; non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds with clear rate improvements over first-order SG-MCMC.
- Practical angle: embarrassingly parallel implementation (two chains), with wall-clock improvements shown on large matrix factorization.
- Empirical validation includes synthetic setups that check predicted rates and a real large-scale task.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions are strong: the analysis requires smoothness up to high order (solution to Poisson equation g ∈ C^9), and technical ergodicity/weak error conditions that may fail for common non-smooth/weakly regular posteriors (e.g., Laplace priors, ReLU networks).
- Practical coupling of stochastic gradients is not leveraged. The theory emphasizes Brownian coupling, but in finite-K regimes the stochastic gradient noise can dominate when B is small; coupling minibatches across levels (coarse vs fine) could further reduce variance but is not explored experimentally or theoretically.
- Computational budget fairness: improvements in wall-clock hinge on parallelism (two processes). On a single core or when parallel resources are constrained, SGRRLD doubles per-iteration cost; the paper does not report accuracy vs compute for a single-threaded setting.
- Limited diversity of posteriors: beyond quadratic models (Gaussian linear regression and Gaussian MF), there is no demonstration on non-Gaussian posteriors (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets), where discretization and gradient noise interplay is more challenging.
- Some clarity/notation issues: in Eq. (5) the updates appear to use θ2k+1 on the RHS inside the gradient, which would be implicit; this is likely a typo and should read ∇Ũ(θ2k) for the first fine substep. Please check indexing in (4)–(6) and normalization in the estimator.

Quality
The paper is technically sound overall, with arguments that closely follow known weak error expansions and multilevel variance control adapted to the SG setting. The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are plausible and useful. However, the “asymptotic variance O(γ^4)” claim should be precisely stated (variance of what, under what normalization and limit?), as it is nonstandard compared to Markov chain CLTs where variance typically scales with integrated autocorrelation rather than a high power of γ. Clarifying the dependence on the variance of the stochastic gradient estimator (as a function of minibatch size) in finite-K bounds would strengthen practical guidance.

Clarity
The exposition is generally clear, but some indexing and update typos should be corrected. The coupling construction (Σ, Wk) is well-motivated; still, it would help to state explicitly how to implement perfect coupling in parallel (e.g., sharing RNG seeds or communicating sums of fine increments). Summarizing the technical assumptions (dissipativity, smoothness, bounds on gradient noise moments) in the main paper would aid readability.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with careful Brownian coupling is, to my knowledge, new at the time and nontrivial. The extension of CLTs for duplicated diffusions to the SG setting is a meaningful theoretical contribution. The idea of attaining higher-order accuracy with a first-order integrator via extrapolation is appealing.

Significance
If broadly applicable, SGRRLD offers a practical path to improved accuracy without sophisticated high-order integrators, especially in parallel environments. The method could influence subsequent SG-MCMC designs and MLMC-style samplers. The scope would be bolstered by demonstrations on nonquadratic models and by addressing compute-budget trade-offs without parallelism.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the apparent typos in Eq. (5) (explicit Euler updates) and check the normalization/coefficients in the RR estimator (Equation (6)).
- Clarify precisely the statement about “variance O(γ^4)” and its regime; include the dependence on minibatch size in finite-K bounds if possible.
- Discuss and, if feasible, experiment with minibatch coupling across levels (e.g., use the same minibatch for coarse and the union of the two fine minibatches), and assess its effect on variance/MSE.
- Add a single-threaded compute vs error comparison to separate algorithmic gains from parallel speedups.
- Include at least one non-Gaussian posterior (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression) to demonstrate robustness.
- Cite foundational MLMC work (e.g., Giles, 2008) alongside [27].

Pros
- Elegant, well-grounded idea with provable gains.
- Minimal modifications to existing SG-MCMC; easy parallelism.
- Strong theoretical and empirical alignment on synthetic tasks.
- Promising results on large-scale matrix factorization.

Cons
- Strong smoothness assumptions; limited model diversity in experiments.
- Reliance on parallelism for practical wall-clock gains; no single-core analysis.
- Missed opportunity to couple minibatches across levels.
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is original and technically solid, with clear improvements over first-order SG-MCMC and a practical parallel implementation. Addressing the noted concerns would strengthen the paper further.
