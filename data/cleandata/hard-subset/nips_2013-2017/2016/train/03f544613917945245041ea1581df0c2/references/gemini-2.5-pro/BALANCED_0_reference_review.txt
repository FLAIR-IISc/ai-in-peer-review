**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The work builds upon the well-established Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm, which adapts the Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm for large-scale Bayesian inference by using stochastic gradients. The primary limitation of SGLD and other similar first-order SG-MCMC methods is the bias introduced by the Euler-Maruyama discretization, which is of order O(γ), where γ is the step size.

The core contribution of this paper is the application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique for accelerating convergence, to the SG-MCMC setting. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm runs two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size γ and another with a step size γ/2 (requiring two steps to cover the same time interval). By taking a specific linear combination of the estimators from these two chains, the leading-order O(γ) bias term is cancelled out. To prevent the variance from increasing prohibitively, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motion for the two chains.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. They prove a central limit theorem and derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). Their key theoretical result is that SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²) and an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) after K iterations, which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate for standard SGLD. The paper validates these theoretical findings through comprehensive experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and demonstrates the practical utility of the method on a large-scale matrix factorization task using MovieLens datasets, showing improved accuracy for a given wall-clock time.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant contribution. It provides a conceptually simple yet powerful way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order numerical integrators using only a first-order integrator (Euler-Maruyama). This is significant because developing and implementing bespoke higher-order integrators can be complex, whereas SGRRLD is a general "wrapper" that can be applied to existing methods. This work opens a new avenue for improving the accuracy of a wide class of SG-MCMC algorithms.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The authors present both a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The analysis correctly identifies the optimal step size decay rate to balance the bias and variance terms, leading to a faster convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for the MSE. This theoretical improvement over the standard O(K⁻²/³) rate is a key strength of the paper.

3.  **Experimental Quality and Rigor:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The experiments on the synthetic linear Gaussian model are particularly well-designed, as the availability of the true posterior allows for a precise quantification of bias and MSE. The results shown in Figures 2 and 3 provide compelling evidence for the theoretical claims regarding the dependency on step size γ and the number of iterations K.
    *   The application of the RR technique to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC) effectively demonstrates the generality of the proposed approach beyond SGLD.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world datasets (MovieLens) successfully showcase the practical benefits of SGRRLD. The improved convergence in terms of RMSE versus wall-clock time is a strong argument for the method's utility, especially given its inherent parallelizability.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the background, the core idea of RR extrapolation, the proposed algorithm, and the main theoretical and empirical results.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While SGRRLD can be parallelized, it fundamentally requires more computation. To advance the simulation by a time interval γ, SGRRLD performs one gradient evaluation for the coarse chain and two for the fine chain, for a total of three. In contrast, SGLD requires only one. The authors rightly point out that parallelization reduces the wall-clock time overhead, but a more explicit discussion of the trade-off between computational cost (total gradient evaluations) and statistical efficiency would strengthen the paper. For instance, a plot of RMSE vs. total gradient computations, alongside the existing RMSE vs. wall-clock time plot, would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's efficiency.

2.  **Direct Comparison with Higher-Order Methods:** A central claim is that SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. The comparison with SGHMC-s is a good start. However, a more direct comparison would be highly beneficial. For example, comparing SGRRLD directly against a second-order SGLD variant (e.g., from Chen et al., 2015 [10]) on the Gaussian model would provide a clear benchmark. This would help answer questions about the relative performance, implementation complexity, and computational cost of these two different approaches to achieving higher-order accuracy.

3.  **Scope of Generality:** The authors claim the technique is "quite generic," which is supported by the SGHMC experiment. However, the theoretical analysis is presented for Langevin dynamics. To make the claim of generality more concrete, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the key conditions an SG-MCMC algorithm must satisfy for the RR extrapolation to be effective. This would likely involve the existence of a specific error expansion in powers of the step size, which is true for many, but not all, numerical schemes. A short paragraph on this would better define the scope of the method.

4.  **Clarification of Estimator Formula:** The definition of the estimator in Equation (6) is compact but could be slightly confusing. It combines the samples in a way that implicitly performs the `2 * (fine estimator) - (coarse estimator)` extrapolation. A brief sentence explaining how this formulation relates to the standard RR formula would improve clarity for readers not deeply familiar with the technique in this specific context.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pro):**
*   Presents a novel and clever synthesis of a classical numerical method (Richardson-Romberg) with modern large-scale Bayesian inference (SG-MCMC).
*   Provides strong and significant theoretical results, demonstrating a provable improvement in the MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) to O(K⁻⁴/⁵).
*   The theoretical claims are rigorously validated through well-designed experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data.
*   The method is practically relevant, showing improved performance in wall-clock time due to its parallelizable nature, and is generalizable to other SG-MCMC methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Con):**
*   The paper could provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost versus statistical efficiency trade-off.
*   A direct experimental comparison against an explicit higher-order SG-MCMC integrator would more strongly substantiate a key claim of the paper.

**Decision**

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel, significant, and well-supported contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The strengths—a clever idea, strong theory, and convincing experiments—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to areas that could be expanded upon. The proposed SGRRLD method provides a new and practical tool for practitioners and a solid foundation for future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
