

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

## Summary
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The method runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel, then combines their outputs through RR extrapolation. The authors theoretically demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}), improving upon SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}) rate. The paper builds on prior SG-MCMC work (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and RR extrapolation techniques for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), but applies them in a novel context to SG-MCMC.

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis. The authors provide a central limit theorem for SGRRLD and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, showing the improved convergence rate. The theoretical claims are supported by careful derivation and references to supplementary proofs. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the requirement for the same mini-batch size for both chains to avoid bias, and the behavior of the estimator when the step size becomes very small. The experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks consistently support the theoretical claims, with SGRRLD demonstrating significantly improved accuracy over SGLD in all tested scenarios. The paper is a complete scientific contribution that properly evaluates both strengths and weaknesses.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the SG-MCMC literature, and the mathematical presentation is precise. The algorithm description and theoretical results are logically presented. The experimental section is detailed, with appropriate metrics and sufficient replication (100 runs for key experiments). The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary technical details for reproducibility. The paper could be improved by more explicitly discussing the computational overhead of running two chains in parallel, though this is partially addressed in the experiments showing nearly identical wall-clock times due to parallelization.

## Originality
This work makes a clear original contribution by applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a technique previously unexplored in this domain. The specific use of correlated Brownian motions to control variance (Equation 8) represents a novel technical contribution that extends prior work on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Lemaire et al., 2015). The paper effectively distinguishes itself from previous SG-MCMC methods by showing that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only a first-order integrator. The related work section properly cites relevant NIPS and other conference papers, including Chen et al. (2015) which establishes the O(γ) and O(γ²) bounds for SG-MCMC methods. The paper demonstrates significant novelty compared to previous NIPS proceedings.

## Significance
The results are highly significant as they establish a clear theoretical advantage for SGRRLD over existing SG-MCMC methods, with a demonstrably better convergence rate. The paper shows that a first-order method can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods, which is a valuable theoretical insight. The practical significance is reinforced by the experiments showing SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in approximately half the time on large-scale matrix factorization tasks. The method's parallelizability is particularly relevant for modern distributed computing architectures. The approach is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms (beyond SGLD), suggesting broad applicability to Bayesian inference in large-scale settings. The results advance the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
1. Theoretical contribution is significant with rigorous proofs showing improved convergence rates (O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3}))
2. Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which has not been explored before
3. Experiments consistently support theoretical claims across multiple datasets
4. The method is parallelizable, making it well-suited for modern distributed computing architectures
5. The approach is general and applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms

**Con:**
1. Limited discussion of computational overhead of running two chains in parallel (though experiments show wall-clock time is nearly identical)
2. Theoretical analysis assumes certain technical conditions that may not always hold in practice
3. The paper doesn't extensively explore the sensitivity of performance to the choice of Σ in the correlated Brownian motion

## Conclusion
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference with a novel algorithm that achieves improved theoretical convergence rates while maintaining practical computational efficiency. The theoretical analysis is sound and thoroughly supported by experiments. The work is clearly original, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem in large-scale Bayesian inference. The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for its significance. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and should be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
