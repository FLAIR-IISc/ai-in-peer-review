- Paper proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD using two coupled discretizations (steps γ and γ/2) to reduce weak error from O(γ) to O(γ^2).
- Key idea: mirror Talay–Tubaro expansions for SDEs, couple Brownian noises so Zγ ≈ (Zfine,1 + Zfine,2)/√2, and form estimator 2π̂γ/2(f) − π̂γ(f).
- The authors prove a CLT under decreasing step-sizes and show optimal variance is achieved by exact Brownian coupling.
- They derive non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds for constant and decreasing step-sizes: bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)).
- Optimizing γ yields a finite-time MSE rate O(K^-4/5), improving over SGLD’s O(K^-2/3).
- Experiments on a linear Gaussian model validate bias/MSE scalings and include a dimension-scaling study.
- MovieLens matrix factorization experiments show practical gains at matched wall-clock time by running two chains in parallel.
- They present an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) that competes with higher-order integrators.
- The work builds on SGLD/SG-MCMC literature and classical RR extrapolation for SDEs; adapting RR to SG-MCMC is novel.
- Theoretical analysis is careful (Poisson equation techniques, high-order Taylor expansions) and variance minimization via Brownian coupling is well motivated.
- Regularity assumptions are strong: require a C^9 solution to the Poisson equation, high-order smoothness, polynomial growth bounds, and geometric ergodicity.
- Results rely on moment conditions and controlled stochastic gradient variance; the CLT’s claim that Gaussian noise dominates stochastic gradient noise depends on step-size regime and should be stated explicitly.
- Necessity of using same mini-batch size across levels is noted; the benefit of sharing mini-batches across levels for variance reduction is not explored.
- Only Brownian noise is coupled; not coupling stochastic gradients may miss further variance reduction, especially with small batches or state-dependent variance.
- Computational cost doubles gradient evaluations per time horizon and practical efficiency relies on parallel resources, which may not be available on single-GPU or limited setups.
- Experimental scope is limited (Gaussian linear model and matrix factorization); missing common benchmarks like Bayesian logistic regression or neural network posteriors and main-text decreasing step-size experiments.
- Non-asymptotic bounds hide constants that may depend on gradient moments and dimension; discussion or empirical evidence on dimension dependence is lacking.
- Clarity is generally good, estimator and coupling construction are clear, but some notational slips (e.g., Eq. (5)) and many technical assumptions are deferred to the supplement.
- Strengths: simple and elegant RR application to SG-MCMC, solid theoretical guarantees, principled variance control, parallel-friendly, and empirically effective on tested tasks.
- Weaknesses: strong smoothness/Poisson assumptions, limited treatment of state-dependent SG noise, no gradient coupling across levels, reliance on parallel computing, and narrower empirical validation.
- Reviewer questions for authors: formalize stochastic gradient noise conditions for O(γ) bias cancellation and effect of differing mini-batch sizes; report results when sharing mini-batches across levels; include decreasing step-size experiment confirming α = 1/5; show impact of imperfect or independent Brownian coupling; comment on dimension dependence of constants in Theorem 2.
- Overall assessment: strong, original, and technically sound contribution with moderate-to-high significance; acceptance recommended if concerns about gradient-noise coupling and assumptions are addressed.
