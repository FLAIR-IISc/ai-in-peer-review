Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The key idea is to run two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon, then combine their ergodic averages with the RR coefficient 2:−1 to cancel the leading weak-discretization bias. To prevent variance inflation, the authors correlate the Gaussian noises across chains using a specific coupling that minimizes the asymptotic variance (Σ = I, yielding Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2). They establish a CLT for the RR estimator, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds, showing improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC (bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ))). This implies optimal MSE scaling O(K−4/5) vs. O(K−2/3) for SGLD under fixed compute. Experiments on a Gaussian model (including an SGHMC variant) and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate improved accuracy/time trade-offs and practical parallelism (two chains in parallel).

This work connects to: (i) SGLD and SG-MCMC extensions (Welling & Teh; Ma et al.; Chen et al.), (ii) weak error expansions and RR for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup), and (iii) high-order integrators for SG-MCMC (Chen, Ding, Carin). To my knowledge, bringing RR extrapolation—with the variance-minimizing correlated noise construction—into the stochastic-gradient setting is novel and conceptually clean.

Strengths
- Originality: A simple, broadly applicable extrapolation layer for SG-MCMC that reduces weak-bias without requiring high-order integrators. The correlated-noise coupling is well motivated by theory and extends RR ideas to the SG setting.
- Theory: Clear asymptotic and non-asymptotic analysis (CLT; explicit bias/MSE bounds; optimal rates with both constant and decreasing step sizes). The result that Gaussian noise dominates stochastic gradient noise in the CLT regime, and that Σ = I minimizes the asymptotic variance, is insightful.
- Practicality: The method is embarrassingly parallel (two chains), which is attractive in distributed settings and addresses a common bottleneck in MCMC. The SGHMC variant shows generality beyond SGLD.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments corroborate predicted bias/MSE scalings and demonstrate that SGRRLD can match the accuracy of higher-order SGHMC-s in appropriate regimes. The matrix factorization benchmarks show consistent RMSE gains at fixed wall-clock.

Weaknesses and questions
- Compute/parallel assumptions: The empirical “same wall-clock” claim relies on running two chains in parallel. On single-threaded or GPU-bound settings, SGRRLD will cost ~2× (or 3× gradient calls per macro-iteration), so comparisons should clarify single-worker speed/quality trade-offs. A plot under single-thread constraints would strengthen the claims.
- Gradient estimator coupling: While the theory suggests Brownian coupling is the key driver, in finite-time regimes, coupling the stochastic gradients (e.g., sharing minibatches or using antithetic subsets) could further reduce variance. The paper urges equal minibatch sizes but does not explore gradient coupling strategies empirically or theoretically; this seems a missed opportunity.
- Baselines and tuning: Comparisons to more SG-MCMC variants (e.g., pSGLD, thermostats, quasi-Newton SG-MCMC, preconditioned variants) are limited. For SGHMC-s, it would be helpful to detail tuning (friction/noise calibration, step sizes) to ensure fairness. For SGLD, decreasing step-size schedules are common for asymptotic correctness—some results use constant γ; a brief discussion on schedule choices in practice would help.
- Assumptions: The Poisson equation solution requirement g ∈ C9 and other smoothness/ergodicity conditions are strong. While standard in this literature, a brief discussion of robustness to misspecification/non-smooth posteriors would be useful.
- Practical guidance: The theory suggests optimal α = 1/5 for decreasing schedules and γ ∝ K−1/5 for MSE under constants. Some hyperparameter selection heuristics and sensitivity plots would aid practitioners.
- Scope of experiments: The matrix factorization setting uses fairly large minibatches (e.g., N/500 on ML-20M), which may not reflect resource-constrained deployments; an ablation on B would clarify sensitivity.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate references and careful statements. Proofs are deferred, but the proof sketches align with known RR/weak error techniques for SDEs and recent SG-MCMC analyses. Empirical results are convincing in the presented setups, though broader baselines and single-thread comparisons would bolster the case.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The method is easy to implement. Providing the pseudocode in the main text (not only the supplement) and a small “best practices” box (choice of Σ, minibatch coupling, step-size schedules) would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. Applying RR extrapolation with variance-minimizing coupling to SG-MCMC appears new and opens a simple, generic path to higher-order accuracy without bespoke integrators.

Significance
Meaningful. If adopted, this could become a standard “wrapper” to upgrade first-order SG-MCMC samplers, especially in distributed/parallel environments. The improved finite-time rates and practical gains on a large-scale task suggest impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel, simple idea with broad applicability
- Solid theory (CLT and finite-time bounds) showing improved rates
- Practical parallelism and clear implementation path
- Empirical evidence on both synthetic and real large-scale data
- Extends naturally to other SG-MCMC methods (SGHMC)

Cons for acceptance
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons depends on parallel resources
- Limited exploration of gradient coupling and broader baselines
- Strong smoothness assumptions; limited discussion of robustness
- Missing single-thread computational trade-off analysis

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold, elegant idea with solid analysis and promising empirical results. Minor experimental extensions and clearer practical guidance would further strengthen the work, but the contribution justifies inclusion.

Confidence: 4/5
