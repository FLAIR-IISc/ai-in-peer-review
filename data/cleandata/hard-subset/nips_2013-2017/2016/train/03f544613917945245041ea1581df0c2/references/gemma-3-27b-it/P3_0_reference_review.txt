## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and theoretically well-motivated extension to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea – applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to accelerate convergence – is insightful and addresses a known limitation of first-order SG-MCMC methods.  The authors provide a strong theoretical analysis, demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to SGLD, and support these findings with comprehensive experiments. While the implementation details could be more thoroughly discussed, and the gains in the matrix factorization experiment are somewhat modest, the paper represents a solid contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets using SG-MCMC.  It builds upon the foundation laid by Welling & Teh (SGLD) and subsequent work focusing on improving the efficiency of these methods. The key contribution is the application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a numerical acceleration technique, to SGLD. RR extrapolation leverages two chains with different step sizes to achieve higher-order accuracy without requiring higher-order integrators directly.  This is significant because higher-order integrators can be computationally expensive and complex to implement in a stochastic setting. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of variance reduction techniques for MCMC, referencing relevant work on RR extrapolation in SDEs (Talay & Tubaro) and its potential benefits.  They also acknowledge related work on higher-order SG-MCMC methods (Chen et al., 2015) and contrast their approach.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of SGRRLD, including convergence theorems (both asymptotic and non-asymptotic) and bounds on bias and MSE. The analysis convincingly demonstrates the improved convergence rate (O(K^-4/5) vs. O(K^-2/3) for SGLD) and justifies the use of correlated Brownian motions.
* **Novelty and Insight:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a genuinely novel idea. It provides a clever way to improve accuracy without the complexity of higher-order integrators.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conduct experiments on both synthetic (Gaussian model) and real (matrix factorization) datasets. The synthetic experiments effectively validate the theoretical results, showing reduced bias and improved MSE. The matrix factorization experiment demonstrates practical benefits, although the gains are not overwhelming.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The mathematical notation is standard and easy to follow.
* **Generality:**  The authors highlight the generality of their approach, demonstrating its application to SGHMC as well, broadening the potential impact of their work.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks detailed discussion of the implementation aspects. For example, the choice of step size schedule (γk) isn’t thoroughly explained, and the practical considerations of maintaining correlated Brownian motions are glossed over.  A more in-depth discussion of these practicalities would increase the reproducibility and accessibility of the method.
* **Modest Gains in Matrix Factorization:** While SGRRLD outperforms SGLD in the matrix factorization experiment, the performance improvement is relatively small. The authors don't fully explore the reasons for this (e.g., potential limitations of the mini-batch size, choice of hyperparameters) and a more detailed analysis is needed.
* **Correlation of Gradients:** The paper notes the importance of correlated gradients, but the discussion of how to achieve this in practice beyond the Brownian motion correlation is limited.  Exploring methods for explicitly correlating gradient estimates could further improve performance.
* **Computational Cost:** While RR extrapolation doesn't *require* higher-order integrators, it *doubles* the computational cost per iteration (running two chains).  A more thorough discussion of this trade-off – comparing the wall-clock time to achieve a certain level of accuracy – would be beneficial.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-supported method for improving the efficiency of SG-MCMC.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods.
* The experiments provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical findings.
* The work has the potential to be highly impactful in the field of Bayesian inference and scalable machine learning.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation details are not sufficiently discussed.
* The gains in the matrix factorization experiment are modest.
* The computational cost of running two chains is not fully addressed.


**5. Recommendation:**

**Accept.**  Despite the minor weaknesses, the paper's strengths—particularly the novel approach, strong theoretical analysis, and convincing experimental results—outweigh its drawbacks.  The contribution is significant and the work is likely to stimulate further research in this area. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly providing more detailed information about the implementation and a more nuanced discussion of the computational trade-offs.
