- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains with different step sizes and combining them to reduce bias.
- Theoretical claim: SGRRLD achieves MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵), improving over SGLD’s O(K⁻²/³).
- Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) and empirical validation on synthetic and real matrix factorization tasks.
- Novel and well-motivated extension of RR extrapolation from SDEs to the stochastic gradient setting.
- Experiments show significant bias and MSE improvements and demonstrate practical parallel implementation advantages.
- Relies on strong technical assumptions relegated to supplements, notably existence of C⁹ solutions to the Poisson equation for CLT proofs.
- Does not analyze how the constant C in Theorem 2 depends on gradient estimator moments, which may affect practical performance.
- Fails to compare directly with higher-order SG-MCMC methods (e.g., Chen et al.), leaving claims of matching higher-order accuracy unsubstantiated.
- Lacks clarity on practical parallel/distributed implementation details and synchronization of the two chains.
- Experimental timing comparisons are misleading: similar wall-clock times occur because SGRRLD runs half as many iterations, not shown at equal wall-clock budgets.
- Paper claims optimal step-size sequences but provides little guidance on sensitivity or how to choose step sizes (γ) in practice.
- Overstates practical significance of improved theoretical rates — improvement may be modest in realistic K (e.g., ~1.5× at K=10⁵).
- Overall: technically interesting and promising but needs stronger justification and practical validation; recommended weak acceptance pending revisions.
- Requested revisions: add direct comparisons with higher-order methods, detail parallel implementation and chain synchronization, compare accuracy at equal wall-clock time, and discuss step-size sensitivity/selection.
- Pro arguments: novel RR application to SG-MCMC, solid theoretical guarantees, strong experimental results, and exploitable parallel implementation.
- Con arguments: theoretical claims not fully substantiated, overemphasis on rate improvements, lack of higher-order comparisons, and insufficient practical implementation/sensitivity discussion.
