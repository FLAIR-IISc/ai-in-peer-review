

# Review for NIPS 2016

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel approach to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The method builds on the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique from numerical analysis, which has previously been applied to SDEs but not to SG-MCMC. SGRRLD runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), then combines their outputs using RR extrapolation to reduce bias. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and improved convergence rates compared to SGLD (the first-order SG-MCMC method). Specifically, they show that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error rate of O(K^(-4/5)) versus O(K^(-2/3)) for SGLD, and that it can match the accuracy of methods using higher-order integrators while only requiring a first-order integrator.

This work extends the NIPS literature on SG-MCMC, building on foundational papers by Welling and Teh (2011) who introduced SGLD, and Chen et al. (2015) who analyzed SG-MCMC with higher-order integrators. The authors also draw on the RR extrapolation literature from Talay and Tubaro (1990) and Lemaire et al. (2015), applying it to the SG-MCMC context for the first time. The paper provides a clear connection between numerical analysis techniques and MCMC sampling, which is valuable for the NIPS community.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a technically sound contribution with thorough theoretical analysis, including asymptotic consistency, central limit theorems, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE.
2. The method is embarrassingly parallel (the two chains can be run independently), which is a significant practical advantage for large-scale applications.
3. The empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization for movie recommendations) effectively support the theoretical claims, showing clear improvements in accuracy over SGLD.
4. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" but fails to provide experimental comparison with such methods (e.g., Chen et al. (2015) which uses higher-order integrators). This is a significant omission for a paper making such a strong claim.
2. There is an inconsistent statement about the step size in the paper (Section 3 mentions "γk = γ1k^(-α)" but earlier claims assume fixed step size).
3. Algorithm 1 in the supplementary document is inconsistent with Equation (5), which would make implementation difficult for readers.
4. The conceptual contribution is somewhat straightforward (applying RR extrapolation to SGLD), which may not be sufficiently novel for NIPS, especially given the recent trend toward more sophisticated SG-MCMC methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The theoretical bounds for bias and MSE are derived carefully, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims. However, the omission of comparison with higher-order integrator methods weakens the significance of the claimed improvements.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The theoretical contributions are presented logically, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. The supplementary material provides additional technical details and the algorithm pseudocode.

**Originality:** The core idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, but the implementation is relatively straightforward. The paper would have been more original if it had demonstrated the approach on a broader range of SG-MCMC methods beyond SGLD, or if it had provided a more comprehensive comparison with existing high-order methods.

**Significance:** The method offers a practical way to improve the accuracy of SG-MCMC without changing the fundamental algorithm structure, and the parallel nature makes it well-suited for distributed computing. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of comparison with existing high-order methods that already achieve similar theoretical accuracy.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- Pro: Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, theoretically sound with improved convergence rates, well-executed experiments, and practical parallelization benefits.
- Con: Incomplete comparison with existing high-order methods, inconsistent technical details, and the conceptual contribution is somewhat limited.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that makes a meaningful improvement to SG-MCMC methods. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the practical benefits and thorough theoretical analysis make it a valuable addition to the literature. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more comprehensive comparison to existing high-order methods and with the technical inconsistencies resolved. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these issues.
