This paper proposes a new method to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to a popular SG-MCMC algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The resulting algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combines their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. This work is situated within the broader effort to scale up Bayesian inference, building directly upon SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and its subsequent analysis. While previous works have tackled the bias issue by using higher-order numerical integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015), this paper presents a novel alternative based on extrapolating results from two parallel runs of a simpler first-order integrator. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, establishing its asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds on its bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate empirically that SGRRLD outperforms SGLD on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs (sketched in the main paper and expanded in the supplement). The analysis of the convergence rates is rigorous and provides clear insights into the algorithm's behavior. The authors derive that SGRRLD achieves an optimal MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of SGLD. The experimental methodology is sound, with clear setups and appropriate metrics for evaluation. The experiments on synthetic data effectively validate the theoretical findings regarding bias, MSE, and optimal step-size schedules. The large-scale matrix factorization experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the method in a real-world setting.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the proposed algorithm is explained logically, starting from the preliminaries of SGLD and RR extrapolation. The theoretical results are presented concisely in the main body, with proofs deferred to the supplement, which is appropriate for a conference paper.

However, there are a few minor points that could be improved:
- In Section 3, the definition of the step size sequence for the second chain, (ηk), is given as η₂k₋₁ = η₂k = γk/2. However, in Equation (5), the step size used for steps 2k+1 and 2k+2 is γₖ₊₁/2. This slight notational inconsistency between the text and the equation could be confusing.
- The key points I noted indicate a potential inconsistency between Algorithm 1 in the supplement and Equation (5) in the main text. The authors should double-check this to ensure the pseudo-code accurately reflects the method described.

**Originality:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is novel. While RR extrapolation is a well-known technique in numerical analysis for SDEs, its adaptation to the stochastic gradient setting for bias reduction in MCMC is a new and insightful contribution. This work presents a fresh perspective on improving SG-MCMC methods, distinct from the more common approach of designing more complex, higher-order integrators. The paper correctly positions this contribution with respect to prior work.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian machine learning. SGRRLD offers a practical and effective way to improve the accuracy of SGLD, a widely used algorithm. The key advantages are its simplicity and parallelizability. Since the two chains can be run independently, the method can leverage multi-core architectures to achieve a wall-clock time similar to a single SGLD run, while delivering higher accuracy. The finding that a simple, first-order integrator can be used to achieve the same theoretical accuracy as more complex higher-order integrators is an important result. This could encourage wider adoption of SG-MCMC in applications where high accuracy is critical.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful method for bias reduction in SG-MCMC by cleverly combining SGLD with the classical Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The work is backed by a solid theoretical analysis that formally proves the improved convergence rates of the proposed SGRRLD algorithm over standard SGLD.
3.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the method, showing significant reductions in bias and MSE on synthetic data and faster convergence to a better solution on a large-scale real-world task.
4.  **Practicality and Parallelizability:** The method is easy to implement and inherently parallel, making it well-suited for modern computational environments.

**Arguments against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The paper claims that SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. While this is supported by theory and a brief experiment on SGHMC (Figure 4), a more direct and thorough experimental comparison against a higher-order SGLD variant (e.g., from Chen et al., 2015) would have been valuable. This would help clarify the practical trade-offs in terms of implementation complexity, computational cost, and statistical efficiency.
2.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the method is parallelizable, it still requires double the total computational resources (CPU-hours) of a single SGLD chain. The paper demonstrates that this cost is justified by faster convergence in wall-clock time, but a more explicit discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful algorithm for improving SG-MCMC. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to expanding the experimental comparisons. The contribution is significant and of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
