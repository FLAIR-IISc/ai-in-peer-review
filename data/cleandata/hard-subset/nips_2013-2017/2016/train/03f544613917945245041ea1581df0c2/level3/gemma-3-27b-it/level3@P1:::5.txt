This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce the bias inherent in standard SG-MCMC methods like SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, a well-known numerical sequence acceleration method, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and then combining their results to obtain a lower-bias estimate.  A key strength is that the two chains can be executed embarrassingly parallel, requiring no communication between them, making the algorithm well-suited for distributed computing architectures.

The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for SGRRLD, demonstrating its asymptotic consistency and deriving bounds on its non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error (MSE).  They show that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates, both asymptotically and in finite-time, compared to SGLD, and even attains the theoretical accuracy of methods employing higher-order integrators. This is a significant result, as it suggests a way to improve accuracy without the increased complexity of higher-order methods. The work builds on prior work in SG-MCMC, particularly the foundational SGLD algorithm by Welling and Teh (2011), and leverages established numerical analysis techniques like RR extrapolation (Talay & Tubaro, 1990). Relevant recent advances in convergence analysis of SG-MCMC (e.g., Teh et al., 2016) are also acknowledged.

The paper's strengths lie in its conceptually simple yet effective approach, the rigorous theoretical analysis, and the supporting empirical results. The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic data, allowing for analytical validation, and real-world data (matrix factorization), demonstrating applicability to practical problems. The synthetic experiments convincingly illustrate the bias reduction and improved convergence rates compared to SGLD.  The real-world experiment on movie recommendation datasets further strengthens these findings, showcasing SGRRLD’s ability to deliver better performance with similar computational cost due to its parallel nature.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it is somewhat difficult to follow, and a clearer connection to previous work on convergence rates for different SG-MCMC schemes would be beneficial.  The authors claim SGRRLD achieves accuracy comparable to methods using higher-order integrators, yet they lack a direct experimental comparison to these methods. This omission weakens the claim and leaves the reader wondering how much more complex those methods are while yielding similar results. A direct comparison would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

There’s a slight inconsistency regarding step size selection that needs clarification.  Additionally, the algorithm description in the supplementary material (Algorithm 1) appears inconsistent with Equation (5) in the main text, potentially leading to confusion for those attempting to implement the method. This should be addressed for reproducibility.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a promising approach for bias reduction.
*   Strong theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency and improved convergence rates.
*   Convincing experimental results on both synthetic and real data.
*   Parallelizable nature of the algorithm makes it well-suited for large-scale applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of direct comparison to higher-order integration methods.
*   Minor inconsistencies in the description of the algorithm.
*   The theoretical discussion could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach to reducing bias and improving convergence rates without significantly increasing computational complexity. While the paper has a few minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the inconsistencies in the algorithm description and potentially including a comparison to higher-order integrators to bolster their claims.
