This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm designed to reduce the discretization bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The core contribution is the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a classic technique from numerical analysis, to the widely used Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs. This work builds a valuable bridge between the literature on SDE approximations and scalable Bayesian inference, presenting an alternative to bias-reduction techniques like higher-order integrators, which have been explored in recent NIPS papers.

The paper is of high quality, presenting a complete and technically sound piece of work. Its primary strength lies in its elegant combination of established techniques to create a novel and practical algorithm. The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution, providing formal convergence results, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic error bounds that clearly delineate the conditions under which SGRRLD improves upon SGLD. The method's originality stems from being the first, to my knowledge, to apply RRE in the SG-MCMC context. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the technical details accessible. Furthermore, the method is significant due to its practical utility; since the two chains can be run in parallel, it offers a computationally convenient way to improve accuracy. The empirical results support the theory, showing large gains on synthetic data and modest but consistent improvements on large-scale matrix factorization problems.

The main weakness is that the contribution, while solid, could be viewed as incremental rather than transformative. It is a straightforward, albeit clever, application of a known method to a new domain. While the gains are consistent, their modest scale on the large-scale experiments might limit the method's immediate practical impact compared to the baseline.

A couple of minor revisions would strengthen the paper:
1.  The caption for Figure 1a should be clarified. It appears to plot a Gaussian density using the empirical mean/variance, not the empirical posterior density itself, which could be misleading.
2.  The text discussing Figure 2a should explicitly address the counter-intuitive increase in bias for very small step sizes, explaining that this is an expected artifact of the O(1/Kγ) error term dominating when K is fixed.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that presents a useful, well-motivated, and theoretically-grounded contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. I recommend acceptance.
