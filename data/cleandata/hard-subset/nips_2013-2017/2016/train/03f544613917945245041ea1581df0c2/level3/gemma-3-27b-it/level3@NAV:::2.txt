## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in gradient estimation. The core idea—running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining the results—is a clever application of a known numerical acceleration technique to the SG-MCMC setting. The authors provide theoretical justification for improved convergence rates compared to standard SGLD and demonstrate bias reduction in synthetic experiments. 

The work builds upon the established foundation of SG-MCMC methods like SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and recent advances in bias reduction via higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015). The application of RR extrapolation, well-established in the context of SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), to SG-MCMC is a worthwhile contribution, and the theoretical analysis establishing convergence and rate improvements is commendable. The algorithm itself is clearly described and appears relatively straightforward to implement.

However, while the synthetic results convincingly demonstrate bias reduction, the real-data experiment on the MovieLens dataset offers only a marginal RMSE improvement. This weakens the practical significance claim.  The authors acknowledge this but stronger empirical evidence is needed to substantiate the benefits on realistic datasets.  Specifically, the improvement is arguable and requires a more robust demonstration. 

Several questions arise from the paper. The conditions on *U* required for Theorems 1 & 2 are not fully clarified.  Can *U* be multimodal, or does the theory rely on convexity?  Furthermore, the impact of gradient noise correlation is not fully explored. If gradient noises are uncorrelated, the parallel chains might decorrelate quickly, diminishing the error reduction. The paper mentions correlated Brownian motions but doesn’t detail observed correlation between the two chains in the real-data runs. Finally, the claim of comparable computation time to SGLD is potentially misleading. SGLD can *also* be parallelized by running multiple independent chains. A direct comparison to multi-chain SGLD (e.g., SGLD with two chains and a step size of γ/2) would better isolate the benefits of the RR extrapolation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence proofs.
*   Clear algorithm description and ease of implementation.
*   Demonstrated bias reduction in synthetic experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Marginal improvement on real-data experiments.
*   Unclear conditions for theoretical guarantees.
*   Insufficient discussion of gradient noise correlation.
*   Lack of comparison to multi-chain SGLD for a fair computational cost assessment.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the questions raised regarding the theoretical conditions, gradient noise, and computational cost.  Substantially more compelling real-data experiments are needed to support the practical significance of SGRRLD. The paper offers a valuable theoretical contribution and a promising approach, but further empirical validation is crucial.
