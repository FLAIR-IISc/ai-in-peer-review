- Proposes SGRRLD: accelerate SG-MCMC by running two parallel chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation.  
- Provides theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE claiming improved convergence rates vs SGLD.  
- Empirical results on synthetic data and matrix factorization show practical gains over standard SGLD.  
- Paper is well-written, situates method within SG-MCMC literature, and connects clearly to numerical-analysis RR extrapolation.  
- Theoretical contributions appear rigorous and provide a strong foundation for the method.  
- Novelty is incremental: prior work (Chen et al. 2015; Lemaire et al. 2015) already addresses higher-order integrators and RR extrapolation for invariant distributions.  
- Authors acknowledge prior connections but arguably underplay their significance.  
- Major practical concern: SGRRLD requires per-step communication between chains to maintain correlation, which may incur nontrivial overhead not addressed in the paper.  
- Experimental claims about similar wall-clock time lack detail on resource allocation and may implicitly use extra compute for SGRRLD.  
- Need clarification: were SGLD and SGRRLD given equal total compute resources, or did SGRRLD effectively use twice the resources?  
- Recommended baseline: include two independent SGLD chains running in parallel (no communication) to isolate RR extrapolation benefits from mere parallelization.  
- Experimental setup needs more detail on how compute was split and how gradient cost impacts wall-clock comparisons.  
- Minor typo: “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” duplicated on lines 25 and 70.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical foundation, clear presentation, promising experiments, and general applicability to SG-MCMC algorithms.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, unaddressed communication costs, unclear experimental resource allocation, and missing embarrassingly-parallel baseline.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and valuable contribution but diminished by incremental novelty and practical concerns; rated as weak accept pending clarification and stronger experimental baselines.
