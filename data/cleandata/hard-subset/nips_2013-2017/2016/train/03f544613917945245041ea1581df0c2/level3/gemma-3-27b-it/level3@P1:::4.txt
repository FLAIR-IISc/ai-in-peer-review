This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce the bias inherent in standard SG-MCMC methods like SGLD, while maintaining reasonable variance. The core idea is to apply a Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, essentially running two SGLD chains in parallel with differing step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on both bias and mean squared error.  Crucially, they claim improved convergence rates compared to SGLD, achieving accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators with a relatively simple first-order approach. The efficacy of SGRRLD is then evaluated through experiments on both synthetic data (Gaussian model, SGHMC) and a real-world problem – matrix factorization for movie recommendation – confirming the theoretical advantages.

The work builds directly on the growing body of research in SG-MCMC, notably stemming from the foundational work of Welling & Teh (2011) on SGLD.  Recent efforts have focused on improving the convergence properties of these methods, often through higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015; Ding et al., 2014).  This paper distinguishes itself by offering a relatively inexpensive method to reduce bias, a common bottleneck in SG-MCMC, without resorting to complex integration schemes. The use of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established technique in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), in the context of SG-MCMC is novel and represents a significant contribution.  Related work leveraging extrapolation techniques for variance reduction, such as multi-level Monte Carlo (Lemaire & Pagès, 2014), is acknowledged, but the application to SG-MCMC specifically appears to be a new direction.

The strengths of this paper are numerous.  The proposed algorithm is conceptually simple and, based on the description, relatively easy to implement. The extensive theoretical analysis is commendable, providing a strong foundation for the claims made about convergence rates and error bounds. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets lends credibility to the method. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the existing literature and correctly position their work within the broader field of Bayesian inference and stochastic optimization.  The potential for parallelization offered by running the two chains independently is a valuable advantage for large-scale applications.

However, there are weaknesses that need to be addressed.  The derivation of the convergence rates presented in Theorem 2, particularly the connection between the theoretical bounds and the observed empirical rates, is not entirely clear. The paper states optimal rates are achieved at α=1/3 for bias and α=0.2 for MSE, yet Figure 3 shows the best performance (lowest bias) at α=0.2. This discrepancy needs further explanation and clarification. The substantial fluctuations in the optimal curve in Figure 3 also raise concerns about the reliability of the empirical rate estimation. A more robust statistical analysis of the convergence behavior would strengthen the claims.

A significant practical concern arises from the timing comparison in Figure 5. SGRRLD inherently requires approximately three times the computation time of SGLD (two chains, one with twice the number of steps) for a single core implementation. Comparing runtimes directly is therefore misleading. A fair comparison would involve scaling the SGLD timing down by a factor of 2/3 to account for the increased computational cost of SGRRLD, which would likely reduce the reported performance gap.

Minor issues detract from the overall presentation.  Equation (1) uses the symbol *ϑ* while the rest of the paper consistently uses *θ*. The abbreviation "RR" is used without definition on line 94. Γ_K is undefined on line 131.  The requirement for "smooth function f" on line 160 is vague and needs to be accompanied by a precise statement of the required smoothness assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity of gradients).  Figure 2 has a labeling error ("Bias vs. time vs. dimension"). Vectors should consistently be represented in bold. Finally, line 256 raises a point about the dominance of the 1/(Kγ) term when γ is very small, which seems counterintuitive given the theoretical bounds and deserves further explanation. Why doesn't this term dominate the error in SGLD as γ approaches zero?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements on both synthetic and real data.
*   Potential for parallelization, making it suitable for large-scale applications.
*   Addresses a crucial issue in SG-MCMC – bias reduction – with a relatively simple approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Unclear derivation of convergence rates and discrepancies between theory and experiment (Figure 3).
*   Misleading timing comparison in Figure 5.
*   Minor presentation issues detract from clarity.
*   Lack of a thorough explanation regarding the behavior of SGLD at very small step sizes.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. However, the concerns regarding the convergence rate derivation, timing comparison, and minor presentation issues need to be addressed before acceptance. I recommend **conditional acceptance** pending revisions that clarify the theoretical results, provide a fair timing comparison, and address the minor errors. The authors should pay particular attention to explaining the discrepancies observed in Figure 3 and providing a more detailed analysis of the convergence behavior of both SGLD and SGRRLD.
