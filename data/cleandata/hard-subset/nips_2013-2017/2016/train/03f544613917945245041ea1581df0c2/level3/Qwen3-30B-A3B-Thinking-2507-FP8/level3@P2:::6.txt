

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors build upon SGLD by running two parallel chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, then combining results via RR extrapolation with correlated Brownian motions. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias O(γ²) and variance O(γ⁴), with non-asymptotic MSE bound O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). Experiments on synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate improved accuracy over SGLD.

The work relates well to prior NIPS literature: it extends the SGLD framework introduced by Welling & Teh (NIPS 2011), builds upon the convergence analysis of SG-MCMC methods by Chen et al. (NIPS 2015), and draws on RR extrapolation techniques from numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and their application to invariant distributions (Lemaire et al., 2015). The paper effectively situates its contribution within this body of work, acknowledging the prior analysis of higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) while arguing that RR extrapolation provides a more efficient path to achieving similar accuracy with first-order methods.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, with clear proofs of asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties.
2. The method is well-motivated by the bias-variance tradeoff in SG-MCMC, and the RR extrapolation approach elegantly addresses this.
3. The experimental results convincingly support the theoretical claims, particularly on the synthetic Gaussian model where analytical posteriors are available.
4. The paper clearly articulates how SGRRLD maintains the parallelizability of SG-MCMC methods, a key advantage for large-scale applications.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty concern:** The most significant limitation is that the core technique (applying RR to SG-MCMC) appears incremental given prior work. Chen et al. (NIPS 2015) already established that higher-order integrators can achieve similar bias reduction, and Lemaire et al. (2015) demonstrated RR extrapolation for invariant distributions. The paper would be stronger with a clearer articulation of how SGRRLD differs from these approaches (e.g., whether it achieves similar accuracy with lower computational cost).
   
2. **Practical applicability:** The paper does not adequately address communication costs in the parallel implementation. The two chains require correlated Brownian motions, which implies per-step communication between chains. This is particularly problematic for distributed systems where communication is expensive. The authors should clarify whether the wall-clock time comparisons in Figure 5 account for this communication overhead, and whether SGRRLD's theoretical speedup is offset by communication costs in practice.

3. **Experimental comparison:** The comparison to SGLD is somewhat unfair because SGRRLD uses two parallel chains (requiring double the computational resources for the same wall-clock time), while SGLD runs sequentially. A more appropriate baseline would be two independent SGLD chains (which would also require double resources but without the communication overhead), or comparing SGRRLD to a single SGLD chain with double the compute resources.

4. **Minor issue:** The paper redundantly defines "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" on lines 25 and 70.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The experimental design is appropriate for the claims, though the resource allocation in experiments needs clarification.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical presentation is precise, though some of the technical conditions in the proofs could be made more accessible.

**Originality:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, but the incremental nature of the contribution relative to Chen et al. (2015) and Lemaire et al. (2015) requires stronger justification.

**Significance:** The method provides a theoretically sound path to achieving higher accuracy with first-order SG-MCMC methods, which could be valuable for practitioners. However, the practical significance is tempered by the communication overhead in parallel implementation, which needs to be addressed for real-world deployment.

**Pro and Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a theoretically rigorous approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC without requiring higher-order integrators
- Demonstrates practical improvements in accuracy on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Maintains the parallelizability of SG-MCMC methods, crucial for large-scale applications

*Con:*
- Novelty appears incremental given prior work on numerical integrators and RR extrapolation for SDEs
- Communication cost in parallel implementation is not adequately addressed, potentially undermining the practical advantage
- Experimental comparison does not fairly account for resource allocation between SGRRLD and SGLD

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The theoretical contribution is sound and the experimental results are convincing, but the paper needs to more clearly articulate the novelty relative to Chen et al. (2015) and Lemaire et al. (2015), and address the communication cost concern by clarifying the experimental setup and resource allocation. A comparison to two independent SGLD chains as a baseline would strengthen the practical claims. The minor duplication of "ULA" should be fixed.
