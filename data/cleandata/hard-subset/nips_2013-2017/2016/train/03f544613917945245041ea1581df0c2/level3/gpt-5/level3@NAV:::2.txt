Summary and relation to prior work: The paper applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, instantiating Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). By running two SGLD chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining them with correlated Brownian increments, the authors prove asymptotically lower bias (O(γ2)) and MSE (O(γ4)) than SGLD, and non-asymptotic rates that translate into an MSE convergence O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3}) for SGLD under optimal schedules. RR extrapolation is classical for SDE weak-error acceleration (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup); the contribution here is its adaptation to SG-MCMC with convergence proofs and variance control via coupling. Synthetic linear-Gaussian experiments clearly show bias and MSE reductions; MovieLens results show only slight RMSE gains. The algorithm is clearly described and easy to implement.

Quality: The theoretical claims are compelling, but the paper defers key assumptions to the supplement. Please state in the main text the precise conditions on U, L, and f for Theorems 1–2 (smoothness order, dissipativity/strong-convexity outside a compact set, Lipschitz/Hölder requirements, polynomial growth). Are multimodal posteriors covered (e.g., coercive but nonconvex U) or do proofs require global strong convexity? What are the exact Poisson equation conditions. On the stochastic gradient side, theory suggests the Brownian coupling dominates; can you quantify regimes in which gradient noise dominates and whether uncorrelated mini-batches across chains degrade the error order? Did you share mini-batch indices between chains, and did you observe empirical state/gradient-noise correlation on real data?

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear; adding a pseudo-code in the main text and a schematic of the coupling would help. Please give guidance on step-size scheduling (α=1/5 for MSE) for practitioners and report sensitivity on real data.

Originality: While RR is known for SDEs, exporting it to SG-MCMC with rigorous convergence and variance control is novel and interesting.

Significance: Theoretical significance is high; practical impact is less clear given modest MovieLens gains. Claims about “same computation time” should be benchmarked against SGLD with two parallel chains averaged post hoc and SGLD at step size γ/2. An ESS/time or bias-vs-time comparison would isolate benefits due to extrapolation vs parallelism and step-size tuning. Additional real-data tasks (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, BNNs) would strengthen the case.

Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated algorithm with clear theory
- Strong synthetic evidence; generic applicability to SG-MCMC
- Careful variance-control via coupling

Cons:
- Assumptions and their applicability to realistic, possibly multimodal U not explicit
- Limited practical gains on real data; missing baselines (multi-chain SGLD, γ/2 SGLD)
- Empirical analysis of chain correlation and mini-batch coupling absent

Overall: Promising and technically solid; needs clarifications and stronger empirical comparisons to substantiate practical benefit.
