## Review of “Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo”

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC method designed to reduce the bias inherent in standard SG-MCMC algorithms like SGLD. The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, commonly found in numerical analysis, by running two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs to achieve a lower-bias estimate. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating reduced bias and improved mean squared error (MSE), along with empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (matrix factorization).

The paper is strong in several aspects. The problem addressed – bias in SG-MCMC – is well-motivated and crucial for reliable Bayesian inference at scale. The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity, requiring minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC implementations. The theoretical results, proving reduced bias and MSE, are a significant contribution, and the connection to higher-order integrators is insightful. Furthermore, the generality of the approach – its potential applicability to various SG-MCMC algorithms – promises broad impact. The paper is also clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and theoretical derivations.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. The authors pose a valid question regarding applicability – whether the expansion π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2) is a necessary condition for the technique to work. Clarifying this point would strengthen the paper. Additionally, exploring applicability to other approximate MCMC schemes like Distributed SGLD would broaden its perceived impact.  Regarding computational efficiency, running one chain for twice as long is inefficient; investigating a three-chain setup with equal steps (one γ, two γ/2) to avoid idle time is warranted. The authors should address the correlation between injected noise and mini-batch correlation. Is the same mini-batch optimal, or is there room for improvement? 

The matrix factorization experiment, while demonstrating the efficacy of SGRRLD, should ideally be run to full convergence and include a standard SGD baseline for a more comprehensive comparison and to highlight the Bayesian benefits. Finally, including an algorithm pseudocode box early in the paper would greatly enhance its accessibility for practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees of reduced bias and MSE.
*   Clear presentation and potential for broad applicability.
*   Promising empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Some questions regarding generality and applicability remain unanswered.
*   Computational efficiency concerns need further investigation.
*   Matrix factorization experiment could be more robust and contextualized.

**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While minor refinements are suggested, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and potential impact of SGRRLD strongly support its acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
