

This paper proposes SG-RR-MCMC, a novel approach to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The key insight is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), then combine their outputs via RR extrapolation. The authors theoretically show SG-RR-MCMC achieves O(γ²) bias and O(γ⁴) variance, with MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), superior to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The method is embarrassingly parallel, making it well-suited for distributed architectures.

The paper makes a clear conceptual contribution by applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a technique previously used for deterministic ODEs but not for stochastic gradient MCMC. It builds on foundational work by Welling and Teh (2011) on SGLD and extends theoretical analysis from Chen et al. (2015) on higher-order integrators. The empirical results on Gaussian models and matrix factorization demonstrate clear improvements over SGLD in both bias and MSE, with consistent performance across datasets.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical analysis, clear exposition, and compelling empirical validation. The embarrassingly parallel nature provides a practical advantage for large-scale applications. However, the paper has significant weaknesses: it fails to provide a direct comparison with high-order integrator methods (e.g., Chen et al.'s work), which claim similar theoretical improvements but require more complex implementations. There are also inconsistencies in the step size description and Algorithm 1 in the supplement contradicts Equation (5). While the paper claims improved rates, it doesn't prove the convergence rate is strictly better than existing high-order methods—it merely shows SG-RR-MCMC achieves the same theoretical accuracy as those methods.

The paper is well-written but would benefit from resolving these inconsistencies and providing the missing comparison. The significance is moderate—while the novelty is incremental, the simplicity and parallel nature make it a valuable practical alternative to complex high-order methods for practitioners. Overall, this is a technically sound contribution with clear practical benefits, though the lack of comprehensive comparison with existing high-order approaches weakens the novelty claim.
