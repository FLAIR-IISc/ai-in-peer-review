

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel method for accelerating Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The authors run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and correlated Gaussian noise, then combine the results using RR extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to a convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for the MSE compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The method is shown to achieve the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only a first-order integrator.

The paper situates its contribution well within the literature on SG-MCMC, acknowledging prior work on numerical integrators for SG-MCMC [10] and RR extrapolation for invariant distributions [23]. The authors extend [23] to the stochastic gradient setting by showing how to properly correlate the Brownian increments to minimize variance. The theoretical results are supported by both synthetic experiments on Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analysis with clear bounds on bias and MSE. The central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and technically sound.

2. **Practical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic data (where the true posterior is known) and real-world matrix factorization tasks convincingly demonstrate the theoretical claims. The results show consistent improvements in accuracy with comparable computational time.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the novel contributions. The connection between the RR extrapolation method and its application to SG-MCMC is well-motivated.

4. **Technical Contribution:** The paper successfully extends RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting with proper correlation of Brownian increments, which is a significant technical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty Concern:** While the theoretical results are strong, the novelty of the approach may be incremental. The paper builds upon [10] (which analyzes numerical integrators for SG-MCMC) and [23] (which applies RR to invariant distributions). The authors should more explicitly differentiate their contribution from these prior works, particularly regarding the specific application to SG-MCMC with correlated noise.

2. **Practical Applicability Concern:** The paper does not adequately address the communication cost required for the two parallel chains. The two chains must be run with correlated noise, which requires communication between chains at each step. This is a significant practical limitation that the authors do not discuss or quantify. For fair comparison, the authors should clarify how compute resources were allocated between the two chains (e.g., were the two chains running on separate processors, and if so, what was the total compute budget compared to SGLD?). The paper states that "their wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same," but this claim requires justification.

3. **Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares against SGLD but does not compare against two independent (embarrassingly parallel) SGLD chains as an alternative baseline. This would better illustrate whether the RR approach provides advantages over simply running two independent chains. The authors should address this in their revision.

4. **Minor Issue:** The term "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is duplicated in lines 25 and 70, which should be corrected.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with sound theoretical results and compelling experimental validation. The main concerns about novelty and practical applicability are significant but not insurmountable. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by extending RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with proper correlation of noise, and the results are promising.

I recommend acceptance with the following revisions:
1. Address the novelty concern by more explicitly differentiating the contribution from [10] and [23].
2. Provide detailed clarification on experimental setup, particularly regarding resource allocation between the two chains and communication cost.
3. Add a comparison against two independent SGLD chains as an alternative baseline.
4. Correct the duplicated ULA definition.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the SG-MCMC literature by demonstrating that a first-order integrator with RR extrapolation can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
