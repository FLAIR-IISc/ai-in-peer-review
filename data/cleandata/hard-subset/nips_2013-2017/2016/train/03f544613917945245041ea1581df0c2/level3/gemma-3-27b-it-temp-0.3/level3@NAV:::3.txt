## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper presents a novel and compelling approach to reducing the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea – leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a well-established technique in numerical analysis – to accelerate convergence is both elegant and intuitively sound. The authors convincingly argue that applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, specifically SGLD, can achieve higher convergence rates than standard methods, approaching the accuracy of higher-order integrators with minimal additional complexity. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of bias in SG-MCMC and positions the proposed solution within the existing literature. The theoretical analysis, demonstrating reduced bias and a corresponding reduction in MSE, is a significant strength. The authors carefully consider the correlation between injected noise in the two chains, a crucial detail for maintaining reasonable variance. The experiments, while relatively simple, provide initial support for the theoretical findings, showing improvements over SGLD and even a comparison to SGHMC with higher-order integration.

The novelty of applying RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC context is noteworthy. While RR extrapolation is a standard tool in numerical analysis, its adoption within the machine learning and Bayesian inference communities appears limited. This work bridges that gap, offering a potentially impactful technique applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms. The simplicity of implementation – requiring minimal changes to existing algorithms – further enhances its practicality.

However, several questions and concerns remain. The authors acknowledge the applicability condition π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2), but a more thorough discussion of how broadly this holds across different SG-MCMC methods would be beneficial. Extending the technique to other approximate MCMC schemes like subsampling MH or Distributed SGLD warrants exploration. The computational cost of running one chain twice as long is a valid concern; investigating a three-chain approach with equal steps per chain, and analyzing the trade-off between compute and variance, would strengthen the work. 

Furthermore, the matrix factorization experiment, while promising, should be run to full convergence and include a baseline SGD comparison to better demonstrate the Bayesian benefits and contextualize the final errors. Finally, a pseudocode box early in the paper would significantly aid practitioners in implementing the method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of a well-established numerical technique to a relevant problem in Bayesian inference.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with proven bias reduction and MSE improvement.
*   Simple implementation and broad applicability across SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation, particularly regarding convergence and comparison to standard optimization methods.
*   Unclear applicability to all SG-MCMC methods.
*   Potential computational inefficiency due to chain length imbalance.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. Addressing the concerns outlined above would further solidify its contribution and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
