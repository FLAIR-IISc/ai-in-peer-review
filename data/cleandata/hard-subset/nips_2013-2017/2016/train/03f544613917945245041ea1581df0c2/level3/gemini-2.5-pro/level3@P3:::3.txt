This paper addresses the problem of asymptotic bias in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, a key limitation for their practical application. The authors propose a novel and elegant solution based on Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a well-established technique from numerical analysis. The core idea, which they name Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) when applied to SGLD, involves running two parallel chains with different step sizes, γ and γ/2. To control the variance of the resulting estimator, the injected Gaussian noise across the two chains is correlated. By taking a specific linear combination of the estimators from these two chains, the leading O(γ) bias term is cancelled, resulting in a new estimator with a much smaller O(γ^2) bias.

This work builds upon the extensive literature on SG-MCMC, which began with SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and has since explored sources of error and bias. While previous work (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) has focused on developing complex higher-order integrators to reduce discretization bias, this paper introduces a conceptually simpler and more general approach to achieve similar theoretical guarantees. The application of RR extrapolation is novel within the SG-MCMC context and represents a valuable cross-pollination of ideas from classical numerical analysis to modern machine learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed method is remarkably simple to implement. It requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms, essentially boiling down to running a standard algorithm twice with different settings and combining the results. This low barrier to entry is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They prove that their method improves the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵), matching the rate of more complex higher-order integrators.
*   **Generality and Potential Impact:** The RR extrapolation principle appears to be broadly applicable to any SG-MCMC method whose bias admits a particular Taylor-like expansion in the step size. The authors demonstrate this by applying it to SGHMC in experiments. This generality suggests the work could have a wide-ranging and significant impact on the practice of large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the method is explained logically, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The connection to the existing literature is also well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The paper is a strong contribution, but several points could be clarified or strengthened to improve it further.

1.  **Conditions for Applicability:** The authors state the technique is "quite generic," but the theoretical justification relies on the existence of an expansion of the form π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²). Could the authors clarify if this property is known to hold for a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD/SGHMC? A brief discussion on which algorithms satisfy this condition (and which might not) would help define the scope of the method's applicability more clearly. For instance, can this be applied to other approximate MCMC schemes like subsampling Metropolis-Hastings or distributed SGLD variants?

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed scheme runs one chain with step size γ and a second with γ/2, where the second chain takes two steps for every one step of the first. This implies that the computational resource allocated to the first chain is idle half the time. Have the authors considered an alternative parallelization scheme? For instance, one could run three chains in parallel for the same number of iterations: one with step size γ and two independent chains with step size γ/2. The estimators from the two γ/2 chains could be averaged before extrapolation. This would ensure full utilization of three parallel workers. How would such a scheme affect the variance of the final estimator compared to the proposed approach?

3.  **Role of Minibatch Correlation:** The paper rightly emphasizes the importance of correlating the injected Gaussian noise to control variance. However, the role of the stochasticity introduced by minibatching is less clear. The theoretical analysis suggests the correlation of stochastic gradients does not impact the asymptotic variance. Does this imply that using the same minibatch for the corresponding steps in the two chains is unnecessary? A comment on the practical effect (or lack thereof) of correlating the data subsamples would be very helpful for practitioners.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the method's benefits. However, the matrix factorization experiment could be made more compelling.
    *   The plots show RMSE over time but seem to stop before either method has fully converged. Extending the runs would provide a clearer picture of the final performance difference.
    *   Including a standard non-Bayesian baseline like SGD would be highly valuable. This would help justify the additional complexity of a Bayesian approach by showing its benefits (e.g., better final accuracy, uncertainty quantification) and would provide a more complete context for interpreting the reported RMSE values.

5.  **Possible Extensions:** The paper notes that RR extrapolation can be applied recursively. Could the authors briefly comment on the feasibility and potential trade-offs of extending this to three or more step sizes (e.g., γ, γ/2, γ/4) to cancel even higher-order bias terms? Would the variance control become significantly more complex?

6.  **Presentation Suggestion:** For the benefit of practitioners eager to apply this method, including a pseudocode algorithm box in the main paper (rather than just the supplement) would be a great addition. This would make the exact implementation steps, including the noise correlation, immediately clear without needing to parse the formal definitions.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a simple, powerful, and theoretically-grounded technique for improving a wide class of SG-MCMC algorithms. The contribution is novel, significant, and presented with great clarity. Addressing the points above would further strengthen an already impressive piece of work.
