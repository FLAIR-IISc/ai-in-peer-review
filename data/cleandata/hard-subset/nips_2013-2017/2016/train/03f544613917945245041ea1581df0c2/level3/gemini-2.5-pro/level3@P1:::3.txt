This paper addresses the well-known problem of asymptotic bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, which arises from the discretization of the underlying stochastic differential equation (SDE). The authors propose a novel and elegant method to reduce this bias by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains for the same target distribution but with different step sizes: one with step size γ and another with step size γ/2. To ensure both chains simulate up to the same time horizon, the γ/2 chain is run for twice as many iterations. The final estimator is constructed by a specific linear combination of the estimators from the two chains (specifically, `2 * estimator(γ/2) - estimator(γ)`), which cancels out the leading order bias term.

The authors apply this framework to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), creating a new algorithm they call Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). A key insight for controlling the variance of the combined estimator is to introduce a correlation between the Gaussian noise injected into the two chains. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, proving that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves a faster rate of convergence (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) compared to standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This theoretical improvement is significant, as it matches the accuracy of methods using more complex higher-order integrators while only requiring a first-order integrator. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Strengths (Arguments Pro-Acceptance)**

1.  **Quality & Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem in a core area of machine learning. The bias of SG-MCMC methods is a known bottleneck, and this work provides a well-founded and effective solution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds that clearly demonstrate the advantage of the proposed method. Achieving the convergence rates of higher-order integrators with a conceptually simpler modification is a strong contribution.

2.  **Originality & Impact:** While RR extrapolation is a known technique in numerical analysis for SDEs, its application to the SG-MCMC setting is novel and impactful. Given the widespread use of SGLD and its variants, this technique has the potential for broad adoption. The method is general and, as demonstrated with SGHMC, can likely be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms, significantly increasing its potential impact on the field.

3.  **Clarity & Simplicity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core idea is intuitive and easy to grasp. The method itself is remarkably simple to implement, requiring minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC codebases—essentially running a second chain in parallel and combining the results. This simplicity is a major advantage for practical adoption.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The authors state the technique is "quite generic," but the theoretical justification for RR extrapolation relies on the existence of a specific error expansion of the form π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²). Could the authors clarify if this expansion is known to hold for the general class of SG-MCMC methods? It would strengthen the paper to discuss the precise conditions an algorithm must satisfy for this technique to be applicable. For instance, could it be applied to methods like subsampling Metropolis-Hastings or distributed SG-MCMC variants?

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm runs one chain for K steps and the other for 2K steps. If run in parallel, the process running the K-step chain will be idle for half the time. While wall-clock time is the same, this represents an inefficient use of computational resources. Have the authors considered an alternative scheme, for example, running three chains: one with step size γ for K steps, and two independent chains with step size γ/2 for K steps each? This would fully utilize the parallel resources. How would averaging the two γ/2 chains before extrapolation affect the variance bounds?

3.  **Noise and Minibatch Correlation:** The paper correctly identifies that correlating the Brownian motions is crucial for variance reduction and shows that perfect correlation (Σ = I_d) is optimal. However, the role of the stochastic gradient noise is less clear. The authors state that the sequences of estimators can be correlated but do not assume an explicit form. Would using the *same* minibatch of data for the corresponding steps in the two chains be beneficial for variance reduction, or is its effect negligible compared to the injected Gaussian noise? Some discussion on this practical choice would be valuable.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are encouraging but could be strengthened. In the large-scale matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5), the runs are stopped after a fixed time. It would be more convincing to run the algorithms until convergence (or until the error plateaus) to see the final performance gap. Furthermore, including a standard non-Bayesian baseline like SGD would help contextualize the results and better demonstrate the practical benefit of using a Bayesian approach for this task.

5.  **Presentation:** The paper would benefit greatly from a clear pseudocode box for the SGRRLD algorithm, preferably early in the paper (e.g., in Section 3). This would make the method immediately accessible to practitioners who may wish to implement it without working through the theoretical sections in detail.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, simple, and effective method for a significant problem in scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical contributions are strong and the potential for impact is high. While there are some points that could be clarified regarding the scope of applicability, computational strategy, and experimental setup, these are minor weaknesses. The strengths of the paper far outweigh them. I recommend acceptance.
