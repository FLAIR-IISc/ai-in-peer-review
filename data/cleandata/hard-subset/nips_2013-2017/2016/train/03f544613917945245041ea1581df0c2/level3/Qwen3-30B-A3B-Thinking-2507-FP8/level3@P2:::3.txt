

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes an elegant extension to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods that significantly reduces bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC algorithms, specifically building on SGLD to create Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The key insight is to run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2, then combine their estimates to cancel out the leading bias term. This builds on foundational work by Welling and Teh (NIPS 2011) on SGLD and recent advances in SG-MCMC by Chen et al. (NIPS 2015), while drawing inspiration from numerical analysis techniques like RR extrapolation (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015).

**Quality**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic consistency, satisfies a central limit theorem, and reduces both bias and MSE compared to standard SG-MCMC methods. The authors prove that SGRRLD's bias is O(γ²) and MSE is O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(γ) bias and O(K⁻²/³) MSE. These bounds are derived under reasonable technical assumptions and are supported by thorough experimental validation on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task. The theoretical contribution is solid and well-justified.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The key concepts are explained clearly with appropriate mathematical formalism, and the presentation flows logically from problem statement to theory to experiments. The authors make an excellent effort to connect their method to existing work in both numerical analysis and machine learning. A pseudocode box for SGRRLD would further enhance clarity for practitioners, as suggested in the key points.

**Originality**: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel to the machine learning community. While RR extrapolation has been established in numerical analysis, its use in SG-MCMC is a fresh perspective that addresses a fundamental limitation of current methods (bias). The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by showing how this technique can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods.

**Significance**: The method is highly significant for several reasons. First, it's remarkably simple to implement, requiring minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC codebases. Second, it's broadly applicable to many SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD (as noted in the paper), potentially having wide impact across Bayesian machine learning. Third, the experiments demonstrate clear computational advantages: on the MovieLens datasets, SGRRLD achieves comparable or better accuracy than SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time. This efficiency gain is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

**Strengths**: The paper's greatest strengths are its theoretical rigor, simplicity of implementation, and broad applicability. The method's elegance lies in how it leverages existing numerical techniques to solve a fundamental problem in SG-MCMC with minimal overhead. The experimental results robustly support the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses/Concerns**: The paper would benefit from addressing several specific points:
1. Clarify whether the technique applies to all SG-MCMC methods or only those satisfying π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ²) (as implied in the paper).
2. Discuss applicability to other approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling MH) or distributed implementations.
3. Address the computational efficiency concern: running one chain twice as long may waste compute; a 3-chain approach (one γ, two γ/2 running equal steps) might avoid idle time.
4. Provide more clarity on the required correlation between injected noise and the effect of correlating minibatches—specifically, whether using the same minibatch is optimal.
5. Include an SGD baseline in the matrix factorization experiments to contextualize the Bayesian benefit and final errors.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
*Pro*: Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, theoretically sound with strong guarantees, simple to implement, broadly applicable, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements over SGLD.
*Con*: Some technical clarifications needed regarding applicability scope, and the computational efficiency concern should be addressed.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical results are well-supported, the method is elegant and practical, and the experimental validation demonstrates clear benefits. The minor concerns raised are important for completeness but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

This work represents a meaningful advancement in SG-MCMC that should be of interest to the NIPS community. It successfully bridges numerical analysis techniques with machine learning, offering a simple yet powerful solution to a well-known problem. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that addressing the minor clarifications would further strengthen the paper.
