### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method to improve the performance of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to reduce the discretization bias inherent in SG-MCMC methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (e.g., γ and γ/2) and combines their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. This work builds upon previous research on SGLD and the application of RR extrapolation to standard SDEs, but is the first to combine these ideas in the stochastic gradient setting. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including proofs of consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). These results show that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard first-order SG-MCMC methods (O(K⁻²/³)). The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on both synthetic and real-world large-scale datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and clever combination of a classical numerical method with modern large-scale Bayesian inference. The idea of using RR extrapolation to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC is new and significant. If the claims hold, the proposed method offers a substantial improvement in convergence rates, which is a critical issue in scalable MCMC.
2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper is supported by strong theoretical contributions. The authors provide a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results rigorously establish the advantages of the proposed method and show that it can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order schemes.
3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is conceptually simple and easy to implement. It requires minimal modification to an existing SGLD implementation—essentially running it twice with different step sizes. The paper is generally well-written and organized.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The method is inherently parallel, as the two chains can be run independently. This makes it well-suited for modern multi-core architectures, a key consideration for large-scale machine learning. The experiments on the MovieLens datasets demonstrate practical benefits on a real-world problem.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Derivations (Major Concern):** A central claim of the paper is the improved convergence rates for bias (O(K⁻²/³)) and MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) for optimally chosen step sizes. While Theorem 2 provides the non-asymptotic bounds, the paper does not show the explicit derivation of how these specific rates are obtained from the bounds. This derivation, which involves optimizing the step size decay rate α in γₖ ∝ k⁻ᵃ, is a crucial link between the main theorem and the paper's headline results. Its omission creates a significant logical gap in the paper's theoretical argument. This should be included in the main text or appendix to make the work self-contained and verifiable.
2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison (Practical Concern):** The experiments, particularly for the large-scale matrix factorization problem (Figure 5), compare SGRRLD and SGLD based on wall-clock time, leveraging the parallel nature of SGRRLD. While valid on a multi-core system, this comparison obscures the fact that SGRRLD performs significantly more computation (1.5x more gradient updates in the paper's setup). On a single-core machine, SGRRLD would be slower. The performance gap might be less pronounced if SGLD were allowed a correspondingly larger number of iterations to match the total computational budget. A more transparent discussion of the computational cost trade-offs is needed.
3.  **Inconsistency in Experimental Results:** There is a direct contradiction between the text and a key figure. In Section 5.1, the text states that the optimal step-size decay rate for the bias is α = 1/3. However, in Figure 3 (left), the plot clearly shows the curve for α = 0.2 (red) achieving the lowest bias, while the curve for α = 1/3 (magenta) performs worse. This discrepancy undermines confidence in the experimental validation of the theory.
4.  **Clarity of Experimental Phenomena:** In Figure 2, the bias/MSE of both SGLD and SGRRLD increases for very small step sizes γ. The paper attributes this to the `1/(Kγ)` term in the bounds dominating for fixed K. This is a reasonable explanation, but a more detailed discussion would be beneficial. For instance, why does this effect appear more pronounced for SGRRLD in the MSE plot? A comparative analysis would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the "optimal" α=0.2 curve in Figure 3 exhibits substantial fluctuations; an explanation for this behavior would be helpful.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   Please provide the full derivation of the optimal convergence rates (O(K⁻²/³) for bias and O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) from the bounds in Theorem 2. This is critical for the paper's completeness.
*   In the experimental section, please discuss the total computational cost (e.g., total number of gradient evaluations) in addition to wall-clock time to provide a fuller picture of the method's efficiency.
*   Please resolve the inconsistency regarding the optimal α for bias between the text (claims α=1/3) and Figure 3 (shows α=0.2 is best).
*   **Minor issues:**
    *   Line 94: The acronym "RR" is used before it is defined.
    *   Line 131: The notation `ΓK` is used before it is defined (it is defined after Theorem 1).
    *   Line 160: The term "smooth function f" is used. Please state the precise smoothness assumptions required for the theorems to hold (e.g., number of continuous derivatives).
    *   Equation 1 uses `ϑ` for the process, while the rest of the paper uses `θ`. Please use consistent notation.
    *   The y-axis label for Figure 1(b) appears to be incorrect. It is labeled "Bias of Var" but the caption describes it as monitoring the MSE of the posterior covariance.
    *   For readability, consider using boldface for vectors throughout the manuscript.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a very interesting and potentially impactful idea. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the proposed method is elegant. However, the review is predicated on a few major concerns that prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. The lack of a clear derivation for the key convergence rates is a significant omission, and the inconsistency in the experimental results needs to be addressed. If the authors can convincingly address these points, particularly the derivation of the rates and the experimental contradiction, this would be a very strong contribution to the NIPS conference.
