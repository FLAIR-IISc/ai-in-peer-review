This paper proposes a method to reduce the discretization bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis. The main idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combine their estimators to cancel out the leading-order bias term. The authors focus on applying this technique to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), resulting in a new algorithm they term Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD).

The work builds upon the extensive literature on SG-MCMC, initiated by Welling and Teh (2011), which aims to scale Bayesian inference to large datasets. A key challenge in SG-MCMC is the bias introduced by the numerical discretization of the underlying stochastic differential equation (SDE). While prior work, such as Chen et al. (2015), has addressed this by developing higher-order integrators, this paper presents an alternative and elegant solution. It draws inspiration from the numerical SDE literature (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), where RR extrapolation is a well-established variance reduction and bias correction technique. The novelty of this paper lies in adapting, analyzing, and demonstrating the effectiveness of this approach within the SG-MCMC framework, which involves the additional complexity of stochastic gradients.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality:** The paper is technically solid. It provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed SGRRLD algorithm, including proofs of asymptotic consistency and a Central Limit Theorem. Crucially, the non-asymptotic analysis derives explicit bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates (e.g., MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This demonstrates that a simple combination of two first-order integrators can match the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. The claims are well-supported by this theoretical foundation.

2.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by successfully bridging the numerical SDE literature with the SG-MCMC literature. While RR extrapolation is a known technique, its application and analysis in the context of stochastic gradients are novel and important. The resulting SGRRLD algorithm is a practical and effective tool for practitioners. It offers a simple way to improve the accuracy of widely used methods like SGLD and SGHMC without requiring the implementation of complex higher-order schemes. The method's inherent parallelism makes it particularly appealing for modern computational environments.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated plainly. The connection to prior work is well-articulated, making it clear where this work fits into the existing literature.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model nicely illustrate the theoretical findings, clearly showing the reduction in bias and MSE compared to standard SGLD. The experiments on large-scale matrix factorization with the MovieLens datasets demonstrate that these theoretical improvements translate into meaningful performance gains on real-world problems, with SGRRLD achieving better accuracy in less wall-clock time. The extension to SGHMC also effectively showcases the generality of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for improving clarity:

1.  In Figure 1(a), the caption should be more precise. It appears the plots for SGLD and SGRRLD are not empirical density estimates (e.g., via kernel density estimation) but rather Gaussian densities parameterized by the empirical mean and variance of the samples. Clarifying this would prevent any potential misinterpretation that the empirical posteriors are perfectly Gaussian.

2.  In the discussion of Figure 2, the paper notes that the bias and MSE increase for very small step sizes γ. While the text mentions that this is due to the `1/(Kγ)` term in the error bounds becoming dominant when K is fixed, this is a somewhat counter-intuitive result that warrants a more explicit discussion in the main text accompanying the figure. Highlighting this trade-off would strengthen the reader's understanding of the practical behavior of the algorithm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses the important and well-known problem of discretization bias in SG-MCMC methods.
*   Proposes a simple, practical, and effective solution that is easy to implement and naturally parallelizable.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees of improved convergence rates, a significant advance over standard first-order methods.
*   Empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over SGLD.
*   The work builds a valuable bridge between the numerical analysis and machine learning communities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The core technique of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is not new in itself. The primary novelty is its application and analysis in the SG-MCMC setting.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method is simple yet powerful, backed by solid theory and compelling experiments. It is likely to be impactful and useful for both researchers and practitioners working with SG-MCMC methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
