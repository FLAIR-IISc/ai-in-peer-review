### Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method to improve the performance of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes—one with step size `γ_k` and another taking two steps of size `γ_k/2` for each step of the first—and combine their outputs. This simple scheme, termed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) when applied to SGLD, is designed to cancel out the leading-order bias term inherent in the Euler-Maruyama discretization. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). These results show that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates (e.g., MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)), effectively matching the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators while using only a first-order scheme. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths (Quality, Originality, Significance)**

1.  **Elegant and Practical Method:** The proposed RR scheme is a clever and practical way to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC methods. Its main appeal lies in its simplicity; it requires minimal modification to existing algorithms like SGLD and is straightforward to implement.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is supported by solid theoretical analysis. The authors provide a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, which formally establish the improved convergence rates. Achieving a higher-order MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) with a first-order integrator is a significant theoretical advance for the SG-MCMC literature.

3.  **Novelty and Generality:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is, to my knowledge, novel. It bridges a classical numerical analysis technique with modern large-scale Bayesian inference. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by applying it to SGHMC, suggesting it could be a valuable tool for a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses (Quality, Clarity)**

The paper is promising, but there are several major and minor concerns that weaken the current submission and require clarification.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Derivation of Convergence Rates:** A central claim of the paper is the improved convergence rates derived from Theorem 2. While the bounds in Theorem 2 are stated, the derivation of the optimal rates (e.g., MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) from γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵) is presented as a quick calculation. Given that this is the main theoretical result, this step is critical and warrants a more detailed and careful explanation in the main paper. The logic seems plausible, but its justification should be more rigorous to be fully convincing.

2.  **Unfair Experimental Comparison:** The practical advantage of SGRRLD is overstated due to a potentially unfair comparison in Figure 5. The authors state that the total computation of SGRRLD for `K` iterations is roughly 1.5x that of SGLD for `K` iterations (the paper compares SGRRLD at `K/2` iterations to SGLD at `K` iterations, meaning `3*(K/2)=1.5K` total gradient steps for SGRRLD vs `K` for SGLD). While the wall-clock time is similar when using two cores for SGRRLD versus one for SGLD, this conflates algorithmic efficiency with hardware utilization. A fairer comparison of algorithmic efficiency would grant SGLD the same total computational budget. For example, the reported wall-clock time for SGLD could be scaled by approximately 2/3 (i.e., `1/1.5`). This would likely reduce the performance gap shown in Figure 5 and provide a more honest assessment of the method's practical speed-up.

3.  **Contradiction in Experimental Results (Figure 3):** There is a direct contradiction between the theory and experimental results. The text (end of Section 4) claims that the optimal rate for the bias is achieved with a step-size decay of `α = 1/3`. However, in Figure 3 (left), the curve for `α = 0.2` (red) is clearly superior, achieving a much lower bias than the `α = 1/3` curve (green). This discrepancy needs to be addressed. Furthermore, the supposed "optimal" curves in Figure 3 fluctuate significantly more than others, which may suggest issues with variance or stability that are not discussed.

4.  **Unexplained Behavior in Figure 2:** In the discussion of Figure 2 (line 256), the authors attribute the U-shaped curve for SGRRLD's bias/MSE to the `1/(Kγ)` term dominating at small `γ`. This is plausible. However, the theoretical bounds for SGLD also contain a `1/(Kγ)` term, yet the corresponding SGLD curves in Figure 2 are monotonic. Why does this term appear to affect SGRRLD but not SGLD in the plotted range? This inconsistency undermines the provided explanation and requires clarification.

**Minor Issues:**

*   **Notation:** Equation (1) uses `ϑ` for the process, while `θ` is used everywhere else. Please use consistent notation. Vectors should ideally be bolded for clarity.
*   **Definitions:** The acronym "RR" is used in the text (line 94) before it is defined. The term `Γ_K` is mentioned as being used before its definition (line 131).
*   **Assumptions:** The theorems state they hold for a "smooth function f" (line 160). The proof sketch mentions `C⁹` smoothness, which is a very strong condition. These precise assumptions should be stated upfront in the main theorem statements.
*   **Figure Labeling:** The user noted a potential labeling error in a figure (line 249, "Bias vs. time vs. dimension"). Please double-check all figure labels and captions for accuracy.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents an interesting, simple, and theoretically-backed method for improving SG-MCMC. The potential for achieving higher-order convergence rates with a first-order method is highly significant. However, the manuscript is currently undermined by several critical issues, including a contradiction between theory and experiments, an unfair computational comparison, and a lack of clarity on key results.

While the core idea is strong, the paper is not yet ready for publication. I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors can thoroughly address the major concerns outlined above, particularly by reconciling the claims with the experimental results and providing a fairer computational comparison.
