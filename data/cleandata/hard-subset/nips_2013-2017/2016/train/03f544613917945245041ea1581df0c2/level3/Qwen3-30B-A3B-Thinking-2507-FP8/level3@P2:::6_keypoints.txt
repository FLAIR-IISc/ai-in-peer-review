- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two correlated chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining outputs.  
- Theoretical results: asymptotic bias O(γ²), variance O(γ⁴), and non-asymptotic MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).  
- Empirical results: improved accuracy over SGLD on synthetic Gaussian and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.  
- Related work: builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh), SG-MCMC convergence analyses (Chen et al.), and RR extrapolation literature (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire et al.).  

Strengths:
- Provides thorough, rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs of convergence properties.  
- Well-motivated solution addressing the bias-variance tradeoff via RR extrapolation.  
- Experiments support theoretical claims, especially where analytic posteriors are available.  
- Retains parallelizability of SG-MCMC, important for large-scale applications.  

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental relative to Chen et al. (higher-order integrators) and Lemaire et al. (RR for invariant distributions); clearer differentiation is needed.  
- Practical applicability concern: correlated Brownian motions imply per-step communication between chains, which may negate speedups in distributed settings.  
- Experimental comparisons may be unfair: SGRRLD uses two parallel chains (double resources); should compare to two independent SGLD chains or adjust resource allocation.  
- Minor duplication: "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" defined redundantly.  

Other assessments:
- Quality: technically sound; experiments appropriate but need clarification on resource use.  
- Clarity: well-organized and precise; some technical conditions could be more accessible.  
- Originality: novel application of RR to SG-MCMC but incremental in context of prior work.  
- Significance: offers a theoretically sound route to higher accuracy with first-order SG-MCMC, but practical significance may be limited by communication overhead.  

Recommendation:
- Accept with minor revisions to better articulate novelty, address communication and experimental resource concerns, add a fair baseline, and remove the ULA duplication.
