This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) for large-scale Bayesian inference. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to SGLD by running two chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees, including consistency and a central limit theorem, and demonstrate improvements over SGLD on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper builds upon a rich body of work in both MCMC and stochastic gradient methods. SGLD itself, introduced by Welling & Teh (ICML 2011), revolutionized Bayesian inference for large datasets. Subsequent work (e.g., Ahn et al., ICML 2012; Chen et al., ICML 2014; Ding et al., NIPS 2014) explored extensions like SGHMC and adaptive Langevin thermostats.  The convergence analysis of SGLD and related methods has been actively investigated (e.g., Sato & Nakagawa, ICML 2014; Teh et al., JMLR 2016; Teh et al., arXiv 2015), providing the foundation for the theoretical results presented here.  The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SDEs, notably by Talay & Tubaro (1990) and Lemaire & Pagès (2002), provides the theoretical justification for the proposed approach, bridging the MCMC and SDE literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The paper is rigorously written with a clear mathematical presentation. The theoretical results appear correct and well-motivated. The derivation of convergence rates and the connection to existing SDE theory are particularly strong.
* **Clear and Well-Organized:** The paper is easy to follow, with a logical structure and clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
* **Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a known technique, its application to SGLD and the resulting SGRRLD algorithm represent a novel contribution. The extension to SGHMC demonstrates generality.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of SGRRLD. The improvements in accuracy and/or speed compared to SGLD are consistent across different datasets.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm is relatively straightforward to implement, requiring only running two SGLD chains in parallel, making it computationally convenient and suitable for distributed architectures.
* **Strong Connection to Existing Work:**  The authors effectively position their work within the context of existing research in both MCMC and numerical methods for SDEs.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Modest Gains:** While statistically significant, the improvements observed on large-scale problems (e.g., MovieLens datasets) are modest. The authors acknowledge this. The method is valuable but not a paradigm shift.
* **Figure 1a Clarification Needed:** Figure 1a displays a Gaussian curve overlaid on the posterior distribution. However, the caption doesn't explicitly state that this Gaussian represents a Gaussian with *empirical* mean and variance, not the actual empirical posterior itself. This is misleading and should be clarified. The figure suggests the Gaussian *is* the posterior, which is not true.
* **Figure 2a Discussion Needed:** Figure 2a shows that the bias may not decrease monotonically as the step size decreases. This counter-intuitive result requires a more in-depth discussion. The authors allude to the 1/(Kγ) term in the bounds, but a more intuitive explanation of why a very small step size can lead to increased bias would benefit the reader.
* **Limited Exploration of Correlation:** The theoretical analysis allows for correlated Brownian increments, but the experiments primarily focus on the simplest case (Σ = Id). A more thorough investigation of the impact of different correlation structures on performance would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of scalable MCMC.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD in improving the accuracy of SGLD.
* The method is relatively easy to implement and can be readily applied to existing SGMCMC algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements over SGLD on large-scale problems are modest.
* Minor issues with clarity in Figures 1a and 2a.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. While the improvements over SGLD are not dramatic, the theoretical and empirical evidence supports the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The minor issues with clarity can be easily addressed with revisions.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should clarify Figure 1a and provide a more detailed discussion of the non-monotonic behavior of bias with respect to step size in Figure 2a.  A brief discussion of potential benefits of exploring different correlation matrices in (8) would also be beneficial.
