

This paper proposes SGRRLD, a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The key insight is running two parallel chains with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors prove SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias (O(γ²) vs. SGLD's O(γ)) and MSE (O(γ⁴) vs. O(γ²)), with convergence rates of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The algorithm is clearly described and easily implementable.

Theoretical strength is evident in the rigorous convergence analysis, though the required conditions on U (e.g., whether multimodal potentials are allowed) remain unclear. The synthetic experiments on linear Gaussian models convincingly demonstrate the bias-reduction advantage, with MSE improvements of orders of magnitude. However, the MovieLens experiments show only marginal RMSE improvements, raising questions about practical significance. The claim that SGRRLD's parallelization cost is comparable to SGLD is questionable, as SGLD could also run multiple parallel chains for similar computational overhead.

Strengths: Clear theoretical contributions, well-structured algorithm, strong synthetic results. Weaknesses: Underwhelming real-data results, insufficient comparison to multi-chain SGLD, unaddressed questions about gradient noise correlation and chain correlation in practice.

The contribution is novel in applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC (though RR is standard for SDEs), and the theoretical results advance understanding of SG-MCMC convergence. However, the practical significance is limited by the modest real-data improvements. The paper would be strengthened by more comprehensive real-data experiments, explicit comparison to multi-chain SGLD, and clarification of the conditions required for the theorems.

Pro: Rigorous theoretical analysis, clear algorithm, strong synthetic results. Con: Limited practical impact demonstrated, unconvincing real-data results, insufficient comparison to alternative parallel approaches.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of SG-MCMC, but the practical benefits need stronger substantiation to justify its significance for real-world applications. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of practical advantage over existing methods.
