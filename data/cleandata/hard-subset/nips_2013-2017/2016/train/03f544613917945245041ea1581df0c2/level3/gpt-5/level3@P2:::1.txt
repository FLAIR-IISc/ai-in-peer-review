Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC sampler that applies the classical Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. Concretely, it runs two SGLD chains with different stepsizes (γ and γ/2) for the same time horizon and combines their trajectory averages in a bias-canceling linear combination. The authors prove a CLT for the estimator, non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and show that with constant step size the asymptotic bias is O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4). For K iterations, the optimal MSE rate improves from O(K^{-2/3}) for first-order SG-MCMC to O(K^{-4/5}). They further show the variance is minimized when the Brownian increments are perfectly correlated across levels. Empirically, they demonstrate substantial improvements on a linear-Gaussian model, an extension to SGHMC, and consistent (though smaller) gains on large-scale matrix factorization.

The work builds a bridge between the SG-MCMC literature (e.g., SGLD [1], SGHMC [6], high-order integrators and analyses [9,10,19,20]) and the numerical SDE literature on RR extrapolation and variance control via correlated noise [21–23]. To my knowledge, this is the first explicit application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, and the theory carefully adapts insights from exact-gradient SDE discretizations to the stochastic-gradient setting.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with strong theoretical backing: RR extrapolation neatly cancels leading-order bias while correlated Brownian increments control variance; the proofs mirror and extend known results for ULA to the SG setting.
- Solid theory: asymptotic CLT, minimization of variance under optimal coupling, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds that explain the O(K^{-4/5}) optimal MSE rate with first-order integrators.
- Practicality: implementation requires running two SGLD chains with different stepsizes and modest coupling; easy to parallelize, so wall-clock cost need not double.
- Generality: the approach is applicable to other SG-MCMC methods; demonstration on SGHMC is encouraging.
- Empirical results: large gains on toy models that match theory; consistent, modest RMSE improvements on large-scale matrix factorization under fixed wall-clock.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Not transformative: the method is a straightforward application of a well-known extrapolation scheme; novelty is in careful adaptation and analysis for SG-MCMC rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.
- Assumptions are somewhat strong: smoothness and Poisson equation solutions (g ∈ C^9) may not hold in many practical posteriors; discussion on robustness to violations would help.
- Coupling requirements: variance control relies on perfectly correlated Brownian increments; the paper is less specific about whether sharing minibatches across levels (or correlating stochastic gradients) can further reduce variance or bias in practice.
- Doubling work per iteration: without parallel hardware, cost increases; a clearer comparison in “effective samples per second” across a range of hardware would be useful.
- Baselines: a broader empirical comparison to other practical accuracy-improving variants (e.g., preconditioned/pSGLD, control-variate SG-MCMC, higher-order SGLD integrators) would strengthen claims about practical superiority.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully reasoned. The asymptotic and non-asymptotic analyses are consistent with and extend prior theory for both ULA and SG-MCMC. The variance-minimizing coupling result is well-motivated and aligns with [23]. The experiments adequately support the theory (scaling laws on the Gaussian model, steady gains on large datasets). Some practical details could be expanded (e.g., recommended coupling/mini-batch strategies and sensitivity to hyperparameters).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The connection to prior SDE RR results and their adaptation to SG-MCMC is explained carefully. Minor presentation issues:
- Fig. 1a is potentially misleading: the plotted “empirical posterior” is a Gaussian fit using empirical mean/variance, not the empirical histogram; please clarify in caption and text or add the histogram.
- Fig. 2a: please add a short discussion in the main text explaining why bias may not monotonically decrease with decreasing stepsize at fixed K (the 1/(Kγ) term dominates), to avoid confusion.

Originality
Moderate. The RR extrapolation idea and correlated-noise variance control are known in SDE numerics, but the application to SG-MCMC, accompanying theory (including CLT and non-asymptotic bounds with stochastic gradients), and practical algorithmic instantiation are, to my knowledge, novel.

Significance
Good. RR extrapolation provides a simple, general technique to push first-order SG-MCMC toward the accuracy of higher-order methods without added integrator complexity. The theoretical rates and the empirical results suggest this could become a useful default trick, especially when parallel resources are available. It is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., multi-level/recursive RR, other SG-MCMC variants).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Ablation: Please include an experiment varying the coupling matrix Σ (e.g., Σ = 0 vs Σ = Id) to empirically verify the variance-minimization prediction.
- Gradient coupling: Have you tried reusing the same minibatch indices across the two levels to further reduce variance? Any theory to support or caution against this?
- Guidance on step-size schedules: The optimal α differs for bias vs MSE; please provide practical guidance for typical use cases.
- Baselines: Consider adding comparisons to pSGLD and other accuracy-boosted SG-MCMC variants; also report effective sample size or autocorrelation metrics if possible.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practical algorithmic idea with strong theory
- Clear improvements in both theory (rates) and practice (RMSE/time)
- Bridges two communities (SG-MCMC and SDE numerics) in a useful way
- Good clarity and thoroughness

Cons against acceptance
- Incremental novelty; relies on known extrapolation techniques
- Strong assumptions; limited discussion of robustness
- Empirical scope could be broader; cost–benefit without parallelism less clear

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is solid, technically sound, clearly written, and likely to be useful to practitioners and researchers. While not revolutionary, it advances SG-MCMC with a principled, easy-to-implement bias-reduction technique supported by convincing theory and experiments.
