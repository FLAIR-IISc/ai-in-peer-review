Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), instantiated as SGRRLD for SGLD. The key idea is to reduce the discretization bias of SG-MCMC by applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation: run two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and combine their ergodic averages with the RR weights. The two chains can be run embarrassingly in parallel without communication. The paper adapts variance-control techniques known for RR in diffusion sampling by correlating Gaussian increments across levels, derives asymptotic (CLT) and non-asymptotic bounds for the estimator, and claims improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC. Empirically, the method improves over SGLD (and, in one experiment, over Euler SGHMC; it approaches the accuracy of a higher-order SGHMC integrator).

This work builds on: SGLD and SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh 2011; Ma et al. 2015), RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures (Talay & Tubaro 1990; Lemaire et al. 2015; Pagès 2007), and high-order integrators for SG-MCMC (Chen et al., NIPS 2015). The novelty is in bringing RR to the SG-MCMC setting, carefully coupling the Brownian increments, and establishing both CLT and finite-time error bounds under stochastic gradients.

Strengths
- Simple, general idea with a clean implementation: two coupled chains at γ and γ/2 combined via RR. No inter-process communication; fits data-parallel settings.
- Solid theoretical development: consistency, a CLT with optimal variance achieved by perfectly correlated Gaussian increments, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The improved rates (bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ))) yield an optimal finite-time MSE rate O(K−4/5) versus O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC.
- Empirical results align with theory on synthetic Gaussian models; improvements persist in a large-scale matrix factorization task at matched wall-clock times.
- Conceptual clarity: an appealing bridge between numerical analysis (RR) and scalable Bayesian sampling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity/consistency issues:
  - Inconsistent step-size statements. At several points it is unclear whether the main guarantees assume constant or decreasing steps, and how the choices map to the finite-time bounds (e.g., Section 4 mixes γk ≡ γ with γk = γ1 k−α; please consolidate assumptions next to each result).
  - Algorithm 1 (supplement) is inconsistent with Equation (5): (i) the fine chain should advance twice per outer iteration (η2k−1 = η2k = γk/2), (ii) the noise scaling must match √(2η) = √γ for the fine steps, and (iii) the estimator should be time-weighted by γk as in Eq. (6) rather than a simple arithmetic mean. Please fix the pseudocode and ensure the Brownian coupling matches Eq. (7).
  - The paper stresses that the two gradient estimators must share the same distribution L (e.g., same mini-batch size) to avoid reintroducing first-order bias, but it is not explicit whether you also couple the mini-batches across levels. A discussion/ablation on mini-batch coupling would help.
- Evidence versus claims on efficiency: While the bounds indicate improved convergence rates, it would be valuable to (i) report constants or at least empirical slopes for the CLT/MSE scaling, and (ii) systematically validate wall-clock efficiency against state-of-the-art higher-order SG-MCMC on the same tasks.
- Missing comparisons: The paper shows SGRRHMC approaching SGHMC-s (higher-order) but does not compare SGRRLD to higher-order SGLD variants (e.g., symmetric splittings/preconditioned variants). Given the claim that RR with a first-order integrator achieves the accuracy of higher-order methods, a direct comparison on SGLD would strengthen the case.
- Assumptions are strong (e.g., solution g ∈ C9 to the Poisson equation, moment conditions on the stochastic gradients). This is typical for such analyses, but a short discussion on how sensitive the results are to weaker regularity would be useful.

Quality
The analysis is technically careful and largely sound, with appropriate references to the diffusion and SG-MCMC literature. The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds support the improved rates; the use of correlated Brownian increments is well-justified and important. Some derivations and constants are deferred to the supplement; moving key expressions (σR2 and μR) into the main text would improve readability. The experimental section is competent and supports the main claims, though broader comparisons would enhance confidence.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, but the inconsistencies noted above should be corrected. The estimator in Eq. (6) uses time-weights; this is easy to miss—highlight it in the algorithm block. Explicitly state step-size schedules per theorem and per experiment.

Originality
Conceptually a straightforward combination of RR extrapolation and SGLD, but novel in the SG-MCMC context with stochastic gradients and in establishing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic guarantees. Moderate methodological novelty; substantial in its theoretical adaptation.

Significance
Reducing bias in SG-MCMC without higher-order integrators and with near-zero communication parallelism is practically valuable. The improved rates and empirical gains suggest the approach can be adopted broadly (beyond SGLD to SGHMC and others). The impact would be strengthened by direct comparisons to high-order SGLD and by guidance on mini-batch coupling.

Specific comments/suggestions
- Fix Algorithm 1 to match Eq. (5) and Eq. (6); ensure Brownian coupling as in Eq. (7). Clarify whether mini-batches are coupled across levels; include an ablation.
- Unify step-size discussions; for each theorem, clearly state whether γk is constant or decreasing and provide the recommended α and rationale.
- Report empirical slopes for MSE vs K and γ to corroborate the O(K−4/5) rate with confidence intervals.
- Add comparisons to high-order SGLD integrators; discuss computational trade-offs under matched wall-clock and memory.
- Discuss the constant factors in the bounds and their dependence on the gradient noise variance and batch size.

Pros
- Simple, general, and parallelizable method.
- Solid theory (consistency, CLT, finite-time bounds) with improved rates.
- Empirical results consistent with theory on synthetic and large-scale tasks.
- Extensible to other SG-MCMC methods.

Cons
- Clarity inconsistencies (step sizes; pseudocode vs equations).
- Limited comparisons to higher-order SGLD and variance-reduced SG-MCMC.
- Strong technical assumptions; limited discussion of robustness.
- Moderate conceptual novelty.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a useful, principled bias-reduction mechanism for SG-MCMC with strong theory and encouraging experiments. Addressing the clarity issues and adding comparisons to high-order SGLD would make the contribution unequivocal.
