

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the foundational work of Welling and Teh (2011) on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) and extend it by running two parallel chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their results through RR extrapolation. The key innovation is that the method achieves bias reduction without requiring additional communication between the two parallel chains, making it well-suited for distributed computing environments.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a formal theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. The authors derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a convergence rate of O(K^(-4/5)) for MSE, which is superior to SGLD's O(K^(-2/3)) rate. This theoretical advantage is well-supported by experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper's empirical results convincingly show that SGRRLD outperforms SGLD in terms of accuracy with equivalent computational time, particularly on large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

The approach is conceptually elegant and builds upon a well-established numerical technique (RR extrapolation) applied to a novel domain (SG-MCMC). The method's embarrassingly parallel nature (two chains running independently with no communication) is a practical advantage for modern distributed computing architectures. The authors also demonstrate the generality of their approach by applying it to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC) to create SGRRHMC.

Several strengths make this paper noteworthy. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-motivated, with careful attention to the technical conditions under which the results hold. The experimental design is appropriate, with both synthetic tests to validate the theoretical claims and real-world applications to demonstrate practical utility. The paper clearly establishes how SGRRLD differs from previous work, particularly in its ability to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods using only first-order integrators.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that warrant consideration. First, while the authors claim SGRRLD achieves "the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators," they fail to provide experimental comparisons with such methods (e.g., the higher-order integrator methods from Chen et al. 2015). This omission is significant because it leaves open the question of whether the RR approach offers practical advantages over existing higher-order methods.

Second, there are inconsistencies in the paper that undermine confidence in the results. The authors state in Section 3 that the two chains should be run for the same time horizon ∑K k=1 γk, but the step size relations in Equation (5) suggest the second chain runs twice as many iterations as the first. This contradicts the claimed computational efficiency. Additionally, Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material is inconsistent with Equation (5), raising concerns about the implementation details.

Third, the paper contains an inconsistent statement about step sizes. In Section 3, the authors claim that the optimal step size for SGRRLD is γ ∝ K^(-1/5), but Theorem 2 implies it should be γ ∝ K^(-1/5) for MSE, while the earlier analysis suggests γ ∝ K^(-1/3) for bias. This inconsistency requires clarification.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The related work is adequately covered, though the paper could better distinguish its contribution from the recent work by Chen et al. (2015) on higher-order integrators.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important practical limitation of SG-MCMC (bias) with a simple, elegant solution that leverages existing computational infrastructure (parallel computing). The method could have substantial impact in large-scale Bayesian inference applications, particularly in distributed settings.

To summarize the key arguments for and against acceptance:

Pro:
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Strong theoretical foundation with improved convergence rates
- Demonstrated empirical improvements over SGLD
- Embarrassingly parallel implementation
- Clear, well-structured presentation

Con:
- Inconsistent step size and implementation details
- Lack of comparison with higher-order integrator methods
- Theoretical claims about achieving higher-order accuracy not fully validated experimentally
- Some inconsistencies in the paper's technical presentation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. While the theoretical results are compelling and the empirical evidence supports the claims, the inconsistencies in the paper and the lack of comparison with higher-order methods weaken the overall argument. With minor revisions to clarify the implementation details and provide additional experimental comparisons, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, it presents an interesting idea with solid theoretical backing but requires some clarifications to fully support its claims.
