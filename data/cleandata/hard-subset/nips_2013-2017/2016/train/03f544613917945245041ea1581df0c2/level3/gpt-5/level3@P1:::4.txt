This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to cancel the O(γ) weak-discretization bias, and uses correlated Brownian increments to control variance. The authors prove a CLT under decreasing step sizes, derive non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds for both constant and decreasing schedules, and show improved rates: for fixed γ after K iterations, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal rates O(K−2/3) for bias and O(K−4/5) for MSE; for γk ∝ k−α, an asymptotic rate O(K−2/5) (α = 1/5) is obtained. Empirically, on a Gaussian model, a SGHMC variant, and large-scale matrix factorization, SGRRLD improves accuracy and is wall-clock competitive when the two chains are run in parallel.

Relation to prior work: The method builds on SGLD [Welling & Teh, ICML’11] and subsequent SG-MCMC theory [Teh et al., JMLR’16; Chen et al., NIPS’15], and imports RR extrapolation ideas from the SDE literature [Talay & Tubaro’90; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup’15]. Compared to using higher-order integrators (e.g., splitting schemes in Chen et al., NIPS’15), RR achieves similar accuracy improvements while keeping first-order updates, with a clean parallelization story.

Quality:
- Theoretical contributions are substantial: a CLT (with explicit regimes depending on Γ(3)K/√ΓK), non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, and a principled variance-control strategy via perfectly correlated Brownian increments. The extension of RR analysis to the SG-MCMC setting is nontrivial.
- Assumptions: The CLT proof hinges on a high-order Poisson equation solution (g ∈ C9) and smoothness/growth conditions on U and f, plus unbiased i.i.d. gradient estimators with bounded moments. While standard in this line of work, the strength of smoothness and moment assumptions should be stated crisply in the main text (only sketched here).
- A major point needing clarification is how the specific convergence rates reported in the text are rigorously instantiated from Theorem 1 versus Theorem 2. The CLT yields a fluctuation scaling in √ΓK and a bias scaling in Γ(3)K/ΓK; turning this into an MSE rate requires quantifying constants and ensuring bias/variance balance, which Theorem 2 addresses. However, the manuscript sometimes blurs constant-γ and decreasing-γ regimes, and the derivations of optimal α and the resulting K-exponents should be explicitly spelled out in the main text (not only in the supplement), including the conditions under which gradient noise is indeed dominated by Brownian noise (as claimed).
- Practical correlation structure: the key variance reduction comes from perfectly correlating Gaussian increments across levels. The handling of stochastic gradient noise correlation is left open; while the theory suggests it is less critical, the practical impact (e.g., reusing the same mini-batches across levels) should be discussed/ablate experimentally.

Clarity:
- The algorithm is simple and well-motivated; the high-level idea is communicated clearly. The exposition would benefit from ironing out several notation and definition issues (see Minor comments).
- The empirical sections are readable, but a few figures and claims conflict with the stated theory (see below).

Originality:
- To my knowledge this is the first application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a full finite-time analysis. The idea is simple yet novel in this context and complementary to existing higher-order integrator approaches.

Significance:
- If broadly applicable, the method could become a go-to way to improve SG-MCMC accuracy with minimal code changes. The parallelization advantage is attractive. The theoretical rates are competitive with higher-order integrators while using first-order steps. The matrix factorization results suggest tangible practical gains.

Pros:
- Simple, generic, and easy to implement; can be applied to SGHMC and others.
- Strong theory: consistency, CLT, explicit non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, improved rates.
- Clear prescription to correlate Brownian increments; well-justified by the CLT.
- Compelling empirical improvements; parallel implementation leverages modern hardware.

Cons and concerns:
- Fairness of timing comparisons: SGRRLD does 1 coarse step plus 2 fine steps per iteration. On a single core, this is roughly 3× the work of SGLD; the reported wall-clock parity relies on running two processes in parallel. For a fair single-core comparison (or for environments without sufficient parallelism), SGLD runtime should be scaled by 2/3 or experiments should be normalized by total gradient evaluations. As-is, Figure 5 overstates the advantage in single-core settings.
- Figure 3 inconsistency: the bias plot appears to favor α = 0.2 (red), but the text claims α = 1/3 is optimal for bias. Please reconcile: is the plotted quantity actually MSE, or is there a labeling/legend error? The “optimal” curve also fluctuates substantially; explain whether this is due to high variance, insufficient burn-in, or step-size–dependent mixing.
- Small-γ regime: Line 256 argues that when γ is very small, 1/(Kγ) dominates for SGRRLD; similar bounds exist for SGLD, so why doesn’t SGLD exhibit the same U-shaped behavior in Figure 2? Is K adjusted differently, or are constants dramatically different? Please clarify.
- Rates from Theorem 1: The transition from the CLT regimes to explicit convergence rates (and the choice α = 1/5 for MSE) should be derived in the main text for readability, including the exact growth rates of ΓK and Γ(3)K and the condition under which κ ∈ (0, ∞).

Questions for the authors:
- Do you correlate mini-batch indices across the two chains? If not, what is the empirical impact of doing so? An ablation (same vs different mini-batches; with/without Brownian correlation) would strengthen the practical guidance.
- How sensitive are results to step-size tuning across datasets? Any heuristic beyond the theoretical guidance?
- Can RR be applied recursively (multi-level) in the SG-MCMC setting in practice, and what is the empirical payoff?

Minor comments and fixes:
- Eq. (1): use consistent symbol (θ versus another symbol) throughout.
- Define “RR” at first use. Define ΓK before Theorem 1. Specify “smooth” precisely (order of derivatives and growth conditions) in the main text.
- Line/figure labeling: at line 249, clarify whether the plot is bias vs time or bias vs dimension; ensure legends and axes match the text. The Figure 3 legend should make clear whether bias or MSE is plotted.
- Notation: vectors/matrices should be bold; ensure consistent use of Id and identity matrix; ensure πγ and π notation are consistent.
- Typos/grammar: a light proofreading pass would help (e.g., “variance at a reasonable level,” “twice more time” → “twice as many steps”).

Overall: A strong and timely paper with a simple idea, solid analysis, and promising results. Addressing the fairness of comparisons, resolving the figure inconsistencies, and clarifying the derivations of the optimal rates would make it a clear accept. As it stands, I lean toward acceptance, conditional on these fixes.
