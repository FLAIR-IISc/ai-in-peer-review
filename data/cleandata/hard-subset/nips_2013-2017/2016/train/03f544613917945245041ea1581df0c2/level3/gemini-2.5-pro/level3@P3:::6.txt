This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to accelerate Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to reduce the discretization bias of the underlying integrator. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size sequence `(γk)` and another with a finer step size `(γk/2)`, which requires two steps to match the time horizon of the first chain. To control the variance of the resulting extrapolated estimator, the paper proposes using correlated Gaussian noise between the two chains.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method. They establish an asymptotic central limit theorem and derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE). These results show that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This is significant as it demonstrates that SGRRLD can attain the accuracy of methods using higher-order numerical integrators while only relying on the first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The theoretical findings are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model, which carefully verify the predicted convergence rates. Furthermore, experiments on a large-scale matrix factorization task show that SGRRLD can achieve better predictive performance than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time.

This work is well-situated in the context of recent advancements in SG-MCMC. It directly addresses the problem of discretization bias, which was analyzed in detail by Chen et al. [10], and builds upon prior work that applied RR extrapolation to SDEs with exact gradients [23], extending the technique to the stochastic gradient setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound and very well-written. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic guarantees that clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. The connection between RR extrapolation, bias reduction, and variance control via correlated noise is explained clearly. The presentation is logical and easy to follow.

2.  **Significance and Originality:** The idea of applying a classic numerical acceleration technique like RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is elegant and effective. The ability to achieve higher-order accuracy using only first-order updates is a significant contribution. While the components (RR for SDEs, SG-MCMC analysis) are known, their combination in this context is novel and provides a new direction for improving SG-MCMC methods.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The synthetic experiments are a major strength. They are carefully designed to empirically validate the theoretical claims about the bias and MSE convergence rates with respect to both the number of iterations `K` and the step size `γ`. This adds strong credibility to the theoretical results. The application to SGHMC also successfully demonstrates the generality of the proposed framework.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

My main concerns relate to the practical applicability of the method and the fairness of the experimental comparisons.

1.  **Communication Overhead and Practicality:** The key to controlling the variance of the SGRRLD estimator is the use of correlated noise between the two parallel chains (Equation 8, with Σ=Id being optimal). This implies that the chains are not independent. Specifically, to compute the noise term `Z(γ)` for the first chain, one must have access to the noise terms `Z(γ/2)` from the second chain at every step. This introduces a synchronization and communication cost between the two parallel processes. This cost is not discussed, analyzed, or measured in the experiments. The claim that SGRRLD is "well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures" seems overly optimistic without considering this overhead, which could become a significant bottleneck, especially in a distributed (multi-node) setting. The claim that the "wall-clock times are nearly exactly the same" (Section 5.2) is difficult to assess without knowing more about the implementation (e.g., shared memory vs. distributed) and without any measurement of this communication cost.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Baselines and Resource Allocation:** The comparison to a single SGLD chain may not be entirely fair, as SGRRLD uses twice the computational resources (two parallel processes).
    *   A more direct and compelling baseline would be to run **two independent SGLD chains** in parallel (an "embarrassingly parallel" approach) and average their final estimates. This baseline uses the exact same computational resources as SGRRLD but has zero communication overhead. It is a very natural alternative for anyone with two available cores. How does SGRRLD compare against this stronger and more practical baseline?
    *   Could the authors please clarify the resource allocation in the experiments? For instance, in Section 5.1, SGLD is run for 21000 iterations and SGRRLD for 10500 iterations. However, each "iteration" of SGRRLD appears to involve three gradient computations (one for the coarse chain, two for the fine chain), whereas SGLD involves one. If gradient computation is the bottleneck, SGRRLD seems to perform 1.5x the work of SGLD per main loop iteration `k`. Clarifying how total gradient computations or FLOPs are matched against wall-clock time would strengthen the experimental claims.

3.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of ideas is novel and valuable, a critique could be that the contribution is somewhat incremental. The analysis of higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC was established in [10], and the use of RR extrapolation for the invariant distribution of diffusions was explored in [23]. The primary contribution here is bridging these two concepts, which, while executed very well, may be viewed as a logical next step rather than a fundamentally new direction.

### Minor Point

*   The term "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is defined twice in the introduction (line 25 and line 70).

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical contribution and a clear, well-executed core idea. The theoretical results demonstrating improved convergence rates are significant. However, the practical claims are weakened by the unaddressed issue of communication overhead and potentially unfair experimental comparisons. The paper would be much stronger if the authors addressed the communication cost of correlated chains and compared SGRRLD against a more robust baseline like two independent SGLD chains.

Given the strength of the theory and the clarity of the presentation, I lean towards acceptance. However, I strongly urge the authors to address the concerns regarding the experimental setup in their rebuttal and camera-ready version to solidify the paper's practical significance.
