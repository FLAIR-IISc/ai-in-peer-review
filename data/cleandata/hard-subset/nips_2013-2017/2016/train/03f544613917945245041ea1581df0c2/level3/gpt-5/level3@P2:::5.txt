Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a simple and general variance-reduced and bias-reduced variant of SG-MCMC. The key idea is to apply Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—well known in SDE discretization error analysis [Talay & Tubaro ’90; Lemaire et al. ’15]—to SG-MCMC. Concretely, the method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step-sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and combines their ergodic averages in the RR form. To control variance, the method uses correlated Gaussian increments, with an optimal coupling where the coarse-noise increment equals a normalized sum of the two fine increments. Theoretically, the authors prove consistency, a CLT with minimized asymptotic variance under the optimal coupling, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. They conclude that SGRRLD improves the finite-time and asymptotic rates over first-order SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD) without resorting to higher-order integrators, and that the approach is generic.

The paper is well positioned relative to SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh ’11; Teh et al. ’16; Ma et al. ’15; Chen et al. ’15) and to RR/Multi-level ideas for SDEs (Lemaire et al.; Pagès ’07). Conceptually, it shows that the accuracy benefits that higher-order integrators yield for SG-MCMC can be matched (in rate) via an RR correction using only first-order schemes, with the additional advantage that the two chains can be parallelized.

Strengths
- Simple, elegant idea with minimal implementation overhead: two SGLD chains with different step-sizes and a carefully chosen coupling of Gaussian increments.
- Provable improvements: asymptotic bias O(γ2), variance O(γ4), and non-asymptotic MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), implying an optimal rate O(K−4/5) (vs. O(K−2/3) for SGLD) under standard step-size schedules; CLT with minimized variance under Σ = Id coupling.
- Generic and potentially applicable to a range of SG-MCMC methods (SGLD, SGHMC, etc.) subject to regularity and ergodicity conditions.
- Empirical validations on synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task show clear accuracy gains at matched wall-clock time, leveraging parallel execution of the two chains.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory relies on strong smoothness/regularity assumptions (e.g., existence of a Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9, high-order Taylor expansions, and technical conditions on the stochastic gradient law). Please move the exact assumptions and their plausibility for typical Bayesian posteriors and minibatch estimators into the main paper, and discuss when they may fail (e.g., non-smooth likelihoods).
- Coupling/parallelization: While the text claims the two chains are “embarrassingly parallel with no communication,” the optimal-variance theory requires correlated Brownian increments across chains (and equal minibatch-size distributions). Please clarify precisely how to achieve the coupling in practice without run-time communication (e.g., via shared RNG seeds and deterministic partitioning) and whether minibatch index sets are also coupled. Include an ablation quantifying the impact of (i) optimal Brownian coupling vs. independent noise and (ii) matched vs. unmatched minibatch schemes.
- Comparison to higher-order integrators: The paper claims to match the “theoretical accuracy” of higher-order SG-MCMC integrators (e.g., Chen et al. ’15). However, for SGLD specifically there is no experimental comparison to an actual higher-order method. Please add head-to-head comparisons (same compute budget) to high-order SGLD-type schemes; for SGHMC you include SGHMC-s, but a direct SGLD-level comparison would strengthen the empirical case.
- Guidance on practice: The non-asymptotic bounds involve unknown constants; practical guidance on choosing γ and the decay exponent α would be valuable. The synthetic experiments partially address this; consider adding a short section with recommended defaults and sensitivity analyses for common regimes (data size, minibatch size).
- Scope of experiments: Beyond Gaussian posteriors and MF, consider an additional non-convex posterior (e.g., Bayesian neural net or logistic regression with strong regularization) to test robustness.
- Clarity issues/inconsistencies:
  - There is an inconsistent statement about the step-size schedule; at places it reads as if γk is constant, elsewhere decreasing. Please unify the exposition and explicitly separate the two regimes in all main theorems.
  - Algorithm 1 in the supplement is inconsistent with Equation (5): Eq. (5) takes two fine steps of size γk/2 for every coarse step γk, while the pseudocode appears to implement a different update/coupling. Please fix the pseudocode and verify indices, Brownian coupling (Equation (7)), and minibatch handling match the analysis.
  - Notation: using γ both for a constant and a sequence can confuse; consider γk for sequences and γ for constants throughout, and define Γ(n)K once.

Quality
- Technically sound overall and well grounded in existing RR/MLMC analysis for SDEs. Proof strategies are appropriate, but assumptions should be surfaced and discussed more explicitly. Empirics substantiate the main claims, though additional baselines (higher-order SG-MCMC for SGLD) would help.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but the above inconsistencies and some notational overload hinder readability. Adding a concise algorithm box in the main text that exactly matches Eq. (4–7) would help.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with an explicit optimal coupling of the injected noise and proving CLT and finite-time rates appears novel and worthwhile, beyond straightforward reuse of ULA theory.

Significance
- The method is simple, broadly applicable, and delivers tangible accuracy improvements at the same wall-clock time when parallel resources are available. The improved rates are theoretically interesting and practically relevant.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with clear theoretical improvements and practical gains.
- Sound analysis (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bounds) and connection to established RR theory.
- Demonstrated benefits on synthetic and large-scale tasks; easily parallelizable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some clarity/consistency issues (step-size statements, Algorithm 1 vs. Eq. (5)) need correction.
- Missing direct experimental comparison to higher-order SG-MCMC for SGLD.
- Practical guidance and ablations on coupling/minibatch correlation are limited.

Recommendation
- Leaning accept, contingent on fixing inconsistencies and strengthening the empirical section with a higher-order SGLD baseline and a brief practical guide to coupling and step-size choices.

Confidence
- High: familiar with SG-MCMC, weak error analysis, and RR/MLMC for SDEs.
