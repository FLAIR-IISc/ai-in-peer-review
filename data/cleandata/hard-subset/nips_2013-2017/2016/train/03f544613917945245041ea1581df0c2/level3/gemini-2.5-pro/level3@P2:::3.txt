This paper addresses the problem of integrator bias in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms, a key factor limiting their accuracy. The authors propose a novel and general method, based on Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, to reduce this bias. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains for the same target distribution but with different step sizes, γ and γ/2. The chain with the smaller step size is run for twice as many iterations to match the total integration time. By taking a specific linear combination of the estimators from these two chains, the leading O(γ) bias term is cancelled, resulting in an estimator with a much smaller bias of O(γ^2). A crucial element of the proposed method, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) in its application to SGLD, is the use of correlated Gaussian noise across the two chains to control the variance of the final combined estimator.

This work connects a classical technique from the numerical analysis of SDEs [21, 23] to the modern, large-scale Bayesian inference setting of SG-MCMC [1]. While higher-order integrators have been proposed to reduce bias in SG-MCMC [10], this paper presents an alternative and elegant approach that achieves the same improved convergence rates using only a first-order (Euler-Maruyama) integrator. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE of their estimator, demonstrating superior theoretical convergence rates compared to standard SGLD.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Potential Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental, well-known limitation of SG-MCMC methods. The proposed RR extrapolation approach is simple, elegant, and general. As demonstrated by its application to both SGLD and SGHMC in the experiments, it has the potential to improve a wide variety of existing and future SG-MCMC algorithms, making this a potentially high-impact contribution.
*   **Originality:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting is novel. While RR is known for SDEs with exact gradients, adapting it and providing a full convergence analysis in the presence of stochastic gradient noise is a non-trivial and valuable contribution to the machine learning community.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing both asymptotic (CLT) and non-asymptotic (bias/MSE) results that formally characterize the benefits of the method. The key theoretical finding—that SGRRLD can achieve an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³)—is a significant result. The claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments on both synthetic and real-world large-scale data, which clearly corroborate the theoretical findings.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the method is developed logically, and the main results are presented in an accessible manner.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

While the paper is of high quality, there are a few points that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Computational Scheme and Parallelism:** The proposed algorithm involves one update of the γ-chain for every two updates of the γ/2-chain. In a parallel implementation, this suggests that the processor running the γ-chain would be idle half the time. Could the authors comment on the practical implementation? For instance, would it be viable to run three chains in parallel (one γ-chain and two independent γ/2-chains) for the same number of wall-clock steps to improve hardware utilization? How would such a scheme affect the variance of the final estimator compared to the proposed correlated noise approach?

2.  **Scope of Applicability:** The authors state the technique is "quite generic." It would be helpful to be more precise about the conditions an SG-MCMC algorithm must satisfy for this RR scheme to be provably effective. Specifically, does it depend on the existence of a weak error expansion of the form π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)? Clarifying this would help researchers immediately see if the method applies to their sampler of interest (e.g., other thermostats, or non-Langevin based methods). Can this idea be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as subsampling Metropolis-Hastings?

3.  **Experimental Baselines and Convergence:** In the large-scale matrix factorization experiments, SGRRLD shows a clear advantage over SGLD in the given time frame. However, it would be more convincing to run the experiments until both methods have fully converged to assess the final quality of the solution. Furthermore, including a non-Bayesian baseline like SGD would provide valuable context. This would help quantify the benefit of the improved Bayesian approximation over a standard point-estimate optimization approach for this task.

4.  **Correlation of Minibatches:** The paper insightfully analyzes the importance of correlating the injected Gaussian noise to control variance. A natural question is whether there is any benefit to correlating the minibatches used for the gradient estimators in the two chains (e.g., by using the same minibatch at corresponding steps). Is this effect negligible compared to the noise correlation, or could it offer further variance reduction?

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   I strongly suggest adding an algorithm box with pseudocode for SGRRLD in the main paper (e.g., in Section 3). This would be immensely helpful for practitioners seeking to implement this excellent method.
*   The paper mentions the possibility of multi-level RR extrapolation. A brief sentence or two discussing how this could be extended (e.g., with step sizes γ, γ/2, γ/4) and the potential trade-offs would be a welcome addition.

In summary, this is a strong, well-executed paper presenting a novel, simple, and effective method for a significant problem in large-scale Bayesian inference. The theoretical and empirical support is convincing. My questions are primarily for clarification and to suggest minor enhancements to an already excellent manuscript.
