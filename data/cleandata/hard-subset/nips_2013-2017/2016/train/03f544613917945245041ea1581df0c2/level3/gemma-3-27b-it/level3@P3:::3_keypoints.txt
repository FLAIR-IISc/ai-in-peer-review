- Introduces SGRRLD, an SG-MCMC method using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias versus standard SGLD.  
- Leverages a well-known numerical analysis technique (Richardson-Romberg) applied novelly to SG-MCMC.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees: reduced bias O(γ^2), reduced MSE, and a central limit theorem (Theorem 1).  
- Simple and easy to implement: runs two correlated chains with different step sizes and minimal changes to SGLD.  
- Demonstrates generality by applying the approach to both SGLD and SGHMC, suggesting broader applicability.  
- Empirically validated on synthetic data and matrix factorization tasks with encouraging results.  
- Builds on prior SG-MCMC literature and complements higher-order integrator approaches (e.g., Chen et al., 2015).  
- Novel contribution because Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is underused in machine learning despite its numerical-analysis pedigree.  
- Paper is well-written and clear, making technical details accessible.  
- Applicability condition (π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2)) may limit use; more discussion needed on algorithms that fail this condition.  
- Unclear applicability to other approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling MH, Distributed SGLD); extending discussion would increase impact.  
- Computational inefficiency concern: running one chain twice as long wastes compute; exploring a three-chain parallel scheme could help.  
- Questionable scalability of extrapolation to more than two step sizes; recursive/ multi-step extensions need analysis.  
- Need clarification on optimal noise/minibatch correlation strategy and how imperfect correlations affect bias reduction.  
- Experimental design improvements suggested: run matrix factorization to full convergence and include a standard SGD baseline for context.  
- Practical usability could be improved by adding algorithm pseudocode early in the paper.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, clear presentation, strong experimental support, and potential broad impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited exploration of computational efficiency, insufficient discussion of applicability/extension, and weaker experimental convergence analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong, promising paper with rigorous analysis and good results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the noted weaknesses.
