## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes the Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm, an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) designed to reduce bias and improve convergence rates. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency, a central limit theorem, and improved bias/MSE bounds compared to standard SGLD. 

The work builds upon the growing body of literature on SG-MCMC methods for large-scale Bayesian inference (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014). It addresses a key limitation of SGLD – its inherent bias – by leveraging a well-established numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg) from the numerical analysis community (Talay & Tubaro, 1990). The theoretical results are a significant contribution, establishing a rigorous foundation for the proposed method. The extension to SGHMC (Section 5.1) demonstrates the generality of the approach.

**Strengths:** The paper is theoretically sound, with clear and well-defined theorems supporting the claims. The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to implement, requiring minimal changes to existing SGLD implementations. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization) support the theoretical findings, showing improved performance over SGLD and, in some cases, competitive performance with higher-order integrators.

**Weaknesses:** A major concern lies in the derivation of the convergence rates stated in the theorem. The reviewer notes a potential flaw in how these rates are obtained, requiring clarification. Practically, the timing comparisons in Figure 5 are misleading. Running two chains inherently increases computational cost (approximately 3x in the reported experiments).  The figure should be adjusted to reflect this, scaling the SGLD timing to allow for a fair comparison.  Furthermore, Figure 3 shows the best bias reduction occurs at α=0.2, contradicting the text's claim of α=1/3. The “optimal” curve also fluctuates substantially, raising questions about the empirical rate matching the stated α=0.2.  Finally, line 256 poses a question regarding the behavior of SGLD with very small step sizes, which the authors should address.

**Minor Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the paper's polish: inconsistent notation (Eq. 1 vs. θ), undefined terms (RR, Γ_K), imprecise smoothness assumptions (line 160), a figure labeling error (line 249), and a lack of bolding for vectors.

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the concerns regarding the convergence rate derivation and the unfair timing comparison, this is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically well-supported contribution. The proposed method is a promising approach for improving the efficiency of SG-MCMC algorithms. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Strong theoretical foundation, clear and simple implementation, promising experimental results, addresses a key limitation of SGLD.
**Arguments for Rejection:** Potential flaw in convergence rate derivation, misleading timing comparisons, inconsistencies in results/text.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the convergence rate derivation, adjust the timing comparisons in Figure 5, clarify the optimal α value in Figure 3, address the question in line 256, and correct the minor issues mentioned above.
