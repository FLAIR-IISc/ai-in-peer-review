This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an acceleration technique for SG-MCMC algorithms like SGLD, leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation by running two parallel chains with differing step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE, claiming improved convergence rates compared to SGLD. Experiments on synthetic data and a matrix factorization task support these claims, showing practical gains over standard SGLD.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a technically sound approach. It effectively situates SGRRLD within the broader context of SG-MCMC, acknowledging prior work such as Welling & Teh (2011) introducing SGLD, and Chen et al. (2015) analyzing higher-order integrators. The connections to numerical analysis, specifically Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2015), are well-explained. The theoretical contributions, including the convergence analysis and error bounds, appear rigorous and provide a strong foundation for the proposed method. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the potential benefits of SGRRLD in both synthetic and real-world scenarios.

However, the novelty of the approach feels incremental. While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is not explicitly ruled out in prior work, [10] (Chen et al., 2015) already analyzes the benefits of higher-order integrators, and [23] (Lemaire et al., 2015) explores RR extrapolation for invariant distributions, providing substantial groundwork. The authors acknowledge these connections but downplay their significance. 

A critical weakness lies in the practical considerations of the parallel chain implementation. SGRRLD necessitates per-step communication between the two chains to maintain correlation, a cost that is not addressed or discussed. This communication overhead could negate the benefits of accelerated convergence, especially in distributed computing environments. The experimental setup doesn't fully clarify this issue. It’s stated that wall-clock times are “nearly exactly the same” due to parallel execution, but doesn’t detail how resources were allocated. Was SGLD given the same total compute resources as SGRRLD, or did SGRRLD effectively benefit from using twice the resources? Understanding this resource allocation is crucial. A more fair comparison would include a baseline of two *independent* SGLD chains, running in parallel without communication. This would isolate the benefit of the RR extrapolation specifically, rather than simply leveraging parallelization.

Several specific points warrant clarification:

* **Experimental Setup:**  More details are needed regarding the resource allocation for SGLD and SGRRLD. Specifically, how were the compute resources split between the chains in SGRRLD, and how does this impact the wall-clock time given the gradient computation cost?
* **Baseline Comparison:**  Including two independent SGLD chains as a baseline would provide a more robust evaluation of SGRRLD's benefits.
* **Minor Typo:** The phrase “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is duplicated on lines 25 and 70.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence analysis.
* Clear presentation and well-motivated approach.
* Promising experimental results demonstrating improved performance over SGLD.
* Generality of the technique – applicable to various SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty – builds on existing work in numerical integration for MCMC.
* Lack of consideration for communication costs associated with correlated chains.
* Unclear experimental setup regarding resource allocation.
* Missing comparison to a straightforward embarrassingly parallel baseline (two independent SGLD chains).



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC, but its significance is somewhat diminished by the incremental nature of the novelty and the lack of practical consideration for communication costs. Addressing the concerns regarding the experimental setup and including a more appropriate baseline would significantly strengthen the paper. I would rate this as a **weak accept**. The technical soundness and clarity of the presentation are strong, but the limitations regarding practicality and novelty require attention.
