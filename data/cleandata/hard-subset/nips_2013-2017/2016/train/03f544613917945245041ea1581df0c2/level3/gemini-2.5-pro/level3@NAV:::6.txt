This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a method to accelerate SG-MCMC by reducing the discretization bias of first-order integrators. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, which involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes. To control the variance of the resulting estimator, the paper proposes using correlated Gaussian noise between the chains. The work is well-situated within the context of prior research on SG-MCMC, particularly building upon the analysis of higher-order integrators [10] and the use of RR extrapolation for sampling invariant distributions of SDEs [23].

The paper has several notable strengths. The presentation is clear and the technical quality is high. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence results, which rigorously demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The synthetic experiments are well-designed and convincingly verify the theoretical claims regarding bias and MSE reduction. Furthermore, the application to large-scale matrix factorization demonstrates a tangible improvement in wall-clock time over SGLD, suggesting practical utility.

However, I have two main concerns. First, the originality may be somewhat incremental, as the work combines two well-established techniques: the analysis of numerical integrators for SGMCMC [10] and the application of RR extrapolation to diffusions [23]. A more significant concern regards the practical applicability and the fairness of the experimental comparison. The proposed method relies on correlated noise between the two chains, which necessitates per-step communication to synchronize the random numbers. This communication overhead is not discussed or accounted for in the wall-clock time experiments. This omission makes the direct comparison against a single SGLD chain potentially unfair, as SGLD has no such overhead.

For improvement, the authors should clarify their experimental setup. Was the single-chain SGLD baseline allocated the same total computational resources as the two-chain SGRRLD? To better isolate the benefits of the RR scheme, I strongly suggest comparing SGRRLD against a baseline of two *independent* SGLD chains running in parallel. This would establish whether the improvement comes from the extrapolation technique itself or simply from using twice the compute resources.

Minor point: The term “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is duplicated on lines 25 and 70.
