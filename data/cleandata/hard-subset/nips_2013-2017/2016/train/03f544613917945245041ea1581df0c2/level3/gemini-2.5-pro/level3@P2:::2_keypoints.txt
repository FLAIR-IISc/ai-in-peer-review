- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two correlated chains (step γ and two steps γ/2) and combining outputs to reduce discretization bias.
- Novelty: first rigorous adaptation and analysis of RR extrapolation within the SG-MCMC framework accounting for stochastic gradient noise.
- Theoretical contributions: proves asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds, and faster MSE rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD’s O(K⁻²/³).
- Technical strength: establishes higher-order convergence using only a first-order integrator, a significant theoretical advance.
- Synthetic validation: linear Gaussian experiments convincingly support theory, showing clear bias and MSE reduction and matching convergence rates.
- Real-data results weak: only marginal RMSE improvements on MovieLens, casting doubt on practical utility in high-dimensional, complex tasks.
- Clarity: well-written and easy to implement, but main text should briefly summarize key technical assumptions (smoothness, growth, convexity) instead of deferring them.
- Assumption ambiguity: unclear if Theorems 1 & 2 require strong convexity or can handle multimodal posteriors common in practice.
- Originality nuance: RR itself is classical, but adapting it to stochastic-gradient noise and proving convergence is non-trivial and valuable.
- Practical significance limited: theoretical promise is strong, but empirical gains on large-scale problems are currently insufficient to demonstrate broad practical impact.
- Computational-comparison concern: claimed efficiency from parallelization should be compared to two independent SGLD chains (or SGLD with γ/2 for twice the iterations) to ensure fairness.
- Role of correlation: correlating Brownian motions is crucial for variance reduction; need clarification on how decorrelation from independent mini-batches affects error reduction and empirical chain correlation.
- Suggested experiments: test on other large-scale Bayesian tasks (e.g., LDA, Bayesian neural networks) to better establish real-world benefits.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel application of RR, strong theoretical guarantees (CLT and faster rates), convincing synthetic validation, and generality to other SG-MCMC methods.
- Arguments against acceptance: weak real-data improvements, potentially unfair computational comparisons, and limited novelty of the core RR technique.
