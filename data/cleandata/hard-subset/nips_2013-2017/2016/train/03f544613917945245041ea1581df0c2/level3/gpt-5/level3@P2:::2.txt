Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC, with a focus on SGLD. The method runs two coupled SGLD chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 to cancel the leading-order O(γ) bias of the Euler-Maruyama discretization, using correlated Brownian increments to control variance. The estimator is a time-weighted combination of the two chains’ function evaluations. The authors prove a CLT and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, showing asymptotic bias O(γ^2), variance O(γ^4), and finite-time MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)). With suitable step-size schedules, the resulting optimal rates are O(K^−4/5), improving over O(K^−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC. They argue this attains the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., [10]) while using only a first-order scheme. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model (including an SGHMC variant) and a matrix factorization task support the theory, with stronger gains on synthetic data and modest RMSE improvements on MovieLens.

RR extrapolation is well known in SDE discretizations (e.g., Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup; multilevel RR), but to my knowledge it has not been developed for SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients. The paper connects these literatures, provides new analysis for the stochastic-gradient setting, and demonstrates practical algorithms.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper states a CLT with correlated Brownian increments (optimal at Σ=Id) and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The results are plausible and technically interesting. However, the main text defers key assumptions to the supplement. Please state clearly in the paper what conditions on U (e.g., smoothness order, dissipativity/one-sided strong monotonicity, local Lipschitz, polynomial growth) and on the gradient noise L (unbiasedness, moment bounds, independence structure) are required for Theorems 1 and 2. In particular:
  - Do the results cover non-convex or multimodal posteriors typical in Bayesian deep models and matrix factorization, or do they require strong log-concavity outside a compact set?
  - To what extent do the bounds rely on the Gaussian noise “dominating” the stochastic gradient noise? Under what conditions on minibatch variance does the claimed rate still hold?
- Implementation details: The use of perfectly correlated Brownian increments is key to controlling variance. In practice, how is coupling implemented? Are the same RNG streams reused across chains, and did you share minibatch indices to induce correlation in gradient noise, or were gradients sampled independently? Please clarify, and quantify how much variance reduction comes from Brownian correlation vs. minibatch coupling.
- Experiments: The synthetic Gaussian experiments convincingly show bias and MSE reductions and agree with the predicted scaling laws. The matrix factorization results show slight RMSE gains under equal wall-clock time, but the empirical case for large practical benefit is not yet strong. Moreover, the claim that SGRRLD benefits from parallelization “whereas SGLD cannot” is misleading: one can run multiple independent SGLD chains in parallel and average their estimates. To fairly assess wall-clock efficiency, please compare against:
  - Two parallel SGLD chains with identical compute and the same total gradient evaluations, averaged in the standard way.
  - SGLD with the smaller step size γ/2 (and matched compute).
  - If possible, higher-order methods (e.g., SGHMC-s [10]) on real data, since SGRRLD aims to match their accuracy with first-order integrators.
  - Ablations showing the effect of (i) Brownian coupling on/off, (ii) sharing vs. not sharing minibatches across chains.

Clarity:
- The algorithm is simple and clearly described; pseudo-code is reportedly in the supplement. Consider moving a concise version into the main text and stating the core assumptions for the theorems inline.
- Provide practical guidance for step size schedules: the optimal α=1/5 (MSE) vs. α=1/3 (bias) trade-off, how to tune γ in fixed-K regimes, and how to choose minibatch coupling in practice.
- Please correct the parallelization claim for SGLD and make compute-matching criteria precise (e.g., report total gradient evaluations and FLOPs alongside wall-clock).

Originality:
- The conceptual novelty is the adaptation of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, along with corresponding theoretical guarantees. While RR is classical for SDE discretizations, the extension to the stochastic-gradient setting with analysis of correlated noise is a meaningful contribution.

Significance:
- If the theoretical improvements translate robustly in practice, SGRRLD could be a useful, easy-to-implement variance/bias-reduction wrapper for many SG-MCMC methods. The synthetic results are strong; the real-data gains are currently modest and need stronger, fairer baselines to establish clear practical impact.

Pros:
- Simple, general idea with low engineering overhead.
- Solid theoretical development (CLT and non-asymptotic bounds) adapted to SG-MCMC.
- Clear empirical confirmation on synthetic tasks; extension to SGHMC shown.
- Naturally parallelizable and compatible with existing SG-MCMC variants.

Cons:
- Practical gains on large-scale real data are limited in current experiments.
- Fairness of experimental comparisons is questionable; SGLD can also parallelize via multiple chains.
- Key assumptions are relegated to the supplement; unclear applicability to non-convex/multimodal targets.
- No ablations on coupling choices (Brownian/minibatch); sensitivity to gradient noise level unclear.

Questions for the authors:
- Precisely state the assumptions on U, f, and L required for Theorems 1–2. Does your analysis hold for multimodal U and non-convex posteriors?
- How were Brownian increments and gradient estimators coupled in practice? Did you share minibatches across chains? What performance change occurs if minibatches are independent?
- Do you observe empirical correlation between the two chains over time on real datasets? Any diagnostics?
- Can you add baselines with two parallel SGLD chains (and SGLD with γ/2), matched by total gradient evaluations and wall-clock? Can you compare to high-order SGHMC-s on real data?

Recommendation:
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is strong and the method is clean, but the current empirical evidence on real applications and the fairness of comparisons are not yet sufficient for acceptance. Strengthening the experimental section with compute-matched baselines (including multi-chain SGLD and higher-order integrators), clear assumption statements, and coupling ablations would significantly improve the paper.
