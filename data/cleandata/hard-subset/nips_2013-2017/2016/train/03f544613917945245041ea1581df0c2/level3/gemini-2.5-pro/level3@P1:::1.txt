This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), for reducing the bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg Extrapolation (RRE), a classical technique from the numerical analysis of Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. In practice, this involves running two SGLD chains in parallel: one with a step size sequence {γ_k} and another with a finer step size sequence {γ_k/2}, run for twice as many steps to cover the same time horizon. The final estimator is a linear combination of the estimators from the two chains, constructed to cancel the leading-order bias term.

This work builds directly upon the SG-MCMC literature, notably SGLD [1], and recent analyses of its error properties [10, 20]. While previous work has focused on reducing bias by using higher-order numerical integrators [10], this paper presents an alternative approach that achieves similar theoretical improvements using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama integrator. The method draws its inspiration from the SDE literature, particularly the work on RRE for SDEs [21, 23], effectively bridging these two related fields. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than standard SGLD. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality:** The paper is technically solid and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, establishing consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for both bias and MSE. These results clearly formalize the intuition that RRE should improve performance and quantify the gains, showing an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD, which is a significant theoretical improvement.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel and elegant application of RRE to the SG-MCMC setting. While RRE is a well-known method in numerical analysis, its use in the context of large-scale Bayesian inference with stochastic gradients is new and well-motivated. This provides a practical, parallelizable alternative to using more complex higher-order integrators for bias reduction. The connection it forges between the SDE and machine learning MCMC literature is valuable and could inspire further work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for reducing bias in SGLD is clearly articulated, the SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated clearly. The experiments are well-designed to support the theoretical claims.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method is simple to implement and computationally convenient. Since the two chains can be run independently, the method is inherently parallelizable, making it well-suited for modern computing architectures. The experimental results, particularly on the large-scale matrix factorization problem, demonstrate tangible benefits in practice: SGRRLD achieves the same level of accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time. This makes the method practically useful and likely to be adopted by practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incremental Nature:** While the application is novel, the core technique (RRE) is not new. The contribution can be seen as a straightforward, though effective, combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach to sampling. However, the thorough theoretical and empirical treatment for the SG-MCMC context largely mitigates this concern.

2.  **Minor Clarity Issues in Figures:**
    *   The left panel of Figure 1(a) is slightly misleading. It appears to plot a Gaussian density parameterized by the empirical mean and variance of the samples, rather than the empirical posterior density (e.g., a histogram or kernel density estimate) of the samples themselves. This should be explicitly clarified in the caption to avoid misinterpretation.
    *   In Figure 2, it is somewhat counter-intuitive that the bias and MSE increase for very small step sizes. The authors correctly state in the text that this is due to the `1/(Kγ)` term in their bounds dominating when K is fixed. It would be beneficial to add a sentence directly in the main body of Section 5.1 discussing this trade-off, as it is a key practical aspect of choosing the step size.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for reducing the bias of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous proofs of improved convergence rates over the state-of-the-art SGLD.
*   The method is practically relevant, easy to implement, parallelizable, and shown to provide significant speed-ups on a large-scale real-world task.
*   The work is of high quality, clearly written, and bridges ideas from numerical analysis and machine learning in a valuable way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution could be considered somewhat incremental, as it applies a well-established technique to an existing algorithm.

Overall, this is a very strong paper. The strengths, particularly the combination of solid theory, practical utility, and clear presentation, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a valuable and likely impactful contribution to the scalable Bayesian inference literature.
