

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel approach to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors leverage Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a well-established technique from numerical analysis, to address the asymptotic bias problem in SG-MCMC methods like SGLD. The key insight is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, then combine their estimates using RR extrapolation. The method requires correlating Brownian increments between the two chains (Z^(γ)_k = (Z^(γ/2)_2k-1 + Z^(γ/2)_2k)/√2) to control variance, and the authors prove that this yields a bias of O(γ²) compared to SGLD's O(γ) bias. The paper demonstrates theoretically and empirically that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error rate of O(K^-4/5) versus SGLD's O(K^-2/3), matching the performance of higher-order integrators while using only first-order methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it successfully bridges numerical analysis techniques to ML, applying RR extrapolation in a novel context to reduce bias in SG-MCMC. This is a clear advance over previous work by Chen et al. (NIPS 2015), which showed that higher-order integrators could improve convergence rates but required more complex implementations. Second, the method is remarkably simple to implement—requiring only minor modifications to existing SG-MCMC codebases—and maintains the parallelizability of SG-MCMC methods. Third, the theoretical analysis is thorough, providing both asymptotic (central limit theorem) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) results for SGRRLD. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendation, showing consistent improvements in accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated, with clear comparisons to related work. The presentation is exceptionally clear, with well-structured mathematical derivations and intuitive explanations. The experiments are well-designed, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and problem scales. The method's simplicity and broad applicability to existing SG-MCMC frameworks represent a significant practical advantage.

However, several important questions remain. The authors note that the RR extrapolation requires the expansion π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²), which may not hold for all SG-MCMC methods. It would be valuable to clarify the exact conditions under which the technique applies, and whether it can be extended to methods like Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC) beyond the specific case shown in Figure 4. The computational efficiency concern is also noteworthy: running one chain twice as long as the other may lead to idle computation time in parallel implementations. The authors could explore alternatives like running three chains (one with step size γ, two with γ/2) that might better utilize parallel resources without increasing variance.

The experiments, while convincing, could be strengthened by including a standard SGD baseline in the matrix factorization task to better contextualize the Bayesian benefit. The paper's claim that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators" is compelling, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this compares to the computational cost of higher-order methods.

The paper's novelty is significant within the NIPS community. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis since Talay and Tubaro (1991), its application to SG-MCMC appears to be entirely new. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work on SG-MCMC convergence (Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016), and their method clearly advances the state of the art in both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of scalable Bayesian inference.

The significance of this work is high. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in SG-MCMC methods that has been acknowledged in the literature (Welling and Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015), and provides a simple yet powerful solution. The method's applicability to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms, combined with its minimal implementation cost, suggests it could become a standard technique in the practitioner's toolkit for large-scale Bayesian inference.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical results are sound, the method is simple and effective, and the empirical results convincingly support the claims. The minor concerns about computational efficiency and applicability to all SG-MCMC methods are important but do not undermine the paper's main contributions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem in a novel way. I recommend acceptance.
