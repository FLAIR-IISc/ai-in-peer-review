

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, using correlated Brownian motions to control variance, and combine their estimates via RR extrapolation. Theoretical analysis shows SGRRLD achieves MSE convergence rate O(K^(-4/5)) compared to SGLD's O(K^(-2/3)), matching the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods with first-order discretization. Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks demonstrate improved accuracy with comparable computation time.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a technique previously used for standard SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) but not for stochastic gradient settings. It builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and recent SG-MCMC convergence analysis (Chen et al., 2015), but the application of RR to SG-MCMC is novel. The theoretical framework is well-developed, with careful analysis of bias, variance, and convergence rates under appropriate conditions. The authors provide clear asymptotic and non-asymptotic bounds, showing how SGRRLD achieves better rates than SGLD while maintaining the parallelizability of SG-MCMC methods.

The paper's strengths include strong theoretical analysis, clear presentation of the algorithm, and compelling experimental results. The synthetic experiments effectively demonstrate the bias reduction claimed in the theory, particularly in the Gaussian model where the posterior is analytically tractable. The real-world matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show consistent improvements in RMSE over SGLD, with SGRRLD achieving comparable accuracy in roughly half the wall-clock time. The authors correctly note that SGRRLD's parallel structure makes it well-suited for distributed computing environments.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the novelty claim appears incremental. Chen et al. (2015) already showed that higher-order integrators can achieve better rates than first-order methods, and the authors' claim that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators" is not sufficiently distinguished from this prior work. The paper should explicitly clarify how their approach differs from simply using a higher-order integrator, which is a more direct method to achieve similar accuracy.

Second, the practical applicability concerns are not adequately addressed. The paper assumes the two parallel chains can be run efficiently, but fails to discuss communication costs between the chains, which could be significant in distributed settings. The experimental setup comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD is potentially misleading: SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel, while SGLD runs one chain. The paper does not specify whether SGLD was given the same total compute resources as SGRRLD (i.e., whether SGLD ran on a single processor while SGRRLD ran on two processors), which is critical for fair wall-clock time comparisons. Without this information, it's unclear whether the observed speedup is due to the algorithm or simply because SGRRLD used double the resources.

Third, the paper should compare against a more appropriate baseline: two independent SGLD chains running in parallel. This would provide a fairer comparison of the RR extrapolation technique versus simply running two separate chains, which would also be embarrassingly parallel. The current comparison of SGRRLD against single-chain SGLD is not a fair test of the RR technique's value.

Minor issues include the duplicate definition of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" (lines 25 and 70) and a few unclear notations in the theoretical analysis that would benefit from clarification.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound method with solid theoretical foundations and promising experimental results. The theoretical contribution is significant for understanding bias-variance trade-offs in SG-MCMC, and the practical implications for scalable Bayesian inference are meaningful. However, the incremental novelty and insufficient discussion of practical implementation details limit its impact. The authors should more clearly distinguish their approach from prior work on higher-order integrators, address the experimental setup details, and provide a more appropriate baseline comparison. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these concerns.
