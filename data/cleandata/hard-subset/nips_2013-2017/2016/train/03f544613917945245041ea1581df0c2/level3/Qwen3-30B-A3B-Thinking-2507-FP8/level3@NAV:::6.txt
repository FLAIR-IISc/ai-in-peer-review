

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two parallel chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analysis showing SGRRLD achieves O(K^{-4/5}) MSE convergence compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), with synthetic and real-data experiments demonstrating improved accuracy.

Strengths include strong theoretical contributions that build upon prior work on numerical integrators for SG-MCMC [10] and RR extrapolation for invariant distributions [23], with clear presentation that properly situates the method in the literature. The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks showing practical gains over SGLD.

However, the novelty appears incremental as the core technique builds directly on prior work [10,23], and the practical applicability is limited by communication requirements. The authors do not address the per-step communication cost required for correlated chains, which is significant in distributed settings. The experimental setup also requires clarification: it's unclear whether SGLD and SGRRLD used equivalent compute resources (the paper states wall-clock times are "nearly exactly the same" but doesn't specify resource allocation between the two chains), and the comparison should include two independent SGLD chains as an embarrassingly parallel baseline.

The paper contains a minor duplication of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" (lines 25 and 70) that should be fixed.

In terms of the review criteria: Quality is strong theoretically but weakened by practical concerns; Clarity is generally good but needs minor editing; Originality is incremental rather than breakthrough; Significance is demonstrated through improved convergence rates and practical gains, though limited by communication considerations.

Pro: Strong theory, clear presentation, good experimental validation. Con: Incremental novelty, unaddressed communication costs, unclear experimental resource allocation.

Overall, the paper makes a solid technical contribution to SG-MCMC that advances the state of the art, but the practical limitations and incremental nature slightly reduce its impact. With clarification on experimental setup and communication costs, this would be a strong contribution.
