This paper proposes a novel sampling algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) that aims to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm, which is a popular SG-MCMC method. The proposed algorithm runs two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlates the injected Gaussian noise to obtain a reduced bias.

The main idea of the paper is to use Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to improve the rate of convergence of the SGLD algorithm. The authors provide a formal theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm and show that it is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. They also derive bounds for the non-asymptotic bias and the mean squared error (MSE) of the algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical analysis. The experiments are also well-designed, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in reducing the bias and MSE of the SGLD algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is that the proposed algorithm is simple to understand and implement, requiring minimal changes to the base SG-MCMC algorithms. The authors also demonstrate the generality of the proposed algorithm by applying it to another SG-MCMC algorithm, namely the Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC) algorithm.

However, there are a few questions and concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, can the technique be applied to all SG-MCMC methods, or only those satisfying the expansion π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2)? Secondly, can this be applied to other approximate MCMC schemes (subsampling MH) or Distributed SGLD? Thirdly, running one chain 2× longer wastes compute; can using 3 chains (one γ, two γ/2 run equal steps) avoid idle time, and how does that affect variance?

In terms of novelty, Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis but not widely used in ML/SG-MCMC, so the contribution is novel to the community.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. However, there are a few places where the notation could be improved. For example, the authors use the notation γ_k to denote the step size at iteration k, but it is not clear what γ_k represents in the context of the algorithm.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in reducing the bias and MSE of the SGLD algorithm. The results of the paper have the potential to impact the field of Bayesian machine learning, where SG-MCMC methods are widely used.
