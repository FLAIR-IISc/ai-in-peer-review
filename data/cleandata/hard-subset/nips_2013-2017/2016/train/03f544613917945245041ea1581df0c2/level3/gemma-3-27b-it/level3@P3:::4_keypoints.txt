- Proposes Richardson‑Romberg extrapolation (run two SGLD chains with different stepsizes) to reduce bias and improve convergence of SG‑MCMC.
- Presents the Stochastic Gradient Richardson‑Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm with clear motivation and implementation simplicity.
- Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing asymptotic consistency, a CLT, and claimed improved convergence rates over standard SGLD.
- Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical improvements.
- Strong theoretical foundation and novel application of RR extrapolation to SG‑MCMC.
- Method is practically applicable with minimal changes to existing SGLD implementations.
- Paper is well written and organized, making methods and results easy to follow.
- Main flaw: derivation of the K^{-4/5} MSE convergence rate is unclear and appears to rely on unstated assumptions; requires detailed justification.
- Timing comparison in Figure 5 is misleading—comparing two parallel SGRRLD chains to one SGLD chain without cost normalization unfairly favors SGRRLD.
- Empirical optimal parameter α is ambiguous (Figure 3 shows best bias at α=0.2, not α=1/3); fluctuations and robustness need discussion.
- Unexplained SGLD behavior: paper does not clarify why the 1/(Kγ) bias term does not dominate at very small stepsizes.
- Several minor technical issues: inconsistent notation (ϑ vs θ), "RR" undefined at first use, Γ_K undefined, imprecise smoothness assumption for f, mislabeled Figure 3, and inconsistent vector formatting.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important Bayesian inference bias problem, theoretically sound, empirically validated, and creatively applies a known numerical technique.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear/flawed convergence rate derivation, unfair computational comparisons, and mismatch between theory and empirical observations.
- Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions contingent on clarifying the convergence rate proof, providing fair timing comparisons, explaining empirical α behavior, and fixing minor technical issues.
