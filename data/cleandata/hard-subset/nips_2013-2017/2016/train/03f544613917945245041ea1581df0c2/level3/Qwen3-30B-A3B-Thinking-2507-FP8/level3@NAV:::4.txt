

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel approach to improve bias and mean squared error (MSE) convergence rates in SG-MCMC methods by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The key idea involves running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and combining them through extrapolation. The authors claim SGRRLD achieves O(K^{-4/5}) MSE convergence compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), while maintaining implementation simplicity.

The theoretical contributions are strong, with solid convergence analysis supporting the claimed rates. The paper effectively relates to prior work on SG-MCMC [1,10] and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SDEs [21,23], extending these to the stochastic gradient setting. The method is particularly appealing due to its minimal implementation changes and compatibility with parallel architectures.

However, several concerns significantly impact the paper's quality assessment. The most critical issue is the practical implementation cost: the method requires approximately 3× computation time on a single core (two chains, one with twice the steps), making Figure 5's timing comparisons misleading. When properly scaled (2/3 of SGLD time), the reported performance gap substantially diminishes, undermining the practical significance claim.

Additional concerns include:
- Figure 3 shows optimal bias at α=0.2 (red curve), contradicting the text's claim of α=1/3
- The "optimal" curve fluctuates substantially, suggesting empirical rate may not match theoretical predictions
- Line 256's observation about SGLD not being dominated by the 1/(Kγ) term when stepsize is small lacks explanation
- Numerous minor issues with notation (e.g., Eq.1, Γ_K, "RR" not defined, vector formatting)

While the theoretical framework is elegant and novel, the practical implementation concerns and unresolved discrepancies in the experimental results weaken the paper's overall contribution. The significance would be substantially improved if the authors could address the timing concerns and clarify the experimental results. Given these issues, I recommend rejection, though the theoretical insights could form a strong basis for future work with proper experimental validation.
