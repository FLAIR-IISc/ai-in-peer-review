

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build on the established work of Welling and Teh (2011) who introduced SGLD, and extend it by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their results via RR extrapolation. This approach differs from previous SG-MCMC extensions that rely on higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) by achieving similar accuracy using only first-order discretization.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides formal theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴) respectively, compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²). The authors prove that SGRRLD satisfies a central limit theorem and achieves a rate of convergence for MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), surpassing SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). Second, they provide a clear algorithmic description with proper attention to the correlation structure of Brownian increments (via Equation 8), which is critical for controlling variance. Third, they demonstrate the method's applicability beyond SGLD by applying it to SGHMC (Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo) to create SGRRHMC.

The paper's strengths are its theoretical rigor, clear exposition, and the practical simplicity of the proposed algorithm. The theoretical results are well-motivated and build appropriately on previous work in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and SG-MCMC (Chen et al., 2015). The synthetic experiments on a Gaussian model provide compelling evidence of bias reduction, with SGRRLD reducing variance estimation bias from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴. The algorithm is straightforward to implement, as noted by the reviewer.

However, several concerns warrant attention. First, the real-data experiments on MovieLens datasets show only modest improvements in RMSE (e.g., SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to SGLD in half the time, but the absolute improvement is small). This weakens the significance claim that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators." More substantial real-data evidence is needed to substantiate the practical benefits. Second, the theoretical conditions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not fully specified. The reviewer is concerned about whether these results hold for multimodal U (a common case in practice), and the paper does not address this limitation.

The paper also raises questions about implementation details. The claim that SGRRLD is "well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures" is questionable since SGLD could also run multiple chains in parallel. The authors should compare against a multi-chain SGLD baseline (with two chains at step size γ/2) to isolate the benefit of RR extrapolation from parallelization. Additionally, the paper does not report whether the two chains in SGRRLD maintained sufficient correlation in practice (as required by Equation 8), which would be crucial for the theoretical variance bounds to hold.

The paper's originality is strong in applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, though it would be strengthened by more thorough comparison to recent work on SG-MCMC acceleration (e.g., Chen et al., 2015 on higher-order integrators).

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical contribution that extends our understanding of SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical results are significant and the approach is well-motivated. However, the practical significance is weakened by modest real-data improvements and insufficient comparison to relevant baselines. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the theoretical conditions for multimodal targets, providing more substantial real-data evidence, and clarifying the comparison to parallelized SGLD.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with formal theoretical analysis
- Clear algorithmic description and implementation details
- Strong synthetic data results demonstrating bias reduction
- Theoretical improvement over SGLD (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³))

Arguments against acceptance:
- Modest real-data improvements that don't strongly justify the theoretical complexity
- Insufficient comparison to parallelized SGLD baselines
- Unclear theoretical conditions for multimodal targets
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field, but its practical significance needs stronger validation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about real-data evidence and theoretical conditions.
