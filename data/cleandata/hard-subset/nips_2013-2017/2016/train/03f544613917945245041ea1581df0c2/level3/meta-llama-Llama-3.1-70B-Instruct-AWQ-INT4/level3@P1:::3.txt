The paper "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo" presents a novel approach to reducing the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The authors propose running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes, correlating the injected Gaussian noise, and subtracting the estimates to obtain a reduced bias of O(γ^2).

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE) of the proposed method, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The authors also provide experimental results on synthetic and real data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

The strengths of the paper include its simplicity and generality, as the method can be applied to various SG-MCMC algorithms with minimal changes. The novelty of the approach lies in the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which is established in numerical analysis but not widely used in machine learning.

However, there are some concerns and questions regarding the applicability and efficiency of the method. For instance, can the technique be applied to all SG-MCMC methods, or only those satisfying the expansion π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2)? Can this be applied to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as subsampling Metropolis-Hastings or Distributed SGLD? Running one chain 2× longer may waste compute, and using three chains (one γ, two γ/2 run equal steps) could potentially avoid idle time, but how does that affect variance?

Additionally, there are some minor suggestions for improvement. Including an algorithm pseudocode box early in the paper could help practitioners implement the method without delving into the theory. The matrix factorization experiment should be run to convergence and include an SGD baseline to demonstrate the Bayesian benefit and contextualize the final errors. Furthermore, clarification is needed on the required correlation between injected noise and the effect of correlating minibatches—is using the same minibatch optimal or negligible?

In terms of originality, the paper contributes a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which is a significant contribution to the field. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the method, including theoretical results and experimental validation.

Regarding the significance of the paper, the proposed method has the potential to improve the accuracy of SG-MCMC methods, which are widely used in Bayesian machine learning. The method is simple to implement and can be applied to various SG-MCMC algorithms, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

In conclusion, the paper presents a novel approach to reducing the bias of SG-MCMC methods using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. While there are some concerns and questions regarding the applicability and efficiency of the method, the paper contributes a significant and original idea to the field of Bayesian machine learning.
