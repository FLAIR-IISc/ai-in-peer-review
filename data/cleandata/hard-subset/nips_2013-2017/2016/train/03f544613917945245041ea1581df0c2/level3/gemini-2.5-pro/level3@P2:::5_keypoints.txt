- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two parallel chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining outputs to cancel leading-order bias.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Shows improved MSE rate: SGRRLD achieves O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD’s O(K⁻²/³).  
- Empirical validation on synthetic data confirms theoretical bias, MSE, and optimal step-size findings.  
- Demonstrates practical benefit on large-scale matrix factorization, outperforming SGLD.  
- High technical quality: detailed proofs (sketches in main paper, full in supplement) and rigorous convergence-rate analysis.  
- Paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear motivation and algorithm exposition.  
- Minor clarity issue: notational inconsistency in Section 3 between the step-size sequence definition and Equation (5).  
- Potential implementation inconsistency: Algorithm 1 in the supplement may not match Equation (5); authors should verify pseudocode.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of RR extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC, distinct from higher-order integrator approaches.  
- Significance: method is simple, parallelizable, and can achieve accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators while using a first-order scheme.  
- Practical advantage: two chains can run independently to leverage multi-core resources for similar wall-clock time as single SGLD with higher accuracy.  
- Argument for acceptance: elegant idea, strong theoretical guarantees, convincing empirical performance, and practical parallelizability.  
- Argument against / improvements: lacks thorough experimental comparison to higher-order integrator SGLD variants (e.g., Chen et al., 2015).  
- Argument against / improvements: needs more explicit discussion of computational cost trade-offs since it uses double the total compute relative to one SGLD chain.
