Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes applying Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to stochastic-gradient MCMC, focusing on SGLD. The method runs two SGLD chains with steps γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and combines them via 2πγ/2(f) − πγ(f), canceling the O(γ) bias typical of first-order integrators. To control variance, the two chains use correlated Brownian increments, ideally perfectly correlated as Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. Theoretical results establish consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds: with fixed γ, the bias is O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and the MSE is O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal finite-time rates O(K−2/3) for bias and O(K−4/5) for MSE under optimal γ. With decreasing steps γk ∝ k−α, the asymptotic optimal α is 1/5 (MSE rate O(K−2/5)), improving on SGLD’s O(K−1/3). The approach matches accuracies of higher-order integrators without implementing them explicitly and extends generically to other SG-MCMC schemes (e.g., SGHMC). Empirically, the method shows large gains on synthetic Gaussian models and consistent, modest gains on large-scale matrix factorization, with parallelism keeping wall-clock comparable to SGLD.

The work builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011), SGHMC (Chen et al., 2014), and analysis of high-order SG-MCMC integrators (e.g., Ma et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2015), and imports RRE/duplicated diffusion ideas from the SDE literature (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup). The theoretical development adapts Poisson-equation techniques used for ULA to the stochastic-gradient setting.

Strengths
- Simple, generic idea with minimal code changes; naturally parallelizable by running the two chains concurrently.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT, explicit role of Brownian correlation, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds with clear optimal step-size schedules. The improved rates over SGLD are well-motivated and derived carefully.
- Bridging SDE numerical analysis with SG-MCMC is conceptually valuable and may spur further cross-fertilization.
- Empirical validation includes toy problems with ground-truth posteriors and a realistic large-scale matrix factorization; fair compute-time comparisons are made by parallel runs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical benefits hinge on parallel resources. Without parallelism, the two-chain cost could negate wall-clock gains; this trade-off could be discussed more explicitly and quantified (e.g., ESS/time).
- Assumptions are strong in places (e.g., solution g ∈ C9 to the Poisson equation; technical conditions on L and ergodicity). The main text could better summarize these and discuss applicability to nonconvex or heavy-tailed posteriors common in practice.
- Empirical scope is limited: beyond the linear-Gaussian and a single application (matrix factorization), no logistic regression or deep models are shown. Comparisons to higher-order SGLD variants are not included; for SGHMC, only a limited comparison to a splitting scheme is provided.
- Sensitivity to mini-batch correlation is not explored. Since both chains must have the same gradient noise distribution (same batch size), sharing the same mini-batch indices across chains might further reduce variance; this is not tested.
- Minor clarity issues in figures (see below) and some key implementation details (e.g., correlated noise across processes) are deferred to the supplement.

Quality
The methodology and analysis are technically sound and build on established techniques (Poisson equation, Taylor expansions). The variance-control argument via correlated Brownian increments is well-justified and aligns with the duplicated diffusion literature. Non-asymptotic bounds are informative, although constants are opaque and depend on moments of the gradient estimator; a brief discussion on realistic magnitudes would help practitioners. Experiments support the theory qualitatively.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The estimator and algorithm are described cleanly. Suggestions:
- Make the pseudocode for SGRRLD part of the main text.
- Explicitly state in the main text the requirement that both chains use the same mini-batch size (and ideally the same mini-batch indices) to avoid bias and to improve variance.
- Minor: Figure 1a appears to plot a Gaussian with empirical mean/variance rather than the empirical posterior itself; this should be clarified to avoid confusion.
- Minor: Figure 2a’s non-monotonic bias vs. stepsize is counter-intuitive; add an explicit explanation that for fixed K, the 1/(Kγ) term dominates as γ → 0, so bias can increase with smaller γ.

Originality
Applying Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC and analyzing it in this stochastic-gradient setting appears novel. The idea is straightforward but non-trivial to analyze; the work clearly differentiates itself from high-order integrator approaches by achieving similar accuracy with first-order steps via extrapolation.

Significance
While not transformative, the method is practical, broadly applicable, and likely to be adopted, especially in settings with parallel resources. The improved rates and empirical gains suggest it can be a useful default upgrade to SGLD/SGHMC.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can sharing the mini-batch indices between the two chains (in addition to Gaussian correlation) further reduce variance? Any theory or ablation?
- How sensitive is performance to imperfect Gaussian correlation (Σ ≠ Id) in practical multi-process implementations?
- Provide guidance for adaptive or decaying step sizes in practice: how to pick α and γ1 robustly, and when to stop?
- Can you report ESS/time or calibration metrics on higher-dimensional, non-Gaussian posteriors (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression)?
- What is the impact when parallelism is unavailable? A wall-clock vs. accuracy comparison at equal computational budgets would be informative.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general method with strong theory and positive empirical results.
- Bridges communities and may inspire further work (e.g., multilevel extensions).
- Practical parallelism and minimal implementation overhead.

Arguments against acceptance
- Modest empirical breadth; limited benchmarks beyond one application.
- Gains may diminish without parallel hardware; trade-offs not fully quantified.
- Strong technical assumptions; some clarity issues and missing ablations.

Recommendation
Weak accept.

Reviewer confidence
Medium–high.
