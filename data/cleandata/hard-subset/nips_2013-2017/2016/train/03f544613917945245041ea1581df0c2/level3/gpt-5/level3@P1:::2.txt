This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, with a focus on SGLD. The method runs two SGLD chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated estimator that cancels the leading-order discretization bias, while controlling variance via correlated Brownian increments. The authors establish consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, showing SGRRLD achieves bias O(γ2), variance O(γ4), and a finite-time MSE rate O(K−4/5), improving over SGLD’s O(K−2/3) with first-order integrators. The approach is claimed to be generic (applicable to other SG-MCMC schemes) and parallelizable. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model (including an SGHMC variant) demonstrate substantial bias and MSE reductions; a matrix factorization task on MovieLens shows modest RMSE gains at comparable wall-clock time.

Relation to prior work: The idea builds on RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire/Pages), which yields second-order bias cancellation for Euler schemes. In SG-MCMC, prior work has analyzed SGLD’s asymptotics and finite-time behavior (Teh et al.; Vollmer et al.) and improved accuracy with higher-order/splitting integrators (Chen et al.). This paper’s novelty is adapting RR to the stochastic-gradient setting, analyzing its asymptotic and non-asymptotic behavior with gradient noise, and leveraging correlated Brownian increments to control variance.

Quality:
- Theory: The results (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds) are well aligned with the RR literature and SG-MCMC analyses. The asymptotic rate improvements and optimal step-size schedules (α=1/5 for overall rate, α=1/3 for bias-only) are coherent and useful. However, the main text defers many conditions to the supplement. It would help to state clearly in the main paper the assumptions on U (smoothness order, dissipativity/strong convexity outside a compact set, Lipschitzness of ∇U, growth conditions), on f (smoothness), and on the gradient noise (unbiased, finite moments, possibly sub-Gaussian). Are multimodal/posteriors with nonconvex U covered by the ergodicity and Poisson equation assumptions used (e.g., C9 solution)? How restrictive are these in practice?
- Variance control: The correlated Brownian increments are well motivated, and Theorem 1 indicates optimality at Σ=Id. The discussion of gradient noise correlation is more cursory. Since the estimator requires the two chains to have gradients drawn from the same distribution, what happens if the mini-batches are independently sampled (uncorrelated) or matched (common random mini-batches)? Empirically, if gradients are uncorrelated, the chains may decorrelate quickly—how sensitive is the RR gain to that?
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments convincingly validate the theory (bias/MSE vs γ and K, correct exponents, dimensional scaling) and show the method can match higher-order integrators using a first-order scheme. The real-data gains are present but small.

Clarity:
- The algorithm is clearly described and simple to implement; the estimator (6) and correlation structure (7)/(8) are explicit. Please add main assumptions to the body (not only the supplement), include pseudo-code inline, and clarify implementation details: how Brownian increments are correlated in discrete-time code; whether common random numbers and/or common mini-batches were used; how the RR weights are applied with burn-in and thinning. Also clarify cost: one SGRRLD “iteration” entails one γ step and two γ/2 steps.
- Some notation is heavy; a brief schematic showing the time grid alignment for the two chains would help.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a complete convergence analysis appears novel. The combination with correlated Brownian increments and the extension to other SG-MCMC (SGHMC) are natural but useful contributions.

Significance:
- The theoretical advance is meaningful: second-order bias cancellation without implementing higher-order integrators, and improved finite-time rates. Practically, the gains on synthetic tasks are strong; on real data, improvements are modest, and the parallelization argument should be contextualized against strong baselines.

Empirical evaluation and fairness of comparisons:
- The matrix factorization results show only slight RMSE reductions; stronger evidence on diverse real tasks (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression on large datasets, Bayesian neural networks) would strengthen the case.
- The wall-clock comparison assumes SGRRLD’s two chains run in parallel, whereas SGLD is “inherently sequential.” However, practitioners often run multiple independent SGLD chains in parallel and aggregate. To isolate SGRRLD’s benefit, please add:
  - SGLD with two independent parallel chains under the same total compute, averaging their estimates.
  - SGLD with step size γ/2 (and/or a finely tuned γ schedule) under the same total compute.
  - A higher-order SG-MCMC baseline on real data (e.g., SGHMC-s) to corroborate the “matches higher-order accuracy” claim beyond the synthetic setting.
- Report whether you observed empirical correlation between the two chains (e.g., trace or lag-one cross-correlation of f(θ) across chains) and whether using shared mini-batches across chains improves performance.

Questions for the authors:
- Precisely state the assumptions on U, f, and the gradient noise L required for Theorems 1 and 2. Are nonconvex/multimodal posteriors admissible? What growth/dissipativity is needed?
- How robust is SGRRLD if gradient noises are independent across chains? Does using common mini-batches across the two chains improve the RR effect?
- In the matrix factorization experiments, did you use the same mini-batches and common random numbers across chains? Please quantify computational overhead and memory footprint versus SGLD.
- Can you provide an ablation comparing Σ=Id (perfect correlation) vs Σ=0 and intermediate Σ to validate the variance analysis?

Pros:
- Simple, general, and easy-to-implement idea with solid theoretical backing.
- Clear improvement in asymptotic and finite-time rates over first-order SG-MCMC.
- Strong synthetic validations; extension to SGHMC; parallelizable design.

Cons:
- Practical gains on real data are modest; experimental scope limited.
- Assumptions and proof conditions are heavy and not sufficiently visible in the main text.
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons needs stronger baselines (multi-chain SGLD; tuned γ; higher-order integrators on real data).
- Sensitivity to gradient-noise correlation and implementation details not fully explored.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is technically sound and the idea is elegant, but the empirical evidence on practical, large-scale problems is not yet compelling, and the comparisons under parallel compute are incomplete. Strengthening the real-data evaluation and clarifying assumptions could elevate this to a strong submission.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
