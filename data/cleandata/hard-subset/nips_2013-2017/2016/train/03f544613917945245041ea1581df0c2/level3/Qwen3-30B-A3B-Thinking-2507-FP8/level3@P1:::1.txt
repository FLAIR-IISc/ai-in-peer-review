

This paper proposes a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, specifically focusing on improving the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) method. The authors introduce Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combines them using RRE to reduce bias while controlling variance. The work bridges the numerical analysis literature on SDEs with MCMC methodology, extending the application of RRE to stochastic gradient settings where it has not been previously explored.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with formal analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴) respectively, compared to O(γ) and O(γ²) for SGLD. The paper demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a rate of convergence for MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). This is significant because it shows that a first-order integrator with RRE can achieve the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators without the computational complexity of those methods. The authors provide a central limit theorem for their estimator and prove consistency under mild conditions, making a compelling case for the method's theoretical soundness.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The connection between numerical analysis techniques and MCMC is explained with sufficient mathematical rigor while remaining accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from related work, particularly noting that their method can be applied to all SG-MCMC algorithms satisfying certain technical conditions, not just SGLD.

Empirical results support the theoretical claims. On synthetic Gaussian models, SGRRLD significantly reduces bias in variance estimation (from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴) compared to SGLD. The MSE improvements are consistent across dimensions and step sizes, with clear confirmation of the theoretically predicted optimal step size schedules. The application to matrix factorization on MovieLens datasets demonstrates practical utility, showing SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy to SGLD in half the wall-clock time, leveraging its inherent parallelism. The results on SGHMC (SGRRHMC) further validate that the approach generalizes to other SG-MCMC methods.

The main strengths of the paper include its theoretical novelty, clear presentation, and practical relevance. The method is straightforward to implement, requires minimal modification to existing SGLD code, and benefits from parallel computation. While not a revolutionary breakthrough, it represents a practical, theoretically grounded improvement to a widely used class of algorithms, potentially benefiting many practitioners.

Two minor issues should be addressed: Figure 1(a) is misleading as it shows a Gaussian fit to the empirical posterior rather than the actual empirical posterior distribution. The authors should clarify this in the revised manuscript. Additionally, Figure 2(a) shows a counter-intuitive increase in bias as step size decreases for small γ values. The authors should discuss this phenomenon, which relates to the theoretical bound term 1/(Kγ) dominating the bias when K is fixed.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the scalable Bayesian inference literature. It provides a practical tool that improves upon existing methods without requiring complex modifications, addressing a real limitation of SG-MCMC (bias due to discretization error). The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners in large-scale Bayesian applications, particularly where computational efficiency is critical.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs of convergence properties and careful experimental design. The authors properly acknowledge related work, including the use of RRE in deterministic settings (Talay & Tubaro, 1991) and its potential application to MCMC (Lemaire et al., 2015), while clearly establishing their novel contribution to the SG-MCMC context.

Overall, this is a strong NIPS submission that makes a solid contribution to the field. The theoretical results are well-supported, the empirical validation is thorough, and the method is practical and implementable. While not transformative in the sense of introducing a completely new paradigm, it represents a significant practical improvement to an important class of algorithms. The minor issues noted are easily addressable in revision.

I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, original in its application of RRE to SG-MCMC, and significant for providing a practical, theoretically grounded improvement to a widely used class of algorithms.
