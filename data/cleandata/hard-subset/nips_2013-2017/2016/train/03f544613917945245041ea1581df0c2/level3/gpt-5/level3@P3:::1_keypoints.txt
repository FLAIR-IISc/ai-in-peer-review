- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with stepsizes γ and γ/2 and extrapolating to cancel leading discretization bias.  
- Uses correlated Brownian increments (optimal Σ = I) between chains to control variance and minimize asymptotic variance.  
- Proves CLT and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds: for constant stepsize bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)), improving over SGLD’s O(γ + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)).  
- With decreasing stepsizes γk ∝ k^−α, obtains optimal MSE O(K^−4/5) at α = 1/5, better than SGLD’s O(K^−2/3) at α = 1/3.  
- Theoretical analysis aligns with prior SDE weak error expansions and adapts RRE results to the stochastic-gradient setting.  
- Empirically shows large gains on linear–Gaussian toys and consistent, modest wall-clock improvements on large-scale matrix factorization; SGHMC variant shows similar benefits.  
- Clear bridge between SG-MCMC and numerical SDE literature, using bias expansion constructively via RRE.  
- Algorithm is simple and practical: just two SGLD chains with correlated noise and identical minibatch law.  
- Theoretical contributions are substantial: asymptotic CLT, optimal correlation choice, and explicit non-asymptotic rates.  
- Empirical validation supports theory and demonstrates parallel speedups where two chains can be run concurrently.  
- Key practical limitation: requires perfectly correlated Brownian increments and identical gradient estimator distributions across chains; effects of approximate satisfaction (e.g., async or different minibatches) need more discussion.  
- Computational tradeoff concern: parallel implementation favors wall-clock comparisons, but single-core users may not benefit—need fixed-total-computation comparisons.  
- Experimental scope limited: would benefit from additional tasks (Bayesian logistic regression, neural nets) and comparisons to other bias/variance reduction methods (control variates, variance-reduced SG-MCMC).  
- Lacks concrete, practitioner-oriented tuning guidance for finite budgets (joint selection of γ and K).  
- Does not improve mixing rates; discussion of RRE behavior under multimodality or poor mixing is missing.  
- Presentation is clear, notation consistent, and proofs appropriate; recommends clarifying two figures (label Fig.1a as Gaussian fit; explain bias non-monotonicity in Fig.2a caption).  
- Suggests emphasizing Σ = I implementation details (shared RNGs) and discussing robustness to heavy-tailed gradients or non-globally Lipschitz potentials.  
- Recommends adding single-core matched-computation experiments, main-paper pseudocode/code for reproducibility, and links to multilevel/Multi-step RR tradeoffs.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple, general, strong theory, practical implementation, bridges communities and likely useful in practice.  
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on parallelism and strict correlation/minibatch conditions, limited experimental breadth, and few comparisons to alternative reduction methods.
