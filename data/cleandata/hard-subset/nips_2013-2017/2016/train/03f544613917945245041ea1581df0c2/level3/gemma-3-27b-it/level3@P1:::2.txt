This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm aiming to reduce the bias inherent in methods like SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique – running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes – to improve the convergence rate and accuracy of Bayesian inference in large-scale settings. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on the non-asymptotic bias and MSE.  Experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (movie recommendation) support their theoretical findings, showing improved performance compared to SGLD.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work in SG-MCMC, particularly stemming from Welling & Teh’s (2011) initial combination of ULA and SGD. Subsequent extensions (Ahn et al., 2012; Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014) have sought to address the bias-variance trade-off in these methods.  Recent work (Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2015) focuses on bounding bias and MSE and exploring higher-order integrators. The proposed SGRRLD explicitly targets bias reduction, positioning itself as a method to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators with a relatively simple, first-order approach. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SDEs, while known (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2015), represents a novel contribution when applied to the context of SG-MCMC and supported by the derived convergence guarantees.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical soundness and clear presentation. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous (though a detailed review of the proofs is beyond the scope of this review) and provides strong justification for the proposed algorithm.  The description of SGRRLD is straightforward, and the algorithm is readily implementable. The synthetic experiments demonstrate the bias reduction benefit convincingly. The authors also appropriately highlight the potential for parallelization, a key advantage for large-scale applications.

However, the paper also exhibits some weaknesses. The results on the MovieLens dataset are less compelling. While improvement is observed, the magnitude appears modest, raising questions about the practical significance of the gains in a real-world setting. The authors claim efficiency gains, but the comparison lacks nuance.  It’s unclear how the parallelization overhead in SGRRLD compares to simply running multiple independent SGLD chains, a standard practice for variance reduction. This comparison is critical, as running multiple SGLD chains might offer a comparable level of improvement without the added complexity of the RR extrapolation. Furthermore, the paper does not provide clear constraints on the potential function *U*. What assumptions on *U* are required for Theorems 1 and 2 to hold? Specifically, can *U* be multimodal, or are there restrictions on its smoothness or convexity?  

Regarding the correlated Brownian motion, the justification for correlation is well-explained through the connection to previous work (Lemaire et al., 2002). However, the impact of the degree of correlation remains unclear. If the gradient noise is largely uncorrelated, will the chains quickly decorrelate, diminishing the benefits of combining them? Did the authors observe any significant correlation between the two chains in their real-data experiments, and if so, how did this correlation evolve over time? 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (asymptotic consistency, CLT, bias/MSE bounds).
*   Clear algorithm description and potential for parallelization.
*   Demonstrated bias reduction on synthetic data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Modest and potentially insignificant performance gains on real-world datasets.
*   Lack of comparison to multi-chain SGLD, a readily available baseline.
*   Unclear constraints on the potential function *U* required for the theoretical results.
*   Insufficient discussion of the impact of gradient noise correlation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While the theoretical results are strong and the algorithm is well-presented, the empirical evidence, particularly from the real-world experiments, is not fully convincing. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a direct comparison to SGLD run with two parallel chains.
2.  Investigate the impact of gradient noise correlation on the performance of SGRRLD.
3.  Clarify the conditions on the potential function *U* necessary for the theoretical guarantees to hold.
4.  Conduct more extensive experiments on a wider range of real-world datasets to demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD more convincingly.
