Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, instantiated with SGLD. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon, and forms an extrapolated estimator that cancels the O(γ) weak discretization bias. The authors show: (i) a CLT for the extrapolated estimator with variance minimized by a specific coupling of Gaussian increments; (ii) non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds showing, for constant γ, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)); (iii) optimal finite-time rates of K−2/3 (bias) and K−4/5 (MSE) versus K−1/2 and K−2/3 for SGLD. Experiments on synthetic linear-Gaussian models confirm bias and MSE reductions; a MovieLens matrix factorization study shows modest RMSE gains at matched wall-clock when two chains are parallelized.

RR extrapolation is classical for SDEs and weak error expansions (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire et al.), and its use to reduce bias in ULA and in invariant measure estimation is known. In SG-MCMC, prior improvements relied on higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) or thermostats/kinetic methods. The main contribution here is to transfer RR to SG-MCMC with correlated Brownian increments and to provide non-asymptotic and asymptotic guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear, simple algorithm requiring only running two SGLD chains with prescribed step-size relation and Gaussian coupling; easy to implement.
- Solid theory: CLT with optimal coupling (Σ = I), explicit non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC. The analysis carefully separates fluctuation and bias terms and shows how RR cancels the O(γ) term.
- Synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate bias/MSE reduction consistent with theory; extension to SGHMC is natural and supports generality.
- The contribution offers a principled alternative to higher-order integrators while keeping a first-order update.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions: Theorems require “appropriate conditions” on U, f, and L, and a Poisson equation solution g ∈ C9 with polynomial growth. Please state precisely in the main text: (i) dissipativity/ergodicity (e.g., one-sided Lipschitz and coercivity) vs global Lipschitz; (ii) whether strongly log-concave is required or if multimodal U with multiple wells is allowed under drift/minorization (geometric ergodicity). In particular, can U be multimodal, and what conditions ensure existence/regularity of g in that case?
- Role of gradient-noise correlation: Theorem 1 emphasizes Brownian coupling; it states that gradient-noise correlation does not impact asymptotic variance. Intuitively, if mini-batch gradient noises in the two chains are independent, the chains may decorrelate rapidly. How then does RR still reduce the weak error order? Is the O(γ) cancellation guaranteed solely because both chains share the same law L and step-size relation, regardless of cross-chain correlation of ∇Ũ? Please clarify which terms in the weak error expansion are unaffected by the lack of gradient-noise coupling and provide guidance on whether sharing the same mini-batch across chains can further reduce variance in practice.
- Empirical correlation: Did you measure empirical cross-correlation between the two chains (e.g., for test functions f) in the MovieLens runs? Reporting this would illuminate how the RR extrapolation behaves with uncorrelated mini-batch noise.
- Fairness of compute-time comparisons: SGRRLD uses two chains in parallel; SGLD could also run two parallel chains and average their predictions at the same wall-clock cost. To isolate the RR benefit, please compare against (a) two independent SGLD chains averaged; (b) SGLD with step size γ/2 (or appropriately tuned γ) for the same total gradient evaluations; and (c) SGLD with the same Gaussian coupling if relevant. Without these baselines, practical gains may conflate extrapolation with extra compute and variance reduction via averaging.
- Real-data gains are modest: The RMSE improvements on MovieLens appear small; error bars are not shown, and it is unclear if differences are statistically significant. Additional real-data tasks (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets, topic models) and sensitivity studies (mini-batch size, step-size schedules) would better substantiate practical impact.
- Mixing vs bias: RR reduces weak discretization bias but does not improve mixing across modes. Please discuss limitations on multimodal targets.

Quality
- The theoretical framework is strong and, conditional on the stated assumptions, appears sound. However, key assumptions are deferred to the supplement; bringing them forward and discussing their practical scope (especially nonconvex/multimodal U) would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments convincingly validate theory on synthetic data. Real-data evidence is suggestive but not decisive; ablations versus multi-chain and half-step SGLD are needed.

Clarity
- The algorithm is clearly described and easy to implement; the Gaussian coupling (Σ = I) recommendation should be highlighted in the main algorithm box.
- Minor notational checks: in Eq. (5) the argument of ∇Ũ(γ/2)2k+1 appears as θ(γ/2)2k+1 in both sub-steps; please confirm if this is intended (semi-implicit) or a typo (should the first sub-step evaluate at θ(γ/2)2k?).
- Please state explicitly that both chains must use the same mini-batch size and the same law L.

Originality
- RR extrapolation for SDEs is known; the novel contribution is its adaptation to SG-MCMC with correlated Brownian increments and concrete convergence proofs and bounds. This is a meaningful and nontrivial synthesis.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is high: improved rates without higher-order integrators and a broadly applicable recipe for SG-MCMC. Practical significance is plausible but currently limited by modest real-data gains and missing baselines; stronger empirical validation could make this impactful for practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: two-chain SGLD averaging; SGLD with γ/2; compute-normalized comparisons (fixed total stochastic gradient evaluations) and hardware-normalized comparisons.
- Report chain cross-correlation and examine sharing mini-batches across chains.
- Clarify assumptions on U, L, and f in the main text; discuss multimodality and mixing.
- Include confidence intervals on MovieLens RMSE, and add at least one more large-scale task.
- Explore recursive RR (multi-level) and compare to high-order integrators on the same budget.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general method with strong theory and improved finite-time/asymptotic rates.
- Clear algorithm, easy to adopt; synthetic results align tightly with theory.
- Bridges classical RR extrapolation with modern SG-MCMC.

Arguments against acceptance
- Real-world improvements are small; lacks rigorous compute-equated baselines.
- Important assumptions and limitations not clearly stated in main text.
- Practical guidance on gradient-noise correlation and coupling is limited.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying assumptions and adding fair, compute-matched baselines (two-chain and half-step SGLD) and stronger real-data evidence. The theoretical contribution and simplicity justify inclusion, and with the suggested additions the practical case would be compelling.
