This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, specifically focusing on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, requiring the parallel execution of two SGLD chains with different step sizes. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and improved convergence rates compared to standard SGLD. Empirical results on both synthetic and real (matrix factorization) datasets support these findings.

The paper addresses a significant problem in scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets: the bias inherent in SG-MCMC algorithms. The proposed approach is elegant and relatively straightforward to implement, building upon existing SG-MCMC techniques. The theoretical contributions, establishing convergence guarantees and improved rates, are valuable additions to the field. The analysis appears generally sound, with careful consideration given to variance control through correlated Brownian motions. The experimental validation, while limited in scope, provides encouraging evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness. This work builds on a strong foundation of existing literature in SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014; Teh et al., 2016) and numerical methods for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2015).

However, there are several weaknesses that need to be addressed. The primary concern lies with the derivation of the convergence rates. While Theorem 1 states the asymptotic behavior, the jump from these theoretical results to the concrete rates of O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) for MSE seems insufficiently justified within the text. A more detailed explanation connecting the parameters of the theorem (particularly regarding the sequence of step sizes γ<sub>k</sub>) to this specific rate is crucial. This is further compounded by the discrepancy in Figure 3, where the bias plot appears to exhibit the lowest error for α=0.2 (red curve), contradicting the paper’s claim of α=1/3 being optimal.  A detailed analysis of why α=0.2 performs best in this setting, and a reconciliation with the theoretical prediction, is required. The substantial fluctuations in the “optimal” curve in Figure 3 also raise questions about the reliability of this empirical rate and warrant further investigation.

A practical concern revolves around the computational cost. Running two chains in parallel, with one requiring twice the number of steps, inherently increases the computational expense. The comparison in Figure 5, which doesn’t account for this difference, presents an unfair advantage to SGRRLD. To provide a valid comparison, the reported SGLD timing should be scaled by a factor of 2/3, which would likely reduce the observed performance gap.

Minor issues detract from the overall clarity and rigor. Equation (1) uses the symbol *ϑ* while the remainder of the paper uses *θ*. Line 94 introduces "RR" without prior definition.  Γ<sub>K</sub> is used in line 131 without being formally introduced.  The statement “smooth function f” in line 160 lacks the necessary specification of smoothness assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity of derivatives). There's a figure labeling error in line 249 ("Bias vs. time vs. dimension"). Finally, vectors are not consistently represented in boldface.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
*   Solid theoretical guarantees regarding convergence and rates.
*   Empirical results demonstrate improved performance on synthetic and real datasets.
*   The method is relatively easy to implement and integrate into existing SG-MCMC frameworks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently detailed justification for the specific convergence rates derived from the theorems.
*   Discrepancy between theoretical predictions and empirical results (Figure 3).
*   Unfair comparison in Figure 5 due to neglecting the increased computational cost of SGRRLD.
*   Minor issues in clarity, notation and presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. The core idea is novel and promising, supported by a strong theoretical foundation. However, the concerns regarding the derivation of convergence rates, the discrepancy in Figure 3, and the unfair comparison in Figure 5 significantly detract from the paper's impact. Addressing these issues, particularly providing a clearer explanation of the convergence rate derivation and a fair comparison with SGLD, would substantially strengthen the manuscript and increase my confidence in its conclusions. I recommend acceptance contingent upon these revisions.
