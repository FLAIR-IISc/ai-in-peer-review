This paper proposes applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, yielding SGRRLD: two SGLD chains run with stepsizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and extrapolated, with correlated Gaussian increments to control variance. The theory gives a CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and shows improved rates relative to first-order SG-MCMC (e.g., MSE O(K^-4/5) under optimal constant stepsize scaling). The idea is simple to implement and broadly applicable beyond SGLD (SGHMC example). The work connects classical weak-error expansions for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro) and RR/duplicated diffusions (Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) to the SG-MCMC literature (e.g., Welling & Teh; Ma et al.; Chen et al. on higher-order integrators). The method/theory overall looks solid and timely.

Quality: The main theorems are well-motivated and, if the assumptions hold, support the bias/MSE improvements. However, a major concern is how the claimed convergence rates are precisely derived from Theorems 1–2. The mapping from conditions on ΓK and Γ(3)K to the K-exponents, and the transition between decreasing-stepsize (Theorem 1) and fixed-stepsize (Theorem 2) regimes, should be made explicit, with constants and assumptions on f, U, and L stated in the main text. Also, clarify why in Fig. 2 the 1/(Kγ) term appears to dominate for SGRRLD at small γ but a similar rise is not evident for SGLD; is this due to different K, burn-in, or averaging?

Clarity: Generally clear, but there are several notational issues: Eq. 1 uses ϑ while the rest uses θ; “RR” is not defined at first use (line 94); ΓK is referenced before definition (line 131); “smooth function f” needs precise assumptions (e.g., C^p with polynomial growth) where used; Fig. labeling around line 249 is inconsistent (bias vs time vs dimension); vectors should be bold.

Originality: Applying RR to SG-MCMC with variance control via correlated Brownian increments is, to my knowledge, new and distinct from NIPS’15 higher-order integrators.

Significance: If the computational tradeoffs are fairly presented, this is a useful tool for practitioners and could influence SG-MCMC practice.

Experimental concerns: On a single core, SGRRLD costs roughly 3× per unit horizon (two chains, one with double steps). Figure 5 compares wall-clock when using two cores in parallel; to be fair under equal compute, SGLD time should be scaled by 2/3, which would shrink the reported gap. In Figure 3 (bias plot), the best curve looks like α=0.2 (red), conflicting with the text’s α=1/3; and the “optimal” curve fluctuates substantially—please explain the discrepancy and provide confidence bands or longer runs.

Pros: simple, general, theoretically grounded, promising results. Cons: rate derivation clarity; fairness of timing; figure inconsistencies; notational issues.

Recommendation: borderline accept, contingent on clarifying rate derivations and fair compute-normalized comparisons.
