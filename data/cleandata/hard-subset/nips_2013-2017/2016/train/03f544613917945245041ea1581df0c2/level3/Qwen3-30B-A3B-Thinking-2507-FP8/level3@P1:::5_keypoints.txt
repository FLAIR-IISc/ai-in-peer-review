- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two parallel chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) and combining results to reduce bias.  
- Key advantage: bias reduction without inter-chain communication, making it suitable for distributed/parallel settings.  
- Extends idea to SGHMC, showing generality beyond SGLD.  
- Provides theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem for SGRRLD.  
- Derives non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds, claiming MSE convergence O(K^-4/5) versus SGLD’s O(K^-2/3).  
- Empirical results (synthetic and real data) show SGRRLD outperforms SGLD for equivalent compute, notably on large-scale matrix factorization.  
- Method is conceptually simple and leverages a well-known numerical technique in a novel domain.  
- Strong points: thorough theoretical work, appropriate experimental design, and clear, well-organized writing.  
- Practical strength: embarrassingly parallel implementation that fits modern distributed architectures.  
- Weakness: no experimental comparison to higher-order integrator methods (e.g., Chen et al. 2015), leaving practical advantage unclear.  
- Weakness: inconsistencies about run lengths — Section 3’s equal time-horizon claim conflicts with Equation (5) implying twice as many iterations for the second chain.  
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 in the supplement contradicts Equation (5), raising implementation/replication concerns.  
- Weakness: inconsistent statements about optimal step size (claims γ ∝ K^-1/5 vs earlier analysis implying γ ∝ K^-1/3), requiring clarification.  
- Overall: promising, impactful idea with solid theory and experiments but needs clarifications and comparisons to higher-order methods before strong acceptance.
