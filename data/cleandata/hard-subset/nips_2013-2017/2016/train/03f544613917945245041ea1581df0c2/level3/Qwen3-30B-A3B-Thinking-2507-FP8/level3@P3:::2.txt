

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces SGRRLD (Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics), a novel algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias from gradient estimation noise. The authors build upon the well-established RR extrapolation method for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and adapt it to the SG-MCMC framework, which has been popularized by Welling & Teh (2011) for Bayesian inference in large-scale settings. The key insight is that by running two parallel chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining them via RR extrapolation, the bias can be reduced from O(γ) to O(γ²) while keeping the variance manageable.

The theoretical contribution is strong: the authors prove that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and MSE than SGLD, with convergence rates of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/₃). They also show that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only a first-order integrator. The algorithm is clearly described with a well-structured pseudo-code in the supplementary material, making it easy to implement. The synthetic experiments on linear Gaussian models provide clear evidence of the bias-reduction advantage, with SGRRLD reducing variance estimation bias from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴.

However, the real-data experiments on MovieLens datasets present a more nuanced picture. While SGRRLD shows improved RMSE compared to SGLD, the gains are modest (e.g., 200 seconds vs. 400 seconds for ML-1M), and the paper fails to provide compelling evidence that these improvements are statistically significant or practically meaningful. The authors claim SGRRLD is more efficient, but without proper statistical testing of the RMSE differences or comparison to other baselines, this claim remains questionable.

Several important questions remain unanswered. The authors do not specify the exact conditions on U (the potential energy function) required for their theorems, particularly regarding multimodality - a critical consideration for real-world applications. The paper also doesn't address how gradient noise correlation affects performance, and whether the two chains in SGRRLD become decorrelated quickly (which could undermine the RR extrapolation benefit). The authors should have investigated whether the two chains in their real-data experiments showed correlation as claimed in the theoretical framework.

The computational cost claim is also problematic. While the authors note SGRRLD's parallelization cost is comparable to SGLD, they fail to compare it to the more natural baseline of running SGLD with multiple parallel chains (which is also possible and commonly used). A proper comparison would involve running SGLD with two parallel chains and comparing to SGRRLD, or running SGLD with step size γ/2 to isolate the RR benefit.

Strengths:
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a well-motivated theoretical contribution
- Clear algorithm description with easy implementation
- Strong theoretical analysis with rigorous convergence proofs
- Synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate bias reduction
- The approach is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms

Weaknesses:
- Real-data results lack statistical significance testing and fail to convincingly demonstrate practical benefit
- Insufficient discussion of conditions on U (e.g., multimodality)
- Incomplete analysis of gradient noise correlation effects
- Unaddressed concerns about parallelization cost comparison to multi-chain SGLD
- Limited real-data experiments that don't provide strong evidence for practical utility

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of SG-MCMC, but the practical significance is questionable based on the current experimental results. The authors should strengthen their case by providing more robust real-data experiments, addressing the theoretical conditions on U, and conducting proper comparisons to multi-chain SGLD. Without these improvements, the practical benefit of SGRRLD remains uncertain, despite the elegant theoretical framework.

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting theoretical advance with clear potential, but the experimental evidence does not sufficiently demonstrate its practical advantages over existing methods. The authors should address the reviewers' concerns before the paper can be confidently accepted as a significant contribution to the field.
