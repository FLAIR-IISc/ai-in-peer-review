This paper proposes a method to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2, with the second chain running twice as many steps) and then combine their outputs in a specific way to cancel the leading-order error term. The proposed algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), is an application of this technique to the popular SGLD algorithm. To control the variance of the resulting estimator, the method employs correlated Gaussian noise increments across the two chains.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed method. They prove that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Importantly, they derive non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and mean squared error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD can achieve faster convergence rates (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) compared to standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This performance is on par with methods using more complex higher-order numerical integrators. The paper situates this work relative to prior analyses of SG-MCMC convergence [10, 19] and the use of RR extrapolation for SDEs without stochastic gradients [23]. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, where SGRRLD demonstrates improved accuracy and efficiency over SGLD.

### Strengths

*   **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound and very well-written. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is explained logically, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The connection between the asymptotic CLT and the non-asymptotic bounds is well-established, and the resulting optimal convergence rates are a significant theoretical result. The experiments are well-designed to validate the specific theoretical claims about bias and MSE reduction.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a key limitation of SG-MCMC methods: the bias introduced by the numerical integrator. The proposed method provides a simple, intuitive way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods without explicitly deriving and implementing complex integrators. By simply running a standard algorithm twice with different settings, practitioners can potentially achieve significant performance gains. The empirical results on the large-scale matrix factorization problem suggest that these gains are not just theoretical but can be realized in practice.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

*   **Originality:** While the application and analysis are novel, the core components are based on existing ideas. The use of higher-order integrators to improve SG-MCMC has been explored (e.g., Chen et al. [10]), and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been analyzed for the invariant distribution of SDEs (Lemaire et al. [23]). The main contribution lies in the synthesis of these ideas: applying RR extrapolation to the *stochastic gradient* setting and providing a complete theoretical analysis of its properties. This is a valuable contribution, but it could be viewed as an incremental step.
*   **Practicality and Unfair Comparison:** The paper's claim that SGRRLD is "well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures" needs more careful consideration. The use of perfectly correlated noise (Eq. 7) is crucial for controlling the variance, but it requires communication and synchronization between the two parallel chains at *every single iteration* to generate Z(γ)k+1. This introduces a communication overhead that is not discussed or measured. In a truly distributed setting (e.g., across different machines), this communication cost could easily dominate the computation time, making the method less practical than suggested.
*   **Experimental Details and Baselines:** The experimental comparison to SGLD may be unfair or at least requires clarification.
    *   **Resource Allocation:** The paper states that "the two chains of SGRRLD are run in parallel" and compares wall-clock time. Does this mean SGRRLD was allocated two CPU cores while SGLD was allocated only one? If so, the comparison is essentially showing that using twice the computational resources can lead to faster convergence, which is not surprising. A fair comparison would either allocate the same total resources to both methods or be explicit about the resource difference.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A more natural baseline for a parallel implementation would be to run two *independent* SGLD chains (an embarrassingly parallel task with no communication overhead) and average their results. This would achieve variance reduction through simple averaging. It is crucial to compare SGRRLD against this baseline to demonstrate that the benefits of RR extrapolation and noise correlation outweigh the costs of implementation complexity and communication. How does SGRRLD perform against two independent SGLD chains running on the same parallel hardware?

### Detailed Comments

1.  The paper claims that "SGLD cannot benefit from this parallel computation architecture due to its inherently sequential nature." While a single SGLD chain is sequential, one can easily run multiple independent chains in parallel. This is a standard approach for improving MCMC estimates and serves as a very strong, communication-free baseline that is missing from the evaluation.
2.  Could the authors clarify the precise setup for the wall-clock time experiments? Specifically, how many CPU cores were used for SGLD versus SGRRLD? If SGRRLD used two cores and SGLD used one, the results should be framed as a trade-off between computational resources and convergence speed.
3.  The discussion of parallelization should be revised to acknowledge the communication overhead required by Eq. (7) and (8). It would be helpful to discuss scenarios where this overhead is negligible (e.g., on a multi-core CPU with shared memory) versus scenarios where it might be a significant bottleneck (e.g., in a distributed cluster over a network).
4.  Minor point: The definition "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is provided in both the Introduction (line 25) and Section 2.1 (line 70). This is redundant.

In summary, the paper presents a well-executed piece of work with strong theoretical and empirical backing. The main concerns relate to the practical implications of the communication overhead and the fairness of the experimental comparison. Addressing these points, particularly by comparing against a baseline of two independent SGLD chains, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about practical utility.
