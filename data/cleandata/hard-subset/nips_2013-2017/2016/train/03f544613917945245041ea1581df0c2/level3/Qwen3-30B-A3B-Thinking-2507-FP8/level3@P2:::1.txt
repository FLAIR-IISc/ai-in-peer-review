

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon Welling and Teh's foundational work on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) and extend the numerical analysis technique of RRE (Talay and Tubaro, 1990) to the MCMC setting. By running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and applying RRE, they demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves better convergence rates than SGLD, with MSE converging at O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}).

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors provide formal proofs for their convergence results, carefully addressing conditions under which the method works. The experimental setup is appropriate, with both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrating the theoretical advantages. The empirical results consistently support the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements in bias and MSE on the Gaussian model (bias reduced by two orders of magnitude) and modest but consistent gains on large-scale matrix factorization tasks. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for correlated Brownian increments to control variance.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with consistent mathematical notation. The exposition of RRE in the context of SG-MCMC is particularly accessible, making the connection between numerical analysis and MCMC clear for both communities. However, Figure 1a is misleading—it shows a Gaussian with empirical mean and variance rather than the empirical posterior distribution, potentially confusing readers about the quality of the approximation.

Originality: The application of RRE to SG-MCMC is novel, as the authors note this technique has not been explored in the SG-MCMC literature. While the method is a straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, the theoretical analysis is non-trivial and demonstrates how this approach can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods. The technique is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms, but the contribution is more of a clever application of existing numerical techniques rather than a transformative breakthrough.

Significance: The results have practical significance for large-scale Bayesian inference, as SGRRLD achieves better accuracy with the same computational time as SGLD (or better, due to natural parallelization). The method is computationally convenient and can be easily integrated into existing SG-MCMC implementations. The improvements are significant on synthetic data and consistent on real-world problems, making it valuable for practitioners. While not revolutionary, it represents a useful addition to the SG-MCMC toolkit.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical analysis with clear convergence results
- Well-written and clearly organized paper
- Empirical results consistently support theoretical claims
- Method is straightforward to implement and naturally parallelizable
- Demonstrates significant improvements on synthetic data

Weaknesses:
- Figure 1a is misleading (shows Gaussian with empirical moments rather than empirical posterior)
- Figure 2a requires better explanation for counter-intuitive bias behavior as step size decreases
- The method is a straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, not truly transformative

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a novel application of RRE to SG-MCMC with strong theoretical backing and empirical validation. The method is practical, easy to implement, and provides clear improvements over SGLD, making it valuable to the Bayesian and MCMC communities.

Arguments against acceptance: The contribution is somewhat incremental, as it applies an existing numerical technique to a new domain without introducing fundamentally new concepts. While the results are significant for practitioners, the theoretical novelty is limited.

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field by providing a practical method to reduce bias in SG-MCMC methods while maintaining computational efficiency. The technique is likely to be adopted by practitioners working on large-scale Bayesian inference problems. While not revolutionary, it is a useful and well-executed addition to the literature on SG-MCMC.
