

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel approach to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The authors propose Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2, correlates the injected Gaussian noise as α_k = (β_{2k-1}+β_{2k})/√2, and subtracts the estimates to obtain O(γ²) bias. The paper builds upon the recent theoretical understanding of SG-MCMC convergence rates (Chen et al., NIPS 2015), extending it to show that RR extrapolation can achieve higher convergence rates without requiring higher-order integrators.

**Quality**: The theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-supported. The authors prove that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias O(γ²) and MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to SGLD's bias O(γ) and MSE O(K⁻²/³). The central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds are derived with appropriate conditions on the potential function and gradient estimators. The experimental results on synthetic and real data (matrix factorization) consistently support the theoretical claims. However, the paper does not fully address the applicability question: the method appears to require that the SG-MCMC satisfies π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ²), which may not hold for all SG-MCMC variants.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear exposition. The technical content is presented logically, and the connection between numerical analysis and MCMC is well-motivated. The presentation would be significantly improved by including a pseudocode box for SGRRLD early in the paper (as suggested in the key points), which would help practitioners implement the method without delving into the theoretical details.

**Originality**: The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to the ML community by applying RR extrapolation—a well-established technique in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Pages, 2007)—to SG-MCMC. While the mathematical framework is based on existing numerical methods, its application to SG-MCMC is original and timely, addressing a fundamental limitation (bias) of current SG-MCMC methods.

**Significance**: The proposed method has broad potential impact. It requires minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC implementations while significantly improving convergence rates. The parallel nature of the algorithm makes it well-suited for distributed implementations, and the authors demonstrate this with matrix factorization experiments. The method's simplicity and effectiveness make it likely to be adopted widely. However, the matrix factorization experiments would be stronger with a convergence run to steady state and an SGD baseline to contextualize the Bayesian benefit.

**Key Strengths**:
- Simple implementation requiring minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC codes
- Proven theoretical improvement in convergence rates
- Broad applicability to many SG-MCMC methods
- Parallelizable, making it suitable for distributed computing
- Well-written with clear theoretical and experimental validation

**Key Concerns**:
1. **Applicability**: The method's requirement that π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ²) may exclude some SG-MCMC variants. The authors should clarify the necessary conditions for applicability.
2. **Efficiency**: Running one chain twice as long as the other may waste computational resources. A three-chain approach (one with step size γ, two with γ/2 run for equal steps) could eliminate idle time, but the paper doesn't address how this would affect the variance.
3. **Extensions**: The paper mentions recursive RR extrapolation (as in Pages, 2007) but doesn't provide a clear path for extending to more than two step sizes.
4. **Noise correlation**: The optimal correlation between minibatch noise (Z^{(γ)} and Z^{(γ/2)}) is established as Σ=Id, but the paper doesn't clarify whether using the same minibatch (rather than correlated noise) would be equivalent or optimal.
5. **Experiments**: The matrix factorization experiment should run to convergence and include an SGD baseline to demonstrate the Bayesian advantage.

**Recommendation**: This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The theoretical advances are well-supported, the method is simple and broadly applicable, and the experimental results validate the claims. The concerns raised are important but do not undermine the core contribution. With minor clarifications addressing the applicability conditions, noise correlation, and computational efficiency, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Arguments for acceptance**: Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, significant theoretical improvement in convergence rates, simple implementation, strong experimental validation, well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance**: Limited discussion of applicability conditions, lack of pseudocode for practitioners, some computational efficiency concerns.

Overall, this paper advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by providing a simple yet effective method to reduce bias without sacrificing computational efficiency. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm has the potential to become a standard technique in Bayesian machine learning for large-scale applications.
