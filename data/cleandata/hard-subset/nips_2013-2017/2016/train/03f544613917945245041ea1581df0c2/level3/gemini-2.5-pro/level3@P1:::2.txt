### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating sequence convergence, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2, with the second chain running twice as many steps) and linearly combining their outputs. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates (e.g., an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The method is evaluated on a synthetic Gaussian model, where it shows a clear reduction in bias, and on a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendations, where it demonstrates improved efficiency in terms of wall-clock time. The work positions itself as a way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order updates, leveraging parallel computation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Contribution:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-known technique for SDEs, its application to the SG-MCMC framework is novel and significant. The paper provides a principled way to reduce the discretization bias of SGLD, which is a major limiting factor for its accuracy. The proposed method is simple to understand and implement.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is technically sound, providing a thorough convergence analysis. The authors establish a Central Limit Theorem and derive non-asymptotic bounds for both bias and MSE. The analysis clearly shows that SGRRLD can attain higher rates of convergence than SGLD, providing a strong theoretical justification for the method.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Method Description:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively motivates the problem of bias in SG-MCMC and explains how RR extrapolation can address it. The algorithm is presented clearly, making it easy for readers to understand and potentially reproduce.

4.  **Convincing Synthetic Experiments:** The experiments on the linear Gaussian model effectively validate the theoretical claims. The results in Figures 1, 2, and 3 clearly demonstrate the superior bias and MSE reduction of SGRRLD compared to SGLD, and confirm the derived optimal convergence rates with respect to the step size schedule. The extension to SGHMC also successfully illustrates the generality of the approach.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Weak Empirical Evidence on Real-World Data:** The practical benefit of SGRRLD is not convincingly demonstrated. In the MovieLens experiments (Figure 5), the improvement in RMSE over SGLD is quite marginal. While SGRRLD reaches a similar RMSE faster, the final performance is not substantially better. For a method that introduces additional complexity, a stronger empirical demonstration of its real-world utility is needed.

2.  **Unfair Computational Cost Comparison:** The paper's claim of improved efficiency is based on the parallelizability of SGRRLD's two chains. However, this comparison is potentially unfair. A user with two parallel cores could also run two independent SGLD chains and average their results, which is a standard technique for variance reduction. A fair comparison would require benchmarking SGRRLD against a baseline that uses the same computational resources, such as:
    *   Two independent SGLD chains run in parallel with step size γ.
    *   A single SGLD chain with step size γ/2 run for twice the number of iterations to match the total number of gradient computations.
    Without these baselines, it is difficult to disentangle the benefit of the RR extrapolation from the simple benefit of parallelization.

3.  **Unclear Practicality regarding Correlation:** The theory highlights the importance of using correlated Brownian motions to control variance. However, the paper does not sufficiently discuss the effect of the stochastic gradient noise. If the mini-batches for the two chains are sampled independently, the chains could decorrelate quickly due to the gradient noise, potentially diminishing the effectiveness of the RR scheme. The paper should clarify how mini-batches were sampled in the experiments and comment on the practical importance of this choice.

4.  **Ambiguity in Theoretical Assumptions:** The main text states that the theorems hold "under appropriate conditions on U, f and L," with the details deferred to the supplement. While this is common, it would significantly improve the paper's clarity to briefly summarize the key assumptions in the main text. For instance, do the proofs require strong convexity of the potential U? If so, this would limit the applicability of the theory to multimodal posteriors, which are common in machine learning.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

*   **Experimental Baselines:** I strongly recommend that you strengthen the experimental section by including the fairer baselines mentioned above (e.g., two parallel SGLD chains). This would provide much stronger evidence that the performance gain comes from the RR extrapolation itself, and not just from using more computational resources than the SGLD baseline.

*   **Real-World Impact:** To better substantiate the paper's claims, could you include experiments on other large-scale models where bias is known to be a significant issue, such as Bayesian neural networks or topic models? A more substantial improvement on a real-world metric would make the paper much more compelling.

*   **Correlation of Stochastic Gradients:** Could you clarify the mechanism for sampling mini-batches for the two chains in your experiments? Were they independent, or did you use the same mini-batch for both chains at each step `k`? How does the correlation (or lack thereof) in the stochastic gradient noise affect the error cancellation? The theoretical analysis focuses on the correlation of the injected Gaussian noise `Z`, but the gradient noise seems equally important. Did you observe if the two chains `θ(γ)` and `θ(γ/2)` remained highly correlated throughout the runs in practice?

*   **Assumptions on `U`:** Could you please provide a brief, intuitive summary of the conditions on the potential `U` required for Theorems 1 and 2 to hold? Specifically, is the theory applicable to non-convex or multimodal potentials, which are of great interest in modern machine learning?

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical contribution. The idea is elegant and the analysis is thorough. However, the experimental evaluation, particularly on real-world data and with respect to fair computational baselines, needs to be strengthened to fully justify the practical significance of the proposed method.
