This paper proposes SG-RR-MCMC, instantiated as Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC to reduce bias while keeping variance controlled via correlated Brownian increments. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 in embarrassingly parallel fashion with no communication, and combines their empirical averages to cancel first-order weak error. The authors prove asymptotic consistency and a CLT, and provide non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds suggesting improved rates (e.g., O(K^−4/5) MSE vs. O(K^−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC). Empirically, on Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization, SGRRLD improves over SGLD at matched wall-clock time. The work relates to NIPS contributions on SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh; Chen et al.; Ma et al.) and to RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès).

Quality: The theoretical development is generally sound and appropriately leverages RR for bias reduction. The claims are supported by finite-time bounds and asymptotics, and the synthetic/real experiments are satisfactory. However, (i) there is an inconsistent statement about the step-size schedule, and (ii) Algorithm 1 in the supplement appears inconsistent with Equation (5) (e.g., number/timing of γ/2 steps per γ step). Please reconcile these to avoid ambiguity. It would also help to report sensitivity to the Brownian correlation setting and to mini-batch correlation choices.

Clarity: The paper is mostly clear and well-organized, with a helpful positioning of assumptions. Moving key assumptions and the precise Σ = I optimality statement from the supplement into the main text would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality: The idea is a conceptually simple but novel combination of RR extrapolation with SGLD/SG-MCMC. While RR is classical in SDE numerics, its careful adaptation to SG-MCMC with variance control and parallelization is new and interesting.

Significance: The approach is practical, parallel-friendly, and potentially impactful. To strengthen significance, include experimental comparisons against high-order SG-MCMC integrators for the same base algorithm (e.g., SGLD with symmetric/second-order schemes), not only SGHMC-s, to isolate the benefit of RR vs. integrator order.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general idea with clear theoretical guarantees and improved finite-time rates.
- Embarrassingly parallel and easy to implement.
- Solid empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale tasks.

Cons against acceptance:
- Inconsistencies in step-size description and algorithm pseudocode.
- Missing head-to-head with high-order SGLD variants; compute-budget vs wall-clock comparisons could be clarified.
- Limited ablations on correlation choices and mini-batch coupling.

Overall, in scope for NIPS and a constructive contribution; address the inconsistencies and broaden comparisons.
