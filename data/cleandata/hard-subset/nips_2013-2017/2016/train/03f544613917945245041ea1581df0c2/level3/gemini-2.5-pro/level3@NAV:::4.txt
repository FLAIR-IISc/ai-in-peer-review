This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which adapts the classical Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to improve the performance of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. By running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs, the method aims to reduce the discretization bias and thereby achieve faster convergence rates for both the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The work builds upon existing literature on SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD) and the application of RR to SDEs, representing a novel and well-motivated synthesis of these ideas for large-scale Bayesian inference.

The paper's strengths lie in its elegance and potential for practical impact. The core idea is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement with minimal changes to standard SG-MCMC frameworks. The theoretical analysis appears solid, providing a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds that suggest superior convergence rates compared to first-order methods like SGLD.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses that currently undermine its contributions. The most critical issue is a significant gap in the theoretical exposition: the paper does not clearly show how the claimed optimal convergence rates (e.g., an MSE rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for a step size decay α=1/5) are derived from the main theorems. This lack of a clear connection is a fatal flaw that weakens the paper's primary theoretical result.

The experimental evaluation is also a major concern. The wall-clock time comparison in Figure 5 is unfair, as SGRRLD requires approximately three times the single-core computation of SGLD (one chain for K steps, another for 2K steps). A fair comparison would require scaling the SGLD runtime, which would substantially reduce the reported performance gap. Furthermore, there are inconsistencies between the text and figures; for example, the optimal curve for bias in Figure 3 appears at α=0.2, conflicting with the text's claim of α=1/3. The paper also needs to better explain the behavior of the `1/(Kγ)` error term at small step sizes.

In summary, while the paper introduces an original and promising approach, the unaddressed gap in the theoretical derivation and the flawed experimental comparison are too significant to ignore. If the authors can rigorously derive their claimed convergence rates and provide a fair computational comparison, this work would represent a strong and significant contribution to the field.
