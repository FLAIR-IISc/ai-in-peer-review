- Paper applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, producing SGRRLD by combining two SGLD chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) with correlated Brownian increments.  
- Authors prove asymptotically reduced bias O(γ^2) and MSE O(γ^4) versus SGLD, and non-asymptotic MSE convergence O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3}) under optimal schedules.  
- Synthetic linear-Gaussian experiments show clear bias and MSE reductions; MovieLens yields only slight RMSE improvement.  
- Algorithm is clearly described and appears easy to implement.  
- Main theoretical assumptions are deferred to the supplement; precise conditions on U, L, and f (smoothness order, dissipativity/strong-convexity, Lipschitz/Hölder, polynomial growth) should be stated in the main text.  
- It is unclear whether proofs cover multimodal/nonconvex (coercive but nonconvex) posteriors or require global strong convexity; Poisson equation conditions need explicit statement.  
- On stochastic gradients, the paper argues Brownian coupling dominates variance—need quantification of regimes where gradient noise dominates and whether uncorrelated mini-batches across chains break the error order.  
- Empirical details missing: did authors share mini-batch indices between chains, and was state/gradient-noise correlation measured on real data?  
- Clarity could be improved with a main-text pseudo-code, a schematic of the coupling, and practitioner guidance on step-size scheduling (e.g., α=1/5) plus sensitivity analysis on real data.  
- Originality: adapting classical RR extrapolation from SDE weak-error theory to SG-MCMC with convergence proofs and variance control is novel and interesting.  
- Practical significance is uncertain due to modest real-data gains; claims of “same computation time” need benchmarking against two parallel SGLD chains and SGLD at γ/2.  
- Missing baselines and metrics: compare ESS/time or bias-vs-time to isolate benefits from extrapolation versus parallelism/step-size tuning.  
- Recommend more real-data experiments (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, BNNs) to demonstrate practical impact.  
- Pros: simple, well-motivated algorithm; strong synthetic evidence; careful variance-control via coupling.  
- Cons: assumptions applicability to realistic/multimodal U not explicit; limited practical gains and missing baselines; lack of empirical analysis of chain correlation and mini-batch coupling.  
- Overall: technically solid and promising but requires clearer assumptions and stronger empirical comparisons to substantiate practical benefits.
