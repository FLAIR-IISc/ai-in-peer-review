This paper proposes a Richardson-Romberg (RR) scheme to improve the bias and convergence rates of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, specifically applied to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and extrapolate the results, a technique borrowed from numerical analysis. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides solid theoretical grounding for the proposed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm. The theoretical analysis demonstrates asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem, with improved convergence rates compared to standard SGLD. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, demonstrating its consistency and convergence properties. The derived convergence rates are compelling and a significant contribution.
* **Practical Applicability:** The proposed method is relatively easy to implement, requiring minimal changes to existing SGLD implementations. This lowers the barrier to adoption.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the methodology and results easy to understand.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of SGRRLD.
* **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea with the potential to significantly improve the performance of these algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate Derivation (Fatal Flaw):** The derivation of the specific convergence rates, particularly the K<sup>-4/5</sup> rate for MSE, requires careful scrutiny. The transition from the Taylor expansion in the proof of Theorem 1 to the stated rate is not immediately clear and appears to rely on assumptions that aren't explicitly stated or justified. A more detailed explanation of this step is crucial.
* **Unfair Timing Comparison:** The timing comparison in Figure 5 is misleading. Running two chains in parallel for SGRRLD while only one for SGLD, then comparing total time without accounting for this difference, is unfair. SGLD’s timing should be scaled by 2/3 to reflect the equivalent computational cost. This scaling would likely reduce the reported gap in performance.
* **Ambiguous Optimal Parameter:** In Figure 3, the best curve for bias appears at α=0.2 (red), contradicting the text's claim of α=1/3. While the authors mention fluctuations, the empirical rate at α=0.2 should be explicitly discussed and explained, potentially hinting at limitations of the theoretical prediction.  The substantial fluctuation in the “optimal” curve raises concerns about the robustness of the empirically observed rate.
* **Unexplained SGLD Behavior:** Line 256 raises a valid question: when the stepsize is very small, the 1/(Kγ) term *should* dominate the bias. The paper does not adequately explain why this does not appear to occur for SGLD.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the polish of the paper: Eq. 1 uses a different symbol (ϑ) than θ, "RR" is not defined until after its first use (line 94), Γ<sub>K</sub> is undefined (line 131), the smoothness assumption for *f* in line 160 needs to be more precise, and there's a labeling error in Figure 3 (Bias vs. time vs. dimension). Vectors should consistently be bold.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian inference—reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The proposed method is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The RR extrapolation technique is a clever application of a well-established numerical method.
* The potential for improved convergence rates is substantial.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The derivation of the convergence rates is unclear and potentially flawed.
* The timing comparison is unfair and misrepresents the computational cost of SGRRLD.
* The discrepancy between theoretical predictions and empirical results (Figure 3) raises concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, particularly the concerns regarding the convergence rate derivation and the unfair timing comparison, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical framework is strong, and the empirical results are promising. However, addressing the identified flaws is crucial. I recommend acceptance with *major revisions*, contingent on a clear and convincing explanation of the convergence rate derivation, a fair comparison of computational costs, and a thorough discussion of the empirical observations regarding the optimal parameter α. Addressing the minor technical issues would also improve the overall quality of the paper.
