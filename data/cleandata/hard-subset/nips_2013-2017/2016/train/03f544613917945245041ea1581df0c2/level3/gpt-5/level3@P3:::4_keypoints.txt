- Paper introduces SGRRLD: Richardson–Romberg extrapolation applied to SG-MCMC (SGLD) using coupled γ and γ/2 discretizations with correlated Brownian increments.  
- Proves CLT (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds (Theorem 2); demonstrates empirical improvements on synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.  
- Builds on RR for SDEs, SG-MCMC theory/higher-order integrators, and variance control via correlated increments; novelty is adapting RR to stochastic-gradient setting with finite-time/asymptotic analysis.  
- Strength: clear, elegant scheme that upgrades first-order SG-MCMC to second-order accuracy with minimal code changes.  
- Strength: solid theoretical development including CLT with optimal correlation and finite-time MSE/bias bounds (constant-step MSE optimized to O(K^-4/5)).  
- Strength: method is generic (example for SGHMC given) and empirically supported.  
- Major technical concern: need explicit reconciliation between Theorem 1 (decreasing steps, RMSE O(K^-2/5)) and Theorem 2 (constant steps, MSE O(K^-4/5)), clarifying regimes and error notions.  
- Major technical concern: make dependence of constants in Theorem 2 explicit (especially on gradient-noise moments and dimension d) and discuss when asymptotic K-optimization predicts finite-K behavior.  
- Request: if reconciliation exists in supplement, bring a short, self-contained derivation into the main text.  
- Practical fairness concern: SGRRLD costs ~3 gradient evaluations per cycle (one coarse + two fine); reported wall-clock parity relied on running two chains in parallel, which biases comparisons for single-core runs.  
- Practical request: compare methods at matched gradient-evaluation counts or normalize wall-clock times to account for parallel resources; scale SGLD curves accordingly.  
- Practical question: clarify whether parallel minibatch gradient computation was enabled for SGLD baselines.  
- Empirical inconsistency: Figure 3 bias slope visually matches α=0.2 not α=1/3 as claimed—reconcile or provide error bars/longer runs/smoothing.  
- Empirical inconsistency: for very small γ the 1/(Kγ) term should dominate MSE for both methods; explain why SGLD does not show this in Figure 2 (e.g., K, burn-in, estimator variance).  
- Quality: theory appears careful and uses standard machinery (Poisson eqn, Taylor, martingale CLT); assumptions (C^9 Poisson solution etc.) are strong but standard—state precise smoothness/Lyapunov/ergodicity conditions in main text.  
- Correlated Brownian increments argument is persuasive and successfully extends duplicated-diffusion variance-control to SG setting.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but fix some definitions/notation issues and add short pseudocode for SGRRLD in main text.  
- Originality: adapting RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with correlated increments and finite-time rates is novel and timely.  
- Significance: practically attractive if overhead can be amortized via parallelism; likely to spur further RR/MLMC work in SG-MCMC.  
- Questions for authors: provide unified table of rates (bias, RMSE, MSE) for SGLD vs SGRRLD under constant/decreasing steps, with optimal α/γ and constant roles.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to imperfect minibatch correlation and whether sharing indices materially helps.  
- Questions for authors: for SGHMC, do you correlate momentum noise in RR coupling and are there differences vs Langevin?  
- Minor fixes: unify ϑ/θ notation, define Richardson–Romberg on first use, define ΓK/Γ(n)K before use, specify smoothness class for f, fix ambiguous figure labels and units, use bold for vectors.  
- Pros summary: simple, general, strong theory, easy to implement, good empirical performance.  
- Cons summary: rate statements need clearer derivation and empirical validation, wall-clock comparisons need normalization for parallelism, plus some notation/figure issues.  
- Recommendation: lean accept conditional on clarifying rate derivations (Theorem 1 vs 2), fixing Figure 3 inconsistencies, and making experimental comparisons fair w.r.t. computational budget.
