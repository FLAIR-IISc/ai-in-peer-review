This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), that aims to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) while keeping the variance at a reasonable level. The proposed algorithm applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to SGLD, which involves running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and extrapolating their estimators to construct a new one.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for its bias and mean squared error (MSE). They also conduct several experiments on synthetic and real data to demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD. The results show that SGRRLD can achieve higher rates of convergence than SGLD in both finite-time and asymptotically, and it can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The technical conditions and proofs are relegated to the supplementary document, which is a good choice to avoid obscuring the main results. The experiments are well-designed and provide strong evidence for the benefits of SGRRLD.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to bridge the gap between the MCMC and stochastic differential equation (SDE) literatures. The authors' use of RRE, a technique from numerical analysis, to improve the convergence of SGLD is a nice example of how ideas from different fields can be combined to achieve better results.

The empirical results are also impressive, with large improvements on toy examples and modest but consistent gains on large-scale problems. The authors' implementation of SGRRLD in C and their use of the GNU Scientific Library for efficient matrix computations are also noteworthy.

However, there are a few minor issues with the paper. Figure 1a is misleading, as it plots a Gaussian with empirical mean and variance plugged in, rather than the empirical posterior. The authors should clarify this in the caption. Additionally, Figure 2a needs discussion on why the bias may not decrease as the step size decreases, which is counter-intuitive.

Overall, the paper is solid, clearly written, and appears technically sound. The method is a straightforward, computationally convenient application of RRE to SGLD and is likely to be useful in practice. While it may not be transformative, it is a nice contribution to the field of Bayesian machine learning.
