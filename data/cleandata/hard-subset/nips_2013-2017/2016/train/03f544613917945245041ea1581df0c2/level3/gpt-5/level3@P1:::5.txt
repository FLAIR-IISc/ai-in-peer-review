This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), instantiated as SGRRLD for overdamped Langevin dynamics. The core idea is to reduce the O(γ) bias of first-order SG-MCMC by Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation: run two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and form a control-variate-like estimator 2·(small-step average) − (large-step average). To control variance, the chains share correlated Brownian increments, with the optimal choice given by coupling Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. The paper provides asymptotic (CLT) and non-asymptotic bounds: asymptotic bias O(γ2), variance O(γ4), MSE rate O(K−4/5) at optimal polynomially decaying steps, compared to O(K−2/3) for SGLD with first-order integrators. The approach is conceptually simple, embarrassingly parallel (two chains, no communication), and in principle applicable to SGHMC and other SG-MCMC schemes under standard weak error and ergodicity assumptions. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model, an SGHMC variant, and large-scale probabilistic matrix factorization show consistent reductions in bias/MSE and improved RMSE vs SGLD at matched wall-clock time.

Relation to prior work: SGRRLD builds on SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011] and the extensive SG-MCMC lineage (SGHMC, thermostats, preconditioning, quasi-Newton). It leverages RR extrapolation for SDEs [Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lamberton & Pagès; Lemaire et al., 2015], where duplicated diffusions and coupled Brownian increments are known to cancel leading-order discretization bias. Prior SG-MCMC accuracy improvements typically rely on higher-order integrators or splitting schemes [Chen et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2015]. This paper’s contribution is to transfer RR extrapolation to the stochastic-gradient setting, prove consistency/CLT/non-asymptotic bounds, and show that a first-order integrator can match the accuracy order of higher-order schemes while remaining easy to implement and parallelize.

Quality:
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are clearly stated, with conditions (smoothness, Poisson equation solvability) in the supplement. The analysis identifies the optimal coupling (Σ = I) and the optimal decay rate α = 1/5 for step sizes γk ∝ k−α to balance bias and fluctuation, yielding the O(K−2/5) error decay in the CLT regime and O(K−4/5) MSE in finite time. The requirement that both chains’ stochastic gradient estimators have the same law L (e.g., same mini-batch size) is important; otherwise, the estimator can reintroduce O(γ) bias.
- Experiments: Results support the theory: bias and MSE scaling with γ and K, dimension scaling, and wall-clock improvements on large datasets. The SGHMC extension (SGRRHMC) shows promising behavior and parity with a higher-order SGHMC-s at appropriate γ.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and situates the contribution within both the SG-MCMC and SDE extrapolation literatures. However, there are inconsistencies that should be corrected:
  - An inconsistent statement about step sizes: the main text alternates between constant and decreasing schedules; clarify when each set of results applies and keep notation uniform (γ vs η) across the main text and supplement.
  - Algorithm 1 in the supplement does not match Equation (5). Ensure the half-step updates, time-horizon synchronization, and Brownian coupling are exactly consistent.
  - Be explicit about the practical construction of the Brownian coupling and whether mini-batch indices are shared between the two half-steps to reduce additional stochastic gradient noise (the analysis allows correlated gradients but does not specify a recommended coupling).
  - Clarify compute-budget fairness: SGRRLD doubles gradient evaluations per unit time; wall-clock parity depends on parallel resources. Report both wall-clock and number of gradient evaluations to facilitate apples-to-apples comparisons.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a careful variance control via Brownian coupling is, to my knowledge, novel in the ML literature. The theoretical development (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE) for the stochastic-gradient setting goes beyond prior RR applications to exact-gradient SDEs.

Significance:
- The approach is simple, generic, and practically attractive: two first-order chains can match the accuracy order of higher-order integrators, with minimal code changes and natural parallelism. This could impact practitioners who currently favor SGLD for its simplicity.
- The empirical scope is good but could be strengthened by broader benchmarks (e.g., logistic regression/posterior with non-Gaussian likelihoods; multimodal posteriors) and by direct comparisons to higher-order SG-Langevin-type integrators from [Chen et al., 2015] in the overdamped setting (the SGHMC-s comparison is helpful but does not fully close this gap for SGLD).

Specific suggestions/questions:
- Unify the step-size schedule presentation; explicitly state conditions for constant vs decreasing steps in each theorem. Resolve the noted inconsistencies (Algorithm 1 vs Eq. (5)).
- Provide an ablation on the Brownian coupling (Σ = 0 vs Σ = I) to empirically validate the variance reduction predicted by the CLT.
- Explore coupling of stochastic gradients (e.g., sharing mini-batches across chains/half-steps) and its effect on variance and bias in practice.
- Report results as a function of gradient evaluations in addition to wall-clock, and discuss parallel resource assumptions.
- Add baselines using higher-order integrators for the overdamped case (e.g., the high-order schemes of [Chen et al., 2015]) to substantiate the claim of “achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators” for SGRRLD.
- Test sensitivity to mini-batch size B and to step-size hyperparameters on non-convex models.

Pros:
- Simple, broadly applicable idea; easy to parallelize.
- Strong theoretical guarantees (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bounds) with improved rates over SGLD.
- Clear guidance on optimal coupling and step-size schedules.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization; extension to SGHMC.

Cons:
- Clarity issues and internal inconsistencies (step size, Algorithm 1 vs Eq. (5)).
- Limited experimental comparison to higher-order overdamped integrators.
- Evaluation scope could be broader; fairness w.r.t. compute budget needs clearer reporting.
- Assumptions (high-order smoothness/Poisson equation) may be restrictive in some ML models.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is novel and well motivated, with solid analysis and promising empirical evidence. Addressing the noted inconsistencies and expanding comparisons would strengthen the paper further.
