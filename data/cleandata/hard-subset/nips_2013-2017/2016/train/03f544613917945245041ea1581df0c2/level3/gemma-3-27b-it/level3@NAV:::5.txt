## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces SG-RR-MCMC, a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm aiming to reduce bias by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD. The core idea—running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining the results—is conceptually simple and elegantly addresses a key limitation of SG-MCMC: its inherent bias. This work builds upon the established foundation of SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011] and recent advances in understanding the convergence properties of SG-MCMC [Chen et al., 2015, Teh et al., 2016], offering a practical approach to improve accuracy without significantly increasing computational cost due to the embarrassingly parallel nature of the implementation.

The strengths of this paper lie in its solid theoretical analysis, demonstrating the asymptotic consistency of SG-RR-MCMC and providing non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. The empirical validation, performed on both synthetic data and a real-world matrix factorization problem, supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors correctly identify the parallelizability as a key advantage, aligning with the growing need for scalable inference methods. The connection to higher-order integrators is also a notable contribution, suggesting a way to achieve comparable accuracy with simpler building blocks.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the efficiency gains are asserted but not rigorously proven via convergence rate analysis. Establishing an improved convergence rate beyond the theoretical accuracy comparison to higher-order integrators would significantly strengthen the contribution. The authors acknowledge this gap, which is commendable. Furthermore, the paper lacks a comparative analysis with other bias reduction techniques or alternative high-order integrators. A discussion of the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy compared to methods like SGHMC with higher-order updates would be valuable. A concerning issue is the inconsistency regarding the step size in the paper and the discrepancy between Algorithm 1 in the supplement and Equation (5), which needs to be addressed for clarity and correctness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and conceptually simple approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees of consistency and bounds on error.
*   Empirical results demonstrate effectiveness on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Potential for significant speedups due to parallel implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a rigorous convergence rate analysis.
*   Missing comparison with alternative bias reduction techniques/higher-order integrators.
*   Inconsistencies in the manuscript (step size discussion & Algorithm 1).



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. While the identified weaknesses warrant attention, the strengths of the work, particularly the theoretical foundation and empirical support, suggest that the paper is a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program, contingent on the authors addressing the inconsistencies and perhaps adding a brief discussion about relative performance against alternatives. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
