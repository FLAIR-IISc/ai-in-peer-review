

# Review: Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

This paper proposes a novel extension to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The authors apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, running two chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining them to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. This builds on the foundational work of Welling and Teh (2011) on SGLD and recent theoretical advances by Chen et al. (2015) on SG-MCMC convergence rates.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling theoretical contribution by showing that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K^(-4/5)), significantly better than SGLD's O(K^(-2/3)). The method is remarkably simple to implement, requiring only minor modifications to existing SG-MCMC code (essentially running two chains in parallel with different step sizes). The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs of asymptotic consistency, central limit theorems, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The experimental results on synthetic and real data support the theoretical claims, demonstrating SGRRLD's improved performance over SGLD and SGHMC.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Theoretical Derivation Discrepancy:** The paper claims that SGRRLD achieves MSE rate O(K^(-4/5)), but this conflicts with the actual derivation in Theorem 2. The theorem states that for γ_k = γ_1 k^(-α), the MSE bound is O(K^(-(4α)∧(1-α))), which is minimized at α=1/5, yielding O(K^(-4/5)). However, Theorem 1 states the rate is O(K^(-(1-α)/2 ∧ 2α)), and for α=1/5, this would be O(K^(-2/5)), not O(K^(-4/5)). The paper should clarify this apparent contradiction between Theorem 1 and Theorem 2.

2. **Unfair Computational Comparison:** The paper's Figure 5 compares SGRRLD against SGLD without accounting for the 3x computational cost of SGRRLD (two chains, one with twice as many steps). On a single core, SGRRLD should cost ~3x SGLD time, so SGLD's timing should be scaled by 2/3 to make a fair comparison. This would significantly reduce the reported performance gap, making the advantage of SGRRLD less dramatic.

3. **Figure 3 Discrepancy:** Figure 3 shows the best bias performance at α=0.2 (red curve), but the paper claims the optimal α is 1/3. The authors should explain this discrepancy and clarify why the empirical results don't match the theoretical prediction.

4. **Small Step Size Behavior:** The paper claims for SGLD that bias is O(γ + 1/(Kγ)), but line 256 states that for small γ, the 1/(Kγ) term should dominate. The paper should clarify why this term doesn't dominate in practice for SGLD.

5. **Minor Issues:** The paper contains several minor but important issues: Equation 1 uses ϑt instead of θ; "RR" is not defined on first use; Γ_K is undefined; "smooth function f" requires precise smoothness assumptions; Figure 5 has a labeling error (Bias vs. time vs. dimension); and vectors should be bold.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound approach to improving SG-MCMC methods with minimal implementation complexity. The core idea is elegant and the theoretical results are well-supported. However, the computational comparison in Figure 5 is misleading due to the unaccounted 3x cost of SGRRLD, and the discrepancy between the claimed optimal α (1/3) and the empirical results (α=0.2) in Figure 3 requires clarification.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, and the method is both theoretically interesting and practically implementable. The minor issues noted above are easily fixable in a revised version. The main concern is the unaddressed discrepancy between theoretical predictions and empirical results regarding the optimal α value, which needs to be resolved to fully validate the theoretical claims.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper has strong theoretical contributions and practical relevance, but the authors should address the computational cost in comparisons, clarify the α discrepancy, and fix the minor issues noted above. The core idea is novel and well-executed, making this an important contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
