- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two parallel chains (step sizes γ and γ/2, second chain twice as many steps) and combining outputs to cancel leading-order bias.  
- Uses correlated Gaussian noise between chains to control the variance of the extrapolated estimator.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Shows improved convergence rates (e.g., MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)), matching higher-order integrator performance.  
- Empirical validation on a synthetic Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrates improved accuracy and efficiency over SGLD.  
- Strength: paper is technically sound, clearly written, and links CLT to non-asymptotic bounds convincingly.  
- Strength: significance in addressing integrator-induced bias and offering a simple way to achieve higher-order accuracy without complex integrators.  
- Weakness: originality is incremental—builds on existing higher-order integrator work and RR analysis for SDEs, with novelty in synthesizing these for stochastic gradients.  
- Weakness: practicality concerns—perfectly correlated noise requires per-iteration communication/synchronization between chains, potentially costly in distributed settings.  
- Weakness: experimental fairness unclear—uncertain whether SGRRLD used two cores while SGLD used one, which would bias wall-clock comparisons.  
- Weakness: missing natural baseline—no comparison to two independent parallel SGLD chains averaged, which is communication-free and should be tested.  
- Request: clarify wall-clock experiment setup (CPU/core allocation for each method).  
- Request: discuss communication overhead of correlated noise, when it’s negligible (shared-memory multicore) versus prohibitive (distributed clusters).  
- Minor: minor redundancy in defining ULA twice in the paper.  
- Overall: solid theoretical and empirical work, but practical utility and experimental fairness need addressing, especially comparison to independent parallel chains.
