This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC method aimed at reducing bias compared to standard SGLD. The core idea leverages Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established numerical analysis technique, to improve convergence rates. The authors demonstrate theoretical guarantees for reduced bias and MSE, supported by experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (matrix factorization).

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in SG-MCMC, notably Welling & Teh (2011) introducing SGLD, and subsequent extensions striving for improved convergence and efficiency (e.g., Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2015). Recent work (Chen et al., 2015) explored higher-order integrators for bias reduction, a direction SGRRLD complements by offering a first-order approach achieving comparable accuracy. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution, as it hasn't been widely adopted in the machine learning community despite its prevalence in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990).

**Strengths:**

* **Simplicity and Implementability:** SGRRLD's elegance lies in its simplicity. It requires minimal modification to existing SGLD implementations, primarily running two chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlating injected noise. This ease of implementation makes it accessible to practitioners.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, proving reduced bias (O(γ^2)) and a corresponding reduction in MSE. The central limit theorem presented in Theorem 1 is a particularly valuable result.
* **Generality and Potential Impact:** The method appears broadly applicable across a variety of SG-MCMC algorithms, suggesting potentially high impact. The demonstrated success with both SGLD and SGHMC underlines this generality.
* **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting is a novel contribution that bridges concepts from numerical analysis and Bayesian inference.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear, even when delving into technical details.

**Weaknesses & Questions:**

* **Applicability Condition:** The paper mentions a condition that SG-MCMC algorithms must satisfy (π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2)) for the method to be applicable. It’s unclear if this limitation is significant in practice. A more detailed discussion of algorithms that *don’t* satisfy this condition, and the potential for adaptation, would be beneficial.
* **Extension to Other Schemes:**  Can this technique be effectively applied to other approximate MCMC schemes beyond SGLD and SGHMC, such as subsampling Metropolis-Hastings or Distributed SGLD?  Addressing this would broaden the perceived impact of the work.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Running one chain twice as long as the other leads to wasted compute.  The authors mention parallelization, but an investigation of running *three* chains (one with γ, two with γ/2, all for the same number of steps) to eliminate idle time, and a comparison of the resulting variance, would be valuable.
* **Extrapolation Beyond Two Step Sizes:** Is there a straightforward extension of this approach to more than two step sizes? A recursive application of RR extrapolation (mentioned briefly) deserves further exploration and analysis.
* **Noise/Minibatch Correlation:** The requirement for correlating injected noise is mentioned. Further clarification is needed regarding the optimal correlation strategy.  Is using the same minibatch optimal, or is there negligible difference with a slightly different correlated minibatch?  The impact of non-identical mini-batch correlations on the bias reduction should be examined.
* **Experimental Design:** The matrix factorization experiment should be run until convergence to better demonstrate the benefits. Including a standard SGD baseline would provide a stronger comparison and contextualize the final errors, clearly demonstrating the Bayesian advantages.
* **Algorithm Pseudocode:** Including an algorithm pseudocode box early in the paper would significantly enhance accessibility for practitioners who want to implement the method without first navigating the theoretical details.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* Clear presentation and strong experimental validation.
* Potential for broad applicability and significant impact on the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of exploration of computational efficiency improvements (e.g., three-chain approach).
* Limited discussion of the applicability condition and potential extensions.
* Experimental design could be strengthened with a convergence analysis and comparison to a standard SGD baseline.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are encouraging. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding computational efficiency and the applicability condition, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
