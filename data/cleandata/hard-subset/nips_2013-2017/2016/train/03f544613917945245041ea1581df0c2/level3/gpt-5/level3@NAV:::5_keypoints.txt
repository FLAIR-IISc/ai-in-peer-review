- Paper proposes SG-RR-MCMC / SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two SGLD chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) in parallel and combining averages to cancel first-order bias.  
- Method controls variance via correlated Brownian increments and requires no communication between chains.  
- Theoretical contributions: proofs of asymptotic consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds showing improved rates (e.g., O(K^−4/5) vs O(K^−2/3)).  
- Empirical results: SGRRLD improves over SGLD on Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization at matched wall-clock time.  
- Related work: builds on SG-MCMC literature (Welling & Teh; Chen et al.; Ma et al.) and RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès).  
- Quality: theoretical development is sound and supported by finite-time bounds and experiments.  
- Major clarity/consistency concerns: inconsistent statements about the step-size schedule and mismatch between Algorithm 1 in supplement and Equation (5) (timing/number of γ/2 steps).  
- Requested experiments/ablation: report sensitivity to Brownian correlation and mini-batch coupling choices.  
- Clarity suggestion: move key assumptions and the Σ = I optimality statement from the supplement into the main text for readability and reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel and simple combination of RR extrapolation with SG-MCMC; careful adaptation for variance control and parallelization is new.  
- Significance: practical, parallel-friendly approach with potential impact; should compare against high-order SG-MCMC integrators to isolate RR benefits.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple/general idea, clear theoretical guarantees, improved finite-time rates, easy parallel implementation, solid empirical gains.  
- Cons against acceptance: step-size and pseudocode inconsistencies, missing head-to-head comparisons with high-order SGLD variants, limited ablations on correlation and mini-batch coupling.  
- Overall: in scope and constructive for NIPS if inconsistencies are resolved and comparisons/ablations are broadened.
