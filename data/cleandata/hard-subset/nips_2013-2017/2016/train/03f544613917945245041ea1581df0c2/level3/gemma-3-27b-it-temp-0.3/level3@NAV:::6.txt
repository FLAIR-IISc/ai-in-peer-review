## Review of “Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo”

This paper proposes a novel acceleration technique, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), for Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to leverage Richardson-Romberg extrapolation – running two parallel chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise – to reduce bias and improve convergence rates. The authors provide a strong theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE. Empirical validation on synthetic data and a real-world matrix factorization task supports the theoretical findings, showing practical gains over standard SGLD.

The paper is well-written and clearly situates itself within the existing SG-MCMC literature, appropriately referencing relevant work like [10] on higher-order integrators and [23] on RR extrapolation for invariant distributions. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the experimental setup appears reasonable, though further clarification is needed (see below). The authors convincingly demonstrate that SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only a first-order scheme. 

However, the novelty feels somewhat incremental. While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a logical extension, the paper doesn't fully articulate how it significantly advances beyond prior work that already explored numerical integrator analysis in this context [10] and the use of RR for invariant distributions [23]. A more detailed discussion of the differences and advantages over these existing approaches would strengthen the contribution.

A significant concern is the practical applicability and communication cost. The method inherently requires per-step communication between the two chains to maintain correlation. The paper doesn't address this overhead, and the experiments don't account for it. It’s unclear whether the observed gains would hold in distributed computing environments where communication is expensive. A comparison to two *independent* SGLD chains (embarrassingly parallel) would provide a useful baseline to assess the benefit of the correlated chains. 

Regarding the experiments, clarification is needed on resource allocation. Was SGLD given the same compute resources as the two-chain SGRRLD? How were resources split between the chains, and how does this impact wall-clock time given the gradient computation cost?

Finally, there's a minor stylistic issue: the phrase “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is duplicated on lines 25 and 70.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees.
*   Clear presentation and well-motivated approach.
*   Empirical results demonstrate performance improvements over SGLD.
*   Potential for accelerating SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty given related prior work.
*   Lack of consideration for communication costs in parallel implementation.
*   Unclear resource allocation in experiments.



Overall, this is a solid paper with valuable theoretical and empirical contributions. Addressing the concerns regarding novelty, communication costs, and experimental details would significantly strengthen the submission.
