Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC estimator that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias and improve mean-squared error (MSE) rates while keeping variance controlled via correlated Brownian increments. Concretely, the method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with stepsizes γ and γ/2 (the latter taking two steps per “outer” iteration), and combines their time-averaged estimates as in classic RR to cancel first-order discretization bias. The authors establish consistency, a CLT with optimal variance attained by fully correlated Gaussian noise across chains, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. They show that, for power-law stepsizes γk ∝ k−α, SGRRLD yields an asymptotic rate O(K−2/5) at α=1/5, improving over SGLD’s O(K−1/3). For fixed stepsizes, the finite-K optimal choices minimize the bounds to yield bias O(K−2/3) and MSE O(K−4/5), again improving over SGLD (O(K−1/2) and O(K−2/3), respectively). Experiments on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization support the theory. The paper relates to SGLD and its generalizations [1, 5–12, 18–20], higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC [10], and RR/MLMC for SDEs [13, 21–23, 27]. To my knowledge, applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, together with optimal Brownian coupling, is novel.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT and finite-time bias/MSE bounds are valuable. The variance minimization via Brownian coupling (Σ = Id) is well-motivated and consistent with [23]. However, the derivation of the rates from Theorem 1 is only sketched; the main text should clearly show how Γ(3)K/√ΓK behaves for γk ∝ k−α, and how this yields the min((1−α)/2, 2α) tradeoff and the α=1/5 optimum. Similarly, for Theorem 2, please show the steps converting the bounds into the reported γ and K scalings.
- Assumptions: The smoothness/regularity requirements on U, f, and the gradient noise law L, and the precise Poisson equation conditions, should be stated in the main text (not only in the supplement). The role of coupling of stochastic gradients across chains is downplayed; while the CLT says Gaussian noise dominates, in practice coupling minibatches can reduce variance further—this deserves discussion or an ablation.
- Experiments: The synthetic experiments are informative. The matrix factorization results are promising, but the comparison is not compute-normalized (see fairness concern below).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but several notational and presentation issues hinder readability:
- Eq. (1) uses ϑ while the rest uses θ; unify notation.
- “RR” is used before being defined (line 94).
- ΓK is used before definition (line 131); please define Γ(n)K upfront.
- “smooth function f” (line 160) needs precise assumptions (e.g., f ∈ Cb,p or polynomial growth, existence of a C9 solution g to the Poisson equation).
- Figure 3 labeling: the caption and axes appear to mix “bias vs time vs dimension”; please fix.
- Boldface vectors (a, θ, etc.) would improve clarity. Include a minimal pseudocode in the main text.

Originality
- The use of RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC with explicit variance control via correlated Brownian increments is, to my knowledge, new. It offers a simple, generic alternative to crafting higher-order integrators [10].

Significance
- If broadly applicable across SG-MCMC variants (as suggested and partially shown with SGHMC), this is a simple technique practitioners can adopt to get better bias/MSE without implementing sophisticated integrators. The theoretical guarantees and empirical gains suggest impact.

Major concerns and questions
1) Deriving the rates from Theorem 1: Please provide the explicit derivation in the main text. For γk = γ1 k−α,
- ΓK ≍ K1−α, Γ(3)K ≍ K1−3α, hence Γ(3)K/√ΓK ≍ K1/2−(5/2)α, which tends to 0 if α>1/5, is constant if α=1/5, and diverges if α<1/5. Then the error scales like max{Γ(3)K/ΓK, 1/√ΓK} ≍ K−min{2α, (1−α)/2}, optimized at α=1/5. Please show these steps and constants.
2) Figure 3 inconsistency: The text claims the bias is optimized at α=1/3 per Theorem 2, but the plotted “bias” curve seems best at α=0.2 (red). Is the panel actually MSE, or are constants/finite-K effects dominant? The “optimal” curve fluctuates substantially; please increase repeats, report confidence bands, and reconcile with theory (bias vs MSE panels and α choices).
3) Small-γ regime: On line 256, you correctly say that for SGRRLD the 1/(Kγ) term dominates when γ is very small at fixed K. The same should hold for SGLD (per [20] and your Section 2.1). Why is this not observed or discussed for SGLD in Fig. 2? Please clarify.
4) Fairness of matrix factorization comparisons: Each SGRRLD “outer” step does 3 gradient evaluations (one coarse, two fine). Although you match wall-clock time by running the two chains in parallel, the total gradient work is ≈1.5× of SGLD in your setup (K=5250 vs 10500). Also, one could run two independent SGLD chains on two cores and average to reduce variance. Please include:
- Results normalized by the number of stochastic gradient evaluations (or total FLOPs).
- Single-core CPU-time plots.
- A baseline with two parallel SGLD chains averaged, to assess the benefit of RR extrapolation beyond parallelization.

Additional suggestions
- Provide an ablation on Brownian coupling: Σ=Id (fully correlated) versus Σ=0 (independent) to show the variance impact empirically.
- Discuss minibatch coupling across chains (e.g., using the same minibatch indices at matched time) as a practical variance reducer; report its effect.
- Sensitivity to minibatch size B and to the stepsize schedules in real data.
- Briefly compare to higher-order SGHMC-s [10] in compute-normalized terms.

Pros
- Simple, generic method; easy to implement.
- Solid theory (consistency, CLT with optimal coupling, finite-time bounds).
- Clear improvement in asymptotic and finite-time rates over first-order SG-MCMC.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale tasks.

Cons
- Compute fairness issues in large-scale experiments; missing compute-normalized baselines.
- Key rate derivations not shown in the main text; some assumptions unclear.
- Figure/label inconsistencies; small notational issues.
- Limited ablations on coupling and minibatch choices.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the rate derivations, fixing experimental fairness, and addressing the noted inconsistencies. The idea is novel and potentially impactful, with theory that appears sound and broadly useful.
