

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the SGLD framework (Welling & Teh, 2011) and recent convergence rate analysis (Chen et al., 2015) to show that running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and applying RR extrapolation can achieve superior convergence rates. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD attains MSE convergence rate O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), with asymptotic bias and variance bounded as O(γ^2) and O(γ^4), respectively. The method is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms and maintains parallelizability.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of asymptotic consistency, central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The proposed method requires minimal implementation changes to existing SG-MCMC codes, making it practically accessible. The experimental validation on both synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks provides convincing evidence supporting the theoretical claims, with SGRRLD consistently outperforming SGLD in accuracy for the same computation time.

However, several concerns need addressing. The primary issue is the experimental comparison in Figure 5, which reports wall-clock time comparisons without accounting for the inherent parallelism of SGRRLD. Since SGRRLD runs two chains (one with twice as many steps), it should be compared at 2/3 the wall-clock time of SGLD to be fair, not 1:1. This means the reported performance gains are overstated by approximately 50%. The authors should recalculate the efficiency gains using proper time scaling.

A second concern arises from Figure 3, which shows the bias as a function of step size. The text claims the optimal α=1/3 for bias, but the plot indicates the best performance occurs at α≈0.2. The authors need to explain this discrepancy and clarify whether the theoretical analysis or empirical results are incorrect. The substantial fluctuations in the "optimal" curve further undermine confidence in the empirical rate.

The paper also fails to address why SGLD doesn't exhibit the expected increase in bias for very small step sizes (where 1/(Kγ) should dominate). This contradicts the theoretical bound in Theorem 2 and requires explanation.

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (θ vs. ϑ in Eq. 1), undefined acronyms (RR in line 94), undefined terms (Γ_K in line 131), imprecise smoothness assumptions for f in line 160, and inconsistent vector notation. Figure 2's labeling should be corrected from "Bias vs. time vs. dimension" to "Bias vs. step size."

The theoretical contribution is strong, and the method is well-motivated. The paper advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods, while maintaining parallelizability. However, the experimental evaluation needs to be revised to account for the computational cost of the two parallel chains.

The paper meets NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound with strong theoretical analysis (Quality), clearly written with good organization (Clarity), and presents a novel combination of RR extrapolation with SG-MCMC (Originality). Its significance is demonstrated by the improved convergence rates and empirical performance, though the experimental comparison needs adjustment.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, practical implementation simplicity, significant improvement in convergence rates, general applicability, and supporting experiments.

Con arguments for rejection: Unfair experimental comparison due to improper time scaling, discrepancy between theoretical claims and empirical results in Figure 3, and failure to address the small step size behavior.

With revisions addressing the experimental comparison and the theoretical-empirical discrepancy, this would be a strong NIPS contribution. As currently written, the experimental results overstate the method's advantages, which is a significant concern for a paper claiming improved efficiency. The authors should re-run experiments with proper time scaling and provide a thorough explanation for the Figure 3 discrepancy.
