### Summary

This paper proposes a new algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to reduce the discretization bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two SGLD chains in parallel: one with a step size sequence (γk) and another with a step size sequence (γk/2), where the second chain performs two steps for each single step of the first. To control the variance of the combined estimator, the Gaussian noise injected at each step is correlated across the two chains. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and mean squared error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K^-4/5) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K^-2/3)). This rate matches that of methods using more complex higher-order integrators. The theoretical findings are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task, where SGRRLD shows improved accuracy and faster convergence in wall-clock time compared to SGLD. This work builds upon the extensive literature of SG-MCMC [1, 6] by adapting classical numerical methods for SDEs [21, 23] to the stochastic gradient setting, offering an alternative to the explicit higher-order integrators previously explored in [10].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is the main strength, providing a rigorous justification for the proposed method. The proofs for the central limit theorem and the non-asymptotic bounds are detailed and appear sound, clearly establishing the superior convergence rates of SGRRLD. The experiments on the synthetic linear Gaussian model are well-designed and provide clear, compelling evidence that corroborates the theoretical claims regarding the behavior of bias and MSE with respect to the step size and iteration count.

However, the evaluation of the method's practical utility has a significant weakness. The paper claims SGRRLD is "well adapted to parallel and distributed architectures," but overlooks the crucial issue of communication overhead. The RR extrapolation requires correlating the noise terms between the two chains at every step (Eq. 7), which necessitates communication and synchronization. This cost is not discussed or measured, potentially making the wall-clock time comparisons in the matrix factorization experiment misleading, especially in a truly distributed (multi-node) environment where communication is a primary bottleneck.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed algorithm is developed logically from established concepts in SGLD and RR extrapolation. The connection to prior work is also clearly explained.
A minor point for correction: The acronym "ULA" for Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm is defined twice, once in the introduction (line 25) and again in Section 2.1 (line 70).

**Originality:** The originality of the paper is somewhat limited. While the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, the core components are not. The challenge of discretization bias in SG-MCMC and the use of higher-order integrators to address it were previously studied by Chen et al. [10]. Furthermore, the use of RR extrapolation for approximating the invariant measure of SDEs with exact gradients is a known technique, analyzed in detail by Lemaire et al. [23]. The main contribution of this paper is to bridge these two areas by demonstrating that the RR technique can be successfully applied in the stochastic gradient setting and by providing the corresponding convergence analysis. This is a valuable and non-trivial connection, but it represents a novel combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem in Bayesian machine learning: improving the accuracy of scalable MCMC methods. The proposed method is elegant and significant in that it provides a "black-box" approach to achieving the benefits of higher-order integration without requiring the user to derive and implement complex, model-specific integrator formulas. The experimental results on the matrix factorization task suggest that this can lead to tangible improvements in real-world applications.

However, the practical significance is tempered by the unaddressed communication overhead and the fairness of the experimental comparison. If the synchronization cost is high, the method's applicability could be limited to shared-memory, multi-core systems. The failure to compare against simple, communication-free parallel baselines (discussed below) makes it difficult to assess whether the complexity of RR extrapolation is truly warranted over more naive parallelization strategies.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

1.  **Communication Overhead and Practicality:** The assumption of correlated Brownian motion (Eq. 7) is key to controlling the variance but introduces a practical challenge. In a parallel implementation, this requires communication between the two processes/threads at every single iteration to share the random variates. Could the authors please comment on this communication cost? How would this overhead affect the reported wall-clock time improvements, particularly if the chains were run on different nodes in a cluster rather than on different cores of a single CPU?

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compare one SGLD chain to the two parallel chains of SGRRLD, noting that wall-clock times are nearly identical. This comparison effectively grants SGRRLD twice the computational resources (e.g., two CPU cores vs. one). A more informative and fairer baseline would be to compare SGRRLD against two *independent* SGLD chains running in parallel for the same wall-clock time, with their samples pooled for final estimation. This "embarrassingly parallel" baseline uses the same computational resources and is trivial to implement. Such a comparison would isolate the benefit of the RR extrapolation itself from the benefit of simple parallelization.

3.  **Applicability to other SG-MCMC Methods:** The paper demonstrates the RR approach on SGHMC in a synthetic experiment, which is a nice illustration of its generality. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss any potential challenges or assumptions required to apply this technique to other variants, such as those with adaptive preconditioning [11] or thermostats [7].

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper presents a solid, rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates for SG-MCMC.
    *   The proposed method is elegant and provides a practical way to reduce sampler bias without deriving complicated higher-order integrators.
    *   The synthetic experiments provide strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The novelty is incremental, as it combines known techniques from numerical analysis for SDEs and prior work on improving SG-MCMC integrators.
    *   The practical evaluation is weakened by the failure to address the communication overhead inherent in the method and by using an experimental comparison that may not be fair in its allocation of computational resources.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a strong theoretical core. However, its claims of practical utility and superiority need to be better substantiated through a discussion of communication costs and a fairer comparison to simple parallel baselines.
