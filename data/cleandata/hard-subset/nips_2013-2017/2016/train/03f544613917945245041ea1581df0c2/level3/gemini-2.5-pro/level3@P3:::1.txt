### NIPS 2016 Review ###

**Paper:** Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a method to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms, a significant issue that limits their accuracy in large-scale Bayesian inference. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a classical technique from numerical analysis, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The resulting method, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs linearly to cancel the leading-order bias term. This work connects the SG-MCMC literature, prominent in machine learning (e.g., Welling & Teh, 2011), with the extensive literature on numerical methods for Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), where RRE is well-established (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990). The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than vanilla SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). Empirically, the method shows significant improvements on synthetic data and consistent, albeit more modest, gains on large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs (provided in the supplement) that build upon existing work in the SDE literature. The analysis establishes consistency, provides a CLT, and derives non-asymptotic bounds for both bias and MSE, which clearly characterize the conditions under which SGRRLD outperforms SGLD. The experiments are well-designed, covering both a controlled synthetic setting where the posterior is known and a realistic large-scale application. The authors are careful in their evaluation, for instance by running the parallel chains of SGRRLD for half the number of iterations of SGLD to ensure a fair comparison of wall-clock time.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the background on SGLD and RRE is presented concisely. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described formally and its implementation is straightforward to understand. The connection between the two parallel chains, particularly the use of correlated noise to control variance, is explained well. The results of the theoretical analysis are stated clearly in the main text, with the technical details appropriately deferred to the supplement.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is the novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework. While RRE is a standard tool in numerical analysis for SDEs, its application to stochastic gradient MCMC methods for large-scale Bayesian inference is new. This work successfully builds a bridge between two communities—numerical analysis for SDEs and machine learning—which could inspire further cross-pollination of ideas. The proposed approach offers an interesting alternative to using higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) for bias reduction, achieving similar theoretical accuracy with a simpler, first-order scheme.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be useful for practitioners. SGRRLD provides a simple, "black-box" way to improve the accuracy of SGLD. The method's main appeal is its practicality: it is easy to implement and is inherently parallelizable, making the doubled computational cost manageable on modern hardware. While not a transformative breakthrough, it represents a solid and useful advancement in the SG-MCMC toolkit. The improvements on the large-scale matrix factorization task, while not as dramatic as on the toy problem, demonstrate practical value by achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the time. This efficiency gain is a meaningful contribution for large-scale applications.

**Detailed Comments:**
The paper is strong overall, but two minor points could be addressed to further improve its clarity:

1.  **Figure 1a:** This figure is slightly misleading. The plot shows a Gaussian density parameterized by the empirical mean and variance of the SGLD/SGRRLD samples, rather than a direct visualization of the empirical posterior (e.g., a kernel density estimate). By assuming a Gaussian form, the plot implicitly smooths the sample distribution and may overstate the quality of the posterior approximation. A clarification in the text or caption stating that this is a Gaussian fit to the samples would be more precise.

2.  **Figure 2a:** The plots of bias and MSE versus step size γ show that for very small γ, the error begins to increase. This is counter-intuitive, as one typically expects discretization error to decrease with the step size. The paper correctly notes this is due to the `1/(Kγ)` term in the error bounds dominating when K is fixed. However, a more explicit discussion of this trade-off in the main text would be beneficial. Explaining that for a fixed number of iterations K, a very small step size γ means the chain explores only a very small time horizon, leading to high variance in the Monte Carlo estimate, would help readers fully grasp the behavior shown in the figure.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and practical application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC methods.
*   Provides a strong and complete theoretical analysis, including a CLT and improved non-asymptotic convergence rates.
*   The paper is very clearly written, technically sound, and well-motivated.
*   The proposed method is simple to implement and parallelizable, making it useful in practice.
*   Effectively bridges the literature on numerical SDEs and large-scale MCMC, which is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The contribution could be viewed as an incremental improvement rather than a fundamentally new approach.
*   The empirical gains on large-scale problems, while consistent, are modest compared to the near-doubling of computational cost (though this is mitigated by parallelization).

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. It addresses an important problem with an elegant and well-analyzed solution. The work is a solid contribution to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference.
