- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining their outputs to cancel O(γ) bias.  
- Fine chain advances twice as often so both chains reach same time horizon; estimator is a weighted combination giving O(γ^2) bias.  
- Controls variance via correlated Gaussian noise Zγ,k = (Zγ/2,2k−1 + Zγ/2,2k)/√2 and shows this coupling is optimal in a broad family.  
- Proves consistency, a CLT identifying optimal coupling, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE.  
- With constant step size: bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)).  
- With decreasing steps γk ∝ k−α: optimal MSE achieved at α = 1/5 giving rate O(K−4/5) (improves over O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC).  
- Empirically reduces bias/MSE on a Gaussian model and matches or outperforms SGHMC and higher-order splitting variants when RR is applied.  
- On MovieLens matrix factorization, SGRRLD attains better RMSE than SGLD under equal wall-clock time by parallelizing the two chains.  
- Situates work with Talay–Tubaro RR and weak-error expansions, and complements analyses of high-order SG-MCMC integrators.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically solid; assumptions (smooth Poisson solution, polynomial growth, ergodicity, weak-error expansion) are standard but relatively strong for complex posteriors.  
- Paper is honest about technical conditions and defers detailed proofs to the supplement.  
- One explicit requirement: both chains must use gradient estimators with the same law (e.g., identical mini-batch size) or RR bias cancellation can fail.  
- Writing and structure are clear; estimator, correlation scheme, and rates are well explained.  
- Recommends adding a self-contained algorithm box (pseudocode) early in the main text for accessibility and reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel in ML to adapt RR to noisy-gradient SG-MCMC and prove finite-time bounds and CLTs for correlated increments.  
- Significance: simple, broadly applicable technique that parallelizes naturally and can be layered onto many SG-MCMC variants.  
- Pros: minimal code changes, practical guidance on noise coupling, strong theoretical guarantees, and demonstrated empirical benefits.  
- Cons/questions — applicability: clarify which SG-MCMC variants satisfy the required O(γ) weak-error expansion and provide concrete sufficient conditions beyond “mild assumptions.”  
- Cons/questions — gradient-noise correlation: provide guidance on correlating mini-batches across chains or between fine steps (sharing indices)—beneficial, neutral, or harmful?  
- Cons/questions — computational efficiency: quantify total gradient evaluations and trade-offs when hardware is limited; discuss parallel vs serial setups and whether running three chains changes variance.  
- Cons/questions — extensions: discuss recursive RR or multilevel ideas and provide stability/variance insights or a brief three-level experiment.  
- Cons/questions — experimental scope: add baselines (SGD/ALS), compare to known higher-order integrators for SGLD, and include diagnostics (ESS/time, posterior-moment bias, step-size sensitivity).  
- Practical details to add: state that both chains must share mini-batch-size distribution, clarify reuse of data across fine steps, and give guidance on choosing Σ (note Σ=I used in experiments).  
- Suggestions: include main-text pseudocode, state precise conditions ensuring the O(γ) weak expansion and failure cases (heavy tails, non-smooth posteriors), discuss applicability to subsampling MH and distributed/asynchronous SGLD, and give practical step-size recipes (γ ∝ K−1/5 for MSE, γ ∝ K−1/3 for bias).  
- Overall assessment: strong, elegant, and empirically promising paper that would benefit from clarifications on applicability, data/gradient coupling, and compute trade-offs.
