This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce the discretization bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The proposed method involves running two SGLD chains in parallel: one with step size γ and another with two steps of size γ/2, using correlated noise. The outputs are then combined to form a new estimator with lower bias. While RR extrapolation is a well-known technique in numerical analysis for solving SDEs (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), its application and rigorous analysis within the SG-MCMC framework, which includes noise from stochastic gradients, is novel. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves a faster rate of convergence for the mean squared error (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)).

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis provides important convergence guarantees, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE. Establishing a provably higher-order convergence rate for an SG-MCMC method using only a first-order integrator is a strong technical contribution.

The synthetic experiments on a linear Gaussian model convincingly support the theoretical claims. The figures clearly illustrate the reduction in bias and MSE compared to vanilla SGLD and verify the derived convergence rates, providing strong empirical evidence for the method's correctness.

However, the experimental validation on real-world data is less compelling. The reported RMSE improvements on the MovieLens datasets are marginal, which raises questions about the practical utility of the proposed method in complex, high-dimensional settings. The paper would be stronger if the authors could clarify the technical conditions required for Theorems 1 & 2 to hold in the main text. For instance, do these theorems require strong convexity of the potential U, or can they handle multimodal posteriors, which are common in practice? While I have not checked the proofs in detail, the overall theoretical framework appears robust.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and organized. The SGRRLD algorithm is described clearly and seems straightforward to implement. The motivation for using RR extrapolation to tackle the bias of SGLD is well-explained and grounded in existing literature. Deferring the full technical assumptions to the supplement is understandable for space reasons, but a brief, high-level summary of these conditions (e.g., smoothness and growth conditions on U) in the main text would improve the paper's self-containedness and clarity.

### Originality

The primary contribution is the novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting. While RR is a classic technique, adapting it to handle the additional noise from stochastic gradients and providing the corresponding convergence proofs is a valuable and non-trivial contribution. The paper clearly situates this work with respect to prior literature on SDEs and SG-MCMC, properly acknowledging the sources of the core ideas while highlighting its own unique contribution to the machine learning community.

### Significance

Theoretically, the paper presents a significant result. Demonstrating a method that can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators (as in Chen et al., 2015) using only a simple first-order integrator is an important advance for the field of SG-MCMC. This could inspire further work on bias-reduction techniques for scalable Bayesian inference.

However, the practical significance is not yet well-established. The experiments on large-scale matrix factorization show only a slight performance gain, which may not be sufficient to justify the added implementation complexity and computational overhead (even if parallelized). To strengthen the claims of practical relevance, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive real-data experiments on a more diverse set of problems.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novel Contribution:** The paper successfully adapts a classic numerical method (RR extrapolation) to the SG-MCMC framework, which is a novel and elegant idea.
2.  **Strong Theory:** The work is supported by a solid theoretical analysis, providing a CLT and proving faster non-asymptotic convergence rates for bias and MSE compared to SGLD.
3.  **Clear Validation on Synthetic Data:** The theoretical claims are convincingly validated on a synthetic problem where the ground truth is known.
4.  **Generality:** The approach is general and, as demonstrated with SGHMC, can likely be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

1.  **Weak Real-Data Results:** The practical benefit of the method is not clearly demonstrated. The improvements on the large-scale MovieLens task are marginal, making the practical significance questionable.
2.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison:** The claim of improved computational efficiency relies on parallelization. A more rigorous comparison would involve benchmarking SGRRLD (using 2 cores) against two independent SGLD chains (also using 2 cores), which is a common strategy for improving SGLD.
3.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the application is new, the core technique of RR extrapolation is well-established, which slightly tempers the overall novelty.

### Questions and Suggestions for Authors:

1.  **Fairer Computational Comparison:** The claim of being more efficient due to parallelization could be strengthened. SGLD can also be parallelized by running multiple independent chains. Could you compare SGRRLD against two independent SGLD chains whose samples are pooled? It would also be insightful to compare SGRRLD against a single SGLD chain run with the smaller step size (γ/2) but for twice the number of iterations, to ensure a fair comparison of total gradient evaluations.
2.  **Role of Gradient Correlation:** Your analysis rightly highlights that correlating the Brownian motions is crucial for variance reduction. Could you elaborate on the role of the correlation between the stochastic gradient noise terms? If the gradients are estimated from independent mini-batches, the two chains might decorrelate quickly. How does the RR combination still manage to achieve a higher-order error reduction in this scenario? Did you measure the empirical correlation between the states of the two chains in your experiments?
3.  **Stronger Real-Data Experiments:** To substantiate the practical benefit of SGRRLD, I encourage you to test your method on other large-scale Bayesian inference problems where SG-MCMC methods are used, such as topic modeling (LDA) or Bayesian neural networks. This would provide stronger evidence of its general utility.
