Summary and relation to prior work
This paper applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a classic bias-reduction technique from numerical SDEs, to stochastic gradient MCMC in the big-data regime. The main instantiation is Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which combines two SGLD chains run with stepsizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and forms an extrapolated estimator to cancel the leading discretization bias. To control variance, the two chains share correlated Brownian increments (with Σ = I yielding minimal asymptotic variance). The authors derive a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds: for constant stepsize, SGRRLD achieves bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), improving over SGLD’s O(γ + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)). With decreasing stepsizes γk ∝ k−α, the optimal MSE rate is O(K−4/5) at α = 1/5, better than SGLD’s O(K−2/3) at α = 1/3. The paper relates to, and extends, results on weak error expansions and RRE for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup), and to SG-MCMC convergence analyses (Teh–Thiéry–Vollmer; Chen et al. on higher-order integrators). Empirically, the method shows large gains on linear–Gaussian toy problems and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale matrix factorization; an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) exhibits similar behavior and competes with high-order SGHMC.

Strengths
- Clear, solid bridge between SG-MCMC and the SDE/numerical analysis literature; the bias expansion is used constructively via RRE.
- Simple, practically convenient algorithm: two SGLD chains with different stepsizes, correlated noise, identical minibatch law.
- Theoretical guarantees are substantial: asymptotic CLT with optimal correlation choice; non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds with explicit rates; clear identification of when SGRRLD outperforms SGLD.
- Empirical validation aligns with theory: pronounced improvements on toy problems; consistent wall-clock gains on MovieLens datasets when using parallel hardware; extension to SGHMC confirms generality.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical conditions: The superiority relies on (i) perfectly correlated Brownian increments and (ii) the two gradient estimators having the same distribution (e.g., equal minibatch sizes). The paper notes this, but a more explicit discussion of the consequences when these conditions are only approximately met (e.g., asynchronous parallelism, different batch sampling) would be useful.
- Computational budget: The parallel implementation makes wall-clock comparisons favorable, but not all users can run two chains concurrently. A cost–benefit comparison at fixed total gradient evaluations per second (single-core scenario) would help assess practical tradeoffs.
- Scope of experiments: Results beyond linear–Gaussian and matrix factorization (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) would strengthen generality claims. Comparisons to other bias-reduction strategies (e.g., control variates, variance-reduced SG-MCMC) could further contextualize gains.
- Tuning guidance: The paper derives optimal α for asymptotic rates, but concrete, practitioner-oriented guidance for finite-budget tuning (including selecting γ and K jointly) would improve usability.
- The method improves bias/MSE of ergodic averages, not mixing per se. Some discussion of how RRE interacts with multimodality or poor mixing would be valuable.

Quality
- Technically sound. The CLT and non-asymptotic analyses are careful, align with prior SDE results, and appropriately handle correlated noise. Assumptions (regularity, ergodicity, Poisson equation solvability) are standard for this line of work, though strong in places.
- Claims are supported by both theory and experiments. The conditions under which SGRRLD can lose its advantage (e.g., very small γ at fixed K; mismatched minibatch distributions) are acknowledged.

Clarity
- Very clear exposition, with a helpful summary of SGLD, RRE, and the proposed estimator. Notation is consistent. Proof details in the supplement seem appropriate.
- Please clarify two figures:
  - Fig. 1a is potentially misleading: the curve labeled with “empirical posterior” appears to be a Gaussian with the empirical mean/variance plugged in, not the empirical posterior density (e.g., KDE or histogram) of the samples. Please relabel to “Gaussian fit to empirical moments” or plot the empirical density directly.
  - Fig. 2a: The observation that bias does not monotonically decrease with smaller stepsizes is counter-intuitive without context. The explanation (1/(Kγ) term dominating at fixed K) appears in the text; consider moving a concise explanation into the figure caption or main narrative near the figure.
- Minor suggestions: briefly restate in the main text that using identical minibatch laws across chains is necessary to avoid reintroducing O(γ) bias.

Originality
- Novel and neat application of a classical extrapolation idea to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients. While RRE for SDE discretizations is known, its careful adaptation to SG-MCMC with variance control via correlated noise and the resulting rate improvements represent a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- Likely to be adopted: it is easy to implement and parallelize; requires minimal changes to existing SGLD code.
- Advances the state of practice for bias/MSE of SG-MCMC estimators at essentially no algorithmic complexity cost. Not transformative conceptually, but a solid, useful step that could influence how practitioners run SGLD-like methods.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Emphasize that Σ = I (perfect correlation) minimizes the asymptotic variance; discuss practical implementations (e.g., shared RNG streams across processes).
- Expand discussion on robustness when gradients are heavy-tailed or potentials are non-globally Lipschitz; do the bounds still hold under dissipativity conditions?
- Consider adding a single-core comparison at matched total gradient computations to quantify the “no parallelism” scenario.
- Provide code or pseudocode in the main paper (beyond supplement) to aid reproducibility.
- Briefly relate to multi-level Monte Carlo and multi-step RR (as mentioned in the conclusion), including potential computational tradeoffs.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method with strong theory and solid empirical evidence.
- Bridges communities (MCMC and numerical SDEs) in a way that can influence practice.
- Clear writing and careful positioning relative to prior work.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical advantages hinge on parallelism and precise correlation/sharing conditions; guidance for less ideal settings is limited.
- Experimental scope could be broader; comparisons to other bias/variance reduction methods are limited.

Overall, this is a solid, clearly written, and technically sound paper. The method is straightforward and computationally convenient; while not transformative, it is likely to be useful in practice and merits presentation at NIPS.
