This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), a method to reduce the discretization bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithms. The core idea is to apply the classic Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to a base sampler, demonstrated here with Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). This involves running two parallel MCMC chains using different step sizes (e.g., γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. This work connects the well-established SG-MCMC literature with classical numerical methods for SDEs, adapting the RR technique to the stochastic gradient setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality & Significance:** The paper effectively addresses the significant problem of bias in SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical analysis is a key strength, providing proofs of asymptotic consistency and, crucially, demonstrating an improved non-asymptotic MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a clear improvement over SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).
*   **Practicality:** The algorithm's design is highly practical. By running two independent chains, it is "embarrassingly parallel" and requires no communication overhead, making it well-suited for modern multi-core architectures.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experiments on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task are well-designed and compelling. They clearly show that the proposed SGRRLD outperforms standard SGLD, providing strong empirical validation for the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the application is novel and impactful, the method itself is a clever combination of two existing, well-understood techniques. The contribution is more in the successful synthesis and analysis rather than the invention of a fundamentally new concept.
*   **Experimental Gaps:** The authors claim their method achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators. While an experiment with SGHMC provides some support, the paper would be stronger with a direct experimental comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD implemented with a higher-order integrator, which would better contextualize the performance gains.
*   **Clarity and Technical Issues:** The manuscript contains some minor but important errors that affect clarity and reproducibility. There is an inconsistent statement regarding the step size schedule that needs to be clarified. More critically, Algorithm 1 in the supplement appears to be inconsistent with the update rule described in Equation (5), which should be corrected.

In summary, this is a solid and well-executed paper presenting a simple, practical, and effective method for improving SG-MCMC. The strong theoretical foundation and convincing empirical results make it a valuable contribution to the field.
