

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) for large-scale Bayesian inference. The authors build a bridge between MCMC and SDE literatures by showing how RRE can be adapted to SG-MCMC, extending previous work on numerical sequence acceleration for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) to the stochastic gradient setting. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves O(γ²) asymptotic bias and O(γ⁴) variance, with a non-asymptotic MSE bound of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate.

The paper's strengths are clear: it presents technically sound theoretical results with precise convergence bounds, provides thorough empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (including large-scale matrix factorization), and offers a computationally efficient parallelizable implementation. The method is well-motivated as a practical enhancement to existing SG-MCMC approaches, with clear advantages in bias reduction without significant computational overhead.

Minor weaknesses include Figure 1a, which misleadingly presents a Gaussian with empirical mean/variance rather than the empirical posterior, requiring clarification. Additionally, Figure 2a shows counter-intuitive behavior where bias doesn't decrease as stepsize decreases, but lacks explanation of this phenomenon. These are easily addressable in revision.

The paper excels in Quality (rigorous analysis), Clarity (well-organized, readable), and Significance (practical improvements for large-scale Bayesian inference), though Originality is somewhat incremental—it's a clever application of established RRE to SG-MCMC rather than a fundamentally new approach. The method is likely to be useful in practice, particularly for practitioners seeking to reduce bias in SGLD without adopting more complex higher-order integrators.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the practical applicability of SG-MCMC methods, with clear theoretical and empirical support. The minor issues noted are easily fixable, and the paper represents a valuable addition to the field that should interest both practitioners and researchers working at the intersection of MCMC and stochastic optimization.
