## Review of “Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo”

This paper introduces a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) for large-scale Bayesian inference. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-known numerical acceleration technique, to SGLD by running two chains in parallel with different step sizes. This builds a bridge between the MCMC and Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) literature, potentially benefiting both communities. The authors provide theoretical convergence results, demonstrating conditions under which SGRRLD outperforms vanilla SGLD, and support these findings with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making the methodology and theoretical contributions accessible. The connection to existing work in SGLD and numerical methods for SDEs is well-established, and the authors appropriately cite relevant literature. The theoretical analysis appears sound, providing asymptotic and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE of the proposed method. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over SGLD, particularly on the larger datasets, suggesting practical utility. The inclusion of experiments with SGHMC further highlights the generality of the proposed approach.

However, while the method is a solid and straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, it doesn’t represent a radical departure from existing techniques. The improvements, while consistent, are often modest, and the computational overhead of running two chains needs to be carefully considered in practice. The discussion surrounding Figure 2a is insufficient. The counter-intuitive observation that bias may not decrease as stepsize decreases requires a more detailed explanation, potentially relating to the interplay between bias and variance in the estimator. Furthermore, Figure 1a is misleading; it depicts a Gaussian fitted to the empirical mean and variance, rather than the empirical posterior itself, and this should be clarified in the caption. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-supported claims.
*   Clear and accessible presentation.
*   Potential practical benefits for large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   Novel application of a known numerical technique to a relevant problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental improvement over existing methods.
*   Modest gains in performance.
*   Lack of detailed explanation for certain experimental observations (Fig. 2a).
*   Misleading figure (Fig. 1a).



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding Figure 1a and the discussion of Figure 2a. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, offering a practical and theoretically grounded improvement to SGLD, even if it isn’t a transformative breakthrough.
