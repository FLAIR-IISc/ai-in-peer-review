This paper applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to stochastic-gradient MCMC, instantiating it for SGLD to produce SGRRLD. The key idea is to run two SGLD chains with stepsizes γ and γ/2 to cancel first-order weak discretization error, using carefully correlated Gaussian increments to control variance. The authors establish a CLT, non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds, and identify optimal schedules: bias O(γ2), variance O(γ4), and MSE O(K−4/5) vs O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC. Empirically, the method yields large accuracy improvements on Gaussian toy problems and consistent, modest gains on large-scale matrix factorization, with parallelism nearly equalizing wall-clock cost. The work builds a welcome bridge between the SG-MCMC and SDE literatures (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup), and situates itself among prior NIPS work on SGLD and higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2015).

Quality: The analysis appears technically sound and careful. The assumptions (smoothness, Poisson equation solvability, ergodicity) are standard for weak-error expansions. The role of Brownian correlation is well-motivated and linked to minimal asymptotic variance. Experiments support theory; comparisons to high-order integrators on SGHMC are informative.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized; proofs and conditions are deferred but adequately summarized. Minor clarity issues: Fig. 1a is misleading—the overlayed Gaussian uses empirical mean/variance, not the empirical posterior histogram; please clarify. Fig. 2a shows bias not monotonically decreasing with stepsize; a more explicit discussion (beyond the 1/(Kγ) term) would help reconcile the counter-intuition.

Originality: Applying RRE to SG-MCMC is, to my knowledge, new and nicely connects two communities. Conceptually the method is straightforward, but the variance control via coupling and full convergence analysis are nontrivial.

Significance: The contribution is practically useful—simple to implement, parallel-friendly, and yielding reliability gains. While not transformative, it offers a principled, low-overhead alternative to higher-order integrators, with conditions under which it outperforms vanilla SGLD.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theory (CLT and non-asymptotic rates) with clear conditions.
- Simple, computationally convenient method; parallelizable.
- Consistent empirical gains; bridges SDE and MCMC literatures.
- Clarifies when RRE beats SGLD.

Cons and suggestions:
- Gains on large-scale tasks are modest; include ablations on Brownian coupling and gradient coupling (same mini-batches vs independent).
- Discuss practicality when parallel resources are unavailable; report single-thread time/accuracy trade-offs.
- Clarify Figs. 1a and 2a; specify how mini-batches are matched across chains and implications if they differ.
