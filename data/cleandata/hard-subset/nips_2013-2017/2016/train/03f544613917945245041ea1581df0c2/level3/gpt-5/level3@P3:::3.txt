Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the well-known issue that SG-MCMC methods with first-order integrators (e.g., SGLD) are asymptotically biased at O(γ), yielding MSE ≈ O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)). The authors propose reducing this bias via a classical numerical-analyses tool—Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—instantiated for SG-MCMC. Concretely, they run two SG-MCMC chains: one with steps γk and one with half-steps η2k−1 = η2k = γk/2, evolving to the same continuous-time horizon; correlate the injected Gaussians by setting Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2; and form an extrapolated estimator that cancels the O(γ) weak error, leaving O(γ2) bias. They analyze the resulting algorithm (SGRRLD) and show reduced bias and MSE, including CLT and non-asymptotic bounds, and demonstrate empirical gains on Gaussian models, SGHMC, and large-scale matrix factorization.

The idea relates to: (i) SGLD and its analyses (Welling & Teh ’11; Teh et al. JMLR ’16; Vollmer et al.); (ii) higher-order SG-MCMC integrators (Chen, Ding & Carin NIPS ’15); and (iii) RR extrapolation and variance control in SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup). While RR extrapolation is classical in SDE numerics, its explicit application to SG-MCMC appears novel and well motivated here.

Strengths
- Simple and practical: implementation requires running two coupled SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and a specific shared-noise construction—minimal code changes for practitioners.
- Solid theory: proves O(γ2) bias and O(γ4) variance for the stationary estimator; gives CLT with optimality of full Gaussian coupling; and finite-time bounds leading to improved rates (MSE O(K−4/5) vs O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC).
- Generality: the framework is positioned to apply beyond SGLD (shown for SGHMC); the analysis uses standard weak error expansions, suggesting broad reach across SG-MCMC variants.
- Clear writing: presentation is accessible; connections to SDE error expansions and to ML literature are well made.
- Empirical support: synthetic Gaussian and matrix factorization experiments show consistent improvements, and SGHMC results indicate parity with methods using higher-order integrators at larger γ.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of applicability: The key weak error expansion πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ2) underpins RR. The paper assumes “mild conditions” on U and gradient-noise law L, but it remains unclear exactly which SG-MCMC variants (e.g., preconditioned/thermostatted variants) satisfy this expansion and which don’t. Please clarify necessary and sufficient conditions on the discretization and the stochastic gradient noise (e.g., moments, tail behavior, state dependence) for the expansion to hold.
- Beyond SGLD/SGHMC: Can RR extrapolation be applied to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not pure SDE discretizations, such as subsampling Metropolis–Hastings (with approximate tests) or to distributed SGLD? For distributed SGLD, how would you realize the required Gaussian coupling across workers without excessive communication, and what is the impact if coupling is only approximate?
- Computation/efficiency: One chain executes twice as many micro-steps, which can lead to idle resources in synchronous settings. Would a 3-replica arrangement—one γ chain and two independent γ/2 chains that each take one step per macro-iteration—avoid idle time while preserving the same estimator 2·avg(f) − f? What is the variance impact relative to the single γ/2 chain taking two steps with pathwise (Brownian) coupling? My understanding is that using two independent γ/2 replicas breaks the Brownian path coupling and will inflate variance; a discussion or experiment would be helpful.
- Extensions to more levels: A natural generalization is multi-level/multi-step RR (or MLMC). The paper mentions this as future work. A brief discussion of how many levels are beneficial given compute budgets, and whether the asymptotically optimal K−1 rate from MLMC for weak error could be approached under SG noise, would strengthen the impact.
- Noise and minibatch correlation: The theory shows optimality when the Gaussian noise is maximally coupled (Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2), and that correlation of stochastic gradients does not affect the asymptotic limit. Practically, is sharing the same minibatch across the two chains at each macro-iteration beneficial for finite-time variance? If so, is “same minibatch” per micro-step or re-using the same minibatch for both γ/2 steps preferable? Some ablation results would be valuable.
- Experiments: The matrix factorization results are compelling but would benefit from (i) curves run to convergence; (ii) an SGD or ALS baseline to contextualize RMSE and highlight Bayesian benefits; and (iii) a fairness check in terms of total gradient evaluations (not only wall-clock), since parallelization advantages are hardware dependent.
- Presentation: Including a boxed pseudocode of SGRRLD (with the Gaussian coupling) in the main text would greatly help practitioners. Right now, key implementational details are deferred to the supplement.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with clear proofs sketches and references; assumptions could be delineated more crisply. Empirical evaluation supports theory, though broader baselines and convergence studies would help.
- Clarity: Very well written; a few practical details (exact coupling recipe, minibatch handling) should be surfaced earlier.
- Originality: RR extrapolation is classical, but its application to SG-MCMC with explicit variance control via noise coupling is novel and valuable for the ML community.
- Significance: High. If broadly applicable, this could become a standard bias-reduction “wrapper” for many SG-MCMC methods, achieving higher-order accuracy without complex integrators.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, general idea with strong theoretical guarantees and practical ease-of-use.
- Demonstrable improvements over SGLD and parity with higher-order methods at lower implementation complexity.
- Clear path to extensions (multi-level, other SG-MCMC).

Cons for acceptance
- Applicability conditions and practical coupling choices need clearer guidance.
- Experimental section could better contextualize benefits vs optimization baselines and explore coupling/minibatch ablations.
- Some efficiency questions (multi-replica variants, distributed settings) remain open.

Questions for authors
1) Precisely which SG-MCMC schemes satisfy the required weak error expansion under stochastic gradients? Any counterexamples?  
2) Can you comment on applying RR to subsampling MH or other approximate MH schemes that lack a clear step-size parameter?  
3) For distributed SGLD, how would you realize Gaussian coupling across workers, and what happens if you only correlate partially?  
4) Would a 3-replica design (one γ, two γ/2 independent replicas) be acceptable in practice, and how much variance inflation would you expect relative to pathwise coupling?  
5) Do you recommend sharing minibatches between chains per macro-iteration? Any empirical evidence?  
6) Could you add main-text pseudocode and an SGD baseline for the matrix factorization experiment, and report convergence-to-tolerance comparisons?
