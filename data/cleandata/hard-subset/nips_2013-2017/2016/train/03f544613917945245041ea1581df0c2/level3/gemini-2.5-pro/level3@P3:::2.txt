### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique for accelerating convergence, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. While RR extrapolation is a known method for reducing the discretization bias of SDEs, this work is the first to apply it to the SG-MCMC setting and provide a rigorous analysis that accounts for both the discretization error and the noise from stochastic gradients.

The authors provide a formal theoretical analysis, proving that under certain conditions, SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they show that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) — O(K⁻⁴/⁵) — compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). This improved rate is on par with methods using more complex higher-order integrators, but is achieved here simply by combining two first-order chains. The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate that SGRRLD has an asymptotically lower bias and MSE than SGLD.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is an elegant and clever idea for tackling the well-known problem of discretization bias. The approach is theoretically well-founded and offers a conceptually simple way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE. The analysis correctly identifies the optimal convergence rates and highlights the importance of using correlated Brownian motion between the two chains to control the variance of the final estimator, which is a key technical insight.

3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is easy to understand and straightforward to implement, requiring only minor modifications to an existing SGLD implementation.

4.  **Convincing Synthetic Experiments:** The experiments on the linear Gaussian model provide a compelling and clear validation of the theoretical claims. They effectively demonstrate the significant bias reduction achieved by SGRRLD compared to SGLD and SGHMC, and the analysis of convergence rates with respect to step size γ and iteration count K aligns perfectly with the theory.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconvincing Real-Data Experiments:** The practical benefit of SGRRLD is not clearly demonstrated in the MovieLens matrix factorization experiments. The reported improvement in RMSE over SGLD is marginal (e.g., in Figure 5, the final RMSE values are very close). For a method that requires twice the computation (albeit parallelizable), this small gain makes its practical utility questionable. The paper would be significantly stronger with experiments on real-world problems where the bias of SGLD is known to be a more dominant factor.

2.  **Flawed Comparison of Computational Cost:** The paper claims computational efficiency by comparing the wall-clock time of a parallelized SGRRLD against a single, sequential SGLD chain. This comparison is not fair. SGLD can also be trivially parallelized by running multiple independent chains. A much more appropriate baseline would be to compare SGRRLD against an ensemble of two independent SGLD chains running in parallel, with their results averaged. Without this crucial comparison, it is impossible to disentangle the benefits of the RR extrapolation from the benefits of simply ensembling two chains.

3.  **Incomplete Baselines:** To better isolate the benefit of the extrapolation step, a comparison is needed against a single SGLD chain run with the smaller step size (γ/2) for twice the number of iterations. This would match the computational effort of the second chain in SGRRLD and help clarify whether the improvement comes from the specific RR combination or simply from using a smaller, more accurate step size.

### **Questions and Detailed Comments**

1.  **Conditions for Theorems:** The technical conditions on the potential function U for Theorems 1 and 2 are deferred to the supplement. Could you briefly state the key requirements in the main paper? For instance, do the proofs rely on strong convexity of U, or can they accommodate multimodal posterior distributions, which are a primary target for MCMC methods?

2.  **Impact of Gradient Noise Correlation:** Your analysis highlights the importance of correlating the Brownian noise terms between the chains. However, in the SG-MCMC setting, the stochasticity from mini-batch sampling introduces another source of noise. If the gradient noise terms for the two chains are uncorrelated (due to independent data subsampling), the chains themselves may decorrelate quickly. How does the RR combination maintain its ability to cancel the leading error term in this scenario? As a related empirical question, did you measure the correlation between the states of the two parallel chains (θ⁽ʸ⁾ and θ⁽ʸ/²⁾) in your experiments?

3.  **Justification for Experimental Comparison:** As noted in the weaknesses, the comparison to a single SGLD chain is insufficient. Can you justify this choice, or better yet, provide a comparison against two parallel SGLD chains (either with step size γ or γ/2) to provide a fair assessment of wall-clock efficiency and accuracy?

### **Overall Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a solid theoretical foundation. The synthetic experiments are well-executed and support the theory. However, the quality of the empirical evaluation is diminished by weak real-data results and an unfair comparison to baselines, which weakens the claims about practical utility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and easy to follow. The core ideas and the algorithm are presented very effectively.
*   **Originality:** While RR extrapolation is a known technique, its application and rigorous analysis within the SG-MCMC framework is novel and constitutes a significant contribution. The work is well-situated within existing literature.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of bias in scalable MCMC methods. The theoretical results demonstrating improved convergence rates are significant. The practical significance is currently limited by the weak empirical results on real data. If the authors can provide stronger experimental evidence and more rigorous comparisons, this work could have a notable impact on the field.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a strong theoretical idea but needs to substantiate its practical claims with more convincing experiments and fairer baseline comparisons. I am currently on the fence but lean towards acceptance, assuming the authors can address the experimental concerns in their rebuttal.
