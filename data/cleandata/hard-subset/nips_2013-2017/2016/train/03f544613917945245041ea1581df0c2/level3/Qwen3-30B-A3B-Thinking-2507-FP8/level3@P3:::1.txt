

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC), specifically to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), resulting in a method called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The authors bridge two literatures—MCMC and SDEs—by applying a numerical sequence acceleration method to SG-MCMC. The key insight is that running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their results via RRE reduces bias while maintaining reasonable variance. This builds on Talay and Tubaro's work on RR extrapolation for SDEs and extends it to the stochastic gradient setting.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, and that its MSE converges at rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis showing how SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods using only a first-order integrator. They also demonstrate that the method is well-suited for parallel and distributed computing architectures, as the two chains can be run independently.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive empirical results. On synthetic Gaussian models, SGRRLD shows dramatic improvements in bias reduction (from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴ in variance estimation) and MSE (an order of magnitude better than SGLD). On large-scale matrix factorization tasks (MovieLens datasets), SGRRLD achieves comparable or better accuracy than SGLD in half the time, demonstrating practical utility. The experiments are well-designed and clearly presented.

The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with MCMC and SDEs. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including pseudo-code in the supplementary material. The paper adequately references related work, including key contributions to SGLD (Welling and Teh, 2011) and RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay and Tubaro, 1990).

The main strengths of the paper are its theoretical novelty in applying RRE to SG-MCMC, the clear theoretical analysis of convergence rates, and the practical demonstration of improved performance on both synthetic and real-world problems. The method is straightforward to implement and computationally efficient, making it immediately useful for practitioners.

The primary weaknesses are minor but notable. Figure 1a is misleading as it displays a Gaussian with empirical mean/variance plugged in rather than the true empirical posterior distribution. Figure 2a shows counter-intuitive behavior where bias increases for smaller step sizes, but the paper lacks discussion of this phenomenon, which would help readers understand the theoretical bounds. Additionally, while the method is useful, it is not transformative—it's a straightforward application of an existing numerical technique to a new domain.

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis and strong empirical results. The authors carefully address both strengths (improved convergence rates) and limitations (increased computational cost for parallel chains).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of both the theoretical contributions and practical implementation. The supplementary material provides additional detail for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, though the technique itself is well-established in numerical analysis. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on SG-MCMC (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Welling and Teh, 2011) by showing how RRE achieves higher-order convergence rates with first-order methods.

**Significance**: While not transformative, the method provides a practical, computationally efficient way to improve the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms without requiring complex higher-order integrators. The consistent improvements across both synthetic and large-scale experiments suggest this approach will be widely adopted by practitioners working with large-scale Bayesian inference.

**Arguments for acceptance**: Strong theoretical contribution that bridges MCMC and SDE literatures; clear empirical validation on both synthetic and real data; straightforward implementation; practical utility for large-scale Bayesian inference.

**Arguments against acceptance**: Method is not fundamentally novel (merely an application of existing numerical technique); minor technical issues in figures; not transformative compared to existing SG-MCMC methods.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by providing a theoretically grounded, practical method for improving convergence rates. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides meaningful improvements over vanilla SGLD. While not revolutionary, it offers a valuable tool for practitioners working with large-scale Bayesian inference problems. I recommend acceptance.
