

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build on the well-known RR extrapolation technique from numerical analysis, which has been applied to SDEs in [21,23], but extend it to the SG-MCMC setting where gradient estimates are noisy. Their key insight is that running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel, combined with correlated Brownian motions, can achieve asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴) respectively, compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²). This leads to a faster convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for mean squared error compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions: Theorems 1 and 2 provide rigorous convergence guarantees, showing SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent with a central limit theorem, and deriving non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The authors carefully address the technical conditions required for their results, though it would be helpful to clarify whether these apply to multimodal target distributions (a common scenario in Bayesian inference). The algorithm description is clear and implementable, with a straightforward pseudo-code in the supplementary material.

The experiments show compelling results on synthetic data. In the linear Gaussian model, SGRRLD significantly reduces the bias in posterior variance estimation (from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴) compared to SGLD, and achieves MSE improvements that scale with the square of SGLD's error. The rate-of-convergence experiments confirm the theoretical predictions. However, the real-data experiments on MovieLens datasets show only modest improvements in RMSE (e.g., SGRRLD achieves equivalent accuracy to SGLD in half the time for ML-1M), which is less impressive than the synthetic results. The authors should provide more convincing evidence of practical benefits, such as comparing to SGLD with two parallel chains (which would have similar computational cost) or SGLD with step size γ/2, to isolate the specific contribution of RR extrapolation.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a domain where this technique had not been previously explored. While RR extrapolation for SDEs is established [21,23], the authors' application to SG-MCMC with theoretical justification represents a meaningful advance. The method is also naturally parallelizable, which aligns well with modern distributed computing architectures.

Several technical concerns require clarification. First, the paper assumes correlated gradient noise between the two chains, but it's unclear how the method performs when this correlation is absent (as would be the case with independent mini-batches). The authors should investigate whether the error reduction persists under independent noise, or whether they need to enforce gradient correlation. Second, the paper claims SGRRLD has better computational efficiency than SGLD, but since both chains run in parallel, the wall-clock time should be similar to SGLD with two parallel chains (which is a common practice). The authors should compare against this more relevant baseline to demonstrate the true advantage of their approach.

The significance of the work is strong in the theoretical domain, as SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only first-order integration. However, the practical significance is somewhat diminished by the modest improvements in real-world applications. The authors should provide more substantial real-data evidence, particularly for larger-scale problems where the computational advantages of parallelism would be most pronounced.

Quality: The theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported, though the conditions on the potential function U require further clarification (e.g., do they hold for multimodal distributions?).

Clarity: The paper is well-written with clear algorithm descriptions, though the connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation could be strengthened.

Originality: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, building on known techniques but extending them to a new domain with proper theoretical justification.

Significance: The method advances the theoretical understanding of SG-MCMC, but the practical benefits need more convincing evidence from real-world applications.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with a novel theoretical contribution that should interest the NIPS community. The authors should address the concerns about real-data performance and clarify the technical conditions for their theorems. With these improvements, the paper would be a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid theoretical advance with promising but not yet fully demonstrated practical benefits. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should strengthen the real-data experiments in the final version.
