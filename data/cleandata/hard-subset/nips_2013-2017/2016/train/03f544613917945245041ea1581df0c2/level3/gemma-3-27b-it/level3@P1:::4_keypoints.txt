- Introduces SGRRLD, a novel SG-MCMC algorithm using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation by running two SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining results to reduce bias.
- Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error.
- Claims improved convergence rates over SGLD, matching higher-order integrators while using a simple first-order approach.
- Empirically validated on synthetic tasks (Gaussian model, SGHMC) and real-world matrix factorization for movie recommendation, supporting theoretical advantages.
- Novelty: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (Talay & Tubaro style) to SG-MCMC, a new direction relative to prior work on higher-order integrators and multilevel methods.
- Strength: conceptually simple and easy to implement, with strong theoretical foundations and parallelization potential.
- Strength: experimental results lend credibility and the paper situates itself well within existing Bayesian inference and SG-MCMC literature.
- Weakness: derivation of convergence rates (Theorem 2) and connection between theoretical bounds and empirical rates is unclear.
- Discrepancy: paper claims optimal α=1/3 for bias and α=0.2 for MSE, but Figure 3 shows best bias at α=0.2, requiring clarification.
- Empirical rate estimation appears noisy (substantial fluctuations in Figure 3); needs more robust statistical analysis of convergence behavior.
- Practical concern: SGRRLD costs roughly three times SGLD runtime on a single core (two chains, one doubled steps); direct runtime comparison in Figure 5 is misleading.
- Suggestion: fair timing comparison should scale SGLD timings to account for SGRRLD’s extra computational cost (expect reduced reported performance gap).
- Presentation issues: inconsistent notation (ϑ vs θ), undefined abbreviations and symbols (RR, Γ_K), vague smoothness assumptions, mislabeled Figure 2, and inconsistent vector formatting.
- Theoretical question: explanation needed for dominance of 1/(Kγ) term when γ is very small and why this term does not dominate SGLD error as γ→0.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel extrapolation application, strong theoretical guarantees, empirical improvements, parallelization potential, and effective bias reduction.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear rate derivations and theory-experiment mismatch, misleading timing comparison, presentation issues, and insufficient explanation of SGLD behavior at tiny step sizes.
- Overall recommendation: conditional acceptance pending revisions to clarify theoretical results, provide fair timing comparisons, fix presentation errors, and better analyze convergence discrepancies.
