This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique – a numerical sequence acceleration method – by running two SGLD chains in parallel with differing step sizes and correlated Brownian motions.  The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on both non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error.  Empirical validation via synthetic datasets and a large-scale matrix factorization task confirms improved convergence rates compared to standard SGLD and, in some cases, SGHMC.

The work builds directly upon the increasingly popular field of SG-MCMC [1, 2], motivated by scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets. Recent research, including Chen et al. [10], has focused on improving the performance of these methods through higher-order integrators. SGRRLD, in essence, offers a different approach to bias reduction *without* increasing the order of the underlying numerical integrator, achieving similar accuracy gains.  The connection to prior work on RR extrapolation for estimating invariant distributions of SDEs [23] is also acknowledged and appropriately contextualized.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing crucial properties like consistency and convergence rates. The derived bounds on bias and MSE are valuable contributions.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the proposed method.
* **Promising Empirical Results:**  Experiments on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the potential benefits of SGRRLD. The matrix factorization results are particularly compelling.
* **Generality:** The authors rightly point out that the proposed technique is broadly applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms, not just SGLD.
* **Parallelizability:** Exploiting the inherent parallel nature of the RR extrapolation via the two chains provides a potential performance advantage, particularly on multi-core architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, the core idea builds heavily on existing work on higher-order integration techniques [10] and RR extrapolation for SDEs [23]. The contribution, while valuable, might be considered incremental.
* **Practical Communication Overhead:**  A significant practical concern not adequately addressed is the communication overhead associated with generating the correlated Brownian motions required by the RR extrapolation.  The authors state the chains can be run independently, but the correlated noise necessitates communication *at each step*. This communication cost isn’t considered in the experimental evaluation. 
* **Experimental Setup and Resource Allocation:** The experiments, especially those using real data, require clarification. The authors should explicitly state whether SGLD was afforded the same computational resources (e.g., number of cores, wall-clock time) as SGRRLD. If SGRRLD benefits from running two chains in parallel utilizing double the resources, this needs to be accounted for when comparing performance.  Specifically, the gradient computation cost should be considered when comparing wall-clock times.
* **Missing Baseline:**  A stronger baseline would be to compare SGRRLD against running two *independent* SGLD chains in parallel ("embarrassingly parallel" SGLD). This would help isolate the benefit derived specifically from the RR extrapolation (correlation of Brownian motions) versus simply leveraging parallel computation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated contribution to the growing field of SG-MCMC.
* The theoretical results are significant and provide valuable insights into the convergence properties of SGRRLD.
* The empirical results, while requiring further scrutiny regarding resource allocation, are promising and support the theoretical findings.
* The method is potentially applicable to a wide range of Bayesian inference problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach might be considered incremental given prior related work.
* The practical communication overhead associated with maintaining correlated Brownian motions is a significant concern not adequately addressed.
* The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail regarding resource allocation and a crucial baseline comparison.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. However, the potential practical limitations associated with communication costs and the need for clearer experimental comparisons raise some concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  The authors should:

1.  **Address the communication overhead:** Discuss the practical implications of maintaining correlated Brownian motions and potentially explore strategies for mitigating the communication cost (e.g., asynchronous updates).
2.  **Clarify the experimental setup:** Provide detailed information regarding resource allocation (number of cores, wall-clock time, gradient computation cost) for both SGLD and SGRRLD experiments.
3.  **Include a comparison to embarrassingly parallel SGLD:** Add a baseline comparison against running two independent SGLD chains in parallel.
4.  **Correct the duplication:** Remove the duplicate mention of “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” (lines 25 and 70).
