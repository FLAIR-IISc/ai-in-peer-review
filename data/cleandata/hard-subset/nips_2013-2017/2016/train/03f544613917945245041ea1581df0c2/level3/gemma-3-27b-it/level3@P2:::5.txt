This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing methods like SGLD. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique – running two chains in parallel with different step sizes – to the SGLD framework. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency and derive bounds for bias and mean squared error, showing improved convergence rates compared to SGLD. Empirical validation on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization) supports these findings.

The paper builds directly upon the growing body of work in SG-MCMC, notably Welling & Teh (2011) who introduced SGLD, and subsequent extensions aiming to improve its performance (e.g., Ahn et al., 2012; Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014). The use of higher-order integrators, as explored by Chen et al. (2015), is also related, with the authors claiming SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy with a simpler first-order integrator.  The RR extrapolation technique itself is well-established in numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), but its application to SG-MCMC is novel. The connection to related work on exploiting correlations in SDEs, as referenced through Lemaire and Pagès (2015) and others, is well-placed.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a conceptually simple yet potentially effective idea.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including convergence proofs and non-asymptotic error bounds. The central limit theorem result is particularly valuable.
* **Parallelism:** The algorithm is easily parallelizable, as the two chains can be run independently, making it well-suited for large-scale applications.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments, on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD, showing improvements over SGLD and, in some cases, competitive performance with higher-order integrators.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the method and its theoretical underpinnings clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While the results show improvement over SGLD, a direct, controlled comparison to higher-order integrators (like SGHMC with a more sophisticated integrator from Chen et al., 2015) would strengthen the claims of achieving similar accuracy with lower computational cost. The synthetic experiments demonstrate this comparison with SGHMC, but not for the larger-scale application.
* **Inconsistency in Step Size:** The paper’s discussion of step size selection feels somewhat disconnected. The optimal step size appears to be derived for different aspects (bias vs. MSE), leading to some confusion.
* **Algorithmic Discrepancy:** A minor, but potentially confusing, issue is the inconsistency between Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material and Equation (5) in the main text. This requires clarification.
* **Minor Technicality:** The statement regarding the selection of Σ in Equation (8) suggesting Σ=Id always minimizes the variance feels strong. It would be useful to provide a more detailed explanation or justification.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of SG-MCMC.
* The method is practical, easily parallelizable, and demonstrates clear empirical benefits.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art higher-order integrators weakens the claim of achieving comparable accuracy with lower complexity.
* The minor inconsistencies (step size discussion, algorithm discrepancy) detract slightly from the overall polish of the paper.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the NIPS conference. The idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is insightful, the theoretical analysis is compelling, and the empirical results are promising. While the comparison to higher-order integrators could be more thorough, the current results justify acceptance.  I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the inconsistencies mentioned above and potentially expand the experimental comparison.  Specifically, the authors should clarify the step size selection process and ensure consistency between the main text and supplementary materials.
