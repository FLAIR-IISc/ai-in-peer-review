Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the well-known asymptotic bias of SG-MCMC estimators when using discretizations without Metropolis correction. The authors propose to reduce this bias via Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation: run two SG-MCMC chains targeting the same SDE with step sizes γ and γ/2 (the smaller-step chain takes two substeps per outer iteration), couple their injected Gaussian noises so that Zγ ≈ (Zγ/2,1 + Zγ/2,2)/√2, and form an extrapolated estimator whose weak error cancels to second order. Instantiated with SGLD, this yields SGRRLD.

Theoretical contributions include: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator under standard SG-MCMC conditions, showing the fluctuation variance is minimized with perfectly correlated Brownian increments across chains; (ii) non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE indicating O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) for fixed γ, respectively, with optimal rates in K of O(K−2/3) for bias and O(K−4/5) for MSE; and (iii) step-size schedules γk ∝ k−α with optimal α = 1/5 in the asymptotic CLT sense, giving improved rates over first-order SG-MCMC without RR (e.g., SGLD’s O(K−2/3) MSE).

Empirically, the authors validate on a linear-Gaussian model (with ground-truth posterior), an SGHMC instantiation (SGRRHMC) compared against SGHMC and a higher-order splitting variant, and large-scale matrix factorization on MovieLens. The results support reduced bias and MSE and competitive or better wall-clock efficiency by exploiting parallelism across the two chains.

This work builds on classical RR for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lamberton–Pagès; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) and recent NIPS work on SG-MCMC theory and high-order integrators (Ma–Chen–Fox 2015; Chen–Ding–Carin 2015), but, to my knowledge, RR has not been explicitly leveraged within SG-MCMC.

Quality
The theory is carefully stated and appears technically sound, relying on standard weak error expansions and Poisson equation techniques. The requirement to correlate Brownian increments is justified and consistent with prior RR results for diffusions. Non-asymptotic bounds are informative and align with the CLT. Empirical studies are appropriate for illustrating bias/MSE reductions and show that extrapolation achieves accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators while only using first-order updates. Some aspects could be strengthened: (i) more systematic ablations on the role of noise and minibatch correlations; (ii) broader benchmarks (e.g., logistic regression, non-Gaussian posteriors); and (iii) baselines contextualizing predictive performance (e.g., SGD) in the matrix factorization task.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The main idea is simple and is conveyed crisply, and the assumptions are discussed (with full technical statements deferred to the supplement). Including the algorithm pseudocode in the main text (rather than only in the supplement) would help practitioners.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is, to my knowledge, new to the ML community and distinct from prior NIPS work that improves accuracy via high-order integrators or thermostats. The coupling design for the stochastic noise in the SG setting and the finite-time analysis add novelty beyond classical RR for SDEs.

Significance
The method is simple, broadly applicable to first-order SG-MCMC methods, and can deliver practically meaningful bias/MSE gains without complex integrators. Because the two chains can be run in parallel, wall-clock can match a single-chain baseline, making the proposal attractive for modern hardware. The idea may extend to multilevel RR and other SG-MCMC variants, potentially impacting scalable Bayesian computation.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, minimal code changes; general recipe applicable beyond SGLD (demonstrated with SGHMC).
- Solid theory: CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and optimal coupling result.
- Practical guidance on step-size schedules and the importance of noise coupling.
- Empirical evidence on synthetic and large-scale real data; competitive wall-clock efficiency using parallelism.
- Connects SG-MCMC to a classical numerical acceleration tool not widely used in ML.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of applicability: The analysis relies on weak error expansions of the form πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ2). Please clarify which SG-MCMC variants (e.g., with momentum, Riemannian preconditioning, thermostats) satisfy these conditions and whether there are common counterexamples.
- Correlation design: You prove optimality for correlating Brownian increments. What about correlating stochastic gradients/minibatches across chains in practice? Is reusing the same minibatch across the γ and γ/2 chains beneficial or neutral (given the requirement of identical L)? An ablation comparing independent vs shared minibatches would be informative.
- Computational efficiency: While parallelism equalizes wall-clock, on a single core SGRRLD doubles computation per effective time unit. Could you discuss efficiency under limited parallel resources? Also, would a batched scheduling (e.g., three workers: one γ, two γ/2) change variance or only throughput?
- Extensions: You mention recursive RR. Can you outline how the analysis and variance control extend to more than two levels (and the practical coupling across levels)? Any pitfalls for SG-MCMC?
- Generalization to other approximate MCMC schemes: Could this be applied to subsampling MH or distributed SGLD variants? What additional conditions are needed?
- Experimental breadth: The matrix factorization experiment would benefit from (i) training curves to near-convergence, (ii) an SGD baseline to contextualize RMSE, and (iii) sensitivity to step sizes and minibatch sizes. For the synthetic studies, adding a non-Gaussian posterior (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression) would test robustness.

Suggestions to improve the manuscript
- Move a concise pseudocode box for SGRRLD to the main text, including the optimal noise coupling.
- Add an ablation on noise and minibatch correlations and report the empirical variance impact, aligning with Theorem 1.
- Clarify the precise regularity assumptions (e.g., the required smoothness class for the Poisson solution) and discuss practical implications when these do not hold.
- Expand discussion on applying RR to other SG-MCMC methods (SGRLD, pSGLD, thermostats) with concrete guidance.
- Provide a short computational cost analysis under varying parallelism budgets.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with strong theory and practical gains.
- Bridges numerical analysis (RR) and scalable Bayesian sampling.
- Demonstrated improvements over common baselines and parity with higher-order methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation could be broader and include stronger baselines and ablations.
- Practical guidance on minibatch correlation and multi-level extensions is limited.
- Assumptions may be restrictive for some posteriors; scope boundaries need clearer articulation.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a clear, impactful idea and solid analysis. With minor extensions and clarifications, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
