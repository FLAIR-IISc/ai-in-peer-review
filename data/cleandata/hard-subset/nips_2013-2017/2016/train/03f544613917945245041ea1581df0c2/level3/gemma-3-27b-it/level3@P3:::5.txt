This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-RR-MCMC), a method aimed at reducing the bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD, effectively running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency and derive bounds on bias and mean squared error. Empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization) support the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The work builds upon the growing body of research on SG-MCMC methods for large-scale Bayesian inference, beginning with Welling & Teh (ICML 2011) and extending through numerous contributions like SGHMC (Chen et al., ICML 2014) and various adaptive Langevin thermostat schemes (Shang et al., NIPS 2015). The paper directly addresses a known limitation of SG-MCMC – its bias – and leverages a well-established numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg) to mitigate it.  Recent work (Chen et al., NIPS 2015) explored improving bounds using higher-order integrators, which this paper aims to achieve using a simpler first-order approach combined with extrapolation.  The application of RR extrapolation to SDEs, though mentioned (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), is relatively unexplored in the context of SG-MCMC.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation with SGLD is a novel approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough theoretical analysis, establishing asymptotic consistency and deriving bounds on bias and MSE.  The convergence rate improvement (O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>) vs. O(K<sup>-2/3</sup>) for SGLD) is a significant theoretical result.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the practical effectiveness of SGRRLD, showing improved performance compared to SGLD and, in some cases, comparable results to higher-order integrators (SGRRHMC compared to SGHMC-s).
* **Parallelizability:** The embarrassingly parallel nature of the two SGLD chains makes the method well-suited for modern parallel computing architectures.
* **Conceptual Simplicity:** The method is conceptually straightforward, building directly on existing SGLD implementations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Inconsistent Step Size Discussion:** The paper mentions a step size in the introduction, but it is not entirely clear how this relates to the γ<sub>k</sub> notation used later. Clarification is needed.
* **Algorithm Discrepancy:** Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material appears inconsistent with Equation (5) in the main text. Specifically, the updating steps for θ<sup>(γ/2)</sup><sub>2k+1</sub> and θ<sup>(γ/2)</sup><sub>2k+2</sub>  do not correctly reflect the RR extrapolation scheme described in the equations. This needs to be resolved.
* **Limited Comparison to Higher-Order Integrators:** While the paper claims to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators, the experimental comparison is limited. A more comprehensive comparison with SGHMC (using a standard implementation) across a wider range of problem settings would strengthen the claims.
* **Variance Control:** The discussion around correlated Brownian increments and the choice of Σ feels somewhat underdeveloped. While theoretically justified, the practical impact of different Σ values is not fully explored experimentally.  The benefits of correlation are mentioned, but the sensitivity of performance to different correlation structures isn't analyzed.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The parallelizability of the method is a significant advantage.
* The work addresses a known limitation of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The inconsistencies in the algorithm and step size discussion require attention.
* The limited comparison to higher-order integrators weakens the claims of equivalent accuracy.
* The discussion on variance control and correlated Brownian motion is not fully fleshed out.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach to bias reduction and demonstrates encouraging results. However, the identified inconsistencies and limited comparisons detract from the overall strength of the work. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the inconsistencies in the algorithm and step size discussion, provide a more comprehensive comparison to higher-order integrators, and clarify the practical implications of variance control through correlated Brownian increments. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and its contribution to the field.
