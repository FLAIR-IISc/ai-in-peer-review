This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), to reduce the discretization bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient MCMC algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to SG-MCMC. The specific implementation, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size sequence (γk) and another that takes two steps of size γk/2 for each step of the first chain. The final estimator is a linear combination of the outputs from these two chains. This work builds upon the well-established SGLD algorithm [1] and the application of RR extrapolation to SDEs with exact gradients [21, 23]. The main contribution is the adaptation and analysis of this technique for the stochastic gradient setting, which is highly relevant for large-scale Bayesian inference. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that their method is asymptotically consistent and achieves a faster MSE convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The practical effectiveness of the method is demonstrated on both synthetic and large-scale real-world experiments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and clever combination of two well-known techniques: SGLD and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. While the components are not new, their application to reduce bias in the popular SG-MCMC framework is original and addresses a significant practical problem. The proposed method offers a new, conceptually simple, and effective way to improve the accuracy of SG-MCMC samplers.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength of the paper. The authors provide a Central Limit Theorem for the SGRRLD estimator and derive non-asymptotic bounds on its bias and MSE. These results rigorously establish that SGRRLD attains a higher rate of convergence than first-order SG-MCMC methods, providing strong theoretical support for the algorithm's design.
*   **Practicality:** A key advantage of SGRRLD is its suitability for parallel computation. The two chains can be run completely independently without any communication overhead, making the method "embarrassingly parallel." For the same number of gradient evaluations, the wall-clock time can be halved on a machine with at least two cores compared to a sequential high-order integrator. This makes the approach highly practical and scalable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing and effectively support the theoretical claims. The synthetic experiments on a Gaussian model clearly illustrate the reduction in bias and MSE compared to SGLD. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets demonstrate that these theoretical advantages translate into significant real-world performance gains, with SGRRLD achieving better accuracy than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Technical Correctness of Algorithm Description:** There are critical inconsistencies in the mathematical description of the algorithm that undermine the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
    1.  In Equation (5), the first update for "Chain 2" is given as: `θ(γ/2)2k+1 = θ(γ/2)2k − (γk+1/2) * ∇Ũ(γ/2)2k+1(θ(γ/2)2k+1) + ...`. The gradient `∇Ũ` is evaluated at the new state `θ(γ/2)2k+1`, which defines an implicit numerical scheme. This is highly unusual for SGLD and contradicts the standard explicit Euler-Maruyama formulation used elsewhere in the paper (e.g., Eq. 2 and the second line of Eq. 5). This appears to be a typo, but it is a significant one that confuses the definition of the core algorithm. It should likely be evaluated at `θ(γ/2)2k`.
    2.  The provided key points note an inconsistency between Algorithm 1 in the supplement and Equation (5). While the supplement was not reviewed, this points to a pattern of imprecision that needs to be carefully addressed by the authors. Such inconsistencies make it difficult for readers to be confident in the implementation details.
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The paper claims that the proposed approach can achieve the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. While this is an important claim, the experimental comparison is limited. A comparison is made against a higher-order SGHMC variant [10] in a synthetic experiment (Figure 4), which is a good first step. However, a more direct and extensive comparison of SGRRLD against a higher-order SGLD integrator on both the synthetic and real-data problems would have made the paper's claims about relative performance much stronger. This would help practitioners understand the trade-offs between SGRRLD and other bias-reduction techniques.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel, simple, and elegant method for a significant problem (bias in SG-MCMC).
    *   Strong theoretical results, including proofs of improved convergence rates.
    *   The method is embarrassingly parallel, making it highly practical.
    *   Empirical results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   Major clarity issues and apparent errors in the core algorithmic equations (Equation 5) severely hinder reproducibility and confidence in the work.
    *   The experimental comparison to alternative bias-reduction methods (higher-order integrators) is not comprehensive enough to fully situate the work.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. The idea is sound, the theory is solid, and the results are promising. However, the inconsistencies in the description of the algorithm are a serious flaw that must be corrected before publication. Assuming these are correctable typos, the paper represents a solid contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
