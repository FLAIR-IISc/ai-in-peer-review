This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm designed to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) for large-scale Bayesian inference. The core idea is to apply the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique – a well-known numerical acceleration method – to SGLD by running two chains in parallel with different step sizes. This work effectively bridges concepts from the stochastic optimization (SGLD) and stochastic differential equation (SDE) literatures, offering potential benefits to both communities.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the asymptotic consistency of SGRRLD and establishing a central limit theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error, showing that SGRRLD achieves higher rates of convergence than standard SGLD, both in the finite-time and asymptotic regimes. This suggests that the proposed method can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors also highlight the method’s suitability for parallel and distributed architectures due to the independent nature of the two required chains.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, beginning with synthetic data using a Gaussian model. These experiments effectively demonstrate the bias reduction achieved by SGRRLD and validate its improved convergence rate. Expanding beyond synthetic data, the authors apply SGRRLD to a large-scale matrix factorization problem on the MovieLens datasets. The results consistently show improvements over SGLD in terms of RMSE, suggesting practical utility. The inclusion of a comparison against SGHMC with and without higher-order integration further strengthens the findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a robust theoretical analysis, providing guarantees for the algorithm’s convergence and performance. The derivation of non-asymptotic bounds is particularly valuable.
* **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the method is explained in detail, and the experimental setup is described thoroughly.
* **Novelty and Significance:** While building on existing techniques, the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD is novel and addresses a significant challenge in scaling MCMC to large datasets—namely, the bias inherent in first-order integrators. The potential for improved convergence rates and parallelization makes this a valuable contribution.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental section covers both synthetic and real-world datasets, providing strong evidence supporting the theoretical claims.
* **Generality:** The authors explicitly state that their approach can be generalized to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, increasing its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Figure 1a Clarity:** The visualization in Figure 1a is potentially misleading. It depicts a Gaussian distribution parameterized by the empirical mean and variance, rather than the empirical posterior distribution itself. This should be clarified in the caption or the figure revised.
* **Figure 2a Discussion:** The observation in Figure 2a, that the bias does not monotonically decrease with decreasing step size, is counter-intuitive and requires further discussion. The authors briefly mention the term 1/(Kγ) but could provide a more detailed explanation of this behavior.
* **Modest Gains in Real-World Experiments:** While the results on the MovieLens datasets are positive, the improvements over SGLD are modest. Further investigation into the sensitivity of the performance gains to hyperparameters or data characteristics could be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** Although the authors mention that the method is well suited for parallelization, a more detailed discussion of the computational overhead associated with running two chains would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in scalable Bayesian inference.
* The theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported.
* The experimental results validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.
* The writing is clear, concise, and well-organized.
* The work is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements over SGLD in the real-world experiments are modest.
* The minor clarity issues with Figures 1a and 2a should be addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic gradient MCMC. The combination of theoretical rigor, clear presentation, and comprehensive evaluation makes this work deserving of acceptance. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a practical and effective way to reduce the bias of SGLD, potentially enabling more accurate and efficient Bayesian inference in large-scale applications. Addressing the minor issues with the figures would further strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should clarify Figure 1a and provide a more detailed explanation for the observed behavior in Figure 2a.
