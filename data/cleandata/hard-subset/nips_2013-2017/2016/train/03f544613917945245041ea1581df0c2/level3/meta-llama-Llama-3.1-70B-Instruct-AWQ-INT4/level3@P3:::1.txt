This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to improve the convergence rate of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The RR extrapolation is a well-known method in numerical analysis that can improve the rate of convergence of a sequence by running two chains with different step sizes in parallel.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and the mean squared error (MSE). They show that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem, and that its non-asymptotic bias and MSE can be bounded. The results also show that SGRRLD attains higher rates of convergence than SGLD in both finite-time and asymptotically, and that it achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods that are based on higher-order integrators.

The paper is solid, clearly written, and appears technically sound. The method is a straightforward, computationally convenient application of RR extrapolation to SGLD, and it is likely to be useful in practice. However, it is not transformative, and the improvements over SGLD are modest but consistent.

The empirical results are also promising, showing large improvements on toy examples and modest but consistent gains on large-scale problems. The experiments on synthetic data demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD in reducing the bias and MSE of the estimated variance, and the results on large-scale matrix factorization problems show that SGRRLD can achieve the same accuracy as SGLD in shorter time or achieve better accuracy by spending the same amount of time as SGLD.

One minor issue with the paper is that Figure 1a is misleading, as it shows a Gaussian with empirical mean and variance plugged in, rather than the empirical posterior. The authors should clarify this in the caption.

Another minor issue is that Figure 2a needs a discussion on why the bias may not decrease as the step size decreases, which is counter-intuitive. The authors should provide some explanation for this phenomenon.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed method is likely to be useful in practice, and the empirical results demonstrate its effectiveness.
