This paper proposes a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to reduce the bias in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs. While RR extrapolation is well-established for deterministic and stochastic differential equations, its application and theoretical analysis in the SG-MCMC context are novel.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The theoretical contribution is solid, proving that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and a faster MSE convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This is an important result for a field where discretization and gradient noise bias are primary concerns. The algorithm is described with excellent clarity and is straightforward to implement, which increases its potential impact. The synthetic experiments on a linear Gaussian model provide a clear and compelling demonstration of the method's bias-reduction capabilities, strongly supporting the theoretical claims.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its current impact. The primary concern is the limited empirical evidence on real-world data. The reported RMSE improvement on the MovieLens dataset is marginal, which raises questions about the practical utility of the asymptotic gains. Stronger results on more diverse and challenging real-world problems are needed to make a convincing case.

Furthermore, the experimental comparison of computational cost is not entirely fair. The paper compares a parallelized two-chain SGRRLD to a single-chain SGLD. A more rigorous comparison would be against an SGLD baseline that also leverages two parallel chains (e.g., by averaging their results), or to a single SGLD chain run with step-size γ/2 for a comparable number of gradient evaluations. This would help isolate the benefits of the RR extrapolation scheme itself from the benefits of simple parallelization.

Finally, some theoretical aspects could be clarified. The main text should specify the conditions on the potential U required for Theorems 1 and 2 to hold—for example, does the theory cover multimodal posteriors? An intuitive explanation of how error cancellation is achieved when the stochastic gradient noise might cause the two chains to decorrelate would also be welcome. Did the authors observe significant correlation between the chains in their real-data experiments?

In summary, this paper introduces a theoretically sound and elegant idea. However, its practical significance is not yet convincingly demonstrated due to weak real-data results and an incomplete computational comparison. Strengthening the experimental section would significantly improve this promising work.
