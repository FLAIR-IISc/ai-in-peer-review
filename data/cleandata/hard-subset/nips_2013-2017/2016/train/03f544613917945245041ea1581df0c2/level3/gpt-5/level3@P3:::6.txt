Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. Concretely, it runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, advances the smaller-step chain twice per outer iteration, and forms an extrapolated estimator whose leading-order discretization bias cancels. To control variance, the method uses correlated Gaussian noise across the two chains, with an analysis showing that perfect correlation (Σ = Id) minimizes asymptotic variance.

Theoretically, the paper establishes a CLT for the extrapolated estimator, and finite-time bounds: for constant γ, bias O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), implying optimal finite-time rates bias O(K−2/3) and MSE O(K−4/5) with appropriate γ. For decreasing steps γk ∝ k−α, the asymptotic rate from the CLT is optimized at α = 1/5, yielding O(K−2/5) in the weak error sense. These results match the accuracy achievable with higher-order integrators (cf. [10]) while only using Euler–Maruyama plus RR extrapolation. The paper also connects to RR for invariant measures of diffusions [23], extending it to the SG-MCMC setting and clarifying the role of correlated Brownian increments.

Empirically, synthetic Gaussian models verify the predicted bias/MSE scalings. An application to large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) shows improved test RMSE vs. SGLD at matched wall-clock time.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated idea: use RR extrapolation to cancel first-order bias in SG-MCMC while controlling variance via correlated noise. Nicely bridges numerical analysis (RR) with scalable Bayesian sampling.
- Solid theory: asymptotic CLT with variance minimization under correlation, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds with explicit rates that improve upon first-order SG-MCMC. The results are consistent with, and complementary to, analyses of higher-order integrators [10] and duplicated diffusions [23].
- Generic framework: applies to multiple SG-MCMC methods (SGLD, SGHMC), with an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) empirically matching higher-order integrator accuracy using simple components.
- Synthetic studies convincingly validate the predicted scalings. Real-data results suggest practical gains.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Incremental novelty: Prior work [10] established improved bias/MSE for SG-MCMC with higher-order integrators, and [23] developed RR extrapolation for invariant distributions with correlated Brownian motion. The contribution here is a thoughtful synthesis and extension to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, but the conceptual leap may be viewed as modest.
- Practical applicability and fairness of experiments: The two-chain scheme requires coupling the Gaussian noise across chains; achieving the Σ = Id coupling typically involves per-step coordination or careful shared RNG scheduling. The paper claims chains “can be generated independently” and “run as two independent processes,” yet simultaneously needs the specific coupling Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. This raises concerns about communication/synchronization overhead that is not measured or discussed. If SGRRLD uses two cores while SGLD uses one, the wall-clock comparisons may be unfair.
- Missing baseline: A strong and natural baseline is two independent SGLD chains run in parallel (same hardware budget) with combined predictions. While this does not reduce bias, it halves variance and may close the empirical gap in tasks where predictive RMSE is the metric.
- Assumptions and clarity: The theoretical results rely on strong smoothness/ergodicity conditions (e.g., high-order differentiability via Poisson equation). The main text defers these to the supplement; summarizing the key assumptions and their plausibility for the matrix factorization model would help. Also, the claim that “Gaussian noise dominates the stochastic gradient noise” (Section 4) is important; a short empirical check of how mini-batch noise scales in practice would strengthen the story.

Quality
- The analysis appears technically sound and carefully executed, with appropriate use of Poisson equation techniques and Taylor expansions. Non-asymptotic rates are clearly derived.
- Experimental methodology on synthetic data is strong and matches theory. On real data, results are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and explicit accounting for compute/communication.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with good positioning relative to [10,23]. Including a brief pseudo-code in the main text (not only supplement) and a schematic of the noise coupling would aid comprehension.
- Minor: “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is duplicated (lines 25 and 70).

Originality
- Combines known ideas (RR extrapolation, correlated Brownian increments) with SG-MCMC and provides nontrivial analysis in the stochastic-gradient setting. Novelty is moderate but meaningful.

Significance
- If the communication/parallelism overhead is negligible in practice, the method could be a practical way to reduce bias in SG-MCMC without implementing complex higher-order integrators. The real-world impact hinges on fair, resource-matched comparisons.

Requests for clarification
- How exactly are the correlated Gaussian increments implemented in the parallel setting? Is there per-step inter-process communication, pre-shared RNG streams with deterministic scheduling, or another mechanism? What is the overhead?
- Resource allocation: In MovieLens experiments, did SGLD use the same total compute resources as SGRRLD? If SGRRLD used two cores for two chains, was SGLD allowed two cores (e.g., two independent SGLD chains) or larger mini-batches? Please report core counts, CPU utilization, and any synchronization cost.
- How are mini-batches selected across chains? Are the indices identical, independent, or partially correlated? Does any correlation among mini-batches materially affect performance?
- Tuning: Were step sizes tuned separately per method to optimize each one’s performance? Are results robust to these choices?

Suggestions for additional experiments
- Add a baseline with two independent SGLD chains (same total hardware) and combined predictions; also report a single SGLD chain with double mini-batch size to match gradient cost.
- Report wall-clock breakdown including any synchronization/communication time for the noise coupling.
- Sensitivity to batch size and dimension on real datasets.
- If feasible, include an SGLD variant with a higher-order integrator (analogous to SGHMC-s) to isolate “RR vs. higher-order” on the same base method.
- Explore recursive RR (multi-level) to demonstrate additional gains or practical limits.

Pros
- Simple, elegant technique with strong theory and clear empirical benefits on synthetic and a real task.
- Achieves higher-order accuracy without complex integrators.
- Well-situated in the literature; presentation is generally strong.

Cons
- Novelty is moderate given [10,23].
- Practical overhead and fairness of real-data comparisons need clearer accounting.
- Missing strong parallel baseline (two-chain SGLD).

Overall evaluation
A solid paper with a compelling idea, rigorous analysis, and promising results. Addressing resource fairness and communication overhead, and adding the parallel SGLD baseline, would substantially strengthen the empirical case. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying these points and fixing minor issues.
