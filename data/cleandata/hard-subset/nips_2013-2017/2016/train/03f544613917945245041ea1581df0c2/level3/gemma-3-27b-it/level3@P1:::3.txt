This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to mitigate the inherent bias of existing methods like SGLD. The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique – a well-established numerical analysis method – by running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), and then combining their estimates to achieve a reduced bias of O(γ^2).  The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including proofs of consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on both bias and mean squared error.

The work builds on the recent surge of interest in SG-MCMC methods for large-scale Bayesian inference, citing Welling & Teh (2011) as a foundational contribution.  It acknowledges existing improvements to SGLD (Ahn et al., 2012; Ding et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014) and recent analysis bounding bias and MSE (Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016). The novelty lies in applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation – previously used for improving convergence of deterministic SDE solvers (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) – to the stochastic gradient setting. This combination has not been explored in the machine learning community, representing a significant contribution.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The proposed method is conceptually simple and requires minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC implementations.  This ease of adoption increases its potential for broad impact. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing strong guarantees on the performance of SGRRLD. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the generality of the approach, successfully applying it to both SGLD and Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC). The presentation is exceptionally clear, with careful notation and a logical flow of ideas.

However, some weaknesses and potential areas for improvement exist. The paper states that RR extrapolation can be applied to "virtually all" current SG-MCMC algorithms, but relies on the expansion π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2) holding.  It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the conditions required for this expansion to hold and thus delineate the exact scope of applicability. Similarly, exploration of applicability to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as subsampling Metropolis-Hastings or Distributed SGLD, would strengthen the argument for broad utility.

A practical concern relates to computational efficiency. While the theoretical benefits are clear, running one chain twice as long as the other introduces potential idle time. Exploring alternative strategies, such as running three chains (one with step size γ, and two with step size γ/2, all for the same number of iterations), and analyzing its impact on variance would be valuable.  Additionally, discussing the potential for extending the approach to more than two step sizes is warranted.

The authors address the importance of correlated Brownian increments, linking it to variance control (Lemaire et al., 2015). Clarification is needed regarding the effect of correlating the mini-batches used to estimate the gradient. Is using identical mini-batches optimal, or is there a negligible difference when using independent but identically distributed mini-batches?

The experimental evaluation is convincing, demonstrating the benefits of SGRRLD on both synthetic and real datasets.  However, the matrix factorization experiment would be strengthened by including a standard Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) baseline. This would better contextualize the final errors and highlight the benefits of the Bayesian approach enabled by SGRRLD.  Furthermore, running the experiments to full convergence, rather than fixing a wall-clock time, would provide more conclusive results.  Finally, including a clear pseudocode box early in the paper would greatly enhance its accessibility for practitioners.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The rigorous theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the performance of the algorithm.
*   **Generality:** The method appears broadly applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scope of Applicability:** The limitations on the required conditions for the RR extrapolation to hold should be explicitly stated.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The potential for idle time due to the differing chain lengths should be addressed.
*   **Experimental Setup:** Inclusion of an SGD baseline and convergence studies would strengthen the empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor concerns regarding efficiency and clarity of scope, this is a strong paper with significant potential to advance the field of large-scale Bayesian inference. The novelty of the approach, combined with its strong theoretical foundation and promising empirical results, warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the concerns outlined above.
