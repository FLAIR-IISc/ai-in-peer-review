This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce the bias inherent in methods like SGLD. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation – a numerical sequence acceleration technique – by running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and improved convergence rates compared to SGLD, substantiated with synthetic and real-world experiments.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in SG-MCMC, notably the foundational SGLD algorithm [Welling & Teh, 2011] and subsequent improvements focusing on reducing bias through higher-order integrators [Chen et al., 2014; Ding et al., 2014; Teh et al., 2015]. The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to stochastic differential equations is not new [Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire & Pagès, 2015], but its adaptation to the SG-MCMC context, coupled with the convergence proofs, represents a significant contribution. The work also relates to broader efforts in variance reduction techniques for MCMC [Pages, 2007].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a clever idea, and the theoretical analysis supporting its effectiveness is strong.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees and bounds on bias and MSE. Theorems 1 & 2 are particularly valuable in understanding the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Clarity:** The algorithm is clearly described, and the mathematical notation is generally well-handled. The pseudo-code provided in the supplementary material is a helpful addition.
* **Synthetic Experiments:** The experiments on the linear Gaussian model provide compelling evidence of the bias reduction achieved by SGRRLD, directly supporting the theoretical claims.
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelism of SGRRLD is a significant advantage, making it well-suited for modern computing architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Real-Data Results:** The MovieLens experiments show only a modest improvement in RMSE, and the justification for the improved computational efficiency is somewhat unclear. The performance gain on real data needs stronger demonstration.
* **Conditions on U:** The paper mentions "appropriate conditions on U" for Theorems 1 & 2 but doesn’t explicitly state these conditions.  Specifically, what restrictions are placed on the potential energy function *U*? Can *U* be non-convex or multimodal? This ambiguity limits the applicability of the theoretical results.
* **Gradient Noise Correlation:**  The impact of correlating the Brownian increments (equation 8) is not fully explored. If the gradient noise is truly uncorrelated, how does combining the chains in this manner lead to a reduction in error order?  The authors state this reduces variance, but further explanation of the mechanism would be helpful. Did the authors observe significant correlation between the two chains in the real-data runs?
* **Comparison to Multi-Chain SGLD:** The claim of improved computational efficiency needs to be carefully considered in the context of parallelization. SGLD itself can be run with multiple parallel chains. A direct comparison to SGLD with two parallel chains (and SGLD with a step size of gamma/2) would be crucial to isolate the benefits of the RR extrapolation. This comparison is needed to determine whether the improvements are attributable to the extrapolation or simply to running more samples.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms.
* The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate the bias reduction properties of SGRRLD.
* The work addresses a significant practical challenge in scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets.
* The inherent parallelizability of the algorithm makes it potentially valuable for real-world applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The real-data results are not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate a clear practical advantage.
* The ambiguity surrounding the conditions on *U* limits the generality of the theoretical results.
* The computational efficiency claims are not fully substantiated by a comprehensive comparison to alternative parallelization strategies for SGLD.
* The link between gradient noise correlation and variance reduction could be more thoroughly explained.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the synthetic experiments are convincing. However, the lack of compelling real-data results and the unanswered questions regarding the conditions on *U* and the role of gradient noise correlation raise concerns. I believe the paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to NIPS, but it would benefit significantly from addressing these weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions. The authors should:

1.  **Clarify the conditions on U** required for Theorems 1 and 2.
2.  **Provide a more thorough analysis of the real-data experiments,** including a comparison to multi-chain SGLD and SGLD with a reduced step size.
3.  **Elaborate on the impact of gradient noise correlation** and provide evidence from the experiments to support the claims.
4.  **Address the reviewers’ concern on computation time**, by explicitly comparing SGRRLD's computational cost to SGLD with multiple parallel chains.
