- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains (γ and γ/2) with correlated Brownian increments to cancel bias and control variance.
- Theoretical contributions: CLT for the estimator, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4) with constant step size.
- Improved MSE rate: optimal rate improves from O(K^{-2/3}) for first-order SG-MCMC to O(K^{-4/5}) for K iterations.
- Shows variance is minimized when Brownian increments are perfectly correlated across the two levels.
- Empirical gains: substantial improvements on linear-Gaussian models, extension to SGHMC, and modest consistent RMSE gains on large-scale matrix factorization.
- Novelty: first explicit application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, adapting SDE numerics theory to stochastic-gradient setting.
- Strength: clear, simple idea with strong theoretical backing and proofs adapting ULA results to SG-MCMC.
- Strength: practical to implement (two chains, modest coupling), easy to parallelize so wall-clock cost need not double.
- Strength: general approach applicable to other SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGHMC).
- Weakness: incremental novelty—straightforward application of known extrapolation rather than a new algorithmic paradigm.
- Weakness: strong assumptions (smoothness, g ∈ C^9, Poisson equation solutions) that may not hold for many practical posteriors.
- Weakness: relies on perfect Brownian coupling; unclear practical benefits of sharing minibatches or correlating stochastic gradients.
- Weakness: doubles computation per iteration without parallel hardware; lacks detailed “effective samples per second” comparisons across hardware.
- Weakness: empirical baselines limited; should compare to pSGLD, control-variate SG-MCMC, higher-order integrators, etc.
- Quality: technically sound and carefully reasoned; analyses extend prior ULA and SG-MCMC theory and align with existing variance-coupling results.
- Clarity: well-written and organized, but minor presentation issues (Fig.1a Gaussian fit caption, Fig.2a explanation of non-monotonic bias).
- Originality: moderate — techniques known in SDE numerics, but application + theory for SG-MCMC is novel.
- Significance: good — simple, general technique to improve first-order SG-MCMC accuracy, likely useful when parallel resources are available.
- Suggested experiments: ablation over coupling matrix Σ (Σ=0 vs Σ=Id) to verify variance-minimization prediction.
- Suggested experiments/practice: test reusing minibatches across levels and provide guidance on step-size schedules and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Suggested baselines/metrics: add comparisons to pSGLD and other variants, and report ESS or autocorrelation metrics.
- Pros for acceptance: strong theory, practical idea, clear empirical improvements, bridges SG-MCMC and SDE numerics communities.
- Cons against acceptance: incremental novelty, strong assumptions, limited empirical scope and unclear cost–benefit without parallelism.
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — solid, useful, and well-supported work though not revolutionary.
