

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors extend the RR extrapolation technique, previously used in numerical analysis for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), to the stochastic gradient setting. They demonstrate that by running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining them via RR extrapolation, their method achieves superior convergence rates compared to existing SG-MCMC approaches like SGLD. The paper builds upon the foundational work of Welling & Teh (2011) on SGLD and extends recent theoretical advances in SG-MCMC by Chen et al. (2015), which showed that higher-order integrators can improve convergence rates.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a conceptually simple yet powerful idea that is easy to implement with minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC code. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing asymptotic consistency, central limit theorem guarantees, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds showing SGRRLD achieves O(K^(-4/5)) convergence for MSE compared to SGLD's O(K^(-2/3)). The experimental results on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks demonstrate significant improvements in accuracy with comparable computational time when parallelized. The method's inherent parallelism is well-suited for modern distributed architectures, and the authors correctly note that their technique is generic and applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness concerns the theoretical derivation of the convergence rates. The paper claims that SGRRLD achieves O(K^(-4/5)) MSE convergence, but the derivation from Theorem 2 appears problematic. Specifically, the claim that α=1/5 yields optimal MSE convergence rate (O(K^(-4/5))) conflicts with Figure 3, where the best empirical performance occurs at α=0.2. The "optimal" curve in Figure 3 fluctuates substantially, suggesting the empirical rate may not match the theoretical prediction, and the paper fails to explain this discrepancy. The authors should clarify how the theoretical rates translate to empirical performance.

A significant practical concern is that Figure 5's comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD is unfair. Since SGRRLD requires running two chains (one with twice the number of steps), it costs approximately 3× the computation time of SGLD on a single core. The paper should have scaled the SGLD runtime to 2/3 of the reported time to make a fair comparison, which would substantially reduce the reported performance gap. Without this correction, the claimed efficiency gains are overstated.

The paper also fails to adequately address line 256's claim that the 1/(Kγ) term dominates for small step sizes. The experiments (Figure 2) show that for very small γ, both bias and MSE increase, but the paper doesn't explain why this behavior differs from the theoretical prediction.

Several minor technical issues need addressing: RR is not defined when first mentioned (line 94), Γ_K is used without definition (line 131), the smoothness assumptions for f are vague (line 160), and Figure 2 has a labeling error (line 249). Equation 1 uses a different symbol (ϑ) than the rest of the paper (θ).

**Overall assessment:**
The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising approach that improves upon existing SG-MCMC methods. The core idea is novel and well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis is mostly rigorous. However, the disconnect between the claimed theoretical rates and the empirical results (Figure 3), combined with the unfair runtime comparison in Figure 5, significantly undermines the paper's main contribution. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be accepted.

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The authors should clarify the theoretical derivation of convergence rates, provide a corrected runtime comparison in Figure 5, and explain the discrepancy between theoretical predictions and empirical results in Figure 3. With these revisions, this could be a strong paper for NIPS.
