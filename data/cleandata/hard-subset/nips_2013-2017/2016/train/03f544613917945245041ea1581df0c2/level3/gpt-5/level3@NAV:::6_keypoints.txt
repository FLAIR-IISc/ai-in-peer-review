- Proposal: SGRRLD applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 with carefully correlated Gaussian noise.  
- Theory: Proves asymptotic consistency and a CLT; derives non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Rates: Shows O(γ^2) bias, O(γ^4) variance, and optimal finite-time MSE O(K−4/5), matching higher-order integrator accuracy using first-order discretization.  
- Experiments: Synthetic Gaussian tests validate predicted bias/MSE scaling; matrix factorization case shows better RMSE vs wall-clock time than SGLD.  
- Positioning: Compared to SGLD/SGHMC and prior high-order SG-MCMC analyses [10] and RR extrapolation for invariant measures [23].  
- Quality: Theory is careful and technically solid; analyses extend prior work coherently and align with experiments.  
- Assumptions: Smoothness and Poisson-equation requirements are strong; need discussion of relevance to common nonconvex posteriors.  
- Clarity: Paper is clearly written and well organized with good supplement pointers; minor duplication (“ULA”) should be removed.  
- Originality: Well-executed combination of RR and SG-MCMC with variance control, but conceptual novelty may be incremental given [10] and [23]; sharper contrast needed.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful and easy to implement if wall-clock gains translate to realistic parallel settings.  
- Practicality concern: Two correlated chains require per-step communication/synchronization to share Gaussian noise (and possibly gradients); quantify this cost and report wall-clock with/without communication.  
- Compute accounting: Clarify whether SGLD had equal total compute as two-chain SGRRLD, how cores/threads were split, and effects when gradient computation dominates.  
- Baseline request: Add a baseline of two independent SGLD chains with the same total compute and wall-clock budget, aggregating by averaging, to isolate RR benefit from parallelism.  
- Implementation details request: Explain how correlated Gaussians were generated across processes, whether stochastic gradients were correlated, and how mini-batches were chosen/kept identical.  
- Pros: Strong theory with matching experiments; clear presentation; broadly applicable idea.  
- Cons: Incremental novelty relative to [10,23]; speedups may weaken after accounting for communication; missing parallel baselines and compute accounting.
