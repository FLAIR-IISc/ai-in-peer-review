

This paper proposes a novel application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors run two SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 (with the second chain running twice as many steps), correlate injected Gaussian noise as α_k = (β_{2k-1}+β_{2k})/√2, and subtract estimates to obtain an estimator with O(γ²) bias instead of the standard O(γ) bias. The paper provides formal theoretical analysis showing reduced bias and MSE, with convergence rates of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).

The work is highly original in applying a well-established numerical analysis technique to the ML community, where it has not been widely adopted. The method is simple to implement with minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC algorithms, making it broadly applicable and potentially impactful across the field. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with strong experimental validation on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task showing significant performance improvements.

However, several important questions require clarification. The applicability of the technique to all SG-MCMC methods is not fully established—can it be applied to methods not satisfying π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ²)? The computational efficiency concern is significant: running one chain twice as long wastes compute. A three-chain approach (one at γ, two at γ/2 running equal steps) might better utilize resources, but the paper doesn't analyze how this would affect variance. The noise correlation details need further clarification—specifically, whether using the same minibatch for both chains is optimal.

The matrix factorization experiment would be strengthened by including an SGD baseline to demonstrate Bayesian benefits and running to convergence to contextualize the final errors. A pseudocode box early in the paper would help practitioners implement the method without delving into theory.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a significant limitation in SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the experimental results convincingly support the claims. The minor concerns raised are important for full adoption but don't detract from the paper's substantial technical contribution. The work represents a valuable step toward more efficient Bayesian inference in large-scale settings.
