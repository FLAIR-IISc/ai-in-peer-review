This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, termed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in popular methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical technique for accelerating sequence convergence, to the SGLD estimator. This is achieved by running two SGLD chains in parallel: one with a step size sequence `(γ_k)` and another with a finer step size sequence (`γ_k/2`, applied twice per step of the first chain to cover the same time horizon). The final estimator is a linear combination of the outputs from these two chains, constructed to cancel the leading `O(γ)` bias term. This approach builds upon the extensive literature on SG-MCMC (e.g., Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014) and connects it with prior work on RR extrapolation for SDEs (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate for the MSE (`O(K^{-4/5})`) than SGLD (`O(K^{-2/3})`). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant contribution. While SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation are established techniques in their respective fields, their combination to tackle the bias problem in scalable Bayesian inference is new and well-motivated. The resulting algorithm is simple to understand and implement.

2.  **Technical Quality (Theory):** The paper is supported by a strong theoretical analysis. The authors provide a Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 1) that characterizes the asymptotic behavior of the estimator and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These results formally prove that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence than first-order methods like SGLD, providing a solid theoretical justification for the algorithm's improved efficiency. This analysis is non-trivial and represents a significant contribution.

3.  **Technical Quality (Experiments):** The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model clearly illustrate the bias reduction and empirically validate the theoretical convergence rates with respect to both the step size `γ` and the number of iterations `K`. The application to large-scale matrix factorization on three real-world datasets demonstrates the practical utility of SGRRLD, showing that it can achieve better prediction accuracy than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time.

4.  **Practicality and Generality:** A key practical advantage of SGRRLD is that the two required chains can be run in a completely parallel fashion with no communication overhead. This makes the method ideally suited for modern multi-core architectures, effectively mitigating the twofold increase in computation. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the generality of the approach by applying it to Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC), suggesting it can be a valuable tool for improving a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The paper claims that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. While this is supported by the theoretical rates, the experimental comparison could be stronger. The SGHMC experiment (Figure 4) compares SGRRHMC to a higher-order splitting integrator (SGHMC-s), which is a valuable inclusion. However, a similar direct comparison for the paper's main algorithm, SGRRLD, against an SGLD variant using a higher-order integrator (e.g., from Chen et al., NIPS 2015, Ref [10]) would have been more direct and would have made the claims about comparative performance more compelling.

2.  **Clarity on Computational Cost:** While the wall-clock time improvements are impressive due to parallelism, the total computational cost (e.g., number of gradient evaluations) of SGRRLD is roughly double that of SGLD for the same number of effective steps `K`. The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of this trade-off between statistical efficiency (faster convergence in `K`) and computational cost per step. The current justification rests heavily on the availability of parallel hardware, which, while common, is not universal.

3.  **Minor Inconsistencies:**
    *   There appears to be a typo in the first update equation for Chain 2 in (5). The gradient `∇Ũ(γ/2)2k+1` seems to be evaluated at `θ(γ/2)2k+1`, whereas the Euler-Maruyama scheme would typically evaluate it at the beginning of the step, `θ(γ/2)2k`. The authors should verify if this is intended or a typographical error.
    *   The provided key points note a potential inconsistency between Equation (5) and Algorithm 1 in the supplement. The authors are encouraged to double-check their supplementary material for consistency with the main paper.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  Novel and elegant idea of using RR extrapolation for bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
    2.  Rigorous theoretical analysis proving faster convergence rates than SGLD.
    3.  Convincing empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world problems.
    4.  The method is practical, highly parallelizable, and generalizable to other samplers.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  The experimental comparison against alternative high-accuracy methods (higher-order integrators) could be more comprehensive.
    2.  The paper contains a few minor clarity issues and potential typos that should be corrected.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed method is simple, well-motivated, and supported by strong theoretical and empirical evidence. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. The paper is well-written, and its results are likely to be of interest and use to both researchers and practitioners working with SG-MCMC methods. I recommend acceptance.
