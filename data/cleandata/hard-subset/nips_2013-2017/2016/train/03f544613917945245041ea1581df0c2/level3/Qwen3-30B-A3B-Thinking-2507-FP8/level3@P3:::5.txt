

# Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes SG-RR-MCMC, a novel approach to reduce bias in stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The key insight is applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two parallel chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, then combining results through extrapolation. The authors focus on applying this to SGLD, resulting in SGRRLD, and provide theoretical analysis showing improved convergence rates.

The work builds on Welling and Teh's (2011) SGLD framework and Talay and Tubaro's (1990) RR extrapolation for SDEs, but applies them in a novel way to the stochastic gradient setting. It relates to Chen et al. (2015), who showed higher-order integrators improve convergence rates, by demonstrating that their approach achieves similar accuracy using only first-order integrators.

**Strengths:**
1. The method is conceptually simple yet effective, combining two well-known techniques in a novel way.
2. Theoretical analysis is strong, showing SGRRLD achieves MSE rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³).
3. The embarrassingly parallel nature makes it well-suited for distributed computing environments.
4. Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets (including large-scale matrix factorization) support the theoretical claims.
5. The approach is general and could be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.

**Weaknesses:**
1. An inconsistent statement about the step size appears in Section 1 ("the step size of the Euler-Maruyama integrator") versus Theorem 2.
2. Algorithm 1 in the supplement is inconsistent with Equation (5) in the main text.
3. The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators" but lacks experimental comparison with these methods.
4. While suggesting similar improved rates could be obtained with high-order integrators, the paper doesn't provide experimental validation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical claims supported by references to supplementary proofs. The empirical results are convincing but would be strengthened by comparing with higher-order integrators. The algorithm inconsistencies are concerning for reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the algorithm inconsistencies and unclear step size statements reduce clarity. The supplementary material is necessary for full understanding.

**Originality:** The combination of RR extrapolation with SG-MCMC represents a novel contribution that effectively combines known techniques. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature.

**Significance:** The method provides a practical way to improve convergence rates without implementing complex higher-order integrators. The embarrassingly parallel nature is particularly valuable for large-scale applications, making it a significant contribution to scalable Bayesian inference.

**Pro Acceptance:** Novel and simple idea that effectively combines known techniques; strong theoretical analysis with improved convergence rates; empirical results support theoretical claims; embarrassingly parallel architecture; general applicability to multiple SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Con Acceptance:** Algorithm inconsistencies; lack of experimental comparison with higher-order integrators; theoretical improvement needs clearer connection to computational efficiency.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The main weakness is the lack of experimental comparison with higher-order integrators, which would better validate the claim about achieving their theoretical accuracy. The inconsistencies in the algorithm description are concerning but likely represent typographical errors that could be fixed in revision.

The paper makes a valuable addition to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference. The method offers a simple yet effective way to improve convergence rates while maintaining computational efficiency. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are convincing. The authors have demonstrated a significant technical contribution that is likely to be valuable for practitioners working on large-scale Bayesian inference.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the algorithm inconsistencies and clarify step size statements. The authors should also consider adding a comparison with higher-order integrators in future work to further validate their claims.
