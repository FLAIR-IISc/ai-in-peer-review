## Review of Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo

This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing SG-MCMC methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation – a well-established technique in numerical analysis – by running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates, supported by experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

The motivation for this work is clear and important. SG-MCMC methods have become increasingly popular for Bayesian inference in large-scale applications, but are known to suffer from bias. Reducing this bias is critical for obtaining accurate posterior estimates. The proposed approach is conceptually simple and elegant, building upon existing SGLD algorithms with minimal modification. This ease of implementation is a significant strength, making the method potentially accessible to a wide range of researchers and practitioners. The theoretical results – establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error – are rigorous and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method. Importantly, the paper highlights that SGRRLD can achieve accuracy comparable to methods utilizing higher-order integrators while relying solely on a first-order numerical scheme.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions of the work. The theoretical derivations, while dense, are presented in a logical manner, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.  Including a pseudocode box early in the paper, as suggested, would further enhance accessibility for practitioners interested in implementation.

The novelty of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a key strength. While the Richardson-Romberg technique is well-known in numerical analysis, its application within the context of Bayesian inference and large-scale data is, to the best of my knowledge, novel and represents a significant contribution.  The authors correctly position this work within the broader landscape of SG-MCMC algorithms, referencing relevant literature and clearly articulating how SGRRLD differs from existing approaches. 

However, a few points require further clarification. The applicability of the technique isn’t fully explored. The paper relies on the expansion  π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2). It's not immediately clear whether SGRRLD can be applied effectively to *all* SG-MCMC algorithms, or only those satisfying this condition.  Expanding on this limitation would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the authors should discuss whether this extrapolation approach could be beneficial for other approximate MCMC schemes beyond SGLD (e.g., subsampling Metropolis-Hastings) or distributed SGLD. 

A potential computational inefficiency is the need to run one chain twice as long as the other. While the authors acknowledge the parallelizability, the inherent idle time of one chain while the other completes its additional steps could be a limiting factor. Investigating whether using three chains (one with step size γ, two with step size γ/2, run for equal iterations) could mitigate this issue without significantly impacting variance would be valuable.  Similarly, exploring the possibility of extending the method to utilize more than two step sizes could potentially lead to further improvements in convergence rates.

Clarification is also needed regarding the correlation of injected Gaussian noise. The paper states a specific relationship between the Brownian increments, but the impact of correlating the *minibatches* used for gradient estimation is not thoroughly addressed. Is using the same minibatch for both chains optimal, or is there a negligible effect?  Addressing this question could refine the practical implementation of SGRRLD.

The experimental evaluation is generally strong, but the matrix factorization experiment could be improved.  Running the experiment to convergence and including an SGD baseline would allow for a more direct comparison and demonstrate the benefits of the Bayesian approach more convincingly. The current presentation focuses on RMSE, but reporting other relevant metrics (e.g., log-likelihood) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees regarding convergence rates and error bounds.
*   Clear presentation and well-motivated problem statement.
*   Potential for broad impact across various SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of applicability to all SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   Potential computational inefficiency due to unequal chain lengths.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of minibatch correlation.
*   Matrix factorization experiment could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the points raised regarding applicability, computational efficiency, and minibatch correlation in a revised version.
