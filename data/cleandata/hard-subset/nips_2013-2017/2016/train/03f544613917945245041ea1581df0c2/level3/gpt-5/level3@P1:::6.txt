This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC to reduce discretization bias while keeping variance controlled. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2, and then form a bias-reduced estimator via RR combination. Crucially, the authors correlate the Gaussian noises across chains (ideally with perfect correlation via Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2), which they show minimizes the asymptotic variance of the RR estimator. They provide asymptotic and non-asymptotic analysis: SGRRLD is consistent, satisfies a CLT, and achieves bias O(γ2) and MSE O(γ4) with constant steps, translating to a finite-time MSE rate O(K−4/5) (optimal α = 1/5 for γk ∝ k−α), improving upon first-order SG-MCMC methods such as SGLD (O(K−2/3)). The approach is generic and extends to SGHMC. Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model confirm the predicted bias/MSE scalings and on MovieLens matrix factorization demonstrate wall-clock improvements over SGLD; an SGHMC variant shows parity with higher-order integrators using only first-order updates.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on SGLD [Welling & Teh ’11], the general theory of SG-MCMC and higher-order integrators [Ma et al. ’15; Chen et al. ’15], and RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant distributions [Talay & Tubaro ’90; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup ’15]. The contribution adapts the RR idea to the SG-MCMC setting with stochastic gradients and shows how to couple the noise to retain variance control in this stochastic-gradient regime.

Quality:
- Theory: The CLT (with smoothness/Poisson equation assumptions) and non-asymptotic bounds are carefully stated and match derivations in the SDE literature adapted to SG noise. The rate improvements (O(K−4/5) vs O(K−2/3)) are consistent, and the role of correlated Brownian increments is clearly analyzed.
- Assumptions: The smoothness requirements (C9 for the Poisson solution) and technical conditions on U and L are strong but standard for this line of analysis; it would help to discuss robustness when gradients are only Lipschitz/smooth of lower order and under heavy-tailed gradient noise.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments convincingly validate the predicted scalings and the need to balance γ and K (e.g., 1/(Kγ) term). Real-data experiments are compelling, though some fairness and resource questions remain (see below).

Clarity:
- The presentation is clear, well-organized, and relates the method to prior work appropriately. The role of correlated noise and the requirement to use the same minibatch size distribution across chains are well explained. Minor typos: “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is defined twice (lines 25 and 70).

Originality:
- The combination of RR extrapolation with SG-MCMC and the analysis of variance control via correlated Brownian increments in the stochastic-gradient setting is novel and useful. However, RR for invariant measures and variance control via coupling are known in the exact-gradient setting [23], and higher-order behavior for SG-MCMC via better integrators is known [10]. The contribution is therefore an incremental but meaningful adaptation, with new finite-time analysis and an implementable algorithmic recipe.

Significance:
- Reducing bias without resorting to higher-order integrators is valuable, especially when first-order methods are simpler to implement. The theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements can influence practice, particularly if parallel resources are available. The technique is generic and may transfer to other SG-MCMC samplers.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification:
- Computational budget fairness: SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel; the paper states wall-clock times are “nearly exactly the same” as SGLD by using two processes. This is a resource advantage that SGLD cannot exploit if restricted to one process. Please report baselines that match computational resources:
  - Two independent SGLD chains run in parallel on the same two processes, with their estimates combined (e.g., by averaging functionals or via RR-style combination without coupling) to illustrate how much of the gain stems from parallelism vs bias reduction.
  - SGLD with twice as many iterations (or twice the gradient evaluations) when parallelism is not allowed.
- Communication/coupling overhead: The correlated-noise construction may require per-iteration synchronization across processes (sharing Z blocks or common RNG seeds) and, if minibatch coupling is used, sharing indices. Please quantify this overhead for high-dimensional parameters and report whether the two processes share minibatches or use independent ones. If the gradient estimators are correlated (e.g., same minibatch in both chains), that should be specified and ablated.
- Sensitivity to step-size tuning: The theory recommends α = 1/5 for MSE and α = 1/3 for bias; experiments largely fix constant γ. Please include decreasing-step experiments on real data and discuss practical tuning guidelines.
- Comparison to higher-order SGHMC baselines: The SGHMC experiment is helpful; including a similar comparison for SGLD vs a second-order integrator baseline (if available) would strengthen the claim that SGRRLD matches higher-order accuracy at first-order cost.
- Scope and limitations: Discuss cases where strong convexity or dissipativity fail, and whether the RR gains persist in multimodal/posterior heavy-tailed settings.

Suggestions to improve the manuscript:
- Add ablations: (i) no correlation vs perfect correlation of Gaussian noise; (ii) shared vs independent minibatches across chains; (iii) effect of running two independent SGLD chains in parallel.
- Clarify implementation details for distributed settings: how random numbers are synchronized; whether minibatches are coordinated; exact communication costs.
- Report results matching total gradient computations and/or total cores used.
- Fix the minor duplication of the ULA definition and any small notational slips.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contribution adapting RR to SG-MCMC with clear variance control via coupling and improved finite-time and asymptotic rates.
- Simple, general, and practical algorithmic idea that can wrap around existing SG-MCMC samplers.
- Empirical results corroborate theory on synthetic data and show meaningful wall-clock gains on large-scale matrix factorization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior RR and higher-order SG-MCMC work.
- Fairness of empirical comparisons is unclear without resource-matched baselines and communication overhead accounting.
- Strong smoothness/regularity assumptions may limit applicability; practical tuning guidance is limited.

Overall: A well-executed and timely contribution that meaningfully improves first-order SG-MCMC via a simple extrapolation scheme. With clearer experimental fairness and additional baselines/ablations, the paper would be strong. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the resource and coupling clarifications.
