This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC estimator that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of SGLD-like samplers while controlling variance via correlated noise. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains targeting the same posterior but with step sizes γ and γ/2, where the γ/2 chain advances twice as often so both chains reach the same time horizon. The estimator forms a weighted combination of the two chains’ f-values that cancels the O(γ) weak-discretization error, yielding O(γ^2) bias. To avoid variance blow-up, the injected Gaussian noises are coupled as Zγ,k = (Zγ/2,2k−1 + Zγ/2,2k)/√2, which is shown to be optimal among a broader correlation family. The authors establish consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds: with constant step size, bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)); with decreasing step size γk ∝ k−α, the optimal asymptotic MSE rate is O(K−4/5) at α = 1/5 (vs O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC). Empirically, the method improves bias/MSE on a Gaussian model and matches or beats SGHMC and a higher-order splitting variant when the RR idea is applied to SGHMC; on large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens), SGRRLD achieves better RMSE than SGLD under equal wall-clock by running the two chains in parallel.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects Talay–Tubaro RR extrapolation and weak-error expansions for SDE discretizations to the SG-MCMC setting with stochastic gradients (Roberts & Tweedie; Lamberton & Pagès; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup). It complements recent non-/asymptotic analyses of SGLD and SG-MCMC with high-order integrators (Teh–Thiéry–Vollmer; Chen–Ding–Carin; Ma–Chen–Fox) by showing that RR with a first-order integrator can match the accuracy of higher-order schemes, provided the noise is appropriately coupled.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically solid. Assumptions (existence of a sufficiently smooth Poisson solution, polynomial growth, ergodicity, and a weak-error expansion) are standard in diffusion-based analyses yet relatively strong for complex posteriors; the paper is honest about technical conditions and defers proofs to the supplement. The CLT clearly identifies the optimal noise coupling, and the non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds connect cleanly to practical step-size choices. One point to emphasize more explicitly is the requirement that both chains use gradient estimators with the same law (e.g., identical mini-batch size); otherwise the RR bias cancellation can be lost.

Clarity: The paper is well written and structured. The estimator definition, correlation scheme, and rate statements are clear. A self-contained algorithm box early in the main text (including the noise coupling and step scheduling) would improve accessibility and reproducibility.

Originality: Bringing RR extrapolation into SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients and proving that correlated Brownian increments control variance is, to my knowledge, novel within the ML literature. While RR is classical in numerical SDEs, adapting it to noisy-gradient samplers and providing finite-time bounds and CLTs is a meaningful contribution.

Significance: The idea is simple, broadly applicable, and potentially impactful: it can be layered atop many SG-MCMC variants (SGLD, SGHMC, thermostats, preconditioned methods), and it parallelizes naturally. The improved finite-time and asymptotic rates make it attractive in large-scale Bayesian inference.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, generic technique with minimal code changes; practical guidance on optimal noise coupling.
- Theoretical guarantees: consistency, CLT, and improved bias/MSE rates (O(γ^2), O(K−4/5)).
- Demonstrated applicability beyond SGLD (SGHMC) with competitive performance vs higher-order integrators.
- Empirical benefits on both controlled synthetic tasks and large-scale matrix factorization under equal wall-clock.

Cons / questions:
- Scope of applicability: RR requires a weak-error expansion πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ^2). Please clarify classes of SG-MCMC (e.g., preconditioned, Riemannian, thermostat-based) for which this holds and provide concrete sufficient conditions beyond “mild assumptions.”
- Gradient-noise correlation: The theory shows optimality for correlated Brownian increments. What about correlating mini-batches across chains (e.g., sharing indices) or across the two fine steps? Is using identical mini-batches beneficial, neutral, or harmful? Practical guidance would help.
- Computational efficiency: With limited hardware, SGRRLD effectively computes three gradient evaluations per coarse “time unit.” The experiments mask this by parallelizing the two chains; please quantify total gradient evaluations and discuss the trade-off between wall-clock and total compute. Could one run three chains (one γ, two γ/2) to avoid idling and does this change variance?
- Extensions: The paper mentions recursive RR and multilevel ideas. A brief experiment with a three-level RR or a discussion of stability/variance in multi-level SG-MCMC would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental scope: The matrix factorization results are compelling. To contextualize performance, consider adding: (i) an SGD/ALS optimization baseline to show the advantage of Bayesian sampling on predictive RMSE or uncertainty metrics; (ii) an SG-MCMC with a known higher-order integrator for SGLD (e.g., Ma–Chen–Fox or Chen–Ding–Carin) to corroborate the “matches higher-order” claim in the Langevin setting; (iii) diagnostic measures (ESS/time, bias of simple posterior moments) and sensitivity to step sizes.
- Practical details: Make explicit that both chains must use the same mini-batch size distribution; clarify whether the two fine steps reuse data; and include guidance on selecting Σ (state that Σ=Id yields the optimal coupling used in experiments).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include a main-text pseudocode of SGRRLD (inputs, step-size scheduling, noise coupling, estimator computation).
- Highlight the precise conditions ensuring the O(γ) weak expansion (e.g., smoothness/convexity, dissipativity), and note cases where it may fail (heavy tails, non-smooth posteriors).
- Discuss applicability to other approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling MH, distributed/asynchronous SGLD) and whether RR can be combined with communication-efficient variance reduction.
- Provide practical step-size recipes derived from Theorem 2 (γ ∝ K−1/5 for MSE, γ ∝ K−1/3 for bias) and note when constant vs decreasing schedules are preferable.

Overall, this is a strong paper: the idea is elegant, theoretically grounded, and empirically promising. With clarifications on applicability and practical guidance on data/gradient coupling and compute trade-offs, it would make a valuable contribution to SG-MCMC methodology.
