This paper presents a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias of existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to leverage the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique – a well-known method for accelerating numerical sequences – by running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Brownian motion. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error. Experimental validation on synthetic and real (matrix factorization) datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of SGRRLD, achieving faster convergence rates and improved accuracy compared to SGLD and, in one experiment, SGHMC.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It effectively situates SGRRLD within the broader context of SG-MCMC methods, building upon prior work such as Welling & Teh (2011) and Chen et al. (2014). The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and is supported by comprehensive experiments. The authors also demonstrate a good awareness of related literature, referencing key papers on Langevin dynamics, numerical integration, and SG-MCMC convergence (e.g., Roberts & Tweedie, 1996; Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016). The inclusion of a pseudo-code for SGRRLD is a valuable addition.

The strengths of this work lie in its sound theoretical foundation and the potentially significant practical benefits. Reducing the bias of SG-MCMC algorithms is crucial for reliable Bayesian inference in large-scale applications, and the proposed approach offers a principled way to achieve this. The experimental results, particularly on the matrix factorization task, are compelling.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC appears novel, the incrementality of the contribution is a concern. The authors acknowledge related work on improving convergence through higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015 [10]) and the use of RR extrapolation in related contexts (Lemaire et al., 2015 [23]). The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how SGRRLD specifically advances beyond these prior efforts.

A significant practical consideration, not fully addressed, is the communication overhead inherent in maintaining correlated Brownian motions across the two parallel chains. The authors state that SGRRLD is well-suited for parallel architectures, but the cost of synchronizing these chains – particularly the requirement for correlated increments as defined in equation (8) – is not discussed. This is a critical factor, as generating correlated random numbers can be computationally expensive and require communication between processing units. The experimental setup raises concerns in this regard. It is stated that the two chains of SGRRLD are run in parallel, leading to similar wall-clock times as SGLD. However, if SGLD is running on a single core while SGRRLD is utilizing two, this comparison is not entirely fair. The gradient computation cost is the same, but the overhead associated with correlation introduces an additional computation that needs justification.

To clarify this, I request the authors to provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup. Specifically, how were the computational resources allocated between SGLD and SGRRLD? Were both algorithms allowed to utilize all available cores, or was SGLD constrained to a single core? The authors should also discuss the impact of the correlation requirement on wall-clock time. An additional, valuable baseline would be to compare SGRRLD to two *independent* SGLD chains running in parallel ("embarrassingly parallel" SGLD). This would help isolate the benefit specifically derived from the correlation between the chains.

A minor point is the duplication of the definition of “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” on lines 25 and 70.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating improved performance over SGLD on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in Bayesian inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential for incremental novelty given existing work on higher-order integrators and RR extrapolation.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the practical communication costs associated with maintaining correlated chains.
*   Experimental setup requires clarification regarding resource allocation and fairness of comparison.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While concerns remain regarding the practical implications of the correlation requirement and the incrementality of the novelty, the strong theoretical foundation and promising experimental results warrant serious consideration. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the points raised regarding experimental setup, communication costs, and providing a more detailed discussion of related work. A clarification of these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
