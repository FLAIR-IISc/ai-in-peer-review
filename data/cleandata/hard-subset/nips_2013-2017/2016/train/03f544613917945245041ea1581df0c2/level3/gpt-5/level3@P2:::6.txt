Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, instantiated primarily with SGLD. The method runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes, γ and γ/2, along a matched time horizon, and forms an extrapolated estimator of expectations: a linear combination of function evaluations from the two chains. To control variance, the chains use correlated Gaussian increments; the choice Σ = Id (i.e., Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2) is shown to minimize the asymptotic variance. The paper establishes consistency, a CLT with explicit bias/variance structure, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. With appropriate step-size schedules, SGRRLD achieves improved rates over SGLD: asymptotically O(K−2/5) for the error of the averaged estimator (optimal α = 1/5 for γk ∝ k−α) and non-asymptotically O(K−4/5) for MSE under constant step size with optimal tuning γ ∝ K−1/5, versus O(K−1/3) and O(K−2/3) for SGLD. Experiments on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization show lower bias/MSE and wall-clock RMSE gains; an SGHMC instantiation (SGRRHMC) also improves over SGHMC, approaching the accuracy of high-order splitting integrators.

The contribution is well situated relative to: (i) Talay–Tubaro expansions and RR for SDE weak error and invariant measures (e.g., Lemaire et al., 2015; Talay & Tubaro, 1990), and (ii) recent high-order integrator SG-MCMC analyses (Chen et al., NIPS 2015). The novelty lies in adapting RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, proving that the Gaussian noise dominates the gradient noise in the asymptotics so that RR still yields higher-order weak accuracy, and showing how correlation across chains controls variance.

Quality
- Theory: The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds are clearly stated and consistent with known weak error expansions. The identification of Σ = Id as variance-minimizing and the rate calculations are convincing. However, the assumptions are strong (e.g., existence of a C9 solution to the Poisson equation, smooth U, polynomial growth), which may exclude common nonsmooth or heavy-tailed posteriors. Please discuss the practical scope of these assumptions and any relaxations (e.g., locally Lipschitz drifts with dissipativity).
- Algorithmic details: The variance control via correlated Brownian increments is central; implementation details for ensuring perfect correlation when running two processes in parallel would help. Also, in Eq. (5) the first substep appears to use ∇Ũ at θ(γ/2)2k+1 (implicit), which seems inconsistent with Euler–Maruyama; likely it should be evaluated at θ(γ/2)2k. Please clarify/correct.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments align with the theory (bias ∼ O(γ2), MSE gains). The matrix factorization study shows wall-clock improvements. However, fairness and resource accounting need clarification (see below).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with solid motivation and connections to prior work. The estimator definition and coupling choice are clear. Suggestions:
- Clarify the computational model: whether SGRRLD’s two chains run on two cores/nodes and whether SGLD is restricted to one core; detail communication/synchronization cost to enforce correlated noise and any sharing of minibatches.
- Explicitly state how the extrapolated estimator is used in downstream metrics (e.g., test RMSE): are predictive means computed via the RR estimator, or via one of the chains?
- Fix minor typos (see below).

Originality
Moderate. RR extrapolation and high-order weak error ideas are classical, and their use for invariant measures of SDEs has precedent. The main novelty is to show that RR carries over to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, with variance controlled via coupling, and to provide non-asymptotic bounds and empirical validation. While conceptually incremental, the adaptation is nontrivial and useful.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a simple, general wrapper applicable to SGLD/SGHMC that achieves higher-order accuracy using only first-order integrators, with straightforward parallelization. Theoretical guarantees and practical gains on a large-scale task strengthen the case. Limitations: (i) requires two chains/resources; (ii) primarily benefits expectation estimation rather than generating improved-quality samples for all downstream tasks (e.g., quantiles); (iii) assumptions may limit applicability in modern deep models.

Pros
- Elegant, simple technique; easy to implement and parallelize.
- Solid theoretical analysis: consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds; clear rates and optimal schedules.
- Demonstrated improvements on synthetic and real large-scale matrix factorization; extension to SGHMC.
- Insightful variance control via correlated Brownian increments; guidance on minibatch choices.

Cons
- Incremental novelty relative to known RR and high-order SG-MCMC literature.
- Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions; unclear applicability to nonsmooth or heavy-tailed posteriors.
- Experimental fairness questions: two-chain method vs single-chain SGLD under equal compute budget; unreported communication/synchronization overhead for coupling.
- Missing baselines: averaging two independent SGLD chains with the same total gradient budget; pSGLD or other improved SGLD variants.
- Extrapolated estimator targets expectations; guidance for tasks needing samples (e.g., predictive intervals) is limited.

Requests for clarification/additional experiments
1) Compute model: Precisely describe hardware, cores, and how resources were allocated. Was SGLD allowed to use both cores if SGRRLD used two? If run on a single core, how do wall-clock comparisons change?
2) Coupling cost: How were perfectly correlated Gaussian increments enforced across processes? Is there per-iteration communication? If so, quantify overhead.
3) Baselines: Compare against (a) two independent SGLD chains with the same aggregate gradient evaluations and averaged estimates; (b) pSGLD or other preconditioned/variance-reduced SG-MCMC; (c) an SGLD variant with a higher-order integrator (as you did for SGHMC).
4) Minibatch coupling: Did you share minibatch indices across the two chains? If yes, what is the impact; if no, please empirically explore independent vs shared minibatches, as theory emphasizes Gaussian coupling but practice may benefit from gradient coupling too.
5) Scope: Discuss the behavior when U is nonsmooth (e.g., piecewise smooth likelihoods) or heavy-tailed priors; can the analysis be extended?
6) Use in prediction: For matrix factorization, how is the RR estimator used to compute test RMSE? Do benefits persist for predictive uncertainty (intervals/quantiles)?

Minor comments
- “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” appears twice; remove duplication.
- Eq. (5) likely has an implicit gradient evaluation; please correct to explicit Euler.
- A brief pseudo-code in the main text (not only in the supplement) would aid clarity.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a clean, theoretically grounded technique that offers tangible improvements with modest implementation effort. Addressing the fairness/resource and coupling-overhead concerns and adding the suggested baselines would strengthen the contribution.

Confidence
Medium.
