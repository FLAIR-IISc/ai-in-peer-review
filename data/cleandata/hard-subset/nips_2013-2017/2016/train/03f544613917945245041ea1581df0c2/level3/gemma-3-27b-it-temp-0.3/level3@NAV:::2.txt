## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel SG-MCMC algorithm leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in gradient estimation. The core idea – running two SGLD chains in parallel with differing step sizes and combining the results – is well-motivated, building upon established numerical analysis techniques for SDEs and extending them to the SG-MCMC setting. The authors correctly identify the bias inherent in SGLD and propose a practical approach to mitigate it. The algorithm itself is clearly described and appears relatively straightforward to implement. 

The theoretical contributions are significant. Proving asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem for SGRRLD, alongside bounding its bias and MSE, provides a strong foundation for understanding its behavior. The claim of faster convergence than SGLD, while dependent on specific conditions, is a compelling result. The synthetic experiments with a linear Gaussian model convincingly demonstrate the bias-reduction advantage of SGRRLD.

However, the practical significance remains questionable. The MovieLens experiment shows only a marginal RMSE improvement, and the authors acknowledge the results are arguable. This is a critical weakness. While the theory suggests benefits, the real-data results don't strongly support them. The authors need to provide more robust evidence of practical gains on more diverse datasets.

Several points require clarification. The conditions on *U* for Theorems 1 & 2 are not fully specified. Specifically, can *U* be multimodal? This is crucial for assessing the algorithm’s applicability to complex Bayesian models. The authors mention the potential for chain decorrelation if gradient noises are uncorrelated, but don’t fully address how combining chains reduces the error order in this scenario. Observing and reporting the correlation between the two chains in the real-data runs would be valuable. 

Furthermore, the claim of comparable computation time to SGLD is potentially misleading. While SGRRLD’s parallelization *cost* is similar, SGLD can also be run with multiple parallel chains. A direct comparison to multi-chain SGLD (e.g., SGLD with two chains and a step size of gamma/2) is essential to isolate the benefits of the RR extrapolation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence proofs.
*   Clear algorithm description and implementation potential.
*   Demonstrated bias reduction in synthetic experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak evidence of practical benefit on real-world data.
*   Unclear conditions on *U* for theoretical guarantees.
*   Lack of comparison to multi-chain SGLD.
*   Insufficient discussion of gradient noise correlation.



**Overall:** I rate this paper as **weak accept**. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the lack of compelling real-world results and the unanswered questions regarding practical implementation and performance necessitate further investigation. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised and providing more substantial evidence of SGRRLD’s practical advantages.
