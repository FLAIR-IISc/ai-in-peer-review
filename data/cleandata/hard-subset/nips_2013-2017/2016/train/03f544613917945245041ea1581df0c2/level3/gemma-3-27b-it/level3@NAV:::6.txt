## Review of “Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo”

This paper proposes a novel acceleration technique, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), for SG-MCMC algorithms, specifically SGLD. The core idea is to leverage Richardson-Romberg extrapolation by running two parallel chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise, effectively reducing bias. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. Experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data (matrix factorization) validate these theoretical findings, showing practical gains over standard SGLD.

The work is technically sound and well-presented. The authors clearly situate SGRRLD within the existing literature on SG-MCMC, acknowledging relevant prior work on higher-order integrators [10] and RR extrapolation for invariant distributions [23]. The convergence analysis is comprehensive and the experimental design appears thoughtful. The extension of RR extrapolation to SGHMC further demonstrates the generality of the proposed approach.

However, the novelty of the technique feels incremental. While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a valuable contribution, the core concept of utilizing higher-order numerical integration schemes to improve SG-MCMC convergence has been explored previously. A major concern is the practical applicability of SGRRLD. Running two correlated chains necessitates per-step communication, which can become a significant bottleneck, especially at scale. This communication cost is not considered or discussed in the paper, leaving open the question of whether the gains from reduced bias outweigh the communication overhead. 

Regarding the experiments, clarification is needed on the resource allocation. Were the two chains of SGRRLD allocated the same compute resources as a single SGLD chain? How were resources split, and how does the potentially partial allocation per chain affect wall-clock time, given the gradient computation cost? A strong baseline comparison would also be to two *independent* (embarrassingly parallel) SGLD chains, to disentangle the benefit from correlation versus the benefit from parallelization. 

Minor note: the phrase “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is duplicated on lines 25 and 70.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis.
*   Clear presentation and well-defined problem statement.
*   Positive empirical results demonstrating performance gains.
*   Generality demonstrated by applying the technique to SGHMC.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty given prior work.
*   Practical concerns regarding communication cost.
*   Insufficient detail on experimental setup and resource allocation.



Overall, this is a solid contribution. However, addressing the concerns regarding practical scalability and providing a more compelling novelty argument would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these points.
