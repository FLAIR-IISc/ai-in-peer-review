- Paper addresses O(γ) asymptotic bias of first-order SG-MCMC by applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to cancel the leading weak error term.  
- Method: run two coupled chains (step γ and two steps γ/2), correlate Gaussian noise via Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2, and form an extrapolated estimator with O(γ^2) bias.  
- Theoretical results: prove O(γ^2) bias, O(γ^4) variance for the stationary estimator, a CLT (optimality of full Gaussian coupling), and finite-time MSE bounds improving rates to O(K−4/5).  
- Empirics: synthetic Gaussian, SGHMC, and large-scale matrix factorization experiments show consistent improvements and parity with higher-order integrators at larger step sizes.  
- Relation to prior work: connects SGLD analyses, higher-order SG-MCMC integrators, and classical RR/SDE discretization literature; explicit RR use for SG-MCMC appears novel.  

Strengths:
- Simple, practical implementation requiring minimal code changes (two coupled chains and shared-noise construction).  
- Solid theoretical backing with CLT and finite-time guarantees; shows variance control via optimal coupling.  
- General framework applicable beyond SGLD (demonstrated for SGHMC) and likely extensible across SG-MCMC variants.  
- Clear writing and good exposition linking SDE error expansions to ML literature.  
- Empirical results support theoretical claims.

Weaknesses / open issues:
- Missing precise conditions for the key weak error expansion πγ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ^2); need necessary/sufficient assumptions on U and stochastic-gradient noise (moments, tails, state dependence).  
- Unclear applicability to non-SDE discretization approximations (e.g., subsampling Metropolis–Hastings) or to distributed SGLD implementations.  
- Practical efficiency: one chain requires twice the micro-steps, causing potential idle resources in synchronous setups; alternatives (e.g., 3-replica designs) may affect variance.  
- No analysis/experiments on variance inflation when replacing pathwise Brownian coupling with two independent γ/2 replicas.  
- Multi-level extensions (MLMC/RR with more levels) are only briefly mentioned; need guidance on levels vs compute budget and whether MLMC optimal rates are achievable under SG noise.  
- Unclear if sharing minibatches across chains reduces finite-time variance and whether to reuse minibatches per micro-step or per macro-iteration—ablation missing.  
- Experiments need convergence-to-tolerance curves, SGD/ALS baselines for contextual RMSE, and fairness checks by total gradient evaluations, not just wall-clock time.  
- Implementation details (boxed pseudocode for SGRRLD and coupling recipe) are relegated to the supplement and should be in main text.

Evaluation summary:
- Quality: technically strong but assumptions should be more explicit; empirical support good but requires broader baselines.  
- Clarity: well written but practical coupling/minibatch details should be surfaced earlier.  
- Originality: novel and valuable use of RR with explicit variance control in SG-MCMC.  
- Significance: high potential to become a standard bias-reduction wrapper if broadly applicable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, general, easy-to-use idea with strong theoretical guarantees and empirical gains.  
- Improves over SGLD and matches higher-order methods without complex integrators.  
- Natural extensions (multi-level, other SG-MCMC) are available.

Cons for acceptance:
- Need clearer applicability conditions and practitioner guidance on coupling choices.  
- Experimental section requires more baselines, convergence studies, and coupling/minibatch ablations.  
- Open efficiency questions in multi-replica and distributed settings.

Key questions for authors:
- Which SG-MCMC schemes provably satisfy the weak error expansion under stochastic gradients; any counterexamples?  
- Can RR be applied to subsampling MH or other approximate MH schemes lacking a clear step-size parameter?  
- For distributed SGLD, how would one realize Gaussian coupling across workers and what is the effect of partial correlation?  
- Would a 3-replica design (one γ, two independent γ/2 replicas) be acceptable in practice, and how much variance inflation is expected vs pathwise coupling?  
- Do you recommend sharing minibatches between chains per macro-iteration or reusing minibatches for both γ/2 steps; any empirical evidence?  
- Please add main-text pseudocode and an SGD baseline for matrix factorization, and report convergence-to-tolerance comparisons.
