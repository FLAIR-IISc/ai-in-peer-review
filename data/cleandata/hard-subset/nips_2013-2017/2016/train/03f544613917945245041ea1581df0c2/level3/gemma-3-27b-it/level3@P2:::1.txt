This paper presents Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm designed to reduce the bias of existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) while maintaining reasonable variance. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration technique, to SGLD by running two chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide a strong theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error. Empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization) support the claim that SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD and achieves accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators.

The paper successfully bridges concepts from the MCMC and Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) literature, which is a valuable contribution. SG-MCMC has become increasingly important for large-scale Bayesian inference, and this work offers a practical and theoretically sound improvement to a foundational algorithm (SGLD). The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, providing a clear understanding of the convergence properties of SGRRLD and justifying the improvement over SGLD. The authors also acknowledge and address potential issues related to variance control, proposing a correlation scheme for Brownian increments. The experimental validation, encompassing both synthetic data and a large-scale real-world problem, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness, clarity of presentation, and practical relevance. The motivation for reducing bias in SG-MCMC is well articulated, and the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a clever and relatively straightforward extension of existing methods. The analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of SGRRLD and establishes a theoretical basis for its advantages. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments strengthens the credibility of the results. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the paper accessible to researchers familiar with both MCMC and stochastic optimization. The computational efficiency of SGRRLD, stemming from the parallelizability of the two chains, is also a significant strength.

However, there are a few minor areas for improvement. As noted in the key points, Figure 1(a) could be clarified. The figure currently displays a Gaussian density using the empirical mean and variance, which doesn't directly represent the empirical posterior distribution. This could be misleading and should be explicitly stated in the caption or adjusted to accurately depict the posterior. Additionally, the discussion surrounding Figure 2(a) lacks sufficient explanation for the counterintuitive observation that the bias might not decrease as the step size decreases. Providing a more detailed explanation of this phenomenon would enhance the understanding of the algorithm’s behavior. While the convergence analysis is thorough, exploring the sensitivity of the method to the choice of step size schedule (beyond constant step sizes) could be beneficial. Finally, while the performance gains on the large-scale matrix factorization task are consistent, they are somewhat modest. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of SGRRLD compared to other advanced SG-MCMC methods would further strengthen the argument for its practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over SGLD in both synthetic and real-world experiments.
*   Practical and computationally efficient algorithm leveraging parallelization.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Performance gains on the real-world task are modest.
*   Minor clarifications needed in Figure 1(a) and the discussion around Figure 2(a).
*   Limited exploration of the impact of different step size schedules.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is a practical and theoretically sound improvement to SGLD, and the paper provides a compelling case for its adoption. The minor weaknesses identified can be readily addressed through revisions. I recommend accepting this paper.
