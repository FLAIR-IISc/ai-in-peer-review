## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper proposes the Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm, a novel SG-MCMC method leveraging the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias and improve convergence rates compared to standard SGLD. The core idea – running two chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs – is elegantly simple and builds upon well-established numerical analysis techniques. This work is well-situated within the growing body of literature on scalable MCMC methods, particularly those addressing the bias inherent in first-order integrators (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014; Teh et al., 2015). The theoretical analysis, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and improved MSE bounds, is a significant strength.  The approach is also commendable for its potential for parallelization. 

The paper's quality is generally high; the theoretical results appear solid, and the overall method is well-motivated. Clarity is good, although some notation could be improved (see minor issues below). The originality lies in the application of RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC context, a novel combination that demonstrably improves performance. The significance is potentially substantial, as reducing bias in large-scale Bayesian inference is a critical challenge, and this provides a readily implementable approach.

However, there are crucial concerns. The derivation of the specific convergence rates (particularly the K<sup>-4/5</sup> MSE rate) from Theorem 1 needs substantial clarification. The logic connecting the Taylor expansion to the rate isn't immediately obvious and warrants a more detailed explanation. Further, the timing comparisons in Figure 5 are flawed. Because SGRRLD requires approximately twice the computation (two chains, one with twice the steps) compared to SGLD for equivalent runtime, the performance gap should be adjusted accordingly (reducing the reported gap by ~33%). This misrepresentation diminishes the perceived advantage of SGRRLD.  

Regarding empirical results, Figure 3 shows the best bias reduction at α=0.2 (red curve), contradicting the text's claim of α=1/3.  Furthermore, the "optimal" curve fluctuates significantly, suggesting the empirical rate might not consistently align with α=0.2, necessitating further investigation. Finally, line 256 raises a question: why doesn't the 1/(Kγ) term dominate for SGLD when the stepsize is very small? 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel application of RR extrapolation, solid theoretical foundation, potential for significant performance gains, and ease of implementation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**  Unclear derivation of convergence rates, unfair timing comparison in Figure 5, discrepancies between theoretical claims and empirical results in Figure 3, and the unresolved question in line 256.

**Minor Issues:** Eq.1 uses a different symbol than θ; line 94 “RR” not defined; line 131 Γ<sub>K</sub> not defined; line 160 “smooth function f” needs precise smoothness assumptions; line 249 figure labeling error (Bias vs. time vs. dimension); vectors should be bold.
