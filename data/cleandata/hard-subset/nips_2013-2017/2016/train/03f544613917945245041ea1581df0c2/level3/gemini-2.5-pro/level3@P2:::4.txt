This paper introduces a novel approach for improving the accuracy of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The proposed method, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce the discretization bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to run two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (e.g., `γ` and `γ/2`) and linearly combine their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. This work builds upon the established literature of SG-MCMC (e.g., Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2014) and connects it with classical numerical analysis techniques for SDEs (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and mean squared error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than standard SGLD. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world large-scale matrix factorization problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel, elegant, and significant contribution. It addresses the well-known problem of discretization bias in a principled manner. If the claims hold, this method provides a general and powerful way to boost the accuracy of a wide range of SG-MCMC samplers with minimal implementation overhead.
2.  **Quality of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is supported by a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE (Theorem 2). The derivation of improved convergence rates—achieving an MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³)—is a key theoretical result that demonstrates the power of the approach.
3.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** A major strength of the proposed method is its simplicity. It requires running two standard SG-MCMC chains in parallel, which is straightforward to implement. The inherent parallelism of the two chains makes the method well-suited for modern multi-core architectures, potentially mitigating the additional computational cost.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper is well-written and the core idea is compelling. However, there are some major concerns regarding the experimental evaluation and presentation of results that weaken the paper's claims.

1.  **Unfair Computational Cost Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the comparison of computational cost in the experiments. The proposed SGRRLD method requires running two chains, one of which takes twice as many steps as the other per unit of "time". An SGRRLD iteration consists of one step of the `γ` chain and two steps of the `γ/2` chain, for a total of 3 gradient computations. In the matrix factorization experiment (Section 5.2), SGLD is run for 10500 iterations, while SGRRLD is run for 5250 iterations. This means SGLD performs 10500 gradient evaluations, whereas SGRRLD performs `5250 * 3 = 15750` gradient evaluations. The authors claim the wall-clock times are nearly the same due to parallelization, but this comparison is misleading as it is not based on a fixed computational budget. SGRRLD is given a 50% larger budget of gradient computations. A fair comparison would grant SGLD the same total number of gradient computations (i.e., run it for 15750 iterations). The significant performance gap reported in Figure 5 might shrink or disappear under a fair comparison. This needs to be addressed to properly evaluate the practical utility of the method.

2.  **Inconsistency in Experimental Results:** There appears to be a direct contradiction between the theoretical claims and the experimental results in Figure 3. The text (line 181) states that the optimal rate for the bias is achieved with `α = 1/3`. However, in Figure 3 (left), the red curve corresponding to `α = 0.2` clearly shows a lower bias than the curve for `α = 1/3`. This inconsistency undermines confidence in the experimental validation. Furthermore, the "optimal" curves in Figure 3 fluctuate substantially; an explanation for this high variance would be helpful.

3.  **Analysis of Small Stepsize Behavior:** In Figure 2, for very small values of `γ`, the bias and MSE of SGRRLD increase, which the authors correctly attribute to the `1/(Kγ)` term in the error bounds. However, the degradation seems much more pronounced for SGRRLD than for SGLD. A brief discussion on why this might be the case would strengthen the analysis. Is SGRRLD inherently more sensitive to the choice of a small stepsize when the iteration count `K` is fixed?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant combination of a classical numerical method (RR extrapolation) with modern scalable MCMC.
*   The theoretical contribution is strong, demonstrating significantly improved convergence rates.
*   The method is simple to implement and applicable to a broad class of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental comparison with SGLD is based on an unequal computational budget, making the reported performance gains questionable.
*   There is a clear contradiction between the theoretical claims and the plotted results regarding the optimal stepsize decay rate.
*   The combination of these issues raises concerns about the paper's overall rigor and the practical significance of the proposed method.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a very promising and theoretically sound method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms. The core idea is highly original and significant. However, the current manuscript is weakened by a flawed experimental comparison and inconsistencies between theory and experimental plots. These issues must be addressed. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a revised experimental comparison based on a fixed computational budget (total gradient evaluations) that still demonstrates a clear advantage for SGRRLD, and also resolve the contradiction in Figure 3.

*   Acronyms: The acronym "RR" is first used on line 94 but is defined later.
