

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors propose running two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation. The theoretical analysis shows SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias O(γ²) and variance O(γ⁴), with non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds that demonstrate superior convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The method is well-suited for parallel architectures as the two chains can be run independently. The authors provide synthetic and real-world experiments on matrix factorization that support their theoretical claims.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical analysis. The central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds are well-motivated and properly derived. The experiments are well-designed and effectively demonstrate the practical advantages of SGRRLD over SGLD and SGHMC. The paper is clearly written with good organization, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. The clever application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which is novel to the best of my knowledge.
2. The rigorous theoretical analysis showing how the method achieves better convergence rates than SGLD while using only first-order integrators.
3. The clear demonstration of practical advantages through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real datasets.
4. The insightful discussion of the parallelization benefits, which is highly relevant for modern distributed computing environments.

However, there are several areas that need improvement for the paper to be accepted:

1. The paper claims SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of the methods that are based on higher-order integrators" but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate this in comparison to actual higher-order methods. While they compare with SGHMC-s (which uses a higher-order integrator), they should provide a more direct comparison with the theoretical limits of higher-order methods. The experiments show SGRRHMC matches SGHMC-s with small γ, but it's not clear how this compares to the theoretical optimal performance of higher-order methods.

2. The paper states that "the mini-batch size has to be the same for the two chains" but doesn't provide a clear theoretical justification for this requirement. The authors should explain why different mini-batch sizes wouldn't work and provide a more rigorous explanation of this constraint.

3. The paper mentions that "the two sequences (Z^(γ/2)_k) and (Z^(γ)_k) should be perfectly correlated" to minimize variance but doesn't adequately explain why this is the case beyond citing [23]. A brief intuitive explanation or sketch of why perfect correlation minimizes variance would strengthen the argument.

4. The paper claims SGRRLD "attains higher rates of convergence than SGLD in both finite-time and asymptotically" but doesn't clearly explain how this is possible with a first-order integrator when higher-order integrators are known to provide better convergence rates. The authors should clarify this apparent paradox.

5. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the parallel efficiency gains beyond the experiments. It would be valuable to include a theoretical analysis of the speedup achieved by parallelizing the two chains, especially for large-scale distributed systems.

The paper's significance is strong as it provides a practical method to improve SG-MCMC performance without requiring complex higher-order integrators. The results are important for large-scale Bayesian inference applications where computational efficiency is critical. The approach is sufficiently novel compared to prior work at NIPS (as evidenced by the lack of similar approaches in the cited literature).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions to the field of SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experiments are convincing, and the method has clear practical benefits. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding the comparison with higher-order methods and the explanation of the mini-batch constraint, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS 2016.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses an important problem in large-scale Bayesian inference with a technically sound approach that advances the state of the art. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points above.
