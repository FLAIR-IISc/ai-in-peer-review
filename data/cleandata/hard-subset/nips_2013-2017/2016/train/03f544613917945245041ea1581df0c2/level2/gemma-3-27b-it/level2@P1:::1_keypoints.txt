- Proposes SGRRLD, a stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce SGLD bias.  
- Core idea: run two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel and combine them for a more accurate estimator.  
- Provides theoretical analysis: asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets show improved convergence versus SGLD and SGHMC.  
- Well-motivated work addressing the known bias limitation of SG-MCMC methods.  
- Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation within SG-MCMC.  
- Strong theoretical rigor offering a rigorous justification for the algorithm.  
- Empirical validation includes both Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks.  
- Clear presentation overall, though some sections require careful reading due to dense notation.  
- Practical relevance: reduced bias and inherent parallelism make the method impactful for real-world applications.  
- Complexity: theoretical analysis is involved and notation can be dense for some readers.  
- Assumptions: results depend on several assumptions that lack thorough discussion of practical implications.  
- Parameter tuning: step sizes and correlation matrix Σ require careful tuning with little practical guidance provided.  
- Computational cost: running two chains increases cost; parallelization mentioned but trade-offs not fully addressed.  
- Limited comparisons: lacks evaluation against other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods (e.g., higher-order integrators, variance reduction).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated approach with rigorous theory and convincing experiments and potential field impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility concerns due to theoretical complexity, potentially restrictive assumptions, and insufficient discussion of cost/tuning and comparisons.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify assumptions, provide guidance on choosing Σ, and discuss computational cost vs. accuracy trade-offs.
