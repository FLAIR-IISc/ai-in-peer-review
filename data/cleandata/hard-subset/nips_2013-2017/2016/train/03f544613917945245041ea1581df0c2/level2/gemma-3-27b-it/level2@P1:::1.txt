This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, based on Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias of SGLD. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and then combine the results to obtain a more accurate estimate. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem, along with bounds on bias and MSE. They also present experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets showing improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a known limitation of SG-MCMC methods – bias. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a clever idea, and the authors do a good job of explaining how it can be applied in this context. The theoretical results are a significant contribution, providing a rigorous justification for the proposed algorithm. The experimental validation, with both Gaussian model and a real-world matrix factorization task, strengthens the claims. Related work is adequately cited, referencing key papers in the SG-MCMC literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including consistency results, a CLT, and bounds on bias and MSE.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the ideas are explained clearly, although some sections require careful reading.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Reducing bias in SG-MCMC is important for real-world applications, making this work potentially impactful. The parallelism inherent in SGRRLD is also a strong point.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While the core idea is relatively simple, the theoretical analysis is quite involved and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp. The notation can also be dense at times.
*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on several assumptions, which are stated but not always thoroughly discussed regarding their practical implications or ease of verification.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of step sizes and the correlation matrix Σ in the correlated Brownian motion implementation require careful tuning. The paper doesn’t offer much guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice.
*   **Computational Cost:** Although the algorithm is parallelizable, running two chains inherently increases the computational cost compared to SGLD. The paper doesn't fully address this trade-off, beyond mentioning the possibility of parallelization. A more thorough discussion of the computational overhead would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons to SGLD and SGHMC are provided, it would be valuable to compare SGRRLD to other state-of-the-art SG-MCMC methods, particularly those incorporating higher-order integrators or variance reduction techniques beyond just bias reduction.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to addressing a key challenge in SG-MCMC.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong support for the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian machine learning by enabling more accurate and efficient inference in large-scale applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper.
*   The assumptions underlying the theoretical results may be restrictive.
*   A more thorough discussion of the computational cost and parameter tuning is needed.
*   A more comprehensive comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the theoretical analysis, and the positive experimental results outweigh the weaknesses. While the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational cost and parameter tuning, these are relatively minor issues. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarifying the assumptions and providing more practical guidance for implementation. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on how to choose the correlation matrix Σ, and add a discussion about the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy.
