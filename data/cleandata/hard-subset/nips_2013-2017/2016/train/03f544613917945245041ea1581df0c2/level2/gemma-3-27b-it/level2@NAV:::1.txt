## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper introduces a novel SG-MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, designed to reduce bias compared to standard SGLD by employing the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique. The core idea – running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes and combining the results – is conceptually sound and builds upon established numerical analysis principles applied to MCMC. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE. These theoretical results are a significant strength, positioning SGRRLD as a theoretically well-grounded method.

The paper clearly outlines the mathematical foundations, building from existing SGLD work (Welling & Teh, 2011) and extending RR extrapolation from SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) into the SG-MCMC domain. The experimental evaluation is commendable, encompassing both synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization problems (MovieLens datasets). The results consistently demonstrate SGRRLD's superior performance over SGLD and, in some cases, even comparable accuracy to higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) with potentially reduced computational cost due to parallelization.

However, the paper suffers from some limitations. The notation is dense and occasionally obscures the clarity of the derivations. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the connection between the theoretical improvements (rate of convergence) and the practical gains observed in experiments could be made more explicit. Further, the experiments, while comprehensive, primarily focus on demonstrating performance gains. A deeper analysis of the sensitivity to step size selection (beyond the explored range) and the impact of the correlation parameter Σ (equation 8) would strengthen the practical recommendations. Additionally, while acknowledging the parallelizability of SGRRLD, a quantitative analysis of speedup relative to SGLD on a larger computational infrastructure would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous analysis.
*   Demonstrated empirical improvements over SGLD on both synthetic and real data.
*   Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.
*   Potential for significant computational efficiency gains through parallelization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and occasionally unclear presentation.
*   Limited sensitivity analysis of key parameters.
*   Lack of detailed scalability analysis.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. Despite some presentation challenges, the strong theoretical backing and promising empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental analysis. The authors should be encouraged to further explore the practical implications of their theoretical results and investigate the scalability of SGRRLD on larger computational platforms.
