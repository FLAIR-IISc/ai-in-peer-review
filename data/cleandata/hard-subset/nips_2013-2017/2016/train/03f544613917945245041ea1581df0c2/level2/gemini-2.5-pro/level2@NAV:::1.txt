This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias inherent in popular methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the SG-MCMC setting. This involves running two parallel chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their estimators to cancel the leading-order error term. This work builds upon the extensive literature on scalable Bayesian inference, particularly SGLD [Welling & Teh, 2011], and adapts established methods for variance reduction in SDE discretizations [Talay & Tubaro, 1990] to the stochastic gradient context.

The paper's primary strength lies in its high quality and significant contributions. Theoretically, the authors provide a rigorous convergence analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). A key result is that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is substantially better than the O(K⁻²/³) rate for SGLD and matches the accuracy of methods using more complex higher-order integrators. This is a novel and important finding. The theoretical claims are thoroughly supported by well-designed experiments on both synthetic data, where the ground truth is known, and large-scale real-world matrix factorization tasks. The clarity of the paper is excellent; it is well-written, logically structured, and presents its complex ideas in an accessible manner.

The main weakness is the computational overhead. The proposed method requires approximately three times the computation of a single SGLD chain per effective iteration. The authors argue this cost is mitigated by the algorithm's parallel-friendly nature and faster convergence, which is compellingly demonstrated in the wall-clock time experiments. However, the paper could be strengthened by a more direct discussion and experimental comparison against a baseline SGLD run for three times as many iterations. Additionally, while deferring detailed technical assumptions to the supplement is standard, a brief, high-level summary of these conditions in the main text would improve the paper’s self-containedness.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a significant and original advancement in the SG-MCMC literature. It introduces a clever, well-motivated method to tackle the critical issue of bias, backing it up with solid theory and convincing empirical evidence. The strengths substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses, and the work is likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners in large-scale Bayesian inference.
