

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel MCMC algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) while maintaining reasonable variance. The key insight is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combine their outputs using RR extrapolation. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves O(K^{-4/5}) mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), and they demonstrate that it can match the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only first-order integrators.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It begins with a thorough review of related work on SG-MCMC methods and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SDEs. The theoretical contributions are substantial: Theorem 1 establishes a central limit theorem for the estimator with minimal variance when using correlated Brownian increments, and Theorem 2 provides non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The authors carefully discuss the optimal step size choices and convergence rates, which align well with their experimental results. The theoretical analysis is technically sound and builds upon prior work in the field, particularly the recent advances in SG-MCMC convergence analysis by Chen et al. (2015) and the RR extrapolation techniques for SDEs by Talay and Tubaro (1990).

The experimental section provides strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims. The synthetic Gaussian model experiments clearly demonstrate SGRRLD's improved bias and MSE compared to SGLD, with results that match the predicted convergence rates. The extension to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) and the large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets further support the practical utility of the approach. The authors thoughtfully address the parallelization aspect, noting that SGRRLD's dual-chain structure is naturally suited for parallel computation, which could be a significant advantage in large-scale settings.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian inference with large-scale data. The main novelty lies in applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, which is a clever technique that effectively reduces bias without requiring higher-order integrators. This is particularly significant because higher-order integrators often come with increased computational complexity. The method is simple to implement (just running two chains in parallel) and can be applied to any SG-MCMC algorithm that satisfies the necessary technical conditions. The paper clearly articulates how this work differs from prior contributions, especially from Chen et al. (2015) on higher-order integrators.

A few minor concerns: The theoretical analysis relies on several technical assumptions about the smoothness of f, the potential energy U, and the gradient estimator distribution L. While these are standard in the field, it would be helpful to discuss how restrictive these assumptions are in practical applications. The authors mention that the mini-batch size must be the same for both chains to avoid introducing bias, but it might be worth elaborating on how this constraint affects practical implementation. The computational overhead of running two chains (even in parallel) could be quantified more precisely, though the authors do note that the wall-clock times are nearly identical due to parallelization.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is thorough, and the approach is both elegant and practical. The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing SG-MCMC methods (bias) with a simple yet powerful technique that doesn't require major changes to the underlying algorithm. The results have clear implications for practitioners working with large-scale Bayesian models, as they demonstrate a straightforward way to improve accuracy without substantially increasing computational cost.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016, as it advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference with a novel, theoretically grounded approach that also demonstrates practical benefits. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
