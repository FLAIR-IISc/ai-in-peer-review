

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel extension of SG-MCMC that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The main claim is that SGRRLD achieves higher convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)) by running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes in parallel, while preserving parallelizability.

The theoretical analysis is strong and well-supported, with formal proofs of asymptotic consistency, central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The paper correctly identifies that correlated Brownian motion (as in Equation 7) minimizes variance, extending prior work on RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The experimental section effectively validates the theory using both synthetic data (Gaussian model) and real-world applications (matrix factorization for movie recommendations). Results consistently show SGRRLD outperforms SGLD in terms of bias and MSE with comparable computational time, with the ML-1M dataset demonstrating SGRRLD achieving SGLD's accuracy in half the time.

The paper demonstrates excellent field knowledge, correctly positioning itself within the SG-MCMC literature and citing relevant work (Welling & Teh, Chen et al., Talay & Tubaro). The novelty is significant - applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a fresh approach that achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods. The paper is well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility and a clear acknowledgment of limitations (e.g., the 1/(Kγ) term dominating for very small step sizes).

The main weakness is limited comparison against higher-order integrator methods beyond theoretical bounds. While the paper mentions SGHMC-s in Section 5.1, more direct experimental comparison would strengthen the claim that SGRRLD "achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators."

Overall, this is a technically sound, well-executed paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The results are convincing, the methodology is clearly explained, and the approach offers a valuable solution to a key limitation in SG-MCMC. The paper makes a strong case for acceptance as a significant contribution to the field.
