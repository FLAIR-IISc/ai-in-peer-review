- Proposes Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation for SG-MCMC (SGRRLD): combine two SGLD-like chains at step sizes γ and γ/2 with correlated Gaussian increments to reduce discretization bias.
- Claims leading-order bias reduced from O(γ) to O(γ^2) while keeping variance controlled, improving finite-time MSE to O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) and achieving O(K−4/5) with γ ∝ K−1/5.
- Shows a CLT for decreasing step sizes with optimal asymptotic rate O(K−2/5) at α = 1/5 (vs. O(K−1/3) for SGLD).
- Adapts RR/diffusion analyses (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) to the subsampled-gradient setting and derives optimal maximal correlation of coarse/fine Gaussian increments.
- Experimental validation on Gaussian model, SGHMC variant, and large-scale matrix factorization supports theoretical gains and demonstrates wall-clock speedups with parallel execution.
- Situates work well within SG-MCMC and discretization-bias literature and claims novelty in transferring RR to stochastic-gradient settings with CLT and non-asymptotic bounds.
- Theoretical results (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE) are clearly stated, using Poisson equation and high-order expansions; assumptions are strong (C9 smoothness, growth, geometric ergodicity).
- Variance-minimization argument for the correlation structure is convincing and consistent with diffusion literature.
- Synthetic experiments validate bias/MSE scalings; SGHMC and matrix factorization experiments show practical benefits, but baselines are limited.
- Reproducibility is good: algorithmic details and pseudocode provided; correlated Gaussian implementation is straightforward; tuning discussed empirically.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; estimator construction and correlated increment design are explained, though minor notational clarifications and intuition about Gaussian vs gradient-noise correlation would help.
- Originality: novel and nontrivial adaptation of RR to SG-MCMC, proving CLT and finite-time improvements without higher-order integrators.
- Significance: practically attractive (minimal code changes, parallelizable) and theoretically strong; useful as a bias-reduction device even without parallel resources.
- Strengths: simple/generic idea, solid theory, clear rate improvements, principled variance control, and diverse experiments including SGHMC extension.
- Weaknesses: strong technical assumptions may limit applicability to nonsmooth/posterior-tail problems; efficiency claims depend on parallel resources.
- Experimental gaps: missing per-gradient or per-epoch efficiency and ESS comparisons, limited comparisons to control-variate or preconditioned baselines, and no ablation on minibatch coupling.
- Practical guidance gaps: step-size constant selection is empirical; no adaptive scheduler provided.
- Suggested improvements: add per-gradient and ESS-per-time comparisons, test on logistic regression or Bayesian neural nets, compare to variance-reduced SG-MCMC and higher-order SGLD, ablate minibatch coupling and Σ choices, and discuss relaxing smoothness assumptions.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, elegant, rigorous, broadly applicable, and empirically convincing under parallelization.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, limited single-core/fair comparisons, and lack of adaptive step-size guidance.
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution recommended for acceptance contingent on strengthening empirical evaluation as suggested.
