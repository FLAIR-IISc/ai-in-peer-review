This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique for improving the convergence rate of numerical methods, to the SG-MCMC framework. This is achieved by running two parallel MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and forming a new estimator by taking a specific linear combination of their outputs. The work provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), which show that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD. The theoretical contributions are supported by experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data.

This work builds upon the well-established literature of SG-MCMC, initiated by Welling and Teh (2011), and the separate field of numerical analysis for Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), where RR extrapolation has been previously studied for SDEs with exact gradients (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The main contribution is the successful application and analysis of RR extrapolation in the stochastic gradient setting, where the noise from data subsampling introduces additional complexity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing both asymptotic (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic (Theorem 2) results. The derivation of improved convergence rates for the MSE—O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for SGRRLD versus O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD—is a strong and well-supported result. The paper is careful to consider important practical details, such as controlling the variance of the combined estimator by correlating the noise terms of the two chains. The experiments are well-designed to systematically verify the theoretical claims, for instance, by analyzing the bias and MSE as functions of the step size and confirming the optimal convergence rate exponents.
*   **Originality:** While neither SG-MCMC nor RR extrapolation are new concepts, their combination is novel and represents a creative way to address a known limitation (bias) of SG-MCMC algorithms. The analysis extends previous work on RR for SDEs to the more challenging SG-MCMC setting. This provides a new and effective approach for improving sampler accuracy that is distinct from the more common approach of using higher-order numerical integrators.
*   **Significance:** The contribution is significant for both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian machine learning. SGRRLD offers a practical method for improving the accuracy of Bayesian inference in large-scale models. The fact that the proposed method achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only simple first-order updates makes it particularly appealing. Furthermore, the algorithm is inherently parallelizable, meaning the increased computational cost of running a second chain can be offset by parallel hardware, leading to improved statistical efficiency without an increase in wall-clock time, as demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments. This makes the method highly relevant for modern large-scale applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It provides the necessary background on SGLD and RR extrapolation before introducing the proposed SGRRLD algorithm. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the experimental setup and results are described in sufficient detail to be understood. The logical flow from theory to empirical validation is compelling.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, and the following points are primarily suggestions for minor enhancements rather than significant flaws.

*   The computational cost of SGRRLD is double that of SGLD in terms of total operations, as it requires running two chains. The paper effectively argues that this is not an issue for wall-clock time due to parallelization. However, a brief discussion on scenarios where this might be a limitation (e.g., on a single-core machine or where model memory is a bottleneck, as two models must be held in memory) could provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs.
*   The claim that the technique is "quite generic and can be virtually applied to all the current SG-MCMC algorithms" is plausible and supported by the SGHMC experiment. This claim could be strengthened with a short discussion on the key properties an SG-MCMC algorithm must possess for the RR extrapolation to be effective. For example, does the algorithm need a specific form of error expansion with respect to the step size?
*   In the experimental section, the comparison with SGHMC with a higher-order splitting integrator (SGHMC-s) is very insightful. It would be beneficial to include this comparison in a main figure, perhaps alongside the SGLD results in Figure 2, to more directly support the claim that SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of higher-order methods.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful method for improving the performance of SG-MCMC algorithms. The strengths in terms of quality, originality, and significance are substantial. It addresses a key challenge in a popular class of algorithms and offers an elegant and effective solution.
