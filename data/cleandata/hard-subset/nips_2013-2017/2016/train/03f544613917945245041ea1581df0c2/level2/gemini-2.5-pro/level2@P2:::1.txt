This paper proposes a novel method for reducing the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The key idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The resulting method, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2 for the 'coarse' and 'fine' chains, respectively). The outputs are then combined in a specific way to cancel out the leading-order error term in the bias. To prevent the variance from increasing excessively, the method uses correlated noise for the two chains.

The paper is well-situated within the context of scalable Bayesian inference. It builds directly upon the foundational SGLD work [1] and the broader SG-MCMC literature [4-12]. The main contribution is importing the RR extrapolation technique, previously studied for SDEs with exact gradients [21, 23], into the stochastic gradient setting. This provides an alternative path to achieving higher-order accuracy, competing with methods that use more complex, higher-order numerical integrators [10].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and elegant idea. It offers a simple, general-purpose "wrapper" to improve the accuracy of existing first-order methods without requiring the design of sophisticated, problem-specific higher-order integrators. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could be widely applicable. The ability to achieve higher-order convergence rates with a method that is easily parallelizable is particularly attractive.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous and comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors prove that their estimator is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem (Theorem 1), and derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) (Theorem 2). The theoretical results are strong, demonstrating that SGRRLD improves the optimal MSE convergence rate from O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD to O(K⁻⁴/⁵). This theoretical justification is a major strength of the work.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured. It begins with a concise introduction to SGLD and RR extrapolation, making the work accessible. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical results are stated clearly. The connection between the asymptotic and non-asymptotic results, particularly regarding the optimal choice of step-size decay rate, is well explained.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the theoretical claims.
    *   The synthetic experiments on the linear Gaussian model are particularly effective. They systematically verify the theoretical predictions about the reduction in bias and MSE (Figures 1, 2) and confirm the derived optimal convergence rates with respect to the step-size schedule (Figure 3).
    *   The demonstration that the RR-extrapolation idea can be successfully applied to another algorithm (SGHMC) supports the claim of generality (Figure 4). The comparison against a higher-order splitting integrator (SGHMC-s) effectively shows that SGRRLD is competitive with more complex methods.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world MovieLens datasets demonstrate the practical utility of the method. The results in Figure 5 clearly show that SGRRLD converges significantly faster in wall-clock time than SGLD, which is a compelling argument for its adoption in practice.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Trade-off:** The primary weakness is the increased computational cost. To achieve one "step" of the SGRRLD estimator, the algorithm requires one gradient evaluation for the coarse chain and two for the fine chain, for a total of three evaluations per time step γ. This is roughly triple the cost of a single SGLD step of size γ. While the authors rightly emphasize that the two chains can be run in parallel, which makes the wall-clock time per iteration comparable to SGLD, the total computational budget (in terms of gradient evaluations or CPU-hours) is higher. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this trade-off. For instance, comparing SGRRLD with K iterations against SGLD with 2K or 3K iterations (on a single core) would provide a clearer picture of computational efficiency versus statistical efficiency.

2.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The main paper defers all technical assumptions for the theorems to the supplementary material. While this is common practice due to space constraints, it makes it difficult for the reader to immediately grasp the scope of the theoretical results. Including a brief, high-level summary of the key assumptions in the main text (e.g., "Under standard assumptions of strong convexity and smoothness of the potential U...") would significantly improve clarity.

3.  **Choice of Step Sizes:** The paper focuses on the RR scheme using step sizes γ and γ/2. It would be interesting to briefly discuss if other ratios (e.g., γ and γ/3) could be used and what the theoretical implications would be. While γ/2 is the standard choice, a short note on this could add completeness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, simple, and powerful technique to the SG-MCMC toolbox. The combination of a strong theoretical foundation with convincing and well-executed experiments makes for a very complete and impactful contribution. The method is of clear interest to both researchers and practitioners working on large-scale Bayesian inference. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to clarifying the presentation of the computational trade-offs. The strengths of the paper—originality, significance, and technical quality—are substantial. I strongly recommend acceptance.
