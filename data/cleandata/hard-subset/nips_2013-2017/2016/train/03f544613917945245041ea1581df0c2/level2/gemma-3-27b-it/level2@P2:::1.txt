This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, based on the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique applied to SGLD. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and combine their outputs to reduce bias. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, including consistency and a central limit theorem, and demonstrate improved convergence rates compared to SGLD in both asymptotic and finite-time settings. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets support the proposed method’s effectiveness.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the well-known bias issue in SG-MCMC methods. The application of RR extrapolation, a common numerical technique, to this domain is a novel and potentially significant contribution. The theoretical analysis is extensive and appears rigorous, providing a strong foundation for the claims made. The experiments are reasonably thorough, covering both synthetic data for validation and a real-world matrix factorization problem to showcase scalability. The connection to higher-order integrators is insightful, framing the work as achieving similar accuracy with a simpler first-order method.  

However, there are some weaknesses. While the paper extensively compares SGRRLD to SGLD, a comparison against other recent SG-MCMC variants (e.g., SGHMC with adaptive step sizes, or methods leveraging variance reduction techniques) would strengthen the analysis.  The discussion on the choice of correlation structure (equation 8) feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors justify choosing Σ = Id, the impact of deviating from this optimal correlation isn’t fully explored. The practical considerations of implementing correlated Brownian motions and the associated computational overhead are not discussed in detail.  The experimental setup in the matrix factorization experiment doesn't explain *why* certain parameters were chosen (e.g., step size). Justification of these parameters, perhaps through a small sensitivity analysis, would enhance the reproducibility and credibility of the results.  Furthermore, the paper frequently refers to “supplementary document” which is inaccessible during review, making full verification of the proofs and detailed experimental setup impossible. This is a major limitation. The presentation, while generally clear, sometimes suffers from dense mathematical notation without sufficient intuitive explanation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a new and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
* **Improved Convergence:** The theoretical and empirical results suggest improved convergence rates compared to SGLD.
* **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method is well-suited for parallel computation, making it applicable to large-scale problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:**  Lack of comparison against state-of-the-art SG-MCMC alternatives.
* **Correlation Structure:** Insufficient discussion regarding the impact of deviating from the optimal correlation structure.
* **Implementation Details:** Missing details regarding implementation and computational overhead of correlated Brownian motions.
* **Experimental Justification:** Lack of justification for parameter choices in experiments.
* **Inaccessible Supplementary Material:**  The inability to review the supplementary material significantly hinders a complete evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
* The experimental results are encouraging and support the theoretical claims.
* The method’s suitability for parallel computation is a significant advantage.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of comparison to more recent SG-MCMC methods limits the assessment of its practical significance.
* The inaccessible supplementary material prevents a complete verification of the results.
* Some aspects of the implementation and analysis remain unclear due to the lack of detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The core idea is strong and the theoretical analysis is impressive. However, the limitations regarding comparison to related work and the inaccessible supplementary material prevent me from giving a stronger recommendation. If the authors can address the concerns about comparison to existing methods and make the supplementary material available, the paper’s impact would be significantly increased. Addressing the parameter choices and providing insight into the practical implications of implementing correlated Brownian increments would also be valuable.
