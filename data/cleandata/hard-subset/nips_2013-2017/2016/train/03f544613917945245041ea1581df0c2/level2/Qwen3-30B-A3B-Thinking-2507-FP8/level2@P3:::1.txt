

The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient MCMC algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in SG-MCMC methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The work builds upon SGLD (Welling and Teh, 2011) and extends it by running two SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes in parallel and combining their outputs. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic consistency, satisfies a central limit theorem, and has bias and mean squared error bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively. The paper shows that SGRRLD attains a convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD, matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators while using only first-order numerical schemes.

The paper makes a significant contribution by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a technique not previously explored in this context. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with formal proofs provided in the supplementary material. The convergence rate results are compelling, demonstrating that first-order methods can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods through extrapolation. The empirical results on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks support the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise mathematical notation and a well-structured presentation of the algorithm (Equations 4-6). The supplementary material provides implementation details that would aid reproducibility. The experiments are well-designed, covering both simple analytical cases and large-scale real-world applications on MovieLens datasets.

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical contribution, which addresses a key limitation of SG-MCMC methods (bias) without requiring more complex integrators. The parallel nature of the algorithm also makes it well-suited for distributed computing environments. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly Chen et al. (2015) which discusses higher-order integrators, by showing that SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy with a simpler implementation.

Some areas for improvement include better clarification of the computational trade-offs compared to higher-order methods. While the paper mentions the parallel nature of SGRRLD, it would benefit from a more detailed comparison of wall-clock time between SGRRLD and higher-order methods, as this is crucial for practical applications. The claim that the technique is "quite generic" could be strengthened with a more detailed discussion of the technical conditions required for application to other SG-MCMC algorithms, particularly regarding ergodicity and weak error conditions.

The paper is well-positioned within NIPS 2016, building upon foundational work from Welling and Teh (2011) while addressing a key challenge in scaling MCMC to large datasets. The problem is significant as bias reduction is a critical issue in practical Bayesian inference for large-scale applications.

Quality: The technical analysis is sound with rigorous proofs for key claims. The experimental results provide strong empirical support for the theoretical findings.

Clarity: The paper is well-written with clear organization and precise mathematical presentation. The algorithm description is accessible to readers familiar with SG-MCMC.

Originality: The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC represents a novel approach to bias reduction in this context, distinct from existing methods that rely on higher-order integrators.

Significance: The method provides better convergence rates without requiring more complex integrators, making it both theoretically interesting and practically useful for large-scale Bayesian inference. The parallel nature of the algorithm also aligns with modern computational architectures.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The proposed technique represents a meaningful advancement in reducing bias in SG-MCMC methods while maintaining computational efficiency. The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016 as it addresses an important problem with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art.
