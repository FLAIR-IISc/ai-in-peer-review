Summary and contributions
The paper proposes applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC, yielding a simple parallel algorithm that combines two SGLD-like chains run at step sizes γ and γ/2 with carefully correlated Gaussian increments. The resulting estimator, SGRRLD, is claimed to reduce the leading-order discretization bias from O(γ) to O(γ2) while keeping variance controlled, and to improve finite-time rates: with constant step size, the MSE bound becomes O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), achieving O(K−4/5) when γ ∝ K−1/5 (vs. O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC). With γk = γ1k−α, a CLT holds and the optimal asymptotic rate is O(K−2/5) at α = 1/5 (vs. O(K−1/3) for SGLD). The analysis adapts RR results for diffusions (Talay-Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup) to the subsampled-gradient setting and shows the asymptotic variance is minimized when the coarse/fine Gaussian increments are maximally correlated. Experiments on a Gaussian model, an SGHMC variant, and large-scale matrix factorization support the theoretical gains and demonstrate practical benefits under parallel execution.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh; Ma–Chen–Fox; Chen–Ding–Carin) and discretization-bias analyses (Roberts–Tweedie; Talay–Tubaro). The RR idea for invariant measures and MLMC for SDEs is appropriately cited (Lemaire et al., Pagès), and the paper’s novelty lies in transferring RR to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, providing a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds with correlated increments, and showing improved rates relative to first-order SG-MCMC without designing higher-order integrators. The comparison to higher-order SGHMC (splitting) is helpful, though a similar comparison for SGLD-based high-order schemes would strengthen the empirical picture.

Quality
- Theory: The results (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds) are clearly stated, with conditions deferred to the supplement. The use of a Poisson equation and high-order Taylor expansions echoes established analyses for ULA. Assumptions appear strong (smoothness up to C9, growth conditions, geometric ergodicity), which may not hold for some common ML posteriors; the paper is careful to flag “technical weak error and ergodicity conditions.” The variance-minimization over Σ leading to the specific correlation structure is convincing and matches diffusion literature. The rates and optimizers (α = 1/5 and γ ∝ K−1/5 for MSE) are consistent across asymptotic and non-asymptotic analyses.
- Experiments: Synthetic Gaussian experiments are appropriate for validating bias/MSE scalings and show the expected O(γ2)/O(γ4) behavior. The SGHMC experiment supports the claim that RR can recover the accuracy of higher-order integrators while remaining algorithmically simple. The matrix factorization experiments are substantive and demonstrate wall-clock gains when two chains can run in parallel. That said, comparisons are limited to SGLD/SGHMC baselines; per-gradient (or per data-pass) efficiency and ESS comparisons are missing.
- Reproducibility: Algorithmic details are straightforward; correlated Gaussians are easy to implement. Pseudocode is in the supplement; constants and step-size tuning are discussed empirically.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The construction of the estimator (Eq. 6) and the correlated increment design are clear. Minor notational issues could be clarified; e.g., Eq. (5) uses the gradient at θ(γ/2)2k+1 for both fine sub-steps—this is consistent with Euler but might be confusing and merits an explicit comment. A brief intuitive explanation of why Gaussian correlation matters but gradient-noise correlation does not (asymptotically) would improve readability.

Originality
Adapting RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting with stochastic gradients, proving a CLT with optimal correlation, and deriving finite-time bounds that match higher-order integrator accuracy using only first-order SDE discretization is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The technique is simple, generic, and likely to inspire follow-on work (e.g., multilevel SG-MCMC).

Significance
The proposed approach is practically attractive (minimal code changes; embarrassingly parallel; applicable to many SG-MCMC variants) and theoretically strong (better rates). If parallel resources are available, the method can deliver clear wall-clock improvements; even without, it is useful as a bias-reduction device when higher-order integrators are unavailable. The work advances understanding of how to control discretization bias in SG-MCMC without accept-reject steps.

Strengths
- Simple, generic idea with solid theory (consistency, CLT, finite-time bounds).
- Clear rate improvements over first-order SG-MCMC, matching higher-order integrators.
- Variance control via optimal correlation is principled and implementable.
- Experiments cover both controlled settings and large-scale applications; SGHMC extension shown.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions (smoothness, Poisson equation solvability) are strong; discussion of applicability to nonsmooth/posterior tails would help.
- Efficiency claims hinge on parallel availability. A per-gradient or per-epoch comparison (and ESS/time) would provide a fairer baseline for single-core settings.
- Comparisons to other bias/variance-reduction methods (e.g., control variates for SG-MCMC, preconditioned SGLD) are limited or absent.
- Impact of correlating minibatches across the two chains is not explored; could variance be further reduced by sharing minibatches?
- Step-size selection: while optimal exponents are derived, practical guidance for constants is empirical; an adaptive scheduler would increase usability.
- Minor: clarify whether the mini-batch seeds and Gaussian seeds are shared in experiments; provide ablations for Σ ≠ Id to empirically confirm variance behavior.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add per-gradient and ESS-per-time comparisons to decouple hardware effects.
- Include a logistic regression or Bayesian neural network experiment to test robustness beyond Gaussian/posterior-conjugate settings.
- Compare against at least one control-variate/variance-reduced SG-MCMC baseline, and against higher-order SGLD-type integrators if available.
- Provide an ablation on minibatch coupling (same vs independent mini-batches across chains) and on Σ to validate the theory.
- Discuss practical conditions under which the strong smoothness assumptions hold or can be relaxed (e.g., via mollification or Moreau envelopes).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant idea with rigorous analysis and tangible practical benefits.
- Generic technique applicable across SG-MCMC family.
- Convincing empirical results on large-scale data under realistic parallelization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong technical assumptions may limit formal applicability to some ML posteriors.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader and fairer in single-core settings.
- Lack of adaptive step-size guidance and limited exploration of gradient-noise coupling.

Overall recommendation
A strong contribution that is both theoretically solid and practically useful. I recommend acceptance, conditional on strengthening the empirical evaluation as suggested.
