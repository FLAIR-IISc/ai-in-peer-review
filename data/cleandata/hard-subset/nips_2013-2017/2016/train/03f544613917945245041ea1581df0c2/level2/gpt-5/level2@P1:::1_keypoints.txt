- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) and combining estimators to reduce discretization bias.  
- Estimator formed as 2·(fine) − (coarse) with variance controlled by correlating Gaussian increments; optimal coupling uses perfectly correlated Brownian increments (coarse increment = sum of two fine increments).  
- Theoretical contributions: CLT and non-asymptotic bounds giving bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal finite-time MSE O(K−4/5) vs SGLD O(K−2/3); decreasing stepsize optimal at α = 1/5.  
- Experiments on Gaussian toy problems, SGHMC variants, and MovieLens show improved accuracy and wall-clock efficiency, especially when the two chains run in parallel.  
- Builds on SGLD and RR literature; novelty is applying RR extrapolation to noisy-gradient SG-MCMC with tailored analysis and optimal Brownian coupling.  
- Strength: Simple, generic idea with clean theoretical justification and applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms (demonstrated for SGHMC).  
- Strength: Clear theoretical gains (CLT, optimal correlation, improved finite-time/asymptotic rates).  
- Strength: Practical and parallelizable with moderate implementation cost (first-order integrators only).  
- Strength: Experiments substantiate theory on synthetic and large-scale tasks; variance-control via Brownian coupling is insightful.  
- Weakness: Wall-clock comparisons exploit parallelism; need compute-normalized (per-gradient or FLOPs) and single-thread results to assess users without parallel resources.  
- Weakness: Missing head-to-head comparisons with high-order SG-MCMC (e.g., Chen et al. 2015); should include compute-normalized baselines on synthetic and MovieLens.  
- Weakness: Unclear minibatch coupling in practice — clarify whether coarse and fine chains share minibatch indices and provide ablation (shared vs independent minibatches).  
- Weakness: Need discussion/experiments on effects of different minibatch sizes or biased gradient estimators (subsampling without replacement, control variates).  
- Weakness: Strong smoothness/ergodicity assumptions (g ∈ C^9, moment conditions); summarize in main text and discuss applicability to non-convex/posterior landscapes (e.g., deep nets).  
- Weakness: Apparent typo in Eq.(5) regarding gradient evaluation; add compact pseudocode in main text to remove ambiguity.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 favors Σ = Id; include experiments comparing Σ = Id (coupled) vs Σ = 0 (independent Brownian) to illustrate effect.  
- Weakness: Provide complexity analysis (gradient evaluations per unit time, memory footprint); note that without parallelism the method costs roughly 3 gradient evaluations per outer iteration.  
- Quality: Theoretical development is careful and convincing; assumptions are standard but should be made explicit; experiments are solid but need more baselines and compute-normalized comparisons.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well organized; minor fixes needed (Eq.5 typo, more pseudocode, minibatch coupling details).  
- Originality: Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with correlated Brownian increments and derived rates appears novel and timely.  
- Significance: Offers a principled, simple way to reduce bias and improve rates without higher-order integrators; well-suited to parallel hardware and potentially impactful.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel, principled idea with clear theoretical and empirical benefits; generic, practical, and grounded in numerical analysis.  
- Cons for acceptance: Empirical comparisons should control for compute; missing high-order baselines and fuller exposition of implementation/assumption details; benefits depend on available parallel resources.  
- Questions for authors: (1) Do you share minibatch indices between chains and how does this affect variance/MSE? (2) Can you provide compute-normalized results and single-thread timings? (3) How does SGRRLD compare to second-order SG-LD/HMC under equal compute? (4) Can analysis extend to mildly biased gradient estimators and will RR still cancel leading-order bias? (5) Practical guidance for choosing α and γ beyond asymptotic rules?
