This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias of existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique for accelerating sequence convergence. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs in a specific way to cancel the leading-order error term. This work builds upon the extensive literature on SG-MCMC methods, particularly SGLD, and also draws from prior work that applied RR extrapolation to the numerical solution of SDEs with exact gradients. The primary novelty lies in adapting and analyzing this technique for the stochastic gradient setting, where gradient noise introduces additional complexity.

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and provides strong guarantees for the proposed SGRRLD algorithm. The authors prove that the estimator is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem (Theorem 1). Importantly, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE) (Theorem 2). These results show that SGRRLD can achieve a faster MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This matches the rate of methods using more complex higher-order integrators, but is achieved using only the simple first-order Euler-Maruyama scheme. The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on both a synthetic linear Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD over SGLD and also show its applicability to other SG-MCMC methods like SGHMC.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for reducing bias in SG-MCMC is well-established, and the introduction to RR extrapolation is clear and concise. The proposed algorithm is described precisely in Section 3, and the theoretical results are stated clearly in Section 4, with technical details and proofs appropriately deferred to the supplement. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the results, presented in figures, are easy to interpret and directly support the paper's theoretical claims.

**Originality:**
The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and clever contribution. While both SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation are established techniques in their respective fields, their combination to address the bias-variance trade-off in scalable Bayesian inference appears to be new. The paper clearly situates its contribution with respect to prior work, highlighting how it differs from and improves upon standard SGLD and how it provides an alternative path to the higher accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. The analysis of how to correlate the noise terms between the two chains to control variance is also a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners in Bayesian machine learning. SGRRLD offers a practical and relatively simple way to improve the accuracy of widely used SG-MCMC samplers. A key advantage of the method is its inherent parallelism; the two required chains can be run independently, making it well-suited for modern multi-core architectures. The demonstration that SGRRLD can match the accuracy of higher-order integrators without the implementation complexity is a strong practical result. This work could inspire further investigation into using other numerical acceleration techniques for scalable inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant method for reducing the bias of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and demonstrates a superior rate of convergence for the proposed method compared to standard first-order SG-MCMC.
*   The empirical results are strong and consistent across both synthetic and large-scale real-world experiments, validating the theoretical claims.
*   The proposed algorithm is simple to implement and naturally parallelizable, enhancing its practical utility.

**Arguments against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The main weakness of the proposed method is its computational cost. SGRRLD requires approximately twice the computational effort of a single SGLD chain (one step at γ and two steps at γ/2). While the authors correctly argue that this can be mitigated by running the chains in parallel to achieve similar wall-clock time, this assumes the availability of parallel resources. A valuable addition to the experimental section would be a comparison against a single SGLD chain run with an equivalent total computational budget (e.g., for twice as many iterations). This would provide a clearer picture for practitioners working in serial environments.
*   A related point is the increased memory footprint from maintaining two chains simultaneously. While likely not an issue in the presented experiments, it could be a consideration for extremely large models and might be worth a brief mention.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically useful algorithm. The strengths significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work represents a solid contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
