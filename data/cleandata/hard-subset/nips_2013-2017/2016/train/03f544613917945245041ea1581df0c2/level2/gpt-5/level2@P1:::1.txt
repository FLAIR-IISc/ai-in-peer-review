Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce discretization bias while keeping variance controlled. Concretely, the method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon and forms a time-averaged estimator equivalent to 2·(fine) − (coarse). Variance is controlled by correlating the Gaussian increments of the two chains; the optimal choice uses perfectly correlated Brownian increments (the coarse increment equals the sum of the two fine increments). The authors establish a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds: for constant step size, the bias and MSE are O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), respectively, implying an optimal finite-time MSE rate O(K−4/5), improving over SGLD’s O(K−2/3). With decreasing step size γk ∝ k−α the optimal rate is attained at α = 1/5. Experiments on a Gaussian model, SGHMC variants, and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) show improved accuracy and wall-clock efficiency, especially when running the two chains in parallel.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and the subsequent non/asymptotic analyses of SG-MCMC (e.g., Teh et al., JMLR 2016; Chen et al., NIPS 2015). RR extrapolation for SDEs traces to Talay–Tubaro and has been developed for invariant measures and MLMC by Lemaire, Pagès and coauthors. The specific contribution here is to carry RR extrapolation over to the noisy-gradient SG-MCMC setting, analyze the estimator under unbiased stochastic gradients, and show optimality of correlated Gaussian increments. To my knowledge, this application to SG-MCMC and the derived rates are novel.

Strengths
- Simple, generic idea with a clean theoretical justification; can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms (demonstrated on SGHMC).
- Clear theoretical gains: a CLT with optimal correlation, non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds, and improved finite-time and asymptotic rates relative to first-order SG-MCMC.
- Practical and parallelizable: two chains can be run concurrently; implementation cost is moderate (first-order integrators only).
- Experiments substantiate theory on synthetic data and a large-scale recommendation task, with convincing improvements.
- Discussion of variance control via Brownian coupling is insightful and actionable.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental fairness and compute budget: Wall-clock comparisons exploit parallelism unavailable to single-chain SGLD. Please add results matching total gradient evaluations (or FLOPs) on a single thread/GPU, and report improvements per gradient computation. This will quantify the trade-off for users without parallel resources.
- Comparison to high-order SG-MCMC: Since a central claim is “accuracy of higher-order integrators with first-order steps,” include head-to-head comparisons with the high-order SG-LD/HMC methods of Chen et al. (NIPS 2015) on MovieLens (and synthetic), normalized by compute budget and wall-clock.
- Coupling of stochastic gradients: The theory highlights Brownian correlation; the role of coupling minibatch gradients is less clear. Please:
  - Clarify whether the two chains use the same minibatch indices per (coarse/fine) time step. An ablation comparing shared vs independent minibatches would be useful for finite-time variance.
  - Discuss consequences when the two chains use different minibatch sizes or biased gradient estimators (e.g., subsampling without replacement, control variates).
- Assumptions and scope: The CLT and bounds require smoothness (g ∈ C9) and standard ergodicity/ moment conditions. Please summarize these assumptions in the main paper and comment on applicability to common non-convex/posterior landscapes (e.g., deep nets).
- Algorithmic clarity: Equation (5) appears to evaluate the gradient at θ(γ/2)2k+1 in the first fine step; this seems like a typo (should be θ(γ/2)2k). Please correct and add a compact pseudocode in the main text to avoid ambiguity.
- Effect of Σ and coupling: Theorem 1 indicates Σ=Id is optimal. It would be informative to include an experiment showing performance for Σ=0 (independent Brownian) vs Σ=Id (coupled).
- Cost analysis: Provide a brief complexity analysis (gradient evaluations per unit time, memory footprint). When parallelism is not available, the method costs roughly 3 gradient evaluations per outer iteration; discussing throughput under typical hardware would help practitioners.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and largely convincing. The CLT and non-asymptotic bounds clearly explain the rate improvements and the role of correlated noise. Assumptions are standard for this line of analysis but should be surfaced more prominently. The experiments are solid and support the claims, though additional baselines and compute-normalized comparisons would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized with helpful context. Minor issues: likely typo in Eq. (5), limited algorithmic pseudocode in the main text, and lack of detail on minibatch coupling. These are straightforward to address.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with analysis tailored to stochastic gradients and correlated Brownian increments appears novel and timely. The extension to SGHMC further supports generality.

Significance
The method offers a principled, simple way to reduce bias and improve rates without higher-order integrators, and is well-suited to modern parallel hardware. If adopted, it could become a standard enhancement to SG-MCMC toolkits.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled idea with clear theoretical and empirical benefits.
- Generic and practical; parallel-friendly with minimal implementation overhead.
- Strong connection to established numerical analysis (RR) adapted to SG-MCMC.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical comparisons could better control for compute; high-order SG-MCMC baselines missing on large-scale tasks.
- Some implementation details (minibatch coupling, Σ experiments) and assumptions are not fully elaborated in the main text.
- Benefits may depend on parallel resources; single-thread budgets need clearer assessment.

Questions for authors
1) Do you share minibatch indices between coarse and fine chains? How does this affect variance/MSE empirically?
2) Can you provide compute-normalized results (per gradient evaluation) and single-thread timings?
3) How does SGRRLD compare to second-order SG-LD/HMC on MovieLens under equal compute?
4) Can the analysis be extended to mildly biased gradient estimators (e.g., with control variates), and would RR still cancel leading-order discretization bias?
5) Any guidance for choosing α and γ in practice beyond the asymptotic rules (e.g., heuristics from preliminary runs)?

Overall, this is a strong submission with a compelling idea, solid theory, and promising empirical evidence. With the suggested clarifications and additional comparisons, it would make a valuable contribution.
