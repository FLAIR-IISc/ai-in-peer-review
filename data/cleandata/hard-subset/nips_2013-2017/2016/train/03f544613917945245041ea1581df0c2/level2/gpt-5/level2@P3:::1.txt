Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a simple and generic bias-reduction technique for stochastic-gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC). The method applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—classically used to accelerate weak-discretization error in SDE simulation (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015)—to SG-MCMC by running two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and combining their ergodic averages via an RR estimator. To prevent an increase in variance when differencing the two chains, the work employs correlated Brownian increments and proves that the asymptotic variance is minimized by using perfectly correlated noise between the coarse and fine chains.

Theoretical results establish: (i) consistency and a central limit theorem (CLT) for the RR estimator; (ii) improved non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds compared to standard SGLD; and (iii) optimal rates with appropriately chosen step-size schedules. With constant step size, the bias and MSE bounds scale as O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), respectively, improving over SGLD’s O(γ + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ2 + 1/(Kγ)) (Teh, Vollmer & Zygalakis, 2016). With decreasing step size γk ∝ k−α, the optimal MSE rate for SGRRLD is O(K−4/5) at α = 1/5, versus O(K−2/3) for SGLD. Empirically, the method improves posterior variance estimation for a linear Gaussian model, matches the accuracy of higher-order integrators for SGHMC while using only first-order updates, and yields better test RMSE versus wall-clock time on large-scale matrix factorization tasks (MovieLens datasets). The approach is readily parallelizable since the two chains can be run independently.

Relation to prior work
SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and its extensions (e.g., SGHMC, Chen et al., 2014; thermostat methods, Ding et al., 2014; Ma et al., 2015) enable scalable Bayesian sampling. Recent analyses bound discretization bias and MSE and show benefits of higher-order integrators (Chen, Ding & Carin, 2015; Teh et al., 2016). RR extrapolation is well-established for SDEs with exact gradients (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup, 2015), including consistency/CLTs for invariant measure approximations. This work appears to be the first to adapt RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients and to analyze both asymptotic and finite-time behavior under correlated Brownian increments.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: a drop-in technique that applies to first-order SG-MCMC algorithms and can be parallelized.
- Solid theory: CLT with variance characterization showing optimality of perfectly correlated Brownian increments; non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds with improved rates over SGLD; discussion of optimal step-size schedules.
- Practical relevance: empirical improvements on both synthetic and large-scale real datasets; parallelization brings wall-clock parity with SGLD despite using two chains.
- Connection to high-order accuracy: achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators while retaining first-order updates, and suggests potential for recursive RR for further gains.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions and regularity: the analysis relies on high-order smoothness (e.g., C9 via 7th-order Taylor expansions) and existence of Poisson equation solutions; practical guidance on when these hold (e.g., dissipativity/strong log-concavity) would improve clarity.
- Gradient noise conditions: to avoid introducing additional bias, both chains must use gradient estimators with the same distribution (e.g., identical minibatch size); the impact of using different minibatch sizes or using shared vs independent minibatch indices is not empirically explored.
- Variance control relies critically on perfectly correlated Brownian increments; an ablation showing the degradation with independent noise would strengthen the empirical story.
- Computational budget fairness: while the two-chain method can be parallelized, single-core or memory-bound settings may not achieve equal wall-clock time; a sensitivity analysis would help practitioners.
- Scope of baselines: the comparison to higher-order methods is shown for SGHMC; a comparison to higher-order Langevin discretizations or to control-variate SG-MCMC variants (e.g., variance-reduced SGLD) could better position the gains.

Quality
Theoretical developments are careful and in line with SDE/RR extrapolation literature, adapted to the stochastic-gradient setting. Claims are supported by proofs (in the supplement) and by experiments that track bias/MSE scaling with γ and K, as predicted. Some constants in non-asymptotic bounds depend on gradient estimator moments; a brief discussion of their magnitude in typical minibatch regimes would aid interpretability.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well-structured. A few notational typos in the fine-chain updates and arguments in Eq. (5) could be corrected for precision. The RR estimator definition and weighting (Eq. (6)) is sound; a brief derivation to connect it to the classical 2πγ/2 − πγ form may help readers. Practical recommendations on constructing correlated Gaussian increments and on whether to reuse minibatch indices across chains would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a rigorous treatment of correlated noise and stochastic gradients appears novel. The combination of theory and practice for this setting is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
The method provides a simple path to improved accuracy and convergence rates for SG-MCMC, with minimal algorithmic complexity and clear parallelization benefits. Given the broad use of SG-MCMC in large-scale Bayesian inference, this has potential impact.

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized)
- Essential:
  - Clarify the precise assumptions on U and L under which the Poisson equation solution and the CLT hold; include concrete examples (e.g., strongly log-concave posteriors).
  - Add an ablation comparing correlated vs independent Brownian increments to empirically confirm variance-minimization claims.
  - Specify recommended practice for minibatch selection across chains (shared vs independent indices) and report its effect.

- Nice to have:
  - Evaluate scenarios where both chains cannot be run in parallel (single-core), reporting wall-clock vs iteration-based comparisons.
  - Include a baseline with a higher-order Langevin integrator for SGLD, or a control-variate SG-MCMC method, to further contextualize gains.
  - Provide step-size tuning guidance (constant vs decreasing schedules) and a brief pseudocode in the main text.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method with theoretically justified improvements in bias/MSE rates.
- Strong alignment between theory and experiments.
- Practical benefits with minimal implementation overhead and good parallelization properties.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong smoothness/regularity assumptions with limited practical guidance.
- Limited analysis of minibatch correlation choices and single-core compute scenarios.
- Baseline coverage could be broader.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and carefully analyzed contribution that is likely to be of interest to the SG-MCMC community. The method’s simplicity and the demonstrated gains support acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions and adding small empirical ablations.
