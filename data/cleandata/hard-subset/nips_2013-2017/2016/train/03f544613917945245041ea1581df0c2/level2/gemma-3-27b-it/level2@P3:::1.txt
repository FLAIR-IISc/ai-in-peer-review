This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel SG-MCMC algorithm designed to reduce bias compared to SGLD. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, requiring two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes. The work provides theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean-squared error, claiming improved convergence rates over SGLD. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real datasets support these claims.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents a compelling approach to addressing a known limitation of SG-MCMC – its inherent bias. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established numerical technique, is a strong point. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, establishing important properties of the proposed algorithm. The experimental results are thorough, demonstrating performance gains on both simple Gaussian models and a more complex matrix factorization task. Comparisons to SGHMC and higher-order integrators (through SGRRHMC) add further value. 

However, several areas could benefit from improvement. While the paper references prior work on SGLD and related techniques extensively, the discussion of the practical challenges and trade-offs associated with implementing RR extrapolation in an SG-MCMC setting feels somewhat limited. Specifically, the paper emphasizes correlated Brownian motions to control variance, but the implications of this correlation on parallelization—beyond stating it *is* well-suited—could be elaborated.  Does maintaining this correlation introduce significant communication overhead in distributed settings?  

The theoretical analysis, while thorough, relies on several assumptions regarding the smoothness of the potential energy function and the properties of the gradient estimators. A more detailed discussion of the robustness of the algorithm to violations of these assumptions would be valuable. Furthermore, the constant C in Theorems 2, while mathematically necessary, provides limited practical insight. An attempt to characterize or bound C in terms of problem-specific parameters would strengthen the analysis.

Regarding the experiments, the step size selection seems somewhat arbitrary. While justified based on finding optimal rates, a sensitivity analysis showing the impact of different step size schedules on performance would be informative.  Also, the experiment comparing SGRRHMC to SGHMC and SGHMC-s shows promising results, but the details of the SGHMC-s implementation (specifically, the higher-order integrator used) should be clarified for reproducibility and a fairer comparison. The impact of the mini-batch size B, a crucial parameter in SG-MCMC, is largely fixed; exploring its influence on the performance of SGRRLD would strengthen the experimental validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, addressing a key limitation of the method.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating desirable properties of the algorithm.
*   Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Potential for significant performance improvements in large-scale Bayesian inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical challenges and trade-offs in implementation (e.g., correlated Brownian motion overhead).
*   Theoretical analysis relies on potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including sensitivity analysis and a more detailed comparison to related methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is a well-motivated and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias and improving convergence rates. While some aspects of the analysis and evaluation could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work is high. The potential impact on applications relying on scalable Bayesian inference justifies acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, elaborating on the practical considerations of correlated Brownian motions and providing more detailed experimental results would significantly enhance the paper’s value.
