This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias of existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. The authors cleverly manage the variance of the resulting estimator by using correlated noise between the two chains.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method. It establishes that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a Central Limit Theorem. More importantly, it derives non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than SGLD. For instance, with an optimally chosen step size, SGRRLD's MSE converges at a rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. This allows a first-order integrator scheme to achieve the theoretical accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on both a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem for movie recommendation.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant idea. It directly addresses one of the primary weaknesses of SG-MCMC methods: the bias introduced by the numerical discretization, which often limits their accuracy. The potential to achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods with a simple modification of a first-order method is a significant contribution.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is backed by a solid theoretical analysis. The authors provide both asymptotic (CLT) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) results that formally characterize the advantages of their method. These results clearly explain why and by how much SGRRLD improves upon SGLD.

3.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and should be straightforward to implement by anyone familiar with SGLD. It essentially requires running two instances of a standard algorithm in parallel. This inherent parallelism makes it well-suited for modern multi-core architectures, as demonstrated by the authors.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method. The synthetic experiments clearly validate the theoretical claims about bias and MSE reduction. The large-scale matrix factorization experiment on real-world datasets shows that SGRRLD can achieve better accuracy in less wall-clock time than SGLD, highlighting its practical utility.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification

1.  **Clarity on Computational Cost and Fairness of Comparison:** The wall-clock time comparison in the matrix factorization experiments (Figure 5) is compelling but relies on a parallel execution environment. An SGRRLD iteration involves three gradient computations (one for the coarse chain, two for the fine chain), while an SGLD iteration involves only one. For the experiment, SGLD runs for 10,500 iterations (10,500 total gradient steps), while SGRRLD runs for 5,250 iterations (15,750 total gradient steps). The authors state that with two parallel processes, the wall-clock time is determined by the longer chain (2 * 5,250 = 10,500 steps), making the comparison fair. This is a valid point, but it would strengthen the paper to:
    *   Explicitly state the total number of gradient evaluations for both methods to make the computational cost transparent.
    *   Briefly discuss the performance implications for users on single-core machines, where SGRRLD would be approximately three times slower per iteration.

2.  **Apparent Discrepancy in Experimental Results:** There seems to be a mismatch between the theoretical claims and the experimental results in Figure 3 regarding the optimal step size decay rate. The text (e.g., line 272, line 280) states that the optimal choice of the exponent α for the bias is 1/3. However, in the left plot of Figure 3, the red curve (α=0.2) appears to achieve a lower final bias than the cyan curve (α=1/3). Could the authors please clarify this observation? Is this due to the constants involved or an artifact of the finite number of iterations?

3.  **High Variance in Convergence Plots:** The convergence plots in Figure 3, particularly for the bias with α=0.2, exhibit significant fluctuations. This makes it difficult to visually confirm the convergence rate. Could the authors comment on the source of this high variance in the plot and whether it is expected?

4.  **Assumptions on Smoothness:** For the theoretical results, the main text refers to a "smooth function f" and "appropriate conditions," deferring the details to the supplement. While this is standard practice, the clarity of the main paper could be improved by briefly mentioning the key smoothness requirements (e.g., that the solution `g` to the associated Poisson equation must be C⁹), as this is a strong condition.

### Minor Comments

*   To improve readability, consider consistently using bold notation for vectors and matrices throughout the paper.
*   The acronym "RR" is used in line 94 before it is defined. Please define it on its first use.
*   In Equation 1, the notation `ϑ` is used for the continuous-time process, while `θ` is used for the discrete approximation. This is standard, but a brief note clarifying this distinction might be helpful for some readers.
*   The discussion in line 256 regarding the `1/(Kγ)` term dominating for small `γ` is for SGRRLD. The plots in Figure 2 show that SGLD exhibits similar behavior. A brief comment comparing the onset or magnitude of this effect in SGLD versus SGRRLD could add value to the analysis.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a clever, well-supported, and practical method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the clarity of the experimental presentation and discussion, which can be addressed in a revision.
