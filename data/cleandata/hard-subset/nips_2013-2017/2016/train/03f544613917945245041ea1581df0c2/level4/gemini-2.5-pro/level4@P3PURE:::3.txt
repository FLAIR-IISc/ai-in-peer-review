### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of asymptotic bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods, which are widely used for Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets. The authors propose reducing this bias using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a classic numerical analysis technique. The core idea is to run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel: Chain 1 with a step size `γ` and Chain 2 with a step size `γ/2`. To maintain equivalent computational cost, Chain 2 is run for twice the number of steps as Chain 1. A crucial element is the introduction of a correlation structure between the injected Gaussian noise of the two chains, where the noise `α_k` for step `k` of Chain 1 is derived from the noise terms `β_{2k-1}` and `β_{2k}` of Chain 2 via `α_k = (β_{2k-1} + β_{2k}) / sqrt(2)`.

The final expectation of a function is computed as a linear combination of the estimates from the two chains. This combined estimator is shown to reduce the bias from `O(γ)` (for a single chain) to `O(γ^2)`. The authors also prove a corresponding reduction in the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the estimates.

### Strengths

A key strength of the proposed method is its simplicity in both concept and implementation. It does not require major modifications to the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm. Furthermore, the approach appears to be general, applicable to a broad class of SG-MCMC algorithms. This generality gives the method the potential to be highly impactful within the SG-MCMC community.

### Novelty and Clarity

While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its application to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC methods appears to be novel and is a valuable contribution. The paper is well-written, and the presentation of the method and its theoretical underpinnings is clear.

### Comments and Questions

*   **Generality:** Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, are there specific conditions required beyond the algorithm satisfying the bias expansion `π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ^2)` detailed in Section 2.2?
*   **Scope of Application:** Could this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as those using sub-sampling in the Metropolis-Hastings test? What about distributed variants like Distributed SGLD?
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed scheme requires the chain with the larger step size to remain idle while the other completes its run, leading to significant wasted computation. As an alternative, would it be possible to run three chains for the same number of steps: one with step size `γ` and two separate chains with step size `γ/2`? How would such a scheme affect the variance of the final estimate?
*   **Higher-Order Extrapolation:** Is there a straightforward extension of this method to use more than two step sizes for even higher-order bias correction?
*   **Correlation of Mini-batches:** The paper establishes that the injected noise between chains must be correlated. However, the effect of correlation between the mini-batches used for gradient estimation is less clear. For variance reduction, is it optimal to use the same mini-batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this correlation negligible?
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) would be more compelling if the methods were run to convergence. A comparison with a standard SGD baseline is also essential. While the current plot demonstrates an improvement over SGLD, showing that the proposed Bayesian method outperforms SGD would better motivate its use in a setting where being Bayesian is advantageous. Including an SGD baseline would also put the final errors of SGRR-LD and SGLD into a clearer perspective.
*   **Accessibility:** While the method is clearly presented, the inclusion of an algorithm box or pseudo-code early in the paper would greatly enhance its accessibility to a wider audience. Many practitioners may not read the theoretical sections in detail, and a concise algorithm would enable them to implement and experiment with the method more readily.
