

This paper applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to develop a novel sampling algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The authors aim to reduce the bias inherent in SG-MCMC methods while maintaining reasonable variance, leveraging RR extrapolation to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel. The paper builds upon existing work on SG-MCMC, particularly Welling and Teh's SGLD (2011) and recent convergence analysis of SG-MCMC methods (Chen et al., 2015), by introducing a numerical sequence acceleration technique that achieves higher convergence rates without requiring higher-order integrators.

The main contributions include formal theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves O(γ²) bias and O(γ⁴) variance bounds, compared to SGLD's O(γ) bias and O(γ²) variance. For fixed step size γ, SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), representing a significant improvement. The authors support their theoretical claims with experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and real-world MovieLens datasets.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical novelty: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a fresh perspective that significantly improves convergence rates without requiring complex higher-order integrators.
2. Clear theoretical results: The paper provides rigorous bounds on bias and MSE, demonstrating SGRRLD's superior convergence properties.
3. Practical implementation: The algorithm is straightforward to implement, with a clear description and pseudocode provided.
4. Strong synthetic experiments: The linear Gaussian model experiments clearly demonstrate SGRRLD's advantage in reducing bias compared to SGLD.
5. Appropriate parallelization: The authors correctly note that SGRRLD's parallel structure is well-suited for distributed architectures.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited real-world validation: While MovieLens experiments show modest RMSE improvements, the magnitude of these improvements is not compellingly demonstrated compared to alternative baselines.
2. Experimental comparisons: The real data experiments compare SGRRLD (using step sizes γ and γ/2) against SGLD with step size γ, rather than against SGLD with step size γ/2 (which would have lower bias and could also run two chains in parallel).
3. Theoretical assumptions: The paper does not sufficiently clarify the conditions required for the potential function U (e.g., whether U can be multi-modal) to satisfy the theoretical results.
4. Chain correlation: The authors do not provide empirical evidence of the correlation between the two chains in practice, which is crucial for the theoretical variance reduction.

The paper excels in Quality and Originality, making a meaningful contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-justified, and the approach represents a novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The clarity of the paper is good, with clear explanations of the algorithm and theoretical results, though the experimental section could be strengthened. The significance is moderate, as the method provides a practical way to improve existing SG-MCMC algorithms, but the real-world impact could be more convincingly demonstrated.

For acceptance consideration, the pros are:
- Theoretical novelty and soundness
- Clear presentation of the algorithm
- Demonstrated improvement in synthetic experiments
- Practical parallelization benefits

The cons are:
- Insufficient real-world validation of the claimed improvements
- Experimental comparisons that do not fully test the theoretical advantages
- Unclear theoretical conditions for the potential function U
- Lack of empirical evidence for chain correlation in practice

I am impressed by the theoretical contribution, which represents a significant improvement to the SG-MCMC framework. However, the real-world experiments do not sufficiently demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The authors should address the experimental comparison issue by comparing against SGLD with step size γ/2 (with two parallel chains) to properly isolate the benefit of RR extrapolation. More comprehensive real-world experiments would strengthen the case for the method's practical value.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to SG-MCMC that advances the state of the art in convergence rates. While the current real-world experiments show some improvement, they could better demonstrate the practical significance of the approach. With minor revisions to address the experimental comparisons and provide more comprehensive real-world validation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
