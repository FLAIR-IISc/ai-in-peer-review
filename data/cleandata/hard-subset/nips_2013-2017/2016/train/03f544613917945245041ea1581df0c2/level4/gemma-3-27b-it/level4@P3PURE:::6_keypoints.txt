- Proposes accelerating SG-MCMC convergence using Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (higher-order integrator) by running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise.  
- Provides theoretical convergence analysis establishing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Synthetic Gaussian experiments verify the theoretical bias and MSE bounds.  
- Large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data show practical benefits over standard SGLD.  
- Well-motivated technique, clear exposition, and good positioning within related work.  
- Concern: novelty may be incremental because effects of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC were studied in prior work [10] and Richardson–Romberg has been used for invariant distributions in [23].  
- Concern: practical applicability limited by required communication — correlated Brownian motion requires per-step communication between the two chains, but communication cost is not discussed.  
- Concern: experimental comparison lacks detail and may be unfair; resource allocation between SGRRLD and SGLD is unclear (e.g., whether SGLD used the same resources as the two SGRRLD chains).  
- Question: unclear how dividing gradient computation across two SGRRLD chains would reduce wall-clock time compared to assigning full resources to a single SGLD chain.  
- Suggestion: authors should report implementation/resource details and compare against running two independent (embarrassingly parallel) SGLD chains.  
- Minor: the phrase "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is duplicated (lines 25 and 70).
