- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two SGLD chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) and combining outputs to reduce bias.
- Uses correlated Brownian motions and adjusted iteration counts (second chain runs twice) to control variance while enabling RR extrapolation.
- Reduces bias from O(γ) (SGLD) to O(γ^2) and shows improved convergence rates and accuracy.
- Provides theoretical guarantees: consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.
- Empirically validated on synthetic data and matrix factorization, showing improvements over SGLD and in some cases SGHMC.
- Novel application of a classical numerical RR technique to SG-MCMC, filling a gap in literature.
- Strong theoretical rigor with detailed proofs and error analysis, increasing credibility.
- Clear exposition of algorithm, theory, and experiments, making the core idea easy to grasp.
- Method is potentially generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms, increasing its impact.
- Main weakness: computational overhead from running two parallel chains, including idle time for one chain.
- Implementation complexity: requirement to correlate Brownian motions adds practical challenges, especially in distributed settings.
- Experimental limitation: matrix factorization comparison lacks a baseline against SGD, reducing practical benchmarking.
- Applicability conditions for RR extrapolation are stated vaguely and need clearer, more explicit criteria.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically supported, simple to implement, and impactful to the SG-MCMC community.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns due to overhead, correlation implementation difficulties, and incomplete experimental comparisons.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
- Suggested improvements: analyze runtime/overhead and alternative scheduling, clarify applicability conditions, include SGD comparison in experiments, add algorithm pseudocode, and discuss practical correlation challenges in distributed environments.
