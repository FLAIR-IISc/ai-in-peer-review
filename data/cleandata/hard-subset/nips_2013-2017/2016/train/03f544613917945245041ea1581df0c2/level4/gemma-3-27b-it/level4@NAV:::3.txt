Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) offers a computationally efficient approach to Bayesian posterior inference with large datasets and has garnered significant attention recently. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias – a failure to precisely converge to the target posterior distribution. This paper introduces a Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique to mitigate this bias. The core idea is elegantly simple: parallel execution of two SG-MCMC chains, where the second chain utilizes half the step size of the first, but runs for twice the number of iterations.  Crucially, the Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated, defined as  alphak = (beta{2k-1) + beta{2k})/sqrt(2), with alphak and betak representing the noise at time step k for Chains 1 and 2, respectively.  Estimates of expectations with respect to the target distribution are then obtained by subtracting the Chain 1 estimate from the Chain 2 estimate. This results in a reduced bias of O(gamma^2), an improvement over the O(gamma) bias inherent in estimates from either chain alone (where gamma denotes the step size). The authors also demonstrate a corresponding reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates. 

I particularly appreciate the method’s conceptual simplicity and ease of implementation, as summarized in the paper, and its minimal disruption to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Furthermore, the technique appears broadly applicable across a range of SG-MCMC methods, suggesting a potentially substantial impact on the field. 

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, its application within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC context is currently limited.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and presents the material in a clear and accessible manner.

**Comments/Questions:**

*   The applicability of this technique to *all* SG-MCMC methods warrants further clarification. Beyond satisfying the bound outlined in section 2.2 (pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)), are there additional necessary conditions?
*   Could this approach be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that do not rely on stochastic gradients, such as those based on sub-sampling in Metropolis-Hastings, or to Distributed SGLD?
*   The disparity in computational effort, with one chain running for twice as long as the other, introduces some inefficiency. Would employing three chains – one with step size gamma and two with step size gamma/2, all running concurrently for the same number of steps – be a viable alternative?  How would this impact the variance of the resulting estimate?
*   Is there a generalization of this method to incorporate more than two step sizes?
*   The correlation structure of the injected noise is described, but the effect of correlation *between* mini-batches remains unclear. Specifically, is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k<sup>th</sup> iteration of Chain 1 and the 2k-1<sup>th</sup> and 2k<sup>th</sup> iterations of Chain 2? Or is this correlation effect negligible?
*   The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) would benefit from being run to convergence, and a comparison against standard SGD should be included. While the current results show an advantage over SGLD, demonstrating superior performance to SGD in a scenario where the Bayesian approach is beneficial would significantly strengthen the paper's impact. Given the limited practical use of Bayesian matrix factorization, empirical evidence of its advantages is crucial for adoption. Presenting an SGD baseline would also provide useful context for interpreting the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   Adding an algorithm box, containing pseudocode, would enhance the paper’s accessibility, particularly for practitioners less familiar with the theoretical underpinnings. Many users may skip the more theoretical sections; providing readily available pseudocode would allow them to implement and evaluate the method without requiring a deep understanding of the theory.
