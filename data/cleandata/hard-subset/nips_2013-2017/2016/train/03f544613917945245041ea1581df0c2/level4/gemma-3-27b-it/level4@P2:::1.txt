This paper introduces a novel approach to improving the efficiency of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, specifically Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE). RRE, a well-established technique in numerical analysis for accelerating convergence, is adapted to the context of large-scale Bayesian inference. The paper bridges a connection between the MCMC and stochastic differential equation (SDE) literature, which is a valuable contribution. The authors propose Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), provide theoretical guarantees for its convergence, demonstrating improvements over standard SGLD, and validate these findings with synthetic and real-world experiments. 

The paper is technically sound and clearly written. The core idea of applying RRE to SGLD is conceptually simple and computationally efficient, requiring only parallel execution of two SGLD chains with different step sizes. The theoretical analysis establishes asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem for SGRRLD, alongside non-asymptotic bounds on bias and mean squared error.  Experimental results on both Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task (movie recommendation) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, with improvements in accuracy and convergence rates compared to SGLD and, in some cases, Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (SGHMC). The authors also highlight the algorithm's suitability for parallel and distributed computing architectures.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying RRE to SG-MCMC is a novel and insightful contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes a solid theoretical analysis, providing convergence guarantees and error bounds.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers from both the MCMC and SDE communities.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of SGRRLD in various settings.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is computationally straightforward to implement and benefits from parallelization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Gains in Large-Scale Experiments:** While the improvements on the large-scale matrix factorization task are consistent, they are relatively modest. Further investigation into scenarios where SGRRLD provides more substantial benefits could strengthen the paper.
*   **Figure 1a Clarification Needed:** The visualization in Figure 1a could be misleading; it depicts a Gaussian distribution fitted to the empirical posterior mean and variance, rather than the empirical posterior distribution itself.  This should be explicitly stated in the caption or clarified in the text.
*   **Step Size vs. Bias Explanation:** The observation that bias does not necessarily decrease with decreasing step size (Figure 2a) is somewhat counterintuitive. A brief discussion explaining this phenomenon would enhance understanding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to improving SG-MCMC methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in various settings.
*   The paper bridges a gap between the MCMC and SDE literature, potentially benefiting both communities.
*   The clarity and rigor of the paper make it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The improvements observed in the large-scale experiments are relatively modest, potentially limiting the immediate impact of the method.
*   Minor clarity issues (Figure 1a) and missing explanations (Figure 2a) could be addressed with minor revisions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper. The proposed method is a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale Bayesian inference, and the theoretical and experimental results are compelling.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. The potential for wider adoption and impact is good, especially within the growing area of scalable MCMC methods.
