To reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, the authors propose a novel algorithm called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). This approach leverages Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established technique for solving Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs). Specifically, SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel, offering potential for straightforward parallelization without inter-chain communication. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on both bias and mean squared error. Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets support the effectiveness of the proposed method. The core idea is appealing, effectively combining Richardson-Romberg extrapolation with SGLD to achieve improved performance. The theoretical results validate this efficiency by showing a faster convergence rate compared to standard SG-MCMC methods, although the authors acknowledge this rate can also be obtained using higher-order integrators (and a comparison with such an integrator would strengthen the paper). 

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. The discussion of step size selection could be clarified, and currently appears somewhat inconsistent. Additionally, there’s a minor discrepancy between Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material and Equation (5) in the main text; resolving this would improve clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a strong theoretical foundation, including proofs of consistency and convergence rates.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Experiments on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD.
*   **Parallelizability:** The algorithm is well-suited for parallel computation, a key advantage for large-scale problems.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Comparison to Higher-Order Integrators:** While the authors acknowledge that higher-order integrators can achieve similar convergence rates, a direct empirical comparison would strengthen the argument for SGRRLD’s practical value.
*   **Step Size Discussion:** The discussion of optimal step size selection could be made more clear and concise.
*   **Minor Discrepancy:** The inconsistency between Algorithm 1 and Equation (5) requires attention to ensure clarity and correctness.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for improving the performance of SG-MCMC.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD.
*   The algorithm is well-suited for large-scale applications due to its parallelizability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a direct comparison to higher-order integrators weakens the justification for the proposed method’s practical utility.
*   The minor inconsistencies require clarification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While some minor improvements could be made, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the inconsistencies and provide a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly higher-order integrators.
