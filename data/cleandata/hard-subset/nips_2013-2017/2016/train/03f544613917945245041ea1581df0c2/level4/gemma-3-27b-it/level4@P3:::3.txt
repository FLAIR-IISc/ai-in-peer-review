## Review of "Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo"

This paper addresses a key challenge in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) – the inherent bias of these methods when applied to large-scale Bayesian inference. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which leverages the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce this bias. The core idea is elegant: running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs to achieve a more accurate estimate. This work builds upon the established line of research in scaling MCMC to large datasets, notably the foundational work of Welling & Teh (2011) introducing Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), and subsequent extensions like SGHMC (Chen et al., 2014) and advancements in bias reduction through higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015). The authors convincingly demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that SGRRLD offers improved convergence rates and accuracy compared to SGLD, achieving results comparable to methods employing higher-order integrators while maintaining computational efficiency.

**Summary:** The paper introduces SGRRLD, a bias-reducing SG-MCMC algorithm. It runs two SGLD chains in parallel, one with step size γ and the other with γ/2, ensuring the second chain runs for twice as many iterations. Correlated Brownian motions are used to maintain reasonable variance. The algorithm then combines the outputs using RR extrapolation to reduce bias to O(γ<sup>2</sup>) from O(γ) in SGLD. Theoretical guarantees for consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE are provided. Experiments on both synthetic and real datasets (matrix factorization) demonstrate improved performance compared to SGLD and, in some cases, SGHMC.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** While RR extrapolation is a known numerical technique, its application to SG-MCMC is novel and fills a gap in the literature. This approach presents a simple yet effective method for bias reduction.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for SGRRLD, including proofs of consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic error bounds. This level of theoretical analysis is commendable and lends credibility to the proposed method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm, its theoretical underpinnings, and experimental results. The core idea of the method is easily graspable.
*   **Generality:** The authors highlight the potential for applying the RR extrapolation to a wide range of existing SG-MCMC algorithms, increasing its potential impact.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Overhead:** The requirement to run two chains in parallel, with one chain effectively idling for half the time, introduces a computational overhead. While the authors argue this is offset by the improved convergence, a more detailed analysis of the practical runtime implications, particularly for very large datasets, would be beneficial.
*   **Correlation of Brownian Motions:** The need to correlate the Brownian motions between the chains adds a degree of complexity to the implementation. A discussion of the practical challenges associated with achieving this correlation in a distributed computing environment would be valuable.
*   **Limited Comparison in Matrix Factorization:** While the matrix factorization experiment demonstrates improvement over SGLD, the lack of a comparison against Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) limits the assessment of the practical advantages of SGRRLD, particularly in a scenario where Bayesian inference provides a clear benefit.
*   **Conditions for Applicability:** The paper acknowledges conditions are necessary for applying the RR extrapolation, but these are presented somewhat vaguely. A more explicit statement of these conditions would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for reducing bias in SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The experimental results provide strong evidence that SGRRLD outperforms existing methods in terms of convergence rate and accuracy.
*   The method is relatively simple to implement and has the potential to be widely adopted by the stochastic gradient MCMC community.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational overhead associated with running two chains in parallel may limit the scalability of the method for very large datasets.
*   The correlation requirement of the Brownian motions may introduce implementation challenges.
*   The comparison in the matrix factorization experiment is incomplete, lacking a benchmark against SGD.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach for reducing bias and improving the accuracy of these methods. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Address the Computational Overhead:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the practical runtime implications of running two chains, potentially exploring alternative scheduling strategies to mitigate the idling time.
*   **Clarify Applicability Conditions:** Explicitly state the conditions under which the RR extrapolation can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   **Expand Matrix Factorization Experiment:** Include a comparison against SGD in the matrix factorization experiment to demonstrate the benefits of Bayesian inference with SGRRLD.
*   **Add Algorithm Box:** Include a pseudo-code box summarizing the SGRRLD algorithm to enhance accessibility.
*   **Discuss Correlation Challenges:** Briefly discuss the practical challenges of implementing correlated Brownian motions in a distributed computing environment.
