

This paper proposes using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation—a higher-order numerical integrator—to accelerate convergence in SG-MCMC algorithms. The method runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injections, establishing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models validate the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates practical efficacy over standard SGLD. Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated technique with clear exposition and solid context within the SGMCMC literature.  

I have two primary concerns regarding novelty and experimental rigor. First, the novelty appears incremental: the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC was previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been applied to approximate invariant distributions in [23]. The paper should more explicitly differentiate its contribution from these prior works.  

Second, the practical implementation may face significant limitations due to unaddressed communication overhead. The requirement for correlated Brownian motion necessitates communication between the two chains at every step, yet the authors assume zero communication cost. This is unrealistic for distributed systems. Crucially, the experimental setup lacks clarity on resource allocation:  
- Is the single SGLD chain executed using the *same* computational resources as the two parallel SGRRLD chains?  
- If resources are shared, how are they split between the two SGRRLD chains during gradient computation?  
- If resources are not shared, the comparison is unfair for practical deployment.  
Since stochastic gradient computation dominates SGMCMC runtime, it remains unclear how partial resource allocation in SGRRLD (for two chains) could yield faster wall-clock time than full-resource SGLD. The authors should provide detailed implementation specifications addressing these points.  

Additionally, an important baseline is missing: comparing against two embarrassingly parallel SGLD chains (without communication) would better isolate the value of the proposed correlation mechanism. This comparison should be included.
