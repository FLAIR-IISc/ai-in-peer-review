This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm by employing a numerical sequence acceleration method, specifically the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. This method involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injections. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for their proposed method. Experimental results on synthetic Gaussian models validate the bias and mean squared error (MSE) bounds established in the theorems, while large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm over the standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, the paper presents a well-crafted technique and a clear exposition. The contribution to the area of SG-MCMC methods is valid, and the authors do an excellent job of contextualizing their method with respect to similar previous approaches. However, I have two primary concerns:

Firstly, the novelty of the paper may be incremental, given that the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23].

Secondly, the practical utility of the algorithm may be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in the experimental evaluation. The proposed method involves a parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm, which requires communication between the two chains at every step due to the use of correlated Brownian motion. However, the authors seem to disregard the communication cost, assuming it to be negligible. It would be beneficial to acknowledge and discuss this aspect. Furthermore, the allocation of computing resources for the SGRRLD and SGLD algorithms in the experiments is unclear. For instance, is the single chain of SGLD implemented using the same resources as the two chains of SGRRLD? If not, this may not be a fair comparison from a practical standpoint. If yes, then it is unclear how the resources are split between the two chains of SGRRLD in the algorithmic procedure. Given that the primary computational cost of SG-MCMC stems from computing stochastic gradients, it is puzzling how the partial resource utilization in SGRRLD for computing gradients at each chain could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to the full resource utilization in SGLD. Additional details on the implementation setup would be helpful. Alternatively, it would be interesting to compare the proposed method with running two chains of SGLD in an embarrassingly parallel manner (without communication).
