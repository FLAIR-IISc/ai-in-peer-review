This paper addresses a key limitation of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods: the asymptotic bias they introduce, which prevents them from converging to the exact posterior distribution. The authors propose a simple yet effective technique, based on Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, to reduce this bias.

The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with correlated noise. The first chain uses a step size γ, while the second uses a step size of γ/2. To ensure both chains process a comparable amount of data, the second chain is run for twice the number of iterations as the first. The injected Gaussian noise for step *k* in Chain 1 (α_k) is constructed by averaging the noise from steps 2*k*-1 and 2*k* in Chain 2 (β_{2k-1}, β_{2k}). The final expectation of a function *f* is computed by taking twice the estimate from the second chain and subtracting the estimate from the first.

The authors provide theoretical justification showing that this procedure reduces the bias of the resulting estimator from O(γ) to O(γ²). A similar reduction is also proven for the Mean Squared Error (MSE).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple to understand and implement. It requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms, making it easy to adopt.
2.  **Generality:** The approach appears to be broadly applicable to a wide class of SG-MCMC algorithms. This generality gives the work significant potential for impact within the SG-MCMC community.
3.  **Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a classic technique in numerical analysis, its application to reduce the bias of stochastic gradient-based MCMC methods is novel and insightful. Introducing this established tool to the machine learning community is a valuable contribution.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the core concepts are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The paper states the method applies to SG-MCMC algorithms that satisfy the bias expansion `E[f] = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)`.
    *   Could the authors elaborate on which specific SG-MCMC methods this condition holds for? Can this technique be directly applied to all popular variants (e.g., SGHMC, SGNHT)? Are there any notable exceptions?
    *   Could this framework be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that do not rely on stochastic gradients, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings methods or distributed SG-MCMC variants like Distributed SGLD?

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed two-chain scheme introduces significant computational overhead, as the chain with the larger step size remains idle for half of the total runtime.
    *   As a more efficient alternative, would it be possible to run three chains for the same number of steps: one with step size γ, and two with step size γ/2? One could then average the results of the two γ/2 chains before applying the extrapolation. How would this alternative scheme affect the variance of the final estimator?

3.  **Higher-Order Extensions:** Is there a straightforward extension of this method that uses more than two step sizes to achieve even higher-order bias reduction (e.g., to O(γ³))?

4.  **Correlation of Minibatches:** The paper carefully specifies how to correlate the injected noise between the chains. However, the effect of correlation between the minibatches used for gradient estimation is not fully explored. For variance reduction, is it optimal to use the same minibatch for iteration *k* of Chain 1 and iterations 2*k*-1 and 2*k* of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this choice negligible compared to the noise correlation?

5.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The matrix factorization experiment in Figure 5 could be strengthened.
    *   The plots should be extended until the methods have clearly converged to provide a complete picture of their final performance.
    *   Crucially, the experiment should include a comparison to a standard non-Bayesian baseline like SGD. The current results show that the proposed method (SGRR-LD) improves upon SGLD, but they do not demonstrate that this improved Bayesian approach is superior to simpler, widely-used optimization methods. Showing an advantage over SGD would provide a much stronger argument for adopting this method in a practical setting like matrix factorization, where Bayesian approaches are less common. This would help put the final error of both SGLD and SGRR-LD into better perspective.

6.  **Presentation:** Although the method is clearly presented, the paper's accessibility could be significantly enhanced by including an algorithm box or pseudocode. Many practitioners may not read the theoretical sections in detail, so providing a concise algorithm early in the paper would enable a broader audience to implement and experiment with this promising technique.
