

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel method for reducing bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation—a well-established numerical technique for improving convergence rates—to SG-MCMC, specifically to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The key insight is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), then combine their outputs through RR extrapolation to reduce bias. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K^-4/5) compared to SGLD's O(K^-2/3), matching the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators without their computational complexity. They also demonstrate the method's parallelizability, which aligns with the distributed nature of SG-MCMC algorithms.

The paper builds on two well-established lines of work: SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The authors extend the application of RR extrapolation to the stochastic setting, where the gradient is estimated from mini-batches, and provide the first formal analysis of this approach for SG-MCMC. The theoretical contributions are substantial, including a central limit theorem for the estimator and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, showing how the method achieves better convergence rates than first-order SG-MCMC methods.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, providing concrete improvements in convergence rates that are valuable for practitioners. The method's embarrassingly parallel nature (two independent chains that can be run simultaneously) is a practical advantage for large-scale applications, as the authors demonstrate in their experiments on matrix factorization problems. The experimental results on both synthetic data and large-scale movie recommendation tasks support the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements in accuracy over SGLD with similar computational time. The paper also makes a strong case for the method's generality, noting it can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the authors claim that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015), but they do not include a direct comparison with these methods in their experiments. This omission weakens their claim, as it's unclear whether the practical benefits of their approach outweigh the complexity of implementing higher-order integrators. Second, there is an inconsistency in the description of step sizes: the paper states that the second chain uses step sizes η2k-1 = η2k = γk/2, but Equation (5) suggests the step size is γk/2 for two steps, which doesn't match the description. Third, the supplementary Algorithm 1 does not correspond to Equation (5), creating confusion about the implementation details. These inconsistencies need clarification.

The paper also has some minor issues with presentation. The connection to the multi-level Monte Carlo approach mentioned in the conclusion (Section 6) is not developed, which could have strengthened the significance of the work. Additionally, the paper's contribution could be more clearly distinguished from related work on bias reduction in SG-MCMC, such as the use of higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) or the use of control variates.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is strong, but the experimental comparison with high-order integrators is missing, which weakens the empirical validation.
- Clarity: The main ideas are well-explained, but the step size inconsistency and algorithm mismatch reduce clarity.
- Originality: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, though the approach is a natural extension of existing numerical techniques.
- Significance: The method provides a practical improvement in convergence rates with minimal implementation overhead, which is valuable for large-scale Bayesian inference.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful method that improves convergence rates without significant additional computational cost. The embarrassingly parallel nature makes it highly suitable for modern distributed computing architectures. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-motivated.

Con-acceptance arguments: The lack of comparison with high-order integrators in experiments weakens the significance claim. The step size inconsistency and algorithm mismatch need to be resolved. The connection to multi-level Monte Carlo is underdeveloped.

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC that is theoretically well-founded and practically useful. With minor revisions to address the inconsistencies and add experimental comparisons with higher-order integrators, this work would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper is technically sound and makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on scalable Bayesian inference, but it would benefit from addressing the minor issues noted above before acceptance. The authors' method offers a simple yet effective way to improve SG-MCMC performance, which aligns well with NIPS's focus on practical, scalable machine learning algorithms.
