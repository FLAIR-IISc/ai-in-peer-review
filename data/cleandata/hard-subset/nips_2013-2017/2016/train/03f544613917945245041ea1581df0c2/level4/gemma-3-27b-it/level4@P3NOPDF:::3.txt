## Review of “Reducing Bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC with Richardson-Romberg Extrapolation”

Stochastic Gradient Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods have emerged as a powerful approach for Bayesian posterior inference in large-scale datasets, garnering significant attention within the research community. A key limitation of these methods is their inherent asymptotic bias, preventing exact convergence to the target posterior distribution. This paper presents a compelling and surprisingly simple technique – Richardson-Romberg extrapolation – to demonstrably reduce this bias. The core idea involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel: one with step size γ and another with step size γ/2, running for twice the number of steps. Crucially, the injected Gaussian noise in the two chains is correlated, defined as α<sub>k</sub> = (β<sub>2k-1</sub> + β<sub>2k</sub>)/√2, where α<sub>k</sub> and β<sub>k</sub> represent the noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2 respectively. The paper shows that subtracting the expectation estimate from Chain 1 from that of Chain 2 yields an estimate with a reduced bias of O(γ<sup>2</sup>), compared to the O(γ) bias obtained from either chain independently. This bias reduction extends to a corresponding decrease in the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the estimates, as theoretically proven. 

The strength of this work lies in its conceptual simplicity and ease of implementation – as my summary highlights, it requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Furthermore, its potential applicability to a broad class of SG-MCMC methods positions it as a potentially impactful contribution to the field. While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its application within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC context appears novel and relatively unexplored. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making the core ideas accessible.

However, several questions remain and could strengthen the work:

* **Generality and Conditions:** Can this technique be universally applied to all SG-MCMC methods? Beyond satisfying the condition pi<sub>γ</sub>(f) = pi(f) + C * γ + O(γ<sup>2</sup>) outlined in section 2.2, are there additional requirements or limitations on the SG-MCMC algorithm for successful application of this method?
* **Beyond Stochastic Gradients:** Could this approach be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not necessarily gradient-based, such as Metropolis-Hastings algorithms utilizing sub-sampling techniques?  Specifically, how might it be adapted for Distributed SGLD?
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The requirement that one chain run for twice as long as the other introduces computational inefficiency. While the bias reduction is valuable, the idle time of the faster chain warrants investigation. Would a configuration employing three chains – one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2, all running for the same number of steps – offer a more balanced approach? Would this introduce increased variance in the final estimate?
* **Higher-Order Extrapolation:** Has the potential for extending this method to incorporate more than two step sizes been considered? Could higher-order extrapolation further reduce bias?
* **Noise Correlation & Mini-Batch Effects:** The paper highlights the correlation between injected noise. Could you elaborate on the specific effect of this correlation, particularly concerning the mini-batches used? Is the lowest variance achieved when using the same mini-batch for the k<sup>th</sup> iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)<sup>th</sup> and (2k)<sup>th</sup> iterations of Chain 2? Or is this correlation effect relatively negligible?
* **Experimental Validation – Matrix Factorization:** The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) would benefit from longer runs, until convergence is reached. Furthermore, including a comparison against standard Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) would provide critical context. While the current results demonstrate an advantage over SGLD, demonstrating superior performance compared to SGD in a scenario where the Bayesian treatment is advantageous (i.e., where the method’s Bayesian nature provides a benefit) would substantially strengthen the paper. Bayesian matrix factorization is not a commonly used technique; providing strong empirical evidence would be highly persuasive. Positioning the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD relative to SGD will offer valuable insights.
* **Algorithm Box:** Despite the method’s clear explanation, an algorithm box containing pseudocode would significantly enhance accessibility, particularly for practitioners unfamiliar with the theoretical details. This would facilitate broader adoption by enabling users to quickly implement and test the method without requiring a deep understanding of the underlying theory.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable and potentially significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. Addressing the questions raised above, especially through more comprehensive experimental evaluation, would further solidify its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
