This paper introduces a Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation scheme—interpretable as a higher-order numerical integrator—to accelerate SG-MCMC. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model corroborate the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, and large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates empirical gains over standard SGLD. Overall, the technique is well presented and contextualized, and the contribution to SG-MCMC methodology is meaningful.

I have two main concerns:

1) Novelty. The incremental nature of the contribution is a concern. Prior work (e.g., [10]) has analyzed the role of numerical integrators in SG-MCMC, and RR extrapolation has been used to approximate invariant measures ([23]). Please clarify precisely what is new here beyond these foundations: for instance, are there novel coupling constructions specific to stochastic gradients, new non-asymptotic rates that hinge on the RR scheme under gradient noise, or implementation insights that materially change practical performance? A clearer positioning relative to [10] and [23]—both in theory and algorithmic design—would help establish the distinct contribution.

2) Practicality and fairness of the empirical evaluation. The proposed approach is inherently parallel but requires per-iteration communication to correlate Brownian increments across the two chains. The paper appears to ignore communication overhead; please discuss and, if possible, measure it. More generally, the resource accounting in the comparisons is unclear:

- What computational budget was allocated to SGLD versus the RR-based method (SGRRLD)? Did SGLD run on the same total hardware resources that the two SGRRLD chains used jointly? If not, the comparison may be unfair from a practical standpoint.
- If the budgets were matched, how were resources split between the two SGRRLD chains (e.g., number of cores/GPUs per chain, minibatch sizes, memory bandwidth)? Since stochastic gradient computation typically dominates SG-MCMC cost, explain how dividing resources across two chains can lead to lower wall-clock time than dedicating the full resources to a single SGLD chain.
- Please report implementation details: hardware, parallelization strategy, synchronization frequency for the correlated noise, communication mechanism and latency, and whether results are normalized by wall-clock time, number of gradient evaluations, or both.

A useful additional baseline would be two independent SGLD chains run in embarrassingly parallel fashion (no communication), combined by averaging estimators. This would help separate the benefits of RR extrapolation from those of parallelism and simple variance reduction via multiple chains. Comparisons under fixed wall-clock time and fixed total gradient evaluations would be informative, as would sensitivity to communication latency (e.g., across machines versus a single node).

Minor comment:
- “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is introduced twice (lines 25 and 70); please remove the duplication.

Despite these concerns, I find the paper promising: the idea is sound, the theory is carefully developed, and the empirical results are encouraging. Clarifying the novelty claims and strengthening the experimental protocol with resource-aware, communication-aware baselines would significantly improve the paper’s impact and practical relevance.
