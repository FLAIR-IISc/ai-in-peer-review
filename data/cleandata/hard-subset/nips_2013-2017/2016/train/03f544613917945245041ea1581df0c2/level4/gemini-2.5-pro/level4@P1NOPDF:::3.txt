Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods, which have recently attracted significant interest from the research community, offer a straightforward approach to Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets. A key drawback of these methods is their asymptotic bias, meaning they do not converge to the exact posterior distribution. This paper demonstrates that this bias can be reduced using a technique called Richardson-Romberg extrapolation.

The core idea is simple: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, where the second chain uses half the step size of the first and is run for twice the number of iterations. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated, such that α_k = (β_{2k-1} + β_{2k}) / √2, where α_k and β_k are the noise terms for Chain 1 and Chain 2 at time step k, respectively. The final estimate for the expectation of a function *f* is then computed by taking a linear combination of the estimates from the two chains. The authors show that the resulting combined estimate has a lower bias of O(γ²) compared to the O(γ) bias from either chain alone, where γ is the step size. They also prove a corresponding reduction in the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the estimates.

I appreciate that the method is simple to understand and implement, requiring no major modifications to the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm. Furthermore, the approach appears to be broadly applicable to a large class of SG-MCMC algorithms, making it potentially very impactful for the community.

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, its application to the machine learning and SG-MCMC communities is novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear.

**Comments and Questions:**

*   Can this technique be applied to all SG-MCMC methods directly? If not, are there conditions required beyond the assumption that the SG-MCMC algorithm's bias has the form π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²) as described in Section 2.2?

*   Could this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as those using sub-sampling in the Metropolis-Hastings test? What about distributed SGLD?

*   The proposed method requires running one chain for twice as many iterations as the other. This means the chain with the larger step size sits idle, leading to wasted computation. Could an alternative approach work? For instance, one could run three chains for the same number of steps: one with step size γ, and two parallel chains with step size γ/2. Would this alternative scheme increase the variance of the final estimate?

*   Is there a straightforward extension of this method to use more than two step sizes for higher-order bias correction?

*   The method requires correlated Gaussian noise between the chains. However, the effect of correlation between the mini-batches used is less clear. To minimize variance, should the same mini-batch be used for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and for the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this mini-batch correlation negligible?

*   The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should be run until convergence. It would also be significantly strengthened by including a comparison to standard Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). While the current plots demonstrate an improvement over SGLD, the true value of a Bayesian approach is most evident in settings where it outperforms point-estimation methods like SGD. Since Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice, demonstrating a clear advantage over SGD would provide a more compelling reason for practitioners to adopt this method. Including an SGD baseline would also help put the final errors of SGRR-LD and SGLD into better perspective.

*   While the method is simple and clearly explained, including an algorithm box with pseudocode would greatly improve its accessibility. Many practitioners may not delve into the theoretical sections, so providing a concise algorithm early in the paper would enable a broader audience to implement and experiment with the method without needing to fully digest the more challenging theoretical derivations.
