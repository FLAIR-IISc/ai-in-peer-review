- Proposes using Richardson–Romberg extrapolation with two coupled SG-MCMC chains (step sizes γ and γ/2, correlated Gaussian noise) to reduce bias from O(γ) to O(γ^2).
- Provides theoretical guarantees showing reduced asymptotic bias and corresponding MSE improvement.
- Method is conceptually simple, easy to implement, and requires minimal changes to base SG-MCMC algorithms.
- Potentially broadly applicable across SG-MCMC variants, making it impactful for practitioners concerned with asymptotic bias.
- Novel in the SG-MCMC/machine-learning context despite RR extrapolation being classical in numerical analysis.
- Paper is well written with clear assumptions, bounds, and a practical estimator presentation.
- Request to clarify precise conditions on the generator/discretization ensuring the O(γ) expansion pi_γ(f) = pi(f) + Cγ + O(γ^2).
- Ask whether RR extrapolation can extend to other approximate MCMC schemes (subsampling MH, pseudo-marginal, control-variate MCMC) given accept/reject discontinuities.
- Question compatibility with distributed or asynchronous setups and sensitivity to delays/stale gradients when correlating noise across chains.
- Raise computational-efficiency concern: running chain 2 twice as long leaves chain 1 idle on single device; suggest alternative using three chains to utilize compute and ask for MSE comparison at fixed budget.
- Ask about extending to higher-order RR (multiple step sizes) and required coupling of injected noise and mini-batch gradients to control variance.
- Request clarification/analysis on correlating mini-batches (whether mirroring noise coupling across mini-batches minimizes variance).
- Experimental suggestions: run matrix factorization experiments to convergence, include an SGD baseline, report final errors to show practical benefit over standard optimization.
- Practical suggestion to include an algorithm box/pseudocode specifying step sizes, iteration counts, noise coupling, and recommended mini-batch coupling to aid adoption.
- Overall assessment: solid, novel, and useful adaptation with clear theory and practicality, but would benefit from clarified applicability, mini-batch analysis, and stronger experiments.
