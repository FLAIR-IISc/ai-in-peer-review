This paper proposes applying a Richardson-Romberg extrapolation scheme to improve the convergence rates of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms in terms of bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The proposed method is appealing due to its simplicity, requiring only minimal modifications to standard SG-MCMC implementations. The claims are supported by solid theoretical results, demonstrating improved rates of convergence.

While the core idea is interesting and the theory appears sound, my main concern relates to the clarity of the derivation of the final convergence rates from the theorems presented. I also have a significant concern regarding the fairness of the experimental comparison.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Derivation of Convergence Rates:** The paper presents its main theoretical results in theorems but does not provide a clear and explicit derivation of how the final, specific convergence rates (e.g., for bias and MSE) are obtained from these general theorems. A more detailed explanation or an appendix showing the steps to derive these rates would greatly strengthen the theoretical contribution and allow for better verification of the results.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** A practical concern arises from the experimental results for matrix factorization in Figure 5. The proposed method runs two parallel chains, with one chain potentially requiring twice as many steps as the other, leading to a total computational cost that is roughly three times that of a standard single-chain SG-MCMC method per epoch. For a fair comparison based on wall-clock time, this significant computational overhead must be taken into account. The current plots in Figure 5 appear to compare the methods on a per-iteration basis. If the time axis for the SGLD baseline were scaled to reflect its lower computational cost (i.e., SGLD is allowed to run three times as many iterations in the same amount of time), the observed performance gap between SGLD and SGRRLD would likely shrink considerably. The authors should clarify the experimental setup or revise the plots to ensure a fair comparison based on total computational effort.

**Questions and Additional Comments:**

*   In the bias plot (Figure 3), the best empirical convergence appears to be achieved by the red curve, which corresponds to α=0.2. However, the text states that the optimal rate is achieved with α=1/3. Could the authors clarify this discrepancy?
*   Furthermore, the curve corresponding to the purportedly optimal rate (α=0.2) exhibits significant fluctuations. This high variance makes it difficult to visually confirm the stated convergence rate from the plot. Is there a reason for this non-monotonic behavior?
*   On line 256, it is stated that when the step size γ is too small, the 1/(Kγ) term will dominate. This is intuitive for the proposed method, but why is a similar effect not observed for the baseline SGLD algorithm? A brief explanation would be helpful.

**Minor Points:**

*   **Eq. 1:** The notation for the parameter is different from θ, which is used throughout the rest of the paper. Please ensure consistent notation.
*   **Line 94:** The acronym "RR" is used without prior definition.
*   **Line 131:** The term Γ_K is not defined.
*   **Line 160:** The paper assumes a "smooth function f." Please specify the precise smoothness assumptions required (e.g., Lipschitz continuous gradient).
*   **Line 249:** The text refers to Figure 1(b) when discussing a plot of Bias vs. dimension. However, Figure 1(b) shows Bias vs. time. This reference should likely be to Figure 1(c).
*   For improved readability, please consider using bold letters to denote vectors.
