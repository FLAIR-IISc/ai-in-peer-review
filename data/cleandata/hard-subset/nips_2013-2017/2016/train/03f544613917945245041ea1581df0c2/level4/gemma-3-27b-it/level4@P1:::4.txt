This paper proposes a novel improvement to Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods based on the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation scheme. The core idea is to reduce bias and improve convergence rates – both in terms of bias and mean squared error – with a relatively straightforward implementation that requires minimal modifications to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. The approach is supported by a solid theoretical analysis and demonstrated through both synthetic and real-world experiments. 

The paper addresses a relevant problem in scaling Bayesian inference to large datasets. The proposed RR extrapolation offers a promising avenue for improving the performance of SG-MCMC, which is often limited by bias. The theoretical results are well-developed, establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds for bias and MSE. The experiments on both Gaussian models and a matrix factorization task provide empirical support for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm, including convergence analysis and error bounds.
* **Practicality:** The implementation of SGRRLD appears relatively straightforward, building upon existing SG-MCMC frameworks.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Parallelizability:** The algorithm is well-suited for parallel and distributed architectures, making it applicable to large-scale problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate Derivation Clarity:** The derivation of the specific convergence rates from the theorems could benefit from further clarification. It’s not immediately obvious how the rates are obtained, potentially hindering understanding for readers.
* **Fairness of Matrix Factorization Experiment:** The comparison in Figure 5 doesn’t fully account for the computational cost of running two chains in SGRRLD (one with twice the steps). The comparison would be more informative if the time for SGLD was adjusted to reflect the equivalent computation.
* **Discrepancy in Figure 3:** The bias plot (Figure 3) suggests that the optimal convergence is achieved for α = 0.2, while the text states that α = 1/3 is optimal. This discrepancy needs clarification.
* **Fluctuations in Figure 3:** The optimal curve in Figure 3 appears quite fluctuating, potentially questioning the consistency of the obtained rate. An explanation for this behavior would strengthen the analysis.
* **Notational Inconsistencies and Missing Definitions:** Eq. 1 uses a different symbol (ϑ) than θ. The term "RR" is not defined when first used (line 94). Γ<sub>K</sub> is used without definition (line 131). The required smoothness of function *f* is not specified (line 160).
* **Figure 1(b) Caption:** The caption for Figure 1(b) incorrectly states the plot shows "Bias vs. time"; it appears to show performance as a function of dimension. Bold lettering is not consistently used for vectors.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
* The algorithm’s parallelizability makes it well-suited for large-scale applications.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian inference and has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of the convergence rate derivation needs improvement.
* The fairness of the experimental comparison in Figure 5 should be addressed.
* The discrepancy in Figure 3 and the fluctuations in the optimal curve need explanation.
* The minor notational inconsistencies and missing definitions detract from the overall clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed method is novel, theoretically justified, and empirically validated. While there are a few areas where clarity and fairness could be improved, these are relatively minor issues. Addressing these concerns would further strengthen the paper and make it more accessible to a wider audience. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. 

Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed explanation of how the convergence rates are derived from the theorems.
2.  Adjust the time axis in Figure 5 to account for the computational cost of running two chains in SGRRLD.
3.  Investigate and explain the discrepancy between the stated optimal α value and the results in Figure 3.
4.  Address the fluctuations observed in the optimal curve in Figure 3.
5.  Correct the notational inconsistencies and provide definitions for all terms used.
6. Correct the figure caption for Figure 1(b) and ensure consistent use of boldface for vectors.
