This paper proposes accelerating Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integration method. The proposed technique requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, using different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analysis. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model verify the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, while experiments on large-scale matrix factorization with real data demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a valid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods. The authors do a commendable job of situating their method in the context of related prior work. However, I have two main concerns regarding the novelty and the practical evaluation of the proposed algorithm.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Novelty:** The contribution may be incremental. The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could further clarify the conceptual advance of their work over these existing methods.

2.  **Practicality and Experimental Comparison:** The practical utility of the algorithm might be limited, and the experimental comparison could be made fairer and more comprehensive.
    *   **Communication Cost:** The parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm relies on correlated Brownian motion, which necessitates communication between the two chains at every step. The current analysis appears to assume this communication is cost-free. It would be beneficial to acknowledge and discuss this potential communication overhead, as it could be a significant factor in practical deployments.
    *   **Resource Allocation and Fair Comparison:** It is unclear how computational resources were allocated between the proposed SGRRLD and the baseline SGLD in the experiments. For a fair comparison, was the single SGLD chain given the same total resources as the two chains of SGRRLD combined? If not, the comparison may not be fair from a practical standpoint. If so, the paper should clarify how resources were split between the two SGRRLD chains. Given that the primary computational cost in SG-MCMC is the stochastic gradient calculation, it is not obvious how using partial resources for each of the two SGRRLD chains could lead to a shorter wall-clock time than dedicating the full resources to a single SGLD chain. Please provide more details on the implementation setup.
    *   **Alternative Baseline:** An insightful alternative would be to compare the proposed method against two independent SGLD chains running in an embarrassingly parallel fashion (i.e., without communication). This comparison would help to disentangle the benefits of the proposed correlation and extrapolation technique from the general benefits of parallelization.

**Minor Comment:**

*   The acronym "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is defined twice (e.g., lines 25 and 70).
