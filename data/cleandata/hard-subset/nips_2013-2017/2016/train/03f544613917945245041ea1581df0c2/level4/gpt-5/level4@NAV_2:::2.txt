This paper introduces Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation into stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics to mitigate the bias induced by stochastic gradient noise. The resulting SGRRLD method is shown to have asymptotically smaller bias and MSE than SGLD. On a synthetic linear Gaussian model, the method clearly outperforms SGLD; on MovieLens data, the test RMSE improvements are modest and open to interpretation.

RR extrapolation is known to accelerate convergence of Monte Carlo estimators for SDEs. This work adapts that idea to SG-MCMC and proves that, under stated conditions, SGRRLD attains a faster convergence rate than SGLD with the same stepsize, at the cost of running two parallel chains. The algorithm is described clearly and appears straightforward to implement. The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate bias reduction for the chosen problem. It would be valuable to include additional real-data studies to show that this simple modification consistently yields substantial bias reduction.

I did not verify the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 in detail, but I would like clarification on the precise assumptions on U required for both results. Do the theorems accommodate multimodal potentials? Intuitively, if the stochastic gradients are uncorrelated, the two chains may decorrelate rapidly even when the Gaussian increments are correlated; how, in that case, does the extrapolated combination still achieve the stated error-order reduction? In the real-data runs, did the authors empirically assess the correlation between the chains?

Regarding the experimental setup, the claim of equal wall-clock time via parallelization is reasonable, but one could likewise run two SGLD chains in parallel under the same budget. If we average two independent SGLD chains, does SGRRLD still show the same RMSE gains? Additionally, in the real-data comparisons, SGRRLD effectively uses stepsizes γ and γ/2, whereas SGLD is run with γ. A fair baseline should include SGLD with stepsize γ/2 (potentially with two parallel chains), given its reduced bias. More real-data experiments would strengthen the empirical case.
