This paper proposes using Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to reduce the asymptotic bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) estimators. SG-MCMC methods provide a convenient way to perform Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets and have attracted substantial interest in recent years. A well-known drawback is their asymptotic bias: they do not converge exactly to the posterior distribution. The paper shows that this bias can be reduced via a simple and general technique.

Method summary: Two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel. Chain 2 uses half the step size of Chain 1 (γ/2 vs. γ) and is run for twice as many steps. The injected Gaussian noises are correlated as follows: α_k = (β_{2k-1} + β_{2k}) / sqrt(2), where α_k and β_k are the Gaussian noises at time k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution, the estimate from Chain 1 is subtracted from the estimate from Chain 2. The resulting estimator is shown to have reduced bias, O(γ^2), compared with the O(γ) bias of either chain alone. The paper also proves a corresponding reduction in MSE.

Strengths:
- The idea is very simple to understand and implement (as summarized above) and requires no major modifications to the base SG-MCMC algorithm.
- The approach appears broadly applicable across a large class of SG-MCMC algorithms, making it potentially impactful for the SG-MCMC community.

Novelty:
- While Richardson–Romberg extrapolation is well known in numerical analysis, it is not widely known in the machine learning/SG-MCMC community. Applying it in this context is therefore novel and useful.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and clearly presented.

Comments and questions:
- Applicability: Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, beyond the bound in Section 2.2 (i.e., π_γ(f) = π(f) + C·γ + O(γ^2)), what additional conditions are needed?
- Beyond SG-MCMC: Can this method be applied to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not stochastic-gradient based, such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests? What about Distributed SGLD?
- Computational efficiency: Because one chain must run for twice as long as the other, there is wasted computation while the larger–step size chain remains idle. Would it be feasible to run three chains—one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2—for the same number of steps to better utilize computation? If so, how would this affect the estimator’s variance?
- Multiple step sizes: Is there a natural extension of this method to more than two step sizes?
- Correlations: The injected noises across chains are correlated, but the effect of mini-batch correlation is not entirely clear. Is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k−1)-th and 2k-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is this effect negligible in practice?
- Experiments (matrix factorization, Figure 5): It would strengthen the empirical section to run the experiment to convergence and include a comparison to SGD. The current plot shows gains over SGLD, but it would be more compelling to demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms SGLD in a setting where being Bayesian is advantageous (i.e., better than SGD). Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice; presenting concrete numbers could convince more practitioners to adopt the method. Including an SGD curve as a control would also put the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD into clearer perspective.
- Presentation: Although the method is simple and clearly presented, an algorithm box with pseudo-code would make the paper more accessible. Many practitioners may not read the more theoretical parts; early pseudo-code would help readers try the method without fully digesting the harder theory.
