Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods have become increasingly popular for performing Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets, attracting significant attention from the research community in recent years. One major drawback of these methods is their asymptotic bias, meaning they do not converge exactly to the posterior distribution. This paper addresses this limitation by introducing Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a technique that reduces the bias of SG-MCMC methods.

The core idea is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with Chain 2 having half the step size of Chain 1. Additionally, Chain 2 is run for twice the number of steps as Chain 1. The injected Gaussian noise in the chains is correlated, with α_k = (β_{2k-1} + β_{2k})/√2, where α_k and β_k are the injected Gaussian noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To compute the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution of the chains, one simply subtracts the estimate computed using samples from Chain 1 from the estimate computed using Chain 2. The resulting estimate is shown to have a lower bias of O(γ^2) compared to O(γ) obtained from Chain 1 or 2 alone, where γ is the step size. The authors also prove a similar reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates.

I appreciate the simplicity and ease of understanding and implementing this method, which does not require significant changes to the base SG-MCMC algorithm. Furthermore, this technique appears to be general and applicable to a large class of SG-MCMC algorithms, making it potentially highly impactful in the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

Novelty: Although Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-known in numerical analysis, it is not widely recognized in the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear.

Comments and questions:

- Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, are there specific conditions, other than the SG-MCMC algorithm satisfying the bound in Section 2.2, π_γ(f) = π(f) + C \* γ + O(γ^2), that need to be met?
- Can this method be applied to other non-stochastic gradient, but approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings tests? What about Distributed SGLD?
- Since one chain has to run for twice as long as the other, there is a significant amount of wasted computation where the chain with the bigger step size stays idle. Instead, is it possible to use three chains, one chain with a step size of γ, and two chains with step sizes γ/2 each, and run all of them for the same number of steps? Does this increase the variance of the estimate?
- Is there an extension of this method to more than two step sizes?
- The injected noise in the chains has to be correlated, but I did not fully understand the effect of correlation between mini-batches. Is the variance lowest when using the same batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1, and the 2k-1-th and 2k-th iteration of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this correlation negligible?
- I think the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should be run until convergence, and you should also compare it to SGD. The current plot shows that your method works better than SGLD, but it would be nice to show that your method works better than SGLD in a setting where being Bayesian is advantageous (i.e., better than SGD). Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice, so showing some numbers will convince more people to adopt your method. Also, having an SGD plot as a control will put the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD in better perspective.
- Although the method is easy and clearly presented, an algorithm box could make the paper more accessible to a wider audience. I believe many practitioners will not read through the more theoretical parts, so pseudo-code presented early on in the paper will enable readers who are not very familiar with this area to try out your method without understanding the more challenging parts.
