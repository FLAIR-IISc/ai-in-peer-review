Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC. The method runs two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and combines their estimators to cancel the O(γ) bias, while using correlated Gaussian noise to control variance. The authors prove consistency and a CLT, and derive non-asymptotic bounds showing bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant step sizes. Optimizing these bounds yields rates O(K^{-2/3}) for the bias and O(K^{-4/5}) for the MSE, improving over first-order SG-MCMC. Empirical studies on a Gaussian model, SGHMC variants, and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support the claims and show better accuracy or wall-clock performance.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh) and later SG-MCMC methods, as well as higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC (e.g., Chen et al.). RR extrapolation is classical for SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire et al.), but, to my knowledge, has not been applied to SG-MCMC. The paper connects these strands by adapting RR to settings with stochastic gradients and by using correlated Brownian increments to tame variance, as suggested in prior SDE literature.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable idea that slots into existing SG-MCMC with minimal code changes.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT, and explicit non-asymptotic bounds that are sharper than those known for first-order SG-MCMC.
- Achieves higher-order accuracy without higher-order integrators; can exploit parallel hardware naturally.
- Empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real data are promising.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Derivation of rates: The paper states improved K-dependent rates derived from Theorems 1–2 (e.g., O(K^{-2/5}) asymptotically, O(K^{-4/5}) for MSE with optimal constant step size), but the transition from the theorem conditions (involving Γ^(n)_K) to these K-rates is only sketched. A concise derivation in the main text would greatly aid clarity (e.g., show how γ_k = γ1 k^{-α} implies Γ_K ~ K^{1-α} and Γ^(3)_K ~ K^{1-3α}, and optimize over α).
- Fairness of wall-clock comparisons: SGRRLD performs three Euler steps per “macro-iteration” (one step for γ and two for γ/2). On a single core, this is roughly 3x the gradient/likelihood work of SGLD. The MovieLens plots compare parallel two-chain SGRRLD against sequential SGLD and conclude equal wall-clock time. For a fair comparison independent of hardware, please normalize by total stochastic gradient evaluations or data passes, or report single-core results. Alternatively, rescale SGLD time by a factor consistent with the 3x work (your text suggests multiplying SGLD time by 2/3), and discuss how much of the observed speedup remains.
- Figure 3 discrepancy: In the bias plot, the best curve appears to be α = 0.2 (red), not α = 1/3 as stated. Moreover, the “optimal” curve fluctuates substantially, which makes reading a rate difficult. Please check the legend and captions, confirm the intended α, and add error bars across the 100 runs or smoother moving averages to stabilize the visual. If α = 0.2 is truly optimal for MSE and α = 1/3 for bias (as theory suggests for constant γ optimized per K), please separate the two cases clearly and ensure the plot reflects that.
- Small-step-size regime: The text notes that when γ is too small, 1/(Kγ) dominates for SGRRLD. Why does an analogous effect not appear for SGLD in the same plot? If it does, please state so; if not, explain the experimental settings (e.g., different constants) that mask the effect for SGLD.
- Assumptions and implementation details:
  - Clarify the smoothness assumptions on f and the growth/regularity conditions on U and the gradient noise law L required for the CLT and non-asymptotic bounds (e.g., f in C^p with polynomial growth; dissipativity and Lipschitz properties for U).
  - State explicitly that the two stochastic-gradient sequences must have the same distribution (e.g., same minibatch size) to avoid reintroducing O(γ) bias, and whether you used correlated minibatches across chains in experiments.
  - Confirm that the “optimal” Brownian coupling (Z^γ = (Z^{γ/2}_{2k-1}+Z^{γ/2}_{2k})/sqrt(2)) was used in all experiments.

Quality
The theoretical framework appears technically sound and well grounded in the SDE literature. A few proof-level details are deferred to the supplement; adding brief intuition in the main text (especially for the variance reduction via coupling) would help readers. Empirical methodology is reasonable, but the computational fairness issue and figure inconsistencies should be addressed before publication.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with some notational and editorial issues (see minor comments). The connection between theorem statements and practical step-size schedules could be clearer.

Originality
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, together with variance control via correlated Brownian increments, is novel to the best of my knowledge. The work distinguishes itself from higher-order integrator approaches by obtaining comparable accuracy with first-order schemes.

Significance
The method is practical, generic, and parallel-friendly. If the theoretical and empirical advantages hold across broader tasks, this could become a standard variance/bias reduction trick for SG-MCMC.

Pros (for acceptance)
- Novel, simple idea with strong theoretical backing.
- Demonstrable gains in both finite-time and asymptotic regimes.
- Easy to implement and compatible with many SG-MCMC variants.
- Good empirical results on large-scale problems.

Cons (against acceptance)
- Hardware-dependent speedups; need hardware-agnostic fairness metrics.
- Ambiguities in rate derivations and a figure/legend inconsistency that could confuse readers.
- Several missing definitions and notational inconsistencies.

Minor comments
- Eq. (1): use a consistent symbol (θ) rather than switching to ϑ.
- Define RR (Richardson–Romberg) at first mention.
- Define Γ_K (and Γ^(n)_K) at first use in the main text.
- Specify the smoothness class for f (e.g., f ∈ C^p with polynomial growth; state required p).
- Line/caption mismatch: the text refers to Figure 1(b) as bias vs. time, but the bias vs. dimension plot appears elsewhere; likely you meant Figure 1(c). Please fix.
- Use boldface for vectors throughout for consistency.

Overall recommendation
Promising contribution. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors (1) clarify the rate derivations and fix the Figure 3 discrepancy, and (2) add fairness analyses normalized by computational work (or single-core runs), alongside the parallel wall-clock plots.
