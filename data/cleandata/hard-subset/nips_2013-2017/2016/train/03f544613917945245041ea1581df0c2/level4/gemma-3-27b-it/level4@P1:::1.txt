This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to recent “big-data MCMC” methods, with a focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a standard numerical integration tool that has recently seen promising applications to numerical stochastic differential equations (SDEs); a strength of this paper is providing a connection between the MCMC and SDE literatures, which could benefit both communities. The authors introduce a new SGLD-RRE method, develop theoretical results on its convergence (along with arguments for its improvement over vanilla SGLD), and demonstrate its performance numerically. They achieve impressive improvements on synthetic examples and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale settings. Overall, this is a solid paper, clearly written, with interesting links between two somewhat disconnected fields, compelling theoretical results, and good empirical performance of the proposed algorithm. The method is a fairly straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, which is computationally convenient. I believe practitioners are likely to adopt these methods, though they are unlikely to revolutionize current practice. 

Minor comments: In Figure 1(a), it would be more precise to state that the plot shows a Gaussian distribution with the empirical posterior mean and variance, rather than the empirical estimated posterior distribution itself. This clarification would be helpful for readers less familiar with these techniques. In Figure 2(a), a brief discussion regarding why the bias does not necessarily decrease as the step size decreases would be beneficial, as this result may be counterintuitive.

The paper begins by outlining Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms and their limitations regarding bias. The authors propose a novel sampling algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), to address this bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The approach leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, effectively running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes. They provide a theoretical analysis establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds for non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error. Results indicate that SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD, achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators. Empirical validation using both synthetic and real datasets supports these findings.

The strengths of this work include the technical soundness of the proposed method and its analysis. The theoretical results, demonstrating improved convergence rates, are well-motivated and convincingly presented. The authors carefully evaluate their method on both synthetic and real datasets, offering strong empirical support for their claims. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The connection drawn between numerical analysis techniques (RRE) and MCMC methods is a valuable contribution. The algorithm's parallelizable nature is also a significant advantage.

Potential weaknesses include the relative complexity of the implementation compared to standard SGLD. While the theoretical gains are shown, the practical benefits might be more pronounced in specific scenarios. The extensive theoretical analysis, while thorough, could be slightly condensed for improved readability. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and analysis.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing SGLD methods in both synthetic and real-world experiments.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potential for practical impact in large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   Novel connection between RRE and SG-MCMC.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Implementation complexity may limit immediate adoption.
*   Performance gains may be scenario-dependent.
*   Lengthy theoretical analysis.
