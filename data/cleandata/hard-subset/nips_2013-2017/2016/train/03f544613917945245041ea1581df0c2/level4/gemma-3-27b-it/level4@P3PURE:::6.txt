This paper proposes to improve the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm by employing a numerical sequence acceleration method – specifically, Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator. The method requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, utilizing different step sizes and correlated injecting Gaussian noise. The authors provide convergence analysis demonstrating both asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model verify the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds established in the theorems, while large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). Overall, this is a strong paper, presenting a well-motivated technique and a clear exposition. I believe this work constitutes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods, and the authors do a good job of situating their approach within the context of related prior work. 

However, I have two main concerns. First, the novelty of the paper may be incremental. The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. Second, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison lacks sufficient detail to assess fairness. The proposed algorithm involves a parallel implementation of SG-MCMC, and the correlated Brownian motion necessitates communication between the two chains at each step. The authors do not address the communication cost, implicitly assuming it is negligible, and it would be beneficial to acknowledge this simplification. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks clarity regarding resource allocation. Specifically, it is unclear how computing resources were assigned to SGRRLD and SGLD. For example, was the single SGLD chain implemented using the resources consumed by the two SGRRLD chains? If not, this may represent an unfair comparison from a practical perspective. If so, the question arises as to how resources were divided for the two chains within the SGRRLD algorithm. Given that the computational cost of SG-MCMC is primarily driven by stochastic gradient computation, it’s unclear how dedicating partial resources to gradient computation in each SGRRLD chain could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to allocating full resources to SGLD. Please provide more details regarding the implementation setup. Additionally, it would be informative to compare against running two SGLD chains embarrassingly (i.e., without communication).
