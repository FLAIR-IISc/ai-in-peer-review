Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods have emerged as a powerful tool for performing Bayesian posterior inference on large-scale datasets, garnering significant attention from the research community. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias, which prevents them from converging to the exact posterior distribution. This paper introduces the use of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation as a technique to reduce this bias.

The core idea is conceptually simple and involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel. The second chain is configured with half the step size of the first and is run for twice the number of iterations. A crucial element is the correlation of the injected Gaussian noise between the chains, defined by `alphak = (beta{2k-1) + beta{2k})/sqrt(2)`, where `alphak` and `betak` are the noise terms for Chain 1 and Chain 2 at time step `k`, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function, the estimate from Chain 1 is subtracted from the estimate derived from Chain 2. The authors prove that this combined estimator achieves a lower bias of `O(gamma^2)`, a notable improvement over the `O(gamma)` bias from either individual chain (where `gamma` is the step size). A similar reduction is also proven for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the estimates.

A primary strength of this work is its simplicity; the method is easy to grasp and implement without requiring fundamental changes to the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm. Moreover, the approach appears to be general, applying to a wide class of SG-MCMC algorithms. This generality gives it the potential to be highly impactful for the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, its application in the context of machine learning and SG-MCMC is not widely known and appears to be novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the concepts are presented clearly.

**Comments/Questions:**
*   Could the authors elaborate on the precise conditions for applicability? Can this technique be applied to any SG-MCMC method, or are there specific requirements beyond the algorithm satisfying the bound `pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)` discussed in Section 2.2?
*   Is it possible to apply this debiasing strategy to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as sub-sampling Metropolis-Hastings or Distributed SGLD?
*   The requirement for one chain to run twice as long as the other introduces significant computational waste, as the faster chain remains idle. Would it be feasible to use an alternative like three chains—one with step size `gamma` and two with step size `gamma/2`—all running for the same number of steps? How would such a modification affect the variance of the resulting estimator?
*   Is there a straightforward extension of this method to utilize more than two step sizes for higher-order corrections?
*   The paper emphasizes correlating the injected noise, but the effect of correlation between the mini-batches used for gradient estimation is less clear. Is variance minimized when the same mini-batch is used for iteration `k` of Chain 1 and iterations `2k-1` and `2k` of Chain 2, or is this correlation of negligible importance?
*   The matrix factorization experiment in Figure 5 would be more convincing if the models were run to convergence. Including a comparison to standard SGD would also be beneficial. This would help demonstrate that the proposed method improves upon SGLD in a regime where a Bayesian approach offers a tangible advantage over point-estimation methods and would provide a better perspective on the final error magnitudes.
*   While the method is presented clearly, including an algorithm box with pseudocode would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility to a broader audience. Practitioners who may not engage with the theoretical derivations would benefit from a concise, practical recipe that allows them to readily implement and experiment with the method.
