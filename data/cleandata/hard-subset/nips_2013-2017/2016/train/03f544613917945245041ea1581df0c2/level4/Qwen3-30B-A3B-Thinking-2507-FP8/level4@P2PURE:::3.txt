

Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods enable efficient Bayesian posterior inference for large datasets and have garnered significant attention in recent years. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias, as they do not converge exactly to the target posterior distribution. This paper demonstrates that Richardson-Romberg extrapolation reduces this bias. The core approach involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel: Chain 2 uses a step size of γ/2 (half that of Chain 1) and runs for twice the number of steps. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated such that α_k = (β_{2k-1} + β_{2k})/√2, where α_k and β_k denote the noise at time step k for Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function f under the target distribution, one subtracts the estimate from Chain 1 (using step size γ) from the estimate from Chain 2 (using step size γ/2). This yields an estimate with bias O(γ²), compared to O(γ) for either chain alone. The authors also establish a corresponding reduction in mean squared error (MSE). The method is highly appealing due to its simplicity, requiring no major modifications to base SG-MCMC algorithms, and its broad applicability across a wide class of SG-MCMC methods, suggesting significant potential impact for the field.  

Novelty: While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, its application to SG-MCMC represents a novel contribution to the machine learning community.  
Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly presented.  

Comments/questions:  
- Can this technique be universally applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, what specific conditions beyond the bound in Section 2.2 (π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)) are required?  
- Can the method extend to non-stochastic gradient approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings or Distributed SGLD?  
- Running Chain 2 for twice as many steps as Chain 1 results in wasted computation (Chain 1 idles during Chain 2's extra steps). Would using three chains—Chain A at step size γ, and Chains B/C at γ/2, all run for the same number of steps—improve efficiency? Does this increase estimator variance?  
- Is there a natural extension to more than two step sizes?  
- The correlation between noise terms requires clarification: Is the variance minimized when the same mini-batch is used for Chain 1's k-th iteration and Chains 2's (2k-1)-th and 2k-th iterations, or is the correlation effect negligible?  
- The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should be run to convergence and compared against SGD. Current results show SGRRLD outperforms SGLD, but demonstrating superiority over SGD in a Bayesian setting (where Bayesian inference is advantageous) would strengthen the case for practical adoption. Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used; including SGD results would contextualize SGRRLD/SGLD errors more effectively.  
- While the method is straightforward, an algorithm box would enhance accessibility. Many practitioners may skip theoretical sections; early presentation of pseudocode would enable broader adoption without requiring deep familiarity with the technical details.
