- Paper proposes Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SG-MCMC: run two parallel chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise, with asymptotic and non-asymptotic guarantees.  
- Experiments: synthetic Gaussian models validate theoretical bias and MSE bounds; large-scale matrix factorization shows practical gains over standard SGLD.  
- Overall: strong, well-motivated paper with clear exposition and solid placement in SGMCMC literature.  
- Novelty concern: contribution appears incremental given prior analysis of numerical integrators in SG-MCMC [10] and prior use of Richardson-Romberg for invariant distributions [23]; authors should explicitly differentiate their work.  
- Practicality concern: correlated Brownian motion requires per-step communication between chains, but the paper assumes zero communication cost—unrealistic for distributed settings.  
- Experimental clarity: unclear whether the single SGLD baseline used the same computational resources as the two SGRRLD chains.  
- Experimental clarity: if resources were shared, it’s unclear how resources were split between the two SGRRLD chains during gradient computation.  
- Fairness concern: if resources were not shared, the comparison against SGLD is unfair for practical deployment; detailed implementation/resource allocation specs are needed.  
- Runtime concern: since stochastic gradient computation dominates runtime, it’s unclear how splitting resources across two chains yields faster wall-clock time than full-resource SGLD.  
- Missing baseline: compare to two embarrassingly parallel SGLD chains (no communication) to isolate the benefit of the proposed correlation mechanism.  
- Minor comments: remove repeated "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" (lines 25 and 70).  
- Minor comments: change "demonstrate of the practical use" to "demonstrate practical utility."
