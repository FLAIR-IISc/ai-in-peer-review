This paper employs Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation within stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics to mitigate the bias arising from stochastic gradient noise. The resulting SGRRLD method achieves asymptotically smaller bias and MSE than SGLD. A synthetic Linear Gaussian Model experiment highlights the advantage of the new algorithm over SGLD. On MovieLens, there is a slight improvement in test RMSE, though the findings are debatable.

Prior work has shown that RR extrapolation can improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimators for SDEs. This paper adapts the technique to SG-MCMC and proves that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD with the same step size, at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithmic description is clear, and the proposed method seems straightforward to implement. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate SGRRLD’s bias reduction for that specific problem. It would be helpful to include more real-data studies to show that a simple modification of SGLD can substantially reduce bias.

I have not checked the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 in detail, but I am curious about the conditions required of U for both theorems. Can U be multimodal? Intuitively, when the gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains should decorrelate quickly even if the Gaussian noises are correlated. How, then, does combining the two chains reduce the error order? In the real-data experiments, do the authors observe correlation between the two chains?

My concerns about the experimental setup, especially for the real data, are as follows. The authors state that computation time is comparable to SGLD due to parallelization. However, one could also run multiple SGLD chains in parallel under the same budget. If we compare estimates from two SGLD chains with those from SGRRLD, does the RMSE improvement remain? Moreover, in the real-data comparison, SGRRLD with step sizes gamma and gamma/2 is evaluated against SGLD with step size gamma. We should also compare against SGLD with step size gamma/2 (which has lower bias) and run two chains in parallel. Finally, additional real-data experiments would strengthen the conclusions.
