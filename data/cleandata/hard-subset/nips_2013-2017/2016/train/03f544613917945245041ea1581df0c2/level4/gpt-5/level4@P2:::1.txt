This paper applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RR) to stochastic-gradient MCMC, focusing on SGLD, and establishes both theoretical and empirical benefits. The key idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2 over matched time horizons) and combine their estimators to cancel first-order discretization bias. The method also uses correlated Gaussian noises to control variance. The authors prove asymptotic consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE; they show that the estimator achieves O(K^−4/5) MSE with a first-order integrator, improving on SGLD’s O(K^−2/3) rate and matching the accuracy of higher-order schemes (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS’15). Empirically, they report large gains on synthetic problems and consistent improvements on large-scale matrix factorization, and they demonstrate extension to SGHMC.

Relation to prior work: RR extrapolation is a classic acceleration technique for discretized SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup). SG-MCMC methods (Welling–Teh; Ma–Chen–Fox; Chen–Ding–Carin) have studied bias/variance trade-offs and higher-order integrators. The novelty here is to transfer RR to the stochastic-gradient setting and show that, with shared-noise coupling, one can reduce bias without inflating variance, yielding better non-asymptotic rates. The work thus connects the SG-MCMC and SDE literatures in a useful way.

Quality: The theoretical development appears careful and sound. The CLT and non-asymptotic analyses are aligned with known RR results for exact-gradient diffusions and are adapted to the noisy-gradient setting. The role of correlated Brownian increments is clearly justified, and the dependence of constants on gradient-noise moments is acknowledged. Experiments are sensible and support the claims.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The construction of SGRRLD and its estimator is easy to follow, and the assumptions are stated (full details in the supplement). A few presentation issues are noted below.

Originality: Conceptually, the method is a natural, straightforward application of RR to SG-MCMC. The main contribution is the adaptation and analysis under stochastic gradients, plus a practical recipe (two chains, shared noise, matched minibatches) that attains higher-order accuracy with first-order integrators.

Significance: The approach is simple, generic, parallel-friendly, and likely to be adopted. It will not fundamentally change SG-MCMC practice, but it offers a practical accuracy/computation trade-off and a bridge to SDE extrapolation techniques.

Strengths
- Simple and generic idea with a clear implementation path; minimal code changes to SGLD.
- Strong theory: consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds; improved rates (bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)); optimal schedules characterized.
- Variance control via shared Gaussian noise is well motivated and theoretically optimal.
- Parallel/distributed suitability: the two chains can run independently; wall-clock fairness is addressed in experiments.
- Empirical validation across synthetic and large-scale matrix factorization; extension to SGHMC and comparison to a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty is incremental: RR is well known for SDEs; the contribution lies in adaptation and analysis for SG-MCMC. This should be stated explicitly.
- Practical overhead: two chains double per-iteration cost. Gains depend on available parallelism. Consider reporting results under single-threaded constraints (fixed FLOP budget) in addition to parallel runs.
- Assumptions are strong (e.g., smoothness up to high order, Poisson equation solution). A brief discussion of robustness to assumption violations would help.
- Coupling requirements: performance hinges on using the same minibatch size and shared Gaussian noise; the impact of imperfect coupling (e.g., different minibatches, partial noise correlation) could be explored empirically.
- Baselines: beyond SGLD and SGHMC, consider comparisons to other improved SG-MCMC variants (e.g., pSGLD, SGNHT/thermostats, preconditioned or quasi-Newton SG-MCMC) to contextualize gains.
- Tuning guidance: provide practical recommendations for choosing γ schedules (e.g., α = 1/5 for optimal MSE rate), batch sizes, and how to implement shared-noise coupling in code.
- Presentation:
  - Fig. 1a: clarify that the plotted curve is a Gaussian with empirical posterior mean/variance, not the empirical posterior density itself.
  - Fig. 2a: add a brief explanation for why bias may not decrease with smaller step sizes (the 1/(Kγ) term dominates when K is fixed), to avoid confusion.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution adapted to the stochastic-gradient setting with meaningful rate improvements.
- Simple, general method with clear practical impact and parallel-fit.
- Consistent empirical gains across settings; matches higher-order integrator accuracy without complex integrators.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is modest; the core idea is a direct transposition of RR.
- Benefits depend on parallel hardware; speedups under serial computation are less clear.
- Limited exploration of broader SG-MCMC baselines and sensitivity to coupling choices.

Overall evaluation: A well-executed and useful contribution that tightens the connection between SG-MCMC and SDE numerics, with both theoretical and practical value. I recommend acceptance (weak accept) after addressing the minor presentation and discussion points above.
