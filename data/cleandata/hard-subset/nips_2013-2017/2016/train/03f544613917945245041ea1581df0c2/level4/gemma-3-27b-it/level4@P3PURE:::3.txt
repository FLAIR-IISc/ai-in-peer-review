Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer a practical approach to Bayesian posterior inference with large datasets and have garnered significant attention recently. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias—they do not converge exactly to the target posterior distribution. This paper demonstrates that this bias can be reduced using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with the second chain utilizing half the step size of the first, but running for twice the number of steps. The injected Gaussian noise in the chains is correlated, specifically,  α<sub>k</sub> = (β<sub>2k-1</sub> + β<sub>2k</sub>)/√2, where α<sub>k</sub> and β<sub>k</sub> represent the injected Gaussian noise at time step *k* in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function *f* with respect to the target distribution, the paper proposes subtracting the estimate computed from samples of Chain 1 from the estimate computed from Chain 2. This results in an estimate with reduced bias, scaling as O(γ<sup>2</sup>), compared to O(γ) obtained from either chain independently (where γ represents the step size). The authors also prove a corresponding reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates. 

I particularly appreciate the method’s simplicity and ease of implementation, as summarized in the paper, and the fact that it requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Furthermore, the technique appears broadly applicable across a wide range of SG-MCMC methods, potentially making it highly impactful for the stochastic gradient MCMC community.

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its application within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC context appears to be novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear.

**Comments/Questions:**

*   Can this technique be universally applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, are there specific requirements beyond satisfying the bound presented in section 2.2—π<sub>γ</sub>(f) = π(f) + C * γ + O(γ<sup>2</sup>)?
*   Is this method adaptable to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not strictly gradient-based, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings tests? What about its applicability to Distributed SGLD?
*   Since one chain requires double the iterations of the other, computational resources are potentially underutilized while the chain with the larger step size remains idle. Would employing three chains—one with step size γ and two with step sizes γ/2—and running them for the same number of steps be a more efficient strategy? Would this approach impact the variance of the estimate?
*   Is there a natural extension of this method to leverage more than two step sizes?
*   The paper details correlated injected noise, but I’m unclear on the effect of correlation between mini-batches. Is the variance minimized when using the same batch for the k<sup>th</sup> iteration of Chain 1, and the (2k-1)<sup>th</sup> and 2k<sup>th</sup> iterations of Chain 2? Or is this correlation effect negligible?
*   The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should ideally be run until convergence. Moreover, including a comparison to standard SGD would be beneficial. While the current plot suggests superior performance compared to SGLD, demonstrating better performance than SGD in a scenario where the Bayesian approach is advantageous (i.e., leading to improved generalization) would be more convincing. Given that Bayesian matrix factorization is not commonly used in practice, providing concrete performance gains will encourage broader adoption of the method. Including an SGD baseline will also provide useful context for interpreting the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   Although the method is straightforward and well-explained, including an algorithm box with pseudocode would enhance the paper’s accessibility, particularly for practitioners. Many may skip the more theoretical sections; therefore, providing pseudocode early in the paper would allow readers less familiar with the field to experiment with the method without needing a deep understanding of the underlying theory.
