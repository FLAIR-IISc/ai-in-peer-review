This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to large-scale Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, with a specific focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a standard technique from numerical analysis that has recently found successful application in the numerical solution of stochastic differential equations (SDEs). A valuable contribution of this paper is that it bridges the MCMC and numerical SDE literature, which could be beneficial to both communities. The authors introduce a new algorithm, SGRRLD, provide a theoretical analysis of its convergence, and demonstrate its practical utility with numerical experiments. The results show impressive improvements on synthetic examples and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale applications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and easy to read. The technical contributions appear to be sound.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of RRE to SG-MCMC methods is a novel and clever idea. It provides an interesting link between two fields that are often disconnected. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is backed by a solid theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE, which convincingly establish its superiority over vanilla SGLD.
*   **Practicality:** The method is a fairly straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, which makes it computationally convenient and relatively easy to implement. Its inherently parallel structure is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are strong. The algorithm achieves significant performance gains on the synthetic Gaussian model and demonstrates consistent, practical improvements on large-scale matrix factorization tasks. Given these benefits, it is likely that practitioners will find this method useful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the proposed method offers a clear and practical improvement over existing first-order SG-MCMC techniques, its overall impact might be considered incremental rather than revolutionary. It elegantly improves upon current practice but does not introduce a fundamentally new paradigm.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The presentation of Figure 1a could be misleading. The plot shows a Gaussian distribution parameterized by the empirical posterior mean and variance, not the empirical distribution of the samples itself. The authors should clarify this in the caption or text to prevent misinterpretation.
*   The results in Figure 2, where the bias and MSE begin to increase as the step size becomes very small, are somewhat counter-intuitive. A brief discussion explaining this phenomenon would be helpful. Presumably, this is because the number of iterations K is fixed, and for very small γ, the O(1/(Kγ)) term in the error bounds from Theorem 2 begins to dominate. Explicitly stating this would strengthen the connection between the theory and the experiments.
