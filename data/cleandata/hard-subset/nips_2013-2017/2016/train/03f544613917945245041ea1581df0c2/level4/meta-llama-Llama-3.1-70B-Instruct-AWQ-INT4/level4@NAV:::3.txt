Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods have gained significant attention in recent years for facilitating Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets. However, these methods are asymptotically biased, failing to converge to the exact posterior distribution. This paper proposes a technique, Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, to mitigate this bias. The approach involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, where Chain 2 operates at half the step size of Chain 1 and is run for twice the number of steps. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated, following the formula alphak = (beta{2k-1} + beta{2k})/sqrt(2), where alphak and betak represent the noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution, one simply subtracts the estimate from Chain 1 from that of Chain 2. This results in an estimate with a lower bias of O(gamma^2), compared to O(gamma) from either chain alone, where gamma denotes the step size. A similar reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates is also demonstrated. The simplicity and ease of implementation of this method are notable, as it does not require significant modifications to the base SG-MCMC algorithm. Its broad applicability to various SG-MCMC algorithms makes it a potentially impactful contribution to the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community. 

Novelty: Although Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its application in the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities is novel.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear.

Comments/questions: 

- Is it possible to apply this technique directly to all SG-MCMC methods, or are there specific conditions beyond the bound in section 2.2 (pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)) that need to be satisfied?

- Can this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings tests or Distributed SGLD?

- Given that one chain must run for twice as long as the other, resulting in wasted computation, would using three chains with different step sizes (gamma, gamma/2, and gamma/2) and running them for the same number of steps be feasible? Would this approach increase the variance of the estimate?

- Is it possible to extend this method to incorporate more than two step sizes?

- The correlation between the injected noise in the chains and its effect on the variance of the estimate is not entirely clear. Is the variance minimized when using the same batch for the kth iteration of Chain 1 and the 2k-1th and 2kth iterations of Chain 2, or is the impact of this correlation negligible?

- The matrix factorization experiment (figure 5) should be run until convergence, and a comparison with SGD would be beneficial. This would demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method in a setting where Bayesian approaches are favorable.

- Although the method is presented clearly, including an algorithm box with pseudo code early in the paper would make it more accessible to a broader audience, particularly practitioners who may not be familiar with the theoretical aspects.
