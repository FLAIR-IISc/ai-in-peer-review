Summary
This paper applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC to reduce bias without resorting to higher-order integrators. The proposed method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two SGLD-like chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2. The small-step chain advances twice per large-step update, and the Gaussian noises are correlated so that Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. The RR estimator subtracts the large-step estimate from the average of the two small-step estimates, yielding an O(γ^2) bias (vs. O(γ) for first-order schemes) and improved MSE. The authors establish consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds, showing SGRRLD achieves MSE O(K^−4/5) with optimal step-size schedules, compared to O(K^−2/3) for SGLD, and matches the accuracy of higher-order integrators while using only first-order updates. Experiments on Gaussian posteriors, SGHMC variants, and large-scale matrix factorization corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011), SGHMC (Chen et al., 2014), and recent analyses of SG-MCMC bias/MSE and higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016). RR extrapolation for SDE discretization bias is classical (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), but has not been explored for SG-MCMC. The paper’s use of correlated Brownian increments to control variance follows ideas from duplicated diffusions and CLTs for RR estimators and adapts them to the stochastic-gradient setting.

Strengths
- Simple and general mechanism: works as a drop-in wrapper around first-order SG-MCMC, requiring minimal code changes.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT, and finite-time bounds that clearly expose the bias–variance trade-offs, optimal step schedules, and variance-minimizing correlation.
- Practicality: naturally parallelizable; experiments show clear gains over SGLD and parity with higher-order SGHMC in appropriate regimes.
- Potential impact: broadly applicable across SG-MCMC variants under mild conditions.

Weaknesses / limitations
- Assumptions (e.g., smoothness for Poisson equation solutions) may be stronger than typical ML models with non-smooth components.
- Practical guidance on minibatch correlations and step-size tuning (fixed vs. decaying) could be expanded.
- Experimental scope could include stronger baselines (e.g., SGD) and convergence-to-accuracy comparisons to better motivate Bayesian benefits in matrix factorization.
- Computational overhead: one chain runs twice as often; discussion of wall-clock trade-offs and resource utilization could be deepened.
- Extension to multilevel/multi-step RR is mentioned but not evaluated.

Quality
The theoretical results are clearly stated and technically sound, with appropriate references to SDE/RR literature. The variance control via correlated noises is well-motivated and justified by the CLT. Non-asymptotic bounds align with asymptotic rates and provide actionable guidance (e.g., α = 1/5 for MSE). Empirical results are consistent with theory.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and clearly written. However, providing a concise algorithm box early (pseudocode of SGRRLD) would improve accessibility for practitioners.

Originality
While RR extrapolation is known in numerical analysis, applying it to SG-MCMC with careful variance control via correlated noise and deriving finite-time rates in the stochastic-gradient setting is novel and valuable for the ML community.

Significance
The approach offers a general, low-overhead path to higher accuracy for SG-MCMC, matching higher-order methods without their complexity, and is amenable to parallel/distributed execution. This is likely to be adopted in practice.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, general, and parallelizable method with strong theory and clear empirical gains.
- Bridges numerical analysis (RR) and SG-MCMC, potentially influencing a wide range of algorithms.

Cons:
- Limited exploration of practical tuning (minibatch correlation, step-size schedules) and broader baselines.
- Assumptions may restrict immediate applicability to models with non-smooth components.

Questions and suggestions
- Applicability conditions: Beyond requiring weak error expansion πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ^2), what precise conditions on ergodicity, smoothness, and gradient-estimator moments are needed for RR to deliver O(γ^2) bias and the stated MSE rates across SG-MCMC variants?
- Other approximate MCMC: Can RR be applied to subsampling-based MH (e.g., noisy acceptance tests) or approximate MH with control variates? What breaks if the invariant distribution of the discretized kernel differs in a more complicated way than a first-order expansion?
- Distributed/parallel SG-MCMC: How does SGRRLD interact with Distributed SGLD or asynchronous settings? Are there pitfalls when replicating mini-batches across workers to induce gradient correlation?
- Resource utilization: Since the γ/2 chain performs twice as many steps, could one run three chains (one at γ, two at γ/2) for the same number of steps to avoid idling, and average the two small-step estimates before extrapolation? How would this affect variance and computational cost?
- Multilevel extensions: Do you envision straightforward generalization to multiple levels (γ, γ/2, γ/4, …) as in multilevel Monte Carlo or multi-step RR (Pagès, 2007)? A brief complexity–variance analysis or small experiment would be informative.
- Minibatch correlation: You show variance is minimized when Gaussian noises are perfectly correlated as in Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2. What is the role of correlating stochastic gradients (e.g., reusing the same minibatch across the paired steps)? Is variance further reduced, and under what conditions does mismatch of gradient-estimator distributions reintroduce first-order bias?
- Practical guidance: Please provide recommended step-size policies for finite budgets (e.g., fixed γ with K scaling, decay schedules with α = 1/5 vs. α = 1/3 depending on MSE vs. bias goals).
- Experiments: For the matrix factorization task, consider running to convergence and adding an SGD baseline. This would position the Bayesian methods against a common non-Bayesian alternative and highlight where posterior uncertainty helps.
- Presentation: Add an algorithm box for SGRRLD in the main text and clarify implementation details for noise correlation and minibatch synchronization.

Overall assessment
A clear, well-executed paper that brings a simple yet powerful idea from numerical analysis to SG-MCMC, with strong theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results. With minor additions on practical guidance and broader comparisons, it would make a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
