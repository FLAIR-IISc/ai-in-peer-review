This paper proposes to use a sequence-acceleration technique—Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation, implemented via a higher-order numerical integrator—to improve the convergence of SG-MCMC. The method requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated injected Gaussian noise. The paper provides both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. On the empirical side, experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model verify the bias and MSE bounds predicted by the theory, and large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data demonstrate practical gains over standard SGLD. Overall, the technique is well presented, the theory is solid, and the paper is clearly positioned within the SG-MCMC literature. I view this as a valid contribution to SG-MCMC.

Main concerns
1) Novelty and positioning. The incremental nature of the contribution should be discussed more explicitly. The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has already been analyzed (e.g., [10]), and RR extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions (e.g., [23]). Please clarify precisely what is new here beyond combining these ideas, and highlight any novel theoretical insights or practical design choices that are not covered by [10, 23].

2) Practicality and fairness of the experimental comparison.
- Parallelism and communication: The proposed method requires correlated Brownian motions, which forces the two chains to communicate at every step. The paper appears to ignore communication costs; please state the communication model and whether such costs were included in the reported wall-clock times.
- Resource allocation: It is unclear how computing resources were assigned to SGRRLD versus SGLD. Did the single SGLD chain use the same total resources that the two SGRRLD chains used combined? If not, the comparison may be unfair from a practical perspective. If yes, please specify how resources (cores/GPUs, memory bandwidth) were split between the two SGRRLD chains in the algorithm and in the implementation.
- Wall-clock time and gradient cost: Since the dominant cost in SG-MCMC is computing stochastic gradients, it is unclear how allocating only partial compute to each SGRRLD chain could lead to lower wall-clock time than allocating full compute to a single SGLD chain. Please detail the parallelization strategy, minibatch sizes per chain, synchronization frequency, and hardware configuration to justify the observed speedups.
- Alternative baseline: A natural comparator is running two independent SGLD chains in embarrassingly parallel fashion (without communication) under the same total resource budget. Please include this baseline or explain why it is not applicable.

Minor comments
- “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” appears twice (lines 25 and 70). Please remove the duplication.
