This paper improves upon existing Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods by proposing a Richardson-Romberg scheme, which enhances the convergence rates in terms of bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The scheme is straightforward to implement, requiring only minimal modifications to traditional SG-MCMC algorithms. Furthermore, the paper is supported by solid theoretical results.

The proposed Romberg-Richardson scheme appears to be an effective method for improving the convergence rates of standard SG-MCMC algorithms, with a strong theoretical foundation. However, the derivation of specific convergence rates from the theorem, as discussed in the "explain fatal flaws" section, is a major concern. Additionally, I have practical concerns regarding the matrix factorization experiment results.

A key issue with the experiment is that, if run on a single-core computer, the proposed method would take approximately three times longer than standard SG-MCMC due to the use of two chains, one of which requires twice as many steps. Therefore, the plots in Figure 5 are not entirely fair, as the time for Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3, which would reduce the difference between SGLD and Stochastic Gradient Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD).

In Figure 3, the bias plot suggests that the best convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, rather than α = 1/3, as stated in the text. Moreover, the optimal curve appears to fluctuate significantly, which may not align with the convergence rate for α = 0.2. It would be beneficial to provide an explanation for this discrepancy.

On a related note, the text states that when the step size is too small, the term 1/Kγ would dominate (line 256). However, it is unclear why this does not occur for SGLD.

Several minor comments and suggestions for improvement are as follows:

* In Equation 1, the use of a different symbol than θ could be clarified.
* The abbreviation "RR" is not defined (line 94).
* The symbol Γ_K is not defined (line 131).
* The description of the smooth function f (line 160) could be more specific regarding the type of smoothness.
* Figure 1(b) plots Bias vs. time, not dimension; it seems that the correct reference should be Figure 1(c).
* Using bold letters to represent vectors would enhance readability.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to improving SG-MCMC methods, but addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the results and enhance the overall quality of the paper.
