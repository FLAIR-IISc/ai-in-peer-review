Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC estimators while controlling variance. The method runs two SGLD chains with different step-size schedules (γ and γ/2) in parallel and combines their estimates with RR weighting. Variance inflation from extrapolation is mitigated by using correlated Gaussian increments; the optimal choice is to share Brownian motion across the two discretizations. The authors provide theory showing consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds. They derive asymptotic bias O(γ^2) and variance O(γ^4), and finite-time MSE rate O(K^-4/5) (vs. O(K^-2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC), matching the accuracy of methods with higher-order integrators but using only first-order updates. The approach is generic and can, in principle, be applied to other SG-MCMC methods; the paper illustrates this with an SGHMC variant. Experiments on a Gaussian model and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate reduced bias/MSE and improved RMSE at equal wall-clock time, leveraging embarrassingly parallel execution of the two chains.

Strengths:
- Simple, general idea: leverages a classical extrapolation technique to improve SG-MCMC with minimal implementation burden.
- Parallel-friendly: the two chains can be run independently with no communication, yielding practical wall-clock gains.
- Solid theoretical development: consistency, CLT, and finite-time bias/MSE bounds; clear optimal step-size schedules and convergence rates; careful treatment of variance control via correlated noise.
- Empirical results broadly consistent with theory, showing clear improvements in bias and MSE, and benefits in a realistic matrix factorization task.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Inconsistent step-size description: there appears to be an inconsistency in the paper’s description of the step-size schedules (e.g., between text/equations), which should be clarified. This affects reproducibility and interpretation of the theoretical rates.
- Algorithm/notation mismatch: Algorithm 1 in the supplement is not consistent with Equation (5). The authors should reconcile these and ensure the presented pseudo-code exactly matches the described update scheme, including the duplication of steps for the γ/2 chain and the correlation structure for the Gaussian increments.
- Missing experimental comparison to high-order integrators: while the theory claims to attain the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods, an explicit empirical comparison to such integrators (beyond what is presented) is not provided. Including a baseline that uses a higher-order integrator for SGLD/SGLD-like methods would strengthen the empirical case.
- Practical constraints: the approach requires the two chains to use stochastic gradients with the same distribution (e.g., identical minibatch sizes), which may constrain some distributed implementations. The impact of imperfectly matched gradient noise or partial correlation of Gaussian increments is not empirically assessed.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound and builds appropriately on prior work on RR extrapolation for SDEs and SG-MCMC convergence. The assumptions (e.g., smoothness of the Poisson equation solution) are strong but standard in this line of work. The need for identical minibatch distributions across chains is correctly highlighted. However, clarity issues (step-size inconsistency, Algorithm 1 vs. Eq. (5)) should be fixed.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, with clear statements of the main theorems and intuition for variance control via correlated Brownian motion. Some typos and minor wording issues are present. More importantly, the inconsistencies noted above need correction. It would also help to explicitly state the recommended choice Σ = Id (i.e., perfectly correlated increments) where it first appears, and to summarize implementation requirements (same minibatch size, correlation of Gaussian noise) in the main text.
- Originality: Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel combination. The connection to multilevel/RR ideas in SDEs is well motivated, and the extension to SGHMC is natural.
- Significance: The method is broadly applicable, easy to implement, and practically attractive due to parallelism and improved rates. If widely adopted, it could become a standard variance/bias-reduction tool for SG-MCMC.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the step-size schedules for both chains throughout the text, figures, and proofs; ensure the same schedules are used in the experiments. State explicitly how γ and η are initialized and updated, and how burn-in is handled for the two chains.
- Fix the inconsistency between Algorithm 1 and Eq. (5). Include pseudo-code that enforces: two steps for the γ/2 chain per one step of the γ chain; the RR estimator’s weighting; and the construction of correlated Gaussian increments (ideally Σ = Id).
- Add an experimental comparison with a representative higher-order integrator for SGLD-like methods at matched wall-clock budget, to substantiate the claim of achieving similar accuracy with first-order updates.
- Provide an ablation on the correlation structure (e.g., Σ = 0 vs. Σ = Id) and on matching vs. mismatched minibatch sizes to illustrate robustness and the practical importance of these choices.
- Report both wall-clock time and number of gradient evaluations to disentangle algorithmic efficiency from parallelization gains.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and practically useful idea with strong theory.
- Clear empirical benefits on both synthetic and large-scale real data.
- Parallelizable design aligns well with modern computing architectures.
- Novel application of RR extrapolation in SG-MCMC.

Cons against acceptance:
- Inconsistencies in the step-size description and algorithm specification that affect reproducibility.
- Missing empirical comparison to higher-order integrator baselines to fully support the key claim.
- Limited ablations on critical implementation choices (correlation, minibatch matching).
