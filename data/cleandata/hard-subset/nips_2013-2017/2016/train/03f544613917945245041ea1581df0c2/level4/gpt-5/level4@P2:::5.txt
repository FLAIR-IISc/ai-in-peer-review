This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while controlling variance. The algorithm runs two SGLD-like chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 over a matched time horizon, and forms an extrapolated estimator. To avoid variance inflation, the chains share correlated Brownian increments; the authors show that perfect correlation (Σ = I) is optimal. The analysis establishes consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds. In particular, the asymptotic bias and variance scale as O(γ2) and O(γ4), respectively, and with K iterations the MSE can achieve O(K−4/5), improving on the O(K−2/3) rate of first-order SG-MCMC with standard averaging. The technique is generic (applicable beyond SGLD), parallel-friendly, and empirically improves accuracy on a Gaussian posterior and large-scale matrix factorization; the authors also illustrate an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC).

Relation to prior work:
- Builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and subsequent SG-MCMC variants (e.g., SGHMC, thermostats).
- Theoretically relates to weak-error expansions for discretized SDEs (Talay & Tubaro) and RR extrapolation for invariant measures (Lemaire, Pagès, Panloup).
- Connects to recent analyses of SG-MCMC bias/MSE and high-order integrators (Chen, Ding, Carin, 2015). The contribution shows that a first-order integrator plus RR extrapolation can match the accuracy of higher-order schemes under appropriate conditions.

Quality:
- The theoretical development is technically solid and, to my knowledge, new in the SG-MCMC setting: a CLT for the RR-extrapolated estimator with optimal Brownian coupling, and finite-time bias/MSE bounds that translate into improved K-dependent rates under constant or decaying step sizes.
- Assumptions and proofs are in the supplement; the main text states the key conditions and results clearly enough for a conference paper. Still, please make explicit which results require decaying step sizes versus constant step sizes, and how the stochastic gradient noise is controlled (moment/variance conditions).
- The empirical evaluation is convincing on the Gaussian model (where ground truth is known) and on large-scale matrix factorization. The wall-clock comparisons leverage the embarrassingly parallel structure of RR (two chains). Please also discuss the scenario where parallel resources are unavailable (compute would approximately double).

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, with a clear presentation of the estimator (Eq. 6) and coupling strategy. A few issues need correction:
  - Step-size policy: Some theoretical statements assume decreasing γk with α = 1/5 for optimal asymptotics, whereas many experiments use constant γ. Although Theorem 2 covers constant γ, the paper should articulate this distinction and align claims to the appropriate regime (constant vs decreasing γk).
  - Algorithmic consistency: Algorithm 1 in the supplement appears inconsistent with Eq. (5) (e.g., indices/states used in the two half-steps and the time-averaging weights). Please reconcile the pseudocode with the equations and define whether the same mini-batch is reused across the two half-steps.
  - Notation: Clarify K (number of macro-steps vs total fine steps), ΓK definitions, and the exact time-averaging weights used in practice.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with noisy gradients and proving optimal coupling/CLT appears novel. The idea is simple to implement yet nontrivial in analysis due to gradient noise and coupling.

Significance:
- The method is broadly applicable (beyond SGLD), easy to parallelize, and delivers better finite-time accuracy in practice. The improved rates and the link to higher-order accuracy are of interest to the SG-MCMC community.

Empirical evaluation:
- Strengths: Synthetic study validates predicted bias/MSE scalings; large-scale matrix factorization demonstrates practical gains at equal wall-clock time due to parallel chains; the SGHMC variant is explored with a comparison to a higher-order SGHMC integrator.
- Missing comparisons: Since the theory claims parity with higher-order methods, a direct comparison to a higher-order SG-Langevin method (e.g., symmetric splitting for SGLD within the framework of Ma, Chen & Fox or Chen, Ding & Carin) would strengthen the case. An ablation on the effect of Brownian coupling (independent vs correlated) and on reusing vs resampling mini-batches across the two half-steps would also be informative.

Specific suggestions:
- Correct the inconsistency between Algorithm 1 and Eq. (5) and clarify whether the fine chain takes two half-steps from the same intermediate state with independent Gaussian noise and the same mini-batch size.
- State explicitly the required moment bounds on the stochastic gradients and whether mini-batch sizes must match across chains to avoid additional bias.
- Provide guidance on practical step-size selection: constant vs decreasing schedules, and how to choose γ for a given K (matching the optimization of γ in Theorem 2).
- Discuss computational cost: memory/compute overhead of running two chains; what to expect without parallel hardware.
- Add ablations: (i) Σ = 0 vs Σ = I, (ii) shared vs independent mini-batches across chains, (iii) impact of batch size on the variance reduction.
- Extend comparisons to a higher-order SG-Langevin integrator to substantiate the “matches higher-order accuracy” claim for SGLD-type methods.

Pros:
- Simple, generic, and parallelizable technique that improves bias/MSE without complex integrators.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT with optimal coupling, and finite-time bounds leading to improved rates.
- Empirical results align with theory and show practical gains on large datasets.

Cons:
- Some clarity/consistency issues (step-size regime, algorithm pseudocode vs equations).
- Limited comparisons to higher-order SG-Langevin integrators; ablations on coupling/mini-batch choices are missing.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters and compute trade-offs could be more explicit.

Overall, this is a promising and well-motivated contribution that meaningfully advances SG-MCMC methodology. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening empirical comparisons would further improve the paper.
