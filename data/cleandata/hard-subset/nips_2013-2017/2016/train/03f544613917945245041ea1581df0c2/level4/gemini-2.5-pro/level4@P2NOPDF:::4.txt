This paper introduces a Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation scheme to improve the performance of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The proposed method aims to accelerate convergence and reduce the asymptotic bias and mean squared error (MSE) by combining the outputs of two parallel MCMC chains running with different step sizes. The approach is conceptually simple, requires only minor modifications to standard SG-MCMC algorithms, and is supported by a solid theoretical analysis demonstrating improved convergence rates.

While the core idea is promising and the theoretical foundation appears strong, I have a significant concern regarding the clarity of the theoretical derivations and some questions about the fairness and interpretation of the experimental results.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Clarity of Convergence Rate Derivation:** My primary concern lies in the derivation of the final convergence rates from the main theorems. While the theorems themselves are presented, the paper does not provide a clear and detailed walkthrough of how these results translate into the specific rates claimed for bias and MSE. For the reader to fully verify the central theoretical claims of the paper, a more explicit derivation, perhaps in an appendix, is essential.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The experimental comparison in Figure 5 (matrix factorization) may not accurately reflect the practical trade-offs in computational cost. The proposed SGRRLD method runs two chains, one of which takes twice as many steps as the other, resulting in a total computational cost that is roughly three times that of a single SGLD chain. The plots appear to be presented against the number of iterations rather than wall-clock time. If computational time were used as the x-axis, the baseline SGLD curve would be compressed horizontally by a factor of approximately 3, which would significantly reduce the perceived performance gap between the methods. For a fair and practical comparison, results should be plotted against wall-clock time or a comparable measure of total computational effort.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Discrepancy in Figure 3:** In the discussion of Figure 3, the text states that the optimal convergence rate is achieved when the step size decay rate α is 1/3. However, the plot seems to show that α=0.2 (the red curve) yields the lowest bias. Could the authors please clarify this discrepancy? Is there a difference between the theoretically optimal α and the empirically best-performing one in this specific setting?

2.  **Fluctuations in Optimal Curve (Figure 3):** Furthermore, the curve corresponding to the empirically best α=0.2 in Figure 3 exhibits significant fluctuations. This high variance seems at odds with it representing a stable and superior convergence rate. Could the authors provide some intuition as to why this might be happening?

3.  **Dominance of the 1/(Kγ) Term:** Line 256 mentions that for very small step sizes (γ), the 1/(Kγ) term can dominate the error for the proposed method. It would be helpful if the authors could explain why this term is not a dominant factor for standard SGLD under similar conditions. A brief explanation comparing the error structures of the two methods would clarify this point.

**Minor Comments and Typos**

*   **Notational Consistency:** Please consider using bold-face font for vectors throughout the manuscript to improve readability.
*   **Equation (1):** The parameter is denoted by a different symbol than θ, which is used elsewhere in the paper. Please ensure notation is consistent.
*   **Line 94:** The abbreviation 'RR' is used before it is formally defined as Richardson-Romberg.
*   **Line 131:** The notation Γ_K is used without a prior definition.
*   **Line 160:** The paper assumes a "smooth function f." Please specify the required level of smoothness (e.g., Lipschitz continuous, Lipschitz continuous gradients, etc.).
*   **Line 249:** The text refers to Figure 1(b) as plotting "Bias vs. time," but the figure caption or axis seems to indicate it is "Bias vs. dimension." Please check and correct the figure reference or its description.
