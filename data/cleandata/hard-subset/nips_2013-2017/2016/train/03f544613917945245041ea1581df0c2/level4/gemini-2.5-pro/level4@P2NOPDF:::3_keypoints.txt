- Problem: SG-MCMC methods have asymptotic bias preventing convergence to the exact posterior; paper proposes Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce this bias.

- Method: Run two correlated SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 (second runs twice as many iterations), correlate noises by averaging, and form estimator 2·estimate(γ/2) − estimate(γ).

- Theory: The procedure reduces estimator bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) and yields a similar MSE improvement.

- Strength: The method is simple, elegant, and easy to implement with minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC algorithms.

- Strength: The approach appears broadly applicable across many SG-MCMC variants, giving it wide potential impact.

- Strength: Novel and insightful application of a classical Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC.

- Strength: Paper is clearly written and presents core concepts well.

- Weakness/Question: Scope of applicability is unclear—need clarification which SG-MCMC algorithms satisfy the bias expansion (e.g., SGHMC, SGNHT) and any exceptions.

- Weakness/Question: Could the framework extend to other approximate MCMC schemes (subsampling Metropolis-Hastings, distributed SG-MCMC)?

- Weakness: Computational efficiency concern—two-chain scheme incurs overhead since the larger-step chain is idle half the runtime.

- Question/Alternative: Could running three chains (one γ and two γ/2 for same number of steps) be a more efficient alternative, and how would that affect estimator variance?

- Question: Is there a straightforward higher-order extension using more step sizes to achieve O(γ³) or beyond?

- Question: The effect of minibatch correlation is not fully explored—should minibatches be shared between corresponding iterations to minimize variance?

- Experimental concern: Matrix factorization experiments should run to clear convergence and include a non-Bayesian baseline (e.g., SGD) to better contextualize performance.

- Presentation suggestion: Add an algorithm box or pseudocode early in the paper to improve accessibility for practitioners.
