### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of asymptotic bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods, a significant drawback for their use in large-scale Bayesian posterior inference. The authors propose applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis, to reduce this bias. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2). The chain with the smaller step size is run for twice the number of iterations, and the injected Gaussian noise between the two chains is carefully correlated. By taking a specific linear combination of the estimates from the two chains, the authors show that the bias of the resulting estimator is reduced from O(γ) to O(γ^2). They provide theoretical guarantees for this bias reduction, as well as for a corresponding reduction in the Mean Squared Error (MSE).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed method is remarkably simple to understand and implement. It does not require major structural changes to the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm, making it an attractive and practical enhancement.

2.  **Generality and Potential Impact:** The technique appears to be broadly applicable to a wide class of SG-MCMC algorithms. Given the popularity of these methods, this work has the potential for significant impact within the machine learning community.

3.  **Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique, its application to debiasing stochastic gradient methods in machine learning is novel and insightful. This cross-pollination of ideas from numerical analysis is a valuable contribution.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the main ideas and theoretical results are presented with clarity.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed scheme requires the chain with step size γ/2 to run for twice as many iterations as the chain with step size γ. This means the first chain remains idle for half the total compute time, which seems inefficient. An alternative could be to run three chains for the same duration: one with step size γ and two independent chains with step size γ/2. This would balance the computational load. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the theoretical and practical implications of such a change, particularly its effect on the variance of the final estimator.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while promising, could be strengthened.
    *   In the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5), the algorithms do not appear to have been run to convergence. Showing that the proposed method maintains its advantage at convergence would make the results more compelling.
    *   The experiments would benefit from the inclusion of a non-Bayesian baseline, such as standard SGD. Bayesian matrix factorization is not the default choice in practice, so demonstrating a clear win over SGLD is only part of the story. A comparison against SGD would help contextualize the performance of all methods and better motivate the use of a Bayesian approach by showing that the improved posterior approximation translates into tangible performance gains.

3.  **Accessibility:** While the paper is clear for experts, its accessibility to a broader audience of practitioners could be improved. The inclusion of an algorithm box or a pseudo-code snippet, placed early in the paper, would allow readers to quickly grasp the implementation details without needing to parse the more theoretical sections.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   **Scope of Applicability:** Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? Are there specific conditions required beyond the bias expansion `pi_gamma(f) = pi(f) + C*gamma + O(gamma^2)` outlined in Section 2.2? Furthermore, have you considered applying this method to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as those using sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings tests, or to distributed variants like Distributed SGLD?

*   **Higher-Order Extrapolation:** Is there a straightforward extension of this method to use more than two step sizes to achieve even higher-order bias correction (e.g., reducing bias to O(γ^3))? What would be the practical trade-offs of such an approach in terms of computation and variance?

*   **Mini-batch Correlation:** The paper clearly explains the need to correlate the injected noise. However, the effect of correlation between mini-batches is less clear. For variance reduction, is it optimal to use the same data mini-batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this correlation negligible compared to other sources of variance? A clarification on this point would be helpful.
