This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SGRRLD) algorithm that improves existing Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods by reducing their bias and achieving higher rates of convergence. The algorithm is based on the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, which involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and bounds for the non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant effort to provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its theoretical properties. The use of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a clever idea, and the authors have successfully adapted it to the context of SG-MCMC.

The theoretical results are solid, and the authors have provided a detailed analysis of the convergence properties of their algorithm. The central limit theorem and the bounds for the non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error are particularly impressive.

The paper also includes a range of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The experiments on synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization problems show that SGRRLD can achieve significant improvements in terms of bias and mean squared error compared to standard SG-MCMC methods.

However, I do have some concerns regarding the presentation of the experimental results. Specifically, the plots in Figure 5 show a comparison between SGRRLD and standard SG-MCMC in terms of computation time. However, as the authors note, SGRRLD requires running two chains in parallel, which can be computationally expensive. To make a fair comparison, the authors should have taken into account the fact that SGRRLD requires more computational resources than standard SG-MCMC.

Additionally, I noticed a discrepancy between the text and the plots in Figure 3. The text states that the optimal convergence rate is achieved when α = 1/3, but the plots suggest that the best convergence rate is actually achieved when α = 0.2. I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify this point.

Finally, I have some minor comments regarding the notation and formatting. In Equation 1, the authors use a different symbol for the variable θ, which can be confusing. Additionally, the authors should define RR and Γ_K before using them in the text. In line 160, the authors mention that the function f should be smooth, but they do not specify what kind of smoothness is required. In line 249, the authors refer to Figure 1(b), but the plot actually shows the bias vs. time, not dimension. Finally, the authors should use bold letters to represent vectors.
