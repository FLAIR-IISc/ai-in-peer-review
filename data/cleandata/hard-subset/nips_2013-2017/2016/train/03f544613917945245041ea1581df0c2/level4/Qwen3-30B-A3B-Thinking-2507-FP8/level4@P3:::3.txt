

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that significantly reduces the bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation—a well-established technique in numerical analysis—to SG-MCMC, running two parallel chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining their outputs to achieve bias reduction from O(γ) to O(γ²). The paper provides comprehensive theoretical analysis and experimental validation across synthetic and real-world datasets.

The work builds upon foundational SG-MCMC research, particularly Welling and Teh's (2011) SGLD and Chen et al.'s (2015) convergence analysis for SG-MCMC methods. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis for decades, its application to SG-MCMC represents a significant contribution to the machine learning community, as the authors correctly note.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper delivers a thorough convergence analysis, establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The key theoretical contribution is showing SGRRLD achieves MSE convergence at O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. This represents a meaningful improvement in convergence rates for large-scale Bayesian inference.

2. **Practical Simplicity and Generality:** SGRRLD requires minimal implementation changes to existing SG-MCMC codes. The method applies broadly to SG-MCMC algorithms meeting mild technical conditions, making it highly impactful for the community. The parallel nature of the algorithm also aligns well with distributed computing architectures.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** Experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks consistently support the theoretical claims. The results demonstrate significant accuracy improvements over SGLD with comparable computational time, with SGRRLD achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in half the time on the MovieLens datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Comparison to Optimization Methods:** While the paper compares SGRRLD to SGLD, it lacks comparison to standard optimization methods like SGD in the matrix factorization experiments. As Bayesian approaches aren't always preferred over optimization in practice, demonstrating SGRRLD's advantage over SGD would strengthen the practical significance claim.

2. **Lack of Pseudocode:** Despite the algorithm's simplicity, the paper would benefit from a clear pseudocode box early in the manuscript. This would enhance accessibility for practitioners who may not engage with the theoretical details.

3. **Correlation Implementation Details:** While the paper establishes that perfect correlation of Brownian increments minimizes variance, it doesn't elaborate on the practical implementation of maintaining this correlation across multiple chains in large-scale settings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a simple yet powerful technique that significantly improves convergence properties of SG-MCMC methods
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by experiments
- The method is broadly applicable to existing SG-MCMC algorithms
- The parallel nature of the algorithm makes it well-suited for modern distributed computing environments

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- The comparison to non-Bayesian optimization methods in the matrix factorization experiment is limited, weakening the practical significance claim
- The absence of pseudocode reduces accessibility for practitioners

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for large-scale applications. The authors successfully bridge numerical analysis and machine learning by applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, resulting in a method that achieves better theoretical convergence rates with minimal implementation complexity. The paper is well-written, clearly presents its contributions, and provides thorough experimental validation.

While the comparison to SGD in the matrix factorization experiments could be strengthened, the overall contribution is significant and likely to be adopted by practitioners. The simplicity of the approach, combined with its theoretical soundness and practical benefits, makes this a valuable addition to the SG-MCMC literature.

I recommend acceptance as a strong paper that advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference. The method's elegance and practical impact make it particularly suitable for the NIPS audience.
