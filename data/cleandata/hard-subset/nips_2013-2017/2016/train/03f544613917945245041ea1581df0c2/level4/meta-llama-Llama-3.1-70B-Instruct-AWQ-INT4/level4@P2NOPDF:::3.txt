Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods have gained significant attention in recent years for their ability to perform Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets. However, a major drawback of these methods is their asymptotic bias, resulting in a failure to converge exactly to the posterior distribution. This paper addresses this issue by proposing the use of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias in SG-MCMC methods.

The core idea is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with Chain 2 having half the step size of Chain 1 and running for twice the number of steps. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated, with the specific correlation defined as alphak = (beta{2k-1) + beta{2k})/sqrt(2), where alphak and betak represent the injected Gaussian noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution, the authors subtract the estimate obtained from Chain 1 from the estimate obtained from Chain 2. This resulting estimate is shown to have a lower bias of O(gamma^2) compared to O(gamma) obtained from either chain alone, where gamma is the step size. Furthermore, the authors prove a similar reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates.

The simplicity and generality of this method are its most significant strengths. The approach is easy to understand and implement, requiring minimal modifications to the base SG-MCMC algorithm. As a result, it has the potential to make a substantial impact on the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

Although Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, its application in the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities is novel. The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear.

Several questions and comments arise from this work:

1. Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods, or are there specific conditions that must be met, such as the bound in section 2.2: pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)?
2. Can this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings tests or Distributed SGLD?
3. Instead of running one chain for twice as long as the other, would it be possible to use three chains with different step sizes (gamma, gamma/2, and gamma/2) and run them for the same number of steps? Would this increase the variance of the estimate?
4. Is it possible to extend this method to more than two step sizes?
5. The correlation between the injected noise in the chains is crucial, but the effect of correlation between mini-batches is not entirely clear. Is the variance minimized when using the same batch for the k^th iteration of Chain 1 and the 2k-1^th and 2k^th iterations of Chain 2?
6. The matrix factorization experiment (figure 5) should be run until convergence, and a comparison with SGD would be beneficial. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance in a setting where being Bayesian is advantageous.
7. Although the method is easy to understand, including an algorithm box with pseudo-code would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience. This would enable readers who are not familiar with the area to implement the method without needing to understand the more theoretical aspects.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community by providing a simple and effective method for reducing bias in SG-MCMC methods.
