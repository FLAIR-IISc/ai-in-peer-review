

Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods enable efficient Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets and have garnered significant attention in recent years. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias, preventing exact convergence to the target posterior distribution. This paper addresses this issue by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias. The core approach involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel: Chain 2 uses half the step size of Chain 1 and runs for twice the number of steps. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated according to $\alpha_k = (\beta_{2k-1} + \beta_{2k})/\sqrt{2}$, where $\alpha_k$ and $\beta_k$ denote the noise terms at time step $k$ for Chains 1 and 2, respectively. The posterior expectation of a function $f$ is then estimated by subtracting the Chain 1 estimate from the Chain 2 estimate. This yields a bias of $O(\gamma^2)$ compared to $O(\gamma)$ for either chain alone (where $\gamma$ is the step size), and the paper establishes a corresponding reduction in mean squared error (MSE).  

The method’s simplicity and minimal modification requirement to existing SG-MCMC algorithms are highly commendable. Its broad applicability across a wide class of SG-MCMC methods suggests significant potential impact for the community. The novelty lies in adapting Richardson-Romberg extrapolation—a well-established technique in numerical analysis—to SG-MCMC, where it remains underutilized. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.  

**Comments and Questions:**  
1. **General Applicability:** The technique appears to require the SG-MCMC algorithm to satisfy $\pi_\gamma(f) = \pi(f) + C \gamma + O(\gamma^2)$ (as in Section 2.2). Are there additional conditions beyond this bound for applicability?  
2. **Extension to Other Methods:** Could this be applied to non-stochastic-gradient approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., sub-sampling Metropolis-Hastings) or distributed SGLD?  
3. **Computational Efficiency:** Running Chain 2 for twice as many steps creates idle time for Chain 1. Would using three chains (one with step size $\gamma$, two with $\gamma/2$) and equal run lengths reduce computational waste? If so, how does this affect the variance of the estimate?  
4. **Multi-Step-Size Extension:** Is there a natural extension to more than two step sizes?  
5. **Noise Correlation:** The correlation between chains’ noise is critical. Is the variance minimized when the $k$-th iteration of Chain 1 uses the same mini-batch as the $(2k-1)$-th and $2k$-th iterations of Chain 2? Is the correlation’s effect negligible otherwise?  
6. **Experimental Validation:** The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should be extended to convergence and include an SGD baseline. Current results show SGRRLD outperforms SGLD, but demonstrating a *Bayesian advantage* (i.e., better performance than SGD in settings where Bayesian inference is beneficial) would strengthen the case for adoption. Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice; including SGD results would provide clearer context for SGRRLD and SGLD’s final errors.  
7. **Algorithm Accessibility:** While the method is well-explained, an algorithm box with pseudo-code early in the paper would enhance accessibility for practitioners who may skip theoretical sections.
