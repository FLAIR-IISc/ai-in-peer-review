- Main idea: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC (SGLD) by running two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining estimators to cancel first-order bias.  
- Implementation detail: use correlated Gaussian noises and matched minibatches to control variance when extrapolating.  
- Theoretical results: prove asymptotic consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds adapted to noisy gradients.  
- Rate improvement: show estimator achieves MSE O(K^−4/5) with a first-order integrator, improving over SGLD’s O(K^−2/3) and matching higher-order schemes.  
- Variance control: shared-noise coupling is justified and shown to be theoretically optimal for variance reduction.  
- Empirical validation: large gains on synthetic problems, consistent improvements on large-scale matrix factorization, and extension to SGHMC demonstrated.  
- Relation to prior work: transfers classic RR/SDE extrapolation (Talay–Tubaro, etc.) to stochastic-gradient setting and connects SG-MCMC and SDE literatures.  
- Novelty: conceptually straightforward application of RR; main contribution is adaptation and analysis for stochastic gradients plus a practical shared-noise recipe.  
- Quality: theoretical development is careful and sound; analyses align with known RR results and account for gradient-noise moments.  
- Clarity: paper is clearly written and organized; construction and assumptions are easy to follow with details in the supplement.  
- Practicality/significance: simple, generic, parallel-friendly method likely to be adopted, offering a practical accuracy/computation trade-off.  
- Strength: minimal code changes to SGLD and clear implementation path.  
- Strength: strong theoretical guarantees (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bounds) and characterized optimal schedules.  
- Strength: suitable for parallel/distributed settings; wall-clock fairness considered in experiments.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental and should be explicitly framed as an adaptation of known RR techniques.  
- Weakness: two-chain approach doubles per-iteration cost and gains depend on available parallelism; serial FLOP-budget performance should be reported.  
- Weakness: strong assumptions (high-order smoothness, Poisson equation) — robustness to violations should be discussed.  
- Weakness: performance depends on coupling (same minibatches, shared noise); impact of imperfect coupling needs exploration.  
- Weakness: limited baseline comparisons — should include other SG-MCMC variants (pSGLD, SGNHT, preconditioned/quasi-Newton methods).  
- Weakness: needs practical tuning guidance for γ schedules, batch sizes, and implementation of shared-noise coupling.  
- Presentation issues: clarify Fig.1a (Gaussian curve vs empirical posterior) and explain Fig.2a behavior (bias dominated by 1/(Kγ) for fixed K).  
- Acceptance arguments pro: solid theoretical contribution with meaningful rate improvements, simple general method, consistent empirical gains.  
- Acceptance arguments con: modest conceptual novelty, benefits hinge on parallel hardware, sensitivity to coupling and limited baseline exploration.  
- Overall recommendation: well-executed, useful contribution with theoretical and practical value; recommend weak accept after minor presentation and discussion fixes.
