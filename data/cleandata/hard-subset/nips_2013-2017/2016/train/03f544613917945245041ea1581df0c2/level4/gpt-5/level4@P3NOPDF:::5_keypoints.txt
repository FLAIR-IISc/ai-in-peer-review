- Summary: Proposes SG-RR-MCMC using Richardson–Romberg extrapolation on two parallel chains to cancel leading discretization bias, with theoretical consistency and supportive experiments.
- Strength: Conceptually simple and broadly applicable, leveraging a known SDE extrapolation technique with minimal engineering.
- Strength: Naturally parallelizable via two independent chains, avoiding inter-chain communication.
- Strength: Theoretical analysis claims asymptotic consistency and improved convergence rates under appropriate conditions.
- Strength: Empirical results generally support reduced bias and improved accuracy.
- Weakness: Lacks direct comparison to higher-order integrators; need matched-compute empirical baselines to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Weakness: Inconsistent and ambiguous step-size schedule descriptions; unclear whether constant or decaying steps and how paired step sizes are chosen.
- Weakness: Algorithmic inconsistency between Algorithm 1 (supplement) and Equation (5), hindering reproducibility and assessment.
- Weakness: Computational trade-offs not fully discussed—two chains double gradient work; wall-clock costs, memory, and scaling with cores need explicit treatment.
- Weakness: Interaction with stochastic gradient noise not fully separated from discretization bias; assumptions and limits where extrapolation helps are unclear.
- Request: Provide a clear, self-contained step-size policy (e.g., (h, h/2), constant vs. decaying) and specify schedules used in experiments.
- Request: Reconcile Algorithm 1 with Equation (5) and provide minimal pseudocode mapping lines to equations for reproducibility.
- Request: Add a comparison to a strong higher-order integrator adapted for stochastic gradients under matched gradient-evaluation budgets, reporting accuracy and wall-clock time.
- Request: Report effective sample size per second (or per gradient eval) and bias metrics to distinguish bias reduction from variance effects.
- Request: Study sensitivity to minibatch size and gradient noise, and state any variance-reduction assumptions explicitly or provide empirical sensitivity results.
- Request: Clarify precise conditions for asymptotic consistency (e.g., Lipschitz/dissipativity, step-size requirements, gradient-noise properties) and interpret constants in the convergence rate.
- Request: Detail parallel implementation choices (shared seeds/preconditioning, reproducibility, asynchronous execution effects).
- Overall assessment: Interesting and timely contribution with promising theory and experiments, but impact requires resolving step-size/algorithm inconsistencies and adding fair higher-order comparisons.
