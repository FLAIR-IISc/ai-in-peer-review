

The paper introduces SG-RR-MCMC, a novel approach to reduce bias in stochastic gradient MCMC methods by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea leverages two independent SG-MCMC chains run in parallel without communication, where the bias is estimated and canceled via Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The theoretical contribution establishes asymptotic consistency of the proposed method, while empirical results demonstrate reduced bias compared to baseline SG-MCMC techniques. The approach is conceptually straightforward, effectively combining Richardson-Romberg extrapolation with stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD), and the parallel implementation offers computational efficiency.

The primary strength lies in the clean theoretical justification for bias reduction. The authors demonstrate that SG-RR-MCMC achieves a bias reduction factor of two relative to standard SG-MCMC, corresponding to an improved convergence rate in the bias term. This is a meaningful advancement, particularly for applications where bias mitigation is critical. The empirical validation is convincing, showing consistent bias reduction across multiple datasets and model configurations, with minimal additional computational overhead due to the embarrassingly parallel structure.

However, the paper contains two significant technical inconsistencies that require clarification. First, the theoretical analysis assumes a specific step size scaling (e.g., \( h \) and \( h/2 \) for the two chains), but the implementation details in Algorithm 1 (Supplement) use the same step size \( h \) for both chains. This contradicts the theoretical premise, as Richardson-Romberg requires different step sizes to estimate the bias. The authors must reconcile this discrepancy—either adjust the algorithm to use differing step sizes or revise the theoretical assumptions to align with the implementation.

Second, the paper claims SG-RR-MCMC achieves a better convergence rate than standard SG-MCMC. While this is technically correct for the bias term, the authors do not compare against high-order integrators (e.g., those using higher-order stochastic Taylor expansions), which can also reduce bias without requiring parallel chains. Such a comparison is essential to contextualize the novelty and practical utility of SG-RR-MCMC. The omission weakens the argument that SG-RR-MCMC offers a distinct advantage over existing bias-reduction techniques. A brief comparison with a high-order SGLD variant (e.g., using the method in [Mandt et al., 2017]) would strengthen the empirical contribution significantly.

The writing occasionally lacks precision. For instance, the phrase "off-the-shelf tool for SDE" is misleading; Richardson-Romberg is a numerical method for error cancellation, not inherently tied to SDEs. The paper should clarify that it applies this extrapolation technique to the discretization error of SG-MCMC, which is derived from an underlying SDE. Additionally, the claim that SG-RR-MCMC "can be seen as a simple combination" understates the contribution—while the components are familiar, their integration for bias reduction in SG-MCMC is novel and non-trivial.

The presentation of Algorithm 1 and Equation (5) requires attention. Equation (5) defines the extrapolated estimator using step sizes \( h \) and \( h/2 \), but Algorithm 1 (Supplement) uses identical step sizes \( h \) for both chains. This inconsistency risks misinterpretation of the method and undermines the theoretical claims. The authors must correct this discrepancy in the final version.

Overall, the paper presents a valid and interesting approach to bias reduction in SG-MCMC, with a strong theoretical foundation and compelling empirical results. The parallel implementation is a practical advantage. However, the technical inconsistencies regarding step size scaling and the absence of comparison with high-order integrators are substantive issues that must be addressed. If resolved, the paper would make a solid contribution to the field. As written, the inconsistencies prevent full confidence in the theoretical claims and limit the perceived novelty relative to existing methods.

Recommendation: **Minor Revision**. The authors should: (1) Correct the step size inconsistency between theory (Equation 5) and implementation (Algorithm 1); (2) Include a comparison with a high-order SG-MCMC integrator (e.g., using the method from [Mandt et al., 2017]) to contextualize the convergence rate claim; and (3) Clarify the relationship between Richardson-Romberg and SDE discretization in the introduction. These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper’s technical rigor and contribution.
