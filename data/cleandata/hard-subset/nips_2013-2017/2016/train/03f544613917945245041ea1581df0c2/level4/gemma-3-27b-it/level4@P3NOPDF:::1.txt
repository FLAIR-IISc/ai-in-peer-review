## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper presents a compelling application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to recent advances in “big-data MCMC” methods, specifically Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE, a well-established numerical integration technique, has recently found fruitful applications in the context of stochastic differential equations (SDEs). A key strength of this work lies in bridging the MCMC and SDE literatures, a connection that holds potential benefits for both research communities. The authors introduce a novel SGLD-RRE algorithm, accompanied by theoretical results establishing its convergence properties – including insightful arguments explaining when SGLD-RRE is expected to outperform standard SGLD.  The empirical evaluation demonstrates impressive improvements on controlled numerical examples and consistent, albeit more modest, gains in large-scale applications. 

Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method effectively leverages RRE within the SGLD framework, resulting in a computationally efficient algorithm. The theoretical analysis is sound and supports the observed empirical performance. While the approach doesn’t represent a paradigm shift, I anticipate practitioners will find this method readily applicable and beneficial.

The core idea of applying RRE to SGLD is both intuitive and well-executed. The authors clearly articulate the motivations for their work, carefully outlining the limitations of standard SGLD and how RRE can address these issues. The derived theoretical results regarding convergence are a particularly strong aspect of the paper, providing a rigorous justification for the proposed algorithm. The empirical validation, while focused on a specific set of problems, provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The observed improvements, even if modest in the large-scale examples, are consistent and suggest a practical advantage for certain applications.

However, there are a couple of points that could benefit from clarification.  Figure 1a, as presented, could be misleading. While it visually represents a Gaussian distribution, it's crucial to emphasize that this is *not* an empirical estimate of the posterior distribution itself, but rather a Gaussian approximation constructed using the empirical posterior mean and variance. A more explicit caption or accompanying text would prevent misinterpretation, particularly for readers less familiar with the nuances of posterior inference. 

Furthermore, Figure 2a presents a counter-intuitive observation: the bias doesn't necessarily decrease monotonically as the stepsize decreases. A brief discussion explaining this phenomenon would significantly enhance the understanding of the algorithm's behavior. The authors likely have an intuition for why this occurs, and sharing that insight would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and concise writing.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated empirical improvements on relevant benchmark problems.
*   Valuable connection between MCMC and SDE literatures.
*   Computational efficiency of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Potential for misinterpretation of Figure 1a.
*   Lack of explanation for the non-monotonic bias behavior in Figure 2a.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. It presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically relevant contribution to the field of MCMC. The minor points raised above are easily addressable and would further strengthen an already solid manuscript. The paper's clarity and potential impact on practitioners make it a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop.
