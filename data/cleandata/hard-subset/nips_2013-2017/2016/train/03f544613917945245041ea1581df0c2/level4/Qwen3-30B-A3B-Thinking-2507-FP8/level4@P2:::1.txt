

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors bridge a gap between the MCMC and numerical SDE communities by extending Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a standard numerical technique for improving convergence rates, to SG-MCMC frameworks. This is particularly relevant given recent work on SG-MCMC methods like SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and the theoretical analysis of bias and MSE bounds for SG-MCMC (Chen et al., 2015). The paper's main contribution is showing that SGRRLD achieves O(γ²) asymptotic bias and O(γ⁴) variance, with a non-asymptotic MSE bound of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), significantly outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors prove asymptotic consistency, establish a central limit theorem for the estimator, and derive non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. These results are carefully derived under appropriate conditions, with the supplementary material providing detailed proofs. The theoretical analysis properly connects to prior work, particularly Chen et al. (2015) on higher-order integrators, showing that SGRRLD achieves similar accuracy to these methods without requiring complex higher-order discretizations. The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-structured mathematical exposition and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation.

The paper's originality lies in the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a technique not previously explored in this context. While the core idea of applying RRE to numerical methods is established, its adaptation to the stochastic gradient setting with correlated Brownian motion is novel. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from prior work by demonstrating that SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods using only first-order integrators, while remaining computationally efficient. The method's generic applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms (beyond SGLD) is an important theoretical contribution.

Regarding significance, SGRRLD offers meaningful improvements for large-scale Bayesian inference. The method's parallelizability aligns well with modern computational architectures, and the improved convergence rates translate to practical benefits: as shown in the matrix factorization experiments, SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy to SGLD in approximately half the wall-clock time. The results on both synthetic and real datasets provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness, with consistent improvements across problem sizes. The paper makes a compelling case for the method's potential impact on practitioners, particularly in settings where computational efficiency is critical.

The paper has minor weaknesses that could be addressed. The description of Figure 1a could be clearer, as it shows a Gaussian approximation based on empirical posterior moments rather than the empirical posterior distribution itself. This distinction should be emphasized to avoid reader confusion. Additionally, while the paper explains that bias doesn't necessarily decrease with smaller step sizes (Figure 2), a brief theoretical explanation would strengthen this point. The authors could clarify that for fixed iteration counts, the 1/(Kγ) term in the MSE bound dominates for small γ, explaining the observed behavior.

The review should also note that while the method is technically sound and well-motivated, its practical impact might be limited to specific problem domains where bias reduction is critical. However, the paper's clear exposition and strong theoretical foundation make it valuable for both theoretical and applied researchers in Bayesian machine learning.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution by adapting a well-established numerical technique to SG-MCMC, resulting in a method that achieves superior convergence rates without compromising computational efficiency. The theoretical analysis is thorough and properly contextualized within existing literature, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The method represents a practical and theoretically sound improvement over existing SG-MCMC approaches, with clear potential for broader application in large-scale Bayesian inference. The minor clarifications suggested would further enhance the paper's impact.

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field, with the minor revisions noted above. The paper effectively demonstrates how insights from numerical analysis can enhance MCMC methods, fostering cross-pollination between two important research areas. The results provide both theoretical guarantees and practical benefits, making this work valuable to both the theoretical and applied communities.
