This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a standard numerical integration technique, to recent MCMC methods designed for large-scale datasets, focusing specifically on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The work introduces a new algorithm, SGLD-RRE, provides a theoretical analysis of its convergence, and demonstrates its performance on both synthetic and real-world problems.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a solid and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution. It successfully bridges the MCMC and numerical Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) literature, a connection that could prove beneficial for both communities. The proposed method is supported by interesting theoretical results and good empirical performance. The paper is very clear, technically sound, and easy to read. The SGLD-RRE method is a fairly straightforward yet computationally convenient application of RRE, and it is likely that practitioners will find it useful. I recommend acceptance.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Insight:** A key strength of the paper is the insightful link it establishes between the MCMC and SDE literature. By framing SGLD as a numerical SDE problem, the authors open the door to applying powerful variance-reduction techniques like RRE, which may inspire further work in this direction.
*   **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper develops valuable theoretical results on the convergence of the proposed SGLD-RRE algorithm. The analysis provides formal arguments for when SGLD-RRE can be expected to improve upon the performance of standard SGLD, adding rigor to the empirical findings.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The numerical experiments effectively demonstrate the utility of the proposed method. The algorithm achieves impressive improvements on toy examples and, more importantly, delivers modest but consistent performance gains in large-scale applications. This demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the method, and presenting their results. The technical content appears sound and is communicated effectively.

**Weaknesses / Opportunities for Improvement**

The primary contribution is the application of a well-established technique (RRE) to an existing algorithm (SGLD). While effective and well-executed, this makes the work more of an incremental, practical improvement rather than a revolutionary new approach. The method is unlikely to fundamentally change current MCMC practices, but it does represent a useful and clever enhancement that practitioners can readily adopt.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions**

1.  **Figure 1a Clarification:** The presentation of Figure 1a is slightly misleading. It appears to show an empirical estimate of the posterior distribution, but it is actually a Gaussian distribution plotted using the empirical posterior mean and variance. This should be explicitly clarified in the caption or text to avoid misinterpretation by the reader.
2.  **Figure 2a Discussion:** In Figure 2a, the bias does not necessarily decrease as the step size decreases, which is a counter-intuitive result. It would be beneficial to add a brief discussion explaining this phenomenon. For example, is this an interaction between the discretization error and the stochastic gradient noise? Elucidating this point would strengthen the paper's analysis.
