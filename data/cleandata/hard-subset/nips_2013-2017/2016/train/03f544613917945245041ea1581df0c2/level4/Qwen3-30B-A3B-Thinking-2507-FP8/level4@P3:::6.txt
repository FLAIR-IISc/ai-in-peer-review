

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors propose running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Brownian motions, then combining their outputs via RR extrapolation. The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves O(K⁻⁴/⁵) convergence rates for mean squared error (MSE), superior to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). The authors validate their approach with experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks, showing improved accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the SG-MCMC literature, building upon prior work on convergence analysis of SG-MCMC methods [10] and RR extrapolation for SDEs [23]. While RR extrapolation has been studied in numerical analysis for SDEs, its application to SG-MCMC represents a novel and useful adaptation. The authors carefully distinguish their work from previous contributions, particularly clarifying how their approach achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators while using first-order methods.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong and well-executed, with formal proofs of asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. The authors establish clear bounds for bias and MSE, demonstrating the superior convergence rates of SGRRLD. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering both synthetic data with analytically tractable posteriors and real-world matrix factorization tasks. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of implementation considerations, particularly regarding communication costs in distributed settings.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, with appropriate technical details provided. The connection between the theoretical results and experimental findings is well-maintained. The only minor issue is the repetition of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" in lines 25 and 70.

**Originality:** While the RR extrapolation technique itself is not new, its application to SG-MCMC represents a meaningful contribution. The paper effectively demonstrates how this technique can bridge the gap between first-order and higher-order SG-MCMC methods, achieving the theoretical accuracy of more complex approaches with simpler implementation. The authors provide a clear justification for why this approach is novel in the SG-MCMC context.

**Significance:** The improved convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) is significant for large-scale Bayesian inference, as it means SGRRLD can achieve better accuracy in less time. The practical experiments on matrix factorization tasks demonstrate this advantage in real-world applications, where SGRRLD achieves comparable accuracy to SGLD in approximately half the wall-clock time. This represents a meaningful advancement for practitioners working with large-scale Bayesian models.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical justification for the proposed method
- Rigorous convergence rate analysis with formal bounds
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Effective demonstration of practical advantages in large-scale settings
- Well-motivated extension of existing numerical techniques to SG-MCMC

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not adequately address practical implementation details, particularly regarding communication costs in distributed settings. The authors assume no communication cost when running two chains in parallel, which may not reflect real-world distributed implementations.
- The experimental comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD does not clearly specify how computational resources were allocated. It is unclear whether SGRRLD's two chains shared the same computational resources as SGLD's single chain, which would make the comparison unfair. If resources were split between the two SGRRLD chains, the paper should explain how this allocation was determined.
- The paper does not compare against an embarrassingly parallel implementation of two SGLD chains (without communication), which would provide a better baseline for evaluating the communication overhead.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* This paper presents a well-executed extension of SG-MCMC methods with strong theoretical guarantees and practical benefits. The improved convergence rates and demonstrated performance gains on real-world tasks make it a valuable contribution to the field.

*Con:* The practical implementation details are insufficiently addressed, potentially limiting the paper's impact for practitioners. The experimental comparison could be strengthened with more transparent resource allocation details.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution and experimental validation are strong, and the practical advantages demonstrated are significant. The authors should address the implementation concerns by adding a brief discussion of communication costs and clarifying the resource allocation in experiments. Specifically, they should explain how computational resources were allocated between the two chains in SGRRLD and whether this represents a fair comparison with SGLD. Additionally, they might consider adding a comparison against an embarrassingly parallel implementation of two SGLD chains as a baseline. These clarifications would strengthen the paper's practical relevance without requiring substantial new experiments. The paper's core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
