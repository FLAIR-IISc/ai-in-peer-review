### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical sequence acceleration technique, to the SGLD framework. In practice, this involves running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2, where the second chain performs two steps for each single step of the first) and combining their outputs. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. They also derive non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating superior rates of convergence (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) compared to standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). The practical effectiveness of the proposed method is validated through experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a well-known and critical limitation of SG-MCMC methods—the bias introduced by the numerical integrator, which often hampers their accuracy. By providing a general and effective method for bias reduction, this work has the potential for significant impact on practitioners and researchers using these scalable Bayesian inference techniques.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are a major strength. The authors provide a rigorous analysis that includes asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and, most importantly, non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. These results formally establish that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD and other first-order methods, providing strong theoretical justification for the approach.

3.  **Originality and Simplicity:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a known technique in the numerical analysis of SDEs, its application to the SG-MCMC context is novel and clever. The proposed method is conceptually simple yet powerful. It achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only a first-order integrator, which is an elegant and practical contribution.

4.  **Practicality and Scalability:** A key advantage of SGRRLD is its suitability for parallel computation. The two chains can be run independently without any communication overhead, making the method "embarrassingly parallel." This design is well-suited to modern multi-core architectures. The experiments on large-scale MovieLens datasets effectively demonstrate that SGRRLD can achieve better accuracy than SGLD in the same amount of wall-clock time, highlighting its practical efficiency.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, the proposed method, and the theoretical and empirical results. The connection to prior work on both SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation for SDEs is well-established.

#### Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Incremental Nature of the Contribution:** The primary criticism could be that the work is a combination of two existing techniques: SGLD and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. While the application is novel and the analysis is thorough, the core algorithmic idea is not entirely new but rather a skillful adaptation of a classical method.

2.  **Inconsistencies and Clarity in Presentation:** There appears to be a discrepancy between the description of the second chain's update in Equation (5) and its implementation as described in Algorithm 1 (in the supplement). Specifically, the algorithm seems to use the state `θ(γ/2)_2k` to compute the gradient for the step leading to `θ(γ/2)_(2k+1)`, whereas Equation (5) suggests the gradient should be evaluated at `θ(γ/2)_(2k+1)`. This should be clarified and corrected to ensure reproducibility. *(Self-correction based on re-reading: The equation seems to use an incorrect index. The gradient for the step from `2k` to `2k+1` should be evaluated at `2k`, not `2k+1`)*.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** While the experiments are generally strong, the comparison to higher-order integrators could be more direct. The authors rightly claim that SGRRLD can match the theoretical accuracy of such methods. The experiment in Figure 4 shows this for SGHMC, which is a valuable demonstration of the method's generality. However, since the paper's main focus is SGLD, the central claim would be more compelling if SGRRLD were also directly compared against an SGLD variant that uses a higher-order integrator on the synthetic benchmark.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   Please double-check and correct the inconsistency between Equation (5) and Algorithm 1 regarding the state at which the gradient is evaluated for the second chain. This is crucial for the paper's technical correctness and reproducibility.
*   The discussion around the optimal choice of the step size decay exponent `α` is insightful. It would be helpful to provide more practical guidance on setting the leading constant (`γ1` or `γ?`) for the step size schedule, as this was determined empirically in the experiments and is critical for achieving the stated performance.
*   The paper makes a strong case for parallelism. It would be interesting to briefly discuss the computational trade-off: SGRRLD performs roughly three times the number of gradient computations per effective iteration (one for chain 1, two for chain 2) compared to SGLD. The benefit is clear when two cores are available, but a brief comment on the cost-benefit analysis on a single core could add nuance. The wall-clock time experiments already provide a strong argument, but an explicit mention of this trade-off would be welcome.

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a simple, effective, and theoretically well-grounded method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms. The core idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is novel in this context and addresses a significant practical problem. The strengths—particularly the rigorous theoretical analysis and convincing large-scale experiments—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are largely related to presentation and are easily addressable. The work represents a solid contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. I recommend acceptance.
