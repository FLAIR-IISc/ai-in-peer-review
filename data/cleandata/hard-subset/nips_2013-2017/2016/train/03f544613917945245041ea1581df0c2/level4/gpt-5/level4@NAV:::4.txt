This paper advances existing SG-MCMC by introducing a Richardson–Romberg scheme that improves convergence rates in terms of bias and MSE. The scheme is straightforward to implement, requiring only minimal changes to traditional SG-MCMC. The work is also supported by strong theoretical results. The paper proposes a Romberg–Richardson scheme that can enhance the convergence rates of standard SG-MCMC algorithms; the method appears elegant and the theory solid. My main concern is how the specific convergence rates are derived from the theorem (as discussed in the “explain fatal flaws” section). Beyond this, I have a practical concern about the matrix factorization experiment. On a single-core machine, the proposed method would take roughly three times longer than standard SG-MCMC because it runs two chains and one of them uses twice as many steps. If we account for this, the plots in Figure 5 may not be fair; the time for SGLD should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3, which would narrow the gap between SGLD and SGRRLD. In Figure 3 (bias plot), the best convergence seems to be achieved by the red curve corresponding to \alpha=0.2, not 1/3 as stated in the text—am I missing something? Also, the purportedly optimal curve fluctuates considerably, so the observed rate does not match \alpha=0.2; can you explain why this happens? In addition, line 256 states that when the stepsize is too small, the term 1/K\gamma will dominate; why does this not occur for SGLD?

Line 249—Figure 1(b) plots bias vs. time, not dimension; should this be Figure 1(c)? Also, please use bold letters to denote vectors.
