- Introduces SGRRLD: uses Richardson-Romberg extrapolation by running two correlated SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to reduce bias.  
- Achieves O(γ²) asymptotic bias vs O(γ) for standard SG-MCMC, improving finite-time MSE convergence (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³)).  
- Matches accuracy of higher-order integrators without their computational complexity.  
- Provides strong theoretical analysis: convergence proofs, central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds.  
- Shows asymptotic consistency and superior convergence rates compared to SGLD and other first-order methods.  
- Empirical results (synthetic Gaussian and large-scale matrix factorization) show significantly lower bias and MSE than SGLD at comparable cost.  
- Method is conceptually simple, minimally invasive to existing SG-MCMC implementations, and remains parallelizable.  
- Demonstrates generality by applying the approach to other SG-MCMC algorithms (e.g., SGHMC).  
- Needs clarification on exact conditions under which RR extrapolation applies beyond the assumed expansion π_γ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ²).  
- Unclear whether the method extends to non-SG-MCMC approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling MH) or distributed implementations.  
- Computational inefficiency concern: one chain runs twice as long; authors did not explore three-chain or alternative configurations to reduce waste.  
- Matrix factorization experiment should include comparison to non-Bayesian baselines (e.g., SGD) to demonstrate practical advantages.  
- Main weakness: limited discussion of practical implementation details such as step-size schedules and mini-batch selection.  
- Suggestion to add algorithm pseudocode to improve practitioner accessibility.  
- Pros summary: novel RR application to SG-MCMC, rigorous theory, simple/general implementation, empirical validation, preserves parallelizability.  
- Cons summary: limited practical guidance, missing non-Bayesian comparisons, unclear applicability conditions, no exploration of alternative chain setups.  
- Overall verdict: valuable and impactful contribution with sufficient theoretical and empirical support; reviewer recommends acceptance.
