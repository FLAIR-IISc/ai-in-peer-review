Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer a practical approach to Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets and have attracted significant attention in recent years. A known limitation is their asymptotic bias: with a fixed step size, they do not converge exactly to the posterior. This paper proposes reducing that bias via Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation.

The idea is straightforward. Run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel: Chain 1 with step size γ and Chain 2 with step size γ/2, with Chain 2 taking twice as many steps as Chain 1. The injected Gaussian noises are coupled: if βk denotes the Gaussian noise used by Chain 2 at step k, then the noise for Chain 1 at step k is set to αk = (β2k−1 + β2k) / sqrt(2). To estimate Eπ[f], form an extrapolated estimator by appropriately combining the two empirical estimates to cancel the O(γ) term (as specified in the paper). This leads to a reduced bias of order O(γ^2), compared to the O(γ) bias of a single chain, and the paper also establishes a corresponding reduction in MSE. The method is simple to implement, requires minimal modifications to existing SG-MCMC algorithms, and appears widely applicable across SG-MCMC variants, making it potentially impactful for the SG-MCMC community.

Novelty: While RR extrapolation is well known in numerical analysis, it is not widely used in the machine learning or SG-MCMC literature. Applying it systematically to SG-MCMC with careful noise coupling is, in my view, novel and useful.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The main ideas are well motivated and the theoretical claims are supported by analysis.

Comments and questions:
- Scope of applicability. Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? Beyond the assumption in Section 2.2 that πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ^2), are there other structural conditions required (e.g., reversibility, smoothness, stability of discretization, or properties of preconditioning matrices)?
- Beyond SG-MCMC. Can the approach be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes not based on stochastic gradients, such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests? What about distributed or asynchronous variants such as Distributed SGLD—are there pitfalls for the coupling or extrapolation in that setting?
- Computational efficiency. As described, one chain runs twice as long as the other, leaving the larger-step chain idle for half the wall-clock time if run sequentially on shared hardware. Would a three-chain design—one chain at γ and two chains at γ/2, all run for the same number of steps—achieve the same bias cancellation with better hardware utilization? How would this affect variance, given the different coupling structure?
- Higher-order extensions. Is there a practical extension to more than two step sizes (e.g., using γ, γ/2, γ/4) to achieve O(γ^3) or beyond? What are the stability and variance trade-offs, and does noise coupling generalize cleanly?
- Coupling of noise and minibatches. The noise variables must be correlated as specified, but the role of minibatch correlation is less clear. Is variance minimized when the same minibatch is used at iteration k of Chain 1 and iterations 2k−1 and 2k of Chain 2, or is this effect negligible relative to the Gaussian noise coupling? Any guidance or theory here would help practitioners.
- Experiments on matrix factorization. The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) would be more convincing if run to convergence, with an additional SGD baseline. The current plot shows your method outperforming SGLD, but it would strengthen the case to demonstrate gains over SGD in a regime where Bayesian modeling is advantageous. Since Bayesian matrix factorization is not yet the standard in practice, concrete performance numbers and an SGD control would better contextualize the final errors of your method versus SGLD.
- Presentation. Although the method is simple, an algorithm box or early pseudo-code would broaden accessibility. Many practitioners may skip the more technical sections; a concise algorithmic summary up front would make adoption much easier.

Overall, this is a clear and compelling paper. The RR extrapolation strategy is easy to implement, theoretically grounded, and broadly applicable, and it has the potential to improve a wide class of SG-MCMC methods. Addressing the questions above—especially around scope of applicability, minibatch coupling, and more comprehensive experiments—would further strengthen the impact.
