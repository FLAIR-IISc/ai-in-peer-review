This paper introduces a method to accelerate the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm by leveraging a numerical sequence acceleration technique – Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator. The approach necessitates running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, employing distinct step sizes and incorporating correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties. Experiments conducted on a synthetic Gaussian model validate the theoretical bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). Overall, this is a well-written paper with a sound technical contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods, effectively contextualizing the proposed technique within existing literature. 

I have two primary concerns. First, the novelty of the paper may be incremental. Prior work [10] analyzes the impact of numerical integrators on SGMCMC, and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has previously been applied to approximate invariant distributions [23]. Second, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison lacks sufficient fairness. The parallel implementation of SG-MCMC, coupled with the correlated Brownian motion requiring inter-chain communication at each step, introduces overhead. The authors do not address or account for this communication cost, and should explicitly acknowledge this assumption. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks clarity regarding resource allocation. Specifically, it is unclear how computational resources were assigned to SGRRLD and SGLD. For example, was a single SGLD chain implemented using resources equivalent to those utilized by the two SGRRLD chains? If not, this presents an unfair comparison from a practical perspective. If so, further details are needed on how resources were partitioned between the two SGRRLD chains during the algorithm’s execution. Given that the computational cost of SG-MCMC is dominated by stochastic gradient evaluation, it’s unclear how dividing resources across two chains in SGRRLD could lead to lower wall-clock time than dedicating full resources to a single SGLD chain. Please provide more details regarding the implementation setup. Finally, an alternative approach would be to run two SGLD chains independently (without communication); a comparison against this would be valuable.
