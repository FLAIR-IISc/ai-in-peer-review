### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the SGLD algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to form a new estimator. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). They demonstrate theoretically that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)), effectively matching the accuracy of higher-order integrators while only using a first-order scheme. The method's effectiveness is evaluated on a synthetic linear Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task using the MovieLens datasets.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and clever idea. It addresses the well-known issue of bias in first-order SG-MCMC methods by combining established techniques from numerical analysis and stochastic simulation in a new context. The resulting algorithm is conceptually simple and elegant.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE of the proposed estimator. Deriving these results in the stochastic gradient setting is non-trivial, and the finding that SGRRLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods is a significant theoretical result.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-explained, the proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely, and the connection to prior work on SGLD and RR extrapolation for SDEs is clearly articulated.

4.  **Practicality and Parallelism:** The proposed method is inherently parallel, as the two required MCMC chains can be run independently. This makes it well-suited for modern multi-core architectures, allowing its wall-clock time to be comparable to that of a single SGLD chain, as demonstrated in the experiments.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the experimental design, which may not provide a fair comparison of computational resources.
    *   The authors compare SGRRLD (using two parallel chains) against a single SGLD chain over the same wall-clock time. A more convincing and equitable baseline would involve using the same computational resources for the competing method. For instance, one could run two independent SGLD chains in parallel and average their results. This would utilize the same number of cores and a similar number of gradient computations as SGRRLD. It is not clear if the observed improvements are due to the RR extrapolation scheme itself or simply the effect of running more MCMC chains.
    *   The SGLD baseline uses a step size γ, while SGRRLD uses step sizes γ and γ/2. A more comprehensive comparison should also include an SGLD baseline running with the smaller step size γ/2, as a smaller step size is known to reduce bias. Without these crucial baselines, the source of the performance gain remains ambiguous.

2.  **Limited Empirical Evidence on Real Data:** While the results on the synthetic Gaussian model are compelling and clearly illustrate the theoretical benefits, the improvements on the MovieLens datasets are less pronounced. The paper claims "significant performance improvements," but the RMSE plots show a relatively modest gain. Given the concerns about the fairness of the baselines, the practical significance of the method is not yet fully established. Including experiments on a wider range of real-world problems would strengthen the paper's claims.

### **Questions for the Authors and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  To strengthen the experimental validation, I strongly recommend including the baselines mentioned above:
    *   An ensemble of two parallel SGLD chains (with step size γ), where results are averaged.
    *   A single SGLD chain (or two parallel chains) run with the smaller step size γ/2.
    This would help disentangle the benefits of the RR scheme from the benefits of parallelism and smaller step sizes.

2.  The theoretical analysis relies on certain assumptions about the potential function U. Could you elaborate on the scope of these assumptions? For example, does the theory hold for multi-modal posterior distributions, which are frequently encountered in machine learning applications?

3.  Could you provide more intuition as to why the RR extrapolation reduces the overall error? The paper notes that correlating the Brownian motion terms is key to controlling the variance. However, the stochastic gradient noise in each chain, driven by different mini-batches, is likely uncorrelated. How does the RR combination effectively cancel bias terms in the presence of this uncorrelated gradient noise?

4.  The experiments on the linear Gaussian model convincingly demonstrate the reduction in bias of the posterior variance. It would be interesting to see if similar improvements hold for other moments or aspects of the posterior in more complex models.

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically sound contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The core idea is innovative and promising. However, the experimental evaluation needs to be more rigorous to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method over appropriate and equally-resourced baselines. Addressing the concerns regarding the experimental comparison would significantly strengthen the paper.
