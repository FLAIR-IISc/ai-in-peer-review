This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, referred to as Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias of existing SG-MCMC methods while maintaining a reasonable variance level. The approach leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of the SG-MCMC algorithm. The SGRRLD algorithm requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated injecting Gaussian noise.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of SGRRLD, including asymptotic and non-asymptotic results. The authors derive bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE) of the SGRRLD estimator and show that it achieves higher rates of convergence than existing SG-MCMC methods with first-order integrators. The theoretical results are supported by experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization problems, demonstrating the practical use of the proposed algorithm.

Overall, the paper presents a valid contribution to the area of SG-MCMC methods and does a good job of putting the method in context with similar previous approaches. However, I have two main concerns: (1) The novelty of the paper may be incremental, given that the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. (2) The practical use of the algorithm can be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in the experiments.

Specifically, the paper presents a parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm, which requires the two chains to communicate at every step. However, the authors do not consider the communication cost, assuming there is no cost. It would be better to mention this and discuss the potential implications on the algorithm's performance. Furthermore, it is not clear how the computing resources are assigned for the SGRRLD and SGLD algorithms in the experiments. For example, is the single chain of SGLD implemented using the resources that the two chains of SGRRLD use? If not, this may not be a fair comparison for practical considerations. If yes, then the question is how to split the resources for the two chains of SGRRLD in the algorithm procedure.

Additionally, the computation cost of SG-MCMC mostly comes from computing stochastic gradients. I would wonder how partial resources used in SGRRLD for computing gradients at each chain could lead to less wall-clock time than full resources used in SGLD. Please add more details on the setup of the implementation. An alternative is to run two chains of SGLD embarrassingly (without communication); it would be interesting to compare this with the proposed approach.

Minor comments: (1) "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" appears twice, on lines 25 and 70.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that achieves higher rates of convergence than existing methods.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of SGRRLD.
* The experiments demonstrate the practical use of the proposed algorithm.

Weaknesses:

* The novelty of the paper may be incremental, given the existing literature on numerical integrators and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation.
* The practical use of the algorithm can be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in the experiments.
* The paper does not consider the communication cost of the parallel implementation.

Recommendations:

* Discuss the potential implications of the communication cost on the algorithm's performance.
* Clarify how the computing resources are assigned for the SGRRLD and SGLD algorithms in the experiments.
* Provide more details on the setup of the implementation, including how to split the resources for the two chains of SGRRLD.
* Consider comparing the proposed approach with running two chains of SGLD embarrassingly (without communication).
