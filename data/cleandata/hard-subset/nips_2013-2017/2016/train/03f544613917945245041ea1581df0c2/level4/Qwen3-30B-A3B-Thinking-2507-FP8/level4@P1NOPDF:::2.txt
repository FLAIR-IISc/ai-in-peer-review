

This paper introduces SGRRLD, which applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias from gradient estimation noise. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD under certain conditions, with a faster convergence rate for the same step size—though this requires running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is clearly described and implementation is straightforward.

Synthetic experiments on a Linear Gaussian Model effectively illustrate SGRRLD's advantage in bias reduction. However, the real-data experiments on MovieLens yield only a marginal improvement in test RMSE, and the significance of this result is questionable. While the authors claim computational cost remains comparable to SGLD (via parallelization), this comparison is problematic: running two parallel SGLD chains within the same time budget would provide a more meaningful baseline for comparison. Specifically, the observed RMSE improvement for SGRRLD should be evaluated against the average estimate from two parallel SGLD chains (with step size γ), not just a single SGLD chain (with step size γ). The current comparison against SGLD with step size γ/2 (which inherently has lower bias) is insufficient; a fairer comparison would include SGLD with step size γ/2 run in parallel.

I have not verified the technical details of Theorems 1 and 2, but I have two key theoretical concerns. First, what specific conditions must the potential function U satisfy to meet both theorems? Crucially, can U be multi-modal? Second, the paper claims RR extrapolation reduces the error order by combining two chains. Intuitively, if gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains should decorrelate quickly even with correlated Gaussian noise. How precisely does this combination suppress the error order? It would be valuable to know whether the authors observe persistent correlation between the two chains during real-data experiments, as this could impact the method's practical effectiveness.

Additional real-data experiments are strongly recommended. The current MovieLens results lack statistical significance and fail to demonstrate that SGRRLD’s bias reduction translates to substantial practical gains. More extensive testing on diverse datasets would better validate the method’s utility. The theoretical contribution is interesting, but the empirical evidence currently falls short of supporting the claim that SGRRLD "significantly" reduces bias in real-world scenarios. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) conduct a fairer comparison against two parallel SGLD chains; (2) include more real-data experiments showing consistent improvements; and (3) clarify the theoretical requirements on U, particularly regarding multi-modality. Without these, the practical relevance of SGRRLD remains uncertain.
