This paper introduces a novel SG-MCMC algorithm, SGRRLD, leveraging the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique to reduce bias and improve convergence rates compared to standard SGLD. The core idea of running two chains in parallel with different step sizes is straightforward to implement, requiring minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC implementations. The theoretical analysis is robust, providing asymptotic and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and demonstrating improved convergence properties. The paper effectively positions the work within the existing literature on SG-MCMC, referencing key contributions such as Welling and Teh [1] and Chen et al. [6].

The strengths of the paper lie in its solid theoretical foundation, the clear presentation of the SGRRLD algorithm, and the empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The convergence analysis, demonstrating rates of O(K−4/5) for MSE, is a significant improvement over the O(K−2/3) rate achieved by SGLD and related methods. The experiments on the Gaussian model effectively illustrate the bias reduction achieved by SGRRLD, while the matrix factorization results on the MovieLens datasets demonstrate its practical applicability. The discussion of correlated Brownian increments to control variance is also a valuable contribution, building upon previous work by Talay and Tubaro [21] and Lemaire et al. [23].

However, there are a few points that require clarification. The derivation of the specific convergence rates from the theorems, particularly regarding the choice of α, could benefit from more detailed explanation. The discussion surrounding the fairness of the timing comparison in Figure 5 is valid. The fact that SGRRLD inherently requires more computation due to the dual chains and step size adjustments should be explicitly acknowledged and addressed in the interpretation of the results – perhaps by including a comparison based on computational cost rather than wall-clock time. In Figure 3, the bias plot suggests that the optimal convergence is achieved with α=0.2, contradicting the stated α=1/3; this discrepancy needs investigation. The fluctuations observed in the optimal curve in Figure 3 also warrant further explanation. Finally, the definition of “smoothness” for the function *f* in the theoretical analysis (line 160) could be more precise.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees demonstrating improved convergence rates.
*   Clear and concise presentation of the algorithm.
*   Empirical results supporting the theoretical findings on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Potential for practical impact in large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor discrepancies and potential ambiguities in the explanation of theoretical results (α value).
*   Need for more careful consideration of computational cost when interpreting experimental results.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the smoothness requirements of the test function *f*.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed SGRRLD algorithm offers a promising approach to reducing bias and improving convergence rates. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
