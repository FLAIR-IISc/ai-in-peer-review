

This paper introduces the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD), yielding the SGRRLD algorithm to diminish bias stemming from gradient estimation noise. SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to SGLD. Experiments on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate a clear advantage of SGRRLD over SGLD in bias reduction. On MovieLens data, SGRRLD shows only marginal improvement in test RMSE, though the significance of this result is debatable. While RR extrapolation has previously enhanced Monte Carlo convergence rates for SDEs, this work extends it to SG-MCMC, proving that SGRRLD attains a faster convergence rate than SGLD with identical step sizes—albeit requiring two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is well-structured and implementation is straightforward. The synthetic experiments robustly validate SGRRLD’s bias reduction. Further real-world experiments are strongly recommended to confirm whether this simple SGLD modification substantially reduces bias. The reviewer did not verify Theorem 1 and 2 details but questions the required conditions for the potential function $U$: can $U$ be multi-modal? Additionally, the reviewer wonders how the error order reduction occurs when gradient noises are uncorrelated (leading to rapid decorrelation of chains despite correlated Gaussian noise) and whether chain correlation is observed in real data runs. A critical concern involves the computational comparison: while the authors claim SGRRLD’s runtime matches SGLD with parallelization, running SGLD with two parallel chains within the same time would provide a fairer benchmark. Specifically, comparing SGRRLD (with step size $\gamma$) against two-chain SGLD (with $\gamma$) rather than single-chain SGLD ($\gamma$) is essential. Furthermore, the real data comparison uses SGRRLD ($\gamma$) vs. SGLD ($\gamma$), but should also include SGLD ($\gamma/2$)—which has lower bias and could run two chains—to isolate SGRRLD’s specific contribution. More extensive real data experiments are necessary to substantiate the method’s practical utility.
