To reduce the bias of SG-MCMC, the authors propose a novel method called stochastic gradient Richardson-Romberg MCMC (SGRRLD). This method employs Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established technique for improving the convergence of numerical solutions to stochastic differential equations. Specifically, SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel, leveraging an embarrassingly parallel structure without inter-chain communication. The authors provide a formal theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on both bias and mean squared error. Empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical findings. The core idea is appealing—a simple yet effective combination of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation and SGLD—and demonstrably improves performance. The theoretical analysis establishes a faster convergence rate compared to standard SGLD, although it achieves similar theoretical accuracy to methods employing higher-order integrators. A direct empirical comparison to higher-order integrators would strengthen this claim. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including proofs of consistency and convergence rates.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD.
*   **Parallelism:** The algorithm is well-suited for parallel and distributed computing architectures.
*   **Generality:** The authors claim the technique can be applied to a variety of SG-MCMC algorithms, expanding its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** While the performance gain over SGLD is clear, the paper lacks a direct empirical comparison to SG-MCMC methods employing higher-order integrators (e.g., those discussed in [10]), which achieve comparable theoretical accuracy.
*   **Step Size Discussion:** There is a potential inconsistency in the discussion of step size selection. Clarification on the optimal step size choice would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithmic Consistency:** A minor inconsistency exists between Algorithm 1 (in the supplementary material) and Equation (5). This should be addressed for clarity.
*   **Presentation:** The mathematical notation could benefit from increased clarity in certain sections, potentially with more explicit definitions of key variables.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over SGLD.
*   The algorithm's inherent parallelism makes it attractive for large-scale applications.
*   The work addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian inference: scaling MCMC to large datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of a direct comparison to higher-order integrators limits the assessment of the method's practical advantages.
*   The minor inconsistencies in the algorithm presentation and step size discussion detract from the paper’s polish.
*   The notation is occasionally dense and could hinder understanding for readers less familiar with the specific mathematical tools used.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic gradient MCMC. While minor issues exist, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation support acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper with a request for the authors to address the inconsistencies mentioned above and to include a comparison to SG-MCMC methods with higher-order integrators in a revised version.
