This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SGRRLD) algorithm that aims to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining a reasonable variance level. The approach is based on the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method that requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, demonstrating that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and has bounded non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error (MSE). They also show that SGRRLD achieves higher rates of convergence than existing SG-MCMC methods with first-order integrators in both finite-time and asymptotic settings.

The paper is well-supported by solid theoretical results and experiments on both synthetic and real data. The proposed algorithm is easy to implement, requiring minimal modifications to traditional SG-MCMC methods. The authors also provide a clear and concise explanation of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation and its application to SG-MCMC.

One potential concern is the derivation of specific convergence rates from the theorem. The authors should provide more details on how they obtained these rates, as the explanation in the current version of the paper is not entirely clear.

Additionally, the matrix factorization experiment results may not be entirely fair. As the proposed method requires running two chains, one of which uses twice as many steps, it would take approximately three times longer than standard SG-MCMC on a single-core computer. To account for this, the time for SGLD should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3, which would reduce the difference between SGLD and SGRRLD.

There are also some minor issues with the paper. For example, in Figure 3, the bias plot suggests that the best convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, not 1/3 as stated in the text. The optimal curve also appears to be fluctuating, which may affect the accuracy of the convergence rate. Furthermore, the authors state that when the step size is too small, the term 1/Kγ would dominate, but this does not seem to happen for SGLD.

Other minor comments include the use of different symbols for θ, the lack of definition for RR, and the undefined notation Γ_K. The authors should also clarify the type of smoothness required for the function f and correct the figure reference in line 249.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to improving the convergence rates of SG-MCMC methods. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to reducing the bias of SG-MCMC methods.
* The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-supported by experiments on both synthetic and real data.
* The proposed algorithm is easy to implement and requires minimal modifications to traditional SG-MCMC methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The derivation of specific convergence rates from the theorem is not entirely clear.
* The matrix factorization experiment results may not be entirely fair due to the computational time difference between SGLD and SGRRLD.
* There are some minor issues with the paper, including inconsistent notation and undefined terms.
