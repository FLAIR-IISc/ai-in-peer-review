This paper introduces the application of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to enhance the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm. By running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with varying step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection, the authors establish the convergence of both asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties. Experimental results on synthetic Gaussian models validate the bias and mean squared error (MSE) bounds of the proposed theorems, while large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithms over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). Overall, this paper presents a well-crafted technique and clear presentation, making a valid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods.

The authors effectively contextualize their method within the framework of similar previous approaches. However, two primary concerns arise: (1) The novelty of this paper may be incremental, given that the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been employed for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. (2) The practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison may lack fairness. As this is a parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm, the use of correlated Brownian motion necessitates communication between the two chains at each step. Nevertheless, the authors seem to overlook the communication cost, assuming it to be negligible. It would be beneficial to acknowledge this aspect.

Moreover, the allocation of computing resources for SGRRLD and SGLD in the experiments is unclear. For instance, is the single SGLD chain implemented using the same resources as the two SGRRLD chains? If not, this may not be a fair comparison from a practical standpoint. If yes, then it is unclear how the resources are split between the two SGRRLD chains in the algorithmic procedure. As the computational cost of SG-MCMC primarily stems from computing stochastic gradients, it is puzzling how partial resource utilization in SGRRLD for computing gradients at each chain could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to full resource utilization in SGLD. Additional details on the implementation setup would be appreciated. Furthermore, an alternative approach would be to run two embarrassingly parallel SGLD chains (without communication); it would be interesting to compare this with the proposed method.

Minor comments include the repetition of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" on lines 25 and 70.
