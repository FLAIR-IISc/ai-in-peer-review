This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation – a well-established numerical technique for accelerating convergence – to the SGLD framework. This is achieved by running two parallel chains with different step sizes and combining their results. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error. Empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (a movie recommendation task) supports the claim that SGRRLD achieves improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and approaches the accuracy of methods utilizing higher-order integrators.

The paper builds upon recent work in SG-MCMC, notably Welling & Teh (2011) who introduced SGLD by combining ideas from the Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm and Stochastic Gradient Descent. The authors correctly position their work within the context of efforts to improve upon SGLD's bias, citing contributions like Chen et al. (2015) and Teh et al. (2016). The use of RR extrapolation is a natural extension to address the known bias limitations, leveraging a technique from numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990) within a stochastic optimization context. The parallelizability of the approach, stemming from the independent chain generation, is a valuable attribute for large-scale applications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel idea, offering a potentially effective way to reduce bias without significantly increasing computational cost.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds for bias and MSE.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD, showing improved accuracy and convergence rates compared to SGLD.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel nature of the algorithm is a significant advantage for scaling to large datasets.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The core idea is clearly explained and the connections to existing literature are well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Comparison to Higher-Order Integrators:** While the authors claim SGRRLD achieves accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators, they only compare it to SGLD and SGHMC. A direct comparison to methods *using* higher-order integrators (like those discussed in Chen et al., 2015) would strengthen the claim.
*   **Step Size Consistency:** The paper mentions a potential issue with step size selection without fully resolving it. Further clarification regarding optimal step size strategies would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithm-Equation Discrepancy:** A minor inconsistency exists between Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material and Equation (5) in the main text, requiring careful review.
*   **Limited Discussion of Variance Control:** While the paper briefly mentions variance control through correlated Brownian increments, a deeper discussion of the impact of the correlation parameter (Σ) on performance would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to addressing a known limitation of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The parallelizable nature of the algorithm makes it well-suited for large-scale applications.
*   The work advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a direct comparison to higher-order integrators weakens the claim of achieving comparable accuracy.
*   The minor inconsistencies (step size, algorithm/equation) suggest a need for more careful attention to detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a promising new algorithm for SG-MCMC. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the empirical results are encouraging. While some minor issues remain, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a direct comparison to higher-order integrator methods and clarification of the step size discussion and discrepancy between the algorithm and equations.
