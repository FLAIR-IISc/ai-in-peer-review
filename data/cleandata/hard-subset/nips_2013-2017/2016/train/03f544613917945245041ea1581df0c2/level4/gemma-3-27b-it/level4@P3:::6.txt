This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), leveraging the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to reduce bias and improve convergence rates. The core idea involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with differing step sizes and correlated Brownian motion increments. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for consistency, asymptotic behavior, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. Experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates improved performance compared to standard SGLD and, in one experiment, SGHMC.

The paper builds upon the growing body of work in SG-MCMC, particularly stemming from Welling & Teh [1] and subsequent extensions [4–12]. It appropriately acknowledges the existing literature on improving SG-MCMC convergence through higher-order integrators [10] and the application of RR extrapolation to diffusion processes [23]. The proposed method represents a thoughtful combination of these concepts, aiming to achieve higher-order accuracy without requiring complex higher-order integrators directly. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** While building on existing techniques, the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution, offering a potentially efficient pathway to reduce bias.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis, providing formal guarantees for the algorithm's convergence and error bounds. This is a significant strength, distinguishing it from purely empirical work.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets provide compelling evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness, showcasing improved accuracy compared to SGLD.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The demonstrated performance improvements in a large-scale matrix factorization task highlight the practical potential of SGRRLD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea builds directly on established concepts ([10, 23]), and the extent of the novelty may be considered incremental. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how it *specifically* advances the state-of-the-art beyond these prior works.
*   **Practical Considerations & Fair Comparison:** The potential communication cost associated with maintaining correlated Brownian motion between the two chains is not adequately addressed. This is a critical practical concern for parallel implementations. The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to determine the fairness of the comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD. Specifically, it’s unclear if SGLD was given comparable computational resources. If SGRRLD effectively uses parallel processing to reduce wall-clock time, it’s essential to demonstrate that SGLD couldn’t achieve similar performance with equivalent resources (e.g., by running two independent SGLD chains). A comparison against embarrassingly parallel SGLD would be valuable.
*   **Implementation Details:** The description of the implementation lacks specifics regarding how resources were allocated to each chain in SGRRLD, and how the gradient computation was handled. Greater clarity on this point is needed to understand the observed performance gains.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to improving the convergence of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong justification for the proposed method.
*   The experimental results demonstrate practical benefits on relevant datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and contributes to the growing literature on scalable Bayesian inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The incremental nature of the novelty and the lack of detailed discussion on practical considerations (communication costs) raise concerns.
*   The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for a fair comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   The potential limitations of the parallel implementation are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, but it is not without its weaknesses. The strengths – particularly the theoretical rigor and empirical validation – outweigh the weaknesses. However, addressing the concerns regarding practical considerations and a more thorough experimental comparison would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the communication costs associated with maintaining correlated Brownian motion in the parallel implementation.
*   Clarify the experimental setup to ensure a fair comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD, including details on resource allocation and a comparison against embarrassingly parallel SGLD.
*   Expand the discussion of the novelty of the paper, explicitly highlighting its advancements over existing methods [10, 23].
*   Correct the repeated mention of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" (lines 25 and 70).
