This paper proposes a Richardson–Romberg (RR) scheme for SG-MCMC that improves convergence rates in terms of bias and MSE. The method is simple to implement, requiring only minimal changes to standard SG-MCMC, and is supported by solid theoretical analysis. Overall, the idea is elegant and well-motivated.

My main concern is how the specific convergence rates are actually derived from the stated theorems (as I elaborate in the “fatal flaws” section). The paper claims improved rates, but it is not fully clear how the bounds translate into the concrete rates reported, especially how the exponents and constants depend on the step-size schedule and the RR coefficients. A more explicit derivation or a clear roadmap showing how each term in the theorem maps to the final rates would help.

I also have a practical concern regarding the matrix factorization experiment. On a single-core machine, the proposed method would take roughly three times longer than standard SG-MCMC because it runs two chains and one chain uses twice as many steps. If we account for this, the comparisons in Figure 5 are not directly fair: the SGLD time should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3 to normalize for computational effort, which would narrow the apparent performance gap between SGLD and SGRRLD. Please clarify whether the experiments control for total compute (wall-clock or gradient evaluations), and, if not, provide adjusted plots.

Comments on specific figures and claims:
- Figure 3 (bias plot): The best convergence appears to be achieved by the red curve corresponding to α = 0.2, not α = 1/3 as stated in the text. Is there an error in the labeling or interpretation? Moreover, the “optimal” curve seems quite noisy; the observed fluctuations appear inconsistent with the claimed α = 0.2 rate. Can you explain this behavior (e.g., variance due to finite K, step-size schedule, or estimator instability in the RR combination)?
- Line 256: You state that when the step size is too small, the term 1/(Kγ) dominates. Why does this effect not show up for SGLD under the same settings? Is there a difference in how K or γ is scheduled across methods, or in how gradients are reused, that would suppress this term for SGLD but not for SGRRLD?

Minor comments and requests for clarity:
- Equation (1): Why introduce a different symbol instead of using θ for parameters? Unless there is a compelling reason, please use θ consistently.
- Line 94: RR is not defined at its first appearance. Please define “Richardson–Romberg” and the specific combination used (coefficients, step sizes).
- Line 131: Γ_K is not defined. Please provide its definition and role in the bounds.
- Line 160: You assume a smooth test function f. What smoothness class do you require (e.g., Lipschitz, C^1/C^2 with bounded derivatives, growth conditions)? Please state the precise assumptions used in the theorems.
- Line 249: The caption says Figure 1(b) plots Bias vs. time, not dimension. Should this refer to Figure 1(c) instead? Please verify and correct.
- Notation: Please use boldface for vectors throughout for consistency and readability.

Overall assessment: The paper presents a neat and practically appealing modification to SG-MCMC with seemingly strong theoretical guarantees. However, I would like to see:
- A clearer, step-by-step derivation of the stated convergence rates from the main theorem(s), including how the RR coefficients, step sizes, and discretization/stochastic-gradient error terms combine.
- Fair experimental comparisons that normalize for computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time, number of gradient evaluations, or total iterations), especially for methods that run multiple chains and/or more steps.
- Clarification of the discrepancies and fluctuations observed in Figure 3, and an explanation of why the 1/(Kγ) term does not similarly affect SGLD.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen both the theoretical clarity and the empirical claims.
