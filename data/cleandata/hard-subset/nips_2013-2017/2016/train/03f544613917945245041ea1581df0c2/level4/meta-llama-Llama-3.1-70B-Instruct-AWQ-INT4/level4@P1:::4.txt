This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which improves the convergence rates of existing SG-MCMC methods. The proposed algorithm applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm, which is a popular SG-MCMC method. The RR extrapolation is a numerical sequence acceleration method that requires running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm, including its asymptotic consistency, central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error (MSE) bounds. The results show that SGRRLD achieves higher rates of convergence than SGLD and other existing SG-MCMC methods with first-order integrators.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical analysis. The use of RR extrapolation is a novel approach to improving the convergence rates of SG-MCMC methods, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of its advantages and limitations. The paper also includes several experiments on synthetic and real data, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

However, there are a few concerns and minor comments that need to be addressed. Firstly, the specific convergence rates obtained from the theorem are not clearly explained, and it is not clear how the authors derived these rates. Secondly, the matrix factorization experiment result is not entirely fair, as the proposed method is run on a single core computer, which would take three times longer than standard SGLD. If this is taken into account, the plots in Figure 5 would not be fair, and the time for SGLD should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3. Thirdly, in Figure 3, the bias plot, it seems that the best convergence is achieved by the red curve, which corresponds to α = 0.2, not 1/3 stated in the text. Lastly, there are a few minor comments, including the use of different symbols, undefined terms, and incorrect figure references.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel and effective algorithm for improving the convergence rates of SG-MCMC methods. However, the authors need to address the concerns and minor comments mentioned above to improve the clarity and fairness of the paper.
