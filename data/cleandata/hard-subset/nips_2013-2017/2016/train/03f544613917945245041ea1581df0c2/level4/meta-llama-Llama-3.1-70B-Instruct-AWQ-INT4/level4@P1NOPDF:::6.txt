This paper proposes a novel application of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm. By running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise, the authors demonstrate improved convergence properties. Theoretical guarantees are provided for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence. Experimental results on synthetic Gaussian models validate the bias and mean squared error (MSE) bounds established in the theorems. Furthermore, experiments on large-scale matrix factorization using real data showcase the practical utility of the proposed algorithm compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods, providing a clear and well-structured exposition of the proposed technique and its connections to previous work. However, I have two primary concerns. Firstly, the novelty of the paper may be incremental, as the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been employed for approximating invariant distributions in [23].

Secondly, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparisons may not be entirely fair. The parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm requires the two chains to communicate at each step, incurring a communication cost that is not considered by the authors. It would be beneficial to acknowledge and discuss this aspect. Additionally, the allocation of computing resources for the SGRRLD and SGLD algorithms in the experiments is unclear. For instance, is the single chain of SGLD implemented using the same resources as the two chains of SGRRLD? If not, this may not be a fair comparison from a practical perspective. If yes, then it is unclear how the resources are split between the two chains of SGRRLD in the algorithmic procedure. Given that the primary computational cost of SG-MCMC arises from computing stochastic gradients, it is unclear how using partial resources for computing gradients at each chain in SGRRLD could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to using full resources for SGLD. Further details on the implementation setup would be helpful. An alternative approach would be to run two chains of SGLD in an embarrassingly parallel manner (without communication), and it would be interesting to compare this approach to the proposed method.
