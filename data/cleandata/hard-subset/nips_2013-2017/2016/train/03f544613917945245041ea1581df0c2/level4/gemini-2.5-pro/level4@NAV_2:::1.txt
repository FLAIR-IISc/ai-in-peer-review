This paper proposes applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical sequence acceleration method, to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The work focuses on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), introducing a new algorithm termed SGRRLD. This method involves running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step size schedules and combining their outputs. This approach builds a valuable connection between the large-scale Bayesian inference literature and established work on applying RR extrapolation to the numerical analysis of stochastic differential equations (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and mean squared error (MSE) for their proposed estimator. The theoretical results predict faster convergence rates for SGRRLD compared to SGLD. These findings are supported by experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world data.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by both a thorough theoretical analysis (including asymptotic and non-asymptotic results) and comprehensive experimental validation.
*   **Significance:** The theoretical results are important, demonstrating that SGRRLD can achieve a higher rate of convergence for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This allows a first-order integrator to match the accuracy of more complex higher-order methods. The empirical results are compelling, showing substantial improvements on synthetic problems and consistent, practical gains on a large-scale matrix factorization task.
*   **Originality:** While RR extrapolation is a known technique, its application, and particularly its rigorous theoretical analysis in the context of SG-MCMC, is novel. The paper successfully bridges concepts from numerical SDE analysis and scalable Bayesian computation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the proposed algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical and empirical results are presented in an easy-to-follow manner. The method's straightforward nature and suitability for parallel execution also make it practically appealing.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   The core methodological contribution is a direct, albeit effective, application of a well-established technique from numerical analysis to the SG-MCMC setting. While the theoretical analysis in this new context is a valuable contribution, the conceptual novelty is incremental rather than revolutionary.
*   The performance gains on the large-scale movie recommendation task, while consistent and valuable, are more modest than the dramatic improvements seen in the synthetic experiments.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   In Figure 1(a), it would be helpful to clarify in the caption or text that the plots represent Gaussian densities parameterized by the empirical mean and variance of the posterior samples, rather than a direct empirical histogram or kernel density estimate of the samples. This would prevent any potential misinterpretation.
*   Regarding Figure 2, the U-shaped behavior of the bias and MSE as the step size γ varies is somewhat counter-intuitive. While the paper correctly alludes to the 1/(Kγ) term in the theoretical bounds, a more explicit sentence discussing this trade-off—where for a fixed number of iterations K, very small step sizes increase the error—would enhance the clarity of the experimental analysis for the reader.
