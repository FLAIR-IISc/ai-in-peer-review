- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining outputs to cancel leading-order bias.
- Theoretical contributions: proves asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for SG-MCMC with RR extrapolation.
- Improved convergence rate: shows MSE rate O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for SGRRLD versus SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), matching higher-order integrator accuracy using only first-order schemes.
- Empirical validation: strong synthetic Gaussian experiments that clearly illustrate bias and MSE reduction.
- Real-data experiments: applied to MovieLens matrix factorization but showed only modest RMSE improvements.
- Technical quality: analysis is rigorous and a meaningful extension of RR extrapolation results to stochastic-gradient settings.
- Clarity: paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides helpful proof sketches.
- Originality: first formal analysis combining RR extrapolation with stochastic gradient noise in SG-MCMC; simple and parallelizable scheme.
- Potential significance: promising practical method (drop-in, parallelizable) but real-world impact not yet convincingly demonstrated.
- Main experimental concern: unfair comparison—need baselines that match computational budget when using two cores.
- Suggested baseline comparisons: two independent parallel SGLD chains averaged, and SGLD with step size γ/2 (or two parallel SGLD chains both with γ/2).
- Practical relevance concern: small RMSE gains on MovieLens raise doubt that asymptotic benefits translate to finite-time real-world gains; more diverse large-scale experiments needed.
- Theoretical assumptions: request clarification on scope of assumptions for U, f, and gradient noise, especially regarding non-convex or multi-modal posteriors.
- Intuition requested: explanation of how RR error cancellation remains effective despite independent mini-batch noise and potential decorrelation between chains.
- Recommendation: novel and theoretically strong but acceptance contingent on stronger, fairer experimental validation; reviewer would raise score if authors add suggested baselines and broader experiments.
