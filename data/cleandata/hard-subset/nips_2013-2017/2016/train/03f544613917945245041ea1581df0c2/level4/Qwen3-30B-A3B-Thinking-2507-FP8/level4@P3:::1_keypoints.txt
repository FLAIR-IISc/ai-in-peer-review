- Proposes SGRRLD, which applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to SGLD to reduce bias while keeping variance reasonable.  
- Extends RRE for SDEs to the stochastic-gradient MCMC setting, bridging numerical SDE analysis and scalable MCMC.  
- Theoretical contribution 1: central limit theorem showing asymptotic normality and variance minimized with perfectly correlated Brownian increments.  
- Theoretical contribution 2: non-asymptotic bounds showing SGRRLD achieves MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs SGLD’s O(K⁻²/³).  
- Theoretical contribution 3: empirical validation supports theoretical claims across synthetic and real-world datasets.  
- Provides clear implementation details, including how to correlate Brownian increments to control variance.  
- Method is computationally attractive, parallelizable, and achieves improved convergence without higher-order integrators.  
- Generality: applicable to any SG-MCMC algorithm that meets certain conditions, increasing practical usefulness.  
- Empirical results show meaningful improvements in accuracy and efficiency on large-scale matrix factorization tasks.  
- Clarity and organization are excellent; math notation and explanations are accessible to readers familiar with MCMC and stochastic calculus.  
- Supplementary material contains sufficient detail for reproduction.  
- Originality: technically nontrivial execution of a conceptually straightforward idea, offering comparable accuracy to higher-order integrators at lower cost.  
- Significance: a valuable incremental advance with immediate practical application in scalable Bayesian inference.  
- Minor weaknesses: Figure 1(a) mislabels a Gaussian approximation as the empirical posterior and should be clarified.  
- Minor weaknesses: Figure 2(a) shows non-monotonic bias vs step size—needs better explanation for counterintuitive behavior.  
- Minor weaknesses: recursive application of RRE is mentioned but not explored experimentally.  
- Recommendation: suitable for NIPS acceptance with minor revisions addressing the above issues.
