This paper proposes a technique to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, which have gained significant attention in the research community for performing Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets. Despite their popularity, SG-MCMC methods are asymptotically biased, meaning they do not converge exactly to the posterior distribution. The authors address this limitation by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a technique well-established in numerical analysis but less familiar in the machine learning and SG-MCMC community.

The core idea of the proposed method is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with the second chain having half the step size of the first chain and being run for twice the number of steps. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated, with the specific correlation structure defined as αk = (β2k-1 + β2k) / √2, where αk and βk are the injected Gaussian noise at time step k in chains 1 and 2, respectively. To compute the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution of the chains, the estimate computed using samples from chain 1 is subtracted from the estimate computed using samples from chain 2. This resulting estimate is shown to have a lower bias of O(γ^2) compared to O(γ) obtained from either chain alone, where γ is the step size. The authors also prove a similar reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates.

The simplicity and generality of the proposed method are significant advantages. It is easy to understand and implement, requiring minimal changes to the base SG-MCMC algorithm. This broad applicability makes the method potentially highly impactful within the SG-MCMC community.

While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-known in numerical analysis, its application in the machine learning and SG-MCMC community is novel. The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear, making it accessible to a wide audience.

Several questions and comments arise from the paper:

1. Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods, or are there specific conditions that need to be satisfied beyond the bound in section 2.2: πγ(f) = π(f) + C * γ + O(γ^2)?

2. Is it possible to apply this method to other non-stochastic gradient but approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings tests, or Distributed SGLD?

3. Given that one chain must run for twice as long as the other, resulting in wasted computation, could using three chains (one with a step size of γ and two with step sizes of γ/2 each, all run for the same number of steps) be an alternative? Would this approach increase the variance of the estimate?

4. Is there an extension of this method to more than two step sizes?

5. The correlation between the injected noise in the chains is crucial, but the effect of correlation between mini-batches is not entirely clear. Is the variance minimized when using the same batch for the kth iteration of chain 1 and the 2k-1th and 2kth iterations of chain 2, or is the effect of this correlation negligible?

6. The matrix factorization experiment (figure 5) should ideally be run until convergence and compared to SGD. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's performance, especially in scenarios where being Bayesian is advantageous.

7. Including an algorithm box with pseudo-code early in the paper would enhance its accessibility to a broader audience, particularly practitioners who may not delve into the more theoretical aspects.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the SG-MCMC community by providing a simple yet effective method to reduce bias. Addressing the questions and comments raised would further strengthen the paper's impact and applicability.
