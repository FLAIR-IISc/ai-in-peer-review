This paper applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce the bias arising from noisy gradient estimates. The resulting algorithm, SGRRLD, is shown to possess an asymptotically lower bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) than SGLD. The core idea builds upon existing literature where RR extrapolation has been used to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs. The authors successfully adapt this technique to the SG-MCMC setting, proving that under certain conditions, SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD for a given step size. This improvement comes at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is described clearly and appears straightforward to implement.

Experiments on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model clearly demonstrate the proposed algorithm's advantage over SGLD in reducing bias for that specific problem. However, the results on the MovieLens dataset are less convincing, showing only a slight and potentially inconclusive improvement in test RMSE. While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more extensive experiments on real-world datasets to demonstrate the practical benefits of this modification.

While I have not checked the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 in detail, I have some questions regarding the theoretical underpinnings:
1.  What specific conditions must the potential function U satisfy for the theorems to hold? For instance, is the analysis applicable to multi-modal potentials?
2.  Intuitively, it seems that uncorrelated gradient noise would cause the two parallel chains to decorrelate quickly, even with correlated driving noise. Could the authors provide more intuition on how combining these chains still achieves a reduction in the error order? Was the correlation between the two chains measured empirically in the experiments?

My primary concern lies with the experimental design, which may not constitute a fair comparison. The authors justify the computational cost of SGRRLD by noting that its two chains can be run in parallel, making its runtime comparable to a single SGLD chain. However, a more appropriate baseline would be to run two independent SGLD chains in parallel for the same wall-clock time and average their estimates. It is unclear if SGRRLD would still outperform this stronger, parallelized SGLD baseline.

Furthermore, the comparison in the real-data experiment is between SGRRLD (using step sizes γ and γ/2) and SGLD (using step size γ). A complete comparison should also include SGLD with the smaller step size γ/2, which would inherently have a lower bias. For a truly fair comparison of computational trade-offs, the authors should compare SGRRLD against an ensemble of two parallel SGLD chains, both running with step size γ/2. Additional real-data experiments with these improved baselines are needed to validate the claims of the paper.
