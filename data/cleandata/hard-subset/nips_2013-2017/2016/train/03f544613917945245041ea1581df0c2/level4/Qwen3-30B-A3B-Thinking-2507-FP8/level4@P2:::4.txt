

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel approach to improve the convergence properties of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The authors propose applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to standard SG-MCMC algorithms, specifically SGLD, by running two parallel chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs. This approach aims to reduce the bias while maintaining reasonable variance, theoretically achieving bias O(γ²) and MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(K⁻²/³) rates. The work builds upon previous SG-MCMC research like Welling and Teh (2011), SGLD, and recent advances in higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015), but offers a simpler alternative to complex higher-order methods.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions. The authors provide formal analysis showing SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves improved non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The theoretical results are well-supported with careful analysis of the conditions under which the rates hold. The approach is also practical, requiring minimal implementation changes to existing SG-MCMC frameworks and maintaining parallelizability, which is crucial for large-scale applications.

The experimental section effectively validates the theoretical claims. The synthetic Gaussian model experiments demonstrate clear improvements in bias and MSE across multiple dimensions, with SGRRLD reducing variance estimation error from ~10⁻² (SGLD) to ~10⁻⁴. The matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets show SGRRLD achieving better accuracy than SGLD in comparable computation time. However, the practical implementation note about parallel execution needs clarification: the authors note SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel, but the experimental setup on single-core machines would require scaling time by 2/3 for fair comparison, which isn't accounted for in Figure 5.

Several specific concerns require clarification. First, the theoretical analysis claims optimal convergence rate for MSE is O(K⁻⁴/⁵) with α=1/5, but Figure 3 shows the best convergence occurs with α=0.2 (not 1/3 as stated in the text), and the optimal curve appears noisy, suggesting possible implementation issues or insufficient runs. Second, the paper states that for small step sizes the term 1/(Kγ) dominates, yet this doesn't seem to affect SGLD's performance as described. This discrepancy needs explanation. Third, the paper uses θ in the abstract but switches to ϑ in the introduction (line 1), which is confusing. The term "RR" is not defined on first use (line 94), and Γ_K is introduced without definition (line 131). The smoothness requirements for f in Theorem 1 (line 160) should be specified. Finally, Figure 1(b) is labeled as "Bias vs. dimension" but plots bias vs. time, suggesting it should be Figure 1(c).

The paper excels in originality, presenting a clever application of numerical analysis to SG-MCMC that avoids the complexity of higher-order integrators while achieving comparable accuracy. The method is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms, which significantly enhances its potential impact. The significance is clear: the approach offers a practical, efficient improvement for large-scale Bayesian inference that can be easily integrated into existing frameworks.

Strengths:
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified
- The approach is simple to implement with minimal changes to existing code
- The parallel nature of the method aligns well with modern computational architectures
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims across multiple domains

Weaknesses:
- The connection between the theoretical α=1/3 (for bias) and observed α=0.2 (for MSE) needs clarification
- The experimental comparison in Figure 5 lacks fair time accounting for the two-chain implementation
- Some notation inconsistencies and undefined terms weaken clarity
- The explanation for why 1/(Kγ) doesn't dominate SGLD as it does for SGRRLD is insufficient

Pro and Con for Acceptance:
PRO: Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC with strong theoretical justification, simple implementation, and clear experimental validation. The work significantly advances the field by offering a practical alternative to complex higher-order methods.

CON: The experimental comparison needs to account for the two-chain implementation in a fair way, and several theoretical claims require more precise explanation (particularly regarding the convergence rates and the 1/(Kγ) term). The paper would benefit from addressing these issues before publication.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The core idea is sound, the theoretical analysis is compelling, and the experimental validation is generally convincing. With minor revisions to clarify the theoretical claims and experimental setup, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
