

This paper applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias from gradient estimation noise, resulting in the SGRRLD algorithm. SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to SGLD. Experiments on a synthetic linear Gaussian model demonstrate a clear advantage of SGRRLD in bias reduction. On MovieLens data, SGRRLD shows a slight improvement in test RMSE, though these results are questionable. While RR extrapolation has previously improved Monte Carlo convergence rates for SDEs, this paper extends the technique to SG-MCMC algorithms. The authors prove that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD attains a faster convergence rate than SGLD with the same step size, albeit at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear, and implementation is straightforward.  

The synthetic experiments effectively illustrate SGRRLD's bias reduction. However, more extensive real-data experiments would strengthen the claim that this simple SGLD modification significantly reduces bias. Regarding the theoretical contributions, I have not verified the proof details of Theorems 1 and 2 but have two concerns: (1) What specific conditions must the potential function $U$ satisfy to satisfy both theorems? Can $U$ be multi-modal? (2) Intuitively, if gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains should decorrelate rapidly even with correlated Gaussian noises. How precisely does combining the two chains reduce the error order?  

Additionally, the experimental design raises concerns. The authors claim computation time is comparable to SGLD with parallelization. However, SGLD could also be run with two parallel chains within the same computational budget. Comparing SGRRLD (step size $\gamma$) to two parallel SGLD chains (step size $\gamma$) would provide a fairer assessment of the improvement. Furthermore, in the MovieLens experiment, SGRRLD ($\gamma$) is compared to SGLD ($\gamma$), but a more appropriate baseline would be SGLD with step size $\gamma/2$ (which inherently has lower bias) run with two parallel chains. More real-data experiments, including this comparison, are necessary to validate the method's practical benefits.
