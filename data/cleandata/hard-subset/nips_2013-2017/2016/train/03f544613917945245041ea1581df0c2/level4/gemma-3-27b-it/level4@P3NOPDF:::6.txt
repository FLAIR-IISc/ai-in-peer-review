This paper proposes an acceleration technique for Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms based on Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method typically used with higher-order numerical integrators. The approach requires running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated injected Gaussian noise. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence, and support these with experiments. Specifically, they demonstrate the validity of the derived bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds on a synthetic Gaussian model, and showcase practical improvements over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) on a large-scale matrix factorization task with real-world data. Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the SGMCMC literature, effectively contextualizing the proposed method within existing work.

I have two primary concerns. First, the novelty of the work may be incremental. Prior research, such as [10], has analyzed the impact of the numerical integrator on SG-MCMC convergence, and RR extrapolation has previously been employed for approximating invariant distributions [23]. While this paper provides a focused application and rigorous analysis of RR extrapolation within the SGMCMC framework, a more thorough discussion of how this work extends or significantly departs from these prior contributions would strengthen the paper.

Second, the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm, which the authors term SGRRLD, could be limited by communication costs, and the experimental comparison with SGLD may not be entirely fair. The parallel implementation of SGRRLD and the requirement for correlated Brownian motion necessitate communication between the two chains at each iteration. The paper does not address the communication overhead associated with this process, implicitly assuming it is negligible. This assumption should be explicitly stated and justified. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to evaluate the fairness of the comparison. Specifically, it is unclear how computational resources were allocated to SGRRLD and SGLD. If a single SGLD chain was implemented using the same resources allocated to the two SGRRLD chains, the comparison may be biased in favor of SGRRLD. Conversely, if the resources were split between the two SGRRLD chains, the rationale for this split and its potential impact on gradient computation efficiency should be explained. The computational bottleneck in SG-MCMC is typically gradient evaluation; it is not immediately apparent how dividing resources between two SGRRLD chains for gradient computation could lead to a reduction in wall-clock time compared to dedicating full resources to a single SGLD chain. More detailed information on the implementation setup, including resource allocation and communication infrastructure, is crucial.  An alternative and potentially insightful comparison would be to benchmark SGRRLD against two independently running SGLD chains (without communication), offering a baseline for evaluating the benefits of the correlated noise and RR extrapolation.
