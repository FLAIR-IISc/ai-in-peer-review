Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC to reduce discretization bias while keeping variance controlled. The method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and combines their ergodic averages in the RR fashion. To control variance, the Brownian increments are coupled (ideally perfectly correlated as in Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2). The authors establish consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds for the RR estimator. They show that, for properly chosen step sizes, the MSE decays as O(K−4/5), improving over the O(K−2/3) rate known for first-order SG-MCMC methods. They argue the approach matches the accuracy of higher-order integrators while retaining the simplicity of first-order schemes.

The work builds on: SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011), SG-MCMC families and high-order integrators (e.g., Chen, Ding, Carin, NIPS 2015), RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire, Pagès & Panloup), and multilevel ideas. The paper also illustrates an RR version of SGHMC and demonstrates empirical gains on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization.

Strengths:
- Simple, general idea: RR extrapolation is an established variance/bias acceleration tool; applying it to SG-MCMC is natural yet, to my knowledge, novel.
- Parallel-friendly: the two chains can be run independently and combined, which is attractive in modern compute settings.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds; clear identification of optimal coupling of Brownian increments; explicit rate comparisons against SGLD.
- Empirical support: synthetic experiments confirm predicted scaling of bias/MSE with γ and K; large-scale matrix factorization shows improved RMSE at matched wall-clock under parallel execution; SGHMC extension compares favorably to a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s).

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- Step-size schedules and “optimal” rates: The paper interleaves results for constant γ (optimized w.r.t. K) and decreasing γk = γ1 k−α. As stated, the CLT-based rate discussion favors α = 1/5, yielding O(K−2/5), whereas the non-asymptotic MSE bound under constant step size (optimized over γ) yields O(K−4/5). This is confusing for readers. Please:
  - Clearly separate the regimes (constant vs decreasing γ) and define precisely which rate (bias, RMSE, or MSE) each result refers to.
  - State, in the main text, the recommended step-size policy under a fixed computational budget (e.g., choose constant γ ∝ K−1/5 for MSE minimization) and reconcile the O(K−2/5) vs O(K−4/5) claims.
- Algorithmic correctness and consistency: Equation (5) appears to evaluate the gradient at θ(γ/2)2k+1 on the right-hand side of the first update, which is likely a typo—the gradient should be evaluated at the current state θ(γ/2)2k. The supplement’s Algorithm 1 is reported to be inconsistent with Eq. (5). Please fix these discrepancies and ensure the correlated Gaussian increments (Eq. (7)) are implemented exactly.
- Fairness of experimental comparisons: Wall-clock comparisons assume access to two cores to run the two chains in parallel. On a single core (or fixed total gradient evaluations), SGRRLD doubles the per-iteration cost. To strengthen the case:
  - Add comparisons under a fixed total number of stochastic gradients (or single-core wall-clock) to isolate algorithmic gains from parallelism.
  - Report the effect of coupling: compare correlated vs independent Brownian increments to demonstrate the variance benefit predicted by the theory.
  - For SGLD, include a comparison to the higher-order SGLD integrators from Chen et al. (NIPS 2015), mirroring the SGHMC-s comparison.
- Assumptions: Many results are stated under “appropriate conditions.” It would greatly help to list the key assumptions in the main text (e.g., smoothness/growth of U, dissipativity/ergodicity, unbiasedness and moment bounds for ∇Ũk) and indicate where each is used.
- Scope of “variance O(γ4)”: Clarify whether this refers to the asymptotic variance of the bias-corrected ergodic average at stationarity for fixed γ, or a term within the MSE decomposition; define the measure of variance precisely.
- Practical guidance: Because performance depends on batch size and correlation structure, provide guidance on minibatch coupling. Using the same minibatch indices across the two chains (common random minibatching) may further reduce variance; discuss feasibility and impact.
- Minor clarity/typos: e.g., “variace” → “variance”; “twice more time” → “twice as many steps”; “Futher” → “Further”; ensure consistent notation for γ, η, and indexation.

Quality:
The technical development appears careful and aligned with the RR literature for SDEs, extended to the stochastic-gradient setting. The results are plausible and largely supported empirically. However, the step-size discussion and minor algorithmic inconsistencies should be corrected to avoid confusion.

Clarity:
Generally well written, but dense. The paper would benefit from consolidating assumptions in the main text, cleaning the update equations, and clearly demarcating the different regimes (constant vs decreasing step size). Including a concise pseudocode of SGRRLD in the main paper would help.

Originality:
Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with variance control via correlated Brownian increments is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The extension to SGHMC and the multilevel outlook are natural and valuable.

Significance:
The method is simple, general, and practical, with clear benefits when parallel resources are available. The improved rates and empirical gains suggest it could be adopted broadly across SG-MCMC variants.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and simple idea with strong theoretical grounding.
- Clear improvement in finite-time bias/MSE rates over first-order SG-MCMC.
- Parallelizable design; tangible empirical gains on large-scale tasks.
- General framework applicable beyond SGLD (demonstrated on SGHMC).

Cons for acceptance:
- Inconsistencies/typos in algorithmic updates and step-size statements.
- Experimental fairness hinges on parallel resources; missing comparisons under fixed gradient budgets.
- Missing direct comparison to higher-order SGLD integrators.
- Assumptions not sufficiently explicit in the main text.

Questions/suggestions for the authors:
1. Please fix Eq. (5) and align Algorithm 1 with the intended updates and coupling in Eq. (7).
2. Provide a consolidated, explicit set of assumptions for the main theorems in the paper body.
3. Clarify the “variance O(γ4)” statement and unify the rate discussion across constant and decreasing step sizes, with concrete recommendations.
4. Add ablations on coupling (correlated vs independent Gaussians; same vs different minibatches).
5. Include comparisons to high-order SGLD integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) and results under fixed total gradient evaluations.
