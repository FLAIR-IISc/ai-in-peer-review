This paper proposes applying a sequence-acceleration idea (a higher-order numerical integrator), namely Richardson–Romberg extrapolation, to enhance the convergence of SG-MCMC. The approach runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and injected, correlated Gaussian noise. The authors present both asymptotic and finite-time convergence results. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model corroborate the bias and MSE bounds in the theorems, and large-scale matrix factorization on real data illustrates practical gains over standard SGLD. Overall, the technique and presentation are solid. I view this as a legitimate contribution to SGMCMC and appreciate how it is positioned relative to prior work.

I have two main concerns: (1) The novelty may be incremental, since the impact of numerical integrators on SGMCMC has been analyzed in [10], and RR extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. (2) The practical utility may be limited, and the experimental comparisons may not be fully fair. The method is a parallel SG-MCMC implementation, and the use of correlated Brownian motion entails per-step communication between the two chains. The communication cost appears to be ignored; this should be discussed. It is also unclear how compute resources were allocated between SGRRLD and SGLD in the experiments. For instance, does a single SGLD chain use the same total resources as the two SGRRLD chains? If not, the comparison may be unfair in practice. If yes, how are the resources split between the two SGRRLD chains in the algorithm? Since the dominant cost in SG-MCMC is typically gradient computation, it is not obvious how allocating partial resources per SGRRLD chain could lead to lower wall-clock time than dedicating full resources to SGLD. Please add implementation details. As an alternative baseline, it would also be informative to compare against running two independent SGLD chains in embarrassingly parallel fashion (without communication).
