- Contribution: Applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to stochastic gradient MCMC, proposing a two-chain extrapolated estimator (SGRRLD) for SGLD.
- Theoretical results: Proves asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and gives non-asymptotic bounds for the extrapolated estimator.
- Improvement conditions: Derives conditions under which SGRRLD reduces bias and improves over vanilla SGLD.
- Practical method: Uses correlated Gaussian noise across two chains, is easy to implement, and parallelizes naturally.
- Empirical evidence: Shows substantial gains on synthetic problems and modest but consistent improvements on large-scale matrix factorization.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on SGLD (Welling & Teh) and Talay–Tubaro/RR literature for SDE weak error expansions; RR not previously applied to SG-MCMC.
- Conceptual strength: Simple, principled idea that reduces bias without large variance increase via correlated noise.
- Theoretical strength: Solid analysis aligning with known RR and SG-MCMC properties.
- Practical strength: First-order integrator usage and low engineering overhead make the approach attractive for practitioners.
- Empirical strength: Results match theory and are computationally realistic.
- Scope weakness: Method is a straightforward application of classical RR extrapolation, so methodological novelty is incremental.
- Impact weakness: Improvements on large-scale tasks are modest, so practical benefit depends on tolerance and compute budgets.
- Quality: Paper appears technically sound and well-supported by experiments.
- Clarity: Very clearly written and well organized; motivates design choices effectively.
- Originality: Novel combination of RR extrapolation with SG-MCMC and variance control via correlation, though conceptually natural.
- Significance: Likely useful to researchers and practitioners seeking practical accuracy gains with little extra engineering cost.
- Pros for acceptance: Clean idea, strong theory (consistency, CLT, bounds), realistic empirical validation, and bridges communities.
- Cons for acceptance: Incremental methodological novelty and limited dramatic gains on large-scale problems.
- Minor comments: Clarify caption for Fig.1(a) that plotted “posterior” is Gaussian with empirical mean/variance; explain non-monotone bias behavior in Fig.2(a) referencing finite-time trade-offs.
- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper that makes a useful, adoptable contribution; recommendation to accept.
