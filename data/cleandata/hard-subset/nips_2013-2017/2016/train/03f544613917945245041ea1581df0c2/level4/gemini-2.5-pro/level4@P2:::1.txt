This paper proposes a method to reduce the bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, with a specific focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a classical technique from numerical analysis for accelerating sequence convergence. The proposed algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. This approach builds upon prior work applying RRE to the numerical solution of stochastic differential equations (SDEs) [21, 23] and extends it to the "stochastic gradient" setting prevalent in large-scale Bayesian inference.

The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. They establish its asymptotic consistency and prove a central limit theorem. Furthermore, they derive non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates—O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE—compared to the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. This theoretical improvement is then validated through a series of experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem for movie recommendation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides an important and elegant bridge between the numerical SDE literature and the large-scale machine learning (SG-MCMC) community. Applying a well-established numerical integration tool like RRE to modern scalable MCMC methods is a novel and impactful contribution. The resulting algorithm is simple to implement, computationally convenient (especially on parallel architectures), and demonstrably effective.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and thorough. The authors provide rigorous theoretical results, including asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. These proofs clearly establish the conditions under which SGRRLD outperforms SGLD, solidifying the paper's claims and moving beyond mere heuristics.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the proposed method is explained precisely, and the theoretical results are presented coherently. The connection to prior work is also clearly articulated.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The experiments on the synthetic Gaussian model effectively isolate and validate the theoretical claims regarding bias and MSE reduction. The application to large-scale matrix factorization demonstrates that the method provides modest but consistent and valuable performance improvements on a practical, real-world problem, achieving the same accuracy as SGLD in roughly half the wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for further improvement:

1.  **Clarity in Figures:** The presentation of Figure 1(a) could be slightly misleading. The plot shows a Gaussian density parameterized by the empirical posterior mean and variance, not the empirical posterior density itself (e.g., from a kernel density estimate). Clarifying this in the caption or text would prevent potential misinterpretation.

2.  **Explanation of Counter-Intuitive Results:** In Figure 2, the bias and MSE are shown to increase for very small step sizes γ. While this is perfectly consistent with the theoretical bounds presented in the paper (where the `1/(Kγ)` term dominates for a fixed number of iterations K), this behavior can be counter-intuitive. A brief sentence explicitly explaining this trade-off in the main text would be helpful for the reader.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. It introduces a novel algorithm by cleverly combining ideas from two distinct fields, provides strong theoretical guarantees for its performance, and demonstrates its practical utility through compelling experiments. The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners due to its simplicity and effectiveness. The paper is clear, technically sound, and significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
