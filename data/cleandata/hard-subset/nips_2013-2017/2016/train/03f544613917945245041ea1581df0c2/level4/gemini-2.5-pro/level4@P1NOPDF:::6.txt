This paper proposes using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of SG-MCMC algorithms. The proposed method, SGRRLD, runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injections. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model are used to verify the theoretical bounds on bias and MSE, while large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that makes a valid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work with respect to prior art. However, I have two main concerns regarding the novelty and the experimental evaluation.

**Main Concerns:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The novelty of the paper may be somewhat incremental. The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation itself has been applied to the approximation of invariant distributions in [23]. The contribution seems to be the specific application of this known technique to the SG-MCMC setting, and while valuable, its novelty should be framed accordingly.

2.  **Practicality and Fairness of Experiments:** The practical utility of the algorithm may be limited by certain design choices, and the experimental comparisons could be made more rigorous.
    *   **Communication Cost:** The proposed method requires running two parallel chains that must communicate at every step due to the correlated Brownian motion. The authors do not appear to account for this communication cost, which could be significant in practice. This potential overhead should be discussed.
    *   **Resource Allocation:** The allocation of computational resources in the experiments is unclear. For a fair comparison, was the single SGLD chain given the same total computational budget (e.g., cores, GPU time) as the two SGRRLD chains combined? If not, the comparison of wall-clock time may not be fair. If so, it would be helpful to understand how the resources were divided between the two SGRRLD chains.
    *   **Wall-Clock Time:** Given that the computational cost of SG-MCMC is dominated by stochastic gradient calculations, it is not immediately obvious how running two chains—each presumably with a smaller portion of the total computational budget for gradients—can outperform a single SGLD chain using the full budget in terms of wall-clock time. More details on the implementation setup are needed to clarify this point.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing: running two independent SGLD chains in an "embarrassingly parallel" fashion (i.e., without communication) and averaging their results. A comparison against this strong and simple alternative would significantly strengthen the experimental section.

**Minor Comment:**

*   The full name "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is spelled out on both line 25 and line 70; the acronym could be used the second time.
