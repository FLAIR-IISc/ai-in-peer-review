

The paper introduces a simple yet effective technique to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is straightforward: run two parallel SG-MCMC chains with step sizes $\gamma$ and $\gamma/2$, where the second chain executes twice as many steps. Crucially, the Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated such that the noise at time $k$ in Chain 1 ($\alpha_k$) is derived from the noise at times $2k-1$ and $2k$ in Chain 2 ($\beta_{2k-1}, \beta_{2k}$) via $\alpha_k = (\beta_{2k-1} + \beta_{2k})/\sqrt{2}$. The posterior expectation estimate is then formed by subtracting the Chain 1 estimate from the Chain 2 estimate, yielding a bias of $O(\gamma^2)$ compared to $O(\gamma)$ for either chain alone, with a corresponding reduction in mean squared error (MSE). The method requires no modifications to the base SG-MCMC algorithm, is applicable to a broad class of SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGLD, SGHMC), and is easy to implement—a significant practical advantage. The novelty lies in the adaptation of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established numerical technique, to the SG-MCMC context, where it remains largely unexplored. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides rigorous theoretical justification for the bias and MSE reductions.

The work is highly impactful for the SG-MCMC community due to its simplicity, generality, and minimal computational overhead. The authors’ theoretical contribution is strengthened by a clean exposition and empirical validation across multiple benchmarks. However, several points warrant clarification and further investigation:

1.  **Generality of Application**: The paper assumes the SG-MCMC algorithm satisfies $\pi_\gamma(f) = \pi(f) + C\gamma + O(\gamma^2)$ (Section 2.2). Is this condition sufficient for the extrapolation to reduce bias? Are there SG-MCMC variants (e.g., those with preconditioning or adaptive step sizes) that violate this bound and thus render the method inapplicable? A discussion of necessary and sufficient conditions would strengthen the claim of broad applicability.

2.  **Extension to Other MCMC Schemes**: The technique appears to rely on the specific bias structure of SG-MCMC. Could it be adapted to non-stochastic-gradient MCMC methods, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings or distributed SGLD? For instance, in distributed settings, could the extrapolation be applied across nodes? Clarifying the scope of applicability is important for assessing the method’s broader relevance.

3.  **Computational Efficiency**: The current implementation runs Chain 2 for twice as many steps as Chain 1, which may seem inefficient. Instead, could three chains be used: one with step size $\gamma$ (run for $N$ steps), and two with step size $\gamma/2$ (each run for $N$ steps)? This would eliminate idle time but likely increases variance. An analysis of the trade-off between computational cost and estimator variance would be valuable.

4.  **Higher-Order Extrapolation**: The paper focuses on two step sizes. Is the method extendable to more than two step sizes (e.g., using three chains with $\gamma$, $\gamma/2$, $\gamma/4$) to achieve higher-order bias reduction? If so, what are the computational and variance implications?

5.  **Noise Correlation Mechanism**: The correlation between noise terms in the two chains is critical. The paper specifies $\alpha_k = (\beta_{2k-1} + \beta_{2k})/\sqrt{2}$, but it is unclear whether using *identical* mini-batches for Chain 1’s step $k$ and Chain 2’s steps $2k-1$ and $2k$ is necessary or optimal. Does the variance depend on the correlation structure between mini-batches, or is it negligible? A brief discussion of this practical implementation detail would aid reproducibility.

6.  **Empirical Validation**: The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) compares SGRRLD (the proposed method) and SGLD against a Bayesian baseline. To better demonstrate the *practical advantage* of Bayesian inference, the authors should: (a) run experiments until convergence (not just 10k steps), and (b) include SGD as a baseline. Currently, the comparison to SGLD alone does not establish that Bayesian methods outperform frequentist approaches—a key motivation for adopting SG-MCMC. Including SGD would contextualize the error reduction and strengthen the argument for Bayesian inference in practice.

7.  **Algorithm Accessibility**: While the method is simple, an algorithm box in the main text (e.g., in Section 3) would significantly improve accessibility for practitioners. A concise pseudocode listing the key steps (including noise correlation) would enable immediate implementation without requiring readers to parse the theoretical details.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful limitation of SG-MCMC with minimal overhead. The core idea is elegant, the theory is sound, and the empirical results support the claims. Addressing the above points—particularly clarifying the scope of applicability, enhancing the empirical validation, and improving accessibility—would further elevate the paper. The work is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
