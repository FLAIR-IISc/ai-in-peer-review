

This paper proposes leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation—a higher-order numerical integrator—to accelerate convergence in stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The approach runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection, establishing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models validate the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates practical efficacy over standard SGLD. The technique is well-motivated, the presentation is clear, and the context with prior work is appropriately situated, making this a solid contribution to SG-MCMC.

However, two significant concerns require clarification before acceptance.  

**First, the novelty appears incremental.** The analysis of numerical integrators for SG-MCMC is covered in [10], and RR extrapolation for approximating invariant distributions is established in [23]. While this work applies RR to SG-MCMC, the core technique is not new. The paper should explicitly position its contribution relative to these prior works—e.g., by clarifying how the *parallel-chain implementation* with correlated noise and *non-asymptotic guarantees* advance beyond [23]’s theoretical framework or [10]’s integrator analysis. Without this, the novelty claim is weak.  

**Second, the practical viability of the method is questionable due to unaddressed communication costs and unclear experimental setup.** The approach requires two chains to communicate at every step to maintain correlated noise, yet the paper assumes zero communication overhead. This is unrealistic for distributed systems. The authors must:  
1. Quantify the communication cost (e.g., bandwidth, latency) and discuss its impact on wall-clock time.  
2. Clarify the resource allocation for experiments. Specifically:  
   - Does SGRRLD use *double* the compute resources of SGLD (e.g., two GPUs)? If so, the comparison is unfair for practical deployment where resources are constrained.  
   - If resources are split (e.g., each SGRRLD chain uses half the capacity of a single SGLD chain), the wall-clock time for SGRRLD should be comparable to SGLD *only if* the gradient computation cost scales linearly with chain count. Given that stochastic gradient evaluation dominates computation, it is unclear how SGRRLD achieves faster wall-clock time with *partial* resource allocation.  
   - The paper should explicitly compare against an embarrassingly parallel baseline: two independent SGLD chains (no communication), which would use identical resources to SGRRLD but without correlation. This would isolate the benefit of RR extrapolation from communication overhead.  

The current experimental setup lacks these details, making it difficult to assess whether SGRRLD offers a practical advantage. The authors should provide a clear implementation table specifying:  
- Hardware (e.g., GPU count, memory)  
- Resource allocation per chain  
- Wall-clock time per iteration  
- Communication protocol and cost  

**Minor issues:**  
- "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is redundantly defined in lines 25 and 70.  
- "pracitcal" (line 32) should be "practical."  

**Overall assessment:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with compelling theoretical results and empirical validation on synthetic data. However, the incremental novelty and unresolved practical concerns about communication costs and experimental fairness undermine the contribution’s significance. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper substantially.  

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The authors must clarify the novelty relative to [10,23], provide a detailed resource/communication analysis, and conduct a fair comparison (e.g., against two parallel SGLD chains). Without these, the practical impact of the method remains ambiguous.
