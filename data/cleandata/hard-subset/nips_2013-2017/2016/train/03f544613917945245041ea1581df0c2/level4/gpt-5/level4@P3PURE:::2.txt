Summary
The paper applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias from noisy stochastic gradients, yielding an algorithm I will refer to as SGRRLD. The authors show that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD. Synthetic experiments on a Linear Gaussian Model clearly illustrate the bias reduction, and experiments on MovieLens show a slight improvement in test RMSE, though the practical significance of this improvement is debatable. Building on prior work showing that RR extrapolation improves the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs, the paper adapts the technique to SG-MCMC and proves that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD with the same step size, at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is clearly described and easy to implement.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and well-motivated extension of SGLD using a classical variance/bias reduction technique.
- Clear algorithmic description; implementation appears straightforward.
- Theoretical results indicate faster convergence (lower bias/MSE) than SGLD under appropriate conditions, with the same step size.
- Synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate bias reduction on a controlled problem.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The improvement on real data (MovieLens) is slight and arguably marginal; more extensive real-data validation is needed to establish consistent practical gains.
- The computational comparison assumes parallelization makes SGRRLD cost comparable to SGLD, but a fair baseline under the same wall-clock budget would also include running multiple parallel SGLD chains. It is unclear whether SGRRLD still outperforms an estimator that averages two SGLD chains.
- In the real-data comparison, SGRRLD is run with step sizes γ and γ/2, whereas SGLD is only run with γ. Since SGLD with γ/2 should have lower bias, a fair comparison should include SGLD with γ/2 as well (potentially with two parallel chains to match compute).

Questions for the authors
- I have not yet checked the proof details of Theorems 1 and 2. What conditions on the potential U are required for both theorems to hold? In particular, can U be multi-modal?
- Intuitively, when the stochastic gradient noises are not correlated, two chains may decorrelate quickly even if the injected Gaussian noises are correlated. In that case, how does combining the two chains reduce the order of the error? In your real-data experiments, did you observe and quantify the correlation between the two chains?

Suggestions for experiments/analysis
- Compare SGRRLD against an estimator formed by averaging two independent SGLD chains run in parallel under the same wall-clock time and step sizes, to assess whether RR extrapolation offers benefits beyond simple parallel replication.
- Include SGLD with step size γ/2 (and, if possible, two parallel chains) in the real-data comparisons to control for bias reductions due to smaller steps.
- Add more real-data experiments across diverse models and datasets to demonstrate that the observed bias/MSE improvements translate into consistent practical gains.

Overall assessment
This is a well-presented and easy-to-implement method that brings a classical extrapolation idea to SG-MCMC and offers appealing theoretical guarantees. The synthetic results are compelling, but the real-data evidence is limited and mixed. Addressing the fairness of computational baselines (parallel SGLD), including step-size-controlled comparisons, quantifying chain correlations, and expanding the empirical evaluation would substantially strengthen the paper.
