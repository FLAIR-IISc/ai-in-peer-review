This paper introduces a method to reduce the bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, which are widely used for large-scale Bayesian inference. The proposed approach leverages a classical numerical analysis technique, Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains for the same problem: one with a step size sequence (γ_k) and a second that takes two steps of size γ_k/2 for each step of the first chain. By using correlated Gaussian noise injections and taking a specific linear combination of the estimators from the two chains, the leading O(γ) bias term is canceled. The authors name their specific implementation on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) as SGRRLD.

The work is situated within the growing literature on scalable MCMC. It builds directly upon SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and the broader SG-MCMC framework. The novelty lies in being the first to apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a technique previously used for standard SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990), to the stochastic gradient setting. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves a non-asymptotic Mean Squared Error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), which is superior to the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD.

### Strengths

*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The method is conceptually simple and elegant. It is easy to implement by making minor modifications to an existing SG-MCMC sampler. The parallel nature of the two chains makes it well-suited for modern multi-core architectures.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper is technically sound and provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on both bias and MSE. These results clearly and formally establish the advantages of the proposed method over base first-order SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   **Generality and Potential Impact:** The approach is not limited to SGLD and can, in principle, be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms. The authors demonstrate this by applying it to SGHMC. This generality means the work could have a significant impact on the practice of large-scale Bayesian inference.
*   **Efficiency:** A key contribution is showing that a first-order integrator, when combined with RR extrapolation, can achieve the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators. This is a significant finding that offers a practical path to higher accuracy without the implementation complexity of higher-order schemes.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The paper is well-executed, but the following points could be addressed to strengthen it further.

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The authors state the method is generic, provided the base algorithm satisfies certain "weak error and ergodicity conditions." Could the authors be more specific about these conditions? It would be helpful to discuss which popular SG-MCMC methods would (or would not) be suitable for this technique. For instance, could this approach be adapted for approximate MCMC schemes that use Metropolis-Hastings corrections with subsampled data?

2.  **Computational Cost vs. Statistical Efficiency:** The proposed method requires approximately three times the number of gradient evaluations per effective iteration compared to standard SGLD (one for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain). While the parallel implementation reduces the wall-clock time overhead, the total computational budget is higher. The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of this trade-off. On a related note, an alternative implementation could be to run three chains in parallel for the same number of steps: one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2. Would such a scheme yield a similar bias reduction, and how would it affect the variance of the final estimator?

3.  **Extensions to Multi-Level Extrapolation:** Richardson-Romberg extrapolation can be applied recursively to cancel higher-order error terms. Is there a straightforward multi-level extension of this method using more than two step sizes? A brief discussion of this possibility and the expected theoretical gains would be interesting.

4.  **Correlation of Stochastic Gradients:** The paper correctly emphasizes the importance of correlating the injected Gaussian noise to control variance. However, the effect of correlating the stochastic gradients is not discussed. For example, one could use the same mini-batch of data for corresponding steps in the two chains. Would this further reduce the variance of the SGRRLD estimator, or is this effect negligible compared to the noise correlation?

5.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The matrix factorization experiments are compelling but could be made stronger.
    *   The plots show results for a fixed number of iterations, where it is not clear if the methods have converged. Running the experiments until both SGLD and SGRRLD have converged would provide a clearer picture of their final performance.
    *   Adding a strong optimization baseline like SGD would help contextualize the results. This would demonstrate the practical benefit of using a Bayesian approach (and SGRRLD in particular) in a setting where it is expected to be advantageous (e.g., in terms of uncertainty quantification or generalization) and put the final error values in perspective.

6.  **Presentation:** The paper is clearly written, but its accessibility, especially to practitioners, could be greatly improved by including a pseudocode algorithm box. Placing this early in the paper would allow readers less familiar with the theoretical details to quickly grasp the implementation of SGRRLD and apply it in their own work.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a simple yet powerful method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms. The claims are well-supported by both strong theory and solid empirical evidence. Addressing the points above would further enhance the clarity and impact of this excellent contribution.
