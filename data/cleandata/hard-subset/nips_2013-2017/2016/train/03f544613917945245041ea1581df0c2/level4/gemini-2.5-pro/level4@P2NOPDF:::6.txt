This paper introduces a method to accelerate Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integration technique. The proposed method, SGRRLD, involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experimental results on a synthetic Gaussian model are presented to validate the theoretical bounds on bias and MSE, while experiments on large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a valuable contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The proposed technique is interesting, and the authors do a commendable job of placing their work in the context of prior art. However, I have two main concerns regarding the novelty and the practical evaluation of the method.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Novelty:** My primary concern regards the novelty of the contribution, which appears to be somewhat incremental. The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation itself has been applied to the approximation of invariant distributions in [23]. It would be beneficial for the authors to more clearly delineate the novel aspects of their work in light of these previous contributions, particularly in the context of stochastic gradient methods.

2.  **Practicality and Experimental Comparison:** My second major concern relates to the practical implementation and the fairness of the experimental comparisons. The proposed algorithm requires running two chains in parallel. Critically, the use of correlated Brownian motion necessitates communication between these two chains at every iteration. The current analysis appears to overlook the associated communication overhead, which can be significant in distributed computing environments. This assumption of cost-free communication should be explicitly stated and justified.

    This leads to a more fundamental question about the experimental comparison with the single-chain SGLD baseline. For a fair comparison in terms of wall-clock time, it is essential to clarify how computational resources were allocated. Specifically:
    *   Was the SGLD baseline allocated the total computational resources available to the two chains of SGRRLD combined? If not, the comparison may not accurately reflect practical performance trade-offs.
    *   If the resources were matched, it is unclear how they were partitioned for the two SGRRLD chains. Given that the dominant computational cost in SG-MCMC is typically the stochastic gradient calculation, it is surprising that splitting resources between two SGRRLD chains (each with a smaller budget for gradient computation per unit of time) would result in a faster wall-clock time than dedicating all resources to a single SGLD chain. The authors should provide more detailed information about the experimental setup to clarify this point.

    To strengthen the experimental results, I suggest comparing against a more direct baseline: running two independent SGLD chains in an embarrassingly parallel fashion (i.e., without communication) and averaging their results. This would provide a clearer assessment of the benefits of the proposed RR extrapolation and communication scheme versus simply using the same parallel resources for the standard algorithm.
