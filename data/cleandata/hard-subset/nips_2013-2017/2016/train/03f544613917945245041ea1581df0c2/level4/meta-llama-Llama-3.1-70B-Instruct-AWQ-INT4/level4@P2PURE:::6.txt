This paper proposes the use of a numerical sequence acceleration method, specifically the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, to improve the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm. The method involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. Experimental results on a synthetic Gaussian model validate the bias and mean squared error (MSE) bounds of the theorems, and large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a valid contribution to the area of SG-MCMC methods, effectively contextualizing the proposed technique within the existing literature. However, I have two primary concerns. Firstly, the novelty of the paper may be incremental, as the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. Secondly, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison may not be entirely fair. The proposed method involves a parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm, requiring two chains to communicate at every step due to the use of correlated Brownian motion. However, the authors seem to ignore the communication cost, assuming it is negligible. It would be beneficial to acknowledge and discuss this aspect. Furthermore, the allocation of computing resources for the SGRRLD and SGLD algorithms in the experiments is unclear. For instance, is the single chain of SGLD implemented using the same resources as the two chains of SGRRLD? If not, this may not be a fair comparison from a practical perspective. If yes, then it is unclear how the resources are split between the two SGRRLD chains in the algorithm. As the computational cost of SG-MCMC primarily stems from computing stochastic gradients, it is unclear how using partial resources for gradient computation in each SGRRLD chain could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to using full resources for SGLD. Additional details on the implementation setup would be helpful. Alternatively, it would be interesting to compare the proposed method with running two embarrassingly parallel chains of SGLD (without communication).
