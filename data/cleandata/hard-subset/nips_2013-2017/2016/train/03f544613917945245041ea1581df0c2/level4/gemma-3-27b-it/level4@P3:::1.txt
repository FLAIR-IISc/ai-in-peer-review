This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to recent “big-data MCMC” methods, specifically Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a well-established numerical integration technique that has recently found applications in numerical stochastic differential equations (SDEs). A strength of this work is its bridging of the MCMC and SDE literatures, which may be beneficial to both communities. The authors introduce a novel SGLD-RRE algorithm, develop theoretical results on its convergence (and arguments for its improvement over standard SGLD), and demonstrate its performance numerically. They achieve compelling improvements on synthetic examples and consistent, though modest, gains in large-scale experiments. Overall, this is a sound contribution, clearly written and technically rigorous. The method represents a relatively straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, which is computationally advantageous. I anticipate practitioners will adopt these methods, though they are unlikely to fundamentally change existing practice.

**Summary:** The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a new sampling algorithm designed to reduce the bias of SGLD while maintaining a reasonable variance. The core idea leverages the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, requiring two SGLD chains to be run in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounded non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error. They prove that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than SGLD, approaching the accuracy of methods using higher-order integrators.  Experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets support these findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD is a novel and insightful approach to improve the performance of SG-MCMC methods.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including proofs of consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and MSE. This theoretical foundation strengthens the validity of the proposed method.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with both the MCMC and SDE literatures. The notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and accurate.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD compared to SGLD and, in one experiment, SGHMC. The scalability shown with the large matrix factorization task is particularly encouraging.
* **Parallelizability**: The algorithm is inherently parallelizable, making it well-suited for modern computational architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** While SGRRLD consistently outperforms SGLD, the improvements are often modest, particularly in the large-scale experiments. This suggests that the practical benefits might be limited in some applications.
*   **Sensitivity to Step Size:** The performance of SGRRLD, like SGLD, appears sensitive to the choice of step size. The theoretical analysis provides guidance, but practical tuning might still be required.
* **Figure 1a Clarity:** Figure 1a could be clarified. It depicts a Gaussian distribution fit to the empirical posterior, but this is not explicitly stated and might mislead readers into thinking it’s the empirical posterior itself. The text should emphasize this is a Gaussian approximation.
* **Counterintuitive Bias Behavior:** Figure 2a would benefit from a discussion of why the bias doesn't always decrease as the step size decreases. This behavior can be counterintuitive and warrants further explanation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of scalable MCMC.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of SGRRLD in improving the performance of SGLD.
*   The clear and well-organized presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvements over SGLD are often modest, raising questions about the practical impact of the method.
*   The sensitivity to step size and the need for careful tuning could limit the ease of use of SGRRLD.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. While the improvements offered by SGRRLD may not be revolutionary, the paper presents a solid and well-justified contribution that is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in the field of Bayesian machine learning. The theoretical and empirical analyses are convincing, and the clear presentation enhances the value of the work. The minor weaknesses can be addressed with minor revisions.
