This paper proposes a method to accelerate the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integration technique. The proposed method, SGRRLD, runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes. Crucially, these chains are coupled through correlated Gaussian noise to facilitate the extrapolation. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analyses, establishing theoretical bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model are used to verify the theoretical bounds, while a large-scale matrix factorization task on real-world data demonstrates the practical advantages of the proposed algorithm over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a valid contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The application of numerical sequence acceleration methods is a promising direction for improving these samplers. The authors do a commendable job of presenting the method, providing theoretical justification, and situating their work within the existing literature.

However, I have two main concerns regarding the paper's novelty and the practical evaluation of the proposed algorithm.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** My primary concern is that the contribution may be somewhat incremental. While applying RR extrapolation specifically to SG-MCMC is new, the core ideas have been explored in related contexts. For instance, [10] has already analyzed the effect of different numerical integrators on SG-MCMC performance, and [23] has previously used RR extrapolation for approximating invariant distributions in the non-stochastic setting. The authors should more clearly articulate the specific technical challenges and novel insights that arise from combining these two concepts, particularly in the presence of stochastic gradients.

2.  **Practicality and Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The practical utility of the proposed algorithm and the fairness of the experimental evaluation need further clarification.
    *   **Communication Overhead:** The SGRRLD algorithm requires running two tightly coupled chains. The use of correlated Brownian motion necessitates communication between these chains at every iteration. This communication overhead is not discussed or accounted for in the experiments. In many distributed or parallel computing environments, this could be a significant bottleneck that negates the benefits of faster convergence. The authors should acknowledge and discuss the potential impact of this communication cost on the method's wall-clock performance.
    *   **Resource Allocation and Fair Comparison:** The experimental comparison with SGLD may not be on an equal footing in terms of computational resources. For a fair comparison, the total computational budget (e.g., total number of gradient computations or total CPU time) should be the same for SGRRLD and the SGLD baseline. It is unclear how resources were allocated.
        *   If SGRRLD used twice the computational resources (one full-budget process for each of its two chains) as the single SGLD chain, its superior performance is expected and does not demonstrate a practical advantage.
        *   If the total resource budget was fixed and split between the two SGRRLD chains, it is not obvious how this would lead to faster wall-clock convergence. The primary cost in SG-MCMC is the stochastic gradient calculation. How can using half the gradient computation resources per chain lead to an overall improvement compared to a single SGLD chain using the full budget? More details on the experimental setup are needed to assess the fairness of the comparison.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial and more practical baseline is missing from the experiments. Instead of comparing to a single SGLD chain, a more direct comparison would be against two *independent* SGLD chains running in an embarrassingly parallel fashion, with their final samples being combined. This baseline would have the same computational footprint as SGRRLD but would avoid the communication overhead. Comparing against this alternative would provide a much clearer picture of the benefits derived specifically from the RR extrapolation and correlated noise, versus the benefits of simply parallelizing the sampling process.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  The acronym "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is defined twice in the manuscript (e.g., line 25 and line 70). Please remove the redundant definition.
