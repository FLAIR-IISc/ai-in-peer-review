

This paper introduces a novel approach to reducing bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a numerical sequence acceleration technique. The authors propose Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes and combines their outputs through RRE. The paper connects the MCMC and SDE literature by demonstrating how RRE can improve convergence rates while maintaining computational efficiency.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a formal theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic consistency and satisfies a central limit theorem. The authors prove that SGRRLD's non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error can be bounded, with the bias decaying at rate O(γ²) and MSE at rate O(γ⁴) for fixed step size γ. Crucially, they demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves the same convergence rate as higher-order integrator methods (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) with only first-order numerical integrators, which is a significant theoretical advance over existing SG-MCMC methods. The paper also establishes that SGRRLD maintains parallelizability, a key advantage for large-scale applications.

The technical arguments are well-structured and supported by rigorous proofs. The authors carefully address the variance control issue by using correlated Brownian increments, which they justify through a central limit theorem. Their theoretical results are well-motivated and extend previous work on SG-MCMC convergence rates. The connection to Richardson-Romberg extrapolation in numerical analysis provides valuable interdisciplinary insight, bridging two communities that have not previously been closely connected in the literature.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. On synthetic Gaussian models, SGRRLD consistently outperforms SGLD in terms of bias and MSE, with the bias reduction being particularly impressive (from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴). The dimensionality experiments demonstrate SGRRLD's scalability, showing MSE improvements that scale quadratically with SGLD's errors. The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on MovieLens datasets confirm the theoretical advantages in real-world applications, with SGRRLD achieving comparable accuracy to SGLD in half the computation time. The authors also successfully extend their approach to SGHMC, demonstrating the method's generality.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and precise technical language. The theoretical results are presented logically, and the experimental setup is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach, noting that for very small step sizes, the 1/(Kγ) term dominates the error bounds.

I have two minor suggestions for improvement. First, Figure 1(a) should be clarified to explicitly state that the Gaussian curves represent empirical posterior means and variances rather than the actual posterior distributions, as the current presentation might mislead readers into thinking these are direct estimates of the posterior. Second, Figure 2(a) could benefit from a brief explanation of why bias does not monotonically decrease as step size decreases, which is counterintuitive and could confuse readers unfamiliar with the theoretical bounds.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the SG-MCMC literature, offering both theoretical advances and practical improvements. The method is straightforward to implement, maintains parallelizability, and provides significant empirical improvements over existing approaches. While it may not revolutionize the field, it represents an important refinement that practitioners will likely adopt for large-scale Bayesian inference problems. The paper's clarity, technical soundness, and empirical validation make it a valuable addition to NIPS proceedings.

In summary, the paper demonstrates how a well-established numerical technique can be fruitfully applied to a modern machine learning problem, providing both theoretical insights and practical benefits. The authors have made a compelling case for the utility of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation in SG-MCMC, and their results are well-supported by both theory and experiments. I recommend acceptance.
