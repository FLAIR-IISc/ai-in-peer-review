Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer a convenient approach to Bayesian posterior inference with large datasets and have garnered significant attention in recent years. A known limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias—they do not converge exactly to the target posterior distribution. This paper demonstrates that this bias can be reduced using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The core idea is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with the second chain utilizing half the step size of the first. Crucially, the second chain is run for twice the number of steps. The injected Gaussian noise in the chains is correlated, defined as α<sub>k</sub> = (β<sub>2k-1</sub> + β<sub>2k</sub>)/√2, where α<sub>k</sub> and β<sub>k</sub> represent the injected Gaussian noise at time step *k* in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function *f* with respect to the target distribution, the estimate from Chain 1 is subtracted from the estimate obtained using Chain 2. This results in an estimate with a lower bias of O(γ<sup>2</sup>) compared to the O(γ) bias obtained from either chain alone (where γ is the step size). The authors also prove a corresponding reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates.

I particularly appreciate the simplicity of the method, both conceptually and in implementation (as summarized below), and the minimal required modifications to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Its broad applicability across a large class of SG-MCMC algorithms suggests potentially significant impact for the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, it appears underutilized within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear.

**Comments/Questions:**

*   Is this technique universally applicable to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, what specific conditions, beyond satisfying the bound in section 2.2—π<sub>γ</sub>(f) = π(f) + C * γ + O(γ<sup>2</sup>)—are necessary for its application?
*   Could this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings tests? What about its applicability to Distributed SGLD?
*   Given that one chain must run for twice as long as the other, there is inherent computational inefficiency with the faster chain remaining idle. Would it be possible to employ three chains—one with a step size of γ, and two with step sizes of γ/2—running all for the same number of steps? Would this approach increase the variance of the estimate?
*   Is there a generalization of this method to utilize more than two step sizes?
*   The injected noise in the chains requires correlation, but the impact of correlation *between* mini-batches is unclear. Is the variance minimized when using the same batch for the *k*<sup>th</sup> iteration of Chain 1, and the (2*k*-1)<sup>th</sup> and 2*k*<sup>th</sup> iterations of Chain 2? Or is this correlation effect negligible?
*   The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should ideally be run until convergence. Furthermore, a comparison to standard SGD would be beneficial. While the current results demonstrate superior performance compared to SGLD, demonstrating improvement over SGD in a scenario where the Bayesian approach is advantageous (i.e., achieving better performance) would strengthen the argument for adoption. Bayesian matrix factorization is not a ubiquitous technique, so providing concrete performance gains will be persuasive. Including an SGD plot would also provide valuable context for interpreting the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   Although the method is easily understood and well-presented, including an algorithm box containing pseudocode would enhance accessibility for a broader audience. Many practitioners may skip the more theoretical sections; therefore, readily available pseudocode early in the paper would allow those less familiar with the field to experiment with the method without needing to delve into the more complex details.
