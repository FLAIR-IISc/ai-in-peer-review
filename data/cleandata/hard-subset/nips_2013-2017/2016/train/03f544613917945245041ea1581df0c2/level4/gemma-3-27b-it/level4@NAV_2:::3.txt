Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods have become increasingly popular for Bayesian inference in large-scale applications, offering a scalable approach to posterior estimation. A key limitation of these methods is their inherent bias, preventing exact convergence to the target distribution. This paper introduces a novel approach to reduce this bias leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a technique well-established in numerical analysis. The core idea involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel – one with step size γ and another with γ/2 – with the latter executed for twice the iterations. The injected Gaussian noise in the chains is correlated, specifically defined as αk = (β2k-1 + β2k)/√2, where αk and βk represent the Gaussian noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. The estimated expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution is then obtained by combining the estimates from the two chains. This approach demonstrably reduces the bias to O(γ2) compared to O(γ) achieved by either chain individually. Correspondingly, the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates is also shown to decrease. The simplicity of the method – requiring minimal changes to existing SG-MCMC algorithms – and its broad applicability across various SG-MCMC schemes are particularly appealing. This work offers a potentially significant contribution to the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

The novelty lies in the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC algorithms, a combination not previously explored within the machine learning literature, despite the established use of RR extrapolation in other fields. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, facilitating understanding of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.

Several points warrant further consideration:

*   **Generality:** While the paper indicates broad applicability, it would be helpful to explicitly delineate the conditions beyond those mentioned in Section 2.2 (pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)) that are necessary for the method to function effectively across different SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   **Extension to other schemes:** Investigating the applicability of this technique to other approximate MCMC methods, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings algorithms, or distributed SGLD, would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The requirement for one chain to run for twice as long as the other introduces potential computational inefficiencies. Exploring alternatives, such as employing three chains – one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2, all running for the same number of steps – could improve efficiency. An analysis of the variance implications of such an approach would be valuable.
*   **Multi-Step Extrapolation:** The potential for extending this method to utilize more than two step sizes merits investigation.
*   **Noise Correlation:** Clarifying the precise effect of correlated mini-batches on performance is important. Specifically, is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)-th and (2k)-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is the impact of this correlation negligible?
*   **Matrix Factorization Experiment:** The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) would be more convincing if run until convergence and included a comparison against Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). Demonstrating superior performance in a scenario where Bayesian inference provides an advantage would strengthen the practical impact of the work. Furthermore, providing an algorithm box would enhance accessibility, especially for practitioners less familiar with the theoretical details.



Arguments for acceptance:

*   Presents a novel and potentially impactful method for reducing bias in SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   Theoretical analysis provides strong justification for the method’s effectiveness.
*   Experimental results demonstrate performance improvements over existing methods.
*   The method is relatively simple to implement and broadly applicable.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   The computational inefficiency of running one chain for twice as long as the other could limit practical applicability.
*   Further investigation is needed to fully understand the impact of noise correlation and explore alternative implementations.
*   The matrix factorization experiment could be strengthened by including a comparison to SGD and running until convergence.
