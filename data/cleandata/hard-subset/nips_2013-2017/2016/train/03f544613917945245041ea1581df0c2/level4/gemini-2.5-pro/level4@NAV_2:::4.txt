This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique for accelerating convergence, to reduce the discretization bias inherent in SG-MCMC methods. This is achieved by running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to produce a higher-order accurate estimator. This work builds upon prior research on RR extrapolation for SDEs (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) and extends it to the stochastic gradient setting, which is highly relevant for large-scale Bayesian inference. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than standard SGLD. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization problem.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant approach to mitigate the bias issue that limits many of these samplers. The method provides a principled way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators while only using a first-order integrator (Euler-Maruyama), which is a significant contribution.
*   **Quality of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is supported by a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide both a central limit theorem for the asymptotic behavior of the estimator and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE. These results formally establish the improved convergence rates (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE, compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD), which is a strong theoretical result.
*   **Practical Relevance and Parallelism:** The proposed algorithm is well-suited for modern parallel computing architectures. Since the two chains can be run independently, the method can achieve significant speedups in wall-clock time compared to sequential algorithms, as demonstrated in the matrix factorization experiments.
*   **Generality:** The proposed RR scheme is quite general. The authors demonstrate this by applying it to SGHMC, and it seems applicable to a wide range of other SG-MCMC algorithms, provided they meet certain technical conditions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
*   **Computational Cost Comparison:** The experimental comparison in Figure 5, based on wall-clock time, effectively highlights the benefits on a parallel system. However, the total computational cost of SGRRLD, measured in gradient evaluations, is 3x that of SGLD for each time step of the slower chain. For users on single-core systems, this would negate the wall-clock time advantage. A comparison of performance versus the total number of gradient evaluations would provide a more complete assessment of the algorithm's efficiency and clarify the trade-offs.
*   **Clarity of Rate Derivation:** The paper states the optimal step-size decay exponent `α` and the resulting convergence rates, which are derived from the bounds in Theorems 1 and 2. For instance, it is stated that the optimal `α` for the MSE is 1/5, derived from minimizing the bound O(K⁻⁽⁴ᵅ⁾∧⁽¹⁻ᵅ⁾). While this is likely correct, providing a more detailed derivation in the main text or appendix would improve the paper's clarity and self-containedness for the reader.
*   **Discrepancy in Experimental Results (Figure 3):** There appears to be an inconsistency regarding the optimal `α` for the bias. The text states the optimal value is `α = 1/3`. However, in Figure 3 (left), the red curve corresponding to `α = 0.2` appears to achieve the lowest bias. Could the authors please clarify this? Furthermore, the curve for the claimed optimal `α` (and others) fluctuates significantly; an explanation for this behavior would be helpful.
*   **Error for Small Step Sizes:** The authors note that when the step size `γ` is very small, the `1/(Kγ)` term in the error bounds dominates. This is an important observation. For completeness, it would be useful to briefly contrast this with the behavior of standard SGLD, which has a similar term in its error bounds, to clarify whether this effect is more pronounced for SGRRLD.

**Minor Comments:**
*   Please define the acronym RR (Richardson-Romberg) on its first use (line 94).
*   In Theorem 1, the term `Γ_K` is used before it is defined (line 131).
*   It would improve precision to state the required smoothness conditions for the test function `f` in the theorems.
*   The text describes Figure 1(b) as a plot of MSE versus dimension, but the figure itself shows the bias of estimated variance versus time. Please correct this inconsistency.
*   In Eq. (1), the symbol `ϑ` is used for the continuous-time process, while `θ` is used for its discrete approximation. This is a reasonable choice, but it might be worth clarifying explicitly to avoid any potential confusion.
*   For improved readability, please consider using a consistent notation, such as boldface, to denote vectors.
