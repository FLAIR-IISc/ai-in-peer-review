This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to mitigate the bias inherent in methods like SGLD. The core idea – applying RR acceleration to reduce bias while controlling variance – is well-motivated, building on prior work demonstrating the benefits of RR extrapolation for SDEs (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990) and higher-order integrators in SG-MCMC (Chen et al., 2015). The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and mean squared error, demonstrating improved convergence rates compared to SGLD. The experimental validation includes a synthetic linear Gaussian model and real-world matrix factorization tasks using the MovieLens datasets.

The strengths of this work lie in its rigorous theoretical foundation and the clear presentation of the SGRRLD algorithm. The application of a well-established numerical technique (RR extrapolation) to the SG-MCMC setting is a novel contribution. The paper effectively demonstrates the potential benefits of reducing bias in stochastic gradient-based sampling. The included experiments, while limited, offer initial support for the theoretical findings, particularly the improved performance on the synthetic dataset. The clarity of the algorithm description and the discussion of parallelizability are also notable positives.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The real-data experiments, while showcasing improvement, feel somewhat limited in scope. The comparison to SGLD feels slightly imbalanced, specifically concerning step size selection. Comparing SGRRLD (with two chains) to SGLD with a fixed step size, while mentioning the possibility of running SGLD with multiple parallel chains, doesn't fully address this point. A more compelling comparison would involve running multiple parallel SGLD chains with a step size equivalent to that used in SGRRLD (e.g., γ/2) and assessing the relative performance. Furthermore, the impact of the correlation between the Brownian motions of the two chains is not fully explored empirically. The authors mention the benefits of correlated increments, but the experiments do not directly demonstrate this effect. Regarding the theoretical analysis, the conditions on the potential energy function *U* required for Theorems 1 and 2 could be clarified – specifically, whether the results hold for multi-modal *U*. Finally, the intuition behind how the combination of potentially decorrelated chains reduces the error order warrants further explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, addressing a known limitation of these methods (bias).
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the improved convergence properties of SGRRLD.
*   Clear and well-defined algorithm with potential for practical implementation due to its parallel nature.
*   Initial experimental results (synthetic data) demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Real-data experiments are limited and the comparison to SGLD could be more thorough (e.g., parallel SGLD chains with adjusted step size).
*   Lack of empirical evidence demonstrating the impact of correlated Brownian motions.
*   The theoretical conditions on the potential function *U* could be clarified.
*   Intuition behind the error reduction with potentially decorrelated chains needs further elaboration.

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While the real-data experiments could be strengthened and some theoretical aspects clarified, the novel approach, theoretical rigor, and potential practical benefits make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
