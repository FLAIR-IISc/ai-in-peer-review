Summary
The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while controlling variance. The algorithm runs two SGLD-like chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2, and combines their estimates using an RR correction. Variance is controlled by correlating the Gaussian noise across chains via shared Brownian increments. The authors provide theory establishing consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. Under standard decreasing step-size schedules, SGRRLD achieves improved rates—O(K^−4/5) for the MSE—matching the accuracy of higher-order integrators while using a first-order discretization. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model validate the predicted bias/MSE scaling, and large-scale matrix factorization demonstrates practical gains over SGLD in wall-clock time. The authors also show that the RR idea extends to SGHMC (SGRRHMC) and can match the accuracy of an SGHMC variant with a higher-order splitting integrator.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on SGLD [1] and subsequent SG-MCMC variants [4–12], on analyses of SG-MCMC convergence and higher-order integrators [10, 20], and on RR extrapolation for SDEs and invariant measures [21–23]. It adapts RR extrapolation to the stochastic-gradient setting and provides variance control via correlated noise as suggested in [13, 23].

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with broad applicability to SG-MCMC methods.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds; explicit discussion of optimal step-size schedules and rates.
- Practicality: easy to implement, and parallelizable by design.
- Empirical validation: synthetic experiments match theoretical scaling; real-data matrix factorization shows improved accuracy vs. time.
- Extension beyond SGLD (to SGHMC) supports generality.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Novelty may be incremental: the effect of integrator order on SG-MCMC is known [10], and RR for approximating invariant measures is established for SDEs [23]. The contribution is mainly to port RR to SG-MCMC with correlated noise and provide analyses in this setting.
- Fairness and practicality of the experimental comparisons: the two-chain design requires coordinated correlated noise. The paper assumes negligible communication/synchronization overhead and reports identical wall-clock time by running two processes. It is unclear how compute resources (CPU cores, memory bandwidth) were allocated between SGRRLD and the single-chain SGLD baselines. Without matching total compute budgets, throughput, and communication cost, the wall-clock comparisons may be optimistic for SGRRLD.
- Missing baselines: an “embarrassingly parallel” alternative—running two independent SGLD chains on the same total hardware budget—could be a strong baseline to assess the added value of RR extrapolation and correlation. Likewise, broader comparisons to higher-order SG-MCMC methods (e.g., [10]) on real data would strengthen the claims.

Quality
- The theoretical analysis is careful and appears technically sound, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The choice of correlated Brownian increments is well-justified, and the bounds and rates are clearly derived.
- Experimental methodology is generally solid for the synthetic setting; for large-scale experiments, more detail on computation budgets and communication would improve credibility.

Clarity
- The paper is well-organized and readable. The estimator definitions and coupling choices are clearly presented.
- Please clarify how correlated Gaussians are generated in parallel (e.g., shared RNG streams vs. per-iteration communication), and whether minibatches are shared or independent across chains.
- Minor editorial issues remain (see below).

Originality
- Conceptually modest: RR extrapolation and correlated noise are known in SDE numerics. The novelty lies in adapting these to SG-MCMC, proving CLTs and non-asymptotic bounds in the stochastic-gradient setting, and demonstrating practical gains.

Significance
- If the computational benefits hold under fair, matched resource settings—including communication overhead—this could be a useful and widely applicable technique for improving SG-MCMC accuracy with minimal code changes.
- The improved rates and simplicity make the method attractive for large-scale inference.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly state the hardware and resource allocation: number of cores, threads, and whether SGLD used the same total compute resources as the two SGRRLD chains. If SGLD used a single core while SGRRLD used two, please rerun SGLD with the same total resources or report throughput-normalized results.
- Quantify or bound the communication/synchronization overhead required for correlated noise. If a shared RNG seed or deterministic coupling obviates communication, explain how this is implemented.
- Compare against: (a) two independent SGLD chains run in parallel with the same hardware and combined via simple averaging; (b) higher-order SG-MCMC methods (e.g., [10]) on the real datasets; (c) SGLD with tuned step sizes and minibatch sizes under the same time budget.
- Report sensitivity to the minibatch size and step-size schedule, and provide ablations on the coupling strength (e.g., varying Σ).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with strong theory and promising empirical results.
- Achieves higher accuracy with first-order integrators; practical for large-scale settings.
- Clear exposition and extension to SGHMC.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to [10, 23].
- Experimental comparisons may not reflect equal resource allocations or communication costs.
- Missing baselines that could contextualize the practical gains.

Minor comments
- “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is defined twice; please remove the duplicate.
- Fix typos: “practical,” “embarrassingly,” “variance,” and minor grammatical errors (e.g., “reducing the bias and keeps the variance” → “reducing bias while keeping variance”).
