This paper introduces a method to accelerate the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration technique typically employed with higher-order numerical integrators. The proposed approach, termed SGRRLD, necessitates running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with differing step sizes and incorporating correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experimental validation on a synthetic Gaussian model confirms the theoretical bounds on bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the practical applicability of SGRRLD to large-scale matrix factorization using real-world data, showing performance improvements over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the existing literature, clearly articulating the relationship to prior research. I do, however, have two primary concerns.

First, the novelty of the approach appears somewhat incremental. Previous work, notably [10], has analyzed the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC, and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been previously utilized for approximating invariant distributions [23]. While the application to SGMCMC is valuable, a more detailed discussion differentiating this work from these precedents would strengthen the paper.

Second, the practical utility of SGRRLD may be limited by implementation overhead, and the experimental comparison lacks sufficient detail to fully assess its advantages. SGRRLD’s parallel implementation, coupled with the requirement for correlated Brownian motion necessitating inter-chain communication at each step, introduces communication costs that are not addressed in the paper. Acknowledging and potentially quantifying this communication cost would be beneficial. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks clarity regarding resource allocation. Specifically, it is unclear whether the single SGLD chain used for comparison utilized the same computational resources as the two chains of SGRRLD. If not, the comparison may not be entirely fair from a practical perspective. If the resources *were* shared, a more detailed explanation of how they were divided between the two SGRRLD chains is needed, particularly considering that the stochastic gradient computation is typically the dominant cost in SG-MCMC. It’s not immediately obvious how dividing resources for gradient computation across two chains in SGRRLD would lead to a reduction in wall-clock time compared to dedicating all resources to a single SGLD chain. More details regarding the implementation setup—including hardware specifications, software libraries, and parallelization strategies—are necessary. Finally, an alternative baseline would be to run two independent SGLD chains in parallel (embarrassingly parallel, without communication). A comparison to this baseline would help isolate the benefits of the correlated noise and extrapolation.
