Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a Richardson–Romberg (RR) variance-reduction scheme for SG-MCMC that cancels leading-order discretization bias and improves convergence rates in both bias and MSE. The method is simple to implement, requiring only minimal modifications to standard SG-MCMC, and is supported by clear theoretical development. Overall, the idea is elegant, with a principled link to multilevel/RR extrapolation, and the theoretical results are compelling.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Adapting RR to SG-MCMC is natural yet nontrivial; the paper articulates the construction well.
- Practicality: The approach integrates with existing SG-MCMC algorithms with little overhead in code.
- Theory: The bounds and claimed rate improvements appear well-founded, under standard SG-MCMC assumptions.
- Empirical support: Experiments generally corroborate the theoretical improvements.

Main concerns and questions
1) How exactly are the stated convergence rates obtained from the theorem?
- The paper states improved bias and MSE rates (e.g., for a step-size schedule γ_k ∝ k^(-α), with α near 1/3), but it is not fully clear how these exponents and constants follow from the main theorem.
- Please include a short derivation (or an expanded proof sketch in the supplement) that explicitly maps the theorem’s terms to the final rates, including:
  - The decomposition of the bias/MSE into stochastic-gradient noise, discretization error, and finite-time terms (e.g., O(γ^p) + O(1/(Kγ)) patterns), and which orders are cancelled by RR.
  - The precise regularity assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz gradient, dissipativity, Lipschitz Hessian, Poisson equation regularity) needed for the order of the local weak error, and how these imply the claimed global bias orders after RR.
  - The admissible range of α and why α = 1/3 is predicted to be optimal under your bounds (including constants).

2) Fairness of the matrix factorization experiment (computational budget)
- The RR scheme runs two coupled chains, one of which uses a smaller step size and thus requires more steps. This induces a higher computational cost per unit “time,” roughly summing to about 3× the cost of a single SGLD chain for the same number of parameter updates.
- To make the comparison with SGLD fair, please report results at equal computational budgets (e.g., equal total gradient evaluations or equal wall-clock time), or rescale curves accordingly. As presented, the performance gap in Figure 5 likely shrinks under such a normalization.
- Please also detail the hardware, number of cores/GPUs, and whether chains were run in parallel.

3) Figure 3 (bias vs step-size exponent) interpretation
- In the bias plot, the red curve (α = 0.2) appears to converge best, whereas the text claims α = 1/3 is optimal. Could you clarify this discrepancy? Is it due to finite-sample effects, constants, or being in a regime where higher-order terms dominate?
- The “optimal” curve fluctuates noticeably. Is this due to increased variance from the extrapolation coefficients, minibatch noise, or coupling? Please provide more repetitions and error bars, or smoothing via independent seeds, to demonstrate the predicted rate more clearly.

4) Small step-size regime and 1/(Kγ) dominance
- The paper notes (line 256) that when γ is very small, the 1/(Kγ) term should dominate. Why is such dominance not observed for SGLD in your plots? Is it masked by minibatch noise or by how K is chosen? A brief explanation or an ablation increasing K at very small γ would clarify.

5) Implementation details of the RR scheme
- Please specify whether the two chains share minibatches and random seeds in a synchronized manner to reduce variance and achieve the theoretical cancellation. The exact coupling is critical for realizing RR benefits in practice.

Minor comments and editorial issues
- Equation (1): Please use consistent notation for parameters. If θ is used throughout, avoid introducing a different symbol here without need.
- Line 94: Define “RR” at first use.
- Line 131: Γ_K is used without definition; please define it when it first appears.
- Line 160: “smooth function f” — specify the required smoothness (e.g., C^2 with Lipschitz gradient, Lipschitz Hessian) needed by the proofs.
- Line 249: You state “Figure 1(b) plots Bias vs. time, not dimension.” This appears to refer to Figure 1(c); please correct the figure reference.
- Notation: Use bold letters for vectors and matrices to improve readability.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Add a short subsection that bridges the theorem to the final complexity/rate statements, with a clear bias/MSE decomposition and the role of RR in cancelling leading terms.
- Include a wall-clock-time or gradient-evaluation-normalized comparison across all baselines. Provide both per-iteration and per-gradient-evaluation metrics.
- Report confidence intervals or shaded standard errors for key plots (e.g., Figures 3 and 5), and increase the number of independent runs to show stability of the claimed rates.
- Discuss the practical trade-off: higher per-iteration cost vs. better asymptotic constants/rates. Indicate regimes (dataset size, model dimension, target accuracy) where RR-SG-MCMC is expected to yield net wall-clock gains.
- If feasible, include a comparison to well-tuned SGHMC/SGRLD or other advanced SG-MCMC variants under equal compute budgets.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and technically solid paper that brings a classical RR idea to SG-MCMC with clear theoretical and empirical benefits. The main reservations concern the explicit derivation of the quoted rates from the theorem and the fairness of some empirical comparisons under matched computational budgets. With clarifications and the suggested experimental normalizations, the contribution would be both rigorous and convincingly demonstrated. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the theoretical mapping to rates and the fairness/interpretation issues in the experiments.
