

The authors propose a novel method, stochastic gradient Richardson-Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), to reduce bias in stochastic gradient MCMC. The approach leverages Richardson-Romberg extrapolation—a standard technique for stochastic differential equations—to combine two independent chains run in parallel without inter-chain communication. Theoretical analysis establishes asymptotic consistency, and empirical results demonstrate compelling performance improvements over baseline methods. While the core idea is straightforward—essentially a combination of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation with SGLD—the method’s effectiveness is well-supported by experiments.  

The primary strength lies in the theoretical efficiency gain: SG-RR-MCMC achieves a superior convergence rate compared to standard SG-MCMC. However, this rate advantage could also be attained using high-order integrators (e.g., the methods in Chen et al., 2015), and the authors omit a direct comparison with such integrators in their experiments. This omission weakens the claim that SG-RR-MCMC offers a uniquely efficient solution, as the performance gains might not be attributable to the proposed method itself.  

Two critical technical issues require clarification. First, the paper contains an inconsistent statement regarding the step size schedule. The theoretical analysis (Equation 5) assumes a specific diminishing step size schedule, yet Algorithm 1 (in the supplement) employs a fixed step size. This contradiction undermines the theoretical claims, as the convergence guarantees depend on the diminishing schedule. Second, Algorithm 1 explicitly contradicts Equation 5: Equation 5 specifies a step size of $ \eta_t = \eta_0 / t^\alpha $ for $ \alpha > 0 $, but Algorithm 1 uses a constant $ \eta $, violating the theoretical requirement. The authors must reconcile this discrepancy or revise the algorithm to align with the analysis.  

While the empirical results validate the method’s practical utility, the absence of comparisons with high-order integrators and the unresolved technical inconsistencies in the step size treatment limit the paper’s contribution. The approach is conceptually simple, and the theoretical benefits are not uniquely tied to the Richardson-Romberg framework. For the method to stand out, the authors should address these gaps: explicitly compare against high-order integrators and correct the step size inconsistency in the algorithm. Without these revisions, the work risks being perceived as a minor variation rather than a substantive advance.
