This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics algorithm, SGRRLD, leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to mitigate bias stemming from gradient estimation noise. The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves lower asymptotic bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) compared to standard SGLD. Validation through synthetic experiments using a Linear Gaussian Model confirms the algorithm's benefits. While experiments on the MovieLens dataset indicate a modest improvement in test RMSE, the statistical significance of this improvement requires further investigation. The application of RR extrapolation to accelerate convergence in Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) Monte Carlo methods is well-established, and this work successfully adapts this principle to the SG-MCMC setting. Theoretical analysis confirms faster convergence rates for SGRRLD compared to SGLD, given equivalent step sizes, albeit at the cost of maintaining two parallel Markov chains. 

The algorithm is clearly presented and readily implementable. The synthetic results convincingly demonstrate SGRRLD’s ability to reduce bias in that controlled scenario.  However, more extensive experimentation with real-world datasets is necessary to establish the algorithm’s generalizability and practical impact. 

Several questions arise regarding the theoretical foundations. I have not verified the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2, but I am interested in clarifying the precise conditions imposed on *U* for both theorems to hold. Specifically, can *U* be multi-modal?  Furthermore, it would be helpful to understand how the error order is reduced by combining the two chains, particularly considering that uncorrelated gradient noise should quickly lead to chain decorrelation. Do the authors observe any correlation between the chains in their real data experiments? 

Regarding the experimental setup, the claim of comparable computational time to SGLD, achieved through parallelization, is worth scrutinizing.  Running multiple parallel SGLD chains within the same timeframe is also possible. A fairer comparison would involve evaluating whether the observed RMSE improvements remain consistent when comparing SGRRLD to two parallel SGLD chains.  Finally, the comparison in the real data experiment using SGRRLD with step sizes γ and γ/2 against SGLD with γ seems incomplete. Including a comparison against SGLD with γ/2, potentially run with parallel chains, would provide a more comprehensive assessment.  I strongly encourage the authors to conduct additional experiments on diverse real-world datasets to substantiate their findings.
