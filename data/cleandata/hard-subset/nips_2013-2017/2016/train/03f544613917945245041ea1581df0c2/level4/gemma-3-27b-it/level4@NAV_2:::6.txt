This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), leveraging the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique to reduce bias compared to standard SGLD. The core idea involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with differing step sizes and correlated Brownian motions. The authors provide theoretical analysis establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error. Empirical validation is presented using both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task, demonstrating improved convergence rates and accuracy compared to SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. It effectively addresses the common issue of bias in SG-MCMC methods and proposes a well-motivated and theoretically grounded approach for mitigation. The analysis is thorough, and the experimental results are promising, demonstrating the potential benefits of the proposed method. The connection to higher-order integrators, and the demonstration of achieving comparable accuracy, is a significant strength.

However, the novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental. While the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, the paper acknowledges related work in numerical acceleration of SDEs [23] and the analysis of higher-order integrators in SG-MCMC [10]. Further distinguishing the contribution beyond combining these existing ideas would strengthen the paper. 

A potential limitation lies in the practical considerations of implementing SGRRLD. The requirement for correlated Brownian motions necessitates communication between the two chains at each step. While the authors acknowledge the parallel nature of the algorithm, a discussion of the communication cost and its impact on performance is missing. The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding resource allocation. Specifically, it’s unclear whether the single chain of SGLD was implemented using the same computational resources as the two chains of SGRRLD, and how resource allocation affected the wall-clock time comparison. A fairer comparison might involve running two independent SGLD chains and comparing that to SGRRLD, or a more detailed breakdown of computational costs. The observation that SGRRLD achieves faster wall-clock time despite gradient computation being the dominant cost is intriguing and warrants further explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous convergence analysis.
*   Demonstrated improvement over SGLD and SGHMC in both synthetic and real-world experiments.
*   Well-written and clearly presented, with a comprehensive review of related work.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem (bias reduction in SG-MCMC).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty given existing work on numerical acceleration and high-order integrators.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of communication costs associated with correlated Brownian motions.
*   Unclear experimental setup regarding resource allocation, potentially leading to an unfair comparison.



**Minor comments:**

*   “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is repeated on lines 25 and 70.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the sensitivity of the method to the choice of step sizes and correlation parameter.
