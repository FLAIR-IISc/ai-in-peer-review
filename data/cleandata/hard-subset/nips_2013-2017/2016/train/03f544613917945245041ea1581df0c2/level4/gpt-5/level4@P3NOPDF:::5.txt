Summary:
The paper proposes stochastic gradient Richardson–Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), which applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias inherent in stochastic-gradient MCMC methods such as SGLD. The approach runs two Markov chains in embarrassingly parallel fashion, without inter-chain communication, and combines them to cancel leading-order discretization bias. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing asymptotic consistency and improved convergence rates, and they present empirical results that are generally convincing.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable: leveraging a well-known extrapolation technique from SDE numerics to debias SG-MCMC is an elegant idea that could be adopted with minimal engineering effort.
- Parallelizable: the two-chain construction suits modern multi-core environments and avoids synchronization overhead.
- Theory: the analysis indicates asymptotic consistency and suggests a better convergence rate than standard SGLD under appropriate conditions.
- Empirics: the experiments support the claim that the method reduces bias and can improve accuracy in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Missing comparison against higher-order integrators: While the paper argues for improved efficiency via better convergence rates, similar gains can often be achieved using higher-order (e.g., second-order) integrators. A direct empirical comparison under matched computational budgets is needed to substantiate the advantage of SG-RR-MCMC.
- Step-size schedule inconsistencies: The manuscript contains inconsistent statements regarding the step-size choices. Because Richardson–Romberg extrapolation typically relies on running chains at two related step sizes, the precise schedule and its role in the extrapolation are critical. The current presentation leaves ambiguity about whether constant or decreasing step sizes are used, how the paired step sizes are selected, and how these choices affect both bias cancellation and stability.
- Algorithmic inconsistency: Algorithm 1 in the supplement appears inconsistent with Equation (5). This discrepancy makes it difficult to reproduce the method and assess the theoretical claims. The paper should reconcile notation and operations so that the algorithmic steps directly instantiate the equations.
- Computational trade-offs: Running two chains doubles the number of gradient evaluations per iteration. Although this can be mitigated by parallelism, the paper should explicitly discuss wall-clock costs, memory usage, and how performance scales with the number of available cores. In particular, results should control for equalized total gradient computations and report wall-clock time where relevant.
- Interaction with stochastic gradient noise: Richardson–Romberg primarily targets discretization bias. In SG-MCMC, gradient noise introduces additional bias/variance effects. The analysis should more clearly separate these sources, state assumptions under which gradient noise does not dominate, and explain how step-size and minibatch choices preserve the extrapolation’s benefits.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Provide a clear, self-contained description of the step-size policy. Specify whether the two chains use (h, h/2), constant vs. decaying schedules, and the exact schedules used in each experiment. If different tasks require different schedules, justify the choices.
- Reconcile Algorithm 1 with Equation (5). Ensure symbols, operations, and the combination rule used for extrapolation match exactly. A minimal working pseudocode block that maps each line to its corresponding equation would resolve the ambiguity.
- Add a comparison against a strong higher-order integrator baseline adapted to the stochastic-gradient setting, under matched computational budgets (e.g., equal total number of gradient evaluations). Report both accuracy and wall-clock time.
- Report effective sample size per second (or per gradient evaluation) and bias metrics where applicable, to isolate benefits due to bias reduction versus increased variance.
- Discuss sensitivity to minibatch size and gradient noise. If the analysis assumes control variates, variance reduction, or specific noise conditions, make these explicit. If not, provide an empirical sensitivity study across minibatch sizes.
- Clarify the asymptotic consistency result: state the precise conditions (e.g., Lipschitz and dissipativity assumptions, step-size requirements, properties of gradient noise) and the convergence rate statement, with a brief interpretation of constants where possible.
- Elaborate on parallel implementation details: whether the two chains share random seeds or preconditioning, any effect on reproducibility, and whether asynchronous execution affects the extrapolation.

Overall assessment:
This is an interesting and timely contribution that adapts a classical bias-reduction technique to SG-MCMC in a practical, parallelizable way. The theoretical results and empirical evidence are promising. However, the paper’s impact would be significantly strengthened by resolving inconsistencies in the step-size description and algorithmic presentation, and by adding a fair comparison to higher-order integrators under matched compute. With these clarifications and additional experiments, I would view the submission as a solid contribution to scalable Bayesian inference.
