This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to recent "big-data MCMC" methods, with a focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a standard numerical integration tool that has recently seen some nice applications to numerical stochastic differential equations (SDEs); one of the nice features of this paper is that it provides a link between the MCMC literature and the SDE literature, which may be beneficial for both communities.

The authors introduce a new SGLD-RRE method, develop some theoretical results on the convergence of the new algorithm (along with arguments for when SGLD-RRE improves on vanilla SGLD), and show some numerical results. They obtain impressive improvements on toy numerical examples, and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale examples.

Overall, this is a solid paper, clearly written. The paper provides a good balance between theoretical results and empirical validation. The method ends up being a fairly straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, which is computationally convenient. I believe practitioners will use these methods, though the methods will not revolutionize current practice.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The introduction provides a good background on the problem and the related work. The proposed method is clearly explained, and the theoretical results are well-presented. The numerical experiments are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The authors have done a good job in providing a detailed analysis of the proposed method, including its convergence properties and the conditions under which it improves on vanilla SGLD. The use of correlated Brownian increments is a nice touch, as it helps to control the variance of the estimator.

The paper has some minor issues that need to be addressed. Figure 1a is slightly misleading, as it shows a Gaussian distribution with the empirical posterior mean and variance, rather than the empirical estimated posterior distribution. This should be clarified or emphasized more for the unwary reader.

Figure 2a shows that the bias is not necessarily decreasing as the step size decreases, which is a bit counter-intuitive. It would be helpful to add a bit of discussion about this phenomenon.

In terms of the quality of the paper, I would rate it as follows:

* Quality: 9/10 (the paper is technically sound, and the results are convincing)
* Clarity: 9/10 (the paper is well-organized and easy to follow)
* Originality: 8/10 (the application of RRE to SGLD is new, but the method itself is not revolutionary)
* Significance: 8/10 (the paper provides some useful insights and improvements, but it is not a game-changer)

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, pending some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel application of RRE to SGLD, which is a useful contribution to the field.
* The theoretical results are convincing, and the numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The method is not revolutionary, and it builds on existing work in the field.
* The paper has some minor issues that need to be addressed, such as the misleading figure and the lack of discussion about the bias.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Clarify or emphasize the issue with Figure 1a.
* Add a bit of discussion about the bias in Figure 2a.
* Consider adding more numerical experiments to demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method.
