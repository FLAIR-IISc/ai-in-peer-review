This paper introduces a novel SG-MCMC algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce bias and improve convergence rates compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply a Richardson-Romberg extrapolation technique, leveraging two parallel chains with different step sizes. The method is presented with strong theoretical guarantees, including asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of SGRRLD through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, including a large-scale matrix factorization task.

The paper builds upon the increasingly popular field of SG-MCMC methods [Welling & Teh, 2011] for scalable Bayesian inference. Recent work has highlighted the bias inherent in these methods [Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016], and attempts have been made to mitigate this through higher-order integrators [Chen et al., 2015]. This work offers an alternative approach by leveraging the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-known numerical technique [Talay & Tubaro, 1990], within the SG-MCMC framework. The approach is notable for its relatively straightforward implementation, requiring minimal modifications to existing SGLD implementations. The theoretical analysis convincingly demonstrates the potential for improved convergence rates, and the empirical results support these claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic error bounds.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is relatively easy to implement, requiring only minor modifications to existing SGLD implementations.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The matrix factorization experiment is particularly compelling, showing performance improvements on a large-scale task.
*   **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallel structure of the RR extrapolation lends itself well to parallel computing architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Fairness:** The comparison in Figure 5 regarding computation time might be misleading. Because SGRRLD requires two chains, with one requiring double the steps, a fair comparison would necessitate adjusting the SGLD runtime by a factor of 2/3. This adjustment could potentially diminish the observed performance gap.
*   **Discrepancy in Figure 3:** The bias plot in Figure 3 suggests that the optimal convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, contradicting the claim in the text that α = 1/3 is optimal. Further investigation is needed to resolve this discrepancy and explain the observed fluctuations in the optimal curve.
*   **Clarification Needed:** The explanation regarding why the 1/Kγ term does not dominate for SGLD when the step size is small (line 256) is unclear and requires further elaboration.
*   **Minor Notational Issues:** Several minor notational inconsistencies exist: Eq. 1 uses a different symbol for the parameter than elsewhere in the paper; "RR" is used without definition in line 94; Γ<sub>K</sub> is used without definition in line 131; and the smoothness requirements for function *f* in line 160 are underspecified. Figure 1(b) plots bias against time, while the caption claims it is against dimension – this seems to be a typo and should likely be Figure 1(c). Vectors are not consistently represented in boldface.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to improving SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian inference and has the potential to impact a broad range of applications.
*   The clarity and organization of the paper are generally good, and the minor issues noted above can be easily addressed with revisions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The potential unfairness in the experimental comparison (Figure 5) raises concerns about the robustness of the reported performance gains.
*   The discrepancy between the theoretical claims and the empirical results in Figure 3 requires clarification.
*   The lack of detailed explanation regarding the 1/Kγ term in line 256 hinders complete understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. While there are some concerns regarding the experimental setup and certain technical details, the novelty of the approach, the strength of the theoretical analysis, and the promising empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically a fairer comparison in Figure 5, clarification of the optimal α value in Figure 3, explanation regarding the 1/Kγ term, and correction of the minor notational inconsistencies.
