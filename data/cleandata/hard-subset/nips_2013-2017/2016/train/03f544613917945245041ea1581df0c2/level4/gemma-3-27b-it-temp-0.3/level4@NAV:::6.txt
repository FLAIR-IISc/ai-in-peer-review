This paper introduces a method to accelerate the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration technique based on higher-order numerical integration. The approach necessitates parallel execution of two SG-MCMC chains with differing step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties, and validate these results through experiments. Synthetic Gaussian model experiments confirm the derived bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The work effectively contextualizes the proposed method within existing literature. 

However, I have two primary concerns. First, the novelty of the work may be somewhat incremental, as previous research [10] has analyzed the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC, and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been previously employed for invariant distribution approximation [23]. Second, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison lacks sufficient detail to fully assess its fairness. The parallel implementation of SG-MCMC, coupled with the correlated Brownian motion requiring inter-chain communication at each step, introduces overhead. The paper does not address the potential communication costs, and assumes they are negligible; this should be acknowledged. Furthermore, the resource allocation for SGRRLD and SGLD in the experiments is unclear. Specifically, it is important to clarify whether the single SGLD chain was implemented using the same computational resources as the two SGRRLD chains. If not, the comparison may not accurately reflect practical considerations. If so, the method for dividing resources between the two SGRRLD chains requires further explanation. Given that the computational cost of SG-MCMC is dominated by stochastic gradient computation, it is unclear how partitioning resources for gradient calculation in each SGRRLD chain can result in lower wall-clock time compared to dedicating full resources to a single SGLD chain. More detailed information regarding the implementation setup is needed. Finally, a comparison against running two independent SGLD chains in parallel (without communication) would be informative.
