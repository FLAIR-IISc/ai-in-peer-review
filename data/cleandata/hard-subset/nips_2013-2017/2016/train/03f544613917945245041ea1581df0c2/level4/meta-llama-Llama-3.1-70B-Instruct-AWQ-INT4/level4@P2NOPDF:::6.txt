This paper proposes a novel application of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The approach involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. Experimental results on synthetic Gaussian models validate the theoretical bounds, while large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data demonstrate the practical efficacy of the proposed algorithm over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, the paper presents a well-structured and coherent proposal, situating the new method within the context of existing SG-MCMC techniques. The contribution appears valid and meaningful, as it offers an innovative solution to enhance the convergence of SG-MCMC algorithms.

However, two primary concerns arise. Firstly, the novelty of the paper may be incremental, given that the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been employed for approximating invariant distributions [23]. Secondly, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison may not be entirely fair.

Notably, the proposed method involves a parallel implementation of SG-MCMC, which requires the two chains to communicate at every step. However, the authors seem to disregard the communication cost, assuming it to be negligible. It would be beneficial to acknowledge and discuss this aspect. Furthermore, the allocation of computing resources for SGRRLD and SGLD in the experiments is unclear. For instance, is the single SGLD chain implemented using the same resources as the two SGRRLD chains? If not, this may not be a fair comparison from a practical perspective. If yes, it is unclear how the resources are split between the two SGRRLD chains during the algorithm's execution. As the computation cost of SG-MCMC primarily stems from computing stochastic gradients, it is unclear how partial resource utilization in SGRRLD for computing gradients at each chain could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to full resource utilization in SGLD. Additional details on the implementation setup would be beneficial.

An alternative approach could be to run two SGLD chains embarrassingly (without communication) and compare their performance with the proposed method. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's practical advantages.

Minor comments include the duplicate mention of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" on lines 25 and 70.
