This paper introduces SGRRLD, an algorithm leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to mitigate bias in stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD). The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to SGLD. A synthetic experiment using a Linear Gaussian Model effectively illustrates the bias reduction benefits of the proposed method. While experiments on the MovieLens dataset show a marginal improvement in test RMSE, the statistical significance of this improvement is questionable.

The application of RR extrapolation to accelerate convergence of Monte Carlo estimates for stochastic differential equations (SDEs) has been previously established. This work extends this principle to stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms, theoretically proving that, under specified conditions, SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD with an equivalent step size. This speedup comes at the cost of maintaining two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is clearly described and appears readily implementable. The synthetic experiment provides strong evidence of SGRRLD's ability to reduce bias in the controlled setting of the Linear Gaussian model.

However, further investigation with more comprehensive real-world datasets is needed to substantiate the claim that this relatively minor modification to SGLD yields a significant bias reduction in practice. Regarding the theoretical foundations, I have not thoroughly verified the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2, but I question the constraints on the matrix *U* required to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can *U* be multi-modal? Intuitively, if gradient noise is uncorrelated, the two chains within SGRRLD should quickly become decorrelated, even with correlated Gaussian noise. Clarification is needed on how the combination of these chains effectively reduces the error order. The authors should comment on observed correlations between the chains in their real data experiments.

My primary concern relates to the experimental setup and interpretation of the real-data results. The authors assert that SGRRLD’s computational cost is comparable to SGLD due to parallelization. However, SGLD itself can be readily parallelized using multiple chains within the same timeframe. Therefore, a fair comparison would involve evaluating whether the same RMSE improvement is observed when comparing SGRRLD to two parallel chains of standard SGLD. 

Furthermore, the current comparison on the MovieLens dataset pits SGRRLD (with step sizes γ and γ/2) against SGLD (with step size γ). A more rigorous evaluation should include SGLD with a reduced step size of γ/2, potentially exhibiting lower bias and also run with two parallel chains. Ultimately, expanding the range of real-world datasets used for evaluation would significantly strengthen the conclusions.
