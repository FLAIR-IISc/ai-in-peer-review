Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC), with a focus on SGLD. The key idea is to run two SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon, couple their Gaussian noises, and form an extrapolated estimator that cancels the leading-order discretization bias. Building on results from numerical SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup), the authors analyze the resulting estimator under SG noise: they prove asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem (CLT), and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. In particular, with fixed step size, SGRRLD achieves bias O(γ2) and MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)), leading to an optimal finite-time MSE rate O(K^-4/5), versus O(K^-2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC. With decreasing step sizes γk ∝ k^-α, the optimal asymptotic rate for the estimator error is O(K^-2/5) at α = 1/5, again improving over SGLD’s O(K^-1/3). The paper also generalizes the extrapolation to other SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGHMC), and demonstrates empirical gains on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens).

This work is well positioned in the literature on scalable Bayesian sampling: it connects SGLD (Welling & Teh), convergence analysis of SG-MCMC (Teh–Thiéry–Vollmer; Chen–Ding–Carin), and variance-reduction/weak-error ideas from SDE numerics via RRE and duplicated diffusions. It also relates to higher-order SG-MCMC integrators, showing comparable accuracy without changing the base integrator.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a simple, principled extrapolation that imports mature ideas from SDE numerics into SG-MCMC, with clear guidance on noise coupling.
- Theory: asymptotic CLT with optimal coupling, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds that yield improved finite-time rates; discussion of optimal step-size schedules.
- Practicality: easy to implement, parallelizable (two chains), and compatible with existing SG-MCMC codebases; empirical results show consistent improvements.
- Generality: technique is applicable beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC) under mild conditions.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Assumptions: the CLT relies on smoothness (e.g., solution of the Poisson equation with high regularity) that may be strong in common ML posteriors; a discussion of how sensitive the conclusions are to weaker regularity would help.
- Gradient estimators: the method requires the two chains’ stochastic gradients to have the same distribution (e.g., identical mini-batch sizes). The practical cost or constraints of achieving this, and whether coupling mini-batch selections across chains helps, are not fully explored.
- Breadth of experiments: while results on Gaussian models and matrix factorization are persuasive, additional domains (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, Bayesian neural networks) and diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, calibration) would strengthen the empirical case.
- Cost model: the method doubles chains (one run at twice the frequency) but claims equal wall-clock time via parallelism. More detailed accounting of compute/memory overhead and scalability (e.g., multi-GPU/cluster latency for correlated noise and shared RNG streams) would be useful.
- Comparison set: for SGLD, a direct comparison against higher-order SG-MCMC integrators (beyond SGHMC-s) would better substantiate the “achieves higher-order accuracy with a first-order integrator” claim.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is careful and technically sound as far as can be assessed from the main text; proofs and conditions are deferred to the supplement. The role of correlated Gaussian increments is clearly justified and shown to be optimal in variance. The empirical results align with the theory (e.g., non-monotone bias at small γ due to 1/(Kγ)).
- Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Two minor presentation issues:
  1) Figure 1a shows a Gaussian with empirical mean/variance rather than the empirical posterior density; this should be clearly labeled to avoid confusion.
  2) Figure 2a’s non-monotone bias as γ decreases is counterintuitive; a brief explanation that 1/(Kγ) dominates at small γ (as reflected in the bounds) would help readers.
- Originality: Applying RRE to SG-MCMC with rigorous analysis and optimal coupling appears novel and bridges two communities. While the algorithmic idea is straightforward, the contribution lies in adapting the technique to stochastic gradients and establishing improved rates.
- Significance: The method offers a concrete accuracy–cost–parallelism trade-off that practitioners can adopt. Theoretical rate improvements are meaningful, and empirical gains are modest-to-strong depending on the task. The approach could stimulate further work (e.g., multi-level SG-MCMC, recursive RRE).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and practically useful idea with theoretical guarantees.
- Clear improvement in finite-time and asymptotic rates over first-order SG-MCMC.
- Solid empirical evidence and a clean parallelization story.
- Provides a valuable bridge between SDE numerics and large-scale Bayesian computation.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong regularity assumptions and limited discussion of robustness in non-ideal regimes.
- Experimental scope could be broader; comparisons to higher-order SGLD integrators are limited.
- Some practical details (mini-batch coupling, RNG/communication for noise correlation) are not fully addressed.

Suggestions to authors
- Clarify Figure 1a (Gaussian fit vs. empirical density) and add a brief note in the text.
- In Figure 2a, explain why bias can increase as γ decreases (finite-time trade-off via 1/(Kγ)).
- Provide implementation details for correlated Gaussian increments in parallel settings, and comment on whether coupling mini-batches across chains further reduces variance.
- Add ablations: uncorrelated vs. correlated noise; unequal mini-batch sizes; recursive (multi-level) extrapolation.
- Expand comparisons to include higher-order SGLD-type integrators; report ESS/time and calibration metrics.
- Offer practical guidance on choosing α and γ in typical data settings, possibly with adaptive or scheduled schemes.

Overall assessment
A clear, technically solid, and practically relevant paper that introduces a simple extrapolation technique to improve SG-MCMC. While the idea is incremental in spirit, the combination of rigorous analysis, practical applicability, and consistent empirical gains makes this a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing clarity and implementation details.
