This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to recent "big-data MCMC" methods, with a focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a standard numerical integration tool that has seen some notable applications to numerical stochastic differential equations (SDEs) in recent years. One of the paper's key strengths is that it provides a link between the MCMC literature and the SDE literature, which may be beneficial for both communities.

The authors introduce a new SGLD-RRE method, develop some theoretical results on the convergence of the new algorithm, and provide arguments for when SGLD-RRE improves on vanilla SGLD. They also present some numerical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The paper obtains impressive improvements on toy numerical examples and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale examples.

Overall, this is a solid paper with interesting links between two somewhat disconnected fields, interesting theoretical results, and good empirical performance of the novel proposed algorithm. The paper is clearly written and easy to read, and its technical soundness is evident. The method, which applies RRE to SGLD, is computationally convenient and relatively straightforward. While it may not revolutionize current practice, I believe practitioners will find this method useful.

The paper's minor flaws are primarily related to presentation. For instance, Figure 1a could be misleading, as it does not depict the empirical estimated posterior distribution. Instead, it shows a Gaussian with the empirical posterior mean and variance plugged in. This distinction should be clarified or emphasized for the benefit of unwary readers.

Additionally, the discussion of Figure 2a could be improved by explaining why the bias does not necessarily decrease as the step size decreases. This counterintuitive result warrants further discussion to help readers understand the underlying dynamics.

In conclusion, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a useful method and interesting insights. With some minor revisions to address the presentation issues mentioned above, it has the potential to be even more effective in conveying its message to the reader.
