This paper proposes using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The method requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analyses. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model are presented to support the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, while experiments on large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD.

Overall, this is a well-presented paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods. The authors effectively situate their work within the context of related prior art. However, I have two main concerns:

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The novelty of the contribution may be incremental. The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been applied to the approximation of invariant distributions in [23].

2.  **Practicality and Experimental Comparison:** The practical utility of the algorithm may be limited by its implementation requirements, and the experimental comparisons could be made more rigorous.
    *   The proposed algorithm is a parallel implementation, but the use of correlated noise necessitates communication between the two chains at every step. The authors do not appear to account for this communication cost, which could be significant in practice. This limitation should be explicitly mentioned.
    *   The paper lacks clarity on how computational resources were allocated between the proposed SGRRLD method and the baseline SGLD. For a fair comparison, the single SGLD chain should be allotted the same total resources as the two chains of SGRRLD combined. If this was the case, it is unclear how resources were divided between the two SGRRLD chains.
    *   Given that the primary computational cost of SG-MCMC lies in the stochastic gradient computation, it is unclear how using partial resources for each of the two SGRRLD chains can result in a shorter wall-clock time compared to using the full resources for a single SGLD chain. The authors should provide more details on the implementation setup to clarify this point.
    *   A compelling alternative baseline would be two independent SGLD chains running in an embarrassingly parallel fashion (i.e., without communication). A comparison against this setup would help to isolate the benefits of the RR extrapolation from the simple effect of using twice the computational resources.
