- Adapts Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC (SGRRLD): runs two coupled SGLD chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 to cancel leading discretization bias, with asymptotic CLT and non-asymptotic bounds showing improved bias/MSE rates and empirical wall-clock gains.

- Builds on prior SG-MCMC integrator analyses and RR for SDE invariant measures; novelty lies in applying RR to stochastic gradients and analyzing variance-minimizing coupling.

- Strength: conceptually simple wrapper that recovers higher-order accuracy without new integrators.

- Strength: solid theoretical development (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bounds) and matching experimental scalings.

- Strength: practical relevance demonstrated on synthetic experiments and a large-scale matrix factorization task; preliminary extension to SGHMC.

- Concern (novelty): contribution is incremental since higher-order SG-MCMC effects and RR for invariant measures are known, though adaptation to stochastic gradients is nontrivial.

- Concern (practicality/fairness): method requires coupled Gaussian increments, implying per-step synchronization/communication unless a shared-PRNG scheme is used, but implementation details are not given.

- Concern (resource fairness): unclear hardware/resources used; wall-clock comparisons may be unfair if SGRRLD used two cores while SGLD used one, since SGLD can also exploit parallelism.

- Concern (gradient parallelization): missing details on threads/cores, BLAS threading, memory/I/O that affect per-iteration latency when splitting resources across two chains.

- Concern (baseline): should compare to two independent parallel SGLD chains under the same computational budget to isolate extrapolation gains from parallelism.

- Quality: theoretical analysis appears careful and sound; recommend an empirical ablation comparing correlated vs independent Gaussian increments to confirm finite-sample variance benefits.

- Quality (stochastic gradients): need clarification on mini-batch assumptions—whether mini-batch indices are shared across chains and how gradient correlation is handled in practice.

- Clarity: generally well written and organized but missing implementation details; contains minor typos and a duplicated definition of ULA.

- Originality: moderate — a thoughtful, well-executed adaptation with new analysis but incremental relative to [10] and [23].

- Significance: potentially useful and easy to implement, but practical impact depends on communication overheads and fair comparisons; recommend comparing to higher-order SGLD variants.

- Pros: simple, general technique with rigorous analysis, provable and observed bias/MSE improvements, applicable to multiple SG-MCMC variants, and parallelizable.

- Cons: incremental novelty, unclear accounting for communication/resource fairness, and missing implementation details and ablation studies.

- Overall: well-executed paper with a clear idea, solid theory, and promising experiments; addressing practical concerns and adding suggested comparisons would significantly strengthen the contribution.
