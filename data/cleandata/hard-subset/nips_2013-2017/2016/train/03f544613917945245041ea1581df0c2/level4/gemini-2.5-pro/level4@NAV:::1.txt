This work leverages Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE), a well-established technique in numerical integration, for application to recent MCMC algorithms designed for large datasets, with a particular emphasis on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). A key strength of the paper is bridging the literature from the MCMC and numerical stochastic differential equation (SDE) communities, a connection that should prove mutually beneficial. The authors propose a novel SGLD-RRE algorithm, support it with theoretical guarantees on its convergence, and provide arguments for when it offers an advantage over the baseline SGLD approach. The experimental results show substantial gains on smaller synthetic examples and more moderate yet consistent improvements in large-scale applications.

Overall, this is a strong contribution and a well-executed paper. The valuable connection it forges between two relatively disparate fields is a highlight, complemented by interesting theoretical analysis and strong empirical validation of the proposed algorithm. The exposition is lucid and the technical content appears sound. The method itself is a direct adaptation of RRE to SGLD, a characteristic that makes it practical and computationally appealing. It is likely that practitioners will adopt this approach, even if it represents an incremental improvement rather than a revolutionary change to existing practice.

Minor points:
*   The presentation of Figure 1a could be misinterpreted; it displays not the direct empirical posterior distribution, but a Gaussian fit using the empirical posterior's mean and variance. This distinction should be made more explicit to avoid potential confusion.
*   Regarding Figure 2a, the paper would benefit from a short discussion on why the bias does not necessarily decrease as the stepsize is reduced. This result is somewhat counter-intuitive and warrants further explanation.
