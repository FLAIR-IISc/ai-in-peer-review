- Proposal: SGRRLD applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, coupling Brownian increments, and combining ergodic averages to reduce discretization bias.  
- Coupling: Optimal variance control relies on strongly correlated Brownian increments (ideal coupling Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2).  
- Theory: Authors prove consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds for the RR estimator.  
- Rates: Under appropriate tuning the paper claims MSE can decay as O(K−4/5), improving over first-order SG-MCMC O(K−2/3).  
- Comparison claim: The approach matches accuracy of higher-order integrators while keeping first-order simplicity.  
- Related work: Builds on SGLD, SG-MCMC high-order integrators, RR extrapolation for SDEs, and multilevel ideas.  
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments validate predicted bias/MSE scaling; large-scale matrix factorization shows improved RMSE with parallel execution; SGHMC-RR compares favorably to SGHMC-s.  
- Strength — simplicity: Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is natural, simple, and seemingly novel.  
- Strength — parallelism: Two-chain setup is parallel-friendly and aligns with modern compute resources.  
- Strength — solid theory: Clear theoretical results and explicit identification of the optimal coupling strategy.  
- Strength — empirical support: Experiments corroborate theory and show practical gains.  
- Weakness — step-size clarity: Confusing mix of constant vs decreasing γ results (CLT suggests α=1/5 → O(K−2/5) vs non-asymptotic O(K−4/5)); regimes and metrics need separation and reconciliation.  
- Weakness — algorithm inconsistencies: Eq. (5) and Supplement Algorithm 1 appear inconsistent (gradient evaluation index typo) and must be fixed; ensure Eq. (7) coupling is implemented exactly.  
- Weakness — experimental fairness: Wall-clock gains assume two cores; need comparisons under fixed total gradient evaluations or single-core wall-clock to isolate algorithmic benefits.  
- Weakness — coupling ablation: Need experiments comparing correlated vs independent Brownian increments and same vs different minibatches to demonstrate variance benefits.  
- Weakness — missing comparisons: SGLD comparisons should include higher-order SGLD integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015).  
- Weakness — assumptions: Key assumptions (smoothness, dissipativity, unbiasedness and moment bounds on stochastic gradients) should be listed explicitly in the main text.  
- Weakness — unclear variance claim: Clarify whether “variance O(γ4)” refers to asymptotic variance at stationarity or a term in the MSE decomposition and define the variance measure used.  
- Weakness — practical guidance: Provide guidance on minibatch coupling strategies (e.g., common random minibatching) and their feasibility/impact.  
- Minor issues: Typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., “variace”, “twice more time”, γ vs η) should be corrected.  
- Quality assessment: Technical development is careful and plausible but needs fixes on step-size discussion and algorithmic details.  
- Clarity assessment: Paper is dense; recommend consolidating assumptions, cleaning update equations, and adding concise pseudocode in the main text.  
- Originality assessment: Novel and nontrivial application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, with useful SGHMC extension and multilevel perspective.  
- Significance assessment: Simple, general, and practical method with clear benefits when parallel resources are available and potential for broad adoption.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel idea with strong theory, improved finite-time rates, parallelizable design, and empirical gains on large-scale tasks.  
- Cons for acceptance: Algorithmic/notation inconsistencies, experimental fairness tied to parallelism, missing higher-order SGLD comparisons, and insufficiently explicit assumptions.  
- Requested fixes/suggestions: (1) fix Eq. (5) and align Algorithm 1 with Eq. (7); (2) list explicit assumptions in the main text; (3) clarify “variance O(γ4)” and unify rate statements with concrete step-size recommendations; (4) add coupling and minibatch ablations; (5) include comparisons to high-order SGLD integrators and fixed-gradient-budget experiments.
