

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, and attains a mean squared error convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate under the same step size. The work builds directly on prior NIPS contributions, particularly Chen et al. (NIPS 2015), which established O(γ) bias bounds for SG-MCMC with first-order integrators. SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods without their computational overhead, while preserving parallelizability.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with formal proofs of asymptotic consistency, central limit theorems, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds (Theorems 1–2). The conditions on U (e.g., smoothness, growth constraints) are well-motivated and align with standard SG-MCMC assumptions (e.g., [10, 19]). The synthetic experiments on Gaussian models provide clear validation of bias reduction (e.g., variance bias reduced from ≈10⁻² to ≈10⁻⁴), while the MovieLens experiments demonstrate practical efficiency gains (≈2× speedup for equivalent RMSE).  
*Clarity:* The algorithm description (Section 3) and pseudo-code are exceptionally clear, with precise definitions of the parallel chains and correlated Brownian increments. The derivation of the RR extrapolation estimator (Equation 6) is logically presented.  
*Originality:* The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel within NIPS proceedings (no prior NIPS work explores this for SG-MCMC). It differs significantly from existing bias-reduction techniques (e.g., higher-order integrators [10] or preconditioning [8]) by requiring minimal modification to SGLD and preserving parallelism.  
*Significance:* The work addresses a key limitation of SG-MCMC (bias vs. variance trade-off) with demonstrable improvements in convergence rates. The algorithm’s compatibility with all SG-MCMC variants (beyond SGLD) and its suitability for distributed architectures make it broadly applicable to large-scale Bayesian inference problems.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical results assume identical mini-batch sizes for both chains (Section 3), but the practical impact of mini-batch correlation on bias requires empirical validation. The analysis does not address multi-modal target distributions (a common challenge in Bayesian inference), though this is a limitation of the broader SG-MCMC framework rather than SGRRLD specifically.  
*Significance:* The MovieLens results show RMSE improvements but do not conclusively demonstrate that SGRRLD’s advantage persists when compared to parallelized SGLD (e.g., running two SGLD chains at step size γ/2). A fairer comparison would benchmark SGRRLD against SGLD with two parallel chains at γ/2, as the current comparison (SGRRLD vs. single-chain SGLD at γ) may overstate the benefit.  
*Clarity:* The supplementary document should explicitly clarify the correlation mechanism between the two chains (Equation 7) in the main text, as this is critical for variance control. The experiment setup for the MovieLens datasets (e.g., step size selection) could be more transparently justified.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Groundbreaking application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC; rigorous theory with tighter convergence rates; clear implementation; significant efficiency gains in large-scale experiments.  
*Con:* Comparison with parallel SGLD chains requires refinement; limited exploration of multi-modal targets; step size choices in real data experiments need deeper justification.  

The paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS by resolving a persistent bias limitation in SG-MCMC with minimal computational overhead. While the real-data comparison could be strengthened, the theoretical and synthetic results are compelling, and the approach is well-positioned to advance large-scale Bayesian inference. The work merits acceptance with minor revisions to address the comparative experimental design.
