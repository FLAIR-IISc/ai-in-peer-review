Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon, and combines them to cancel the leading-order discretization bias. To keep variance controlled, the authors use correlated Brownian increments, following ideas from RR for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015). The paper focuses on SGLD but also sketches an extension to SGHMC. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a CLT for the RR estimator (with the correlation choice shown to minimize asymptotic variance), (ii) non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, and (iii) rates showing improvements over first-order SG-MCMC (e.g., bias O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)), MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)) for constant steps). The authors argue that SGRRLD attains the accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS 2015) while using only first-order updates. Experiments on a linear-Gaussian model and on large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support the claims. The work is grounded in classic MCMC and SDE literature (Roberts & Tweedie, 1996; Welling & Teh, 2011; Teh et al., 2016) and adapts RR extrapolation to the SG-MCMC setting in a practical way.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, easy to implement, and compatible with parallelism (two chains run in parallel).
- Solid theoretical development: consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds; clear identification of an optimal correlation structure for the Gaussian noise.
- Demonstrated improvements over SGLD in synthetic and real-data settings; initial evidence that RR confers benefits to SGHMC as well.
- Provides a path to higher accuracy without building bespoke higher-order discretizations.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Derivation of the optimal rates from the theorems is not fully transparent in the main text. In particular:
  - From Theorem 1: the stated rate O(K^{-((1−α)/2)∧(2α)}) and the claim that α=1/5 is optimal should be briefly derived in the paper (not only in the supplement). A one-paragraph sketch would help readers and reviewers verify the argument.
  - From Theorem 2: for constant γ, the bias/MSE bounds lead to γ∝K^{-1/3} (bias) and γ∝K^{-1/5} (MSE). Please show the simple optimization steps in the main text.
- Experimental fairness in matrix factorization: the wall-clock comparison leverages parallelism for SGRRLD (two chains as two processes) while SGLD is inherently sequential. On a single core, SGRRLD requires about 3× the work (two chains, with the finer chain taking two steps per coarse one). To make the comparison fair, please:
  - Report results in “equivalent sequential compute” time (or FLOPs), or
  - Include an additional plot where SGLD time is multiplied by 2/3 to reflect the difference in work, and discuss how this changes the gap.
- Figure 3 (bias vs. K with decreasing steps): the text states the optimal α for bias is 1/3, but the red curve (α=0.2) appears best, and the “optimal” curve shows noticeable fluctuations. Please check labeling and explain:
  - Whether the dependence on the estimated constants γ* for bias/MSE affects the apparent optimal α in finite samples.
  - Whether variability (lack of error bars) obscures the asymptotic trend; adding confidence bands or more runs could clarify.
- Small-step regime: the text notes that for small γ the term 1/(Kγ) dominates the bias/MSE for SGRRLD (as per Theorem 2). The same functional form holds for SGLD (O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) and O(γ^2 + 1/(Kγ)) in prior work). Please explain why this small-γ deterioration is not visible (or is less visible) for SGLD in your plots, or adjust K/γ ranges so both methods exhibit the theoretically predicted trade-off.
- Assumptions: the CLT proof assumes the Poisson equation solution g∈C^9 and other smoothness/ergodicity conditions. These are strong; please state the precise assumptions in the main text (not only in the supplement), give common examples where they hold, and discuss the role of stochastic gradient noise (the claim that Gaussian noise dominates deserves a brief justification or an empirical check).

Quality:
- The analysis is technically sound overall, with clear alignment to prior SDE RR theory and recent SG-MCMC results. Some steps (rate derivations, assumption statements) should be made more explicit in the main text. The correlated noise construction and the requirement of equal minibatch distributions are important and well-motivated.

Clarity:
- Generally well written, but a few notational and editorial issues (listed below) hamper readability. A concise algorithm box for SGRRLD in the main text would help.

Originality:
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with a variance-minimizing coupling is novel in the ML literature. The resulting bounds and CLT tailored to SG-MCMC appear original.

Significance:
- The method is simple, practical, and potentially impactful: many existing SG-MCMC samplers could adopt RR to reduce bias without redesigning integrators. The empirical results are promising, though fair compute accounting is needed for a definitive assessment.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple and broadly applicable idea with theoretical guarantees.
- Clear improvements in both asymptotic and finite-time bounds.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and large-scale applications.
- Bridges higher-order accuracy and first-order implementations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Compute fairness in the main experimental comparison needs correction/qualification.
- Some derivations and assumptions are buried in the supplement.
- Minor inconsistencies in figures and text (optimal α, small-γ behavior) need clarification.

Specific comments and minor issues:
- Eq. (1): you switch from θ to ϑ for the SDE state. Please use a single symbol consistently (or explain the distinction).
- First use of “RR” (line 94): define Richardson–Romberg at first mention.
- Define ΓK and Γ(n)K (line 131) in the main text before using them.
- “Smooth function f” (line 160): state the exact smoothness class (e.g., f∈C^p with polynomial growth) required by the theorems.
- Figure reference (line 249): bias vs. time vs. dimension—appears to refer to Figure 1(c), not 1(b). Please correct.
- Use boldface (or another clear convention) for vectors, and be consistent throughout.
- Clarify in Section 3 that the two stochastic gradient estimators must have the same distribution (e.g., same minibatch size and sampling scheme) to avoid introducing additional bias; state whether the minibatches are shared or independent across chains in experiments.
- Briefly explain how to implement the correlated Brownian increments in practice when using pseudorandom number generators.

Overall recommendation:
This is a strong submission with a simple, well-motivated idea, sound analysis, and promising results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the rate derivations in the main text, addressing the compute fairness in experiments, and fixing the noted inconsistencies.
