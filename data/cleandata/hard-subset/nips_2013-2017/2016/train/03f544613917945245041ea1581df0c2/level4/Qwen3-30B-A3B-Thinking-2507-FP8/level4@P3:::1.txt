

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the connection between stochastic differential equations (SDEs) and MCMC methods, particularly extending the work of Talay and Tubaro [21] on RRE for SDEs to the stochastic gradient MCMC setting. While SGLD and related methods have been widely studied in recent NIPS papers (e.g., [1, 6, 10]), this paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating how RRE can be adapted to SG-MCMC to achieve better convergence rates without requiring higher-order integrators.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations with three key contributions: (1) a central limit theorem showing asymptotic normality of the estimator with variance minimized when using perfectly correlated Brownian increments; (2) non-asymptotic bounds showing SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³); and (3) empirical validation across synthetic and real-world datasets. The theoretical results are carefully derived, building upon previous work on SG-MCMC convergence [10, 18-20] while extending it to the RRE framework. The authors also provide a clear explanation of how to implement the method, including the necessary correlation between Brownian increments to control variance.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. The authors properly acknowledge the connection to existing literature on RRE for SDEs [21, 23] while clearly demonstrating how their extension to SG-MCMC is novel. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproduction, and the empirical results consistently support the theoretical claims across multiple datasets and problem sizes. The method is also computationally attractive, maintaining the parallelizable nature of SG-MCMC while improving convergence rates.

The paper's clarity is excellent. The organization is logical, moving from background to method, theory, and experiments. The mathematical notation is well-defined, and the explanations of the theoretical results are accessible to readers familiar with MCMC and stochastic calculus. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproduction.

For originality, this work makes a meaningful contribution by bridging two areas: the numerical analysis of SDEs (where RRE has been used) and the emerging field of scalable MCMC methods. While the core idea of applying RRE to SGLD is conceptually straightforward, the technical execution is nontrivial, and the authors demonstrate that the approach achieves theoretical accuracy comparable to higher-order integrators without their computational complexity. The method's generality—applicable to any SG-MCMC algorithm meeting certain conditions—further enhances its novelty.

Regarding significance, SGRRLD provides a practical improvement to existing scalable MCMC methods that practitioners could immediately apply. The empirical results on large-scale matrix factorization tasks demonstrate meaningful improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency. While this is unlikely to revolutionize the field, it represents a valuable incremental advance that addresses a real limitation in current SG-MCMC methods (bias in the estimator).

Minor weaknesses include: (1) The claim in Figure 1(a) that the Gaussian approximation is the "empirical estimated posterior distribution" is slightly misleading; the figure actually shows a Gaussian with the empirical mean and variance, not the empirical posterior density. This should be clarified in the text. (2) The behavior in Figure 2(a) where bias doesn't monotonically decrease with smaller step sizes could benefit from more detailed explanation, as this is counterintuitive to readers unfamiliar with the theoretical bounds. (3) While the paper mentions the potential for recursive application of RRE [13], the practical implications of this extension aren't explored in the experiments.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a technically sound contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The theoretical results are well-supported, the method is clearly explained, and the empirical validation is thorough. The paper is well-written and addresses a meaningful problem in the current literature. While the core idea is relatively simple (applying RRE to SGLD), the technical execution is sophisticated and the results are compelling. The method has clear potential for practical application in large-scale Bayesian inference tasks.

The authors have made a significant contribution by connecting the SDE and MCMC literature in a way that provides immediate practical benefits. The paper is suitable for publication at NIPS, and I recommend acceptance with the minor revisions noted above.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear writing, meaningful empirical results, practical utility, well-motivated extension of existing work.
Con: Minor figure misrepresentation, need for better explanation of non-monotonic behavior in bias, limited exploration of recursive RRE application.
