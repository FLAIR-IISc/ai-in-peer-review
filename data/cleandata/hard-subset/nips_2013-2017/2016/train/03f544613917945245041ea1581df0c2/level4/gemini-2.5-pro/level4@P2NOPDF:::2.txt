This paper introduces an extension of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias arising from noisy gradient estimates. The resulting algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), is shown theoretically to possess an asymptotically lower bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) than standard SGLD.

The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a well-established technique for improving numerical integration, to the domain of Stochastic Gradient MCMC is a novel and interesting contribution. The paper is well-written, and the proposed algorithm is described clearly and appears straightforward to implement. The theoretical analysis, which proves that SGRRLD can achieve a faster convergence rate than SGLD, is a significant part of this work. The value of the method is demonstrated on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model, where SGRRLD shows a clear advantage over SGLD in reducing estimation bias.

Despite these strengths, the paper's primary weakness lies in its empirical evaluation, which does not provide compelling evidence for the practical utility of the proposed method on real-world problems. My main concerns are centered on the experimental design and the limited scope of the experiments.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Incomplete Experimental Baselines:** The central argument for SGRRLD's efficiency is that it requires running two Markov chains but can be parallelized to match the runtime of a single SGLD chain. However, the experimental comparisons are not entirely fair given this premise.
    *   A more appropriate baseline would be to run two *independent* SGLD chains in parallel for the same amount of time and average their posteriors. This is a common and simple way to improve estimates. It is crucial to show that SGRRLD outperforms this stronger, parallelized SGLD baseline, not just a single SGLD chain.
    *   The SGRRLD algorithm uses two different step sizes, γ and γ/2. The experiments compare its performance against SGLD using only step size γ. Since a smaller step size like γ/2 is known to reduce the bias in SGLD, a critical comparison is missing. The authors should compare SGRRLD against SGLD running with the smaller step size γ/2. To ensure a fair comparison of computational cost, this SGLD baseline could also be run with two parallel chains.

2.  **Limited Real-Data Validation:** The synthetic experiment successfully illustrates the algorithm's bias-reduction properties in an idealized setting. However, the only real-world experiment, conducted on the MovieLens dataset for matrix factorization, shows only a marginal improvement in test RMSE. This result is not convincing enough to demonstrate a significant practical advantage. To strengthen the paper, the authors should apply their method to a wider variety of real-world models and datasets where SGLD is commonly used. This would help to establish whether the observed gains are consistent and meaningful across different problem domains.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Conditions for Theoretical Results:** Could you please clarify the conditions that the potential function U must satisfy for Theorems 1 and 2 to hold? For example, does the theoretical analysis apply to multi-modal potential functions, which are prevalent in many challenging machine learning problems?

2.  **Intuition Behind the Mechanism:** The RR extrapolation's effectiveness relies on the correlation between the two chains, which is induced by using the same sequence of Gaussian random numbers. However, the noise from mini-batch gradients is independent for each chain. This independent noise source could cause the trajectories of the two chains to diverge, potentially weakening the effect of the extrapolation. Could you provide more intuition on how the combination of the two chains effectively reduces the error order in the presence of this independent gradient noise? Furthermore, did you empirically measure the correlation between the states of the two parallel chains in your experiments?

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting idea. However, its practical significance is not yet established due to weaknesses in the experimental evaluation. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide results from more thorough experiments with stronger, more appropriate baselines on a diverse set of real-world tasks.
