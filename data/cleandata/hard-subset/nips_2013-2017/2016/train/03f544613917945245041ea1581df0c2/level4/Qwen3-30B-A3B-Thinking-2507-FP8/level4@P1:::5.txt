

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel method for reducing bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the SGLD framework by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, which requires running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes. The key insight is that by appropriately combining these two chains using RR extrapolation, the bias can be reduced from O(γ) to O(γ²) while controlling the variance through correlated Brownian increments. The paper demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate and matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature, which has seen numerous extensions since Welling and Teh's seminal SGLD work. While prior work has explored higher-order integrators to improve convergence rates [10], this paper demonstrates that a first-order integrator with RR extrapolation can achieve comparable accuracy without the computational overhead of higher-order methods. The authors effectively connect their work to previous contributions by showing that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order methods while maintaining the simplicity and parallelizability of first-order approaches.

The theoretical analysis is strong and well-executed. The paper provides formal proofs for asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The key technical contribution is the demonstration that the variance of the RR estimator can be controlled through correlated Brownian increments, with the optimal choice being perfect correlation (as in Equation 7). The authors also provide careful analysis of the optimal step size schedule, showing that α = 1/5 yields the best convergence rate. The theoretical results are well-supported by both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test SGRRLD on a simple Gaussian model where the posterior is analytically known, demonstrating significant bias reduction compared to SGLD. They further validate their approach on a large-scale matrix factorization task using MovieLens datasets, showing that SGRRLD achieves comparable or better accuracy than SGLD in the same wall-clock time. The parallel nature of SGRRLD is particularly well-suited for distributed architectures, and the authors correctly note that the two chains can be run independently, making the method naturally parallelizable.

The paper's main strengths include:
1. Clear theoretical justification for the improved convergence rates
2. Thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets
3. Strong connection to prior work on SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation
4. Practical implementation details for real-world applications
5. Well-structured presentation of the algorithm and its theoretical properties

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed:
1. The experimental section does not compare SGRRLD with higher-order integrator methods [10], which the authors claim SGRRLD theoretically matches. This comparison would strengthen the empirical validation of the theoretical claims.
2. There is an inconsistency in the step size notation between the text and the equations. Specifically, the paper states "η₂ₖ₋₁ = η₂ₖ = γₖ/2 for k ≥ 1" but the description of the algorithm in Equation (5) suggests different step size patterns.
3. The supplementary algorithm (Algorithm 1) appears inconsistent with the formal description in Equation (5), which could confuse readers attempting to implement the method.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC with a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach. The idea of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and well-motivated, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing first-order methods. The paper meets all the NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel combination of existing techniques (Originality), and provides significant improvements in convergence rates that are likely to be adopted by practitioners (Significance).

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple modification to SGLD can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods while maintaining the computational efficiency of first-order approaches. This work has clear potential for practical applications in large-scale Bayesian inference where computational efficiency is paramount.

In summary, this is a strong paper that advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC. With minor revisions to address the inconsistencies noted above and the addition of a comparison with higher-order integrators in the experiments, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. As it stands, it provides a theoretically rigorous and empirically validated method for improving the convergence rates of SG-MCMC algorithms, making it suitable for acceptance at NIPS.
