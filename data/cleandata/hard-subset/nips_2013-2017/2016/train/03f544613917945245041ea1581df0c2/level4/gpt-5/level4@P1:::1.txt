This paper applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to scalable Bayesian sampling, with a focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a classical tool in numerical analysis and has been successfully used for SDE discretizations. The key contribution here is to bridge SG-MCMC with this SDE literature: the authors propose a two-chain extrapolated estimator (SGRRLD), give theoretical guarantees (including asymptotic consistency and a central limit theorem), and provide conditions under which it improves upon vanilla SGLD. Empirically, they show substantial gains on synthetic problems and modest but consistent improvements on large-scale matrix factorization, all while preserving computational convenience.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on SGLD (Welling & Teh, 2011) and connects to the Talay–Tubaro line of work on weak error expansions and RR extrapolation for SDEs. To my knowledge, RR has not been applied to SG-MCMC before. The paper thus offers a useful cross-pollination between numerical SDE analysis and scalable Bayesian sampling.

Strengths
- Conceptual: A simple, principled idea—use RR extrapolation over two SGLD chains with different step sizes—to reduce bias without incurring large variance, aided by correlated Gaussian noise.
- Theory: The authors establish asymptotic consistency and a CLT for the extrapolated estimator and provide non-asymptotic bounds. They also argue when/why SGRRLD improves over SGLD.
- Practicality: The method is easy to implement, uses first-order integrators, and readily parallelizes by running two chains concurrently.
- Empirics: Clear gains on toy problems and consistent improvements on large-scale matrix factorization. The method appears robust and computationally friendly.

Weaknesses
- Scope: The core method is a fairly straightforward application of RR extrapolation to SGLD. While elegant and useful, it is not a radical change to current practice.
- Impact: Improvements on large-scale tasks are modest (though consistent), so the practical significance will depend on application tolerances and compute budgets.

Quality
- The paper appears technically sound. The theoretical statements align with known properties of RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC, and the empirical results support the claims.

Clarity
- Very clearly written and well organized. The exposition effectively connects the two literatures and motivates the design choices (e.g., correlated Brownian increments).

Originality
- While RR is classical, applying it in this SG-MCMC setting and working through the variance control via correlation is, to my knowledge, new. The combination is natural but nontrivial.

Significance
- The approach should interest both researchers and practitioners who use SG-MCMC. It offers a practical accuracy boost at little additional engineering cost, especially in parallel settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, principled idea that meaningfully improves a widely used baseline.
- Solid theoretical analysis (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bounds).
- Empirical results consistent with the theory and computationally realistic.
- Bridges two communities (SG-MCMC and numerical SDEs).

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological novelty is incremental (a direct application of RR).
- Large-scale gains, while present, are not dramatic.

Minor comments
- Figure 1(a): The plotted “posterior” is a Gaussian with the empirical posterior mean and variance, not the full empirical posterior. Please clarify this prominently in the caption/text to avoid confusion.
- Figure 2(a): It would help to explain why the bias does not necessarily decrease monotonically with decreasing step size. This is counterintuitive for some readers; a brief discussion referencing the finite-time trade-off (e.g., the 1/(Kγ) term) would improve clarity.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed paper that introduces a simple and effective idea, backed by theory and experiments, and likely to be adopted by practitioners. While not revolutionary, it makes a clear, useful contribution and strengthens the connection between SG-MCMC and numerical SDE methods. I recommend acceptance.
