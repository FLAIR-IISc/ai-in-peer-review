This paper applies a sequence acceleration technique—Richardson–Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator—to improve the convergence of SG-MCMC. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated injected Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model validate the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, and large-scale matrix factorization on real data shows practical gains over standard SGLD. Overall, the technique and presentation are solid. The paper makes a legitimate contribution to SG-MCMC and situates the method well relative to prior work.

I have two main concerns:
1) Novelty. The contribution may be incremental. The impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has already been analyzed in [10], and RR extrapolation has been used to approximate invariant distributions in [23].
2) Practicality and fairness of experimental comparisons. The algorithm is a parallel SG-MCMC implementation, and the use of correlated Brownian motions requires per-iteration communication between the two chains. The paper appears to ignore this communication overhead; it would be better to acknowledge and discuss it. Moreover, the allocation of computing resources in the comparisons between SGRRLD and SGLD is unclear. For example, does the single SGLD chain use the same total resources as the two SGRRLD chains? If not, the comparison may be unfair from a practical perspective. If yes, how are resources divided between the two SGRRLD chains in the algorithm? Since most of the computational cost in SG-MCMC comes from stochastic gradient evaluations, it is not obvious how allocating only a fraction of the resources to each SGRRLD chain could result in lower wall-clock time than dedicating full resources to a single SGLD chain. Please add implementation details to clarify this setup. In addition, a natural alternative baseline is to run two independent SGLD chains in embarrassingly parallel (without communication); could you compare against this?
