### Summary

This paper introduces a method to improve the convergence rates of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The proposed algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step-size schedules (γ and γ/2) and linearly combines their outputs. This combination is designed to cancel the leading-order error term, thereby reducing the bias of the estimator. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), which demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than standard SGLD. The theoretical contributions are supported by experiments on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a classic numerical analysis technique, to the modern SG-MCMC framework is a novel and well-motivated contribution. It provides a principled and effective method for reducing the discretization bias, which is a key limiting factor for the accuracy of many SG-MCMC methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is supported by solid theoretical results. The authors establish that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE, showing that SGRRLD achieves a superior convergence rate for the MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD. This demonstrates a clear theoretical advantage.

3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed scheme is elegant and simple to implement, requiring minimal modifications to existing SGLD code. The method is also naturally suited for parallel execution, making it computationally efficient on modern multi-core architectures, a claim well-supported by the experimental results.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

Despite the strengths, there are several points that require clarification, primarily concerning the experimental setup and the connection between the theory and the empirical results.

1.  **Fairness of Computational Cost Comparison:** The experimental comparison in Figure 5 measures performance against wall-clock time, leveraging a parallel implementation. However, the total computational cost of SGRRLD is higher than that of SGLD. For each "outer" iteration, SGRRLD performs three gradient update steps (one for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain). On a single-core machine, SGRRLD would take approximately three times longer than SGLD per iteration. To provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's efficiency, it would be fairer to also compare the methods based on the total number of gradient computations. For example, if the time axis for SGLD in Figure 5 were compressed by a factor of three (or 2/3 as stated in my original thoughts to be precise, as SGLD runs for 1 step while SGRRLD runs for 1+2=3 steps in parallel, making it 1 unit of time vs 1 unit of time. But if serialized, SGLD takes 1 unit of time and SGRRLD takes 3 units of time. So SGLD is 3 times faster per iteration on single core. Let's make this point clearer: the wall-clock time comparison in Figure 5 is valid *only* on a parallel architecture. To make the comparison fair in terms of total computational budget, the SGLD curve should be compared against an SGRRLD curve that takes 3x the wall-clock time. Rephrasing the reviewer's original point more directly: if you scaled the SGLD time axis to reflect this (i.e., multiply by 1/3), the performance gap between the two methods would shrink considerably. A discussion of this trade-off is necessary.

2.  **Discrepancy in Experimental Results (Figure 3):** There appears to be an inconsistency between the text and the bias plot in Figure 3. The text following Theorem 2 states that the optimal rate for the bias is achieved with α = 1/3. However, in the plot, the red curve corresponding to α = 0.2 seems to achieve the lowest bias. Could you please clarify this? Additionally, this curve exhibits significant fluctuations. Is there a reason for this noisy behavior, which seems at odds with a stable convergence rate?

3.  **Clarity on Error Term Dominance:** On line 256, the authors state that when the step size γ is too small, the 1/(Kγ) term dominates the error for SGRRLD. This is an interesting finding. To provide a stronger comparative analysis, could you explain why a similar effect is not observed or discussed for the baseline SGLD algorithm in the experiments?

4.  **Derivation of Optimal Rates:** While the theorems are stated clearly, the paper would benefit from a more detailed derivation of the optimal convergence rates. For instance, after Theorem 1, it is claimed that a step size schedule γk ∝ k⁻α yields a rate of O(K⁻((¹⁻α)/²)∧(²α)), with an optimal choice of α = 1/5. Including a brief derivation showing how this expression is obtained and optimized would improve the paper's self-containedness and make the theoretical contribution easier to follow.
