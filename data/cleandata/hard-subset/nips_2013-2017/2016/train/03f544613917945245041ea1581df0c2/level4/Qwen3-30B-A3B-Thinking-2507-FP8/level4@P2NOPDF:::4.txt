

This paper introduces a Richardson-Romberg scheme to enhance the convergence rates of stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, improving both bias and mean squared error (MSE) rates. The proposed method, SGRRLD, requires minimal implementation changes to standard SG-MCMC, making it practically accessible. The theoretical foundation is robust, with well-supported convergence guarantees. However, several critical issues require clarification before acceptance.

The most pressing concern pertains to the derivation of convergence rates. The paper claims improved rates via Theorem 1, but the argument for how these rates are achieved (notably the role of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation) lacks sufficient detail. Specifically, the connection between the asymptotic bias terms and the stated rates needs explicit justification in the proofs. Without this, the theoretical contribution remains partially opaque.

A significant practical concern arises from the matrix factorization experiments (Figure 5). The authors report SGRRLD's superior performance over SGLD, but the time comparison is misleading. SGRRLD uses two chains (one with twice the steps), requiring approximately three times the computational time of SGLD for equivalent step counts. To fairly compare performance at identical computational budgets, the SGLD runtime in Figure 5 should be scaled by a factor of 2/3 (i.e., SGLD’s time is effectively reduced to 2/3 of its reported value). This adjustment would substantially narrow the observed performance gap, potentially undermining the claimed advantage. The authors should revisit this analysis with a time-normalized metric.

Figure 3 (bias vs. step size) reveals additional inconsistencies. The optimal convergence rate is visually achieved at α=0.2 (red curve), contradicting the text’s assertion that α=1/3 is optimal. Moreover, the optimal curve exhibits pronounced fluctuations, suggesting instability that may invalidate the theoretical rate. The authors should address why this discrepancy exists and whether the fluctuations indicate a breakdown in the convergence assumptions.

Regarding line 256’s claim that "for sufficiently small step sizes, the term 1/(Kγ) dominates," it is unclear why this does not manifest in SGLD’s behavior. Given SGLD’s similar step-size dependence, this omission warrants explanation to avoid undermining the proposed method’s theoretical novelty.

Minor but necessary corrections include:  
- Equation (1): Use consistent notation (θ vs. θ̃).  
- Line 94: Define "RR" (Richardson-Romberg) upon first use.  
- Line 131: Define Γ_K.  
- Line 160: Specify the smoothness condition for function f (e.g., Lipschitz gradients).  
- Line 249: Figure 1(b) labels "Bias vs. time," but the axis should match Figure 1(c) ("Bias vs. dimension").  
- Vectors (e.g., θ) should be bolded per standard notation.

In summary, the paper presents a promising extension to SG-MCMC with strong theoretical underpinnings and practical simplicity. However, the experimental fairness, theoretical derivations, and figure inconsistencies must be resolved. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. The core idea is valuable, but current presentation risks overstating the empirical and theoretical gains.
