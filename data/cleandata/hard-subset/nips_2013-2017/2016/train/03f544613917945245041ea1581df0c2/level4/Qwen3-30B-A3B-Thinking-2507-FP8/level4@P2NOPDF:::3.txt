

This paper addresses a key limitation of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods: their asymptotic bias, which prevents exact convergence to the posterior distribution. The authors propose a simple yet effective solution using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias. The core technique involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel: Chain 1 uses step size γ, while Chain 2 uses γ/2 and runs for twice as many steps. Crucially, the Gaussian noise injected at step k in Chain 1 (denoted αₖ) and in Chains 2 at steps 2k-1 and 2k (denoted β₂ₖ₋₁ and β₂ₖ) are correlated via αₖ = (β₂ₖ₋₁ + β₂ₖ)/√2. The posterior expectation of a function f is then estimated as 2·E₂[f] - E₁[f], where E₁ and E₂ are estimates from Chains 1 and 2. This estimator achieves bias O(γ²) compared to O(γ) for either chain alone, with a corresponding reduction in mean squared error (MSE). The method requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms and is broadly applicable across the SG-MCMC family, making it a potentially significant contribution to the field.  

The novelty lies in adapting a well-established numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg extrapolation) to SG-MCMC, where it has not been widely adopted despite its potential. The paper is clearly written and the technical exposition is accessible.  

**Strengths:**  
- **Simplicity and generality:** The approach is straightforward to implement without altering the core SG-MCMC algorithm, enhancing its practical appeal.  
- **Theoretical rigor:** The bias and MSE reductions are rigorously proven under standard SG-MCMC assumptions (e.g., π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)).  
- **Impact potential:** The method’s broad applicability across SG-MCMC variants suggests immediate relevance to the community.  

**Major Concerns and Suggestions:**  
1. **Scope of applicability:** The paper claims generality but does not explicitly state whether the method applies to *all* SG-MCMC algorithms. The bias bound in Section 2.2 (π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)) is necessary, but are there additional constraints (e.g., on the noise structure or step-size schedule)? Clarify the exact conditions required for validity.  

2. **Computational efficiency:** Running Chain 2 for twice as many steps as Chain 1 leads to significant idle time for Chain 1. Could a three-chain strategy (one chain at γ, two chains at γ/2, all running for N steps) mitigate this? If so, how does it affect estimator variance? This would improve practicality without sacrificing bias reduction.  

3. **Extension to multiple step sizes:** Is the method extendable to more than two chains (e.g., using γ, γ/2, γ/4)? If not, what prevents such an extension?  

4. **Noise correlation mechanics:** The paper states that noise correlation is essential but does not specify how batch-level correlation affects variance. Is the minimal variance achieved when Chain 1’s k-th step uses the *same* mini-batch as Chains 2’s 2k-1 and 2k steps? Clarify this relationship.  

5. **Experimental validation:**  
   - The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should include convergence curves for SG-MCMC methods (SGLD, SGRRLD) *and* SGD. Comparing to SGD is critical to demonstrate that the Bayesian approach provides tangible benefits over non-Bayesian baselines in relevant settings.  
   - Bayesian matrix factorization is not commonly used in practice; showing that SGRRLD outperforms SGD (not just SGLD) in a scenario where Bayesian uncertainty matters would strengthen the practical case for the method.  

6. **Accessibility:** While the method is simple, including a concise algorithm box early in the paper would greatly aid practitioners. Many readers may skip theoretical sections, so a clear pseudocode would lower the barrier to adoption.  

**Minor Notes:**  
- The claim that "the method applies to a large class of SG-MCMC algorithms" should be reinforced by explicitly listing examples (e.g., SGLD, SGHMC) where it is applicable.  
- The noise correlation formula (αₖ = (β₂ₖ₋₁ + β₂ₖ)/√2) should use consistent notation (e.g., β instead of beta).  

**Overall:** This work presents a compelling, practical solution to a fundamental limitation of SG-MCMC. The core idea is elegant, the theoretical foundation is sound, and the method’s simplicity makes it highly adoptable. Addressing the concerns above—particularly clarifying applicability conditions, optimizing computational efficiency, and strengthening experimental comparisons—would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. The proposed technique is a valuable addition to the SG-MCMC toolkit and merits publication with minor revisions.
