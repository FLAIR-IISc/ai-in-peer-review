This paper applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce the bias arising from noisy gradient estimates. The resulting algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), is shown to have an asymptotically lower bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) than SGLD.

The application of RR extrapolation to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs is known in the literature, and its application to SG-MCMC algorithms is a valuable contribution. The paper proves that under certain conditions, SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD for a given step size, at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is described clearly and appears straightforward to implement. Experiments on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model effectively demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD in reducing bias for that particular problem.

However, the experimental validation and theoretical clarity could be improved in several areas:

**1. Experimental Evaluation:**
*   The experiments on the MovieLens dataset show only a slight improvement in test RMSE, and the significance of these results is debatable. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including more real-world data experiments to demonstrate that this simple modification to SGLD consistently yields a significant bias reduction in practice.
*   The comparison to SGLD is not entirely fair. The authors argue that SGRRLD's computational time is comparable to a single SGLD chain by running the two SGRRLD chains in parallel. However, one could also run two parallel SGLD chains within the same time budget. A more compelling baseline would be to compare SGRRLD's performance against the combined estimate from two parallel SGLD chains.
*   The choice of step sizes for comparison could also be improved. The experiments compare SGRRLD (using step sizes γ and γ/2) against SGLD (using step size γ). Since SGLD with a smaller step size (γ/2) would also have a lower bias, a more appropriate comparison would be against two parallel chains of SGLD running with step size γ/2.

**2. Questions on Theory and Intuition:**
*   While I have not checked the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 in detail, it is unclear what conditions the potential function `U` must satisfy for both theorems to hold. For instance, can `U` be multi-modal?
*   Could the authors provide more intuition on how combining the two chains reduces the order of the error? Intuitively, if the per-iteration gradient noises are uncorrelated, one might expect the two chains to become uncorrelated quickly, even if the driving Gaussian noises are correlated. In the real-world experiments, did the authors observe a strong correlation between the two chains?
