- Paper proposes using Richardson–Romberg extrapolation by running two correlated SG-MCMC chains to accelerate convergence, with asymptotic and non-asymptotic theory and experiments on synthetic and real data.  
- Contribution is clearly presented and connects to SG-MCMC literature, showing empirical gains over standard SGLD in matrix factorization.  
- Main concern 1 — Novelty: the contribution seems incremental given prior work analyzing integrators [10] and RR extrapolation for invariant measures [23]; need clarification of conceptual/technical advances beyond those.  
- Specify what new error analysis is specific to the stochastic-gradient setting, which aspects of coupling/variance reduction are novel, and which insights/guarantees are not available in [10, 23].  
- Main concern 2 — Practicality/fairness: the method requires correlating Brownian noise across two chains, implying per-step communication; the paper assumes zero communication cost and should quantify this overhead.  
- Clarify compute-resource allocation and fairness: did the single SGLD chain use the same total compute as the two SGRRLD chains combined; if matched, how were resources split (cores/GPUs, batch sizes, bandwidth)?  
- Explain why two partially-resourced chains would achieve lower wall-clock time than one fully resourced chain given gradient-evaluation dominates cost; provide hardware/parallelization details, minibatch sizes, synchronization frequency, wall-clock times, total gradient evaluations, and communication overhead.  
- Recommend running baseline: two independent SGLD chains (no communication) and compare per-chain, aggregated sample performance, and results under matched gradient budgets and wall-clock time to separate RR benefits from parallelism.  
- Minor: remove duplicate definition of “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” (appears twice).
