- Introduces Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SG-MCMC: run two correlated-noise chains (step sizes γ and γ/2, second runs twice as long) to reduce estimator bias from O(γ) to O(γ²).  
- The method is simple, easy to implement, minimally invasive to existing SG-MCMC algorithms, and potentially broadly applicable.  
- Novelty lies in applying RR extrapolation to the ML/stochastic-gradient MCMC community, which appears new despite RR being classical in numerical analysis.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly presented.  
- Question: What additional conditions (beyond πγ(f)=π(f)+Cγ+O(γ²)) are required for applicability to all SG-MCMC methods?  
- Question: Can the technique extend to other approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling Metropolis-Hastings) and to distributed SGLD?  
- Question: Running one chain twice as long is computationally inefficient—would using three chains with equal lengths (one at γ, two at γ/2) be a viable alternative and how would it affect variance?  
- Question: Is there a generalization to more than two step sizes (higher-order extrapolation)?  
- Question: Impact of correlation between mini-batches is unclear—does using the same mini-batch across paired iterations minimize variance, or is the effect negligible?  
- Experimental suggestion: Run the matrix factorization experiment to convergence and include SGD as a baseline to better assess practical benefits and competitiveness.  
- Practical suggestion: Add an algorithm box/pseudocode early in the paper to help practitioners implement the method without deep theoretical engagement.  
- Argument for acceptance: The method provides a simple, effective bias reduction with solid theoretical backing and empirical results.  
- Argument for acceptance: The approach is generally applicable across many SG-MCMC algorithms and could have significant impact.  
- Argument against acceptance: Doubling computational cost by running two chains may limit applicability in some settings.  
- Argument against acceptance: The effect of correlated mini-batches needs further study.  
- Argument against acceptance: The matrix factorization comparison lacks an SGD baseline, weakening practical evaluation.
