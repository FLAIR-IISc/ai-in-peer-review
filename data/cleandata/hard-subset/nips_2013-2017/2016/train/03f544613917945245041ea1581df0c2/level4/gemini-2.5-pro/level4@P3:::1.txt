This paper introduces a novel approach to reduce the bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE). Focusing on the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm, the authors propose a new method called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to leverage RRE, a standard technique from numerical analysis for accelerating sequence convergence that has also been successfully applied to numerical solutions of stochastic differential equations (SDEs). The proposed method involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term in the bias. The authors demonstrate that using correlated noise between the two chains is crucial for controlling the variance of the final estimator.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of SGRRLD, establishing its asymptotic consistency, deriving a central limit theorem, and providing non-asymptotic bounds for its bias and mean squared error (MSE). These results formally show that SGRRLD achieves faster convergence rates than standard SGLD, matching the theoretical accuracy of methods that use more complex higher-order integrators. The authors validate their theoretical claims with experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and demonstrate the practical utility of their method on a large-scale matrix factorization problem, showing consistent performance improvements over SGLD.

**Strengths**

*   **Novel and Insightful Connection:** A key strength of this paper is the successful connection it establishes between the recent literature on SG-MCMC for large-scale machine learning and the established literature on numerical methods for SDEs. This link is valuable and may inspire further cross-pollination of ideas between these two active research communities.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is supported by solid theoretical results. The formal convergence analysis, including the central limit theorem and non-asymptotic error bounds, provides a strong foundation for the proposed algorithm. The analysis clearly demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a superior MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), a significant improvement over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance and Practicality:** The proposed algorithm demonstrates impressive empirical performance. The experiments on synthetic data clearly show a substantial reduction in bias, aligning well with the theoretical predictions. More importantly, the method yields consistent and significant improvements in convergence speed (in terms of wall-clock time) on large-scale, real-world matrix factorization tasks. The fact that the method is inherently parallelizable makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures, and as the authors show, practitioners can readily use it.
*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on both SGLD and RRE before presenting their method, making the work accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Computational Overhead:** The method inherently requires more computation than a single SGLD chain. Each effective iteration of SGRRLD involves one gradient evaluation and update for the coarse chain and two for the fine chain. While the authors effectively leverage parallelization to demonstrate comparable wall-clock times, this relies on the availability of parallel resources. In a serial computing environment, the algorithm would be approximately three times slower per iteration. A brief discussion of this computational cost versus statistical efficiency trade-off would strengthen the paper.
*   **Incremental Originality:** While the application of RRE to SGLD is novel, effective, and well-executed, the approach can be viewed as a fairly direct combination of two well-established techniques from different fields. This does not diminish the value or utility of the contribution, but it frames the work as more of an evolutionary improvement rather than a revolutionary new paradigm for SG-MCMC.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   In Figure 1(a), it appears the plots show Gaussian distributions parameterized by the empirical mean and variance of the posterior samples, rather than the empirical distributions (e.g., histograms) themselves. It would be helpful to clarify this in the figure caption or main text to avoid potential misinterpretation.
*   The results in Figure 2, where the bias and MSE begin to increase as the step size becomes very small, are interesting and slightly counter-intuitive at first glance. It would be beneficial to add a short discussion explaining this observation. This is likely due to the `1/(Kγ)` term in the error bounds of Theorem 2, which dominates the error when `K` is fixed and `γ` approaches zero. Explicitly mentioning this would better connect the theoretical analysis with the empirical results.

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a simple, practical, and theoretically-grounded method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms. The link it forges between numerical SDEs and large-scale MCMC is valuable, and the empirical results are convincing.
