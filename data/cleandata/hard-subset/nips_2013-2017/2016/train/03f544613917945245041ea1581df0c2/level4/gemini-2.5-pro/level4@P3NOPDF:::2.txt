This paper introduces a novel algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to the standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The primary motivation is to reduce the bias introduced by the noise inherent in stochastic gradient estimation. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves an asymptotically lower bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) compared to SGLD. This improved convergence is achieved at the computational cost of running two parallel, coupled Markov chains instead of one.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The application of RR extrapolation, a well-established technique for improving Monte Carlo estimates of Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), to the Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) framework is a novel and valuable contribution. If proven effective, it offers a principled way to improve the accuracy of a widely used class of algorithms.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper supports the proposed algorithm with a theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 & 2), proving that under certain conditions, SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD for a given step size. This provides a solid foundation for the method.

3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The algorithm is described clearly and appears straightforward to implement. It represents a simple yet potentially powerful modification to the standard SGLD procedure.

4.  **Synthetic Data Validation:** The experiment on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model provides a clear and compelling demonstration of SGRRLD's ability to reduce bias effectively in a controlled setting where its advantages can be easily isolated and observed.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The primary weaknesses of the paper relate to the experimental evaluation, particularly the choice of baselines and the limited scope of real-world experiments.

1.  **Limited Real-World Evidence:** The empirical validation on real-world data is not entirely convincing. The experiments on the MovieLens dataset show only a marginal improvement in test RMSE, which makes the practical benefits of the proposed method somewhat arguable. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including experiments on a wider range of real-world datasets and models to demonstrate that the observed theoretical advantages translate into meaningful practical gains.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** My main concern lies with the experimental setup and the comparison to baselines.
    *   The authors argue that the computation time of SGRRLD is comparable to that of SGLD because its two chains can be parallelized. However, this comparison overlooks a more powerful baseline: running two independent SGLD chains in parallel and averaging their results. It is essential to demonstrate that SGRRLD outperforms this stronger, equally computationally expensive baseline. Can the authors clarify if the observed improvement is superior to what one would gain from simply averaging two parallel SGLD runs?
    *   The SGRRLD algorithm uses one chain with step size `gamma` and another with `gamma/2`, and compares this against a single SGLD chain with step size `gamma`. Since a smaller step size (`gamma/2`) inherently reduces the discretization bias in SGLD, a crucial comparison is missing. The authors should also compare SGRRLD against a single SGLD chain running with the smaller step size `gamma/2`. To make the comparison truly fair in terms of computational resources, the most rigorous baseline would be two parallel SGLD chains, both running with step size `gamma/2`.

3.  **Questions on Theory and Mechanics:** While I have not checked the proof details of Theorems 1 & 2, I have a few questions regarding their scope and the underlying intuition.
    *   What are the precise conditions that the potential function `U` must satisfy for both theorems to hold? For example, does the theory apply to multi-modal potential functions, which are common in many machine learning problems?
    *   Could the authors provide more intuition on the mechanism behind the bias reduction? When the stochastic gradient noises are uncorrelated between iterations, one might expect the two chains to decorrelate rapidly, even if the injected Gaussian noise is coupled. How is sufficient correlation between the chains maintained to enable the cancellation of the leading error term? Was the correlation between the two chains measured empirically during the experiments?

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and theoretically motivated idea for improving SGLD. However, its practical utility is not yet fully established due to the limited and arguably unfair experimental comparisons. Addressing the concerns about baseline comparisons and providing more extensive real-world experiments would make a much stronger case for the adoption of this new algorithm.
