Stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer a practical approach to posterior inference on large datasets and have attracted considerable interest in recent years. A known limitation, however, is their asymptotic bias: they do not converge exactly to the true posterior. This paper shows that Richardson–Romberg extrapolation can substantially reduce that bias. The idea is straightforward: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with Chain 2 using half the step size of Chain 1 and running for twice as many steps. The injected Gaussian noises are correlated as follows: let αk denote the noise used at step k of Chain 1, and let βt denote the noise at step t of Chain 2. Set αk = (β2k−1 + β2k)/sqrt(2). To estimate Eπ[f], compute the Monte Carlo estimate from Chain 2 and subtract the estimate from Chain 1. The resulting estimator has reduced bias O(γ^2), compared to O(γ) for either chain alone (γ is the step size). The paper also establishes a corresponding MSE reduction.

I appreciate that the method is simple to understand and implement and requires no major modifications to base SG-MCMC algorithms. It appears broadly applicable across a wide class of SG-MCMC methods and could therefore be quite impactful for the SG-MCMC community.

Novelty: While Richardson–Romberg extrapolation is well known in numerical analysis, it is not widely used in the machine learning or SG-MCMC literature, so applying it here is a useful contribution.

Clarity: The paper is well written and clearly presented.

Comments and questions:
- Applicability: Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, beyond the condition in Section 2.2 (i.e., that the bias expansion holds: πγ(f) = π(f) + C·γ + O(γ^2)), what additional assumptions are required?
- Beyond SG-MCMC: Can the approach be adapted to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests? What about distributed SGLD?
- Resource utilization: Because Chain 2 runs twice as long as Chain 1, the chain with the larger step size is idle for half the wall-clock time. Could one instead run three chains in parallel—one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2—for the same number of steps each, and then combine them analogously? How would this affect the estimator’s variance?
- More than two step sizes: Is there a natural extension to k-level Richardson–Romberg schemes with more than two step sizes, and if so, how would you recommend choosing coefficients and computational budgets?
- Correlation structure: The injected noises must be correlated as described, but I am unclear about the treatment of minibatch correlation. Is variance minimized when using the same minibatch for step k of Chain 1 and for steps 2k−1 and 2k of Chain 2, or is the effect of minibatch coupling negligible relative to noise coupling?
- Experiments (matrix factorization, Fig. 5): I suggest running until convergence and including an SGD baseline. The current plot shows improvements over SGLD, but it would be more compelling to demonstrate gains in a setting where Bayesian inference is known to help (i.e., outperforming SGD). Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice; reporting final errors for SGRRLD, SGLD, and SGD would better contextualize the benefits and encourage adoption.
- Accessibility: Although the method is simple, an algorithm box with pseudocode would make the paper more accessible. Many practitioners may skip the theoretical sections; presenting pseudocode early would lower the barrier to trying the method.

Overall, this is a clear and potentially high-impact paper that introduces a simple, general bias-reduction technique for SG-MCMC with promising theoretical and empirical properties.
