- Introduces SGRRLD: use Richardson-Romberg extrapolation by running two correlated SG-MCMC chains (step γ and two steps γ/2) and linearly combining them to cancel leading O(γ) bias.  
- Novelty: first application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the stochastic-gradient MCMC setting, building on SGLD/SG-MCMC literature.  
- Theoretical contributions: proves asymptotic consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵), improving over SGLD’s O(K⁻²/³).  

Strengths:
- Simplicity & practicality: conceptually simple, easy to implement with minor modifications and well-suited to multi-core parallelism.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees: comprehensive analysis with bias, MSE bounds and a central limit theorem.  
- Generality & impact: approach extends beyond SGLD (demonstrated on SGHMC) and could broadly improve SG-MCMC methods.  
- Efficiency: first-order integrator plus RR achieves accuracy comparable to higher-order schemes without their complexity.  

Weaknesses / Questions:
- Scope of applicability: unclear which specific “weak error and ergodicity” conditions are required and which SG-MCMC variants (e.g., MH-corrected subsampled methods) are compatible.  
- Computational cost vs. statistical efficiency: requires ~3× gradient evaluations per effective iteration; needs discussion of this trade-off and whether a 3-chain alternative (one γ, two γ/2) yields similar bias/variance.  
- Multi-level extrapolation: asks if straightforward recursive/multi-level RR with more than two step sizes is possible and what theoretical gains to expect.  
- Correlation of stochastic gradients: queries whether reusing the same mini-batches across corresponding steps would further reduce variance beyond injected-noise correlation.  
- Experimental evaluation: matrix factorization experiments should show results at convergence, run until both methods converge, and include strong baselines like SGD to contextualize practical benefits.  
- Presentation: recommend adding an early pseudocode/algorithm box to improve accessibility for practitioners.  

- Overall: high-quality, well-supported contribution; addressing the above points would further strengthen clarity, applicability, and empirical validation.
