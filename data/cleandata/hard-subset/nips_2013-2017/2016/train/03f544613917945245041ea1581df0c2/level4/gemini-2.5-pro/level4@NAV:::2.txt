This paper introduces an application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) with the goal of mitigating bias induced by gradient estimation noise. The proposed method, named SGRRLD, is shown to possess an asymptotically lower bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) than standard SGLD. This advantage is clearly demonstrated in an experiment on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model. However, when applied to the MovieLens dataset, the algorithm shows only a slight and arguable improvement in test RMSE.

The work's main contribution lies in adapting the RR extrapolation technique, previously used to enhance the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs, to the context of SG-MCMC algorithms. The authors provide theoretical proofs indicating that, under specific conditions, SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD using the same step size. This improvement comes at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The description of the algorithm is clear, and its implementation appears to be straightforward. The synthetic experiments effectively highlight SGRRLD's capacity for bias reduction in that specific setting.

To strengthen the paper's claims, it would be beneficial to include more experiments on real-world datasets to demonstrate that this relatively simple modification to SGLD can yield significant bias reduction in practice. Regarding the theory, while the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 have not been checked in detail, it would be helpful to clarify the conditions the potential function U must satisfy for both theorems to hold. For instance, can U be multi-modal? Furthermore, some intuition is requested regarding the error reduction mechanism. If the gradient noises are uncorrelated, one might expect the two chains to decorrelate quickly, even with correlated Gaussian noise. It is not immediately obvious how their combination reduces the error's order. Did the authors empirically observe the correlation between the chains in their experiments?

There are also concerns about the experimental design. The authors argue that SGRRLD's runtime is comparable to SGLD's due to parallelization. However, a fairer comparison would be against two independent SGLD chains, also run in parallel within the same time budget. It is not clear if SGRRLD would maintain its advantage in RMSE against an estimate derived from two parallel SGLD chains. Additionally, the real-data experiment compares SGRRLD (using step sizes γ and γ/2) with SGLD (using step size γ). The comparison should also include SGLD with a step size of γ/2, as this would naturally have a lower bias. A more rigorous baseline would be to run two parallel SGLD chains at the smaller γ/2 step size. Finally, broadening the scope of real-data experiments is strongly encouraged.
