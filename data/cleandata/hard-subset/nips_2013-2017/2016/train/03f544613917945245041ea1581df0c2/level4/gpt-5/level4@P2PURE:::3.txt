Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer a convenient way to perform Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets and have attracted substantial interest in recent years. A known drawback is their asymptotic bias: they do not converge exactly to the true posterior. This paper shows that Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation can reduce this bias.

The core idea is straightforward. Run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, where Chain 2 uses half the step size of Chain 1 and is run for twice as many iterations. The injected Gaussian noises are coupled so that, if alpha_k and beta_k denote the Gaussian noise at iteration k in Chains 1 and 2 respectively, then alpha_k = (beta_{2k-1} + beta_{2k}) / sqrt(2). To estimate the expectation of a test function f under the target distribution, compute the usual Monte Carlo estimate from Chain 2 and subtract the corresponding estimate from Chain 1. The resulting estimator has reduced bias O(gamma^2), compared to the O(gamma) bias of either chain alone (gamma denotes the step size). The paper also proves a corresponding reduction in the MSE of the estimator.

I appreciate that the method is simple to understand and implement (see my summary) and does not require significant modifications to the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm. It appears broadly applicable across a large class of SG-MCMC methods and could have substantial impact on the SG-MCMC community.

Novelty: While RR extrapolation is well known in numerical analysis, it is not widely known within the machine learning and SG-MCMC communities.

Clarity: The paper is well written and clearly presented.

Comments/questions:
- Can this technique be applied directly to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, beyond the condition in Section 2.2 (pi_gamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)), what additional assumptions are required?
- Can the approach be extended to other approximate (non–stochastic gradient) MCMC schemes, such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests? What about Distributed SGLD?
- Because one chain must run for twice as long, there is idle computation when the larger-step-size chain waits. Could one instead run three chains—one with step size gamma and two with step size gamma/2—for the same number of iterations each? Would this increase the estimator’s variance?
- Is there a natural extension to more than two step sizes?
- The injected noises must be correlated, but I did not fully grasp the role of mini-batch correlation. Is the variance minimized when the same mini-batch is used for iteration k of Chain 1 and iterations 2k-1 and 2k of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this correlation negligible?
- For the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5), please run until convergence and also compare against SGD. The current plot shows gains over SGLD, but it would be helpful to demonstrate improvement over SGLD in a setting where being Bayesian is advantageous (i.e., beating SGD). Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice; concrete numbers would help persuade practitioners. Including an SGD curve as a control would also place the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD in better context.
- Although the method is easy and clearly explained, an algorithm box would increase accessibility. Many practitioners may skip the theoretical sections, so including pseudocode early in the paper would help readers implement the method without delving into the more technical parts.
