

This paper introduces a novel approach to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors propose Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to standard SG-MCMC algorithms. The core idea involves running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and correlated Brownian motions. The chain with smaller step size runs twice as many iterations, and the final estimator is constructed as 2πγ/2(f) - πγ(f), which theoretically reduces the bias from O(γ) to O(γ²) while maintaining the variance at a reasonable level. The paper provides formal theoretical analysis showing SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves a mean squared error rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to O(K⁻²/³) for standard SG-MCMC methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendation, showing significant improvements in accuracy over SGLD and SGHMC.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the NIPS community by addressing a fundamental limitation of SG-MCMC methods (asymptotic bias) with a simple yet effective technique. The work builds upon the theoretical foundation established in previous NIPS papers on SG-MCMC (e.g., Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and extends the numerical analysis technique of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC domain. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

Strengths:
- The method is conceptually simple, requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC implementations, and is highly generalizable to various SG-MCMC algorithms.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides clear convergence rates that are superior to existing first-order SG-MCMC methods.
- The empirical results consistently demonstrate significant improvements over SGLD and SGHMC across multiple datasets and experimental settings.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with excellent technical exposition of the theoretical results.

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not fully address whether the RR extrapolation technique can be applied to all SG-MCMC methods or specify the exact conditions beyond those mentioned in Section 2.2.
- The experimental comparison on matrix factorization should include a baseline comparison with standard SGD to better demonstrate the Bayesian advantage of the proposed method.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed discussion about the practical implications of the correlation between the Brownian increments and the mini-batch sampling strategy.
- The paper could include a more comprehensive exploration of multi-step RR extrapolation (beyond the brief mention in the conclusion).

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of SG-MCMC methods with a theoretically sound and practically implementable solution.
- The theoretical results provide clear improvement over existing methods (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs O(K⁻²/³) convergence rate).
- The method is simple to implement and generalizable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.
- The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority across multiple experimental settings.
- The work builds upon and extends important prior work in the NIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the specific conditions required for the RR extrapolation to be applicable to all SG-MCMC methods.
- The matrix factorization experiments could be strengthened by including SGD as a baseline to better demonstrate the Bayesian advantage.
- The paper does not fully explore the potential of multi-step RR extrapolation, which could further improve the method's performance.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis that is well-supported by experimental results. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with a logical structure that effectively presents the theoretical contributions and experimental results. The technical exposition is accessible to readers familiar with SG-MCMC methods.

Originality: While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a known technique in numerical analysis, its application to SG-MCMC represents a novel contribution to the machine learning community. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by applying this numerical technique to the specific challenges of SG-MCMC.

Significance: The method provides a practical solution to a fundamental limitation of SG-MCMC methods, potentially improving the accuracy of Bayesian inference in large-scale applications. The technique is simple to implement and generalizable, making it highly applicable across the SG-MCMC community. The demonstrated improvements in convergence rates and accuracy on both synthetic and real-world datasets highlight the method's potential impact.
