

Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer an efficient approach to Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets, attracting significant attention in recent years. A key limitation is their asymptotic bias—they do not converge exactly to the target posterior. This paper addresses this by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias. The core idea is straightforward: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel. Chain 2 uses half the step size (γ/2) of Chain 1 and runs for twice as many steps. Crucially, the Gaussian noise injected at each step is correlated: for iteration *k*, the noise in Chain 1 is *β<sub>k</sub>*, while Chain 2 uses *α<sub>k</sub> = (β<sub>2k-1</sub> + β<sub>2k</sub>)/√2*. To estimate the expectation of a function *f* under the target distribution, simply subtract the Chain 1 estimate from the Chain 2 estimate. The authors prove this yields bias *O*(γ²), compared to *O*(γ) for either chain alone. They further establish a corresponding reduction in mean squared error (MSE).  

The method’s conceptual simplicity and minimal implementation overhead are major strengths. It requires no modifications to the base SG-MCMC algorithm and applies broadly across SG-MCMC variants, making it highly impactful for the community. The novelty lies in adapting a well-known numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg extrapolation) to SG-MCMC, where it remains underutilized despite its potential. The paper is well-written, with clear exposition and logical flow.  

**Major Comments:**  
1. **Generality:** The method appears applicable to SG-MCMC algorithms satisfying the bias expansion *π<sub>γ</sub>(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O*(γ²) (as in Section 2.2). However, the paper does not explicitly state whether this condition is necessary for all SG-MCMC methods. Are there specific classes of algorithms (e.g., those with non-standard noise structures) where this approach fails?  
2. **Broader Applicability:** Could this technique extend to non-stochastic-gradient MCMC schemes? For instance, could it improve sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings or distributed SGLD? The authors should address this.  
3. **Computational Efficiency:** Running Chain 2 for twice as many steps creates idle time for Chain 1. Could a three-chain strategy mitigate this? Specifically, run one chain at step size *γ* and two chains at *γ/2* (each for *N* steps), then combine estimates. Would this increase estimator variance compared to the two-chain approach?  
4. **Multi-Step-Size Extension:** Is the method extendable to more than two step sizes (e.g., *γ*, *γ/2*, *γ/4*)? If so, how would the estimator construction and bias reduction scale?  
5. **Noise Correlation Mechanism:** The correlation between chains relies on shared mini-batches. The paper states that *α<sub>k</sub>* uses *β<sub>2k-1</sub>* and *β<sub>2k</sub>*, but does this imply the same mini-batch is used for Chain 1’s *k*-th step and Chains 2’s (2k-1)-th and 2k-th steps? If not, what is the optimal correlation structure for minimizing variance? The effect of mini-batch correlation needs clarification.  
6. **Empirical Validation:** The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should include a comparison with SGD to demonstrate Bayesian advantage. Current results show SGRRLD outperforms SGLD, but without SGD as a baseline, it’s unclear whether Bayesian inference provides tangible gains over standard optimization. Bayesian matrix factorization is rarely used in practice; a clear comparison to SGD would strengthen the argument for adopting the method. Additionally, the plot should show convergence behavior, not just a single point.  
7. **Algorithm Accessibility:** While the method is well-explained, an algorithm box early in the paper would enhance accessibility. Many practitioners may skip theoretical details; a concise pseudocode would enable immediate implementation without deep mathematical engagement.  

**Summary:** The paper presents a conceptually simple and broadly applicable solution to reduce bias in SG-MCMC. The technical contributions are sound, and the potential impact on the community is significant. Addressing the above points—particularly the generality conditions, computational efficiency, and empirical validation—would substantially strengthen the work. The core idea is promising, and with minor clarifications, this could become a standard technique in SG-MCMC.
