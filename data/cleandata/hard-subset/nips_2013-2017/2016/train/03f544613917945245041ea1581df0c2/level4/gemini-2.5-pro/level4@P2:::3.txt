This paper addresses the problem of asymptotic bias in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. SG-MCMC methods are widely used for Bayesian inference on large datasets, but their reliance on first-order numerical integrators (like Euler-Maruyama) and noisy gradients introduces a bias that prevents them from converging to the true posterior distribution. The authors propose a novel and elegant approach to reduce this bias using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique from numerical analysis.

The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains for the same target distribution but with different step sizes. Specifically, one chain uses a step size sequence `γ_k`, while the second chain uses a sequence `γ_k/2` but performs two steps for each single step of the first chain. This ensures both chains simulate the underlying process up to the same time horizon. By constructing a new estimator as a specific linear combination of the estimators from the two chains (essentially `2 * (estimate from chain 2) - (estimate from chain 1)`), the leading-order bias term cancels out. The authors show that controlling the variance of this combined estimator is crucial and can be achieved by using correlated Gaussian noise across the two chains.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which they name Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). They prove that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and has a non-asymptotic bias of order O(γ²) and an MSE with a convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵). This is a significant improvement over standard SGLD, which has a bias of O(γ) and an MSE rate of O(K⁻²/³). The authors demonstrate that their method achieves the theoretical accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators while only using simple first-order updates. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem for movie recommendations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of SG-MCMC methods. The proposed solution is general and could be highly impactful for the large community of researchers and practitioners using these scalable Bayesian inference techniques.
2.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** A key strength of the proposed method is its simplicity. It is easy to understand and implement, requiring only minor modifications to an existing SG-MCMC algorithm. The parallel nature of the two chains makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures.
3.  **Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, its application to the SG-MCMC setting is novel and insightful. The analysis of how to control the variance via correlated noise is a key contribution that makes the technique practical for sampling.
4.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is technically sound, providing a thorough convergence analysis with a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds for both bias and MSE. The theoretical results clearly quantify the advantages of SGRRLD over SGLD.
5.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents the main ideas, theory, and experiments in an accessible manner.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The authors demonstrate the method on SGLD and SGHMC and state it is quite generic. It would be beneficial to be more explicit about the conditions under which the RR extrapolation technique can be applied. For example, is it sufficient that an SG-MCMC algorithm's invariant distribution has a weak error expansion of the form `π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)`? Furthermore, could the framework be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings algorithms?
2.  **Computational Considerations:** The proposed parallel scheme involves one chain performing one update while the other performs two. This implies that the processor running the first chain will be idle for roughly half the computation time. Have the authors considered alternative schemes to mitigate this? For instance, could one run three chains in parallel (one with step size γ, and two with step size γ/2) for the same number of iterations? This might improve hardware utilization, but it is unclear how it would affect the variance of the final estimator. A brief discussion on this would be valuable.
3.  **Correlation of Mini-batches:** The paper thoroughly analyzes the importance of correlating the injected Gaussian noise but is less clear about the effect of correlating the mini-batches used for the gradient estimators. What is the optimal strategy here? Should the same mini-batch be used for step `k` of the first chain and steps `2k-1` and `2k` of the second? Clarifying the impact of this choice on the estimator's variance would strengthen the paper.
4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The matrix factorization experiment effectively demonstrates the superiority of SGRRLD over SGLD. However, its conclusiveness could be improved in two ways:
    *   It would be useful to include a non-Bayesian baseline like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). This would help contextualize the performance of both SG-MCMC methods and better demonstrate the practical utility of the Bayesian approach in a setting where it has been shown to be advantageous.
    *   The plots show performance as a function of wall-clock time but seem to stop before either algorithm has fully converged. Running the experiment for longer would provide a clearer picture of the final performance difference.
5.  **Presentation:** Although the method is clearly explained, the paper's accessibility to a broader audience of practitioners could be enhanced by including a pseudo-code algorithm box in the main text. This would allow readers who are less familiar with the underlying theory to quickly grasp the implementation details and apply the method.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a simple, effective, and well-grounded method for improving the accuracy of a wide class of popular SG-MCMC algorithms. The strengths in novelty, theoretical contribution, and potential impact are significant. The weaknesses are minor and are primarily suggestions for clarification and strengthening the experimental results, which can be addressed in a final version.
