Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) offers a computationally efficient approach to Bayesian posterior inference with large datasets and has garnered significant attention recently. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias – they do not converge exactly to the target posterior distribution. This paper presents a compelling solution using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce this bias. The core idea is elegantly simple: running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with the second chain utilizing half the step size and twice the number of iterations as the first. The injected Gaussian noise in the two chains is appropriately correlated, defined as alphak = (beta{2k-1) + beta{2k})/sqrt(2), where alphak and betak represent the noise at time step k for Chains 1 and 2, respectively.  The authors demonstrate that subtracting the expectation of a function *f* estimated from Chain 1 from the estimate obtained from Chain 2 yields a lower bias of O(gamma^2), compared to the O(gamma) bias of individual chains (where gamma is the step size). A corresponding reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) is also proven. 

I particularly appreciate the method’s conceptual simplicity and ease of implementation, requiring minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Its broad applicability across a wide range of SG-MCMC methods suggests potentially significant impact for the community.

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, its application within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC context appears novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear and logical.

**Comments/Questions:**

*   Is the applicability of this technique universal across all SG-MCMC methods? Beyond satisfying the bound outlined in section 2.2 (pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)), are there other necessary conditions?
*   Could this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not strictly stochastic gradient-based, such as those employing sub-sampling in Metropolis-Hastings tests? What about its potential application to Distributed SGLD?
*   The differing runtimes of the two chains introduce computational inefficiency, with the chain using the larger step size remaining idle for a portion of the process. Would employing three chains – one with step size gamma and two with step size gamma/2, all running for the same number of steps – be a more efficient alternative?  Would this approach impact the variance of the estimate?
*   Is there a natural extension of this method to utilize more than two distinct step sizes?
*   The correlation structure of the injected noise is crucial, but the effect of correlation *between* mini-batches is not fully clear. Specifically, is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k^th iteration of Chain 1 and the 2k-1^th and 2k^th iterations of Chain 2? Or is this correlation effect negligible?
*   The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) would benefit from being run to convergence, and a comparison to standard SGD would be valuable. While the current results demonstrate an advantage over SGLD, showcasing superior performance compared to SGD in a scenario where the Bayesian approach is advantageous would strengthen the argument for adoption. Given the limited practical use of Bayesian matrix factorization, providing quantitative results will be crucial for wider acceptance. Including an SGD baseline would also provide a clearer context for the final errors achieved by SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   Although the method is presented clearly, including an algorithm box with pseudo-code would enhance accessibility, particularly for practitioners who may not delve into the theoretical details. This would enable easier implementation and experimentation with the method.
