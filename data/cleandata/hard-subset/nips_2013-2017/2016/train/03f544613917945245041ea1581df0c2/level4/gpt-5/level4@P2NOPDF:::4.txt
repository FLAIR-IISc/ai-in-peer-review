This paper proposes a Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation scheme for SG-MCMC that improves convergence rates in bias and MSE. The method is simple to implement, requiring only minor modifications to standard SG-MCMC, and is supported by solid theoretical analysis. Overall, the idea is appealing and the theory appears sound.

My main technical concern is the clarity of how the specific convergence rates claimed in the text are obtained from the main theorem (as I noted in the “fatal flaws” section). The mapping from the theorem’s assumptions and bounds to the stated rates is not transparent. Please clarify exactly:
- which terms are canceled by the RR construction,
- how the rates depend on the step-size schedule and the order of smoothness assumed for the bias expansion,
- how stochastic gradient noise and finite minibatches enter the constants and rates,
- and whether the rates are finite-time (in K and gamma) or asymptotic.

A practical concern arises in the matrix factorization experiments. On a single-core machine, the proposed method requires roughly three times the computation of standard SGLD (two chains, with one using twice as many steps). As a result, Figure 5’s wall-clock comparisons are not directly comparable. A fair comparison would normalize by computational budget, e.g., by compressing SGLD’s time axis by a factor of about 3 (equivalently, multiplying its reported times by roughly 2/3). This would reduce the apparent performance gap between SGLD and the RR-accelerated method (SGRRLD). Please provide runtime-normalized plots or per-iteration comparisons to isolate algorithmic gains from additional compute.

Regarding Figure 3 (bias vs. time): the best convergence visually appears to be achieved by the red curve with alpha = 0.2, which differs from the text’s claim that alpha = 1/3 is optimal. Is there a mismatch between the figure and the description, or some setting-dependent effect that explains this? In addition, the purportedly optimal curve shows substantial fluctuations, which seems inconsistent with the expected rate for alpha = 0.2. Please explain the source of these oscillations (e.g., variance from minibatch gradients, coupling effects between the two chains, or insufficient averaging), and reconcile the observed behavior with the theory.

Finally, in line 256 you state that when the step size is too small, the term 1/(K gamma) dominates. Why does this effect not appear to dominate in the SGLD baseline under the same small-step regime? Some discussion or evidence (e.g., showing the relevant term sizes across methods) would help.

Minor comments and presentation:
- Equation (1): Why introduce a parameter symbol different from theta when theta is used elsewhere? Please unify notation.
- Line 94: Define the RR acronym at first use.
- Line 131: Gamma_K is not defined before use; please define it clearly.
- Line 160: “Smooth function f” is vague. Specify the smoothness class (e.g., Lipschitz gradient, bounded Hessian, bounded higher-order derivatives) required for the RR bias expansion.
- Line 249: The caption says Figure 1(b) plots bias versus dimension, but it appears to be bias versus time; should this be Figure 1(c)?
- Please use boldface for vectors to improve readability and maintain consistent notation throughout.

In summary, the paper presents a neat and practical RR-based improvement to SG-MCMC with promising theoretical support. Clarifying how the advertised rates follow from the main theorem and ensuring fair, compute-normalized experimental comparisons would significantly strengthen the submission.
