This paper proposes using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The resulting algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise. The paper provides both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analyses. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model verify the theoretical bounds on bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), while experiments on large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD.

This is a well-written paper that presents a valid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods. The authors have done a good job of contextualizing their work with respect to previous methods.

**Strengths:**
*   **Methodology:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a clever technique to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC. It effectively achieves the accuracy of a higher-order integrator using only first-order updates, which is a notable advantage.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is theoretically sound, providing a comprehensive convergence analysis that includes a Central Limit Theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. These results formally establish that SGRRLD achieves higher convergence rates than standard SGLD.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and supports the theoretical claims. The experiments on synthetic data effectively demonstrate the claimed reductions in bias and MSE, while the large-scale matrix factorization experiments highlight the method's practical utility and efficiency in terms of wall-clock time.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
My main concerns relate to the novelty of the contribution and the fairness of the experimental comparisons.

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The paper's novelty may be viewed as incremental. The impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has already been analyzed in [10], and RR extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in the context of SDEs [23]. While the application and analysis in the SG-MCMC setting are valuable, the core ideas build heavily on existing work.

2.  **Practicality and Fairness of Experiments:**
    *   **Communication Cost:** The proposed algorithm is parallel, and using correlated Brownian motions requires the two chains to communicate at every step. This communication overhead is not mentioned or accounted for in the experiments. It would be beneficial to discuss this cost, as it could be a limiting factor in practice.
    *   **Resource Allocation:** The experimental comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD is not entirely clear about the allocation of computational resources. For a fair comparison of wall-clock time, was the single SGLD chain given the same total resources as the two chains of SGRRLD combined? For example, if SGRRLD uses two processor cores, a fair baseline might involve running two independent SGLD chains on two cores and averaging their results. The primary computational cost in SG-MCMC comes from calculating stochastic gradients. It is not obvious how SGRRLD could achieve a shorter wall-clock time (as shown in Figure 5) if each of its chains has access to only half the resources of the single SGLD chain. Please provide more details on the implementation setup to clarify this point.
    *   **Alternative Baseline:** An alternative approach in a parallel setting is to run two independent SGLD chains "embarrassingly parallel" (i.e., without communication) and average their outputs. A comparison against this simple but strong baseline would help clarify the specific benefits of the RR extrapolation and noise correlation scheme.
