Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) provides a practical route to Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets and has garnered substantial attention recently. A well-known limitation is asymptotic bias: these methods do not target the exact posterior. This paper shows that Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation can systematically reduce that bias. The core idea is straightforward: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with Chain 2 using half the step size of Chain 1 and running for twice as many steps. The injected Gaussian noises are coupled via αk = (β2k−1 + β2k)/sqrt(2), where αk and βk denote the Gaussian perturbations at iteration k for Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate Eπ[f], one forms an extrapolated estimator by differencing the estimates from the two chains (effectively implementing the RR combination), which yields a bias of order O(γ^2) versus O(γ) for either chain alone (γ is the step size). The paper also establishes a corresponding improvement in MSE. The approach is appealing because it is simple to implement (see summary above), requires only minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC schemes, and appears broadly applicable across many SG-MCMC variants, suggesting meaningful impact for the SG-MCMC community.

Novelty: RR extrapolation is classical in numerical analysis, but to my knowledge it is not widely used in machine learning or specifically within the stochastic gradient MCMC literature.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized.

Comments/questions:
- Applicability: Can this be applied directly to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, beyond requiring an expansion of the form πγ(f) = π(f) + C γ + O(γ^2), what additional conditions are needed?
- Scope beyond SG-MCMC: Could the idea transfer to other approximate MCMC procedures (e.g., subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests)? How about distributed SGLD?
- Parallelization/work allocation: Since the finer-step chain runs twice as many iterations, the larger-step chain may sit idle in wall-clock-synchronous settings. Could one instead run three chains—one with step size γ and two with γ/2—for the same number of steps in parallel? Would this change the variance properties of the estimator?
- Extensions to multiple levels: Is there a natural extension to more than two step sizes (multi-level RR) within this SG-MCMC context?
- Mini-batch correlation: The Gaussian noises are coupled, but the role of correlating the stochastic gradients (mini-batches) is less clear. Is the variance minimized by using the same mini-batch for Chain 1 at iteration k and for Chain 2 at iterations 2k−1 and 2k, or is this effect negligible relative to noise coupling?
- Experimental evaluation: For the matrix factorization study (Figure 5), it would be informative to run to convergence and also compare against SGD. This would demonstrate whether the Bayesian approach (and your acceleration) outperforms a strong non-Bayesian baseline, helping contextualize the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
- Presentation: Although the method is simple and well explained, an algorithm box with pseudo-code early in the paper would improve accessibility, especially for practitioners who may not delve into the theoretical sections.
