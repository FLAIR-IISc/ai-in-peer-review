This paper proposes using a numerical sequence acceleration method—a higher-order numerical integrator via Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—to improve the convergence of SG-MCMC. The approach runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated injected Gaussian noise. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence results. On a synthetic Gaussian model, the experiments verify the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, and on a large-scale matrix factorization task with real data, the method shows practical gains over standard SGLD. Overall, the technique is well presented, and the paper makes a valid contribution to the SG-MCMC literature, with good discussion of related work.

My two main concerns are as follows:

1) Novelty. The contribution appears somewhat incremental. The impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and RR extrapolation has been used to approximate invariant distributions in [23]. Please clarify the precise conceptual and technical advances beyond these works. For example, what is new in your error analysis specific to the stochastic-gradient setting, what aspects of coupling/variance reduction are novel, and what insights or guarantees are unavailable in [10, 23]? A sharper positioning would help establish the distinct contribution.

2) Practicality and fairness of the empirical evaluation. The method is a parallel SG-MCMC implementation that requires correlating Brownian noise across two chains, which in turn requires communication at every step. The paper appears to assume zero communication cost. Please discuss and, if possible, quantify this overhead. It is also unclear how compute resources were allocated between SGRRLD and SGLD:

- Did the single SGLD chain use the same total compute resources as the two SGRRLD chains combined? If not, the comparison may be unfair from a practical standpoint.
- If the total resources were matched, how were they split between the two SGRRLD chains during training (e.g., cores/GPUs, batch sizes, memory bandwidth)?
- Since SG-MCMC cost is dominated by stochastic gradient evaluations, why would two chains with partial resources each achieve lower wall-clock time than a single fully resourced SGLD chain? Please provide implementation details (hardware, parallelization strategy, minibatch sizes per chain, synchronization frequency) and report wall-clock time in addition to iteration counts. It would also be helpful to report total gradient evaluations and any synchronization/communication overhead.

As an additional baseline, consider running two SGLD chains in embarrassingly parallel fashion (no communication) and compare: (a) per-chain performance, (b) aggregated performance via averaging or combining samples, and (c) under matched gradient budgets and matched wall-clock time. This would help disentangle gains due to RR extrapolation from gains due to parallelism.
