

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD with first-order integrators by running two parallel Markov chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), then combining their estimates through RR extrapolation. Theoretical analysis shows SGRRLD attains asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, leading to a mean squared error (MSE) convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). This work builds on previous NIPS contributions in SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and extends the application of RR extrapolation from SDE discretizations (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015) to stochastic gradient settings.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, establishing asymptotic consistency, central limit theorems, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The authors clearly demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order methods.
2. The algorithm is conceptually simple and straightforward to implement, with the parallel structure well-suited for distributed architectures.
3. The synthetic experiments on a Gaussian model provide clear, compelling evidence that SGRRLD significantly reduces bias compared to SGLD, with the variance bias reduced from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻⁴.
4. The paper effectively connects to prior work in SG-MCMC and RR extrapolation, properly citing relevant NIPS proceedings.

Weaknesses:
1. The real data experiments on MovieLens datasets show only marginal improvements in RMSE (2-3% better for ML-1M), which may not be statistically significant given the scale of the problem. The paper would be stronger with additional real-world applications demonstrating more substantial gains.
2. The comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD appears misleading: SGRRLD uses step sizes γ and γ/2 while SGLD uses γ. To fairly evaluate the method, the authors should compare SGRRLD (with γ and γ/2) against SGLD with step size γ/2 (which would have lower bias) and also against two parallel SGLD chains with step size γ/2.
3. The paper does not address whether the two parallel chains in SGRRLD maintain sufficient correlation to achieve the theoretical benefits. Intuitively, when gradient noise is high, the chains might decorrelate quickly despite correlated Brownian increments, potentially negating the RR advantage.
4. The computational time comparison is problematic: SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel (theoretically doubling computation time), while SGLD cannot leverage this parallelism. The paper claims "nearly identical wall-clock times" but doesn't address how the authors would achieve this in practice (e.g., by reducing the step size for SGLD to match the same total computation time).

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- The theoretical contribution is strong and novel, providing a simple yet effective method to improve SG-MCMC convergence rates.
- The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical exposition and appropriate references.
- The method is broadly applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.
- The synthetic experiments convincingly validate the theoretical claims.

Con-acceptance arguments:
- The real data experiments show only modest improvements that may not justify the additional complexity.
- The comparison framework in experiments is not fully fair to SGLD.
- The paper lacks sufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges when applying RR extrapolation to real-world SG-MCMC problems.
- The authors do not provide evidence that the correlation between the two chains is maintained in practice, which is critical for the theoretical benefits.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of SG-MCMC, but the real-world impact appears limited based on the current experiments. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct additional experiments comparing SGRRLD (with step sizes γ and γ/2) against SGLD with step size γ/2 (and possibly two parallel SGLD chains with step size γ/2).
2. Provide more detailed analysis of the correlation between the two parallel chains during sampling.
3. Include additional real-world applications where the improvement is more pronounced.
4. Discuss the practical implementation challenges of maintaining correlation between chains in distributed settings.

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical contribution with a clear algorithmic proposal, but the practical significance needs stronger empirical validation to justify acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive experimental validation of the claimed advantages in real-world applications.
