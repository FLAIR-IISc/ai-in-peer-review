

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms while maintaining reasonable variance. The approach runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and uses correlated Brownian motions to control variance, resulting in asymptotic bias and variance of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods, matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods without requiring their computational complexity. The authors validate these claims through synthetic experiments on Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks using MovieLens datasets.

The paper builds upon prior work in two key areas: Welling and Teh's SGLD [1] and Talay and Tubaro's RR extrapolation for SDEs [21]. It extends the convergence analysis of SG-MCMC methods [10] by showing how RR extrapolation can achieve higher convergence rates with first-order integrators. The connection to [23], which discusses RR extrapolation for invariant distributions of diffusions, is particularly relevant to the proposed method.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The theoretical results are well-supported with clear theorems and proofs, and the authors provide non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE that directly demonstrate the superiority of SGRRLD over SGLD. The experimental section is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real-world validations that align with the theoretical predictions. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and places the work in proper context with relevant prior art.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical contribution is strong, the novelty is somewhat incremental. The paper builds directly on [10] (which analyzes the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC) and [23] (which discusses RR extrapolation for invariant distributions), and it would benefit from a more precise discussion of how this work differs from and extends these prior contributions. The paper could better highlight the unique aspects of applying RR extrapolation specifically to SG-MCMC rather than to standard MCMC.

The practical implementation considerations require more attention. The authors assume no communication cost between the two parallel chains, which is unrealistic for distributed implementations. In practice, maintaining correlated Brownian motions would require communication between the chains at each step. The paper does not clarify how computational resources are allocated between the two chains in SGRRLD versus a single chain in SGLD. Specifically, it's unclear whether SGRRLD uses the same total computational resources as SGLD (with each chain receiving half the resources) or more resources (with both chains using full resources), which significantly impacts the practical comparison. The authors should provide details about their implementation setup, including how they divided computational resources between the two chains and whether they accounted for communication costs.

Additionally, the paper could strengthen its experimental comparison by including a baseline that runs two independent SGLD chains (without correlation) in parallel, which would represent a more realistic alternative to SGRRLD. This would clarify whether the communication cost is a significant factor in the practical performance.

**Overall assessment:** This is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical contributions that advance the state of the art in SG-MCMC methods. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by comprehensive experiments. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments are significant, particularly for large-scale applications. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the implementation details and providing a more precise discussion of how it extends prior work on RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference, with clear theoretical advances and practical benefits. The minor concerns regarding implementation details and novelty differentiation do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. The authors should address the implementation details in the final version to ensure the practical benefits are fully understood.
