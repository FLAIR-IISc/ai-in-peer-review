Summary
The paper adapts Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD), yielding an algorithm (SGRRLD) that combines estimators from two coupled step sizes to cancel leading-order bias due to discretization and stochastic gradient noise. Under stated regularity assumptions, the authors prove improved asymptotic bias and mean-squared error (MSE) relative to SGLD with the same step size, at the cost of running two parallel chains. Synthetic experiments on a linear Gaussian model show clear bias reduction; results on MovieLens show small gains in test RMSE.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is natural and well-motivated by the SDE literature.
- Theoretical contribution: the paper provides conditions under which SGRRLD improves the convergence rate (bias/MSE) over SGLD with matched step size.
- Practicality: the algorithmic description is clear and simple to implement.
- Synthetic results convincingly demonstrate bias reduction in a controlled setting.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Fairness of empirical comparisons: the real-data evaluation compares SGRRLD (using γ and γ/2) against SGLD with γ only. This favors SGRRLD because smaller step sizes already reduce bias. A fair comparison should include SGLD with γ/2 and, importantly, average across two independent SGLD chains run in parallel within the same wall-clock/compute budget.
- Compute budget parity: the paper argues SGRRLD has similar wall-clock time due to parallelization. However, the same parallelization could run two standard SGLD chains and average their estimates, potentially reducing variance and bias. The paper does not report this baseline.
- Variance vs. bias trade-off: RR extrapolation reduces bias but can increase variance unless the two chains are sufficiently correlated (e.g., via common random numbers and shared mini-batches). The empirical results do not quantify the induced correlation or how it impacts finite-sample MSE.
- Scope of empirical validation: beyond the linear Gaussian model, the real-data improvement is small and arguably within noise. The paper would benefit from more diverse, realistic posteriors (including non-Gaussian and possibly multimodal settings) and tasks beyond a single collaborative filtering dataset.
- Assumption clarity: the conditions required on the potential U and the gradient noise to obtain the weak error expansion and bias cancellation are not fully transparent in the main text. It is unclear whether U can be multimodal and whether non-asymptotic bounds hold under common SG-MCMC assumptions.

Questions for the authors
- Assumptions on U: What precise conditions on U are required for Theorems 1 and 2? Do you assume (i) globally Lipschitz gradient, (ii) dissipativity/strong convexity outside a compact set, and/or (iii) bounded higher-order derivatives (for weak error expansions)? Can U be multimodal, and do your results still hold under standard Lyapunov conditions rather than global convexity?
- Gradient noise model: What moment and independence assumptions are required for the stochastic gradients to ensure the bias expansion (e.g., unbiasedness, bounded variance, independence across steps)? Are mini-batches shared across chains to maintain coupling?
- Chain coupling: How exactly are the two chains coupled? Do you use common Gaussian noise and common mini-batches (i.e., common random numbers) across the γ and γ/2 chains? What is the empirical correlation between the two estimators over the run? Please report cross-correlations and how they evolve with time.
- Error decomposition: Can you provide empirical decompositions of bias and variance for representative test functions to show that the net MSE improves in finite time? Does RR increase variance in your setting, and if so, by how much?
- Practical stability: How sensitive is SGRRLD to step-size choice relative to SGLD? Does the coupling constrain adaptive or preconditioned variants?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add fair baselines under matched compute:
  - SGLD with step sizes γ and γ/2.
  - Two parallel SGLD chains with γ/2 whose estimates are averaged (matching the wall-clock of SGRRLD if both can be parallelized).
  - Report MSE of posterior expectation estimates under matched total gradient evaluations and under matched wall-clock.
- Report coupling details and their impact:
  - Use and ablate common Gaussian noise and shared mini-batches between chains.
  - Quantify estimator correlation and its role in variance control and bias cancellation.
- Expand empirical scope:
  - Include at least one non-Gaussian and one multimodal posterior (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression with heavy-tailed priors; mixture or banana-shaped targets) to test robustness beyond the linear Gaussian case.
  - Evaluate downstream quantities beyond RMSE (e.g., log predictive density, calibration) to better reflect posterior quality.
- Clarify theory:
  - Move precise assumptions on U and gradient noise into the main text or a highlighted appendix section; specify whether results require strong convexity, contractivity, or merely Lyapunov/ergodicity conditions compatible with multimodality.
  - State whether the weak error expansion holds for commonly used SG-MCMC settings (with/without replacement mini-batches) and whether your results cover preconditioned versions.
- Measure efficiency:
  - Report effective sample size (ESS) per second and per gradient evaluation for key test functions.
  - Provide confidence intervals to assess whether MovieLens gains are statistically significant.

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent notation for the proposed method (SGRRLD vs. RR-SGLD) throughout.
- Explicitly state the combination rule (e.g., 2 A_{γ/2} − A_{γ}) and how it is computed online.
- Discuss whether RR extrapolation can be integrated with variance-reduced gradients or control variates.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a neat and implementable application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, with solid theoretical motivation and promising synthetic results. However, the current empirical evaluation does not yet convincingly demonstrate advantages under fair compute budgets, and the assumptions behind the theoretical guarantees need clearer exposition. Strengthening the experimental comparisons (particularly against parallel SGLD baselines with matched step sizes and compute), reporting chain coupling and variance effects, and clarifying assumptions would significantly improve the paper’s impact and credibility.
