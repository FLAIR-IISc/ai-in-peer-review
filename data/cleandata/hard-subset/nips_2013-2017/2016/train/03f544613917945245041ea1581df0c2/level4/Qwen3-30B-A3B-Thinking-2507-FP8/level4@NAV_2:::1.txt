

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon recent work in SG-MCMC, particularly SGLD (Welling and Teh, 2011), and connect it to numerical methods for SDEs, specifically extending Talay and Tubaro's (1991) Richardson-Romberg extrapolation results to the stochastic gradient setting. The paper demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}), significantly better than SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), and shows theoretical bounds for bias and MSE that are supported by experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured theoretical analysis and straightforward presentation of the method. The authors carefully explain the relationship between the two parallel chains required for Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, including the importance of correlated Brownian increments to control variance. The technical content is sound, with rigorous proofs for asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties, and the experimental section provides convincing evidence of the method's superiority over SGLD in both small-scale and large-scale settings.

In terms of quality, the paper delivers on its theoretical promises with well-supported claims about convergence rates, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed. The significance of the work lies in its practical improvement to existing SG-MCMC methods without requiring complex modifications, making it immediately applicable to practitioners. The originality is moderate but meaningful, as the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC represents a novel combination of techniques from numerical analysis and MCMC literature, with the paper clearly distinguishing itself from previous work through its convergence rate analysis.

The main weakness is that the method itself is a relatively straightforward application of a known numerical technique to SG-MCMC, which may limit its perceived novelty for readers familiar with either SDE numerical methods or SG-MCMC. However, the theoretical and empirical results are compelling enough to justify the contribution. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a clear technical contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.

The theoretical results are particularly strong, with Theorem 2 providing non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE that are superior to SGLD. The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over SGLD across different problem settings, with the most dramatic improvements observed on synthetic data (where the posterior is analytically available) and more modest but still significant gains on large-scale matrix factorization tasks. The implementation details regarding parallelization of the two chains are practical and well-explained.

I recommend accepting this paper as it makes a solid technical contribution that advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference. The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners working with large-scale MCMC, and the paper provides sufficient theoretical and empirical justification for its use. The minor concerns about figure clarity (specifically Fig 1a) are easily addressed with a brief clarification in the main text.
