This paper proposes applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to reduce the bias introduced by noisy gradient estimates. The resulting algorithm, which the authors call SGRRLD, is shown to have an asymptotically lower bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) than SGLD. Experiments on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the proposed algorithm's advantage over SGLD. However, experiments on the MovieLens dataset show only a slight improvement in test RMSE, and the results are not conclusive.

The paper builds upon existing literature where RR extrapolation has been used to improve the convergence of Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs, and successfully applies this technique to the SG-MCMC setting. The authors prove that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD for the same step size, at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is clearly described and appears straightforward to implement. The synthetic experiments effectively demonstrate SGRRLD's ability to reduce bias in the chosen problem.

However, several aspects of the paper could be strengthened.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**

1.  **Experimental Validation and Baselines:** My main concern lies with the experimental setup and the fairness of the comparisons.
    *   The authors argue that SGRRLD's computational cost is comparable to SGLD because its two chains can be run in parallel. However, a fairer comparison would be against an ensemble of two standard SGLD chains, also run in parallel. It is unclear if SGRRLD would still show a significant RMSE improvement over the averaged estimate from two independent SGLD runs.
    *   Furthermore, the real-data experiment compares SGRRLD (using step sizes γ and γ/2) against SGLD with step size γ. A more rigorous comparison should also include SGLD with the smaller step size γ/2, which is known to have lower bias. To maintain a fair comparison of computational cost, this baseline should also consist of two parallel chains. Without these more direct comparisons, the claimed advantages of SGRRLD are not fully substantiated.
    *   The evidence from real data is currently weak. It would be beneficial to see more experiments on real-world datasets to demonstrate that this simple modification to SGLD can yield significant bias reduction in practice.

2.  **Theoretical Conditions and Intuition:**
    *   While I have not checked the proofs in detail, it would be helpful if the authors could clarify the conditions that the potential function `U` must satisfy for Theorems 1 and 2 to hold. For instance, can these results be applied to multi-modal potentials?
    *   I would appreciate more intuition on why the combination of two chains reduces the order of the error. Intuitively, if the gradient noises are uncorrelated, it seems the two chains would also become uncorrelated quickly, even with correlated driving Gaussian noise. Could the authors comment on how the correlation between the two chains is maintained and whether this was measured in their experiments?
