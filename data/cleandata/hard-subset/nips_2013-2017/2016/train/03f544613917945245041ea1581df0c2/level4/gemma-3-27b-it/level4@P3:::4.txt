This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply a Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, effectively running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes. The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and bounds on bias and mean squared error. Empirical results on synthetic and real datasets support the claim that SGRRLD achieves improved convergence rates compared to SGLD and comparable performance to higher-order integrators.

This work builds upon the growing field of SG-MCMC, spurred by Welling & Teh’s (ICML 2011) seminal work combining Langevin dynamics with stochastic gradient descent. Subsequent research, such as that by Chen et al. (ICML 2014) and Ding et al. (NIPS 2014), has focused on refining these techniques. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of SG-MCMC – its bias – and address it using a well-established numerical technique (Richardson-Romberg extrapolation) from numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, Stochastic Anal. Appl., 1990).  The extension to Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, presented briefly, shows the generality of the approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel and promising approach to reduce bias and improve convergence.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including consistency proofs, central limit theorems, and non-asymptotic error bounds.
* **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm is clearly explained, and the connection to existing SG-MCMC methods is well-established.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets provide compelling evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness. The matrix factorization experiment demonstrates practical benefits.
* **Parallelizability:** The inherent parallelism of SGRRLD, arising from the independent chain generation, is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate Explanation:** The derivation of the specific convergence rates (e.g., O(K<sup>-4/5</sup>)) is not fully transparent within the text. A more detailed explanation of how these rates are obtained from the theorems would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Fairness:** The comparison in Figure 5 regarding the matrix factorization experiment may be somewhat misleading.  Running two chains with differing step sizes inherently requires more computational effort.  A fairer comparison would account for this by adjusting the SGLD runtime, as the authors acknowledge.  The impact of this adjustment should be explicitly addressed.
* **Figure 3 Discrepancy:** The optimal value for α in Figure 3 appears to be 0.2, not 1/3 as stated in the text. Clarification is needed to reconcile this discrepancy. The fluctuating behavior of the optimal curve warrants further explanation; a discussion of the sensitivity to initial conditions or other factors would be helpful.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Several minor issues exist: Eq. 1 uses a symbol different from θ, "RR" is not defined upon first use (line 94), Γ<sub>K</sub> is not defined before its usage (line 131), and the type of smoothness required for the function *f* (line 160) is not specified. The label in Figure 1(b) incorrectly states "Bias vs. time;" it should be "Bias vs. dimension." Vector notation is also inconsistent (use bold letters).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for improving SG-MCMC algorithms.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in SG-MCMC – reducing bias – and offers a practical solution.
* The parallelizable nature of the algorithm is a valuable asset.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The explanation of the convergence rate derivation is lacking clarity.
* The experimental comparison in Figure 5 may be biased and requires further justification.
* Minor technical inconsistencies detract from the overall polish of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The proposed algorithm is novel, well-motivated, and theoretically justified. The empirical results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not fundamentally undermine the value of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the convergence rate derivation.
2.  Discuss the impact of the increased computational cost of SGRRLD in the matrix factorization experiment and consider adjusting the comparison accordingly.
3.  Clarify the discrepancy in Figure 3 and explain the fluctuating behavior of the optimal curve.
4.  Address the minor technical issues related to notation and figure labels.
