

This paper introduces a novel approach to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods through Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The authors propose Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, then combines their outputs to achieve improved convergence properties. The key insight is that by applying RR extrapolation to the SGLD framework, the bias can be reduced from O(γ) to O(γ²), while maintaining reasonable variance through correlated Brownian motion increments.

The paper builds on the established work of Welling and Teh (2011) who introduced SGLD, and extends the recent theoretical analysis of SG-MCMC methods by Chen et al. (2015). The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³), matching the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using only first-order discretizations. This represents a significant improvement in convergence rates for practical SG-MCMC applications.

Theoretical analysis is thorough, with the authors establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The key insight that correlated Brownian increments (specifically, Z^(γ)_k = (Z^(γ/2)_2k-1 + Z^(γ/2)_2k)/√2) minimize variance is well-justified by extending results from Talay and Tubaro (1991) to SG-MCMC settings. The analysis also carefully addresses the technical conditions under which the method applies, including the requirement that the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm satisfies π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²).

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendation. Results consistently show SGRRLD achieving significantly lower bias and MSE than SGLD across various parameter settings. The matrix factorization experiment is particularly compelling, showing SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in approximately half the wall-clock time, leveraging the inherent parallelism of the RR approach.

Strengths of this paper include:
1. The simplicity and elegance of the proposed approach, which requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC implementations
2. The generality of the method, which can be applied to various SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD
3. The strong theoretical guarantees that are well-supported by empirical results
4. The practical demonstration of computational efficiency through parallel implementation

The paper's clarity is excellent, with clear mathematical exposition and well-structured presentation. The theoretical results are presented with sufficient rigor, and the experimental design effectively validates the theoretical claims. The authors provide detailed implementation notes and a clear description of the parallel nature of the method.

I have a few minor suggestions for improvement:
1. The paper could more explicitly characterize the conditions under which RR extrapolation applies to other SG-MCMC algorithms. While it mentions "weak error and ergodicity conditions," a more precise statement would help readers understand the scope of applicability.
2. The experimental comparison with SGD in the matrix factorization task would strengthen the practical argument. While Bayesian methods are not always preferred in practice, demonstrating that SGRRLD outperforms optimization-based approaches would better highlight the value of the Bayesian approach.
3. The paper could explore whether the computational inefficiency of running one chain twice as long could be mitigated through alternative parallelization schemes (e.g., using three chains as suggested in the draft review).

The novelty of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is significant. While RR extrapolation is well-known in numerical analysis, its application to SG-MCMC represents a meaningful contribution to the field, particularly given the widespread use of SG-MCMC methods in large-scale Bayesian inference.

The significance of this work is substantial. By achieving the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods using only first-order discretizations, SGRRLD provides a practical path to more accurate Bayesian inference in large-scale settings without the computational overhead of higher-order methods. The parallel nature of the approach also makes it well-suited for distributed computing environments, which is increasingly important for modern machine learning applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the method is both simple to implement and broadly applicable. The improvements in convergence rates represent a meaningful advance in the field.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making complex theoretical concepts accessible to readers.

Originality: While RR extrapolation is known in numerical analysis, its application to SG-MCMC is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

Significance: The method provides a practical and efficient way to improve the accuracy of SG-MCMC without increasing computational complexity, making it highly relevant to the machine learning community.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the experimental comparison with SGD and to clarify the scope of applicability. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
