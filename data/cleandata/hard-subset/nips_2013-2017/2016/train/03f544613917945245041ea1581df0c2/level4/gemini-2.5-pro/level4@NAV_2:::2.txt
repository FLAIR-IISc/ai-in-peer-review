This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias inherent in methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical technique for accelerating convergence in numerical analysis. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and linearly combining their outputs to cancel out the leading-order error term. This work builds upon existing literature that has applied RR extrapolation to improve Monte Carlo estimates for standard Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs) [21, 23], and represents, to my knowledge, the first application of this technique to the SG-MCMC setting. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves improved asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence rates for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) compared to SGLD, reaching an O(K⁻⁴/⁵) rate that matches the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Potential Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant idea. It offers a new, conceptually simple path to higher-order accuracy without requiring the implementation of complex high-order integrators. If the theoretical benefits are borne out in practice, this could be a widely adopted technique.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is technically sound, providing a thorough convergence analysis that includes a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE. These results formally establish the improved convergence rates of SGRRLD over SGLD.
*   **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed algorithm is clearly explained and straightforward to implement as a modification to existing SGLD code. Its inherently parallel structure is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Convincing Synthetic Experiments:** The experiments on the linear Gaussian model effectively validate the theoretical claims. They clearly demonstrate SGRRLD's ability to reduce bias and MSE compared to SGLD in a controlled setting where ground truth is known. The application to SGHMC also successfully illustrates the generality of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection and questions for the authors):**

*   **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** My primary concern is the experimental setup for evaluating computational cost. The authors claim SGRRLD takes roughly the same wall-clock time as SGLD by running its two chains in parallel. However, a more appropriate baseline using the same computational budget would be to run two independent SGLD chains in parallel and average their results. It is unclear whether the observed improvement of SGRRLD over a single SGLD chain is due to the RR extrapolation or simply an ensembling effect from using twice the computation. I would urge the authors to include a comparison against an ensemble of two parallel SGLD chains.
*   **Choice of Baseline Parameters:** The real-data experiments compare SGRRLD (using step sizes γ and γ/2) against SGLD with step size γ. Since a smaller step size generally leads to lower bias, a crucial baseline is missing: SGLD running with step size γ/2. To isolate the effect of the RR extrapolation, SGRRLD should be compared against SGLD chains running with both step sizes γ and γ/2.
*   **Limited Real-Data Evidence:** While the results on the MovieLens datasets show an improvement, the gain in test RMSE appears modest compared to the dramatic improvements seen in the synthetic experiments. To substantiate the claim of practical significance, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness on a wider range of real-world problems and models.
*   **Clarity on Assumptions and Intuition:**
    *   Could the authors briefly summarize in the main paper the key assumptions required for Theorems 1 and 2 to hold? For example, are there any restrictions on the potential function U, such as convexity or uni-modality?
    *   The variance reduction relies on correlating the Brownian motion across the two chains. However, the stochastic gradient noise is a dominant source of error and remains independent. Could the authors provide more intuition on why correlating only the Gaussian noise is sufficient to control the variance of the final estimator and achieve the improved rates? It seems plausible that the uncorrelated gradient noise could quickly de-correlate the two chains. Do the authors observe strong correlations between the chains in practice?
