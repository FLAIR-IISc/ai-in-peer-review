

This paper proposes leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator, to accelerate convergence in SG-MCMC algorithms. The approach requires running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with distinct step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injections. Asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties are rigorously established. Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models validate the bias and MSE bounds derived in the theorems, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods over standard SGLD. Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a solid technical contribution and clear presentation. The work makes a valid contribution to SG-MCMC methods and effectively contextualizes the proposed technique relative to prior approaches. However, two main concerns arise: (1) The novelty appears incremental, as the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC was analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has previously been applied to approximate invariant distributions in [23]. (2) The practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited due to unaddressed communication costs. The parallel implementation requires correlated Brownian motion, necessitating communication between the two chains at every step. The authors assume zero communication cost, which warrants explicit discussion. It is unclear how computational resources were allocated for SGRRLD versus SGLD in experiments: if SGLD used the same total resources as the two SGRRLD chains, how were resources split between the two chains? Conversely, if SGLD was run on dedicated resources, the comparison is not practically fair. Given that stochastic gradient computation dominates SGMCMC runtime, it is unclear how partial resource allocation for gradient computation across the two SGRRLD chains could yield lower wall-clock time than full resource allocation for a single SGLD chain. The authors should provide detailed implementation setup. Additionally, an alternative comparison with two embarrassingly parallel SGLD chains (without communication) should be included. Minor comments: (1) "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is duplicated at lines 25 and 70.
