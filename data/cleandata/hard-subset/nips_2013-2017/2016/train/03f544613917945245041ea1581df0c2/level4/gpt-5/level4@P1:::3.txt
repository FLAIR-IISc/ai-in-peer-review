Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the well-known bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms such as SGLD. The authors propose a simple and broadly applicable remedy based on Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, where Chain 2 uses half the step size of Chain 1 and is run for twice as many steps. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated as follows: if βk denotes the noise at step k in Chain 2, then the noise αk for Chain 1 is set to αk = (β2k−1 + β2k)/sqrt(2). The final estimator for Eπ[f] subtracts the estimate from Chain 1 from the estimate from Chain 2 (the RR combination), which cancels the leading-order discretization error. The result is an estimator with bias O(gamma^2) instead of O(gamma) (gamma is the step size), and a corresponding improvement in MSE. The method is deliberately lightweight: it does not require modifying the underlying SG-MCMC dynamics beyond running two step-size schedules and correlating the Gaussian increments.

While RR extrapolation is classical in numerical analysis and has been applied to SDE discretizations, it has not, to my knowledge, been adopted in the SG-MCMC literature. The paper provides theoretical guarantees (consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE) and empirical evidence on both synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization. The approach is generic and should transfer to other SG-MCMC variants (the paper also demonstrates an SGHMC version).

Quality:
The theoretical results are clearly stated and, insofar as I can assess, technically sound. The key insight—correlating Brownian increments to control variance while RR cancels the O(gamma) bias—is well justified. The paper derives asymptotic consistency and a CLT for the RR estimator, and provides non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds showing improved rates over standard first-order SG-MCMC. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model validate the bias/MSE trends, and the matrix factorization experiments demonstrate practical gains under equal wall-clock time.

Clarity:
The paper is well written and the main idea is easy to follow. The description of the two coupled chains and the correlation scheme for the Gaussian noise is clear. I found the presentation accessible, though a concise algorithm box (pseudo-code) early in the paper would help practitioners implement the method without reading ahead.

Originality:
RR extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, but its explicit use in SG-MCMC appears novel. Applying RR with correlated noise to reduce bias and improve MSE in stochastic-gradient settings is a new and potentially influential contribution for the SG-MCMC community.

Significance:
The method is simple, general, and practical. It can be applied to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms and benefits from parallelism, which is important for large-scale Bayesian inference. The theoretical improvements and the empirical results suggest it will be of interest to both researchers and practitioners.

Comments and questions:
- Scope of applicability: Can the RR scheme be directly applied to any SG-MCMC method? Beyond the weak error expansion π_gamma(f) = π(f) + C*gamma + O(gamma^2), are there other concrete assumptions (e.g., ergodicity, smoothness, growth conditions) practitioners should check? A brief checklist and examples would be helpful.

- Beyond SG-MCMC: Could a similar RR idea be used with other approximate MCMC methods that introduce bias, such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests? What about distributed SGLD variants—are there pitfalls or changes needed to preserve the variance control from the correlated noise?

- Parallel efficiency: Because Chain 2 must take two steps per step of Chain 1, the larger-step chain would be idle in a naïve synchronous setup. Would running three chains (one with step size gamma, two with gamma/2), all for the same number of steps, preserve the benefits while improving hardware utilization? How would this affect the estimator’s variance?

- Multi-level extensions: Is there a practical extension to more than two step sizes (i.e., multilevel/multi-step RR)? The paper briefly mentions recursive RR; a short discussion of complexity vs. variance trade-offs would be valuable.

- Correlation of stochastic gradients: The paper shows the importance of correlating the Gaussian increments. What is the effect of correlating the mini-batches across chains? Is variance minimized when Chain 1 at step k and Chain 2 at steps 2k−1 and 2k use the same data mini-batch, or is this negligible compared to the Brownian correlation?

- Experimental baselines: For the matrix factorization experiments (Figure 5), it would strengthen the case to run until convergence and include an SGD baseline. This would help demonstrate that the Bayesian approach (and your improved sampler) yields benefits over optimization in a setting where uncertainty matters, and would place the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD in clearer perspective.

- Presentation: An algorithm box for SGRRLD (and the SGHMC variant) would make the paper more accessible to a broad audience.

Pros:
- Simple, general technique that plugs into existing SG-MCMC methods with minimal changes.
- Solid theory (consistency, CLT, non-asymptotic bounds) and clear intuition.
- Empirical results align with theory on synthetic data and show wall-clock gains on large-scale matrix factorization.
- Naturally parallelizable due to independent chains and thus well suited for modern hardware.

Cons:
- Practical guidance on applicability conditions and the role of mini-batch correlation could be more explicit.
- Some inefficiency in synchronous execution unless parallelism is exploited; discussion of multi-chain scheduling strategies would help.
- Experimental section would be stronger with convergence runs and an SGD baseline to contextualize gains.

Overall, this is a clear and well-motivated paper that introduces a broadly useful idea to the SG-MCMC literature. With minor clarifications and stronger baselines, it could have substantial impact.
