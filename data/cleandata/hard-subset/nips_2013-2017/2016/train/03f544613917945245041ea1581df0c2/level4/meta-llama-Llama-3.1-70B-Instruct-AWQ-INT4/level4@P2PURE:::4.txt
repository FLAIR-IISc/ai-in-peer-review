This paper proposes an improvement to existing Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods by introducing a Richardson-Romberg scheme. This scheme enhances the convergence rates in terms of bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), and its implementation requires only minimal modifications to traditional SG-MCMC. The paper is supported by solid theoretical results, presenting a promising method for improving the convergence rates of standard SG-MCMC algorithms. The theoretical foundation of the proposed Romberg-Richardson scheme appears robust.

However, there are several concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the derivation of the specific convergence rates from the theorem, as explained in the section "Explain Fatal Flaws," is not entirely clear. Furthermore, there are practical concerns regarding the matrix factorization experiment results. Running the proposed method on a single-core computer would take approximately three times longer than standard SG-MCMC, due to the use of two chains, one of which requires twice as many steps. This discrepancy should be taken into account when interpreting the plots in Figure 5. Specifically, the time for Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3, which would reduce the difference between SGLD and Stochastic Gradient with Romberg-Richardson Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD).

Additionally, there are several minor issues and questions. In Figure 3, the bias plot suggests that the best convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, rather than α = 1/3, as stated in the text. Moreover, the optimal curve appears to fluctuate significantly, which may affect the convergence rate. It is also unclear why the term 1/Kγ does not dominate when the step size is too small for SGLD, as mentioned in line 256.

Minor comments include: Equation 1 uses a different symbol than θ; the abbreviation "RR" is not defined before its use; Γ_K is not defined in line 131; the type of smoothness for the function f is not specified in line 160; and Figure 1(b) plots Bias vs. time, not dimension, which seems inconsistent with the text. Finally, it is recommended to use bold letters to represent vectors for clarity.
