Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while controlling variance. The core idea is to run two SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), coupled through correlated Gaussian noise, and to combine their estimates via RR extrapolation. The authors prove asymptotic consistency and a CLT, and derive non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE. Under standard decreasing step-size schedules, SGRRLD is shown to achieve a faster MSE rate O(K−4/5) than first-order SG-MCMC methods (typically O(K−2/3)). They further show that, with fixed steps, the bias and variance scale as O(γ2) and O(γ4), respectively, and that variance is minimized by perfectly correlating the Gaussian increments across the two chains (Σ = Id). The method is demonstrated on a Gaussian model and on large-scale matrix factorization; an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC) is also evaluated and compared against SGHMC and a higher-order SGHMC-s integrator.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and widely applicable: requires only running two standard SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and combining them; minimal code changes.
- Solid theoretical analysis: consistency, CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds, with clear comparison to SGLD and to higher-order integrator methods. The optimization of noise correlation (Σ = Id) is well-motivated.
- Practical relevance: generic wrapper applicable beyond SGLD (SGHMC example), and compatible with parallel/distributed architectures.
- Empirical support: synthetic experiments match the predicted bias/MSE improvements; large-scale matrix factorization results show better test RMSE within comparable wall-clock time.

Weaknesses and questions
- How the rates are obtained. The paper states that with γk ∝ k−α, the estimator converges at O(K−((1−α)/2)∧(2α)), with the optimal α = 1/5 yielding O(K−2/5) (CLT-based), and that the non-asymptotic MSE bound is O(K−(4α)∧(1−α)), optimized at α = 1/5 to give O(K−4/5). Please make explicit:
  - Which terms in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 dominate for general α, and how the exponents are combined to reach the min operations.
  - The precise smoothness and growth conditions on f and U under which these rates hold, and whether they differ between Theorem 1 (CLT) and Theorem 2 (finite-time bounds).

- Figure 3 and optimal α. In the bias plot, the fastest empirical convergence appears to correspond to the red curve (α = 0.2), not α = 1/3 as stated. Also, the “optimal” curve fluctuates heavily, making its rate hard to read. Please:
  - Reconcile this with the theory (the bias bound suggests α = 1/3 is optimal; the MSE bound suggests α = 1/5).
  - Explain the observed fluctuations (e.g., residual variance at finite K, sensitivity to constants, or step-size–dependent mixing), and consider averaging over more runs or plotting confidence bands.

- Small step-size regime. The paper explains that when γ is very small, a 1/(Kγ) term dominates the bias/MSE (Theorem 2), and this behavior is visible for SGRRLD. Why is a similar upturn not shown for SGLD in the corresponding plots? Please clarify whether SGLD exhibits the same phenomenon but at different constants/scales, or whether the plotting range masks it.

- Fair comparison in large-scale experiments. In SGRRLD each “macro-iteration” performs one step of the γ chain and two steps of the γ/2 chain (three stochastic gradient computations total). On a single core, this would make SGRRLD about three times slower per macro-iteration than SGLD; even on two cores, chain 2 still performs two sequential steps. Although you parallelize the two chains as separate processes and report similar wall-clock times, this leverages additional hardware that SGLD cannot use. For a fair comparison under equal computational budget, please also report performance as a function of:
  - Total number of stochastic gradient evaluations (or total processed data points), or
  - CPU-hours or core-seconds,
and adjust the SGLD curve accordingly (e.g., scaled by the relative number of gradient computations). This will likely narrow the gap observed in Figure 5.

- Coupling of stochastic gradients. You note that both chains must use unbiased gradient estimators with the same law L (e.g., same minibatch size) to avoid introducing O(γ) bias. It would be useful to state explicitly whether you reuse the same minibatch indices across chains at matched times to reduce variance further, or sample them independently, and whether this affects the constants in the bounds.

Specific comments and clarity/notation
- Equation (1): you switch from θ to ϑt. Please use consistent notation or explain the distinction.
- Line 94: RR should be defined at first use.
- Line 131: Define ΓK when it first appears; later you define Γ(n)K = ∑K k=1 γn k+1 and ΓK = Γ(1)K.
- Line 160 and Theorem 1: “smooth function f” — please specify the required smoothness. The sketch uses g ∈ C9(Rd); what are the exact conditions on f (e.g., f ∈ Ck with polynomial growth, for which k)?
- Line 249: The text refers to Figure 1(b) as bias vs. time; per the description, the dimension-varying plot should be Figure 1(c). Please correct the reference.
- Vectors: please use boldface for vectors consistently.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and simple extrapolation-based improvement for SG-MCMC with clear theoretical advantages.
- General technique applicable to multiple SG-MCMC variants.
- Strong theoretical backing (CLT and finite-time bounds) and convincing synthetic results.

Cons for acceptance
- Some ambiguity in how the claimed rates are derived and how to choose α in practice; apparent mismatch in Figure 3.
- Empirical comparison on large-scale data relies on parallelization benefits; fairness under equal compute is not fully established.

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and technically solid paper proposing a practical method that can materially improve SG-MCMC accuracy with modest implementation effort and clear theoretical guarantees. Addressing the points above—especially clarifying the rate derivations and providing compute-fair comparisons—would strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on these clarifications.
