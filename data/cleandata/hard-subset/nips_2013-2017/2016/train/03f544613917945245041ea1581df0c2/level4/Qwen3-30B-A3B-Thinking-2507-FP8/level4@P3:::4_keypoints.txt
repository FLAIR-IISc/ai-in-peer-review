- Introduces SGRRLD: applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two chains with different step sizes to reduce bias while maintaining variance.  
- Theoretically shows SGRRLD attains bias O(γ²) and MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵), improving over SGLD's bias O(γ) and MSE O(K⁻²/³).  
- Builds on Welling & Teh (2011) and Chen et al. (2015), adapting Talay–Tubaro style analysis to stochastic-gradient settings.  
- Provides rigorous technical results: asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Empirical results on synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization support the theoretical claims.  
- Key advantage: achieves higher-order accuracy using only first-order discretizations and is naturally parallelizable.  
- Computational trade-off: requires running two chains in parallel, which may cost ~1.5× time on a single core and needs clarification in experiments.  
- Experimental concern: Figure 5’s comparison with SGLD does not clearly account for parallel hardware vs. single-core timing.  
- The reported optimal MSE exponent (α≈1/5) appears noisy in Figure 3; authors should explain fluctuations or experimental variance.  
- The MSE term 1/(Kγ) claimed to dominate for very small γ is not clearly visible in SGLD results and needs discussion.  
- Minor technical/presentation issues: RR extrapolation not defined on first use, Γ_K undefined, smoothness requirements for f unspecified, and Figure 1(b) axis mislabeled.  
- Notation suggestion: use boldface for vectors for consistency.  
- Method is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms (e.g., SGHMC) and connects well to numerical-analysis literature.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify experimental setup, address noisy empirical curves, and fix notation/definition issues.
