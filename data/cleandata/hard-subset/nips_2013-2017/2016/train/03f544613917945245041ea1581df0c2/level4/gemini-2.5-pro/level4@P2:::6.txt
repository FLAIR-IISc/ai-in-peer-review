This paper introduces a method to reduce the bias and improve the convergence rate of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The proposed approach, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The core idea involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes. Crucially, these chains use correlated Brownian motion to control the variance of the combined estimator. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), which show superior convergence rates compared to standard SGLD. This work builds upon the established literature of SG-MCMC [1] and connects two distinct research lines: the use of higher-order integrators to improve SG-MCMC [10] and the application of RR extrapolation to the invariant measures of SDEs [23]. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are solid and well-supported. The derivation of improved non-asymptotic convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) is a significant result, demonstrating a clear advantage over the O(K⁻²/³) rate of standard SGLD. The analysis is comprehensive and provides strong justification for the proposed method.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the RR extrapolation technique and adapting it to the SG-MCMC setting.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the theoretical claims. The synthetic Gaussian model experiments provide a clear verification of the improved bias and MSE bounds. Furthermore, the large-scale matrix factorization experiment on real-world datasets demonstrates the practical utility and computational efficiency of the proposed method in a relevant application.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

While the paper presents a valuable contribution, I have some concerns regarding its originality and the experimental setup, which could be clarified by the authors.

1.  **Originality:** The primary contribution is the application of RR extrapolation, a well-known numerical analysis technique, to the SG-MCMC context. While novel in this specific combination, the core components have been explored separately. The benefits of higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC were analyzed in [10], and RR extrapolation for approximating invariant distributions of SDEs was studied in [23]. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could further emphasize the conceptual novelty of their work beyond the direct combination of these known techniques.

2.  **Practicality and Experimental Fairness:** The parallel nature of the algorithm raises important practical questions about the experimental comparison.
    *   **Communication Cost:** The use of correlated Brownian increments (Eq. 7 or 8) implies communication or synchronization between the two parallel chains at every iteration. The paper seems to assume this is cost-free, stating that the chains are run as "two independent processes". Could the authors comment on the potential communication overhead, especially in a distributed computing environment where this might become a bottleneck?
    *   **Resource Allocation and Baselines:** The comparison with SGLD needs more detail to assess its fairness. The wall-clock time comparison is compelling, but it is unclear how computational resources were allocated. For example, if SGRRLD utilized two CPU cores (one for each chain) and SGLD used only one, the comparison is more about hardware utilization than algorithmic efficiency per unit of computation. The main computational cost in SG-MCMC is typically the gradient estimation. A more direct competitor for a two-core SGRRLD setup might be running two *independent* SGLD chains in an embarrassingly parallel fashion and averaging their results. A comparison against such a baseline would provide a stronger argument for the necessity of the RR extrapolation and the correlated noise structure. Could the authors please provide more details on the implementation and resource allocation?

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper proposes a novel and principled way to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC methods.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and demonstrates significantly improved convergence rates.
*   The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data are strong and support the theory.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-presented.

**Arguments for Rejection (or areas for improvement):**
*   The novelty could be considered incremental, as it combines existing techniques from numerical analysis and SG-MCMC literature.
*   The practical comparison with SGLD may not be entirely fair without further clarification on resource allocation and communication costs. The lack of comparison to an "embarrassingly parallel" SGLD baseline is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the specific benefits of the RR scheme.

Overall, this is a very good paper with a solid contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. My concerns are primarily about positioning the work and clarifying the experimental setup. I believe the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance, but it could be made even stronger if the authors address the points raised above.
