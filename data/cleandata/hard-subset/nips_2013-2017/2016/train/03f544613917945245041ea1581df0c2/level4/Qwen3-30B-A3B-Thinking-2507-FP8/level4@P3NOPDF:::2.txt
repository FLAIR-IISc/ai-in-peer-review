

Review: This paper introduces SGRRLD, a novel stochastic gradient MCMC method that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias from gradient estimation noise. The authors demonstrate theoretically that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD under standard assumptions, with a faster convergence rate for the same step size. This is achieved by running two parallel Markov chains and combining their outputs via RR extrapolation. The algorithm is clearly described and straightforward to implement.  

The synthetic experiments on a Linear Gaussian Model compellingly illustrate SGRRLD’s advantage in bias reduction, with results aligning with the theoretical claims. However, the real-data experiments on MovieLens yield only marginal improvements in test RMSE, raising questions about the practical significance of the method. The authors compare SGRRLD (with step size γ) against SGLD (with step size γ), but this comparison is problematic. Since SGLD with step size γ/2 inherently has lower bias (and would require similar computational time), a fairer benchmark would compare SGRRLD (γ) against:  
(i) SGLD (γ/2), or  
(ii) Two parallel SGLD chains (each with step size γ, combined via averaging).  
The current comparison overlooks these alternatives, potentially overstating SGRRLD’s advantage. The authors claim computation time is comparable to SGLD due to parallelization, but running two parallel SGLD chains within the same time budget would provide a direct baseline for the proposed method’s efficiency gain.  

Further, the theoretical analysis requires clarification. The authors state that potential function U must satisfy conditions for Theorems 1 and 2, but do not specify whether U can be multi-modal. This is critical, as multi-modality is common in real-world applications and may affect the validity of the RR extrapolation. Additionally, the authors note that gradient noise is uncorrelated across chains, yet the theoretical benefit relies on the correlation structure of the two chains. It would be valuable to know whether the authors observe significant correlation between the parallel chains in practice and how this impacts the error reduction.  

The paper’s contribution is theoretically sound and well-motivated, but the empirical evaluation lacks rigor. The marginal gains on MovieLens suggest the method’s benefits may be context-dependent, and additional real-data experiments are needed to validate its general utility. For instance, testing on larger datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) or with more complex models would strengthen the case. The current results do not convincingly demonstrate that SGRRLD provides a *significant* bias reduction over SGLD in practical scenarios.  

To address these concerns, the authors should:  
1. Re-evaluate MovieLens results using fairer baselines (SGLD with γ/2 or two parallel chains).  
2. Conduct additional experiments on diverse real-world datasets.  
3. Clarify the theoretical conditions on U (e.g., multi-modality) and provide empirical evidence of chain correlation behavior.  
4. Discuss the computational trade-offs more precisely—specifically, whether the MSE reduction justifies the doubled chain overhead in time-constrained settings.  

The paper’s core idea is promising, and the RR extrapolation technique is appropriately adapted to SG-MCMC. However, the current empirical validation does not sufficiently support the claim that SGRRLD is broadly advantageous over SGLD. With more rigorous experiments and theoretical clarification, this work could make a meaningful contribution to scalable Bayesian inference. As presented, the results are suggestive but not definitive, particularly for real-world applications where computational efficiency is paramount.  

The writing is clear, and the algorithm is well-motivated. The theoretical framework is plausible, though the experimental design undermines the practical impact of the proposed method. The authors should address the benchmarking issues before the work can be considered robust.
