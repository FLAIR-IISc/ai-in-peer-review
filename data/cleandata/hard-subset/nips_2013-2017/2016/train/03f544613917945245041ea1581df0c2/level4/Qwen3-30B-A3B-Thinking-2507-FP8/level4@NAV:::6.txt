

This paper introduces the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator, to accelerate convergence in SG-MCMC algorithms. The approach necessitates running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with distinct step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection. Theoretical guarantees for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties are established. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model validate the bias and MSE bounds derived in the theorems, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates the practical efficacy of the proposed method (SGRRLD) over standard SGLD. Overall, this is a strong contribution with a well-presented technique and effective contextualization within existing SGMCMC literature. However, two primary concerns require attention:  
(1) The novelty appears incremental, as the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC was previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for invariant distribution approximation was employed in [23].  
(2) The practical applicability may be constrained due to unaddressed communication costs. The requirement for correlated Brownian motion mandates communication between chains at every step, yet the authors assume zero communication overhead. It is essential to clarify:  
- How computational resources are allocated for SGRRLD (two chains) versus SGLD (one chain). Specifically, is SGLD executed using the combined resources of the two SGRRLD chains? If not, the comparison lacks fairness for practical deployment. If yes, how are resources partitioned between the two SGRRLD chains?  
- Given that stochastic gradient computation dominates SGMCMC cost, how does partial resource allocation per SGRRLD chain yield reduced wall-clock time compared to full-resource SGLD? Additional implementation details are necessary.  
- A fair comparison should include an embarrassingly parallel two-chain SGLD baseline (without communication).
