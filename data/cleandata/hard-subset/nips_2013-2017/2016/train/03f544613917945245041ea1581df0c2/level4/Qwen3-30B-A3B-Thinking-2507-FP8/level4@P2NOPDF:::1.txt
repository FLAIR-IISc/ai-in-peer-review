

This paper bridges the MCMC and numerical SDE literatures by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to big-data MCMC methods, with a specific focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The authors present a novel SGLD-RRE algorithm, establish theoretical convergence guarantees, and demonstrate its empirical efficacy. A key strength is the paper’s clear articulation of connections between these two fields, which could foster cross-pollination of ideas. The theoretical analysis rigorously identifies conditions under which SGLD-RRE outperforms standard SGLD, while the empirical results show significant gains on toy problems and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale settings. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and presents a straightforward adaptation of RRE to SGLD—making it computationally practical for real-world applications.  

The work’s most compelling contributions lie in its interdisciplinary linkage, rigorous theoretical foundation, and strong empirical validation. The authors effectively demonstrate that RRE can mitigate bias in SGLD without substantially increasing computational overhead, a valuable property for scalable Bayesian inference. The clarity of exposition and logical flow enhance the paper’s accessibility, making it suitable for both MCMC and SDE practitioners. While the method itself is not revolutionary, it offers a practical, immediately applicable refinement to existing SGLD implementations, which practitioners are likely to adopt.  

Two minor points warrant clarification:  
1. **Figure 1a**: The plot depicts a Gaussian approximation using the empirical posterior mean and variance, not the actual posterior distribution. This should be explicitly stated to avoid misinterpretation by readers unfamiliar with the approximation.  
2. **Figure 2a**: The non-monotonic bias behavior as stepsize decreases is counterintuitive and merits a brief discussion. The authors should clarify whether this stems from the interplay between discretization error and stochastic noise, or other algorithmic factors.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the practicality of SGLD through a principled, well-executed extension. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the paper’s readability significantly enhances its impact. While the method does not fundamentally alter current MCMC practice, it provides a valuable tool for practitioners seeking more accurate posterior approximations in large-scale settings. The work is deserving of publication in its current form, with the noted clarifications addressing minor presentation issues.
