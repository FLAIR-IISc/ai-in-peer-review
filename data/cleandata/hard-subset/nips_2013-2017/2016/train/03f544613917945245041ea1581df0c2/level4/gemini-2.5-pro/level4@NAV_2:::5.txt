This paper introduces a novel sampling algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to mitigate the bias inherent in Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical acceleration technique, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This is achieved by running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to form a new estimator. The authors show that using correlated noise between the two chains effectively controls the variance of the resulting estimator. This work builds upon the established literature of SG-MCMC (e.g., Welling and Teh, 2011) and connects it with numerical analysis techniques for SDEs (e.g., Talay and Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), with the goal of achieving the accuracy of higher-order integrators (Chen et al., 2015) using only first-order updates.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles the well-known and significant problem of discretization bias in SG-MCMC algorithms, which is a primary factor limiting their accuracy in practice. The proposed solution is both elegant and practical.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical contributions are substantial. The authors provide a formal convergence analysis, proving that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a Central Limit Theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This theoretical improvement is a key strength of the paper.
*   **Originality:** While SGLD and RR extrapolation are existing methods, their combination in the stochastic gradient setting is novel. The analysis of how to control variance via correlated noise is a particularly valuable contribution that makes the approach viable. The work demonstrates how a classical numerical method can be successfully adapted to a modern large-scale machine learning context.
*   **Clarity and Empirical Validation:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained. The experiments are thorough and effectively support the theoretical claims.
    *   The synthetic experiments on a Gaussian model convincingly demonstrate the theoretical improvements in bias and MSE as a function of step size and dimensionality.
    *   The experiments verifying the optimal convergence rates provide strong empirical validation for the theory.
    *   The application to SGHMC and comparison against a higher-order integrator (SGHMC-s) effectively shows that the proposed method can achieve the accuracy of more complex schemes.
    *   The large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real-world datasets showcase the practical utility and computational efficiency of SGRRLD, highlighting its suitability for parallel architectures.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Algorithm Presentation:** There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the update rule for "Chain 2" in Equation (5). The second step of the update, for `θ(γ/2)_{2k+2}`, evaluates the stochastic gradient `∇Ũ(γ/2)_{2k+2}` at `θ(γ/2)_{2k+1}`. A more standard Euler-Maruyama step would evaluate the gradient at the beginning of the interval, i.e., at `θ(γ/2)_{2k+1}`. However, the update for `θ(γ/2)_{2k+1}` evaluates the gradient `∇Ũ(γ/2)_{2k+1}` at `θ(γ/2)_{2k+1}` which seems to be a typo and should likely be `θ(γ/2)_{2k}`. The authors should clarify this and ensure that the provided pseudo-code in the supplement is consistent with the intended algorithm.
*   **Comparison with Higher-Order Integrators:** The comparison with a higher-order integrator for SGHMC (SGHMC-s) is excellent and supports a central claim of the paper. To make the argument even more compelling, it would be beneficial to include a direct comparison of SGRRLD against an SGLD variant that uses a higher-order integrator, if a standard one exists. This would provide a more direct baseline for the SGLD-focused claims.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in a popular class of algorithms (SG-MCMC).
    2.  The proposed method is novel in its context, theoretically well-founded, and demonstrates superior convergence rates.
    3.  The method is inherently parallelizable, making it computationally efficient and highly relevant for large-scale applications.
    4.  The empirical results are comprehensive and strongly support the theoretical claims across both synthetic and real-world tasks.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  The core ideas (SGLD, RR extrapolation) are not new, but their synthesis and the accompanying analysis are the primary contributions.
    2.  There are minor clarity issues in the mathematical formulation of the algorithm that should be addressed.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated and effective method for improving SG-MCMC. The theoretical and empirical contributions are solid and significant. With minor clarifications to the algorithm's presentation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
