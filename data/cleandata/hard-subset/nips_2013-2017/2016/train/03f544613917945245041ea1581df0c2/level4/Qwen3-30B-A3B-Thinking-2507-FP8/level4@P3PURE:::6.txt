

This paper proposes leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator, to accelerate convergence in SG-MCMC algorithms. The approach requires running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection. The authors establish both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. Experiments on synthetic Gaussian models validate the bias and MSE bounds from the theorems, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed SGRRLD algorithm compared to standard SGLD. Overall, the paper presents a well-written contribution with a clear technical presentation and effective contextualization within existing SGMCMC literature. The proposed technique constitutes a meaningful contribution to the field.

I have two main concerns regarding novelty and experimental fairness. First, the novelty appears incremental: the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC was previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been applied to approximate invariant distributions in [23]. Second, the practical applicability of the method may be limited due to insufficient experimental comparison. The algorithm requires parallel implementation with correlated Brownian motion, necessitating communication between chains at every step. However, the paper does not account for communication costs, implicitly assuming zero overhead. This should be explicitly acknowledged. Furthermore, the experimental resource allocation is unclear: is the single SGLD chain run on the same computational resources as the two parallel SGRRLD chains? If not, the comparison is unfair for practical deployment. If yes, how are resources split between the two SGRRLD chains? Given that SG-MCMC computation is dominated by stochastic gradient evaluation, it is unclear how partial resource allocation for the two SGRRLD chains could achieve lower wall-clock time than full resource allocation for SGLD. The authors should provide detailed implementation setup specifications. Additionally, an alternative comparison to running two independent SGLD chains (without communication) would strengthen the practical evaluation.

Minor comment: The phrase "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is duplicated on lines 25 and 70.
