Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) offers a computationally efficient approach to Bayesian posterior inference in large-scale datasets and has garnered significant attention recently. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias – they do not converge precisely to the target posterior distribution. This paper demonstrates that Richardson-Romberg extrapolation can effectively reduce this bias. The core idea is straightforward: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with the second chain employing half the step size of the first, but running for twice the number of iterations. The Gaussian noise injected into the chains is correlated, defined as alphak = (beta{2k-1) + beta{2k})/sqrt(2), where alphak and betak represent the injected noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution, the authors propose subtracting the estimate obtained from Chain 1 from the estimate obtained from Chain 2. This yields an estimate with a reduced bias of O(gamma^2), compared to the O(gamma) bias obtained from either chain individually (where gamma represents the step size). They also prove a corresponding reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates. I appreciate the method’s simplicity and ease of implementation (as summarized below), and its minimal impact on existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Furthermore, its broad applicability across various SG-MCMC algorithms suggests potentially high impact within the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

Novelty: While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, it appears to be relatively unknown within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

Comments/questions:

- Does this technique generalize to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, are there specific requirements beyond the condition outlined in section 2.2 – that the SG-MCMC algorithm satisfies pigamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)?
- Could this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings or Distributed SGLD?
- Given that one chain requires twice the iterations of the other, computational resources are underutilized while the chain with the larger step size idles. Could using three chains – one with step size gamma and two with step size gamma/2 – and running them for the same number of steps offer a more efficient alternative? Would this approach introduce increased variance in the estimate?
- Has the authors considered extending this method to incorporate more than two step sizes?
- The injected noise in the chains is correlated; however, the impact of correlation between mini-batches is not fully clear. Is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k^th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)^th and (2k)^th iterations of Chain 2? Alternatively, is the effect of this correlation negligible?
- In the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5), running the experiment until convergence and including a comparison to standard SGD would be beneficial. While the current results demonstrate superiority over SGLD, demonstrating improved performance compared to SGD in a scenario where Bayesian inference provides an advantage would strengthen the results. Given the limited practical use of Bayesian matrix factorization, providing quantitative results could encourage wider adoption of the method. An SGD control plot would also provide valuable context for interpreting the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
- Although the method is clearly explained and presented, including an algorithm box with pseudo-code would increase accessibility for a broader audience. Many practitioners may not engage with the theoretical details; therefore, providing pseudo-code early in the paper would allow those less familiar with the area to experiment with the method without needing to fully grasp the theoretical foundations.
