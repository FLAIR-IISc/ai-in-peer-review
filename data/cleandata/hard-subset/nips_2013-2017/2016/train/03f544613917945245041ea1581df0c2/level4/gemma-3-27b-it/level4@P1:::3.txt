Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods have become increasingly popular for Bayesian inference in large-scale applications. A key limitation of these methods is their inherent bias, preventing exact convergence to the posterior distribution. This paper introduces a novel approach to reduce this bias using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is elegantly simple: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with the second chain utilizing a step size half that of the first, and running for twice the number of steps. The injected Gaussian noise in the two chains is correlated, specifically defined as  αk = (β(2k-1) + β(2k))/√2, where αk and βk represent the Gaussian noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. The resulting estimator, formed by combining the outputs of the two chains, exhibits a reduced bias of O(γ²) compared to the O(γ) bias obtained from either chain independently (where γ is the step size). The authors also establish corresponding improvements in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates.  I particularly appreciate the simplicity and ease of understanding and implementation of this method – as described in my summary – and its minimal impact on the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm. Its potential generality across a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms also suggests a significant potential impact on the field. 

**Novelty:** While RR extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, its application to the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities appears novel. 

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Comments/Questions:**

*   What are the specific conditions, beyond satisfying the bound mentioned in Section 2.2 (πγ(f) = π(f) + C * γ + O(γ²)), required for this technique to be applicable to all SG-MCMC methods?
*   Could this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes beyond stochastic gradient methods, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings tests? What about Distributed SGLD?
*   The requirement for one chain to run for twice as long as the other implies some computational inefficiency. Would employing three chains – one with step size γ and two with step sizes γ/2 – and running all for the same number of steps be a viable alternative?  Would this affect the estimator’s variance?
*   Is there a generalization of this method to utilize more than two step sizes?
*   The correlation between the injected noise is mentioned, but the impact of correlation *between* the mini-batches used in each chain is less clear. Is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this correlation negligible?
*   Regarding the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5), running the experiment until convergence and including a comparison to Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) would strengthen the results. While the current plot demonstrates improvements over SGLD, demonstrating superior performance compared to SGD – particularly in scenarios where the Bayesian approach offers advantages – would enhance the method’s appeal and potential for wider adoption. Bayesian matrix factorization is not as common in practice, so demonstrating clear benefits would be persuasive. Including an SGD plot as a baseline would also provide valuable context for the final error rates achieved by SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   While the method is presented clearly, an algorithm box containing pseudo-code could enhance accessibility, particularly for practitioners less familiar with the theoretical details. Providing readily accessible pseudo-code early in the paper would allow a broader audience to experiment with the method without needing to fully grasp the more complex theoretical aspects.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method offers a simple and effective way to reduce bias in SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong justification for the method’s improved convergence properties.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The method is generally applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of running two chains in parallel may be a limitation in some applications.
*   Further investigation is needed to fully understand the impact of correlated mini-batches.
*   The comparison to SGD in the matrix factorization experiment could be more comprehensive.
