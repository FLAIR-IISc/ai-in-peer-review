Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), an SG-MCMC method that applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce discretization bias while controlling variance. Concretely, the method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon, and forms a bias-canceling estimator by a linear combination of their empirical averages. To control variance inflation typically induced by RR, the authors correlate the Gaussian increments across chains, showing that the ideal choice is to couple them via Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/sqrt(2). Theoretical contributions include: consistency; a CLT showing the limiting variance is minimized under the above coupling; non-asymptotic bounds that yield O(γ2) bias and O(γ4) variance at fixed γ; and an MSE rate of O(K−4/5) with optimal step-size schedules, improving over O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC.

This work builds on several strands of literature: SGLD and its extensions for scalable Bayesian sampling; analyses of SG-MCMC with higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS 2015), which achieve reduced bias by changing the integrator; and RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay–Tubaro; Lemaire–Pagès–Panloup), including its use to accelerate convergence of ULA and estimates of invariant distributions. The novelty here lies in adapting RR to the stochastic-gradient setting, proving that correlated Brownian increments recover higher-order accuracy without higher-order integrators, and showing improved finite-time rates under standard step-size schedules. The approach is also illustrated on SGHMC (SGRRHMC) and compared to a higher-order SGHMC splitting scheme.

Strengths:
- Clear idea with simple implementation: two chains, different step sizes, linear combination, and shared randomness.
- Solid theory: consistency, CLT with optimal coupling, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds that match higher-order accuracy while using first-order integrators.
- Practical potential: parallelizable by design; demonstrated gains on synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task.
- Useful insight: in the asymptotics, Gaussian noise dominates stochastic-gradient noise, and the Brownian correlation is the key to variance control.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Novelty is somewhat incremental relative to (i) prior RR extrapolation for ULA/invariant measures and (ii) the higher-order integrator analyses for SG-MCMC; the main contribution is their combination and the analysis under stochastic gradients.
- Practicality and fairness of experimental comparisons need clarification. The method requires coupling the two chains’ Gaussian increments (shared randomness) and matching mini-batch sizes; if the two chains run on separate workers, the degree of communication/synchronization (if any) should be stated, as well as its cost. The current results appear to assume negligible overhead.
- Compute budget fairness: Please detail how wall-clock parity was enforced. Were the two SGRRLD chains run on two cores while SGLD used one core, or were both assigned the same total hardware? If SGLD can also use multiple cores (e.g., for batched gradient computations), how was that accounted for?
- Baselines: A natural baseline is running two independent SGLD chains “embarrassingly parallel” and averaging their estimates, or a time-matched SGLD with doubled iterations. Comparisons to such baselines would help isolate the benefit of RR beyond mere parallelism.
- Sensitivity: The method requires identical distributions for the two stochastic-gradient estimators (e.g., same mini-batch size). What happens when mini-batch sizes, data partitions, or samplers differ? An ablation would be useful.
- Clarity/notation: There appear to be small typos in the Chain 2 updates (e.g., using θ(γ/2)2k+1 as both input and output in the first line; likely the input should be θ(γ/2)2k). Also, “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is defined twice. Pseudo-code is only in the supplement; a short algorithm box in the main text would improve readability.

Assessment by reviewing criteria:
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound and carefully executed; claims are supported by both asymptotic and finite-time results, and by empirical verification on synthetic data. Some experimental details (computational setup, communication overhead, multi-core usage) are missing and should be added for full reproducibility and fair assessment.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Adding pseudo-code in the main text and clarifying the computational model would help. Correct the minor typographical issues in the update equations and deduplicate definitions.
- Originality: Moderate. RR extrapolation and higher-order SG-MCMC are known; applying RR to SG-MCMC with a variance-control coupling and deriving corresponding theory for stochastic gradients is a meaningful, if incremental, advance.
- Significance: Potentially impactful, as the method is simple and broadly applicable to SG-MCMC variants. Demonstrated improvements are consistent and suggest that higher-order accuracy can be had without higher-order integrators. Wider empirical validation (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural networks) would further support significance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a detailed description of the hardware and parallelization: number of cores/threads per method, process affinity, synchronization/communication (if any) for shared randomness, and how wall-clock fairness was enforced.
- Add baselines: (i) two independent SGLD chains averaged; (ii) time-matched SGLD with doubled iterations; and (iii) where applicable, higher-order SGLD-type integrators.
- Include ablations testing robustness to slight mismatches in mini-batch sizes, different random seeds, and weaker/no coupling of Brownian increments.
- Report additional diagnostics (e.g., bias and variance decomposition, effective sample size proxies) on the matrix factorization task.
- Move a compact pseudo-code of SGRRLD to the main paper; fix minor typos (ULA duplication; Chain 2 indices).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general technique with solid theory and practical gains.
- Achieves higher-order accuracy with first-order integrators.
- Applicable beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC), with empirical support.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to known RR and higher-order SG-MCMC results.
- Experimental comparisons lack full clarity on computational fairness and communication overhead.
- Empirical scope is somewhat narrow; stronger baselines would better quantify benefits.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying and strengthening the experimental methodology and baselines, and fixing minor presentation issues.
