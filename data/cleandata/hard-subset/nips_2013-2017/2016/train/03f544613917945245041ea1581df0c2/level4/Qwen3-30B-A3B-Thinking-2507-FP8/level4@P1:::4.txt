

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The approach runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and combines their outputs through extrapolation. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error (MSE) rate of O(K^{-4/5}), which is superior to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}) rate, and demonstrate the method on both synthetic and real-world matrix factorization problems.

The work builds upon recent advances in SG-MCMC, particularly the theoretical bounds established in Chen et al. (NIPS 2015) showing that SG-MCMC methods with first-order integrators have MSE rates of O(K^{-2/3}). The authors' key insight is that Richardson-Romberg extrapolation can improve these rates without requiring higher-order integrators, which represents a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's strengths include strong theoretical foundations with formal convergence rate analysis, straightforward implementation requiring minimal modifications to existing SG-MCMC code, and compelling experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The method's parallel nature makes it well-suited for distributed architectures, and the authors demonstrate its applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.

Several important weaknesses require attention. First, there is a significant inconsistency between the theoretical claims and experimental results in Figure 3. The paper states that the optimal step size exponent is α = 1/5 (corresponding to K^{-0.2}), but the bias plot shows the best performance at α = 0.2 (which is the same as 1/5), yet the text claims α = 1/3. The experimental curve appears highly fluctuating, making it difficult to verify the claimed convergence rate. This discrepancy needs clarification.

Second, the wall-clock time comparison in Figure 5 does not properly account for SGRRLD's parallel implementation. Since SGRRLD runs two chains (with one chain requiring twice as many steps), the actual wall-clock time for SGRRLD should be approximately 2/3 of SGLD's time for equivalent computational effort. Without this correction, the performance gains appear larger than they would be in practice. The authors should replot Figure 5 with properly normalized time.

Third, the paper states in line 256 that for very small step sizes, the term 1/(Kγ) dominates the bias and MSE. However, this effect is not observed in the SGLD results (Figure 2), raising questions about the comparison between the two methods. The authors should clarify why this term dominates for SGRRLD but not for SGLD.

Additional minor concerns include:
- Equation 1 uses ϑt instead of θt, creating inconsistency with the rest of the paper
- Richardson-Romberg (RR) is not defined when first introduced (line 94)
- The notation Γ_K is used without definition (line 131)
- The paper refers to "smooth function f" without specifying the required smoothness conditions (line 160)
- Figure 1(b) is described as plotting bias vs. dimension, but the caption indicates it's bias vs. time (line 249)
- Vectors should be consistently represented with bold letters throughout

The paper makes a valuable contribution to SG-MCMC by showing how Richardson-Romberg extrapolation can improve convergence rates without increasing computational complexity. However, the inconsistencies between theoretical claims and experimental results, along with the improper time comparison in Figure 5, are significant concerns that need resolution before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.

Pro and con acceptance arguments:

Pros:
- Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Strong theoretical foundation with formal convergence rate analysis
- Practical implementation requiring minimal modifications to existing code
- Demonstrated performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Well-suited for parallel architectures

Cons:
- Theoretical claims about optimal step size α = 1/5 do not match the experimental results in Figure 3 (where the optimal α appears to be 0.2, but the text claims 1/3)
- Wall-clock time comparison in Figure 5 does not properly account for SGRRLD's parallel implementation (2/3 time factor)
- Inconsistency in explaining why the 1/(Kγ) term dominates for small γ in SGRRLD but not in SGLD
- Several minor technical inconsistencies in notation and presentation

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to improving SG-MCMC methods. With clarification of the theoretical-optimal step size relationship and proper accounting for wall-clock time in the experiments, this would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, the inconsistencies between theory and experiments require resolution before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.
