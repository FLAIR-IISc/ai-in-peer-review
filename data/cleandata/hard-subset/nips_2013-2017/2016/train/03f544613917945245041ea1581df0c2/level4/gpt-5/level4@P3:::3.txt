Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a simple and general technique to reduce the discretization bias of stochastic-gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while controlling variance. The approach adapts Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—well known in numerical SDE analysis (Talay & Tubaro; Lemaire et al.)—to SG-MCMC. Concretely, it runs two SG-MCMC chains driven by the same target SDE but with step sizes γ and γ/2 to the same time horizon, correlates their injected Gaussian noises via Z(γ)k = (Z(γ/2)2k−1 + Z(γ/2)2k)/√2, and forms an extrapolated estimator equivalent to 2·π̂γ/2(f) − π̂γ(f). For SGLD, the authors prove asymptotic consistency, a CLT, and non-asymptotic bounds: asymptotic bias O(γ2) (vs O(γ) for first-order methods), asymptotic variance O(γ4), and finite-time MSE O(γ4 + 1/(Kγ)) optimized at O(K−4/5), improving on the O(K−2/3) rate of standard first-order SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD). They argue the method matches the accuracy of higher-order integrators (e.g., Chen et al., NIPS 2015) while using only first-order updates. Experiments on Gaussian models, SGHMC variants, and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) support these claims. The idea is generic and, under weak error and ergodicity conditions, can be applied beyond SGLD.

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides clear asymptotic and finite-time analyses. The CLT shows the fluctuation term dominates gradient noise, and it identifies the optimal Gaussian coupling (Σ = I) that minimizes asymptotic variance. Non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds align with the CLT rates and yield practical guidance (e.g., α = 1/5 for decreasing steps).
- Assumptions: Some technical conditions (e.g., smoothness of the Poisson solution g ∈ C9) are strong; it would help to highlight which are essential versus technical artifacts. The requirement that both chains use gradient estimators with the same distribution (e.g., identical minibatch size) is important to avoid reintroducing O(γ) bias and should be emphasized in the main text.
- Experiments: The synthetic studies convincingly illustrate bias and MSE reductions, including a demonstration on SGHMC and comparison to a higher-order splitting integrator. The matrix factorization results show better wall-clock RMSE at equal time by exploiting parallelism.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear description of the estimator and coupling. Minor notational slips and typos can be fixed easily (e.g., “variace,” “th Conference”). I recommend adding an algorithm box for SGRRLD in the main text (not only in the supplement) that specifies:
- the two step-size schedules (γk, ηk = γk/2 with two fine steps),
- the Gaussian coupling,
- the requirement on the gradient estimator distributions (same minibatch size),
- the final estimator (2·fine − coarse form).

Originality
While RR extrapolation is standard in numerical SDEs, applying it to SG-MCMC with a careful variance control via correlated Gaussian increments, and deriving CLT and non-asymptotic bounds in the presence of stochastic gradients, is novel to the SG-MCMC literature. The work is well positioned relative to SGLD (Welling & Teh), SGHMC (Chen et al.), and high-order SG-MCMC integrators (NIPS 2015).

Significance
The method is simple, general, and practical. It can be added to existing SG-MCMC implementations with minimal changes and leverages parallel or distributed hardware naturally. Achieving higher-order accuracy without a higher-order integrator is valuable, and the improved finite-time MSE rate is likely to matter in large-scale applications.

Questions and suggestions
- Applicability beyond SGLD/SGHMC: Please state explicit sufficient conditions (e.g., weak order-1 discretization error πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ2), geometric ergodicity, moments of the stochastic gradient) under which RR improves bias/MSE. A concise checklist would help practitioners.
- Gradient estimator correlation: The CLT indicates that Brownian coupling matters for variance, while gradient estimator correlation is less critical. Still, in practice, should one reuse the same minibatch at iteration k of the coarse chain and iterations 2k−1 and 2k of the fine chain? Does this help or hurt finite-time MSE?
- Compute budget and fairness: When run on a single device, SGRRLD roughly doubles the number of gradient evaluations per “time horizon” because the fine chain takes twice as many steps. The experiments exploit parallelism to equalize wall-clock time. Please also report results controlled for total gradient computations to quantify gains independent of hardware parallelism.
- Practical step-size guidance: The theory suggests α = 1/5 for decreasing step sizes to balance bias and variance, and γ ∝ K−1/5 for constant-step finite-time MSE. Provide a brief practical recipe for choosing γ or α and constants (e.g., via a short pilot run).
- Extensions: You mention recursive RR and multilevel strategies. A brief discussion of the three-chain variant (one coarse, two fine running concurrently to avoid idling) and the variance/cost trade-off would be helpful. Are there stability or variance penalties?
- Beyond SG-MCMC: Could RR be used with other approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling MH tests, distributed SGLD) where discretization or estimator bias appears? What breaks if the two chains have different minibatch sizes or data partitions?
- Experiments: For the matrix factorization task, consider adding an SGD baseline (as a control) and running to convergence where feasible. This would contextualize Bayesian RMSE gains and the practical value of reduced bias.

Pros
- Simple, general, and parallel-friendly technique that can wrap around many SG-MCMC methods.
- Strong theory: consistency, CLT with optimal coupling, and finite-time bias/MSE bounds with improved rates.
- Clear empirical gains on synthetic problems and a large-scale application.

Cons
- Some technical assumptions are heavy; clearer presentation of minimal conditions would improve accessibility.
- Experimental comparisons focus on SGLD/SGHMC; additional baselines and compute-controlled metrics would strengthen the empirical case.
- Practical guidance (minibatch correlation, step-size tuning) could be more explicit.

Overall assessment
This is a high-quality, well-motivated contribution that brings a classical extrapolation idea to SG-MCMC with solid theory and practical benefits. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions to clarify applicability conditions, provide an algorithm box, and strengthen empirical comparisons where possible.
