Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic-gradient MCMC, focusing on SGLD as the main example. The method runs two SGLD chains in parallel with step sizes γ and γ/2 up to the same time horizon, and constructs an extrapolated estimator that cancels the leading discretization bias term. To control variance, the Gaussian noises of the two chains are coupled optimally (the γ chain’s increments are the average of the two γ/2 increments). The authors prove consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds: asymptotic bias O(γ^2), variance O(γ^4), and finite-time MSE rate O(K^−4/5) under an optimal schedule, improving over SGLD’s O(K^−2/3) with first-order integrators. They show the approach is generic and can be applied to SGHMC. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model and on MovieLens matrix factorization illustrate reduced bias and modest RMSE gains at similar wall-clock times, exploiting parallel execution of the two chains.

The work is well situated. SGLD was introduced by Welling & Teh (2011); convergence and finite-time analyses for SG-MCMC and higher-order integrators have been developed (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016). RR extrapolation for SDE discretizations and invariant measure estimation is classical (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015; Pagès, 2007), but to my knowledge has not been adapted to the stochastic-gradient setting. The paper bridges these literatures by showing that RR can improve bias and MSE for SG-MCMC when the two chains share the same gradient-estimator law and when Brownian increments are appropriately correlated.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with a simple and practical algorithm. Using RR extrapolation to cancel leading-order bias in SG-MCMC is elegant and requires only running two nearly identical chains with different step sizes and a specific noise coupling.
- Theoretical contributions. The paper proves consistency, a CLT for the RR estimator, and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The analysis clarifies when the RR estimator improves the rate (e.g., α = 1/5 for step-size schedules) and shows the optimality of fully correlated Brownian increments (Σ = I).
- Generality. The approach is modular and, in principle, applies to other SG-MCMC methods (demonstrated with SGHMC).
- Clarity of the algorithm and main ideas. The estimator definition, coupling, and extrapolation are clearly presented.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Assumptions. Theorems depend on “mild” conditions on U, f, and the stochastic gradient law L. Please state them more concretely in the main text (smoothness/order of derivatives, growth, dissipativity/ergodicity). Can U be multimodal and still satisfy the necessary drift and regularity conditions? How sensitive are results to violations (e.g., heavy tails)?
- Role of stochastic gradients. The analysis emphasizes that correlating the Brownian increments is crucial, while correlation of stochastic gradients is not. Intuitively, if mini-batches are drawn independently, the two chains may decorrelate quickly even with coupled Gaussians. Can the authors give more intuition (beyond the Poisson equation expansion) on why the RR combination still cancels the leading bias term under independent gradient noise? Empirically, did you measure the correlation between the chains over time?
- Equality of gradient-estimator laws. The method requires the two gradient estimators to have the same distribution (e.g., same batch size). In practice, do minor deviations (e.g., slightly different batch sizes, different data sharding across workers) materially hurt the gains?

Clarity
- Practical guidance. It would help to summarize in the main text: (i) recommended step-size schedules and how to pick α and constants for finite budgets; (ii) whether to correlate mini-batch selection across chains (same indices vs independent); (iii) explicit pseudo-code and the cost model (one γ step plus two γ/2 steps per iteration).
- Notation. A short reminder that the two chains are run to equal time horizon helps interpret K and the averaging weights.

Originality
- Applying RR to SG-MCMC appears novel and is well justified. The extension to SGHMC further supports generality.

Significance
- Empirical evidence. The synthetic linear Gaussian example convincingly shows reduced bias and improved MSE. On MovieLens, RMSE gains are noticeable but modest. Given the parallel-friendly nature of both SGRRLD and multi-chain SGLD, a stronger empirical case would compare:
  - SGRRLD vs two independent SGLD chains averaged over the same wall-clock time.
  - SGRRLD (γ, γ/2) vs SGLD at γ/2 (and two parallel chains), since smaller γ reduces bias.
  - Against higher-order SGHMC/SGNHT variants where applicable.
Without these baselines, it is hard to attribute RMSE gains solely to RR extrapolation rather than to an implicit increase in computation or more favorable coupling.
- Generality in practice. Additional real-data tasks (e.g., logistic regression, Bayesian neural nets) would help demonstrate robustness beyond matrix factorization.

Specific requests and suggestions
- State the exact assumptions on U, f, and L (smoothness orders, moments of gradient noise, ergodicity/drift/minorization) in the main paper, and discuss multimodal targets explicitly.
- Add an ablation on mini-batch coupling: same vs independent mini-batches across chains, and measure chain cross-correlation and estimator variance.
- Include fair runtime baselines: two SGLD chains with γ and γ/2; report wall-clock parity and effective sample size per second if possible.
- Provide guidance for finite-budget tuning: for fixed K, recommend γ ∝ K^−1/3 for bias and γ ∝ K^−1/5 for MSE (as your theory suggests), with practical constants estimated via small pilot runs.
- Discuss memory/communication overheads in distributed settings and scalability of the noise coupling.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, broadly applicable idea with solid theoretical backing.
- Clear improvement in rates over first-order SG-MCMC without resorting to higher-order integrators.
- Parallelizable and easy to implement.

Cons against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation on real data is limited; comparisons lack some strong baselines (multi-chain SGLD, smaller step sizes).
- Assumptions and practical conditions are mostly deferred to the supplement, limiting accessibility.

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and technically competent paper that introduces a simple tool to improve SG-MCMC accuracy. With stronger empirical comparisons and clearer presentation of assumptions and practical guidance, it would make a compelling contribution. As it stands, I view it as borderline-to-positive: promising theory and method, with empirical validation that could be tightened to fully substantiate practical gains.
