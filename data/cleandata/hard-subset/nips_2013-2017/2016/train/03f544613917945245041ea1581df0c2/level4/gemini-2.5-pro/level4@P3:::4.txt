### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD). The core idea is to reduce the bias inherent in standard SG-MCMC methods, such as Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD), by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The proposed scheme is straightforward to implement: it involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2, with the second chain taking twice as many steps) and combining their outputs. To control the variance of the resulting estimator, the method utilizes correlated Brownian motion for the two chains.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, proving that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD can achieve a faster convergence rate (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This allows a first-order integrator to achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on both a synthetic linear Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization problem for movie recommendations, where SGRRLD demonstrates improved performance over SGLD.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and elegant contribution. It provides a principled and practical way to improve the accuracy of widely used sampling algorithms by reducing their discretization bias. Achieving higher-order accuracy with only a first-order integrator is a significant result.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper is supported by strong theoretical results. The authors provide a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for both bias and MSE, which rigorously establish the advantages of their proposed method and provide clear guidance on optimal step-size scheduling.

3.  **Clarity and Practicality:** The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is conceptually simple and easy to implement, requiring minimal modifications to existing SGLD code. Its inherent parallelism makes it particularly well-suited for modern multi-core architectures, as demonstrated in the experiments.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method. The comparison on the Gaussian model clearly illustrates the bias reduction, and the application to SGHMC showcases the generality of the RR approach. The large-scale matrix factorization results highlight the method's efficiency in a real-world setting.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Derivation of Convergence Rates:** A central claim of the paper is the improved convergence rates (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE). While Theorems 1 and 2 provide the general bounds, the derivation of these specific optimal rates from the theorems could be made more explicit in the main text. For instance, a clearer walkthrough of how the optimal step-size schedule (e.g., γ ∝ K⁻¹/⁵) is chosen to balance the terms in the MSE bound would greatly enhance the paper's clarity for the reader.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Wall-Clock Time Comparison:** The experimental comparison in Figure 5 relies on the parallel execution of the two SGRRLD chains. The paper states that wall-clock times are nearly identical due to this parallelization. While this is a valid use case, the comparison is less straightforward for single-core environments. An SGRRLD "iteration" involves three gradient computations (one for the γ chain, two for the γ/2 chain) compared to SGLD's one. It would strengthen the experimental section to explicitly acknowledge this and discuss the trade-offs. A comparison based on the total number of gradient evaluations, a more hardware-agnostic measure of computational cost, would provide a valuable alternative perspective.

3.  **Potential Discrepancy in Figure 3:** In the discussion of Figure 3 (bias plot), the text states that the optimal convergence is achieved for α = 1/3. However, visually, the red curve (α = 0.2) appears to achieve a lower bias for the given number of iterations. Could the authors clarify this apparent discrepancy? Furthermore, the optimal curve exhibits significant fluctuations; a brief explanation for this behavior would be helpful.

4.  **Behavior for Small Step Sizes:** The paper astutely observes that for very small γ, the 1/(Kγ) variance term dominates the error for SGRRLD, causing performance to degrade. This effect should also be present for SGLD. For completeness, it would be useful to briefly discuss why this is not as apparent for SGLD in the provided plots (e.g., is it due to the range of γ chosen, or are the constants in the bounds different?).

**Minor Suggestions**

*   For improved readability, please consider using bold letters to represent vectors consistently throughout the paper.
*   In Equation (1), the continuous-time process uses the symbol `ϑ`, while the discrete-time approximation uses `θ`. Using a consistent base symbol (e.g., `θ_t` and `θ_k`) might be slightly clearer for the reader.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The contribution is theoretically sound, with rigorous proofs of improved convergence rates.
*   The method is practical, easy to implement, and well-suited for parallel computation, which is highly relevant for large-scale machine learning.
*   The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over a standard baseline.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental comparison of wall-clock time could be presented more transparently by also considering the total computational cost in terms of gradient evaluations, as the current presentation heavily favors parallel architectures.
*   There are minor clarity issues regarding the derivation of the final convergence rates from the theorems and a potential visual discrepancy in one of the figures.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution, solid theory, and compelling experiments. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed in a revised version. The proposed method is a valuable addition to the SG-MCMC literature. I recommend acceptance.
