This paper proposes a method to reduce the discretization bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is to run two parallel Markov chains with different step sizes (e.g., `γ` and `γ/2`) and combine their outputs in a way that cancels the leading-order error term. The resulting algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), is shown theoretically to achieve a faster convergence rate for both bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) compared to the standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The authors support their theoretical claims with experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model, which clearly demonstrates the bias reduction, and on large-scale matrix factorization tasks using the MovieLens datasets, which show performance improvements over a standard SGLD baseline.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a classic numerical analysis technique, to the SG-MCMC setting is a novel and clever contribution. If the demonstrated gains hold up under further scrutiny, this technique could provide a simple and effective way to improve the accuracy of a wide range of SG-MCMC samplers.

*   **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a formal convergence analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and MSE. These results rigorously establish that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence (e.g., an MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)).

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the proposed algorithm is described clearly. The method is conceptually simple and appears straightforward to implement as a modification of existing SGLD code, enhancing its practical appeal.

*   **Empirical Validation (Synthetic):** The experiments on the linear Gaussian model provide a compelling and clear demonstration of the algorithm's primary advantage. The results in Figures 1-3 align well with the theoretical predictions, showing a significant reduction in bias and MSE compared to SGLD in this controlled setting.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** My primary concern lies with the experimental design, particularly for the real-data experiments. The authors claim that SGRRLD has a similar wall-clock time to SGLD because its two chains can be run in parallel. However, this comparison is not entirely fair, as the same parallel resources could be used to improve the SGLD baseline. A more convincing comparison would involve benchmarking SGRRLD against:
    1.  An ensemble of two independent SGLD chains run in parallel, each with step size `γ`, whose results are then averaged. This would match the computational budget of SGRRLD.
    2.  An ensemble of two independent SGLD chains run in parallel, each with the smaller step size `γ/2`. This is a crucial baseline because a smaller step size inherently reduces the bias of SGLD, and this configuration would also match the computational budget.
    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to determine if the observed improvement in RMSE for SGRRLD is due to the RR extrapolation itself or simply a byproduct of using more computation or a smaller effective step size.

*   **Limited Real-Data Experiments:** While the MovieLens results are encouraging, they show a relatively modest improvement over the SGLD baseline. To strengthen the paper's claims of practical utility, it would be beneficial to see experiments on a broader range of real-world problems and models.

*   **Questions on Theory and Intuition:**
    1.  The theorems rely on certain technical conditions for the potential `U` (deferred to the supplement). Could the authors briefly clarify the scope of these assumptions in the main text? For example, is the theory applicable to multi-modal potentials, which are common in machine learning applications?
    2.  Could the authors provide more intuition as to how combining the two chains reduces the error order, especially when the stochastic gradient noise in each chain is independent? Intuitively, one might expect the chains to diverge due to the independent gradient noise, even with correlated Brownian motion, potentially undermining the effectiveness of the extrapolation. Did you empirically measure the correlation between the two chains in your experiments? Such an analysis could be very insightful.
