

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon Welling and Teh's SGLD (2011) and extend the theoretical framework developed in Chen et al. (2015) to show that their method achieves improved convergence rates compared to existing SG-MCMC approaches. The paper demonstrates that by running two chains with different step sizes in parallel and applying RR extrapolation, SGRRLD achieves a bias of O(γ²) and MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to SGLD's bias of O(γ) and MSE of O(K⁻²/³).

The work makes a significant contribution to the NIPS literature on scalable Bayesian inference. Previous NIPS papers have explored SG-MCMC extensions (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015), but this paper introduces a simple yet powerful technique to improve convergence rates without requiring complex higher-order integrators. The theoretical foundation draws appropriately on Talay and Tubaro (1990) and Lemaire et al. (2015), showing how RR extrapolation can be adapted to the stochastic gradient setting.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The technical quality is high, with rigorous theoretical analysis establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging the computational trade-offs of running two parallel chains. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured exposition of the algorithm, theory, and experiments. The significance is compelling: the method achieves the theoretical accuracy of higher-order integrator methods using only first-order discretizations, while maintaining parallelizability. The experiments on synthetic data and large-scale matrix factorization consistently support the theoretical claims.

However, some concerns require clarification. The practical implementation detail regarding parallel computation is critical: the paper claims SGRRLD achieves better performance than SGLD in the matrix factorization experiments (Figure 5), but the comparison does not account for the fact that SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel. If implemented on a single core, SGRRLD would require approximately 1.5× the time of SGLD for the same number of iterations, which would significantly reduce the observed performance advantage. The authors should clarify whether their experiments were conducted on parallel hardware (which is implied by the discussion of parallelization) or if the time comparison should be adjusted.

Regarding the theoretical claims, the paper states that for fixed step size, the optimal MSE rate is O(K⁻⁴/⁵) achieved when α = 1/5, but the results in Figure 3 suggest the optimal α for MSE is closer to 0.2 (which corresponds to α = 1/5), though the curve appears noisy. The authors should explain why the optimal curve shows significant fluctuations, as this could indicate issues with the experimental setup or theoretical bounds. Additionally, the text states that for very small γ, the term 1/(Kγ) dominates the MSE (line 256), but this effect is not clearly visible in SGLD results. The authors should address why this effect appears to be less pronounced in standard SGLD.

Minor technical issues need correction: RR extrapolation is not defined upon first use (line 94), Γ_K is not defined (line 131), the smoothness requirements for f are unspecified (line 160), and Figure 1(b) appears to plot bias vs. dimension rather than time as described (line 249). The notation for vectors should use boldface as recommended.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed technique is elegant, theoretically sound, and practically implementable. The core insight—applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC to achieve higher-order convergence with minimal modifications to existing algorithms—represents a significant advancement over previous work. The connection to the broader literature on numerical analysis (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Pages, 2007) is well-motivated, and the extension to other SG-MCMC methods (e.g., SGHMC) demonstrates the method's generality.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: Quality (rigorous theoretical analysis and experimental validation), Clarity (well-organized with clear exposition), Originality (novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC), and Significance (improved convergence rates with practical implications for large-scale Bayesian inference).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Solid theoretical foundation with non-asymptotic bounds
- Practical implementation with minimal modifications to existing algorithms
- Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Theoretical advantage over SGLD and other first-order methods

Arguments against acceptance:
- The experimental comparison in Figure 5 does not properly account for the parallel nature of SGRRLD (though this may be resolved by clarifying the hardware context)
- Minor notational and presentation issues that should be addressed
- The optimal convergence rate in Figure 3 appears noisy, requiring clarification

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With minor clarifications regarding the experimental setup and notation, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance. The core idea is elegant, the theory is sound, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the relevant literature and have made a compelling case for their approach.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the implementation details in the experiments and clarify the theoretical claims regarding convergence rates. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS 2016 proceedings.
