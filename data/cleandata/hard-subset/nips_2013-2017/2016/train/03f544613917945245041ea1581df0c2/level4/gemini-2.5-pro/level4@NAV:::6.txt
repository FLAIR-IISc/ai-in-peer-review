This paper introduces a method for accelerating the convergence of SG-MCMC algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integration technique. The proposed approach requires running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with distinct step sizes, coupled by correlated Gaussian noise. The authors provide a theoretical analysis covering both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. The experimental validation includes a synthetic Gaussian model to confirm the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, and a large-scale matrix factorization task on real-world data to highlight the algorithm's practical advantages over standard SGLD.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper in its technical approach and presentation. It makes a solid contribution to the SGMCMC literature and does an excellent job of situating the proposed method relative to prior work. However, I have two primary concerns:

1.  The novelty of the contribution could be viewed as incremental. The influence of numerical integrators on SGMCMC has been previously explored in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has already been applied to approximate invariant distributions in [23].

2.  The practical utility of the algorithm may be constrained, and the experimental comparisons could be more rigorous. The parallel implementation, which relies on correlated Brownian motion, necessitates inter-chain communication at each iteration. This communication overhead, which could be significant, appears to be unaccounted for in the analysis and should be acknowledged. Furthermore, the allocation of computational resources in the experiments is not clearly described. For a fair comparison, was the total computational budget for the single SGLD chain equivalent to the combined budget for the two SGRRLD chains? If so, it raises the question of how resources were partitioned between the two SGRRLD chains. Given that stochastic gradient computation is the primary bottleneck, it is unclear how dividing resources between two chains in SGRRLD could achieve a lower wall-clock time than dedicating the full resources to a single SGLD chain. The manuscript would benefit from additional details on the implementation setup. Additionally, a valuable point of comparison would be to benchmark against two independent SGLD chains running in an embarrassingly parallel fashion, a setup that would not incur communication costs.
