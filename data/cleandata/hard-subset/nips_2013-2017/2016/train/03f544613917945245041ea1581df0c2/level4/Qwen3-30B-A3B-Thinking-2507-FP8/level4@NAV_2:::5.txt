

The paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that reduces bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique, which has been previously applied in numerical analysis for SDEs [21, 23], and adapt it to the SG-MCMC framework. This work extends recent NIPS contributions by Chen et al. [10] that analyzed convergence properties of SG-MCMC methods, showing that higher-order integrators can improve convergence rates. The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves the same theoretical accuracy as higher-order integrator methods while using only first-order integrators, making it applicable to a wider range of SG-MCMC algorithms.

Quality: The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing asymptotic and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and mean squared error (MSE). The paper demonstrates SGRRLD achieves O(K^{-4/5}) MSE convergence rate compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), which is superior to first-order SG-MCMC methods. The empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets support the theoretical claims. However, the paper lacks a direct comparison with higher-order integrator methods (e.g., those in [10]) that achieve similar theoretical rates, which would better establish the practical advantages of the proposed approach. Additionally, there is an inconsistency in the description of the step size sequences (γ_k and η_k) between the main text and Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the experimental setup is well-described. The supplementary material provides sufficient details, including the pseudo-code for SGRRLD.

Originality: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC represents a novel contribution to the field. While RR extrapolation has been used in numerical analysis for SDEs, its adaptation to SG-MCMC is innovative. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work by demonstrating that first-order integrators with RR extrapolation can achieve the same theoretical accuracy as higher-order integrators. However, the authors should more explicitly acknowledge that the theoretical convergence rate improvement (O(K^{-4/5}) vs O(K^{-2/3})) has been previously achieved using higher-order integrators [10], and clarify the practical advantages of their method over those approaches.

Significance: The proposed method is significant as it improves convergence rates of SG-MCMC methods without requiring complex higher-order integrators, making it applicable to a wider range of problems. The parallel nature of the algorithm also makes it well-suited for distributed computing environments. The empirical results on both synthetic data and real-world matrix factorization tasks demonstrate practical improvements in accuracy and computational efficiency. The significance would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with higher-order integrator methods that achieve similar theoretical rates.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with comprehensive convergence rate guarantees
- Clear demonstration of improved convergence rates through both theory and experiments
- Well-designed empirical validation on diverse datasets
- Parallel nature of the algorithm enables efficient distributed implementation
- Generalizable framework applicable to various SG-MCMC algorithms

Weaknesses:
- Inconsistent description of step size sequences between text and supplementary algorithm
- Lack of direct comparison with higher-order integrator methods that achieve similar theoretical rates
- Some claims about the step size sequence require further clarification
- Insufficient discussion of practical advantages over existing higher-order methods

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. With minor clarifications regarding the step size sequences and a more thorough comparison with higher-order integrator methods, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper meets all criteria for acceptance.
