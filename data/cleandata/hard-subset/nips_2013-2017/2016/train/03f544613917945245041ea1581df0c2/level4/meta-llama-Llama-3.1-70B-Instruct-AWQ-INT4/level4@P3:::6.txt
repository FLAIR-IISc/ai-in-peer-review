This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, called Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), that aims to reduce the bias of existing SG-MCMC methods while keeping the variance at a reasonable level. The proposed algorithm applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm, which requires running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated injecting Gaussian noise.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of SGRRLD, including asymptotic and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE). The theoretical results show that SGRRLD attains higher rates of convergence than existing SG-MCMC methods with first-order integrators in both finite-time and asymptotically.

The experimental results on synthetic Gaussian models verify the bias and MSE bounds of the theorems, and the experiments on large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrate the practical use of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD. The results show that SGRRLD yields significant performance improvements over SGLD in terms of computation time.

However, I have two main concerns: (1) The novelty of the paper may be incremental, given that the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed RR extrapolation has been used for approximation of invariant distributions in [23]. (2) The practical use of the algorithm can be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in experiments.

Specifically, the parallel implementation of SG-MCMC algorithm requires the two chains to communicate at every step, which may incur additional communication costs. However, the authors did not consider this cost and assumed it to be negligible. It would be better to mention this and provide a more detailed analysis of the communication costs.

Additionally, it is not clear how the computing resources were assigned for the SGRRLD and SGLD in the experiments. For example, was the single chain of SGLD implemented using the same resources that the two chains of SGRRLD used? If not, this may not be a fair comparison for practical consideration. If yes, then the question is how to split the resources for the two chains of SGRRLD in the algorithm procedure.

The computation cost of SG-MCMC mostly comes from computing stochastic gradients. I would wonder how partial resources used in SGRRLD for computing gradients at each chain could lead to less wall-clock time than full resources used in SGLD. Please add more details on the setup of implementation.

An alternative approach would be to run two chains of SGLD embarrassingly (without communication) and compare the results with SGRRLD. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the benefits and limitations of the proposed algorithm.

Minor comments: (1) "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" appears twice, line 25 and 70.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that reduces the bias of existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis provides a thorough understanding of the convergence properties of SGRRLD.
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical use of the proposed algorithm over standard SGLD.

Weaknesses:

* The novelty of the paper may be incremental, given the existing work on numerical integrators and RR extrapolation.
* The practical use of the algorithm can be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in experiments.
* The communication costs and resource allocation for the parallel implementation of SGRRLD are not clearly discussed.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that improves the convergence properties of existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrate the benefits of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the paper may be incremental, and the practical use of the algorithm can be limited.
* The lack of fair comparison in experiments and the unclear discussion of communication costs and resource allocation may raise concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm.

Recommendation:

I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with revisions that address the concerns mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the communication costs and resource allocation for the parallel implementation of SGRRLD, and consider alternative approaches such as running two chains of SGLD embarrassingly. Additionally, the authors should clarify the novelty of the paper and its contributions to the existing literature on SG-MCMC methods.
