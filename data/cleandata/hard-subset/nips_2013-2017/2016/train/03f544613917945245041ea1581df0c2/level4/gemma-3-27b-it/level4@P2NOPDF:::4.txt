This paper presents an improvement to Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) via the application of a Richardson-Romberg (RR) scheme, demonstrably improving convergence rates in terms of both bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). A key strength of the proposed method, SGRRLD, is its ease of implementation, requiring only minor modifications to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. The work is well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis.

The core idea – leveraging a Romberg-Richardson extrapolation to accelerate convergence – appears promising, and the theoretical foundation seems sound. However, I have concerns regarding the derivation of the specific convergence rates presented in the theorem (detailed in the “fatal flaws” section of my notes). 

Beyond this theoretical concern, I have a practical observation regarding the experimental results, specifically Figure 5 (matrix factorization). The experimental setup, as currently presented, appears potentially misleading.  SGRRLD utilizes two chains, with one requiring twice the number of steps as standard SG-MCMC. This effectively translates to a threefold increase in computational cost if implemented on a single core.  To ensure a fair comparison, the reported runtime for SGLD in Figure 5 should be adjusted by a factor of 2/3, which would likely reduce the observed performance gap between SGLD and SGRRLD.

Further investigation is warranted regarding the results presented in Figure 3 (bias). The plot suggests that the optimal convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, rather than the stated α = 1/3.  Moreover, the curve corresponding to the optimal α exhibits significant fluctuations, which appears inconsistent with the expected convergence rate for this parameter value. Clarification on the reasons for these fluctuations would be beneficial.

The paper states (line 256) that when the stepsize is sufficiently small, the term 1/Kγ dominates the error.  I am unclear why this behavior is not observed in the SGLD results. An explanation of this discrepancy would strengthen the analysis.

Finally, several minor points require attention:

*   **Equation 1:** The use of a symbol different from θ is potentially confusing. Consider standardizing notation.
*   **Line 94:** The abbreviation “RR” is used without prior definition.
*   **Line 131:** The symbol Γ<sub>K</sub> is used without definition.
*   **Line 160:** When referring to a “smooth function f,” please specify the type of smoothness assumed (e.g., Lipschitz continuous gradient).
*   **Line 249:** Figure 1(b) plots Bias vs. time; the caption suggests it should be Figure 1(c) which plots Bias vs. dimension.
*   Throughout the paper:  Please consistently use bold letters to represent vectors for improved clarity.



In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC with a relatively simple and theoretically grounded improvement. Addressing the concerns regarding the convergence rate derivation, the experimental fairness in Figure 5, the observations in Figure 3, and the clarification of the behavior of SGLD will significantly strengthen the paper. The minor notational issues should also be rectified.
