

This paper applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias introduced by gradient estimation noise, resulting in SGRRLD. SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD. Experiments on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate SGRRLD's clear advantage in bias reduction. On MovieLens data, the method yields a slight improvement in test RMSE, though the results are questionable. While RR extrapolation has been shown to accelerate Monte Carlo convergence for SDEs in prior work, this paper extends the technique to SG-MCMC algorithms and proves that under specific conditions, SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD with the same step size—albeit at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear and straightforward to implement.  

Theoretical concerns remain: I have not verified Theorem 1 and 2 details but question the required conditions for the potential function $U$. Specifically, can $U$ be multi-modal? Intuitively, if gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains may decorrelate rapidly even with correlated Gaussian noise. How does combining the two chains reduce the error order? Additionally, in real-data experiments, do the authors observe correlation between the parallel chains?  

Regarding experimental design, the claim that computation time is comparable to SGLD (with parallelization) is questionable. SGLD could also run multiple parallel chains within the same timeframe. If we compare SGLD with two parallel chains against SGRRLD, would the RMSE improvement persist? Furthermore, the real-data comparison pits SGRRLD (step size $\gamma$) against SGLD ($\gamma$), but a fairer benchmark would include SGLD with step size $\gamma/2$ (which has lower bias) and two parallel chains. The authors should also conduct additional real-data experiments to validate whether a simple SGLD modification significantly reduces bias.
