This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) variant, SGRRLD, leveraging Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to mitigate bias stemming from gradient estimation noise. The authors demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to standard SGLD, validated through experiments using a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model. While experiments on the MovieLens dataset show a marginal improvement in test RMSE, the significance of this result is debatable. Building on existing literature demonstrating RR extrapolation’s ability to accelerate convergence in stochastic differential equation (SDE) Monte Carlo estimation, this work successfully applies the technique to stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The authors prove, under specific conditions, that SGRRLD exhibits a faster convergence rate than SGLD for a given step size, albeit requiring the maintenance of two parallel Markov chains. 

The algorithm is clearly described and readily implementable. The synthetic experiments effectively illustrate SGRRLD’s bias reduction capabilities. However, further investigation with more comprehensive real-world datasets is needed to establish the practical significance of this bias reduction. Regarding the theoretical foundation, I have not verified the proof details of Theorems 1 and 2, but I request clarification on the conditions required for matrix *U* to satisfy both theorems – specifically, can *U* be multi-modal? Furthermore, intuitively, if gradient noises are uncorrelated, the parallel chains should rapidly become uncorrelated, even with correlated Gaussian noise.  It would be helpful to understand how the combination of these chains effectively reduces the error order.  Do the authors observe any correlation between the chains when applied to real data?

My primary concern lies with the experimental setup and the interpretation of the real-data results. The claim of comparable computational cost to SGLD, achieved through parallelization, is potentially misleading.  Running multiple parallel SGLD chains within the same timeframe offers a direct comparison point.  Would the observed RMSE improvement persist when comparing SGRRLD to two parallel SGLD chains?  Additionally, the comparison in the real data experiment uses SGRRLD with step sizes γ and γ/2 against SGLD with γ. A more appropriate comparison would include SGLD with a step size of γ/2, also run with two parallel chains, to account for the inherent bias reduction achieved by simply decreasing the step size.  Finally, expanding the scope of real-data experiments would significantly strengthen the conclusions.
