This paper applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce bias from noisy gradient estimates. The resulting method, SGRRLD, is shown to have asymptotically lower bias and MSE than SGLD. On a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model, SGRRLD clearly outperforms SGLD. On MovieLens, it yields a slight test RMSE improvement, though the significance of this gain is debatable.

RR extrapolation is known to accelerate convergence of Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs. This paper brings that idea to SG-MCMC and proves that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate than SGLD with the same step size, at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm is clearly described and straightforward to implement. The synthetic results convincingly demonstrate bias reduction on that task. It would be valuable to see additional real-data experiments to establish that a simple modification of SGLD can consistently and substantially reduce bias in practice.

I did not verify the full details of Theorems 1 and 2, but I have several questions about the assumptions and implications:
- What precise conditions on the potential U are required for both theorems to hold? In particular, can U be multi-modal, and if so, how do the results adapt?
- Intuitively, if the stochastic gradient noises across the two chains are uncorrelated, the chains may decorrelate quickly even when the injected Gaussian noises are correlated. In that regime, how exactly does combining the two chains reduce the order of the error? Do the theoretical guarantees rely on a measurable correlation structure between the chains?
- In the real-data experiments, do the authors observe nontrivial empirical correlation between the two chains? If so, how is it quantified over time?

I also have concerns about the experimental setup and the fairness of the real-data comparison. The paper argues that, with parallelization, the wall-clock time is comparable to SGLD. However, one could also run multiple SGLD chains in parallel within the same computational budget. If we compare an estimator built from two independent SGLD chains to SGRRLD, do we still observe the same RMSE improvement?

Relatedly, in the real-data experiments, SGRRLD with step sizes γ and γ/2 is compared against SGLD with step size γ. For a fairer comparison, please also include SGLD with step size γ/2 (which should have lower bias), ideally with two chains run in parallel to match compute, and report the resulting estimator’s RMSE. This baseline is important for isolating the benefit of RR extrapolation from the benefit of smaller step sizes and increased sampling.

In summary, the paper presents a clear and appealing idea with solid theoretical motivation and promising synthetic results. The main weaknesses are the limited real-data evidence and the absence of stronger baselines under matched computational budgets. Addressing the questions above, adding SGLD(γ/2) and multi-chain SGLD baselines, and expanding the set of real-data experiments would significantly strengthen the empirical case.
