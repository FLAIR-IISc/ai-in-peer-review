This paper introduces a method to accelerate the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration technique leveraging a higher-order numerical integrator. The approach necessitates the parallel execution of two SG-MCMC chains with differing step sizes, incorporating correlated Gaussian noise injection. Theoretical guarantees are provided for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence properties. Empirical validation, using a synthetic Gaussian model, confirms the derived bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds. Furthermore, experiments on large-scale matrix factorization with real-world data demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithm over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that represents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. The authors effectively contextualize their method within existing related work.

I have two primary areas of concern. First, the novelty of the work may be somewhat incremental, as previous research [10] has explored the influence of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC, and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been previously employed for approximating invariant distributions [23]. Second, the practical applicability of the algorithm may be limited, and the experimental evaluation lacks a comprehensive consideration of implementation costs. The proposed method’s parallel implementation and reliance on correlated Brownian motion require communication between the two chains at each iteration. However, the authors do not address the potential communication overhead, implicitly assuming it is negligible. This assumption should be explicitly stated. Additionally, the resource allocation for SGRRLD and SGLD during experimentation is unclear. Specifically, it is important to clarify whether the single SGLD chain was implemented using the same computational resources as the two SGRRLD chains; if not, the comparison may not be entirely fair from a practical perspective. If the resources were shared, further detail is required on how these resources were divided between the two SGRRLD chains during algorithm execution. Given that the computational cost of SG-MCMC is primarily driven by stochastic gradient computation, it is unclear how partitioning resources for gradient calculation in SGRRLD could result in a lower wall-clock time compared to utilizing full resources in SGLD. More detailed information regarding the implementation setup is therefore needed. Finally, a comparison with two embarrassingly parallel SGLD chains (i.e., without communication) would be beneficial.
