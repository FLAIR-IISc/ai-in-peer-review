This paper introduces a method to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The proposed algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), runs two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes. The outputs are then combined to cancel the leading-order error term, thereby reducing bias. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Their non-asymptotic bounds demonstrate that the method achieves a faster convergence rate for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) than standard SGLD, matching the theoretical performance of methods that use more complex higher-order integrators. The empirical results on both synthetic and large-scale real-world problems are compelling and support the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents an elegant and effective solution to the well-known bias problem in SG-MCMC. While SGLD and RR extrapolation are established techniques, their combination in this context is novel and constitutes a valuable contribution. The method is particularly appealing due to its simplicity and practicality, as it improves accuracy without the implementation complexity of higher-order integrators.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and rigorous. The authors clearly derive the improved convergence rates for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic settings, providing strong justification for the proposed method. The experimental validation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the practical benefits of SGRRLD over SGLD in terms of accuracy and wall-clock time.
*   **Practicality:** A key advantage of SGRRLD is its amenability to parallelization. Since the two chains run independently without communication, the method is "embarrassingly parallel" and can readily leverage multi-core architectures, making it a highly practical tool for large-scale Bayesian inference.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Comparisons:** The authors claim that SGRRLD achieves the theoretical accuracy of methods based on higher-order integrators. While they provide an experiment comparing an RR-enhanced SGHMC to a higher-order SGHMC, a direct comparison between SGRRLD and an SGLD variant using a higher-order integrator is missing. Including such a comparison would have substantially strengthened the paper's empirical claims.
*   **Clarity and Consistency:** There are a couple of minor points that could be clarified for the reader.
    1.  The discussion of the optimal step-size schedules following Theorem 2 could be made clearer. Different optimal exponents (e.g., `α = 1/3` for bias, `α = 1/5` for MSE) are derived, and a more consolidated discussion on the practical guidance for choosing a single step-size schedule would be helpful.
    2.  There appears to be an inconsistency between the update rule for Chain 2 in Equation (5) and its implementation in Algorithm 1 (in the supplement). In Equation (5), the gradient for the second step (`θ(γ/2)2k+2`) seems to be evaluated at `θ(γ/2)2k+1`, whereas one might expect it to be evaluated at the start of the full step, `θ(γ/2)2k`. The authors should verify and clarify this point.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written paper with solid theoretical and empirical contributions. The idea of using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is simple yet powerful, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness convincingly. Despite the minor weaknesses regarding experimental comparisons and clarity, the work presents a valuable and practical advance for scalable Bayesian inference. The method is interesting, effective, and likely to be useful to other researchers in the field.
