This paper proposes using a numerical sequence acceleration method – a higher-order numerical integrator, specifically Richardson-Romberg extrapolation – to improve the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm. The method requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, employing different step sizes and correlated injected Gaussian noise. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model verify the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds established in the theoretical results, and experiments on large-scale matrix factorization using real data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm compared to standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). 

Overall, this is a strong paper with a well-presented technique. I believe it makes a valid contribution to the field of SG-MCMC methods and effectively positions the work within the context of related prior research. However, I have two main concerns.

First, the novelty of the paper may be incremental. The impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been previously analyzed in [10], and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been utilized for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. 

Second, the practical applicability of the algorithm could be limited, and the experimental comparison may not be entirely fair. The parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm, coupled with the use of correlated Brownian motion, necessitates communication between the two chains at each step. However, the authors do not appear to account for this communication cost, effectively assuming it is negligible. This should be acknowledged. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks clarity regarding resource allocation. For instance, was the single SGLD chain implemented using the same computational resources as the two chains of the proposed SGRRLD algorithm? If not, this introduces an unfair comparison from a practical perspective. If so, the question arises as to *how* resources were allocated to the two chains of SGRRLD during the algorithm’s procedure. Given that the computational cost of SG-MCMC is dominated by stochastic gradient calculations, it’s unclear how dedicating a portion of resources to gradient computation in each SGRRLD chain could result in faster wall-clock time than dedicating full resources to SGLD. More detailed information on the implementation setup is needed. Additionally, it would be valuable to compare the proposed method against running two independent SGLD chains in parallel (embarrassingly parallel, without communication).
