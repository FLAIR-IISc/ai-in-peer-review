This paper presents an improvement to existing Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods by introducing a Richardson-Romberg scheme. This scheme enhances the convergence rates in terms of bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), and its implementation requires minimal modifications to traditional SG-MCMC algorithms. The paper is supported by solid theoretical results, which provide a strong foundation for the proposed method.

The proposed Romberg-Richardson scheme appears to be a promising approach for improving the convergence rates of standard SG-MCMC algorithms. The theoretical results presented in the paper are convincing, and the method seems to be well-motivated. However, there are a few concerns that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the derivation of the specific convergence rates from the theorem is not entirely clear. This is discussed in more detail in the "explain fatal flaws" section. Furthermore, there are some practical concerns regarding the matrix factorization experiment results. Specifically, if the proposed method were to be run on a single-core computer, it would take approximately three times longer than standard SG-MCMC due to the use of two chains, one of which requires twice as many steps. Taking this into account, the plots in Figure 5 would not be entirely fair, as the time for SGLD should be multiplied by a factor of 2/3. This would reduce the difference between SGLD and SGRRLD.

Additionally, there are some minor issues with the plots and text. In Figure 3, the bias plot appears to show that the best convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, rather than α = 1/3 as stated in the text. Moreover, the optimal curve seems to fluctuate significantly, which may affect the convergence rate. It is also stated in line 256 that when the step size is too small, the term 1/Kγ would dominate. However, it is not clear why this does not happen for SGLD.

Some minor comments on the text and notation are as follows:

* In Equation 1, a different symbol is used instead of θ. It would be more consistent to use θ throughout the paper.
* On line 94, "RR" is not defined. It would be helpful to define this abbreviation earlier in the text.
* On line 131, Γ_K is not defined. A definition or explanation of this term would be useful.
* On line 160, the term "smooth function f" is used. It would be helpful to specify what type of smoothness is being referred to.
* On line 249, Figure 1(b) is mentioned as plotting Bias vs. time, but it is actually Figure 1(c) that does this. It would be more accurate to refer to the correct figure.
* It would be more consistent to use bold letters to represent vectors throughout the paper.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and promising approach to improving SG-MCMC methods. However, addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide more clarity on the proposed method.
