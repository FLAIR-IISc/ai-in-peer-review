This paper introduces a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg MCMC (SG-RR-MCMC), designed to reduce the bias inherent in standard Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a well-established technique for improving the numerical accuracy of solutions to Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs). A key feature of the proposed method is its implementation using two MCMC chains that run independently without communication, making the approach embarrassingly parallel. The authors provide a theoretical analysis to demonstrate the asymptotic consistency of the estimator, and the method's effectiveness is supported by satisfactory empirical results.

**Strengths:**

*   The central idea of leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to mitigate the bias of SG-MCMC methods is both interesting and novel.
*   The paper is supported by a theoretical analysis that proves the asymptotic consistency of the proposed method.
*   The empirical results presented in the paper are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the SG-RR-MCMC approach.
*   The parallel nature of the algorithm is a significant practical advantage, as it allows for efficient implementation on modern hardware.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison with High-Order Integrators:** While the proposed method is effective, its primary contribution is a novel way to reduce discretization bias. However, another common approach to achieve this is through the use of high-order SDE integrators. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a thorough discussion and, more importantly, an experimental comparison against relevant high-order integrators. Without this baseline, it is difficult to fully assess the practical advantages and trade-offs of SG-RR-MCMC compared to existing alternatives for bias reduction.

2.  **Theoretical Analysis of Convergence Rate:** The current theoretical analysis establishes asymptotic consistency, which is a valuable contribution. However, to more rigorously validate the method's efficiency, an analysis of the non-asymptotic convergence rate would be highly beneficial. Providing a tighter convergence rate would offer a stronger theoretical justification for why this method is preferable to standard SGLD.

3.  **Inconsistency Regarding Step Size:** There appears to be an inconsistency in the manuscript's description of the step size schedule. The authors should carefully review the text and clarify the exact requirements and implementation of the step size to ensure the method is reproducible and the theoretical claims hold.

4.  **Discrepancy Between Algorithm and Equation:** There is a mismatch between the mathematical formulation in Equation (5) and the procedure outlined in Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material. This discrepancy should be corrected to ensure the paper is clear, correct, and self-consistent.

Overall, this is a promising paper with an interesting contribution. The main weaknesses relate to the positioning of the method with respect to existing techniques and some inconsistencies that obscure the details of the work. Addressing these points would substantially improve the quality and impact of the paper.
