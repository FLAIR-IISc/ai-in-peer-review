This paper applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce the bias introduced by gradient estimation noise. The resulting Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm has an asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD. The experimental results on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD over SGLD. However, the experiments on the MovieLens data show only a slight improvement in test RMSE, and the results are somewhat arguable.

The application of RR extrapolation to Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms is a novel contribution of this paper. The authors prove that, under certain conditions, the convergence rate of SGRRLD is faster than that of SGLD with the same step size, albeit at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear, and the proposed algorithm is relatively easy to implement.

The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD in reducing bias. However, it would be beneficial to see more real-data experiments to confirm that this simple modification to SGLD can significantly reduce bias. While I have not thoroughly checked the proof details of Theorem 1 and 2, I am curious about the conditions required for U to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can U be multi-modal?

Intuitively, when the gradient noises are not correlated, the two chains will become uncorrelated quickly, even if the Gaussian noises are correlated. It is not entirely clear how combining the two chains reduces the order of the error. In the real-data experiments, do the authors observe any correlation between the two chains?

I have some concerns regarding the experimental setup and the real-data experiments. The authors argue that the computation time is approximately the same as SGLD with parallelization. However, it is also possible to run SGLD with multiple parallel chains within the same time. If we compare the estimation from two chains of SGLD with SGRRLD, can we still observe the same improvement in RMSE?

Another issue is that, in the real-data experiment, SGRRLD is compared with SGLD using a step size of gamma and gamma/2. However, it would be more informative to compare SGRRLD with SGLD using a step size of gamma/2, which has a lower bias, and run two chains in parallel. Furthermore, more real-data experiments should be conducted to confirm the efficacy of SGRRLD.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC algorithms, and the experimental results on synthetic data are promising. However, more real-data experiments are needed to fully demonstrate the advantages of SGRRLD over SGLD. Addressing the concerns mentioned above will strengthen the paper and provide more conclusive evidence for the benefits of SGRRLD.
