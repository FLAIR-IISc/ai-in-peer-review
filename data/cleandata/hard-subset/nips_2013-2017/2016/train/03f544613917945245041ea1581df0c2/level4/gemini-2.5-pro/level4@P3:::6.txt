This paper introduces the Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm, a novel method for improving the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) samplers. The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration technique, to the popular Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. This involves running two parallel SGLD chains with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and correlated Gaussian noise. The final estimator is a linear combination of the outputs from these two chains, designed to cancel out the leading-order bias term. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). The experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task are used to validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical benefits of SGRRLD over standard SGLD.

This work builds upon the well-established line of research in SG-MCMC methods, initiated by Welling and Teh (2011). The main contribution lies in adapting a classical numerical analysis technique (RR extrapolation) to the stochastic gradient setting to reduce discretization bias. This is conceptually related to the work of Chen et al. (2015) [10], which also sought to reduce bias in SG-MCMC by using higher-order numerical integrators. The application of RR extrapolation to SDEs (without the stochastic gradient component) for approximating invariant distributions has also been explored by Lemaire et al. (2015) [23]. This paper effectively bridges these two areas by analyzing the RR technique in the full SG-MCMC context.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed SGRRLD algorithm. The derivation of asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE provides a solid foundation for the method.
2.  **Improved Convergence Rates:** A key strength is the demonstration that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the MSE (O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) compared to SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This shows that by simply using two first-order integrators in parallel, one can achieve the accuracy of more complex higher-order integrators, which is a significant result.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed. The synthetic experiments on the Gaussian model convincingly verify the theoretical findings regarding the bias and MSE bounds as a function of the step size and iteration count. The application to large-scale matrix factorization on real-world datasets demonstrates the practical utility and computational efficiency of the algorithm.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and does an excellent job of placing the proposed method within the context of related work. The motivation and mechanics of the algorithm are explained effectively.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

My primary concerns relate to the originality and the practical implementation details of the experimental comparison.

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is new, the core ideas are not. The benefits of reducing discretization error in SG-MCMC via higher-order methods were established in [10]. Similarly, the use of RR extrapolation for improving SDE approximations is known from [23]. The main contribution appears to be the synthesis of these two concepts and the corresponding theoretical analysis for the stochastic gradient case. While valuable, this could be viewed as an incremental step.

2.  **Practicality and Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The paper highlights the parallel nature of SGRRLD and reports improvements in wall-clock time. However, crucial details about the experimental setup are missing, which makes it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison.
    *   **Communication Overhead:** The use of correlated Brownian motion (Eq. 7) is essential for controlling the variance of the RR estimator. In a parallel implementation, this requires communication between the two chains at every single step to synchronize the random numbers. This communication cost is not mentioned but could be significant, especially in a distributed setting. Could the authors comment on the potential impact of this overhead?
    *   **Computational Resource Allocation:** The comparison of wall-clock time between SGRRLD (using two chains) and SGLD (using one chain) is potentially misleading without clarifying the allocation of computational resources. Was SGRRLD run on two CPU cores while SGLD was run on a single core? If so, the speedup is a result of using twice the hardware, not necessarily superior algorithmic efficiency. A fair comparison would grant both algorithms the same total computational budget. If SGRRLD and SGLD were both run on a single machine with multiple cores, it is still unclear how resources were allocated. Could the authors please provide more detail on the exact hardware setup for these experiments?
    *   **Alternative Baselines:** A more compelling baseline for a parallel algorithm would be to run two *independent* SGLD chains (i.e., embarrassingly parallel without communication) and average their results. This would utilize the same parallel hardware as SGRRLD but without the communication overhead. How does SGRRLD compare to such a baseline? This comparison would better isolate the benefit of the RR extrapolation itself from the benefit of simple parallelization.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated algorithm.
    *   The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and demonstrates superior convergence rates over standard SGLD.
    *   The empirical results on both synthetic and real data strongly support the theoretical claims and show practical benefits.
    *   The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:**
    *   The novelty may be considered incremental, as it combines known techniques from numerical analysis and SG-MCMC.
    *   The experimental comparison with SGLD may not be fair due to unclarified computational resource allocation and unaddressed communication overhead, potentially exaggerating the practical benefits of the proposed method.

**Minor Comments**

*   The term "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is introduced in both the introduction (line 25) and Section 2.1 (line 70). One of these can be removed.
