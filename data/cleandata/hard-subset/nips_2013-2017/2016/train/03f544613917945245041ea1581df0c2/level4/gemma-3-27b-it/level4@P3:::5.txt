This paper proposes a novel Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing SG-MCMC methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation – a well-known numerical sequence acceleration technique – by running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a Central Limit Theorem, and bounds on bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). Experiments on both synthetic and real datasets support the claim that SGRRLD achieves improved convergence rates compared to SGLD.

This work builds directly on the growing body of research into scalable MCMC methods for Bayesian inference [1, 2], particularly those leveraging stochastic gradient descent [3]. The authors correctly identify the bias-variance trade-off in SG-MCMC and aim to mitigate the bias without significantly increasing variance. The application of RR extrapolation to this setting is a natural and potentially valuable extension, as it offers a way to improve convergence without resorting to more complex high-order integrators [4, 5]. The theoretical results, demonstrating improved convergence rates, are a significant contribution.  The connection to work on RR extrapolation for SDEs [6, 7] is clearly established.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear presentation of a novel algorithm, its rigorous theoretical analysis, and the supporting empirical results. The authors effectively demonstrate the potential of SGRRLD to improve the efficiency of Bayesian inference in large-scale settings. The fact that the two chains in SGRRLD can be run in parallel is a particularly attractive feature for modern computing architectures. The generality of the approach, potentially applicable to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, as highlighted by the SGHMC experiment, is also a strength. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors mention that higher-order integrators can achieve similar accuracy, a direct empirical comparison with a well-implemented higher-order method would strengthen the argument for SGRRLD. The motivation for choosing correlated Brownian motions, while theoretically justified, could be explained more intuitively.  A minor inconsistency exists between Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material and Equation (5) in the main text, which could cause confusion for readers trying to implement the algorithm. Furthermore, while the authors present a convergence analysis, the constant factors hidden within the big-O notation are not discussed, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the improved rates. Finally, the experimental section, while convincing, could benefit from a more thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the RR extrapolation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, offering a potentially significant improvement in convergence rates.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the claims of consistency and improved performance.
*   Empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of SGRRLD on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The parallelizability of the algorithm makes it well-suited for modern computing environments.
*   The potential for broad applicability beyond SGLD.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of direct empirical comparison with higher-order integrators.
*   Minor inconsistency between the algorithm description in the supplementary material and the main text.
*   Limited discussion of constant factors in the convergence analysis.
*   A more thorough ablation study would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some minor issues exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the inconsistencies and potentially expand the experimental evaluation.
