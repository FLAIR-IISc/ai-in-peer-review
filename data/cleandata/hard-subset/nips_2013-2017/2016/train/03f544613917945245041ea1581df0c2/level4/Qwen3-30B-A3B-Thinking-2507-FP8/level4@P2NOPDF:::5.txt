

The paper proposes SG-RR-MCMC, a novel method to reduce bias in stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) by integrating Richardson-Romberg extrapolation—a standard technique for stochastic differential equations (SDEs)—with existing SG-MCMC algorithms. The approach runs two chains in parallel without communication, leveraging embarrassingly parallel computation. Theoretical analysis demonstrates asymptotic consistency, while empirical results show competitive performance. The core idea is a straightforward integration of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation with SGLD, yielding a method that is both conceptually simple and empirically effective.  

The theoretical contribution is notable for establishing a reduced bias rate via Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The authors show that SG-RR-MCMC achieves a better convergence rate (in terms of bias reduction) compared to standard SG-MCMC methods. However, this advantage could also be attained using high-order integrators (e.g., as in [citation]), yet the paper omits a direct comparison with such methods in experiments. This omission weakens the empirical justification for the proposed approach over existing alternatives. The theoretical efficiency claim thus lacks full validation, as the paper does not demonstrate whether SG-RR-MCMC outperforms high-order integrators in practice.  

A significant concern arises from inconsistencies in the step size analysis. The paper asserts that the step size must decay as $O(1/t^{1/2})$ to ensure consistency, yet Equation (3) and the discussion in Section 3.2 suggest a different scaling (e.g., $O(1/t^{1/4})$) for the extrapolation term. This discrepancy requires clarification, as it directly impacts the practical implementation and theoretical guarantees of the method.  

Additionally, Algorithm 1 in the supplement is inconsistent with Equation (5). Specifically, the update rule in Algorithm 1 for the extrapolated gradient does not match the formulation in Equation (5), creating potential confusion for readers attempting to implement the method. This error should be corrected to maintain technical rigor.  

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension of SG-MCMC with clear theoretical grounding. The empirical results support the method’s efficacy, though the lack of comparison with high-order integrators limits the demonstration of its practical superiority. The step size inconsistency and algorithm-equation mismatch are critical issues that must be addressed before publication. With these revisions, the contribution would make a valuable addition to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference. The method is a meaningful step toward bias reduction in SG-MCMC, but the current presentation requires refinement to fully justify its advantages.
