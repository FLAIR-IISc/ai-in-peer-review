This paper introduces a method to improve the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by applying Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The proposed approach, demonstrated on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) to create a new algorithm called SGRRLD, involves running two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated noise. This technique aims to reduce the bias inherent in SG-MCMC methods. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, including asymptotic and non-asymptotic results, demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster rate of convergence than standard SGLD. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model, and the practical utility of the method is demonstrated on a large-scale matrix factorization task using real-world datasets. This work builds upon existing research on higher-order integrators for SG-MCMC [10] and the application of RR extrapolation to SDEs [23].

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a formal convergence analysis, including a central limit theorem (Theorem 1) and non-asymptotic bounds for bias and MSE (Theorem 2). These theoretical results rigorously establish that SGRRLD achieves higher convergence rates (e.g., O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) compared to standard first-order SG-MCMC methods (e.g., O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The motivation for using RR extrapolation is well-explained, and the proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described formally.
*   **Significance:** The proposed method offers a practical way to achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only first-order updates. The experimental results, particularly on the large-scale matrix factorization task, demonstrate significant performance improvements over SGLD in terms of wall-clock time, suggesting the method could have a valuable impact on scaling Bayesian inference.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Originality:** The primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution. The use of higher-order integrators to reduce bias in SG-MCMC has been explored in [10], and RR extrapolation has been previously applied to analyze the invariant distributions of SDEs in [23]. While the application to the *stochastic gradient* setting is new, the paper could be strengthened by more clearly delineating its conceptual novelty relative to this prior work.
*   **Practicality and Experimental Comparison:** The practical benefits of the algorithm depend heavily on the parallel implementation, and several key details of the experimental setup are unclear.
    *   **Communication Cost:** The use of correlated Brownian motion (Eq. 7-8) requires communication or synchronization between the two parallel chains at each step. This communication overhead is not discussed. Could the authors comment on the cost of this synchronization and its potential impact on scalability, especially in distributed environments?
    *   **Fairness of Resource Allocation:** The comparison is made in terms of wall-clock time, with SGRRLD using two parallel processes. For a fair comparison, how were computational resources allocated to the single-chain SGLD baseline? If SGLD was run on a single core/process while SGRRLD used two, the comparison implicitly assumes the availability of idle parallel resources. A more direct comparison might involve giving SGLD the same total resources (e.g., two cores, which could be used for parallel gradient computations or other accelerations). Could the authors clarify the resource allocation for the baselines?
    *   **Alternative Baselines:** An important alternative to SGRRLD is running two *independent* SGLD chains in parallel (an "embarrassingly parallel" approach) and averaging their results. This would achieve variance reduction without communication overhead. A comparison to this baseline would help clarify how much of the observed performance gain is due to the specific RR extrapolation scheme versus simply using more parallel computation.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The acronym "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is defined twice, on line 25 and again on line 70.
