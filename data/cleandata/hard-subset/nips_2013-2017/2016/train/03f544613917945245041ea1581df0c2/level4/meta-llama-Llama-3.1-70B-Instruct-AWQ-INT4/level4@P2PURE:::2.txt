This paper applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to mitigate the bias introduced by gradient estimation noise, resulting in the stochastic gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm. The SGRRLD algorithm has an asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD. The experimental results on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD over SGLD. However, the results on the MovieLens data show only a slight improvement in test RMSE, which is arguable.

The Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been shown in the literature to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates for stochastic differential equations (SDEs). This paper applies this technique to stochastic gradient Markov chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms and proves that, under certain conditions, the convergence rate of SGRRLD is faster than that of SGLD with the same step size, albeit at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear, and implementing the proposed algorithm is relatively straightforward. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD in reducing bias in the specific problem considered.

However, it would be beneficial to conduct more real-data experiments to demonstrate that a simple modification to SGLD can significantly reduce bias. I have not verified the proof details of Theorems 1 and 2, but I am curious about the conditions required for U to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can U be multi-modal? Intuitively, when the gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains will become uncorrelated quickly, even if the Gaussian noises are correlated. It is unclear how combining the two chains reduces the order of the error. Did the authors observe any correlation between the two chains in the real-data experiments?

I have concerns regarding the experimental setup and real-data experiments. The authors argue that the computation time is approximately the same as that of SGLD with parallelization. However, it is also possible to run SGLD with multiple parallel chains within the same time frame. If we compare the estimates from two chains of SGLD with those of SGRRLD, can we still observe the same improvement in RMSE? Furthermore, in the real-data experiment, the authors compare SGRRLD with a step size γ and γ/2 against SGLD with a step size γ. I believe it would be more appropriate to also compare SGRRLD with SGLD using a step size γ/2, which has a lower bias, and running two chains in parallel. Additional real-data experiments should be conducted to further validate the results.
