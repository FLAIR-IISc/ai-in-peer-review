

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel method for improving the convergence properties of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The authors propose using Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias while maintaining reasonable variance, by running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and correlated Brownian motions, then combining their outputs through extrapolation. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing SGRRLD achieves an MSE convergence rate of O(K^{-4/5}) compared to SGLD's O(K^{-2/3}), and demonstrates improved performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper properly situates its contribution within the context of SG-MCMC (Welling & Teh, 2011; Chen et al., 2015) and RR extrapolation for SDEs (Talay & Tubaro, 1990; Lemaire et al., 2015), clearly distinguishing its specific application to SG-MCMC.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing both asymptotic (central limit theorem) and non-asymptotic (bias and MSE bounds) results. The paper convincingly demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves convergence rates comparable to higher-order integrators using only first-order methods.
2. The experimental validation is thorough, with synthetic Gaussian models (where the posterior is analytically available) and real-world matrix factorization tasks. Results consistently show SGRRLD outperforming SGLD in terms of bias, MSE, and wall-clock time.
3. The method is generalizable to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, as demonstrated with SGHMC.
4. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides appropriate technical depth.

Weaknesses:
1. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the novelty may be incremental. The application of RR extrapolation to SDE approximation was explored in Lemaire et al. (2015), and the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC convergence was analyzed in Chen et al. (2015). The paper would benefit from a more precise statement of how it differs from these prior works.
2. The practical implementation details require clarification. The paper assumes no communication cost between the two parallel chains, but in distributed settings, this correlation would require communication at each step. The authors should address this in the discussion.
3. The experimental comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD needs more detail about computational resource allocation. When stating SGRRLD achieves the same accuracy as SGLD in half the time, it's unclear whether SGLD was run on half the computational resources (making the comparison unfair) or if SGRRLD's two chains were run on the same total resources as SGLD's single chain. The paper should explicitly state the resource allocation for both methods.

The paper's main contribution is showing that RR extrapolation can improve the convergence rate of first-order SG-MCMC methods to match that of higher-order methods while maintaining parallelizability. The theoretical results are compelling, and the experimental validation supports the claims.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The paper is well-written, the analysis is thorough, and the results are convincing. The minor weaknesses regarding novelty clarification and implementation details are important but do not undermine the paper's technical contribution.

Pro: Clear theoretical analysis, thorough experimental validation, generalizable approach, well-written
Con: Incremental novelty (compared to prior work on RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC convergence), unclear computational resource allocation in experiments

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference.
