This paper proposes a novel sampling algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SGRRLD), that aims to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) while keeping the variance at a reasonable level. The algorithm is based on a numerical sequence acceleration method, namely the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, which involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes. The authors provide formal theoretical analysis and show that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and its non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error can be bounded. The paper also presents experiments on synthetic and real data, demonstrating the practical use of the proposed algorithm.

The main ideas of the paper are related to previous work on SG-MCMC methods, particularly the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) algorithm. The authors build upon this work by introducing the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to improve the convergence of SG-MCMC. The paper also draws connections to other related work, such as the use of higher-order integrators in SG-MCMC.

The strengths of the paper include its clear presentation of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical analysis. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation and its application to SG-MCMC. The paper also presents a comprehensive set of experiments, including both synthetic and real data, to demonstrate the practical use of the proposed algorithm.

However, there are some weaknesses and concerns with the paper. One concern is the novelty of the paper, given that the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in previous work [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximation of invariant distributions in [23]. Additionally, the practical use of the algorithm may be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in the experiments. Specifically, the paper assumes that there is no communication cost between the two chains, which may not be realistic in practice. Furthermore, the computation cost of SG-MCMC mostly comes from computing stochastic gradients, and it is unclear how partial resources used in SGRRLD for computing gradients at each chain can lead to less wall-clock time than full resources used in SGLD.

To address these concerns, the authors could provide more details on the setup of the implementation, including how the computing resources are assigned for SGRRLD and SGLD. Additionally, the authors could consider comparing their algorithm with other alternatives, such as running two chains of SGLD embarrassingly (without communication).

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the technical aspects of SG-MCMC and the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors also provide a thorough explanation of the theoretical analysis and the experiments.

However, there are some minor issues with the paper. For example, the term "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" appears twice in the text. Additionally, some of the references are not properly cited in the text.

Overall, the paper proposes a valid contribution to the area of SG-MCMC methods, and the authors do a good job of putting this method in context with similar previous methods. However, the novelty of the paper may be incremental, and the practical use of the algorithm may be limited. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel sampling algorithm that aims to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC.
* The authors provide formal theoretical analysis and show that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and its non-asymptotic bias and mean squared error can be bounded.
* The paper presents experiments on synthetic and real data, demonstrating the practical use of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the paper may be incremental, given that the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in previous work.
* The practical use of the algorithm may be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in the experiments.
* The paper assumes that there is no communication cost between the two chains, which may not be realistic in practice.
