- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) with correlated Gaussian noise and linearly combining outputs to cancel leading-order bias.  
- Provides rigorous theory: asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Shows improved convergence rate: MSE O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for SGRRLD versus O(K⁻²/³) for SGLD.  
- Claims practical efficiency by achieving higher accuracy using two first-order integrators instead of complex higher-order integrators.  
- Empirical validation on a synthetic Gaussian model verifies theoretical bias/MSE behavior with step size and iterations.  
- Real-world experiments on large-scale matrix factorization demonstrate practical benefits and computational efficiency.  
- Places work in context: synthesizes prior SG-MCMC higher-order methods (Chen et al. 2015) and RR for SDEs (Lemaire et al. 2015).  
- Strength: strong theoretical contribution grounding the algorithm.  
- Strength: clear improvement in convergence rates with a simple parallel scheme.  
- Strength: thorough and well-designed experiments supporting theoretical claims.  
- Strength: clear writing and good presentation of motivation and related work.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental—mainly synthesizes known RR and SG-MCMC ideas rather than a wholly new concept.  
- Weakness/concern: required correlated Brownian motions imply per-step synchronization—possible significant communication overhead in parallel/distributed implementations.  
- Weakness/concern: unclear experimental resource allocation—wall-clock comparisons may be misleading if SGRRLD used more hardware than SGLD.  
- Weakness/concern: missing baseline comparison to two independent parallel SGLD chains (no communication) to isolate RR benefit from parallelization.  
- Acceptance arguments: technically sound, strong theory, empirical support, and clear exposition.  
- Rejection arguments: incremental novelty and potentially unfair/practically misleading experimental comparisons.  
- Minor: duplicate introduction of the term “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” should be cleaned up.
