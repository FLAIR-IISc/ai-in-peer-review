This paper proposes a novel application of the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration method, to improve the convergence of the Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm. By running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injections, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties of the proposed method, and the experimental results on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization tasks on real data verify the theoretical findings and showcase the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed contribution to the area of SG-MCMC methods. The authors do an excellent job of contextualizing their work within the existing literature, highlighting the connections to previous methods and demonstrating the value of their approach. However, I have two primary concerns regarding the paper.

Firstly, while the proposed method is innovative and effective, its novelty may be considered incremental. The impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in previous work [10], and the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been employed for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. Although the authors' application of this technique to SG-MCMC is new, it is essential to acknowledge the potential limitations of the paper's novelty.

Secondly, the practical applicability of the proposed algorithm may be limited, and the experimental comparison with standard SGLD may not be entirely fair. The parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm using correlated Brownian motion requires communication between the two chains at each step. However, the authors appear to assume that there is no communication cost associated with this process. It would be beneficial to discuss this aspect and its potential implications for the algorithm's performance.

Furthermore, the experimental setup and resource allocation for the proposed algorithm (SGRRLD) and SGLD are not entirely clear. For instance, is the single chain of SGLD implemented using the same resources as the two chains of SGRRLD? If not, this may not be a fair comparison from a practical perspective. If yes, then it is essential to explain how the resources are split between the two chains of SGRRLD during the algorithm's execution. Given that the primary computational cost of SG-MCMC arises from computing stochastic gradients, it is unclear how using partial resources for computing gradients at each chain in SGRRLD could lead to reduced wall-clock time compared to using full resources for SGLD.

To strengthen the experimental evaluation, I suggest providing more details on the implementation setup and considering alternative scenarios, such as running two chains of SGLD in parallel without communication. This would help to better understand the performance benefits and limitations of the proposed algorithm.
