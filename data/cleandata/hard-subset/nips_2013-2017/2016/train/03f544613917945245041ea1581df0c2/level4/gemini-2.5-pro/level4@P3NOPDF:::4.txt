### **Summary**

This paper introduces a Richardson-Romberg extrapolation scheme to improve the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods. The proposed method is straightforward to implement, requiring minimal modifications to existing algorithms, and aims to reduce both the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the resulting estimators. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis to support the claimed improvements in convergence rates. Overall, the application of this classical numerical technique to modern inference algorithms is a nice contribution, and the theory appears to be sound. However, I have some significant concerns regarding the clarity of the theoretical derivations and the fairness of the experimental evaluation, as well as several smaller points that need clarification.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The core idea of applying Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to accelerate SG-MCMC is elegant and well-motivated. The resulting algorithm is simple and practical, as it primarily involves running two parallel chains with different step sizes and combining their outputs.

2.  **Theoretical Support:** The paper is backed by a rigorous theoretical analysis that establishes improved rates of convergence for bias and MSE over standard SG-MCMC methods.

3.  **Ease of Implementation:** The proposed method requires only minor changes to existing SG-MCMC samplers, making it a practical and accessible tool for practitioners.

### **Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Derivations:** My main concern revolves around the derivation of the specific convergence rates from the main theorems. The paper presents its theoretical results, but the steps connecting these theorems to the final stated rates for bias and MSE could be more explicit. I would appreciate it if the authors could provide a more detailed walkthrough or an appendix section clarifying this derivation, as it is central to the paper's contribution.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Evaluation (Matrix Factorization):** A significant practical concern is the fairness of the experimental comparison in Figure 5. The proposed method, SGRRLD, requires running two MCMC chains, with one effectively using twice the number of steps. This leads to a total computational cost that is approximately three times that of a single standard SGLD chain if run on a single core. The plots comparing performance against wall-clock time do not seem to account for this increased cost. To provide a fair comparison, the timeline for the baseline SGLD should be scaled to reflect an equivalent computational budget (e.g., its results at time `T` should be compared with SGRRLD's results at time `3T`). Without this adjustment, the performance gap between the methods may be significantly exaggerated.

3.  **Inconsistencies in Experimental Results (Figure 3):** There appear to be some inconsistencies in the bias experiment presented in Figure 3.
    *   First, the text states that the optimal convergence rate is achieved with a step size decay rate of `α = 1/3`, yet the plot clearly shows the red curve (`α = 0.2`) achieving the lowest bias. Please clarify this discrepancy.
    *   Second, the curve corresponding to `α = 0.2`, while achieving the best final bias, exhibits significant fluctuations. This behavior seems at odds with a stable convergence rate and warrants an explanation. What might be causing this instability?

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   In line 256, it is stated that for a very small step size `γ`, the `1/(Kγ)` term will dominate the error. Could you elaborate on why this effect is specific to the proposed method and does not similarly dominate the error for standard SGLD in the same regime?

### **Minor Comments and Typos**

*   **Eq. 1:** For consistency, please consider using `θ` as the variable of integration, in line with the rest of the paper's notation.
*   **Line 94:** The abbreviation "RR" is used before it is formally defined as Richardson-Romberg.
*   **Line 131:** The term `Γ_K` is used without a prior definition.
*   **Line 160:** The paper assumes a "smooth function f". Please specify the required degree of smoothness (e.g., Lipschitz continuity, differentiability class).
*   **Line 249:** The caption for Figure 1(b) states it plots "Bias vs. dimension", but the plot shows "Bias vs. time". This appears to be a typo, and the caption may be intended for Figure 1(c).
*   **Presentation:** For improved readability, please consider using a bold-face font for all vectors throughout the manuscript.
