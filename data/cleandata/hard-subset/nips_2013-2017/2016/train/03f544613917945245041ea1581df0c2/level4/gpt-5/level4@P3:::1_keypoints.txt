- Proposes SGRRLD: apply Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC by running two coupled SGLD chains (step sizes γ and γ/2) and forming an extrapolated estimator to cancel leading discretization bias.  
- Couples Gaussian noises across chains to cancel bias and minimize variance, building on Talay–Tubaro and related SDE numerical results.  
- Theoretical results: proves asymptotic consistency, an optimal-coupling CLT, and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds under stochastic-gradient noise.  
- Fixed-step results: bias O(γ^2), MSE O(γ^4 + 1/(Kγ)), yielding optimal finite-time MSE O(K^-4/5) vs. O(K^-2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC.  
- Decreasing-step results: with γk ∝ k^-α, optimal asymptotic error O(K^-2/5) at α = 1/5, improving over SGLD’s O(K^-1/3).  
- Generalizes the extrapolation approach beyond SGLD (e.g., SGHMC) under mild conditions.  
- Empirically validated on synthetic Gaussian models and large-scale matrix factorization (MovieLens) showing consistent improvements.  
- Strength: simple, principled adaptation of RRE to SG-MCMC with explicit noise-coupling guidance.  
- Strength: rigorous theory including CLT and non-asymptotic bounds that show improved finite-time rates and step-size schedules.  
- Strength: practical — easy to implement, parallelizable (two chains), and compatible with existing SG-MCMC codebases.  
- Strength: general applicability to other SG-MCMC samplers.  
- Weakness: CLT and analysis rely on strong smoothness/regularity (high regularity Poisson equation solutions) that may not hold in typical ML posteriors.  
- Weakness: requires the two chains’ stochastic gradients to have the same distribution (e.g., identical mini-batch sizes); practical costs and coupling strategies are not fully explored.  
- Weakness: experimental scope is limited — needs more domains (e.g., Bayesian logistic regression, neural nets) and diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, calibration).  
- Weakness: cost model glosses over doubled computation; more detailed compute/memory and multi-GPU/cluster scalability analysis is needed.  
- Weakness: limited direct comparisons to higher-order SG-MCMC integrators to substantiate claims of higher-order accuracy with a first-order integrator.  
- Quality: theoretical analysis appears careful and technically sound; correlated Gaussian increments are justified and variance-optimal.  
- Clarity: paper is well written, but Figure 1a should label empirical mean/variance vs. density, and Figure 2a needs a brief explanation of non-monotone bias at small γ.  
- Originality: novel application of RRE to SG-MCMC with rigorous analysis, bridging SDE numerics and large-scale Bayesian computation.  
- Significance: provides a concrete accuracy–cost–parallelism trade-off with meaningful theoretical and empirical improvements, likely to stimulate follow-up work (e.g., multilevel SG-MCMC).  
- Pros for acceptance: simple, general, practically useful idea with theoretical guarantees and demonstrated finite-time rate improvements.  
- Cons for acceptance: strong regularity assumptions, limited experiments and comparisons, and missing practical implementation details (mini-batch coupling, RNG/communication).  
- Suggestions: clarify figures and explain non-monotone bias; provide implementation details for correlated increments and discuss mini-batch coupling.  
- Suggestions: add ablations (uncorrelated vs correlated noise, unequal mini-batches, recursive extrapolation) and expand comparisons to higher-order integrators with ESS/time and calibration metrics.  
- Suggestions: give practical guidance on choosing α and γ in typical datasets and consider adaptive/scheduled schemes.  
- Overall assessment: clear, technically solid, and practically relevant contribution; recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing clarity and implementation details.
