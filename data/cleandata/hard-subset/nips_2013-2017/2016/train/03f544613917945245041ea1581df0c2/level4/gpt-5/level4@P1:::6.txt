Summary
This paper adapts Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—a classical sequence-acceleration technique—to stochastic-gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC). The proposed method, SGRRLD, runs two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2), couples their Gaussian perturbations, and combines their estimates to cancel the leading discretization bias. The authors provide asymptotic results (consistency and a CLT) and non-asymptotic bounds showing improved rates: bias O(γ2) and MSE O(γ4) asymptotically; with K iterations, an MSE rate O(K−4/5) versus O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC. They also justify the variance-minimizing coupling of the Brownian noise. Experiments on a linear Gaussian model verify the predicted bias/MSE scalings, and a large-scale matrix factorization task demonstrates practical gains in wall-clock time against SGLD. An RR variant for SGHMC (SGRRHMC) is also explored and compared to a higher-order splitting integrator.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on: (i) SG-MCMC foundations and analyses of bias/MSE with first- and higher-order integrators (e.g., [10]); and (ii) RR extrapolation for SDE discretizations and invariant measures (e.g., [21], [23]). The novelty is applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients, giving theory and empirical validation, and showing how to control variance via correlated Brownian increments.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable wrapper: can be layered on top of first-order SG-MCMC algorithms to recover higher-order accuracy without deriving new integrators.
- Solid theoretical analysis: asymptotic CLT and non-asymptotic bounds; clear identification of optimal coupling of Gaussian noise; rate improvements are carefully derived and match experiments.
- Practical relevance: experiments include both controlled synthetic settings that probe scalings and a large-scale application (matrix factorization) with observable wall-clock gains.
- Extension beyond SGLD: preliminary evidence that the approach benefits SGHMC as well.

Main concerns
1) Novelty. While applying RR to SG-MCMC appears new, the individual components are known: higher-order effects in SG-MCMC were analyzed in [10], and RR extrapolation for invariant measures in closely related settings appears in [23]. The contribution is therefore incremental in technique, though the adaptation to stochastic gradients and the coupling analysis are nontrivial and useful.

2) Practicality and fairness of comparisons. The method requires two chains with correlated Brownian increments. This induces a need for synchronization/communication at every step (to form Z(γ) from the two Z(γ/2) terms), unless some shared-randomness scheme eliminates inter-process communication. The experiments seem to assume no communication cost and report similar wall-clock time for SGLD and SGRRLD. Please clarify:
- How are the coupled Gaussian increments implemented in practice? Do the two processes communicate per step, or do they share a deterministic PRNG scheme that guarantees the required correlations without runtime communication?
- What hardware resources are allocated to each method? The paper states SGRRLD runs two processes in parallel while SGLD is “inherently sequential,” but a single SGLD chain could also exploit multiple cores (e.g., parallelizing mini-batch gradient computations or using BLAS multithreading). If SGRRLD uses two cores while SGLD uses one, then the wall-clock comparison is not resource-fair.
- How is gradient computation parallelized? Since gradient evaluation dominates cost, splitting resources across two chains could increase per-iteration latency unless memory bandwidth or other bottlenecks are addressed. Please provide details on threads/cores, BLAS threading, and I/O.
- As a stronger baseline, please compare to running two independent SGLD chains in parallel (no coupling or communication) under the same total computational budget. This would help isolate the benefit of RR extrapolation from mere parallelism.

Quality
- The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound, with appropriate conditions (albeit deferred to the supplement). The role of correlated Brownian increments is clearly analyzed; it would be helpful to include an empirical ablation comparing correlated vs independent increments to confirm the variance effect in finite samples.
- Assumptions on stochastic gradients: the analysis emphasizes equal mini-batch sizes; please clarify whether the mini-batch indices are shared across chains (i.e., identical subsets) and whether any gradient correlation is used in practice. While the CLT suggests gradient correlation is asymptotically less critical than Gaussian coupling, finite-time performance may still be sensitive.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. Some important implementation details (above) are missing and should be added.
- Minor issues:
  - “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is defined twice (around lines 25 and 70).
  - There are a few typos (e.g., “mean squarederror,” “embarassingly”); a careful proofreading would help.

Originality
- Moderate. The contribution is a thoughtful and well-executed adaptation of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC with stochastic gradients and coupled noise, supported by new analysis and experiments. The conceptual novelty is incremental relative to [10] and [23] but still of interest to the SG-MCMC community.

Significance
- Potentially useful, as the method is simple to implement and widely applicable. The observed gains are encouraging. However, the practical value hinges on resource-fair comparisons and communication/synchronization costs in realistic distributed settings. Including a baseline with two independent SGLD chains and a discussion of communication overheads would strengthen the case.
- It would also be informative to compare SGRRLD to higher-order SGLD variants (in the spirit of [10]) on at least one task, to substantiate the claim of matching higher-order accuracy with first-order integrators.

Pros
- Simple, general technique with rigorous analysis.
- Provable and observed improvements in bias/MSE and convergence rates.
- Applicable to multiple SG-MCMC variants; demonstrated on SGLD and SGHMC.
- Parallelizable structure.

Cons
- Incremental novelty relative to known RR and higher-order integrator analyses.
- Unclear accounting for communication costs and resource fairness; missing baseline with two independent SGLD chains.
- Some implementation details and ablations are absent.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a clear idea, solid theory, and promising empirical results. Addressing the practical concerns and adding the suggested comparisons would significantly strengthen the contribution.
