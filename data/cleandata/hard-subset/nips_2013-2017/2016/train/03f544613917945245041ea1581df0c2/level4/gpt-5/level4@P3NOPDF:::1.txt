This paper applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to “big-data MCMC” methods, focusing on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). By importing a classic bias-reduction tool from numerical analysis and SDE discretization into the MCMC setting, the work creates a useful bridge between two communities. The authors propose SGLD-RRE, provide theoretical results on its convergence and conditions under which it improves over vanilla SGLD, and present both toy and large-scale experiments. The method is straightforward to implement, computationally convenient, and the paper is clearly written and technically sound.

Contributions and significance:
- Conceptual bridge: The paper convincingly links MCMC methodology with SDE numerical analysis, opening doors for further cross-pollination.
- Algorithmic proposal: A simple yet effective RRE-based modification of SGLD that aims to reduce discretization bias without significantly complicating implementation.
- Theory: Convergence results and arguments showing when and why SGLD-RRE outperforms SGLD, which help demystify the bias–variance trade-offs at play.
- Empirics: Strong gains on controlled toy problems and modest but consistent improvements on larger-scale tasks, demonstrating practical value.

Strengths:
- Clarity and exposition: The paper is easy to follow, with careful motivation and transparent presentation.
- Technical soundness: The theoretical arguments appear correct and well aligned with the algorithmic choices.
- Practicality: RRE is a drop-in modification that practitioners can readily adopt, with limited code complexity and runtime overhead relative to baseline SGLD.
- Empirical credibility: The experiments support the main claims; the balance of toy and large-scale settings is appropriate.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Incremental novelty: The core idea—applying RRE to SGLD—is relatively straightforward. While this is a feature (simplicity) for practice, the conceptual novelty is moderate.
- Scope of gains: Improvements on large-scale tasks are modest; this is still useful, but suggests careful guidance to users on when the method is most impactful.
- Practical guidance: The paper would benefit from more explicit recommendations for choosing the step-size pair(s) and weights in RRE (e.g., how to tune the two levels, schedules, and minibatch sizes; stability considerations). Any rules of thumb distilled from theory or experiments would help practitioners.
- Cost–benefit analysis: A clearer accounting of computational overhead (e.g., extra gradient evaluations, wall-clock vs. error) would contextualize the observed gains. Reporting effective sample size per second or error vs. time could strengthen the empirical case.
- Positioning among alternatives: A brief discussion comparing SGLD-RRE to other bias-reduction or multilevel/debiasing approaches for SGLD (and to closely related samplers like SGHMC or preconditioned/variance-reduced variants) would situate the contribution more fully.
- Assumptions vs. practice: It would help to comment on how the theoretical assumptions (smoothness, dissipativity, noise conditions) relate to the experimental models, and whether any violations might explain performance differences across tasks.

Minor comments:
- Figure 1a: The current panel is a Gaussian with the empirical posterior mean and variance plugged in, not the empirical posterior itself. Please state this clearly in the caption and main text to avoid confusion.
- Figure 2a: The non-monotonic behavior of bias with decreasing step size can be counterintuitive. A short discussion would be valuable. Possible contributors include: (i) RRE cancels leading-order discretization bias but higher-order terms can dominate at small step sizes; (ii) stochastic gradient noise and subsampling variability interact with discretization in nontrivial ways; (iii) finite-time or burn-in effects that depend on step-size schedules; and (iv) numerical coupling error across levels used by RRE. Clarifying which of these factors is most relevant here would help readers.
- Notation and reproducibility: If space permits, provide the exact coefficients/level combinations used for RRE in experiments and any default hyperparameters, so results are easily reproducible.

Overall evaluation:
This is a solid, well-executed paper that brings a standard but powerful idea from SDE numerics into modern stochastic-gradient MCMC. The theoretical developments and experiments are persuasive, the writing is clear, and the method is easy to adopt. While the novelty is moderate and the large-scale gains are not dramatic, the work is technically sound and practically useful. I expect practitioners to find SGLD-RRE a worthwhile enhancement, even if it does not change the landscape of large-scale Bayesian computation. I recommend acceptance.
