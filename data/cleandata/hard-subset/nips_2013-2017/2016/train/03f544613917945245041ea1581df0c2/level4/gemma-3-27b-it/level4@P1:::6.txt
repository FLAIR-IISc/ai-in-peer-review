This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the convergence of Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms by leveraging the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation method, a technique commonly used for numerical sequence acceleration. The core idea involves running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and incorporating correlated Brownian motion. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence analyses, and validate their method through experiments on synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task. Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of SG-MCMC. It effectively contextualizes the proposed technique within existing methods and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature.

My main concerns center around the incremental nature of the novelty and practical considerations regarding implementation. While the application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel, the underlying principles of improving convergence through higher-order numerical integration have been explored in previous work, such as [10]. Furthermore, the use of RR extrapolation for approximating invariant distributions is established, as demonstrated in [23]. The practical utility of the proposed algorithm could be limited by the need for communication between the two chains due to the correlated Brownian motion. The paper does not explicitly address the communication costs associated with this correlation, which is a critical aspect for parallel implementations. It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation.

The experimental setup also raises some questions. It’s unclear how computational resources were allocated to SGRRLD and SGLD. Specifically, was a single chain of SGLD implemented using the same computational resources as the two chains of SGRRLD? If not, the comparison may not be entirely fair from a practical perspective. If so, further details are needed regarding how resources were split between the two chains within the SGRRLD algorithm. Given that the dominant cost in SG-MCMC typically arises from computing stochastic gradients, it is not immediately obvious why using partial resources for gradient computation in each chain of SGRRLD would lead to a reduction in wall-clock time compared to dedicating full resources to a single SGLD chain. Providing a more detailed explanation of the implementation setup would strengthen the experimental results. Finally, comparing SGRRLD against two independent, embarrassingly parallel SGLD chains (without communication) would provide a useful baseline. 

Minor comments: The phrase "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" appears redundantly on lines 25 and 70.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a technically sound and well-motivated method for improving the convergence of SG-MCMC algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis provides rigorous guarantees for the asymptotic and non-asymptotic properties of the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed technique and its theoretical foundations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the paper may be incremental, building upon existing work in higher-order integration and RR extrapolation.
*   The practical implications of the algorithm, particularly regarding communication costs in parallel implementations, are not fully addressed.
*   The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding resource allocation and comparison with alternative parallel implementations of SGLD.
