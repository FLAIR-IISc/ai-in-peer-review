Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) offers a computationally attractive approach to Bayesian posterior inference with large datasets and has garnered significant attention. A key limitation of these methods is their asymptotic bias, preventing exact convergence to the target posterior distribution. This paper proposes a compelling solution: Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce this bias. The technique is conceptually simple and elegant – running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with the second chain employing half the step size and twice the iterations of the first. Crucially, the injected Gaussian noise in the two chains is correlated, defined as  α<sub>k</sub> = (β<sub>2k-1</sub> + β<sub>2k</sub>)/√2, where α<sub>k</sub> and β<sub>k</sub> represent the noise at time step *k* in Chains 1 and 2, respectively.  The authors demonstrate that subtracting the expectation estimate derived from Chain 1’s samples from that of Chain 2 yields a lower bias, scaling as O(γ<sup>2</sup>) compared to the O(γ) bias obtained from either chain alone (γ representing the step size).  A corresponding reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates is also proven. 

I found the method’s simplicity and ease of implementation highly appealing (as summarized below), and its minimal disruption to existing SG-MCMC algorithms is a significant strength. Its broad applicability across a range of SG-MCMC methods suggests potentially substantial impact for the field.

**Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is established in numerical analysis, its application within the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC context appears novel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and presents the material clearly.

**Questions and Comments:**

*   **Generality:** Can this technique be universally applied to all SG-MCMC algorithms? Beyond satisfying the bound outlined in section 2.2 (π<sub>γ</sub>(f) = π(f) + C * γ + O(γ<sup>2</sup>)), are there additional necessary conditions?
*   **Applicability Beyond SG-MCMC:**  Could this method extend to other approximate MCMC schemes that aren’t strictly gradient-based, such as those employing sub-sampling in Metropolis-Hastings acceptance tests? What about its potential application to Distributed SGLD?
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The requirement for one chain to run for twice the duration of the other introduces computational inefficiency. Would employing three chains – one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2, all running for the same number of steps – be a viable alternative?  Would this approach impact the variance of the estimate?
*   **Higher-Order Extrapolation:** Has the authors considered extending the method to incorporate more than two step sizes for potentially further bias reduction?
*   **Noise Correlation and Mini-Batches:** The correlation between the injected noise is a key aspect of the method. However, the effect of the correlation between mini-batches isn’t fully clear. Is minimal variance achieved when using the same batch for the k<sup>th</sup> iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)<sup>th</sup> and 2k<sup>th</sup> iterations of Chain 2? Or is this correlation’s impact negligible?
*   **Experimental Validation (Matrix Factorization):** The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should ideally be run until convergence.  Furthermore, a comparison to standard Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is crucial. While the current results demonstrate an advantage over SGLD, establishing superior performance compared to SGD in a scenario where the Bayesian treatment provides a benefit (i.e., improved generalization) would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact. Bayesian matrix factorization isn’t a widely adopted technique, so demonstrating tangible improvements with quantitative results will encourage wider adoption.  Including an SGD performance curve would provide valuable context for interpreting the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
*   **Algorithm Box:** Despite the clear presentation, an algorithm box containing pseudocode would enhance accessibility, particularly for practitioners less familiar with the theoretical underpinnings. Many practitioners may skip the more theoretical sections; therefore, readily available pseudocode would allow them to experiment with the method without needing a deep understanding of the theory.
