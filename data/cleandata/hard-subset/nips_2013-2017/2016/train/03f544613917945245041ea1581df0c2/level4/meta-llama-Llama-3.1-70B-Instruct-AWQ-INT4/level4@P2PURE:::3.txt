Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods have become a popular approach for Bayesian posterior inference in large datasets, attracting significant interest from the research community in recent years. However, one major drawback of these methods is their asymptotic bias, which prevents them from converging exactly to the posterior distribution. This paper proposes a technique called Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce this bias. The core idea is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with Chain 2 having half the step size of Chain 1 and running for twice the number of steps. The injected Gaussian noise in the chains is correlated, following the relationship alphak = (beta{2k-1} + beta{2k})/sqrt(2), where alphak and betak are the injected Gaussian noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To compute the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution, one simply subtracts the estimate computed using samples from Chain 1 from the estimate computed using Chain 2. The resulting estimate exhibits lower bias, O(gamma^2), compared to O(gamma) obtained from either Chain 1 or 2 alone, where gamma is the step size. The authors also prove a similar reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates. I appreciate the simplicity and generality of this method, which does not require significant modifications to the base SG-MCMC algorithm. Its potential impact on the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community is substantial.

Novelty: Although Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is well-established in numerical analysis, its application in the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities is novel.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear.

Comments/questions:

- Is this technique directly applicable to all SG-MCMC methods, or are there specific conditions, beyond the bound in Section 2.2, that must be satisfied?
- Can this method be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling based Metropolis-Hastings tests or Distributed SGLD?
- Given that one chain must run for twice as long as the other, resulting in wasted computation, is it possible to use three chains with different step sizes (gamma, gamma/2, and gamma/2) and run them for the same number of steps? Would this approach increase the variance of the estimate?
- Is there a straightforward extension of this method to more than two step sizes?
- The correlation between the injected noise in the chains is crucial, but I am unclear about the effect of correlation between mini-batches. Is the variance minimized when using the same batch for the kth iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)th and 2kth iterations of Chain 2, or is this correlation effect negligible?
- I suggest running the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) until convergence and comparing the results to SGD. The current plot demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms SGLD, but it would be more convincing to show that the method is advantageous in a setting where Bayesian inference is beneficial (i.e., better than SGD). Including an SGD plot as a control would provide a better perspective on the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
- Although the method is easy to understand and implement, including an algorithm box would make the paper more accessible to a broader audience. Providing pseudo-code early in the paper would enable readers unfamiliar with this area to try out the method without needing to understand the more technical aspects.
