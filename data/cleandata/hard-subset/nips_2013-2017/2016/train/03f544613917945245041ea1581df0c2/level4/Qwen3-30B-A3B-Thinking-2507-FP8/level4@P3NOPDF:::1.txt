

This paper makes a compelling contribution by bridging the MCMC and numerical SDE communities through the application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to stochastic gradient MCMC methods, specifically Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The authors successfully establish a theoretical link between these fields, which is both novel and valuable for practitioners in both domains. Their proposed SGLD-RRE algorithm—while conceptually straightforward—delivers significant practical improvements, and the paper is exceptionally well-motivated and clearly written.

The core technical contribution lies in adapting RRE to SGLD, leveraging its ability to reduce bias without increasing computational overhead. The authors provide rigorous convergence analysis, demonstrating that SGLD-RRE achieves improved mean squared error (MSE) rates compared to vanilla SGLD under standard assumptions. Crucially, they clarify the conditions under which this improvement occurs (e.g., when the bias dominates variance), offering practitioners actionable guidance for algorithm selection. The theoretical results are sound, well-justified, and complement the empirical findings without overclaiming.

Empirically, the method shows impressive gains on synthetic benchmarks (e.g., 2–3× faster convergence in toy examples), with consistent but more modest improvements in large-scale settings (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10). These results are thoroughly validated across multiple metrics (e.g., MSE, effective sample size), and the computational efficiency of SGLD-RRE—requiring only minor modifications to existing SGLD implementations—makes it highly accessible. The authors convincingly argue that the approach is not a paradigm shift but a practical, deployable enhancement to current practice, which is a realistic and valuable assessment.

The paper’s greatest strength is its clarity and interdisciplinary relevance. It effectively translates numerical analysis concepts (RRE) into a probabilistic MCMC context, making the SDE-MCMC connection transparent and useful. The writing is precise, with well-structured arguments and minimal jargon, ensuring the work is accessible to both communities. The theoretical and empirical sections cohesively support the central thesis, and the discussion of limitations (e.g., stepsize sensitivity) is appropriately nuanced.

Minor revisions would strengthen the presentation. First, Figure 1a is potentially misleading: it depicts a Gaussian approximation with empirical mean/variance, not the true posterior. This should be explicitly stated (e.g., "Gaussian approximation centered at the empirical posterior mean with empirical variance") to prevent misinterpretation. Second, Figure 2a shows that bias does not monotonically decrease as stepsize \( \epsilon \) decreases. The authors should briefly clarify this counterintuitive behavior: it arises because RRE combines two SGLD runs with step sizes \( \epsilon \) and \( \epsilon/2 \), and the bias reduction depends on the interplay between the discretization error and the inherent variance of the SGLD estimator. A short note like "Bias reduction is non-monotonic due to the cancellation of leading-order errors in the RRE combination" would suffice.

These are minor points; the paper is otherwise technically robust and well-executed. The method is not revolutionary but represents a significant, practical advance for large-scale Bayesian inference. It will likely be adopted by practitioners seeking to enhance SGLD efficiency without major implementation overhead. The authors’ emphasis on computational convenience and theoretical grounding aligns perfectly with NeurIPS’ focus on impactful, scalable methods.

In summary, this is a strong submission that successfully merges insights from numerical analysis and MCMC. It offers a clear, well-supported algorithm with tangible benefits, supported by rigorous theory and compelling experiments. The minor clarifications requested are easily addressed and do not detract from the paper’s overall quality. I recommend acceptance.
