This paper addresses the issue of high bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, which are widely used for scalable Bayesian inference. The authors propose a novel method, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which adapts the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation technique from numerical analysis to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC estimators. The core idea is to run two parallel instances of an SG-MCMC algorithm (specifically SGLD) with different step sizes (γ and γ/2). To ensure the chains cover the same time horizon, the chain with the smaller step size takes twice as many iterations. A new estimator is then constructed as a linear combination of the estimators from the two chains. A key aspect of the method is the use of correlated Brownian increments across the two chains to control the variance of the final estimator. This work builds upon the foundational SGLD algorithm by Welling and Teh (2011) and connects to theoretical work analyzing the bias of SG-MCMC methods (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Teh et al., 2016). While RR extrapolation is a known technique for stochastic differential equations (e.g., Talay & Tubaro, 1990), its application in the context of SG-MCMC is novel. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE), showing that SGRRLD achieves a faster convergence rate (MSE of O(K⁻⁴/⁵)) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)).

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to the SG-MCMC framework is a novel and clever idea. Given that bias is a primary limitation of many SG-MCMC methods, this work offers a potentially high-impact solution. The technique appears to be general and, as demonstrated with SGHMC, could be applied to a broad class of SG-MCMC algorithms, making it a valuable contribution to the community.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis that supports their claims, including asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and non-asymptotic bounds on the bias and MSE. The derived convergence rates clearly demonstrate the theoretical advantage of the proposed method over first-order SG-MCMC methods.
*   **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is conceptually simple and elegant. The core idea of running two chains in parallel and combining their outputs is easy to understand and implement, which should facilitate its adoption by practitioners. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data effectively support the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Experimental Comparison:** The matrix factorization experiments compellingly show that SGRRLD outperforms SGLD. However, to better demonstrate the significance of the results, the comparison could be extended. Including a strong optimization-based baseline like SGD would help contextualize the final performance of both SGLD and SGRRLD. Furthermore, running the experiments until convergence would provide a clearer picture of the final accuracy gains in a practical setting where Bayesian methods are expected to excel.
*   **Scope of Applicability:** The authors suggest the method is broadly applicable, which is a major strength. It would be beneficial to more formally delineate the conditions required for an SG-MCMC algorithm to be compatible with this RR scheme. For instance, are there any constraints beyond the existence of a weak error expansion in powers of the step size? A brief discussion on its applicability to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as those employing Metropolis-Hastings corrections or non-Gaussian driving noise, would further clarify the scope.
*   **Practical Guidance on Implementation:** The theoretical analysis shows that correlating the Brownian noise is critical for variance control, but the impact of correlating the data mini-batches is less clear. The paper states the effect on asymptotic variance is negligible, but some discussion on the finite-sample performance trade-offs (e.g., using the same vs. independent mini-batches for the coupled steps) would be a valuable addition for practitioners.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed parallel scheme leaves one computational core idle while the faster chain waits for the slower one to complete its two steps. This is an inefficient use of parallel resources. The authors could strengthen the paper by acknowledging this and briefly discussing alternative strategies. For example, could the idle core be used to run an independent third chain to potentially reduce variance further, and what would be the theoretical implications?
*   **Presentation:** While the method is described clearly, the inclusion of a pseudo-code algorithm box in the main body of the paper (rather than just the supplement) would greatly enhance its accessibility to a wider audience, allowing practitioners to implement the method without needing to parse the theoretical sections.
