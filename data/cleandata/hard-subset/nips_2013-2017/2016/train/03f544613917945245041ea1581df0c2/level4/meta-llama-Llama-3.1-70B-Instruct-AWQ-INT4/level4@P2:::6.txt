This paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient Markov chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias of SG-MCMC while maintaining a reasonable variance. The approach is based on the Richardson-Romberg extrapolation method, a numerical sequence acceleration technique that requires running two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Brownian motions. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including convergence results and bounds for the bias and mean squared error (MSE) of the proposed algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of putting their method in context with similar previous approaches. The experiments on synthetic Gaussian models verify the bias and MSE bounds of the theorems, and the experiments on large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrate the practical use of the proposed algorithm over standard Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD).

However, I have two main concerns. Firstly, the novelty of the paper may be incremental, given that the effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed in [10], and the proposed Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant distributions in [23]. Secondly, the practical use of the algorithm can be limited, and there may be a lack of fair comparison in the experiments.

Specifically, the parallel implementation of the SG-MCMC algorithm requires the two chains to communicate at every step, which may incur significant communication costs. However, the authors seem to assume that there is no communication cost. It would be better to mention this and discuss the potential implications. Additionally, it is not clear how the computing resources are assigned for SGRRLD and SGLD in the experiments. For example, is the single chain of SGLD implemented using the same resources that the two chains of SGRRLD use? If not, this may not be a fair comparison for practical considerations.

Minor comments include the repetition of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" on lines 25 and 70. Overall, this is a nice paper that proposes a valid contribution to the area of SG-MCMC methods. However, addressing the above concerns would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel SG-MCMC algorithm that improves the convergence of existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experiments verify the bias and MSE bounds of the theorems.
* The paper demonstrates the practical use of the proposed algorithm on large-scale matrix factorization problems.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the paper may be incremental, given the existing work on numerical integrators and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation.
* The practical use of the algorithm can be limited due to the communication costs and resource allocation.
* The experiments may not provide a fair comparison between SGRRLD and SGLD due to the unclear resource allocation and potential communication costs.
