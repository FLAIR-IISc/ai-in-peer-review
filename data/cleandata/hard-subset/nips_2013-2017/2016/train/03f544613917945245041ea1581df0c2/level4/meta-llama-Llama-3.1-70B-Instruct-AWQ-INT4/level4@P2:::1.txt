This paper applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation (RRE) to recent "big-data MCMC" methods, with a focus on Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). RRE is a standard numerical integration tool that has recently seen some nice applications to numerical stochastic differential equations (SDEs); one of the nice features of this paper is that it provides a link between the MCMC literature and the SDE literature, which may be beneficial for both communities.

The authors introduce a new SGLD-RRE method, develop some theoretical results on the convergence of the new algorithm (along with arguments for when SGLD-RRE improves on vanilla SGLD), and show some numerical results. They obtain impressive improvements on toy numerical examples, and modest but consistent improvements in large-scale examples.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The theoretical results are solid, and the empirical performance of the novel proposed algorithm is good. The method ends up being a fairly straightforward application of RRE to SGLD, which is computationally convenient. I believe practitioners will use these methods, though the methods will not revolutionize current practice.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, I would like to evaluate the paper based on the four criteria: quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence of the new algorithm. The theoretical results are well-supported by experimental evidence.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the method and its theoretical underpinnings.

Originality: The paper combines two existing techniques (RRE and SGLD) in a novel way, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Significance: The paper has the potential to impact the field of MCMC, particularly in the context of big-data applications. The method is computationally convenient and has shown promising results in both toy and large-scale examples.

Minor comments:

* I found Fig 1a slightly misleading - this is not the empirical estimated posterior distribution here, but rather a gaussian with the empirical posterior mean and variance plugged in. This should be clarified / emphasized a bit more for the unwary reader.
* Fig 2a – it would be helpful to add a bit of discussion about why the bias is not necessarily decreasing as the stepsize decreases; this is a bit counter-intuitive.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel combination of two existing techniques (RRE and SGLD) that has the potential to impact the field of MCMC.
* The theoretical results are solid, and the empirical performance of the novel proposed algorithm is good.
* The method is computationally convenient and has shown promising results in both toy and large-scale examples.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The method is not revolutionary, and the improvements over vanilla SGLD are modest in large-scale examples.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method and potential avenues for future research.
