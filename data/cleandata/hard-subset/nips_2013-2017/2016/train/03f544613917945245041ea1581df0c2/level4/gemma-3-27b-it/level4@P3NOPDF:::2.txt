This paper presents an interesting application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD), resulting in a new algorithm, SGRRLD. The core idea is to reduce the bias inherent in SGLD due to gradient estimation noise. The authors demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that SGRRLD achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to standard SGLD. The clarity of the algorithm description is a significant strength, making implementation straightforward.

The synthetic experiments using a Linear Gaussian Model convincingly illustrate SGRRLD’s ability to reduce bias in a controlled setting. However, the conclusions drawn from the MovieLens experiments are less definitive. While a slight improvement in test RMSE is reported, the magnitude of this improvement is marginal and its statistical significance is not fully established. More substantial evidence from real-world datasets is needed to demonstrate the practical benefits of this technique.

The theoretical contributions, building on existing work that leverages RR extrapolation to improve convergence rates for Monte Carlo estimates of solutions to stochastic differential equations, are noteworthy. The authors prove, under specific conditions, that SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD for the same step size, albeit at the cost of maintaining two parallel Markov chains. Regarding these theoretical results, I have a question about the conditions required for the matrix *U* to satisfy both Theorems 1 and 2. Specifically, can *U* be multi-modal? A clearer understanding of these conditions would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.

Furthermore, I am curious about the interplay between gradient noise correlation and chain decorrelation. Intuitively, if gradient noise is uncorrelated, the two chains should rapidly become independent. How does the combination of these chains in SGRRLD effectively reduce the order of the error, and how is this reduction influenced by the level of correlation in the Gaussian noises? Observing and reporting the correlation between the two chains in the real data experiments would provide valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.

A key claim regarding computational cost – that SGRRLD requires roughly the same computation time as SGLD due to parallelization – raises a point for clarification. While running SGRRLD's two chains in parallel can achieve comparable wall-clock time, one could also run two parallel chains of SGLD within the same timeframe. A direct comparison between the estimation quality of two SGLD chains versus SGRRLD would help substantiate the claimed advantage. 

The experimental setup also warrants further discussion. The real data experiments compare SGRRLD with step sizes γ and γ/2 against SGLD with a step size of γ. A more comprehensive evaluation would include a comparison with SGLD also employing a step size of γ/2, as this would likely reduce SGLD’s bias and provide a more fair comparison. Expanding the set of real-world datasets used for evaluation is also crucial for establishing the robustness and generalizability of SGRRLD.

In summary, this paper introduces a promising approach to bias reduction in SGLD. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the synthetic experiments are convincing. However, the empirical evidence from real-world datasets is currently insufficient to definitively demonstrate a significant and consistent improvement over existing methods. Addressing the questions raised regarding the theoretical conditions, chain correlation, and experimental setup, along with the inclusion of more extensive real data evaluations, would significantly strengthen this work.
