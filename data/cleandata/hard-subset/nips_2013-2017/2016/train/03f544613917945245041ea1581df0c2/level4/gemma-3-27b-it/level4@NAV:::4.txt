This paper presents a valuable improvement to Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) through the introduction of a Richardson-Romberg scheme, demonstrably enhancing convergence rates with respect to both bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE). A key strength is the simplicity of implementation, requiring only minor modifications to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. The method is supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. 

The proposed Romberg-Richardson scheme effectively accelerates the convergence of standard SG-MCMC. While the theoretical foundation appears sound, a point of clarification is needed regarding the derivation of the specific convergence rates from the presented theorem (as detailed in the “explain fatal flaws” section). 

Beyond this, a practical consideration arises from the computational cost of the method. Running the proposed approach on a single core would require approximately three times the computational time of standard SG-MCMC due to the use of two chains, with one utilizing double the number of steps.  Consequently, the time comparisons in Figure 5 may not be entirely representative; scaling the SGLD timings by a factor of 2/3 would likely reduce the observed performance gap between SGLD and SGRRLD. 

Regarding Figure 3, the bias plot suggests optimal convergence is achieved with α = 0.2, rather than the stated α = 1/3.  Furthermore, the optimal convergence curve appears considerably fluctuating, which may explain the discrepancy with the α = 0.2 rate; further investigation into the cause of this fluctuation would be beneficial. The explanation provided in line 256, concerning the dominance of the 1/Kγ term with small step sizes, raises a question regarding its absence in the behavior of SGLD – clarification here would be appreciated.

Finally, several minor points require attention: In Equation 1, the notation differs from the standard use of θ. The abbreviation “RR” in line 94 should be defined prior to its use.  Similarly, Γ<sub>K</sub> in line 131 requires definition.  In line 160, the type of smoothness assumed for the function *f* should be specified. Line 249 contains an apparent error: Figure 1(b) depicts Bias vs. time, while the text refers to it as a plot against dimension – this appears to be a reference to Figure 1(c). Lastly, for clarity, vectors should be represented using bold letters.
