

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel approach to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors leverage Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a numerical sequence acceleration technique from computational mathematics, to construct a simple yet effective method that runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes (one half the other) and correlates their Brownian increments. By combining these chains through RR extrapolation, the method achieves O(γ²) asymptotic bias compared to O(γ) for standard SG-MCMC methods like SGLD, resulting in improved convergence rates (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) vs. O(K⁻²/³) for finite-time MSE). The work builds on recent advances in SG-MCMC (e.g., Chen et al. 2015) that established the O(γ) bias for first-order methods, but extends them by showing how RR extrapolation can achieve the accuracy of higher-order integrators without their computational complexity.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical rigor, providing formal convergence analysis including central limit theorems and non-asymptotic bias/MSE bounds. The authors prove that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves superior convergence rates compared to SGLD and other first-order SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical results are well-supported by synthetic experiments on Gaussian models and real-world applications on large-scale matrix factorization for movie recommendation tasks. The empirical results consistently show that SGRRLD achieves significantly lower bias and MSE than SGLD with comparable computational cost, and importantly, the method maintains the inherent parallelizability of SG-MCMC algorithms.

The work's strengths are substantial. The method is conceptually simple, requiring minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC implementations, and the theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed. The paper makes a clear contribution to the SG-MCMC literature by demonstrating how a classical numerical technique can be applied to improve convergence properties without increasing computational complexity. The approach is general and applicable to various SG-MCMC algorithms (e.g., SGHMC), as demonstrated in the experiments. The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate technical depth for the NIPS audience.

However, several important questions remain. First, the authors should clarify the exact conditions under which RR extrapolation can be applied to SG-MCMC methods beyond those satisfying the specific bound in Section 2.2 (π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²)). Second, the paper does not address whether the method can be extended to non-SG-MCMC approximate MCMC schemes (e.g., subsampling-based Metropolis-Hastings) or distributed implementations. Third, while the authors note the method requires running one chain twice as long as the other, they don't explore whether a three-chain approach (one with step size γ, two with γ/2) could reduce computational waste without increasing variance. Fourth, the matrix factorization experiment would be strengthened by comparing to SGD (the non-Bayesian alternative) to demonstrate the practical benefits of Bayesian inference.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of practical implementation considerations. While the method is theoretically elegant, the authors could provide more concrete guidance on how to select the optimal step size schedule and mini-batch sizes for different applications. An algorithm box with pseudocode would significantly enhance accessibility for practitioners, as the current presentation requires readers to navigate through complex theoretical derivations to implement the method.

In terms of significance, this work has the potential to substantially impact the SG-MCMC community by providing a simple yet powerful technique to improve convergence rates without major algorithmic changes. The ability to achieve higher accuracy with the same computational resources makes this method particularly valuable for large-scale Bayesian inference problems. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical evidence supports the claims across multiple datasets.

For acceptance, I would highlight the following arguments:

PROS:
- Novel application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC, a technique not previously explored in this context
- Strong theoretical analysis with clear improvements in convergence rates
- Simple implementation that can be applied to various SG-MCMC methods
- Empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world problems
- Maintains the parallelizability of SG-MCMC algorithms

CONS:
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations
- Insufficient comparison to non-Bayesian baselines (e.g., SGD) in the matrix factorization experiment
- Unclear conditions for applicability to all SG-MCMC methods
- No exploration of alternative chain configurations to reduce computational waste

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of scalable Bayesian inference. While some implementation details and comparisons could be strengthened, the theoretical insights and empirical results are sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance. The method's simplicity and potential impact on the SG-MCMC community make it an important addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
