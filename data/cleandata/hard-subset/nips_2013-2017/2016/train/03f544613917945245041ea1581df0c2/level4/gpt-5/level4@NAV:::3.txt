Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods offer a convenient way to perform Bayesian posterior inference at scale and have attracted significant attention in recent years. A known limitation is their asymptotic bias, i.e., they do not converge exactly to the posterior. This paper demonstrates that Richardson–Romberg extrapolation can mitigate this bias. The core idea is straightforward: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, where Chain 2 uses half the step size of Chain 1 and is run for twice as many iterations. The injected Gaussian noises are coupled via alpha_k = (beta_{2k-1} + beta_{2k}) / sqrt(2), where alpha_k and beta_k denote the injected noise at step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To estimate the expectation of a test function f under the target distribution, one subtracts the estimator based on Chain 1 from the estimator based on Chain 2. The resulting estimator achieves a reduced bias of order O(gamma^2), compared to O(gamma) for either chain alone (gamma is the step size). A similar improvement in MSE is also established. I appreciate that the method is easy to grasp and implement (see my summary) and requires no substantial modifications to the underlying SG-MCMC algorithm. It appears broadly applicable across a wide class of SG-MCMC methods and could therefore be quite impactful for the SG-MCMC community.

Novelty: While Richardson–Romberg extrapolation is well known in numerical analysis, it is not widely recognized within the machine learning / stochastic gradient MCMC community.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well presented.

Comments/questions:
- Can this technique be applied directly to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, are there additional conditions beyond the requirement in Section 2.2 that pi_gamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)?
- Can this approach be used for other approximate (non-stochastic-gradient) MCMC schemes, such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests? What about distributed SGLD?
- Because one chain must run for twice as long, there is wasted compute while the chain with the larger step size is idle. Could one instead run three chains—one with step size gamma and two with step size gamma/2—for the same number of iterations? How would this affect the variance of the estimator?
- Is there a natural extension to more than two step sizes?
- The chains’ noise terms must be correlated, but the impact of correlating mini-batches was not entirely clear to me. Is the variance minimized when using the same mini-batch for the k-th iteration of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th iterations of Chain 2? Or is the influence of this correlation negligible?
- For the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5), I suggest running to convergence and including a comparison to SGD. The current plot shows your method outperforming SGLD, but it would be valuable to demonstrate superiority in a regime where Bayesian methods shine (i.e., also better than SGD). Since Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely adopted in practice, reporting final numbers would strengthen the case. Including an SGD curve as a control would also contextualize the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD.
- Although the method is simple and clearly explained, an algorithm box would make the paper more accessible. Many practitioners may skip the theory; providing pseudocode early on would help readers implement the method without delving into the more technical sections.
