- Proposes using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SG-MCMC: run two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, correlate noise, and combine estimates to reduce bias from O(γ) to O(γ^2) with theoretical bias and MSE guarantees.

- Strength: method is simple and easy to implement without major changes to SG-MCMC algorithms.

- Strength: broadly applicable to many SG-MCMC variants and potentially impactful for large-scale Bayesian inference.

- Strength: novel application of a classical numerical-analysis technique (Richardson-Romberg) to debias stochastic gradient methods.

- Strength: paper is clearly written and presents main ideas and theory lucidly.

- Weakness: computational inefficiency — γ/2 chain must run twice as long, leaving the γ chain idle for half the compute time; suggests exploring running three chains (one γ, two γ/2) to balance load and analyze variance implications.

- Weakness: experiments need strengthening — matrix factorization runs may not be at convergence; should demonstrate the advantage holds at convergence.

- Weakness: experiments lack a non-Bayesian baseline (e.g., SGD); comparisons against SGD would better contextualize benefits and motivate the Bayesian approach.

- Weakness: accessibility could be improved for practitioners by adding an algorithm box or pseudocode early in the paper.

- Question: can the technique be applied to all SG-MCMC methods, and are there extra conditions beyond the stated bias expansion pi_gamma(f)=pi(f)+C*gamma+O(gamma^2)?

- Question: can the method extend to higher-order extrapolation using more than two step sizes (e.g., achieve O(γ^3) bias), and what are the practical computation/variance trade-offs?

- Question: what is the role of mini-batch correlation — is using the same mini-batch across corresponding iterations optimal for variance reduction, or is its effect negligible relative to other variance sources?
