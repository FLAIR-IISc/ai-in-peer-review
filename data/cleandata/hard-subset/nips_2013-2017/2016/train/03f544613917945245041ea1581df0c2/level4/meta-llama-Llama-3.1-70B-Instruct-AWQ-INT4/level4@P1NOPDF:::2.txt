This paper applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce the bias introduced by gradient estimation noise. The resulting Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm has an asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD. The experiments conducted on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the advantage of the new algorithm over SGLD. However, the results on the MovieLens data show only a slight improvement in test root mean squared error (RMSE), and the findings are somewhat debatable.

The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates on stochastic differential equations (SDEs) has been established in the literature. This paper extends this technique to stochastic gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms and proves that, under certain conditions, the convergence rate of SGRRLD is faster than that of SGLD with the same step size. However, this improvement comes at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear and easy to implement.

The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD in reducing bias for the particular problem considered. Further real-data experiments would be valuable in showing that this simple modification to SGLD can significantly reduce bias. I have not verified the proof details of Theorem 1 and 2, but I am curious about the conditions required for U to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can U be multi-modal? Intuitively, when the gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains will become uncorrelated quickly, even if the Gaussian noises are correlated. I am interested in understanding how combining the two chains reduces the order of the error.

In the real-data experiments, did the authors observe any correlation between the two chains? I have concerns regarding the experimental setup and real-data experiments. The authors argue that the computation time is approximately the same as SGLD with parallelization. However, it is also possible to run SGLD with multiple parallel chains within the same time frame. If we compare the estimation from two chains of SGLD with SGRRLD, can we still observe the same improvement in RMSE? Furthermore, in the real-data experiment, the authors compare SGRRLD with a step size γ and γ/2 against SGLD with a step size γ. I believe it would be beneficial to also compare SGRRLD with SGLD using a step size γ/2, which has lower bias, and run two chains in parallel. Additional real-data experiments would be valuable in further evaluating the performance of SGRRLD.
