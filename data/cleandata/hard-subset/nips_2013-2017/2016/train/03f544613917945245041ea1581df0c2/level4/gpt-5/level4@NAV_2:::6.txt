This paper applies a numerical sequence acceleration technique—Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation—to improve the convergence of SG-MCMC. The method runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and introduces correlated Gaussian perturbations; the resulting estimator reduces bias while controlling variance. The authors establish both asymptotic and non-asymptotic guarantees, including consistency, a CLT, and bounds on bias and MSE, and they instantiate the approach on SGLD (SGRRLD). Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model corroborate the bias and MSE rates from the theory, and large-scale matrix factorization experiments demonstrate practical gains over standard SGLD. Overall, the technique and presentation are solid. The work makes a legitimate contribution to SG-MCMC and is positioned reasonably with respect to prior methods.

Two main concerns:
1) Novelty. The incremental nature of the advance should be discussed more explicitly. Prior work [10] analyzes the effect of higher-order integrators on SG-MCMC, and RR extrapolation has been used for approximating invariant measures in related contexts [23]. Clarifying how the proposed application of RR to SG-MCMC differs technically and conceptually from these threads (beyond the specific construction and analysis here) would strengthen the paper’s originality claim.

2) Practicality and fairness of the experimental comparison. The method is effectively a parallel implementation of SG-MCMC, and using correlated Brownian increments implies that the two chains must communicate at every iteration. The experiments appear to assume negligible communication overhead; this assumption and its implications should be stated and, if possible, quantified. It is also unclear how computational resources were allocated between SGRRLD and SGLD. Did the single SGLD chain receive the same aggregate resources that the two SGRRLD chains used? If not, the comparison may not be fair from an applied standpoint. If yes, please describe how resources were split between the two SGRRLD chains in practice. Since gradient evaluation dominates the cost in SG-MCMC, it is not obvious how dividing resources across two chains yields lower wall-clock time than assigning all resources to a single SGLD chain; more implementation detail would help reconcile this. Finally, a natural alternative baseline is to run two independent SGLD chains (no communication) in parallel and combine their estimates; it would be useful to include this comparison.
