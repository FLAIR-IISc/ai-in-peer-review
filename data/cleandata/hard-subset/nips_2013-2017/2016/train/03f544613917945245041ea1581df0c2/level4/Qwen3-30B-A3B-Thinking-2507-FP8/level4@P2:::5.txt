

This paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon the foundational work of Welling and Teh (2011) on SGLD and the numerical analysis work of Talay and Tubaro (1991) on RR extrapolation for SDEs. SGRRLD runs two SGLD chains with different step sizes in parallel (with step size ratio 2:1), then combines their outputs through RR extrapolation to achieve improved convergence rates.

The paper's main strengths include a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent, satisfies a central limit theorem, and achieves better non-asymptotic bounds for bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to SGLD. Specifically, the authors show that SGRRLD achieves a convergence rate of O(K^(-4/5)) for MSE, superior to SGLD's O(K^(-2/3)) rate. This theoretical improvement is well-supported by empirical results on both synthetic data (Gaussian models) and a large-scale matrix factorization task (MovieLens datasets), where SGRRLD consistently outperforms SGLD in accuracy within equivalent computational time.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a simple yet effective technique to reduce bias in SG-MCMC methods without requiring higher-order integrators, which are typically more complex to implement. The parallel nature of the approach is well-suited for distributed computing environments, making it practically appealing for large-scale applications. The authors also demonstrate the method's generality by applying it to SGHMC as well as SGLD.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, while the theoretical analysis claims SGRRLD achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods (as referenced in Chen et al., 2015), the empirical evaluation does not include a direct comparison with these methods. This omission weakens the argument that RR extrapolation can match higher-order integrator performance, which would have been a compelling validation of the authors' claim.

Second, there are inconsistent statements about step size sequences. The abstract claims the method "boils down to running almost the same SG-MCMC algorithm twice in parallel with different step sizes," while the main text describes more complex step size scheduling (e.g., η_{2k-1} = η_{2k} = γ_k/2). This inconsistency could confuse readers about the practical implementation of the method.

Third, Algorithm 1 in the supplementary document appears inconsistent with Equation (5) in the main text, which would require correction before publication. The supplementary algorithm seems to use a different step size scheduling than described in the main text.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though some technical details about the implementation of the correlated Brownian motions (Equation 8) could be clarified. The related work section adequately covers relevant NIPS papers from 2011-2015, demonstrating the novelty of applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC.

In terms of the four NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is strong, though the lack of comparison with higher-order integrators in experiments is a notable gap.
- Clarity: The paper is mostly clear, but the step size inconsistency needs resolution.
- Originality: The application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC is novel and well-motivated.
- Significance: The method offers a practical, parallelizable approach to reduce bias in SG-MCMC with significant empirical benefits for large-scale Bayesian inference.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and valuable contribution to the SG-MCMC literature. The method is simple yet effective, and the theoretical analysis is sound. With the minor inconsistencies addressed and the missing empirical comparison included, this would be a strong paper for NIPS. As it stands, the paper's main claim that RR extrapolation achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators is not fully validated by the experimental results, which weakens the significance of the contribution. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these issues.
