

This paper introduces a novel approach to improve the convergence rates of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods through the application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, specifically targeting the reduction of bias while maintaining reasonable variance. The proposed Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm extends existing SG-MCMC frameworks by running two parallel chains with different step sizes and applying RR extrapolation, which requires minimal modifications to standard implementations. The theoretical analysis is thorough, demonstrating asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and improved non-asymptotic bounds for bias and mean squared error compared to existing methods like SGLD.

The paper's strengths include a well-motivated theoretical contribution that achieves higher convergence rates (O(K^(-4/5)) for MSE versus O(K^(-2/3)) for SGLD), clear presentation of the algorithmic framework, and strong empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The method is particularly appealing due to its compatibility with parallel architectures, as the two chains can be run independently.

Several concerns warrant attention. First, the derivation of specific convergence rates in Theorem 2 requires more detailed explanation regarding how the bounds translate to the observed rates. Second, the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) appears to misrepresent the computational advantage: since SGRRLD requires two chains with one chain running twice as many steps, its wall-clock time should be approximately 2/3 that of SGLD for equivalent computational effort, which would reduce the apparent performance gap. Third, Figure 3 suggests the optimal convergence rate for bias is achieved at α=0.2 rather than the stated α=1/3, and the optimal curve exhibits significant fluctuations, potentially questioning the stability of the rate. Fourth, the text claims that for small step sizes the 1/(Kγ) term dominates, yet this effect does not appear to manifest in the SGLD results as expected. Minor issues include: Eq.1 uses a different symbol than θ, RR is not defined upon first use (line 94), Γ_K is not defined (line 131), the required smoothness for function f is unspecified (line 160), Figure 1(b) should be labeled as plotting bias vs. dimension rather than time (line 249), and vector notation should consistently use bold letters.
