Summary
The paper proposes to accelerate convergence in SG-MCMC by applying Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to higher-order numerical integrators. Concretely, it runs two SG-MCMC chains in parallel with different step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise, and then combines them to cancel leading-order discretization bias. The authors provide both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Experiments on a synthetic Gaussian model validate the bias and MSE bounds, and large-scale matrix factorization experiments on real data suggest practical gains over standard SGLD. The paper is clearly written and places the work in the context of related SG-MCMC methods.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing use of a classical variance/bias reduction tool (RR extrapolation) in the SG-MCMC setting.
- Theoretical results covering asymptotic and finite-time behavior, with empirical checks on toy problems.
- Empirical demonstration on a real matrix factorization task indicating potential practical benefits.
- Clear exposition and positioning relative to related work.

Main concerns
1) Novelty and positioning:
- The effect of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC has been analyzed previously (e.g., [10]), and RR extrapolation has been studied for approximating invariant measures (e.g., [23]). While the combination of these ideas in the specific SG-MCMC setting is interesting, the contribution may be viewed as incremental. Please sharpen the novelty claim: what new technical challenges arise specifically due to stochastic gradients and how are they addressed here beyond prior analyses? For instance, does the theory handle gradient noise and minibatching in a way not covered by [23]? Are there new coupling or bias-cancellation arguments that are essential in the stochastic-gradient regime?

2) Practicality and fairness of the experimental evaluation:
- The proposed method requires running two coupled chains with step-by-step communication to inject correlated Brownian noise. This imposes a synchronization/communication cost that appears to be ignored in the reported wall-clock or throughput comparisons. Please quantify and include this cost, or explicitly state that it is excluded and discuss implications.
- It is unclear how computing resources are allocated between RR-SG-MCMC (two chains) and baseline SGLD (one chain). A fair comparison should fix a budget—e.g., total wall-clock time including communication, total number of stochastic gradient evaluations, total FLOPs, or a fixed hardware budget—and report performance under that constraint. Currently, it is ambiguous whether the single SGLD chain uses the same aggregate resources as the two RR chains.
- If the RR method uses the same total hardware as SGLD but splits it across two chains, then (a) how are minibatch sizes and gradient computations split, (b) what is the per-step cost for each chain, and (c) why should this yield lower wall-clock time than dedicating all resources to a single SGLD chain? Please add implementation details that clarify these points.
- A strong additional baseline is to run two independent SGLD chains in embarrassingly parallel fashion (no communication) and average their estimates. This would isolate the value of RR extrapolation and noise correlation from the mere benefit of parallelism. Please include this comparison or discuss it.

Additional questions and suggestions
- Sensitivity: How sensitive is the method to the ratio of the two step sizes and to the strength of gradient noise (e.g., via minibatch size)? An ablation varying these would be informative.
- Robustness: Does the correlation structure of the injected noise have to be exact? How does performance degrade with imperfect or delayed coupling (e.g., due to asynchronous execution)?
- Metrics: In addition to bias/MSE of functionals, please consider reporting effective sample size per second (or per gradient evaluation) and autocorrelation metrics to quantify mixing improvements relative to cost.
- Scope: The theory and experiments appear focused on Langevin-type dynamics. Are there insights or caveats for applying RR extrapolation to momentum-based SG-MCMC (e.g., SGHMC, SGNHT)? Even a brief discussion would help scope the contribution.
- Implementation details: Please specify hardware, parallelization framework, communication mechanism for correlated noise, batch sizes, and the exact accounting used for wall-clock measurements.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well written. The explanation of how RR extrapolation cancels leading-order bias in the SG-MCMC discretization is clear, but a short intuitive diagram or pseudocode block summarizing the two-chain coupling and combination step would further aid readers.
- When presenting theory, explicitly state assumptions (smoothness, dissipativity, bounded variance of stochastic gradients, etc.) and highlight which ones differ from previous works such as [10] and [23].
- In the empirical section, make explicit whether reported gaps are statistically significant (e.g., mean ± std over multiple runs).

Minor comments
- “Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)” is introduced twice (around lines 25 and 70). Please remove the duplication.
- Fix typos and wording: “practical,” “resource,” “embarrassingly parallel,” “correlated Gaussian noise injections.”
- Consider harmonizing notation for the proposed method (e.g., RR-SGLD or RR-SG-MCMC) and use it consistently.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and clearly presented paper that brings a classical extrapolation technique to SG-MCMC, with sound theory and suggestive empirical gains. However, the novelty relative to prior analyses of integrator effects and RR extrapolation appears incremental, and the practical value is difficult to assess without a fair, resource-matched comparison that includes communication costs and stronger baselines (e.g., multiple independent SGLD chains). Addressing these concerns—by clarifying the unique technical contributions, detailing the implementation and resource allocation, and expanding the empirical comparisons under fixed budgets—would significantly strengthen the paper.
