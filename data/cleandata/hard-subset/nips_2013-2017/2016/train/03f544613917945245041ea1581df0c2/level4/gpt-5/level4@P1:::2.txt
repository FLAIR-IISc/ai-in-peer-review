Summary
The paper proposes Stochastic Gradient Richardson–Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which applies Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to SG-MCMC, with a focus on SGLD. The key idea is to run two SGLD chains in parallel with stepsizes γ and γ/2 over the same time horizon and combine their ergodic averages to cancel the leading-order discretization bias, while using correlated Gaussian increments to control variance. The paper provides asymptotic results (consistency and a CLT) and non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE, showing that SGRRLD achieves O(γ2) bias and O(γ4) variance (vs. O(γ) and O(γ2) for SGLD), and an optimal finite-time MSE rate O(K−4/5) with stepsizes decaying as k−1/5 (vs. O(K−2/3) for first-order SG-MCMC). This parallels classical RR for SDEs (e.g., Talay–Tubaro) and is related to higher-order SG-MCMC integrators (e.g., Chen et al., 2015), but requires only first-order discretization plus correlated noise. Experiments include a linear Gaussian model with known posterior, an SGHMC variant (SGRRHMC), and a large-scale matrix factorization task on MovieLens datasets.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable idea: RR extrapolation layered on top of existing SG-MCMC methods.
- Clear algorithmic description; easy to implement and naturally parallelizable.
- Solid theoretical development: consistency, CLT with variance-minimizing correlated increments, and finite-time bounds that improve on SGLD.
- Synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate reduced bias and improved MSE, and the SGHMC variant behaves as predicted.
- Real-data experiments show wall-clock advantages by exploiting parallelism.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions: Many conditions on U, f, and the gradient noise law L are deferred to the supplement. What precise conditions on U are needed for both Theorem 1 (CLT) and Theorem 2 (non-asymptotics)? Can U be multimodal (e.g., non-convex posteriors), and are the required Lyapunov/ergodicity conditions realistic in such cases?
- Correlation structure: The analysis leverages correlated Gaussian increments across chains, but the stochastic gradient estimators may be uncorrelated (or only weakly correlated) across chains in practice. Intuitively, if gradient noise is independent, the two chains may decorrelate quickly even if the Gaussian noise is correlated. How robust is the error reduction to weak/absent gradient correlation? Did the authors empirically measure inter-chain correlation (e.g., of states or test function values) in the real-data runs?
- Experimental design and fairness:
  - Wall-clock comparison: SGRRLD runs two chains in parallel and matches SGLD’s wall-clock time by parallelization. However, a fair baseline is also to run two independent SGLD chains in parallel and average their estimates. Does SGRRLD still outperform this “2×SGLD” baseline in RMSE and/or bias for the same wall-clock time?
  - Stepsize comparison: In MovieLens, SGRRLD uses γ and γ/2 while SGLD uses γ. A natural additional baseline is SGLD with stepsize γ/2 (lower bias), possibly with two chains in parallel to match time. How do the results compare then?
  - Real-data gains are modest (“slight improvement” in RMSE). More real datasets and tasks would strengthen the empirical case and test robustness.
- Practical guidance: The theory recommends γk ∝ k−1/5 for optimal MSE. Some discussion on tuning in practice (e.g., constant vs. decaying stepsizes, batch sizes, and when RR is most beneficial) would help practitioners.
- Minor presentation issues: There are scattered typos and formatting issues; consolidating the main assumptions in the main text (not only the supplement) would improve readability.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong; the approach is well-motivated by the RR literature, and the theoretical claims are plausible and carefully stated. I did not check all proof details, and I would like clarity on the exact conditions on U (including multimodality) under which Theorems 1–2 hold.
- Experimental methodology is generally sound for the synthetic studies; the real-data comparisons would benefit from the additional baselines noted above.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, and the algorithm is clearly presented. However, important assumptions are buried in the supplement; summarizing them (e.g., Lipschitz/gradient growth, dissipativity/ergodicity, smoothness of test functions, requirements on L) would improve clarity. A brief intuitive explanation of why correlated Brownian increments control variance in the RR estimator would also help readers.

Originality
- Applying RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC appears novel. Prior work improved bias via higher-order integrators; here, similar accuracy is achieved with first-order integrators plus a simple extrapolation scheme. The extension to other SG-MCMC (e.g., SGHMC) is promising.

Significance
- The method is simple, general, and compatible with parallel hardware. If the improved finite-time rates translate consistently to practice, this could be broadly useful. The current real-data improvements are modest; stronger empirical validation would elevate the impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- State the precise assumptions for Theorems 1–2 in the main text and discuss whether they allow multimodal U.
- Empirically assess and report inter-chain correlations and the sensitivity to using independent vs. shared minibatches across chains.
- Add baselines: (i) two parallel SGLD chains averaged (same wall-clock); (ii) SGLD with γ/2 (and two chains) vs. SGRRLD with γ and γ/2.
- Include additional real datasets and tasks to demonstrate robustness and practical gains.
- Provide practical guidance on stepsize schedules and minibatch choices when using SGRRLD.

Pros
- Simple, general technique with clear theory.
- Improved bias and MSE rates without higher-order integrators.
- Parallel-friendly; easy to implement.
- Convincing synthetic results; extension to SGHMC.

Cons
- Assumptions and applicability conditions not fully transparent in the main text.
- Real-data gains are modest; missing stronger baselines (2×SGLD, SGLD with γ/2).
- Limited empirical analysis of inter-chain correlation and robustness.

Overall
A solid and elegant contribution that bridges RR extrapolation and SG-MCMC, with strong theoretical backing and promising empirical results. With clearer assumptions and more comprehensive real-data comparisons, this paper would be a strong accept. As is, I lean positive but would like the experimental baselines and assumptions clarified.
