Stochastic Gradient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods have gained significant attention in recent years due to their ability to facilitate Bayesian posterior inference in large datasets. However, one major drawback of these methods is their asymptotic bias, meaning they do not converge exactly to the posterior distribution. This paper addresses this issue by proposing the use of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce the bias in SG-MCMC methods.

The core idea behind this technique is straightforward: two SG-MCMC chains are run in parallel, with Chain 2 having half the step size of Chain 1. Additionally, Chain 2 is run for twice the number of steps as Chain 1. The injected Gaussian noise in the chains is correlated, with αk = (β2k-1 + β2k)/√2, where αk and βk are the injected Gaussian noise at time step k in Chains 1 and 2, respectively. To compute the expectation of a function f with respect to the target distribution of the chains, one simply subtracts the estimate computed using samples from Chain 1 from the estimate computed using Chain 2. The resulting estimate is shown to have a lower bias of O(γ^2) compared to O(γ) obtained from Chain 1 or 2 alone (where γ is the step size). The authors also prove a similar reduction in the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimates.

I appreciate the simplicity and ease of implementation of this method, as it does not require significant changes to the base SG-MCMC algorithm. Furthermore, this technique appears to be highly generalizable and applicable to a broad class of SG-MCMC algorithms, making it a potentially impactful contribution to the Stochastic Gradient MCMC community.

Although Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis, it is not widely known in the machine learning and stochastic gradient MCMC communities, making this paper a valuable contribution in terms of novelty.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. However, I have several comments and questions that require further clarification:

* Can this technique be directly applied to all SG-MCMC methods? If not, are there specific conditions other than the bound in Section 2.2 (πγ(f) = π(f) + C \* γ + O(γ^2)) that need to be satisfied?
* Can this method be applied to other non-stochastic gradient, but approximate MCMC schemes, such as sub-sampling-based Metropolis-Hastings tests? What about Distributed SGLD?
* Since one chain has to run for twice as long as the other, there is a significant amount of wasted computation where the chain with the bigger step size remains idle. Is it possible to use three chains, one with a step size of γ and two with step sizes γ/2 each, and run all of them for the same number of steps? Does this increase the variance of the estimate?
* Is there an extension of this method to more than two step sizes?
* The injected noise in the chains must be correlated, but I did not fully understand the effect of correlation between mini-batches. Is the variance lowest when using the same batch for the kth iteration of Chain 1 and the 2k-1th and 2kth iteration of Chain 2? Or is the effect of this correlation negligible?
* I believe the matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should be run until convergence, and you should also compare it to SGD. The current plot shows that your method works better than SGLD, but it would be nice to demonstrate that your method works better than SGLD in a setting where being Bayesian is advantageous (i.e., better than SGD). Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice, so providing some numbers will convince more people to adopt your method. Additionally, having an SGD plot as a control will put the final errors of SGRRLD and SGLD in better perspective.
* Although the method is easy and clearly presented, an algorithm box could make the paper more accessible to a wider audience. I believe many practitioners will not read through the more theoretical parts, so pseudo-code presented early on in the paper will enable readers who are not very familiar with this area to try out your method without understanding the more challenging parts.
