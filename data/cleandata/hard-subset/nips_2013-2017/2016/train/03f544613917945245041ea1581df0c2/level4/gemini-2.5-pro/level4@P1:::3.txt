This paper addresses the problem of bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, which are widely used for Bayesian inference in large-scale settings. The authors propose to reduce this bias using a classical numerical analysis technique known as Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation. The core idea is to run two parallel SG-MCMC chains derived from the same underlying process but with different step sizes. Specifically, one chain uses a step size sequence (γk), while the second chain takes two steps of size γk/2 for every one step of the first chain, keeping the total time horizon synchronized. The Gaussian noise injected into the two chains is correlated to control the variance of the final estimator. The final estimate for an expectation is constructed by taking a specific linear combination of the estimates from the two chains. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, showing that their method, which they call Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), reduces the asymptotic bias from O(γ) to O(γ²), leading to a faster MSE convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵) compared to O(K⁻²/³) for standard SGLD. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both synthetic data and a large-scale matrix factorization task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed method is appealingly simple to understand and implement. It requires minimal modification to existing SG-MCMC algorithms and can be readily integrated into current workflows. The parallel nature of the two chains makes it well-suited for modern computing architectures.
*   **Generality and Impact:** The technique appears to be general and applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, as demonstrated by the experiment on SGHMC. This gives the work significant potential impact, as it offers a straightforward path to improving the accuracy of many existing methods in the SG-MCMC literature.
*   **Novelty:** While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a classic technique in numerical analysis, its application to the SG-MCMC framework is novel and introduces a promising new direction for bias reduction in this domain. The community has focused on higher-order integrators, and this work provides a compelling alternative.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the core concepts are presented clearly. The theoretical results are well-supported and the connection to prior work in SDEs is well-established.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The authors state that the technique can be applied to other SG-MCMC methods. It would be beneficial to clarify the precise conditions an algorithm must satisfy beyond the existence of a weak error expansion of the form πγ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ²). For instance, could this approach be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes, such as those employing Metropolis-Hastings corrections with subsampled data, or to distributed SGLD variants?

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed parallel scheme involves an imbalance in computation: the chain with the larger step size is idle for half of the updates. Have the authors considered alternative schemes to better utilize computational resources? For instance, could one run three chains in parallel for the same duration—one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2—and combine their outputs? How would such a scheme affect the variance of the final estimator?

3.  **Multi-level Extensions:** The paper briefly mentions that the RR extrapolation can be applied recursively. A brief discussion on the practical trade-offs of using more than two step sizes (e.g., a multi-level approach) would be an insightful addition.

4.  **Correlation of Gradient Estimators:** The paper thoroughly analyzes the effect of correlating the injected Gaussian noise. However, the impact of correlating the stochastic gradient estimates is less clear. The authors state that the mini-batch sizes must be the same, but is there an optimal strategy for sampling the mini-batches? For example, would using the same mini-batch for the k-th step of Chain 1 and the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th steps of Chain 2 further reduce variance, or is this effect negligible?

5.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The matrix factorization experiments are promising but could be strengthened. To provide a more complete picture, the experiments should be run until the methods appear to converge. Furthermore, including a comparison against a standard non-Bayesian baseline like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) would be valuable. This would help contextualize the performance of both SGLD and SGRRLD and better demonstrate the practical advantages of the Bayesian approach in a setting where it is not always the default choice.

6.  **Presentation:** For accessibility, particularly for practitioners who may not delve into the theoretical sections, including a pseudo-code algorithm box early in the paper would be a significant improvement. This would make it much easier for a broader audience to adopt and experiment with the proposed method.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a simple, effective, and broadly applicable technique for improving the accuracy of SG-MCMC methods. The theoretical contributions are solid, and the experimental results are convincing. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already excellent submission.
