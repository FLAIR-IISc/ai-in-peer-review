### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), which aims to reduce the bias inherent in first-order methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a classical numerical analysis technique, to the SGLD algorithm. This involves running two SGLD chains in parallel with different step sizes (γ and γ/2) and combining their outputs to cancel the leading-order error term. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, showing that SGRRLD is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they demonstrate that SGRRLD achieves a higher rate of convergence for the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), compared to SGLD's O(K⁻²/³). This allows SGRRLD to attain the accuracy of methods using higher-order integrators while only requiring a first-order scheme. The method is validated on a synthetic Gaussian model and a large-scale matrix factorization task using MovieLens datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound and rigorously extends existing results on RR extrapolation for SDEs to the more challenging SG-MCMC setting. The derived non-asymptotic bounds and the resulting optimal convergence rates are a strong contribution. The experiments on the synthetic linear Gaussian model are well-designed and clearly illustrate the theoretical claims, showing a significant reduction in bias and MSE compared to SGLD.

However, the quality of the experimental validation on real-world data is a point of weakness. The performance gains on the MovieLens datasets are quite modest, and the experimental setup does not include comparisons against the most appropriate baselines, which undermines the claims of practical superiority (see Detailed Comments).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for using RR extrapolation is well-explained, and the proposed SGRRLD algorithm is described precisely. The theoretical results are stated clearly, and the high-level sketch of the proofs is helpful for understanding the main arguments.

**Originality:** While RR extrapolation is a well-established technique in numerical analysis for SDEs (as acknowledged by the authors, citing Talay and Tubaro [21] and Lemaire et al. [23]), its application to the SG-MCMC framework is novel. This work is the first to formally analyze the combination of RR extrapolation with stochastic gradient noise. The idea of using a simple, parallelizable scheme to achieve higher-order accuracy is an original and valuable contribution to the scalable MCMC literature.

**Significance:** The work is potentially highly significant. It offers practitioners a simple and "drop-in" modification to SGLD that promises improved accuracy without the implementation complexity of higher-order integrators. Since SGRRLD is inherently parallelizable, it is well-suited for modern computing architectures. However, the current experiments do not fully demonstrate this significance in practice. If the method can be shown to robustly outperform fairly-matched parallel baselines on a wider range of real-world problems, its impact could be substantial.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

My main concerns relate to the experimental evaluation and the practical implications of the theoretical results.

1.  **Unfair Experimental Comparison:** The central argument for SGRRLD's efficiency is that its two chains can be run in parallel, resulting in a wall-clock time comparable to a single SGLD chain. However, this comparison is incomplete. A fair comparison should consider what could be achieved with the same computational budget using the baseline method.
    *   Could you please compare SGRRLD against an ensemble of two independent SGLD chains run in parallel, where the final estimate is the average of the two? This is a natural alternative for utilizing two cores and represents a much stronger baseline.
    *   SGRRLD combines chains with step sizes γ and γ/2, while the SGLD baseline uses only γ. It is well-known that a smaller step size reduces bias in SGLD. A crucial baseline is missing: SGLD running with step size γ/2. The fairest comparison would be SGRRLD against two parallel SGLD chains, both running with step size γ/2. This would match both the computational budget and the smallest step size used.

2.  **Marginal Gains on Real Data:** The RMSE improvements on the MovieLens datasets are quite small (e.g., from ~0.845 to ~0.840 on ML-1M). While statistically significant, this raises the question of whether the asymptotic advantages translate into meaningful practical gains in finite-time, real-world applications. More experiments on diverse, large-scale models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, topic models) would be necessary to make a stronger case for the practical utility of SGRRLD.

3.  **Assumptions for Theoretical Results:** The theorems rely on certain technical conditions on the potential function `U`, the test function `f`, and the gradient noise distribution `L`. Could you briefly comment on the scope of these assumptions? For instance, do the assumptions on `U` permit multi-modal or non-convex posteriors, which are common in machine learning applications like the matrix factorization model used in the experiments?

4.  **Intuition on Error Cancellation with Stochastic Gradients:** Could you provide more intuition on how the error cancellation from RR extrapolation remains effective in the presence of stochastic gradient noise? The two chains share correlated Brownian motion, but they are driven by independent sequences of mini-batch gradients. One might expect this independent noise to quickly decorrelate the two chains, potentially negating the benefits of the extrapolation. Is the correlation induced by the shared Brownian motion strong enough to keep the chains sufficiently coupled for the bias cancellation to work effectively? Did you empirically measure the correlation between the two chains in your experiments?

**Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and elegant application of a classical numerical method (RR extrapolation) to the important problem of bias reduction in SG-MCMC.
*   **Theoretical Strength:** Provides a solid theoretical foundation with improved asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence rates.
*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed method is simple to implement and naturally parallelizable, making it an attractive alternative to complex higher-order integrators.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Weak Experimental Validation:** The experiments on real-world data show only marginal improvements and, more importantly, lack comparisons to fairer and stronger parallel baselines.
*   **Uncertain Practical Significance:** Due to the weak experimental results, the practical impact of the proposed method remains unclear.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting idea. The connection between Richardson-Romberg extrapolation and SG-MCMC is novel and elegant. However, the current experimental evaluation is not sufficient to fully support the claims of practical superiority over SGLD. The lack of fair baselines is a significant flaw. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can provide results against the stronger parallel baselines I have suggested.
