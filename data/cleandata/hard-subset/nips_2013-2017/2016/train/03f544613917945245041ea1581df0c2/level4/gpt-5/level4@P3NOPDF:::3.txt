Summary
This paper proposes using Richardson–Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the asymptotic bias of Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods. The idea is straightforward and practical: run two SG-MCMC chains in parallel, with Chain 2 using half the step size of Chain 1 and twice as many steps. The injected Gaussian noises are correlated so that, for time index k, the noise in Chain 1 satisfies alpha_k = (beta_{2k-1} + beta_{2k}) / sqrt(2), where beta_t are the Gaussian noises used by Chain 2. The estimator of E_pi[f] is formed by subtracting the estimate from Chain 1 from that of Chain 2, yielding an O(gamma^2) bias compared to the O(gamma) bias of either chain alone (gamma is the step size). The paper also proves a corresponding improvement in MSE. The technique is simple to implement, requires no major changes to base SG-MCMC algorithms, and appears broadly applicable.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and easy to implement; the paper’s construction is clear and practically motivated.
- General and potentially applicable to a wide range of SG-MCMC methods.
- Theoretical results are clean and intuitive, showing a reduction in both bias and MSE.
- Likely to be impactful within the SG-MCMC community, where asymptotic bias is a long-standing concern.

Novelty
While RR extrapolation is classical in numerical analysis, it is not widely used in machine learning or SG-MCMC. Applying it in this context, with a careful coupling of injected noise and an analysis of bias/MSE, is novel and valuable.

Clarity
The paper is well written and easy to follow. The theoretical assumptions and bounds are clearly stated, and the proposed estimator is presented in a way that highlights its practicality.

Questions and suggestions
- Scope of applicability. Can the method be directly applied to all SG-MCMC variants (e.g., SGLD, SGHMC, SGNHT), or are there necessary conditions beyond the bound in Section 2.2, namely pi_gamma(f) = pi(f) + C * gamma + O(gamma^2)? It would help to list explicit conditions on the generator/discretization under which the O(gamma) expansion holds and the RR cancellation is valid.
- Beyond SG-MCMC. Can RR extrapolation be applied to other approximate MCMC schemes such as subsampling-based Metropolis–Hastings tests, stochastic pseudo-marginal methods, or control variate MCMC? Are there obstacles due to discontinuities from accept/reject steps?
- Distributed or asynchronous settings. Is the method compatible with distributed SGLD or asynchronous parallel schemes? How sensitive is the bias/MSE improvement to communication delays or stale gradients when correlating the noise across chains?
- Computational efficiency. Because Chain 2 runs for twice as long, Chain 1 is idle for half the wall-clock time if run on a single device. An alternative is to run three chains for the same number of steps: one with step size gamma and two with step size gamma/2. One could then average the two gamma/2 estimates before applying RR against the gamma chain. Would this preserve the O(gamma^2) bias while reducing variance, and how would its MSE compare at fixed compute budget?
- Extensions to more than two step sizes. Can the approach be extended to higher-order RR using multiple step sizes (e.g., gamma, gamma/2, gamma/4) to achieve O(gamma^p) bias for p > 2? What coupling of injected noise and mini-batch gradients would be required to control variance?
- Correlating mini-batches. The paper specifies how to correlate Gaussian noise but is less clear on the role of mini-batch correlation. Is the variance minimized when the k-th mini-batch of Chain 1 is used for both the (2k−1)-th and 2k-th steps of Chain 2 (mirroring the noise coupling), or is this effect negligible relative to noise correlation? A brief analysis or ablation would be helpful.
- Experimental evaluation. The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) should be run to convergence and include an SGD baseline. The current plot shows improvement over SGLD, but demonstrating that the RR method yields better performance than SGD in a regime where Bayesian modeling adds value would strengthen the case for practical impact. Since Bayesian matrix factorization is not widely used in practice, reporting final errors and including SGD as a control would provide clearer perspective on the benefit of reduced bias.
- Implementation details. Although the method is simple, an algorithm box would improve accessibility. Many practitioners may not read the theoretical sections closely; early pseudo-code specifying (i) step sizes and iteration counts, (ii) noise coupling alpha_k = (beta_{2k-1} + beta_{2k}) / sqrt(2), and (iii) any recommended coupling of mini-batches, would lower the barrier to adoption.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed paper that adapts a classical technique in a novel and useful way for SG-MCMC. The method’s simplicity, generality, and theoretical guarantees make it attractive. Addressing the questions above—especially clarifying applicability conditions, the role of mini-batch correlation, and strengthening the experimental evaluation—would further enhance the paper’s impact and practical relevance.
