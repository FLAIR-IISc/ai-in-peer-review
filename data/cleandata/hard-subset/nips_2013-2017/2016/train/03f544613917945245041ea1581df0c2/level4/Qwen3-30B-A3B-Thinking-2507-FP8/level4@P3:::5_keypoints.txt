- Proposal: Introduces SGRRLD, which applies Richardson–Romberg extrapolation to SGLD by running two chains with step sizes γ and γ/2 and combining outputs to reduce bias.  
- Theoretical claim: Shows SGRRLD attains MSE O(K^-4/5) vs SGLD's O(K^-2/3), matching higher-order integrator accuracy without extra complexity.  
- Parallelizability: Method is embarrassingly parallel (two independent chains), aligning well with distributed SG-MCMC.  
- Novelty: First formal analysis of RR extrapolation in the stochastic-gradient setting for SG-MCMC.  
- Theoretical contributions: Provides a central limit theorem and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds.  
- Empirical results: Experiments on synthetic data and large-scale movie recommendation demonstrate consistent accuracy improvements over SGLD at similar runtime.  
- Generality: Claims applicability to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD.  
- Missing comparisons: No direct experimental comparison to higher-order integrator methods (e.g., Chen et al., 2015), weakening empirical claims.  
- Step-size inconsistency: Text description of step sizes (η2k-1 = η2k = γk/2) conflicts with Equation (5) and algorithm statements.  
- Algorithm mismatch: Supplementary Algorithm 1 does not correspond to Equation (5), causing implementation confusion.  
- Presentation gap: Connection to multi-level Monte Carlo is mentioned but not developed.  
- Related work clarity: Contribution could be more clearly distinguished from other bias-reduction approaches like higher-order integrators and control variates.  
- Quality assessment: Strong theoretical analysis but empirical validation is incomplete due to missing comparisons.  
- Clarity assessment: Main ideas clear but reduced by step-size and algorithm inconsistencies.  
- Originality assessment: Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC, though conceptually a natural extension.  
- Significance assessment: Provides practical convergence improvements with minimal overhead, valuable for large-scale Bayesian inference.  
- Pro-acceptance: Theoretically sound, practically useful, and well-suited to distributed computing.  
- Con-acceptance: Needs experimental comparison to high-order integrators and fixes for the step-size/algorithm inconsistencies; multi-level MC connection underdeveloped.  
- Overall recommendation: Promising and technically sound but should address minor inconsistencies and add comparisons before acceptance.
