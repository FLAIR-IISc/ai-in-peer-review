This paper applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics to reduce the bias introduced by gradient estimation noise. The resulting SGRRLD algorithm achieves asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to SGLD. Experiments with a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the advantages of the new algorithm. Experiments on the MovieLens datasets show a marginal improvement in test RMSE, though the significance of this improvement could be further investigated. The use of RR extrapolation to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates for stochastic differential equations (SDEs) has been established in prior work. This paper effectively applies this technique to stochastic gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms, proving that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD converges faster than SGLD with the same step size, at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear and relatively straightforward to implement. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the bias reduction achieved by SGRRLD in that specific setting. Further real-data experiments would strengthen the evidence for the general applicability and significant bias reduction offered by this method. 

I haven’t meticulously verified the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2, but I am curious about the specific conditions required for the potential energy function *U* to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can *U* be multi-modal? Intuitively, if the gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains may quickly become uncorrelated despite the correlation of the Gaussian noise increments. Clarification on how the combination of these chains leads to the observed reduction in error order would be helpful. In the real data experiments, did the authors observe any correlation between the two chains generated by SGRRLD? 

My primary concern regarding the experimental setup and the real data experiment is that while the authors claim comparable computation time to SGLD due to parallelization, one could also run multiple parallel chains of SGLD within the same timeframe. If the estimation from two SGLD chains were compared to SGRRLD, would the same improvements in RMSE be observed? Furthermore, the comparison in the real data experiment uses SGRRLD with step sizes γ and γ/2, while comparing it to SGLD with a step size of γ. A more informative comparison would include SGLD with a step size of γ/2, run with two parallel chains to maintain a comparable computational cost, as this would reduce the bias of SGLD and provide a fairer baseline. Finally, including more real-data experiments would bolster the conclusions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in scaling Bayesian inference: reducing bias in SG-MCMC.
*   The proposed SGRRLD algorithm is theoretically sound, with proven convergence guarantees.
*   The synthetic experiments effectively demonstrate the bias reduction achieved by SGRRLD.
*   The algorithm is relatively easy to implement and can leverage parallel architectures.
*   The paper builds on existing work in numerical analysis (RR extrapolation) and applies it to a relevant problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The real data experiments offer only modest improvements, raising questions about the practical impact of the method.
*   The experimental comparison to SGLD could be improved by considering parallel SGLD chains as a baseline.
*   The theoretical conditions for *U* in Theorems 1 & 2 could be clarified.
*   Further investigation is needed to understand the correlation between the two chains in SGRRLD, especially in non-synthetic settings.
