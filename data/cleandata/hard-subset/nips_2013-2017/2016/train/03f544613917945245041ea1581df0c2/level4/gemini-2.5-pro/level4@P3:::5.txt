### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithm, named Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), designed to reduce the bias inherent in existing methods like Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD). The core idea is to apply Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a well-established numerical analysis technique for improving the convergence of sequences, to the SGLD algorithm. In practice, this involves running two parallel SGLD chains: one with a step size sequence {γₖ} and another with a step size of γₖ/2 for each corresponding step, effectively running for the same total time. The outputs of these two chains are then combined to form a new estimator with reduced bias. To control the variance of this combined estimator, the authors propose using correlated Brownian motion for the two chains.

The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of SGRRLD. They prove that the method is asymptotically consistent and satisfies a central limit theorem. Crucially, they derive non-asymptotic bounds for the bias and Mean Squared Error (MSE), demonstrating that SGRRLD achieves a faster rate of convergence (O(K⁻⁴/⁵) for MSE) than standard SGLD (O(K⁻²/³)). This improved rate matches the theoretical accuracy of more complex methods that rely on higher-order integrators. The paper supports these theoretical claims with experiments on both synthetic Gaussian models and a large-scale matrix factorization task for movie recommendations, showing significant empirical improvements over SGLD.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing proofs for asymptotic consistency, a central limit theorem, and, most importantly, non-asymptotic bounds on bias and MSE that clearly quantify the advantage of the proposed method. The claims are well-supported by this analysis. The experimental validation is also strong; the synthetic experiments are well-designed to specifically verify the theoretical claims regarding bias, MSE, and convergence rates as a function of the step size. The application to a large-scale real-world problem demonstrates the practical utility of the method. The authors are also careful to show the generality of their approach by applying it to SGHMC.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the background on SGLD and RR extrapolation provides the necessary context. The derivation of the SGRRLD algorithm from these two components is straightforward and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

However, there are a couple of minor points that could be clarified:
1.  There appears to be a small inconsistency between the update rule for the second chain as described in Equation (5) and its implementation in Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material. In Eq. (5), the gradient `∇Ũ` is evaluated at `θ(γ/2)₂ₖ₊₁` for the second step, whereas the pseudocode seems to suggest it is evaluated at `θ(γ/2)₂ₖ`. Clarifying this would improve the reproducibility of the work.
2.  The discussion on choosing the step size schedule (constant vs. decreasing) and the optimal constants (γ*b, γ*M) could be slightly more unified to provide clearer practical guidance to a user of the method.

**Originality:** The core components of the method—SG-MCMC and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation—are not new in themselves. SGLD is a foundational algorithm in the field, and RR extrapolation is a standard technique in numerical analysis for SDEs. However, the application of RR extrapolation to the *stochastic gradient* MCMC setting is novel and insightful. The authors are the first to propose this combination, analyze it rigorously in the context of SG-MCMC, and demonstrate its practical effectiveness. This represents a clever and valuable contribution that builds upon existing work in a non-trivial way.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. Bias is a well-known limiting factor for first-order SG-MCMC methods. This paper presents a simple, practical, and theoretically sound method to significantly reduce this bias. The key advantages are:
1.  **Improved Accuracy:** It achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrators using only a first-order integrator (SGLD), which is simpler to implement.
2.  **Parallelizability:** The method is inherently parallel, as the two chains can be run independently without any communication. This makes it extremely well-suited for modern multi-core architectures and distributed systems, which is a major practical advantage over sequential algorithms.
3.  **Generality:** The framework is generic and can be applied to other SG-MCMC algorithms beyond SGLD, as demonstrated by the SGRRHMC experiment.

The presented results, especially the wall-clock time improvements on the large-scale matrix factorization task, suggest that researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt this technique to accelerate and improve their large-scale Bayesian inference pipelines.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Novel and Effective Idea:** The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to SG-MCMC is a novel contribution that effectively addresses the important problem of bias in these methods.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, including a CLT and non-asymptotic bounds that prove a faster convergence rate than standard SGLD.
*   **Excellent Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the theoretical claims on synthetic data and show significant performance gains in terms of accuracy and wall-clock time on a challenging, large-scale real-world problem.
*   **Practicality and Simplicity:** The method is easy to implement and inherently parallel, making it highly practical and appealing for real-world applications. It achieves the benefits of higher-order methods without their implementation complexity.

### **Arguments Against Acceptance**
*   The paper has minor clarity issues regarding the implementation details in the algorithm pseudocode versus the main text, which should be addressed. However, this is a minor point that can be easily fixed.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, simple, and effective method for improving SG-MCMC. The strong theoretical and empirical results make a compelling case for its utility. I strongly recommend acceptance.
