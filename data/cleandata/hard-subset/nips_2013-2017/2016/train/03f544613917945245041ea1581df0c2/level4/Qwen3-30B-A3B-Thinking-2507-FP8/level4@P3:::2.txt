

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel sampling algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to reduce the bias of Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) while maintaining reasonable variance. The authors build upon previous work in SG-MCMC, particularly the foundational SGLD framework by Welling and Teh (2011) and the bias analysis of SG-MCMC methods by Chen et al. (2015). They extend Talay and Tubaro's (1991) RR extrapolation technique, which has been used to improve Monte Carlo estimates for SDEs, to the stochastic gradient setting. This represents a meaningful extension of existing numerical methods to the SG-MCMC domain, where such techniques have not been previously explored.

The paper demonstrates significant theoretical advances, proving that SGRRLD achieves asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively, compared to SGLD's O(γ) and O(γ²) bounds. Crucially, the authors show that SGRRLD attains a mean squared error convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), substantially faster than SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. These theoretical results are well-supported by rigorous analysis, including a central limit theorem for the estimator and non-asymptotic bias and MSE bounds. The paper also provides a clear algorithm description with precise implementation details, making it straightforward to implement.

The experimental validation is comprehensive, with compelling results on synthetic data demonstrating SGRRLD's ability to significantly reduce bias in Gaussian models. The results align well with the theoretical predictions, showing approximately 100x reduction in variance estimation bias. The real-world application on MovieLens datasets provides additional evidence of the method's practical utility, with SGRRLD achieving comparable or better RMSE performance than SGLD in roughly half the computational time. The authors effectively leverage the parallel nature of SGRRLD, running two chains in parallel with minimal overhead.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contribution is strong, with clear mathematical justification for the proposed method. The algorithm is well-motivated, and the connection to established numerical techniques (RR extrapolation) makes the approach both elegant and principled. The experimental design is thoughtful, with appropriate synthetic and real-world validation. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility. The work advances the state of the art in SG-MCMC by demonstrating that a first-order integrator can achieve the accuracy of higher-order methods through a simple modification, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that warrant consideration. First, the real data experiments would be more convincing if the authors compared SGRRLD against SGLD with a smaller step size (γ/2), which would have lower bias than the standard SGLD implementation but would require twice as many iterations. The current comparison against SGLD with step size γ is less informative since the bias of SGLD with step size γ/2 would naturally be smaller. Second, the paper does not address whether the correlation between the two parallel chains is maintained in practice, which is critical for the theoretical variance reduction to hold. The authors should provide evidence of this correlation in their experiments.

The paper also lacks sufficient detail about the conditions required for the potential function U. While the authors reference technical conditions in the supplement, it would be helpful to briefly state the key assumptions (e.g., smoothness, growth conditions) that enable their theoretical results, particularly regarding whether the method works for multimodal distributions. The claim that the method "can be virtually applied to all current SG-MCMC algorithms" requires stronger justification, especially for algorithms with different convergence properties.

Regarding significance, while the paper demonstrates a clear theoretical advantage with minimal computational overhead, the practical benefits might be marginal in some applications. The authors should more explicitly discuss scenarios where the bias reduction would be most valuable, such as when precise posterior estimation is critical.

Strengths:
- Novel application of RR extrapolation to SG-MCMC
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear convergence rate improvements
- Well-structured algorithm description and implementation details
- Comprehensive synthetic and real-world experimental validation
- Effective use of parallelism for computational efficiency

Weaknesses:
- Inadequate comparison with SGLD using step size γ/2 in real data experiments
- Insufficient discussion of practical correlation between parallel chains
- Limited detail about required conditions for U (e.g., multimodality)
- Overstated generality of the method across all SG-MCMC algorithms
- Limited discussion of when the bias reduction would be most valuable

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of SG-MCMC with a principled approach to bias reduction. The method is elegant, well-supported by theory, and demonstrates practical utility. While the real data experiments could be strengthened with more thoughtful comparisons, the core contribution is significant and well-executed. The paper meets all quality criteria and makes a meaningful advancement over previous work, particularly in demonstrating that first-order methods can achieve higher-order accuracy through RR extrapolation. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors might address the experimental comparisons in the final version.
