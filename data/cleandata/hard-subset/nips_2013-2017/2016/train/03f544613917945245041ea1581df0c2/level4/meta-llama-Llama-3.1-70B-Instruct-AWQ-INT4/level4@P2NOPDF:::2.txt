This paper proposes the application of Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to mitigate the bias introduced by gradient estimation noise. The resulting algorithm, SGRRLD, exhibits asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) compared to SGLD. Experimental results on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD over SGLD. However, the experiments on the MovieLens data show only a marginal improvement in test RMSE, and the results are somewhat inconclusive.

The application of RR extrapolation to stochastic gradient Markov chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms is a novel contribution, building upon existing literature that has shown the technique to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates on stochastic differential equations (SDEs). The authors prove that, under certain conditions, SGRRLD converges at a faster rate than SGLD with the same step size, albeit at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear and easy to implement.

The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate the bias reduction capabilities of SGRRLD in a specific problem setting. However, it would be beneficial to include more real-data experiments to further substantiate the claim that a simple modification to SGLD can significantly reduce bias.

While I have not thoroughly examined the proof details of Theorem 1 and 2, I am curious about the conditions required for the potential function U to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can U be multi-modal? Intuitively, when gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains may become uncorrelated quickly, even if the Gaussian noises are correlated. It is unclear how combining the two chains reduces the order of the error. Did the authors observe any correlation between the two chains in the real-data experiments?

I have concerns regarding the experimental setup and real-data experiments. The authors argue that the computational time is comparable to that of SGLD with parallelization. However, it is also possible to run SGLD with multiple parallel chains within the same time frame. If we compare the estimates from two chains of SGLD with those of SGRRLD, can we still observe the same improvement in RMSE? Furthermore, in the real-data experiment, SGRRLD with a step size γ and γ/2 is compared to SGLD with a step size γ. It would be more comprehensive to also compare SGRRLD with SGLD using a step size γ/2, which has a lower bias, and running two chains in parallel. Additional real-data experiments would be beneficial to further validate the proposed algorithm.
