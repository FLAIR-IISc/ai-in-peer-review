

This paper introduces Stochastic Gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin Dynamics (SGRRLD), a novel algorithm that applies Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce bias in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods while maintaining reasonable variance. The approach runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with different step sizes and correlated Brownian motions, achieving asymptotic bias and variance bounds of O(γ²) and O(γ⁴), respectively. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that SGRRLD achieves a mean squared error convergence rate of O(K⁻⁴/⁵), outperforming SGLD's O(K⁻²/³) rate. The authors validate their theoretical claims through experiments on synthetic Gaussian models and real-world matrix factorization tasks, showing consistent improvements in accuracy and computational efficiency.

The paper is technically sound (Quality), with comprehensive theoretical analysis that builds upon prior work in SG-MCMC [10] and Richardson-Romberg extrapolation for SDEs [23]. The presentation is clear and well-organized (Clarity), with precise mathematical formulations and effective experimental design. The approach demonstrates significant practical significance (Significance), as it achieves the accuracy of higher-order integrator methods while using first-order integrators, and the parallelizable nature of SGRRLD makes it suitable for distributed computing environments.

Regarding originality (Originality), while Richardson-Romberg extrapolation has been previously applied to SDEs [23], its application to SG-MCMC represents a novel contribution to the field. The paper effectively contextualizes its work against prior SGMCMC methods [1-12] and establishes clear distinctions from previous approaches.

The main concerns involve implementation details for practical application. The parallel nature of SGRRLD requires correlated Brownian motions, which necessitates communication between chains at each step. The authors do not address the communication costs or resource allocation implications. Specifically, it's unclear how the computational resources for SGRRLD (two chains) are compared to SGLD (one chain) in the experiments. If the two chains of SGRRLD share the same computational resources as a single SGLD chain, the resource allocation strategy for splitting between the two chains is not explained. If the two chains use separate resources, the comparison becomes unfair for practical applications. Additionally, it would be valuable to compare against an embarrassingly parallel implementation of two independent SGLD chains (without communication), which would provide a more complete understanding of the practical trade-offs.

The paper is well-written with a strong theoretical foundation, but the practical implementation considerations should be addressed to fully establish the method's real-world utility. The minor repetition of "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" in lines 25 and 70 should be corrected.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution with formal bounds, clear experimental validation, practical applicability to distributed computing, and significant improvement over existing SGMCMC methods.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations and resource allocation, which could affect the perceived utility of the method in real-world settings.
