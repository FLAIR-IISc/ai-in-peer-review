This paper applies the Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation to stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) to reduce the bias introduced by gradient estimation noise. The resulting stochastic gradient Richardson-Romberg Langevin dynamics (SGRRLD) algorithm has an asymptotically lower bias and mean squared error (MSE) than SGLD. The experimental results on a synthetic Linear Gaussian Model demonstrate the advantage of the new algorithm over SGLD. However, the experiments on the MovieLens data show only a slight improvement in test RMSE, and the results are arguable.

The application of Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to improve the convergence rate of Monte Carlo estimates on stochastic differential equations (SDEs) has been established in the literature. This paper extends this technique to stochastic gradient Markov chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) algorithms and proves that, under certain conditions, the convergence rate of SGRRLD is faster than that of SGLD with the same step size, albeit at the cost of running two parallel Markov chains. The algorithm description is clear, and the proposed algorithm is relatively easy to implement. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the advantage of SGRRLD in reducing bias for the particular problem considered.

However, it would be beneficial to see more real-data experiments to demonstrate that a simple modification to SGLD can significantly reduce bias. I have not verified the proof details of Theorem 1 and 2, but I am curious about the conditions required for U to satisfy both theorems. Specifically, can U be multi-modal? Intuitively, when the gradient noises are uncorrelated, the two chains will become uncorrelated quickly, even if the Gaussian noises are correlated. It is unclear how combining the two chains reduces the order of the error. Did the authors observe any correlation between the two chains in the real-data experiments?

I have some concerns regarding the experimental setup and the real-data experiment. The authors argue that the computational time is approximately the same as SGLD with parallelization. However, it is also possible to run SGLD with multiple parallel chains within the same time frame. If we compare the estimates from two chains of SGLD with those of SGRRLD, can we still observe the same improvement in RMSE? Furthermore, in the real-data experiment, the authors compare SGRRLD with a step size γ and γ/2 against SGLD with a step size γ. I believe it would be more informative to also compare SGRRLD with SGLD using a step size γ/2, which has a lower bias, and running two chains in parallel. Additional real-data experiments should be conducted to further validate the proposed algorithm.
