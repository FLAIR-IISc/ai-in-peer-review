

This paper introduces Richardson-Romberg (RR) extrapolation, a higher-order numerical integrator, to accelerate convergence in Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) algorithms. The method runs two parallel SG-MCMC chains with distinct step sizes and correlated Gaussian noise injection, providing both asymptotic and non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. Synthetic experiments validate the theoretical bias and MSE bounds, while large-scale matrix factorization on real data demonstrates practical utility over standard SGLD. Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution with clear technical execution and contextualization within existing SG-MCMC literature.

However, two concerns warrant attention. First, the novelty appears incremental: the impact of numerical integrators on SG-MCMC is established in [10], and RR extrapolation for invariant distribution approximation is previously applied in [23]. While the specific integration of RR into SG-MCMC is valid, the paper should more explicitly differentiate its technical novelty from these prior works.

Second, the practical implementation and experimental comparison require clarification. The parallel SGRRLD (SG-MCMC with RR) implementation necessitates synchronized communication between chains at every step due to correlated noise, yet the authors assume zero communication cost. This oversight is critical: in distributed settings, communication overhead could negate theoretical speed advantages. The experimental setup also lacks detail on resource allocation. Specifically, it is unclear whether SGRRLD’s two chains share the same computational resources as a single-chain SGLD implementation. If SGLD uses the full resource while SGRRLD splits it across two chains, wall-clock time comparisons become misleading. Conversely, if SGRRLD runs on the same hardware as SGLD, the resource partitioning strategy (e.g., 50% per chain) must be specified to assess fairness. Since stochastic gradient computation dominates SG-MCMC runtime, splitting resources between chains may not yield faster wall-clock time than a full-resource SGLD. The authors should clarify the experimental setup, including hardware configuration, resource allocation per chain, and whether communication costs were measured. Additionally, comparing against an embarrassingly parallel two-chain SGLD (without communication) would provide a more meaningful baseline.

Minor issues: "Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA)" is repeated at lines 25 and 70. The presentation would benefit from consolidating this reference.

In summary, the paper proposes a technically sound extension to SG-MCMC with empirical validation. Addressing the implementation details and experimental fairness concerns—particularly regarding communication costs and resource allocation—would strengthen the contribution. With these clarifications, the work would be a valuable addition to the SG-MCMC literature.
