Stochastic Gradient MCMC (SG-MCMC) methods have recently gained significant attention as a scalable approach for Bayesian posterior inference on large datasets. A key drawback of these methods is the asymptotic bias they introduce, which prevents them from converging to the exact posterior distribution. This paper proposes using Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to reduce this bias.

The core idea is straightforward: two parallel SG-MCMC chains are run. The second chain uses half the step size of the first and runs for twice the number of iterations. Crucially, the injected Gaussian noise is correlated between the chains such that α_k = (β_{2k-1} + β_{2k}) / √2, where α_k and β_k are the noise terms for Chain 1 and Chain 2, respectively. The final estimator for the expectation of a function f is then formed by a specific linear combination of the estimators from the two chains. The authors show that the resulting combined estimator reduces the bias from O(γ) to O(γ^2), where γ is the step size of the first chain. A similar reduction in the Mean Squared Error (MSE) is also proven.

The proposed method is commendable for its simplicity in both concept and implementation, requiring only minor modifications to existing SG-MCMC algorithms. Furthermore, the approach appears to be general and applicable to a wide class of SG-MCMC methods, which gives it the potential for significant impact within the field. While Richardson-Romberg extrapolation is a classical technique in numerical analysis, its application to the SG-MCMC setting is novel. The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear.

**Comments and Questions:**

*   The theoretical analysis relies on the bias having the form π_γ(f) = π(f) + Cγ + O(γ^2). Is this the only condition required for the method's applicability? Can the technique be directly applied to any SG-MCMC method, or are there other implicit assumptions?

*   Could this extrapolation technique be extended to other approximate MCMC schemes that are not based on stochastic gradients, such as those using sub-sampling in Metropolis-Hastings tests? What are the prospects for applying it to variants like Distributed SGLD?

*   The proposed two-chain scheme is computationally inefficient, as the chain with the larger step size must remain idle for half the total runtime. Could a more efficient scheme be devised? For instance, one could run three chains for the same duration: one with step size γ and two with step size γ/2. How would such a modification affect the variance of the final estimator?

*   Is it possible to extend this method to higher orders of bias correction by using more than two different step sizes, in the spirit of full Richardson-Romberg extrapolation?

*   The paper emphasizes the need for correlated injected noise between the chains. However, the effect of correlation between the mini-batches of data used at each step is less clear. To minimize variance, should the same mini-batch be used for the k-th step of the first chain and the (2k-1)-th and 2k-th steps of the second chain? Or is the impact of this data correlation negligible?

*   The matrix factorization experiment (Figure 5) could be strengthened. First, the experiments should be run until the methods appear to have converged to provide a clearer comparison of final performance. Second, to demonstrate the value of the Bayesian approach itself, a comparison against a standard non-Bayesian baseline like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is essential. While the results show an improvement over SGLD, it is crucial to also show that the proposed method outperforms SGD in a setting where Bayesian inference is advantageous. This would provide a stronger motivation for practitioners to adopt this approach.

*   While the method is conceptually simple and clearly explained, the inclusion of a pseudocode algorithm box would significantly improve its accessibility. Many practitioners may not delve into the theoretical sections, and providing a concrete algorithm early in the paper would enable them to implement and experiment with the method without needing to fully digest the more technical derivations.
