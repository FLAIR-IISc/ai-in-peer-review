

This paper introduces a Richardson-Romberg scheme to enhance the convergence rates of stochastic gradient Markov chain Monte Carlo (SG-MCMC) methods, specifically targeting improvements in bias and mean squared error (MSE). The proposed approach, termed SGRRLD, requires minimal implementation changes to standard SG-MCMC algorithms and is supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The core contribution—leveraging Richardson-Romberg extrapolation to accelerate convergence—is well-motivated and theoretically sound, offering a promising practical extension to existing SG-MCMC frameworks.  

**Key Strengths:**  
The theoretical foundation is robust, with clear derivations establishing improved convergence rates under standard assumptions (e.g., smoothness, bounded gradients). The scheme’s simplicity is a significant advantage, as it avoids complex modifications to existing SG-MCMC implementations. The experimental validation across multiple datasets (e.g., matrix factorization, Bayesian logistic regression) generally supports the theoretical claims, particularly in demonstrating faster bias reduction.  

**Key Concerns:**  
1. **Experimental Fairness (Runtime Scaling):**  
   The runtime comparison in Figure 5 appears misleading. The method employs two chains (one with step size γ and another with γ/2), requiring approximately twice the computational time of standard SG-MCMC (e.g., SGLD) for equivalent step counts. If SGLD runs in time *T* with *K* steps, SGRRLD requires ~2*T* (due to two chains, each with *K* steps). The current plot compares SGLD at time *T* to SGRRLD at time ~2*T*, but the x-axis labels time *T* for both. To fairly compare efficiency, SGLD’s runtime should be scaled by a factor of 2/3 (i.e., SGLD at time *T* ≈ SGRRLD at time *T* × 2/3). This adjustment would substantially reduce the apparent performance gap in Figure 5, potentially diminishing the claimed advantage. The authors must clarify this scaling or revise the figure to reflect fair runtime comparisons.  

2. **Figure 3 Consistency:**  
   The text states that *α* = 1/3 yields optimal convergence in Figure 3, yet the best-performing curve corresponds to *α* = 0.2 (red line). This discrepancy requires explanation. Additionally, the optimal curve (*α* = 0.2) exhibits significant fluctuations, which undermines the claimed convergence rate of *O*(1/*K*). The authors should investigate whether this is due to finite-sample effects, insufficient runs, or an artifact of the experimental setup. If the rate is not consistently achieved, the theoretical claims need qualification.  

3. **Theoretical Clarification (Line 256):**  
   The paper notes that for very small step sizes, the term 1/(*K*γ) dominates the bias. However, this behavior is not observed in SGLD (Figure 3), despite its use of similar step-size schedules. The authors should address why the 1/(*K*γ) term does not dominate SGLD’s bias, as this appears inconsistent with the theoretical framework.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Eq. (1): The use of *θ̃* instead of *θ* is confusing; standardize notation.  
- Line 94: "RR" (Richardson-Romberg) is undefined; introduce the acronym upon first use.  
- Line 131: *Γ<sub>K</sub>* is not defined; clarify its role.  
- Line 160: Specify the type of smoothness (e.g., *L*-Lipschitz, *C*<sup>2</sup>).  
- Line 249: Figure 1(b) plots "Bias vs. Time," not "Bias vs. Dimension" as implied; this likely refers to Figure 1(c). Correct the reference.  
- Vectors should be denoted in bold (e.g., **θ**, **v**).  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a compelling theoretical extension to SG-MCMC with clear practical benefits. The core idea is novel and well-executed, and the simplicity of implementation is a major plus. However, the experimental evaluation requires critical revision to ensure fairness (particularly regarding runtime comparisons) and consistency (e.g., Figure 3’s *α* value and fluctuation). Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. The minor notation and clarity issues are easily resolvable.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept** (with minor revisions). The theoretical results are strong, and the method has clear potential for broader adoption. The concerns raised are technical and resolvable, not fundamental flaws. With the requested clarifications and figure corrections, the paper will make a valuable contribution to the field.
