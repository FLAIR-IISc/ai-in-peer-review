- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NSM) with a provable globally convergent algorithm and linear (geometric) convergence under a verifiable condition.
- Global optimality holds when the spectral radius of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix is below one; the condition is architecture-dependent and efficiently checkable.
- Novel framing that leverages spectral properties to obtain strong guarantees for a nonconvex training problem.
- Practical verifiability: the certification involves a small matrix, making the test computationally light.
- Strong modeling restrictions: requires nonnegative weights and specific, non-standard activations, substantially limiting expressivity versus typical architectures.
- Potential performance impact: nonnegativity and specialized activations may hinder modeling complex decision boundaries; empirical results lag behind SVM baselines.
- Limited scope of generalization: unclear how the approach extends to signed weights or common activations (ReLU, tanh, sigmoid) without losing guarantees.
- Practical convergence claim overstated: linear convergence rate is proven but the assertion of convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is not substantiated without constant bounds.
- Empirical evaluation does not show clear advantages over strong baselines and weakens the practical case for adoption.
- Suggested additional experiments: compare to constrained baselines (projected gradient/coordinate descent), modern linear/kernel methods, and report runtimes and iteration counts with confidence intervals.
- Report the size of the nonnegative matrix used in experiments and the cost of computing its spectral radius relative to training time.
- Presentation is generally clear; the spectral condition construction and its relationship to network parameters are strengths.
- Recommend more explicit upfront statement of architectural assumptions and intuition for why the spectral radius ensures contractivity/global optimality.
- Questions for authors: can nonnegativity be relaxed (e.g., reparameterizations), does analysis extend to broader monotone or piecewise-linear activations, how sensitive is the spectral condition to depth/width, and can biases/input preprocessing mitigate expressivity limits?
- Overall: technically solid and original but limited practical impact due to restrictive assumptions and underwhelming empirical performance; reviewer leans toward rejection while acknowledging novelty.
