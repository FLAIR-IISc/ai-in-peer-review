The paper introduces a particular class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimum can be achieved, and presents an algorithm that provably attains this global optimum with linear convergence. The mathematical development is illuminating, though several aspects are unclear to me.

1. Theorem 3 appears to rely on first-order derivatives, as indicated by the inequalities used there, whereas Theorem 4 seems to invoke second-order considerations. What necessitates moving to second derivatives in Theorem 4?

2. The architecture seems to handle the output layer differently from the hidden layer(s). Since u is a matrix of hidden-layer parameters, is ∇uΦ in GΦ (equation (3)) taken with respect to a vectorized form of u? Similar questions arise elsewhere (e.g., when constraining u within a norm ball).

A further concern (perhaps I am missing something) is the objective’s inclusion of a term that effectively maximizes the 1-norm of the connection weights in (2). In most NN formulations, regularization shrinks (rather than enlarges) weight norms. It seems the arbitrarily small ε in (2) is introduced to ensure the gradient is strictly positive (i.e., lies in V++), which feels primarily like a technical device for the proof rather than something well-justified in practical training. Also, what grounds the claim that there are no free parameters here? The method requires nonnegative inputs and strictly positive weights (not even zeros), so activations continually accumulate; how practical is this modeling choice?

In the experiments, the proposed network is compared to SVM (presumably because SVM training is globally optimal). In practice, in what ways would this family of networks provide benefits over more commonly used architectures (e.g., ReLU-based networks)?
