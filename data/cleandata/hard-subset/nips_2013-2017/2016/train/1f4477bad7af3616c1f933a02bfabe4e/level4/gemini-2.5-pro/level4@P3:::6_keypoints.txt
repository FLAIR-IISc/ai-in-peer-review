- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training 1–2 hidden-layer feedforward nets with a provable linear-rate convergence to the global optimum.  
- Convergence guarantee requires non-negative inputs/weights, generalized polynomial activations (x^α, α≥1), and a modified objective; checkable via spectral radius ρ(A)<1 of an explicit matrix.  
- Positions itself as the first practically feasible algorithm offering such a strong optimality guarantee compared to prior theoretical work.  
- Empirical results on UCI datasets are competitive but not state-of-the-art.  
- Main theoretical strength: rigorous connection of optimization to nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and fixed-point theory on convex cones.  
- Provides a complete, a priori computable construction of the convergence condition and matrix A.  
- Experimental validation is weak: limited baselines and missing crucial comparisons.  
- Fails to compare against SGD/other optimizers applied to the paper’s own objective, leaving optimizer vs model benefits unclear.  
- No wall-clock time or computational complexity analysis despite claiming the method is “very fast.”  
- Datasets are low-dimensional, avoiding scalability issues (spectral radius ρ(A) grows with hidden units).  
- Objective Φ (Eq. 2) lacks upfront motivation; the role of output sum and ε is only explained later.  
- Theorem 1 is dense and needs an informal high-level explanation before the formal statement.  
- Overgeneralized claim that the method “converges very fast” is presented as general but is only an empirical observation.  
- Several notation and typo issues (e.g., R++, “polyomial”) hurt readability.  
- Approach is highly original in applying nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to guarantee global optimality for a neural model.  
- Theoretical significance is high, but practical significance is limited by restrictive assumptions on data, weights, activations, and modified objective.  
- Lacks analysis of the model’s expressiveness and what function classes it can/cannot represent.  
- For acceptance: novel theoretical contribution, provable and checkable convergence, advances prior work.  
- Against acceptance: limited practical utility, insufficient experimental validation, and clarity/exposition problems.  
- Overall: strong and interesting theoretical result that needs substantially improved experiments and clearer exposition to establish practical relevance.
