- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably trains certain feedforward networks to global optimum with linear convergence.
- Demonstrates the method on networks with one and two hidden layers and on several real-world datasets.
- Main contribution: establishes conditions under which global optimality can be guaranteed for neural network training.
- Method depends on restrictive assumptions: non-negativity of weights and generalized polynomial activation functions.
- Authors note these constraints but need stronger justification and analysis of their practical impact and alternatives.
- Claim that the restricted network class retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported by experiments.
- Empirical results are often inferior to linear SVMs, undermining claims about expressive advantage.
- Baseline choice is questionable: comparing to linear SVMs instead of nonlinear kernel SVMs (polynomial/Gaussian) is unfair.
- Experiments use small, low-dimensional UCI datasets, limiting generalizability of empirical findings.
- Spectral radius bound scales poorly with number of hidden units, raising scalability concerns for high-dimensional data.
- Although optimality conditions can be checked before training, the large parameter space makes finding valid configurations computationally expensive.
- Paper should explore strategies to efficiently narrow the parameter search space for valid settings.
- Recommend investigating whether SGD might empirically reach global optima on the tested datasets for a useful comparison.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical novelty, rigorous analysis, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, weak empirical validation, and scalability issues.
