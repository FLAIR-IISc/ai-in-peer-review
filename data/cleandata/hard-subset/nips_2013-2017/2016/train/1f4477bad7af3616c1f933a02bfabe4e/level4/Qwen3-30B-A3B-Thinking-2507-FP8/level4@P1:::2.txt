

This paper presents a novel method for achieving globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods, with linear convergence guarantees. The authors restrict their analysis to one- and two-hidden-layer networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions, which are required to satisfy certain technical conditions for global optimality. The method's key innovation is that it provides a practically feasible algorithm for globally optimal training, contrasting with previous approaches that either lack practical applicability [8] or require unknown data-generating measures [11].

The paper makes a technically interesting contribution by connecting neural network training to nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical foundation, including a detailed proof of the algorithm's convergence properties under specific conditions. Their main result (Theorem 1) establishes that for appropriately chosen parameters, the proposed nonlinear spectral method converges linearly to the global optimum.

However, the paper's restrictive assumptions raise significant concerns about practical applicability. The requirement for nonnegative weights and the use of generalized polynomial activation functions (rather than standard ReLU or sigmoid functions) seem quite counterintuitive for real-world applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not sufficiently justify why these constraints are reasonable or how they might be relaxed while maintaining global optimality. A more detailed discussion of how these constraints might be justified from an application perspective would strengthen the paper.

The experimental evaluation presents several weaknesses that undermine the claims about the method's practical value. The authors compare their approach (NLSM1 and NLSM2) with linear SVM as a baseline, which is problematic since NLSM is a nonlinear model. A fair comparison would include nonlinear SVM with polynomial or Gaussian kernels. The results in Table 1 show that linear SVM consistently outperforms NLSM methods, while nonlinear SVM (not reported) would likely provide a more appropriate benchmark. The authors' claim that "NLSM achieves competitive performance" is not convincingly supported by the results, as they fail to demonstrate that their method outperforms or matches nonlinear alternatives.

Moreover, the UCI datasets used in the experiments are relatively small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. Given the analysis in Section 5 that shows the spectral radius bounds grow with the number of hidden units, it's questionable whether this method would scale to higher-dimensional datasets where neural networks are typically applied. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not propose any practical solutions for handling high-dimensional data.

The paper also lacks clarity regarding the practical feasibility of the global optimality condition. While the authors note that the spectral radius condition can be checked without running the algorithm, they don't address how one would efficiently identify valid parameter combinations that satisfy ρ(A) < 1. The condition appears to be highly sensitive to parameter choices, and it's unclear how one would practically select parameters to ensure global optimality without extensive search over a potentially enormous parameter space.

The experimental comparison with SGD (Figure 2) is somewhat illuminating, showing that the proposed method converges faster than SGD with various learning rates. However, the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why the method achieves linear convergence, nor does it address whether this convergence rate would be maintained on larger datasets where the bounds on ρ(A) become more restrictive.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretically rigorous treatment of a challenging optimization problem, providing the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks. However, this theoretical contribution is significantly weakened by the restrictive assumptions and the limited empirical validation on small datasets. The authors should more clearly articulate the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical applicability, and provide stronger evidence that their approach offers practical advantages over standard neural network training methods.

In summary, while the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, the restrictive assumptions, limited empirical validation on appropriate baselines, and lack of discussion about practical implementation challenges significantly reduce its impact for the NIPS community. The authors should address these concerns to strengthen their contribution.
