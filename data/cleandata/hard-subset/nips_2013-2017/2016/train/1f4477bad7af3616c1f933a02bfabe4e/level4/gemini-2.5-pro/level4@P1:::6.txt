### Summary

This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks, which feature generalized polynomial activation functions and are constrained to have non-negative weights. The main contribution is a proof that, under certain checkable conditions related to the network architecture, the proposed iterative method converges linearly to the unique global optimum of a modified objective function. This work connects the theory of nonlinear eigenproblems and Perron-Frobenius theory to the challenge of neural network training. The authors position their method as a more practical alternative to other recent approaches with global optimality guarantees [8, 11], as it does not require knowledge of the data-generating distribution or involve complex tensor decompositions. The method is demonstrated for networks with one and two hidden layers, and its performance is evaluated on several UCI datasets.

### Overall Assessment and Main Comments

The problem of achieving globally optimal training for neural networks is highly significant, and theoretical advances in this area are a welcome contribution. The main strength of this paper is its proposal of a practical algorithm that comes with a formal guarantee of convergence to a global optimum. The fact that the conditions for this guarantee can be verified *a priori* is a distinct advantage.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that currently limit its impact. The primary concern is the practical utility and applicability of the highly constrained network model. The paper would be much stronger if it included a more thorough discussion of the model's expressiveness, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and significant improvements to the clarity of its presentation.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths)

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents what appears to be the first *practical* algorithm for globally optimal training of a non-trivial neural network class, which is a significant theoretical result in a challenging area.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded in nonlinear spectral theory, and the convergence proof appears solid. The guarantee of a linear convergence rate is a strong theoretical property.
*   **Practical Guarantees:** The conditions required to guarantee global optimality can be checked before training begins, which is a major practical benefit. The method also avoids the difficult task of learning rate tuning associated with SGD-based methods.

### Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses)

*   **Limited Practical Utility:** The paper is not fully convincing about the practical utility of the proposed network architecture. The non-negativity constraints on both weights and data, combined with the specific generalized polynomial activations, define a restrictive model class. While the authors mention expressiveness, they do not provide a clear analysis of what this model can and cannot represent, which is crucial for understanding its practical limitations.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments feel like a preliminary proof-of-concept rather than a thorough evaluation.
    *   The comparison is limited to an RBF SVM and a standard ReLU network. A more convincing evaluation would include comparisons against other relevant baselines and explore the model's performance on a wider range of tasks to better situate its performance.
    *   The experiments do not clarify the boundaries of the model's applicability (e.g., how it scales with data dimensionality or problem complexity).
    *   A discussion of computational costs (e.g., wall-clock training time, memory usage) is missing, which is essential for a paper that claims practical feasibility.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is difficult to read, particularly from Section 2 onwards.
    *   The custom objective function $\Phi$ in Equation (2) lacks sufficient motivation. The purpose of adding the sum of all network outputs and the small $\epsilon$ term is not explained, making the problem setup seem ad-hoc.
    *   Theorem 1 is presented without any preceding intuition or informal explanation, making its dense mathematical statement very difficult to parse.
    *   The strong claim that the method "converges very fast in only a few iterations" needs to be better contextualized. It should be clarified whether this is an empirical finding from the experiments or a direct consequence of the theoretical bounds.

### Detailed Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Presentation:** I strongly recommend that the authors restructure Section 2 to improve readability. Before diving into the formalism, it would be helpful to provide a high-level overview of the approach and the motivation for the chosen objective function. An informal explanation of Theorem 1 should precede its formal statement to provide intuition.
*   **Expressiveness:** The paper would benefit from a dedicated section discussing the representation power of the proposed network class. What types of decision boundaries can it form? How do the non-negativity constraints limit its capacity compared to standard unconstrained networks?
*   **Experiments:** The experimental section should be broadened to include a more comprehensive comparison and an analysis of computational efficiency. It would also be valuable to understand the model's sensitivity to its parameters (e.g., the choice of exponents $\alpha_l$).

### Minor Typos and Clarifications

*   Abstract: "Up to our knowledge" would be more natural as "To our knowledge".
*   Introduction: "polyomial" should be corrected to "polynomial".
*   The notation $\mathbb{R}_{++}$ is non-standard. Although it is defined, its definition could be made more prominent to avoid potential confusion.
