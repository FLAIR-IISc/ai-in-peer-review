This paper introduces a particular class of feedforward neural networks for which the existence of a unique global optimum is guaranteed. Correspondingly, an algorithm is proposed to find this optimizer, and it is shown to converge linearly. The mathematical derivations are largely insightful; however, certain aspects would benefit from further clarification.

1.  Regarding the theoretical results, Theorem 3 relies on first-order derivatives for its inequalities, whereas Theorem 4 transitions to second-order derivatives. What is the underlying reason for this shift in the order of derivatives between the theorems?
2.  The network design appears to handle the final layer distinctly from the hidden layers. For instance, in G^\Phi (3), is the gradient with respect to the parameter matrix `u` computed with respect to its vectorized form? This question also applies to other sections, such as the constraint that bounds `u` within a ball.

A primary concern is the model's objective in (2), which involves maximizing the L1-norm of the connection parameters. This approach is contrary to conventional regularization methods like weight decay, which typically penalize large L1 or L2 norms. It appears this maximization might be a theoretical requirement to ensure the gradient is strictly positive (in V_++) for the proof, rather than a principle justified for practical neural networks.

Furthermore, the model's practicality is questionable. It seems to lack free parameters, requiring all input data to be non-negative and all weights to be strictly positive. This structure results in a monotonic, additive model. How applicable is such a constrained design in practice?

Finally, the experiments compare the proposed network with SVMs, likely due to the shared property of having a global optimizer. A more illuminating comparison would be against more conventional neural network architectures (e.g., those with ReLU activations). It remains unclear what tangible benefits this specialized network provides over commonly used NNs to warrant its use in practical applications.
