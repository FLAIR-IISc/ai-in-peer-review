This paper presents a compelling theoretical framework demonstrating global optimality and linear convergence for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The key constraint is non-negativity for both input data and network weights, coupled with the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The theory is initially established for networks with a single hidden layer and then extended to those with multiple layers. An optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method is also proposed, and experimental results on synthetic and UCI datasets demonstrate promising performance.

**Strengths:** The authors convincingly argue that this is the first work to propose a practically viable neural network model with guaranteed global optimality and a linear convergence rate. Previous approaches with similar guarantees often suffer from complex preconditions, some being intractable in practice due to their dependence on the unknown data-generating measure. In contrast, the conditions presented here are comparatively simple, relying on checking the spectral radius of a readily computed nonnegative matrix derived from network parameters. The extension of the theory to networks of arbitrary depth and the reasonable performance on benchmark datasets further strengthen the contribution.

**Weaknesses:** The applicability of the proposed network architecture remains limited. The requirement of non-negative data and weights, alongside the generalized polynomial activation constraint, may restrict its use in certain applications. While the non-negativity constraint on data is manageable through shifting, the positivity constraint on network parameters could potentially limit the model’s expressive power. Furthermore, the stipulation that all activation functions in a hidden layer must be distinct feels somewhat unnatural and may also hinder practical application. The authors acknowledge that satisfying the spectral radius constraint might necessitate decreasing the weights of linear units as input dimensionality and network size increase. This raises concerns about a potential trade-off between satisfying the theoretical conditions and maintaining model expressiveness, particularly as the network depth increases – a scenario where diminishing weights could effectively reduce the network to a series of linear transformations.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training, differentiating itself from existing methods by providing more practical and verifiable conditions. The use of nonlinear spectral methods in this context appears to be a new contribution. The thorough comparison to related work, including [8, 11], highlights the advantages of the proposed approach in terms of feasibility and applicability.

**Significance:** While the current limitations might prevent immediate widespread adoption, the theoretical results are significant. Demonstrating global optimality, even under constraints, is a valuable step towards a better understanding of neural network training dynamics. The potential for developing algorithms with guaranteed convergence properties is highly desirable. The framework offers a unique perspective on network design and optimization and could inspire further research in this direction. The practical demonstration on UCI datasets, though limited in scale, provides initial evidence of the approach's viability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretically grounded approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training, a long-standing challenge in the field.
*   Offers a more practical alternative to existing methods with similar guarantees, avoiding intractable preconditions.
*   The linear convergence rate is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent-based methods.
*   The extension to multiple hidden layers demonstrates the potential for scaling the approach.
*   The experimental results, though preliminary, show promising performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The restrictive constraints on data, weights, and activation functions limit the immediate applicability of the method.
*   Concerns about the potential loss of expressiveness due to the need to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, especially in deep networks.
*   The limited scale of the experimental evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the limitations, this is an encouraging and valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are strong, and the practical demonstration, though modest, is promising. The potential benefits of a globally optimal training algorithm with a guaranteed convergence rate warrant acceptance.

**Minor Comments:**

*   It would be valuable to investigate the relationship between the parameters (p1, p2, pw, pu), input dimension, and network size on the spectral radius ρ(A). An empirical analysis showing how ρ(A) scales with these factors, and the corresponding adjustments needed for pw and pu, would be insightful. While a formal proof might be challenging, visualizing this relationship could provide a better understanding of the practical constraints.
*   A discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed nonlinear spectral method, particularly in relation to the matrix computations involved, would be beneficial.
