The paper proposes a method to train -under certain assumptions- a specific type of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate that their method can be applied on a selection of "real world" problems. The problem under investigation seems interesting to me and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated.

The authors provide a clear summary of the main ideas in the paper, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. They discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, addressing the criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. Specifically, they appreciate the theoretical advance in the direction of training neural networks, but raise concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed class of neural networks.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its clarity. The authors provide a well-written introduction that effectively conveys the problem and its significance. However, the presentation in subsequent sections has some room for improvement. For example, Section 2 does not read fluently, and there are some typos throughout the paper.

The authors also appreciate the originality of the paper, as it proposes a new method for training neural networks. However, they raise concerns about the significance of the results, as the experimental evaluation is limited to a selection of "real world" problems, and the comparison to other methods is not comprehensive.

In terms of quality, the authors appreciate the theoretical advance in the direction of training neural networks. However, they raise concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed class of neural networks, as the experimental results do not clarify the limitations of applicability of their model.

Some specific remarks include:

* The definition of the loss function is straight-forward, but the function Φ lacks motivation. The role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal, and it would be helpful to add this explanation to the paper.
* Theorem 1 should be explained informally before being stated. The statement itself is hard to parse.
* The very general statement that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum", given immediately after Theorem 1, stems from experimental observation. This should be clarified in the paper.
* There are some typos throughout the paper, including "polyomial" instead of "polynomial", and "one CAN model" instead of "one can model".
* The notation R_{++} is not introduced, which is unfortunate since there are several other variables whose definition points to that one.

Overall, the authors appreciate the theoretical advance in the direction of training neural networks, but raise concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed class of neural networks. They recommend adding more thoughts about what can be done and what is out of reach for their approach, as well as discussing concurrent approaches and contrasting them with their method. Additionally, they suggest providing more information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new method for training neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a clear summary of the main ideas in the paper, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere.
* The paper is well-written, and the introduction effectively conveys the problem and its significance.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental evaluation is limited to a selection of "real world" problems, and the comparison to other methods is not comprehensive.
* The authors do not provide enough information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms.
* There are some typos throughout the paper, and the notation R_{++} is not introduced.
