This paper presents a compelling theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks possesses a global optimum that can be reached at a linear convergence rate. The primary constraints are that all input data and the weights of the linear layers must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The theory is first proven for neural networks with a single hidden layer and is subsequently extended to multi-layer networks. Additionally, the authors propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on several small real-world UCI datasets.

**Pros:**

As the authors claim, this is one of the first works to propose a potentially practical neural network framework with both optimality and convergence guarantees. In contrast to previous work, which often requires complicated and sometimes practically infeasible methods to check preconditions (as they can depend on the underlying data-generating distribution), the theory presented here has comparatively simple preconditions. These preconditions depend only on the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix composed of the network's parameters. The authors also demonstrate that their theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth, showing reasonable results on small real-world datasets.

**Cons:**

The proposed network architecture has several limitations that may restrict its practical application. The theory requires that both the input data and network parameters are positive, that activation functions are generalized polynomials, and that the spectral radius condition (Theorem 1) is met (ρ < 1).

The non-negative constraint on the input data is likely not a major barrier, as most training data can be easily shifted to be positive. However, the constraint that all network parameters must be positive may significantly limit the model's expressiveness. Furthermore, the requirement that each hidden layer must have a different activation function is unconventional and could also limit applicability.

Finally, the authors state that satisfying the spectral radius constraint requires increasing the exponents (p1, p2) and decreasing the weights (pw, pu), which effectively reduces the magnitude of the linear unit weights. If my understanding is correct, this implies that for higher-dimensional inputs or larger network layers, the linear weights must be made smaller to satisfy the constraint. When these linear weights are very small, the generalized polynomial activations will behave similarly to linear functions. In such a regime, stacking additional layers would be akin to composing linear functions, which would not increase the network's expressive power. Consequently, I am concerned that this approach may not scale effectively to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging result as an initial step in this direction, and I recommend acceptance.

**Minor Comments:**

I am curious about the relationship between the spectral radius ρ(A) and the various hyperparameters (p1, p2, pw, pu), the input dimension, and the size of the network layers. For instance, as the input dimension increases, does ρ(A) grow linearly or exponentially? To counteract this effect, must the weights (pw, pu) be decreased linearly or exponentially? While a formal proof may be difficult, an empirical study on synthetic data plotting this relationship would be a valuable addition to the paper.
