This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimizing the training of generalized polynomial neural networks. The core idea is compelling: under relatively weak data assumptions, the authors demonstrate global convergence with a linear convergence rate, a significant advantage over standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. This work builds upon recent advances in neural network optimization [2, 8, 11], attempting to address the practical challenges associated with applying theoretical global optimality guarantees. Specifically, the authors circumvent the difficulties of approaches relying on tensor decompositions [11] or complex structured matrix factorizations [8] by leveraging the Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contribution: providing a practically feasible algorithm with provable global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model. The condition for guaranteeing convergence – checking the spectral radius of a relatively small matrix – is a significant advantage. The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of traditional optimization techniques and positioning the proposed method within the existing literature. The extension to two hidden layer networks (Section 4.1) demonstrates a reasonable effort to broaden the applicability of the method.

However, the imposed constraint of non-negativity on network weights is a substantial limitation. While acknowledged by the authors, its impact on the expressiveness of the network and potential performance degradation require careful consideration. The reliance on generalized polynomials as activation functions, restricted by the non-negativity requirement, further narrows the scope of applicability compared to more commonly used activations like ReLU. This choice, while justified by the theoretical framework, may limit the practical utility of the method. The experimental results (Section 5) support this concern, showing competitive but not superior performance compared to SVM and ReLU networks on the tested UCI datasets. While the authors correctly state this is a proof of concept, the performance gap raises questions about the scalability and effectiveness of the approach in more complex scenarios. The claim of "typically a few iterations" for convergence (L.90-91) appears overstated; linear convergence guarantees a *rate*, but doesn’t define the number of iterations required to reach a specific accuracy without knowing the constant factor. The discussion on the bounds for pu, pw (around Eq. 4) highlights potential conservatism and room for improvement, which would enhance the practical applicability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach offers a unique and theoretically sound method for achieving global optimality in neural network training, a long-standing challenge.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, building upon established results in nonlinear spectral theory and the Perron-Frobenius theorem.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the method and its theoretical underpinnings.
*   **First Demonstration:** This is the first work to demonstrate a practically feasible algorithm with global optimality guarantees for neural networks, even if limited in scope.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong Limitations:** The non-negativity constraint on weights and the specific activation function choice severely restrict the applicability of the method.
*   **Modest Experimental Results:** The experimental results, while demonstrating proof-of-concept, do not convincingly show superior performance compared to existing techniques.
*   **Conservatism:** The derived bounds might be too conservative, potentially hindering practical application in larger datasets or more complex architectures.



**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but faces significant practical limitations. While the guarantee of global optimality is valuable, the restrictive constraints and modest empirical results raise concerns about its broader impact. I recommend **weak acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the limitations in future work and providing more compelling evidence of scalability and performance on more challenging datasets. A thorough discussion of the implications of the non-negativity constraint and exploration of alternative activation functions within the framework would significantly strengthen the paper.
