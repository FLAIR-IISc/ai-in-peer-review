

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to training a specific class of neural networks with provable global optimality and linear convergence guarantees. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that networks with non-negative inputs, non-negative weights, and generalized polynomial activations achieve these guarantees under a simple spectral radius condition (Theorem 1). The theory is first established for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently extended to arbitrary-depth architectures. The authors also propose a practical optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods, validated on synthetic 2D data and small UCI datasets.  

**Strengths.** The work is notable as the first to provide *practical* convergence guarantees for neural networks, addressing a key limitation in prior literature. Existing methods often rely on preconditions that depend on the unknown data-generating distribution, rendering them infeasible to verify in practice. In contrast, the authors’ conditions—centered on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix derived from network parameters—are computationally tractable. The extension to arbitrary depths and empirical results on real-world datasets further strengthen the contribution. This represents a significant step toward theoretically sound, scalable deep learning.  

**Weaknesses.** The theoretical framework imposes restrictive assumptions that limit applicability. Specifically:  
1. **Non-negativity constraints:** While input shifts can address non-negative input requirements, the requirement for *all* network weights to be non-negative fundamentally restricts model expressiveness. Standard neural networks use both positive and negative weights to capture complex patterns; forcing positivity may prevent the network from learning critical features.  
2. **Activation function constraints:** The need for *all* hidden layers to use distinct generalized polynomial activations (e.g., different powers of ReLU) is unnatural and impractical. Most architectures use identical activation functions across layers (e.g., ReLU everywhere), and the authors provide no justification for layer-specific variations.  
3. **Spectral radius trade-offs:** To satisfy the spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1), the authors propose scaling parameters (e.g., decreasing linear weights *p_w*, *p_u* while increasing *p_1*, *p_2*). Critically, as input dimension or network depth increases, these adjustments force weights toward zero. When weights become sufficiently small, activation functions (e.g., ReLU) behave linearly, rendering additional layers redundant. For instance, a network with tiny weights reduces to a composition of linear functions—equivalent to a single-layer model. This directly undermines the value of depth, raising serious concerns about scalability to deeper architectures. The authors acknowledge this trade-off but do not address how it impacts expressiveness in practice.  

**Overall Assessment.** Despite these limitations, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution. The simplicity of the convergence conditions is a major advance over prior work, and the empirical results on small-scale data demonstrate practical viability. The constraints, while restrictive, are acknowledged by the authors, and the work provides a promising foundation for future research. Given that the authors explicitly frame this as an *initial attempt* toward provable guarantees, the paper merits acceptance. The weaknesses are substantive but not fatal; they highlight clear directions for future work rather than invalidating the core insight.  

**Minor Comments.**  
- The relationship between input dimension, network parameters, and spectral radius ρ(A) warrants deeper investigation. For example: Does ρ(A) scale linearly or exponentially with input dimension? If exponential, would weights *p_w*, *p_u* need to decrease exponentially to maintain ρ(A) < 1? An empirical analysis of this scaling on synthetic data would strengthen the theoretical claims.  
- Clarify how *p_1*, *p_2* (activation parameters) interact with *p_w*, *p_u* (weight parameters) to influence ρ(A). The current discussion suggests a trade-off but lacks quantitative insight.  

**Recommendation.** This paper presents a novel and rigorously justified approach to neural network optimization with meaningful theoretical advances. While the assumptions limit immediate applicability, the work provides a crucial stepping stone toward practical, provably convergent deep learning. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the limitations are appropriately contextualized. I recommend **acceptance**.
