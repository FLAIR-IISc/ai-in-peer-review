This paper develops a theory showing that a certain class of neural networks admits a global optimum that can be reached at a linear convergence rate. The main assumptions are: (i) all input features and linear-layer weights are nonnegative, (ii) activations are generalized polynomials, and (iii) a spectral-radius condition holds. The theory is first established for a single hidden layer and then extended to multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Empirically, the method performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable results on small UCI datasets.

Pros:
- To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work proposing a potentially practical neural network architecture with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior results with convergence guarantees typically require intricate and often impractical conditions—sometimes involving the data-generating distribution—that are difficult or impossible to verify.
- In contrast, the conditions here are relatively simple: they depend primarily on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix constructed from network parameters.
- The theoretical framework is extended to networks of arbitrary depth, and the empirical results, though modest in scale, support the feasibility of the approach.

Cons and concerns:
- The applicability remains limited. The model requires nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (Theorem 1) strictly less than 1.
- While the nonnegativity constraint on inputs may be manageable in practice (e.g., via shifting/scaling), constraining all weights to be nonnegative could significantly reduce expressiveness.
- Requiring distinct activation functions across all hidden layers is somewhat unnatural and may limit practical adoption.
- To satisfy the spectral-radius constraint, the paper indicates that p1 and p2 must be increased while pw and pu must be decreased, effectively shrinking the linear weights. If, as dimensionality grows, this forces progressively smaller linear weights, the activations may behave nearly linearly. In that regime, adding layers yields little expressive benefit, which raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to deeper architectures.

Minor comments and questions:
- It would be helpful to clarify how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimensionality, and network size jointly affect the spectral radius ρ(A). For instance:
  - As input dimension increases, how does ρ(A) typically scale (e.g., linearly, sublinearly, exponentially) under reasonable parameterizations?
  - To compensate, should pw and pu be reduced linearly, polynomially, or exponentially?
- While a full theoretical characterization may be difficult, an empirical study on synthetic data plotting ρ(A) as a function of dimensionality and these parameters would provide useful intuition and practical guidance.

Overall assessment:
Despite the restrictive assumptions, this paper makes a meaningful contribution by identifying a class of neural networks with verifiable conditions that guarantee global optimality and linear convergence, and by providing an accompanying optimization method. The theoretical clarity, simplicity of the verifiable condition (via spectral radius), and extensions to multilayer settings are noteworthy. Given the novelty and potential to inspire further work that relaxes these constraints, I recommend acceptance.
