This paper examines a special class of neural networks, in which the authors succeed in showing that it has only a single stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method that converges to the unique fixed point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper addresses an interesting problem: demonstrating that a relatively complex model, under carefully chosen constraints, can have only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (or generalizations like geodesic convexity), the common approach for establishing uniqueness of a stationary point is via fixed-point theory; this is the approach used here. The authors construct a contractive mapping whose fixed point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One drawback of the proposed method is that the convergence proof depends on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix. As the authors mention, this is why they did not attempt networks with a larger number of hidden units. Another issue is the many parameters that must be tuned, which makes the procedure rather unintuitive. Furthermore, for each parameter choice, the spectral condition must be verified. In the experiments, the authors tried about 150,000 different combinations of parameter values and selected the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result calls into question the practical strength of the neural network being investigated.
