- Restrictive modeling assumptions: non-negativity on all weights, maximal regularization, and use of a non-standard generalized polynomial activation feel overly constraining and need practical justification.  
- Expressive power not convincingly demonstrated: empirical results do not show NLSM models outperforming simple baselines and comparisons to only a linear SVM are unfair.  
- Missing comparable nonlinear baselines: the paper should compare against nonlinear SVMs (polynomial, RBF) and other competitive nonlinear models.  
- Limited experimental scope: datasets are small in sample size and feature dimensionality, limiting conclusions about real-world performance and scalability.  
- Scalability concerns from spectral radius bounds: bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with hidden units, threatening applicability to high-dimensional networks.  
- Feasibility of optimality condition: the global-optimality condition seems unlikely under practical parameter settings and searching candidate parameters may be infeasible without fast screening methods.  
- Dependence on objective form: clarify whether the global optimality guarantee relies critically on the specific objective function used.  
- SGD comparison suggested: investigate whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically reach the same global optimum on the tested datasets.  
- Fair hyperparameter tuning needed: ensure comparable levels of tuning across methods to make performance comparisons meaningful.  
- Test on larger, higher-dimensional datasets: include bigger, more modern datasets to evaluate scalability and practical relevance.  
- Provide application-driven motivation: justify non-negativity, maximal regularization, and generalized polynomial activations with real-world use cases or empirical evidence.
