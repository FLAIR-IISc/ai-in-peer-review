This paper introduces a novel class of feedforward neural networks possessing a uniquely identifiable global optimizer, alongside an algorithm to locate it with a provably linear convergence rate. The mathematical treatment is generally well-presented and illuminating, though some aspects could benefit from further clarification. 

Specifically, the transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4 requires additional explanation. It’s unclear what necessitates this shift in derivative order. Furthermore, the network architecture appears to distinguish the final layer from preceding hidden layers in its treatment. Given that ‘u’ represents parameters connecting to the hidden layers, the gradient calculation within G<sup>Φ</sup> (3) seems to operate on the vectorization of ‘u’. Similar observations arise when considering bounds on ‘u’, such as the ball constraint. 

A significant question concerns the motivation for maximizing the 1-norm of connection parameters in (2), a strategy contrary to the typical weight decay regularization employed in standard neural networks. The justification appears tied to maintaining a strictly positive gradient via the small epsilon value, seemingly a requirement of the proof rather than a practical necessity for general neural network training. It’s important to establish whether this constraint introduces any unacknowledged degrees of freedom. The restriction to non-negative data and the subsequent use of exclusively positive weights raise concerns about the practical applicability of this architecture, potentially leading to unbounded accumulation.

The experimental evaluation compares the proposed network to SVMs, presumably due to SVMs also possessing global optima. However, it remains unclear whether this novel network offers substantial advantages over more conventional neural network architectures (e.g., those utilizing ReLU activation functions) in practical applications.
