This paper introduces a restricted class of feedforward neural networks that admit a unique global optimizer and provides an algorithm with linear convergence to attain it. The theoretical development is interesting and generally well argued, but several aspects need clarification, and the practical relevance remains unclear.

Major questions and requests for clarification:
1) Theorem 3 vs. Theorem 4: Theorem 3 appears to rely on first-order derivative bounds (as reflected in the stated inequalities), whereas Theorem 4 invokes second-order derivatives. What necessitates this shift to Hessian-level arguments? Is it due to stronger regularity assumptions (e.g., strong convexity/concavity in a subspace), a need to certify uniqueness, or a specific property of the last layer? Please state precisely which assumptions trigger second-order analysis and why first-order arguments are insufficient there.

2) Treatment of layers and parameterization: The last layer seems to be handled differently from the hidden layers. Let u denote the matrix of parameters into a hidden layer. In G^Φ (3), is the gradient with respect to u to be interpreted as the gradient with respect to vec(u) (i.e., stacking u into a vector)? If so, please make this explicit. Similarly, when you “bound u within a ball,” in which space and norm is the ball defined (matrix norm vs. vector norm on vec(u))? Clear dimensioning/shapes for all gradients and constraints would help.

3) Objective and positivity constraints: Equation (2) appears to maximize the L1 norm of connection weights, which contrasts with standard weight decay that penalizes (reduces) L1 or L2 norms. My reading is that the small epsilon is introduced to make gradients strictly positive so that they lie in V_++. If this is primarily a technical device to ensure the proof goes through, please justify its practical necessity and discuss alternatives. More broadly:
- What guarantees that there is no free parameter under this setup?
- The model assumes nonnegative inputs and strictly positive weights (no zeros), leading to a monotone, purely additive accumulation of activations. How restrictive is this in practice? What is the representational cost of disallowing negative or zero weights?
- How should practitioners handle real data with negative features? Do you recommend shifting/scaling or other preprocessing?
- If L1 is being maximized, what prevents parameter blow-up? Is there a competing constraint (e.g., normalization, bounded domain) that controls scale? Please clarify the role and sensitivity of epsilon.

4) Experimental evaluation: The comparison to SVM seems motivated by shared global optimality. However, to assess practical value, please compare against standard neural baselines (e.g., ReLU MLPs) matched for parameter count and training budget. What advantages—accuracy, sample efficiency, robustness, calibration, interpretability, convergence speed—does the proposed network provide in realistic settings? Ablations isolating the impact of positivity constraints and the L1-maximizing component would be valuable, along with wall-clock convergence and sensitivity analyses (e.g., varying epsilon).

5) Presentation and proof clarity:
- Explicitly state all assumptions (data nonnegativity, positivity of weights, norm definitions) in the main text.
- Define V_++ clearly and relate it to the parameter constraints.
- Provide exact dimensions for all variables and gradients; if gradients are taken over vec(u), say so.
- Bridge the gap between the first-order inequalities in Theorem 3 and the second-order arguments in Theorem 4 with a brief proof sketch or intuition.
- If space permits, move key technical lemmas from the appendix into the main text or provide more detailed summaries.

6) Practical guidance:
- Discuss how to handle negative or zero features in inputs; what happens when inputs contain zeros if weights are strictly positive?
- Describe regularization or normalization strategies that ensure numerical stability when maximizing an L1 norm.
- Provide implementation details (initialization respecting positivity, projection steps, constraints handling) and pseudocode with input/output shapes.

Minor comments:
- Ensure consistent notation for layers and parameters across sections; avoid treating the last layer in a way that obscures the generality of the approach.
- Specify which norms are used in all constraints and bounds (L1 vs. L2, matrix vs. vectorized forms).
- Include full hyperparameter settings and data preprocessing steps for reproducibility.

Overall assessment: The paper offers a neat theoretical result—global optimality with a linearly convergent algorithm—for a carefully constrained network class. To be compelling for NeurIPS, the paper should more clearly explain the derivative-level arguments, tighten notation and assumptions, and, most importantly, demonstrate tangible benefits over standard neural models on practical tasks.
