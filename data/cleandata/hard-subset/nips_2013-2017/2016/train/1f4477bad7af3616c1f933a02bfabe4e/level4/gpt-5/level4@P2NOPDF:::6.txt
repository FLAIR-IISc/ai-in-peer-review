This paper introduces a method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions. The authors establish optimal convergence guarantees and demonstrate applicability on several real-world tasks. The problem is interesting, and theoretical progress in this area is valuable. However, the paper does not fully convince me of the practical utility of the proposed network class.

My main concern is expressiveness. Since the paper itself raises the question of how restrictive the model is (in two places), the authors should add a clear discussion of what kinds of functions or tasks are within reach, and what is not. Without this, it is difficult to assess when the method is preferable to standard architectures and training procedures.

Relatedly, the experimental section does not clarify the method’s scope. The comparisons are limited to a few SVM baselines, omitting both other training methods for neural networks and alternative algorithmic paradigms that could be competitive on the same tasks. A broader set of baselines is needed to place the proposed method in context. In addition, the paper should report computational resources and efficiency metrics for all methods (e.g., wall-clock time, iteration counts, hardware, memory), since training-time guarantees are central to the contribution.

The introduction is clear and well written. The presentation that follows could be improved, especially Section 2, which is hard to follow at times. Several definitions and notational choices are insufficiently motivated or introduced.

Specific comments:
- Loss function and Phi: While the loss is straightforward, the role of the function Phi is under-motivated. The explanation of the epsilon term provided in the rebuttal should be included in the paper to make the objective and constraints self-contained.
- Theorem 1: Provide an informal explanation and intuition before the formal statement. As written, the theorem is difficult to parse. Clarify assumptions, the class of networks covered, and the meaning of “optimal convergence” in practical terms.
- Empirical convergence claim: Immediately after Theorem 1, the paper states that the nonlinear spectral method “converges quickly, typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum.” The rebuttal indicates this is an empirical observation; please make that explicit in the text and qualify the claim accordingly (e.g., with references to specific experiments and conditions).
- Experiments: Expand baselines to include established neural network training methods and other relevant approaches. Discuss where the proposed model performs well or poorly, and analyze failure modes. Report computational costs for all algorithms to substantiate the practical benefits suggested by the theory.

Minor issues and typos:
- Section 2 is difficult to read; consider reorganizing definitions and introducing notation before use.
- Line 60: “polyomial” → “polynomial.”
- Line 67: “one CAN model” → “one can model.”
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are used without prior definition; please introduce them.
- Line 67: R_{++} is used without definition. Since several other variables refer to it, define it where first used (e.g., as the set of strictly positive reals).
- Notation: Ensure all symbols and sets are defined on first appearance and used consistently throughout.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Solid and nontrivial theoretical result with clear convergence guarantees; interesting problem setting; promising preliminary empirical evidence.
- Weaknesses: Practical value of the restricted architecture remains unclear; limited experimental comparisons; missing discussion of expressiveness and applicability; presentation and notation issues.

Recommendation: I encourage the authors to (1) articulate the expressive capacity and limitations of the model, (2) broaden and strengthen the experimental evaluation with appropriate baselines and resource reporting, and (3) improve the exposition of key definitions and theorems. These changes would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact and clarity.
