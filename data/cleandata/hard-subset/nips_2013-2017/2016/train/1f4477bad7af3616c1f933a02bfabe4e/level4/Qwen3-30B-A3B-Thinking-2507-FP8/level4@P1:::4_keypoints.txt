Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward networks with theoretical linear convergence guarantees.  
Reduces global optimality check to computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix (ρ(A) < 1), making verification tractable before training.  
Builds on tensor methods and structured matrix factorization while avoiding density estimation and impractical steps from prior work.  
Provides rigorous theory connecting the method to Perron-Frobenius and fixed-point analysis for one- and two-hidden-layer networks.  
Achieves linear convergence without hyperparameter tuning, a practical advantage over SGD.  
Presents the first empirical validation of a globally optimal neural-network training method on real datasets.  
Requires nonnegative weights and specific activation functions (real powers αl ≥ 1), which substantially restrict model expressiveness.  
Cannot use common activations like ReLU and forbids negative weights, limiting applicability to standard neural architectures.  
Empirical performance on UCI datasets is competitive but generally inferior to SVMs and sometimes ReLU networks.  
Method is constrained to shallow networks (one–two hidden layers) in experiments, limiting scalability claims.  
Spectral radius ρ(A) grows with hidden-unit count, explaining restriction to low-dimensional datasets and limiting larger models.  
Claim that convergence occurs "typically in a few iterations" is overstated; linear rate holds but constant factors may require more iterations and absolute counts are not reported.  
Theoretical global optimality is a significant contribution, but it does not clearly translate to superior practical performance on benchmarks.  
Overall: technically sound and innovative theoretically, but strong architectural constraints reduce practical relevance and warrant further work to close the performance gap.
