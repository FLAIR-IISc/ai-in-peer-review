

This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks, with theoretical guarantees of linear convergence. The authors demonstrate that under mild data assumptions (nonnegativity), their approach achieves global optimality by reducing the problem to checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, rather than relying on iterative optimization methods like SGD. This represents a significant theoretical advance, as it is the first practically feasible method to provide such guarantees for a non-trivial neural network architecture. The work builds on recent research in tensor methods [11] and structured matrix factorization [8], but overcomes their practical limitations by eliminating the need for density estimation and providing easily verifiable conditions for global optimality.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, the theoretical framework is sound, establishing a clear connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point analysis, with rigorous proofs for both one and two hidden layer networks. The conditions for global optimality (ρ(A) < 1) are computationally tractable and verifiable before training begins. Second, the method achieves linear convergence without requiring hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD), which is a major practical advantage. Third, the authors provide the first empirical validation of a globally optimal neural network training method on real datasets, demonstrating that the constrained architecture remains expressive enough for meaningful classification tasks.

The primary limitations concern the method's architectural constraints. The requirement for nonnegative weights and specific activation functions (real powers αl ≥ 1) significantly restricts the model's expressiveness compared to standard neural networks. This constraint is substantial, as it prevents the use of ReLU or other common activation functions that enable negative weights. While the authors demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets (Table 1), their method generally lags behind SVMs and sometimes ReLU networks, particularly on problems where unconstrained architectures can easily achieve perfect accuracy (e.g., Iris, Banknote). The theoretical benefits of global optimality do not clearly translate to superior performance in these experiments, raising questions about the practical value of the constraint.

The experimental section is appropriately modest in scope, acknowledging the method as a proof of concept. The authors correctly note that the spectral radius ρ(A) increases with the number of hidden units, which explains their restriction to low-dimensional datasets. While the paper claims linear convergence "typically in a few iterations" (L.90-91), this is an overstatement—the convergence rate is linear but the constant factor R may be large, requiring more iterations than implied. The authors' experimental results (Figure 2) show faster convergence than SGD, but the absolute number of iterations needed for convergence is not specified.

The paper makes a theoretically valuable contribution by establishing a globally optimal training method for a specific neural network class, but the strong architectural constraints limit its practical applicability. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory is elegant, and the verification of global optimality conditions is a notable technical achievement. However, the performance on standard benchmarks does not justify the restrictive assumptions in most real-world scenarios.

Pro:
- First practically feasible method for globally optimal neural network training with verifiable conditions
- Linear convergence rate without hyperparameter tuning
- Rigorous theoretical foundation connecting to fixed-point theory and Perron-Frobenius theory
- First empirical demonstration of global optimality in neural networks on real datasets

Con:
- Nonnegative weight constraint severely limits architectural expressiveness
- Non-standard activation functions (real powers) are not widely applicable
- Performance on UCI datasets is competitive but generally inferior to SVM and ReLU networks
- Limited to shallow networks (1-2 hidden layers) in experiments
- Theoretical guarantee of global optimality does not translate to superior empirical results

The paper is technically sound and makes an important theoretical contribution, but the strong architectural constraints significantly limit its practical relevance. While the method is a valuable theoretical advance, the reviewers should weigh whether this contribution is sufficiently significant for NIPS, given the limited empirical advantages over existing approaches. The authors should address the performance gap with standard methods in future work.
