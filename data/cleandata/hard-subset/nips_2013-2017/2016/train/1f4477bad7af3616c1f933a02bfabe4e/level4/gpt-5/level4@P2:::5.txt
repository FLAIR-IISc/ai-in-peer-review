Summary
The paper studies a restricted class of feedforward neural networks—generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative inputs and weights—and proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably finds a unique global optimum under a verifiable condition (the spectral radius of a small, nonnegative matrix A is < 1). The method has a linear convergence rate and requires no learning-rate tuning. The main development covers one hidden layer and is extended to two hidden layers. The objective differs from standard practice: it maximizes (equivalently, minimizes the negative of) the cross-entropy plus the sum of outputs, and includes a small positive linear term in the weights to ensure strict positivity of gradients. Experiments on several UCI datasets show competitive accuracy relative to ReLU networks and slightly worse performance than RBF-SVM.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against recent global optimality results for neural networks that are challenging to apply (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal) or rely on difficult-to-estimate score function tensors (e.g., Janzamin et al.). By contrast, the proposed method yields a practically checkable condition (via ρ(A)) and does not require density estimation.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a globally convergent algorithm with a linear rate for a nontrivial network class, accompanied by a checkable spectral condition.
- Elegant use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and fixed-point contractions on positive cones.
- No learning-rate hyperparameter; the convergence rate is tied to ρ(A), which can be computed upfront.
- Extension to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.
- Empirical results demonstrate that the constrained model can produce nontrivial decision boundaries and competitive performance on small datasets.

Weaknesses and questions
- Order of derivatives: Theorem 3 provides a contraction result for GΦ using first-order information (Lipschitz bounds via a matrix A), but Theorem 4 appears to require second derivatives (since ∇GΦ involves ∇2Φ) to construct A. Please clarify the smoothness requirements: where exactly are second derivatives of Φ used, and what assumptions on C2 regularity are necessary for the stated bounds?
- Treatment of u as a matrix: GΦ applies ψ and normalization to ∇uΦ. Is this computed elementwise on u (i.e., equivalent to vectorizing u and applying the mapping componentwise)? Similarly, when constraining u within an ℓpu ball, is the norm over all entries of u (matrix reshaped as a vector)? A brief note on shapes and vectorization conventions would aid readability.
- Positivity and the ε term: Equation (2) includes an arbitrarily small positive linear term in the weights, ostensibly to ensure ∇Φ is strictly positive on S+. While theoretically convenient, this effectively maximizes the ℓ1 norm of the weights (and the sum of outputs). Could the authors:
  - Report which ε was used in experiments and how sensitive performance is to ε?
  - Explain whether the added linear terms have a practical effect (e.g., discouraging sparsity), or whether they are negligible numerically?
  - Discuss if alternative mechanisms (e.g., interior-point style constraints) could ensure strict positivity without modifying the objective?
- “No free parameter” claim: Although there is no learning rate, the method still requires architectural and norm parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, pw, pu, and ε), and the spectral condition ρ(A) < 1 often pushes pw, pu to large values. Please qualify the claim and explain practical guidance for choosing these hyperparameters, beyond cross-validation.
- Nonnegativity and expressivity: Requiring x ≥ 0 and strictly positive weights forbids sign cancellations and enforces monotonicity. How does this affect expressivity and calibration in practice, especially on standardized or mean-subtracted features? Do the authors recommend data preprocessing (e.g., shifting/scaling) to satisfy x ≥ 0, and how sensitive is performance to such preprocessing?
- Distinct α per hidden unit: The paper argues that identical exponents lead to permutation invariances and trivial optima. In practice, how are α chosen and tuned? Are there heuristic defaults or regularization schemes that avoid overfitting while keeping ρ(A) < 1? An ablation on α choices would be helpful.
- Conservativeness of ρ(A) bounds: The paper notes that the bounds defining A are conservative. Can the authors provide empirical ρ(A) values, iteration counts to convergence, and any tightened bounds that reduce dependence on the number of hidden units?
- Experimental scope and baselines: The datasets are low-dimensional, and performance is slightly below RBF-SVM on average. Could the authors comment on:
  - Scalability to higher dimensions (given ρ(A) grows with hidden units)?
  - Wall-clock comparisons and iteration counts to reach a target objective gap?
  - Additional baselines that also enforce nonnegativity (e.g., monotone networks, nonnegative matrix/tensor factorization-based classifiers)?
  - A comparison against unconstrained ReLU with nonnegativity preprocessing to isolate the impact of positivity constraints?

Clarity and presentation
- The exposition is mathematically rich but dense. Adding a short “map of the proof” (what is used where, and which smoothness/positivity assumptions are essential) would help.
- Explicitly state vectorization conventions for matrix variables in gradients and norms.
- Summarize the role of each term in Φ and its practical impact.
- Provide pseudocode with shapes and norms specified, and report ε and ρ(A) used in experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and rigorous global optimality result with a practical check (ρ(A) < 1) and linear convergence.
- A clean algorithm with no learning-rate tuning and interpretable convergence behavior.
- First demonstration (to my knowledge) of a globally optimal NN training method yielding nontrivial empirical performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical limitations due to nonnegativity and positivity constraints; unclear expressivity and scalability.
- Objective modification (sum of outputs and ε-weight term) may affect practical relevance; empirical impact not fully quantified.
- Conservative bounds and limited experimental scope; performance generally trails RBF-SVM.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with promising algorithmic implications. Clarifying the derivative requirements, matrix-variable handling, and the practical role of the ε and positivity constraints, along with broader experiments or ablations, would significantly strengthen the paper.
