- Paper studies a specific class of feedforward neural nets that can be globally optimized with linear convergence via the nonlinear spectral method, applied to 1–2 hidden layers and tested on real datasets.

- The considered network class is limited and counterintuitive, requiring non-negative weights and maximal regularization.

- Optimality requires a generalized polynomial activation function, which is an unrealistic or restrictive assumption.

- Authors claim sufficient expressive power, but empirical results do not convincingly support this claim.

- Linear SVM surprisingly outperforms NLSM1/NLSM2 despite appearing suboptimally tuned, undermining the reported benefits of NLSM.

- Using a linear SVM baseline (instead of nonlinear SVMs like polynomial or Gaussian kernels) produces unfair comparisons and weakens conclusions.

- Experiments rely on small UCI datasets with limited sample size and feature dimensionality, restricting the evaluation’s generality.

- Spectral radius bounds grow with the number of hidden units, making the method problematic for high-dimensional or larger networks.

- The optimal condition is not guaranteed by parameter settings, and verifying the condition without exhaustive search is difficult when no priors exist.

- A fast method to produce a small set of valid parameter candidates is needed to make the approach practical.

- It should be investigated whether the model’s optimality comes from the special objective function and whether SGD can empirically reach the global optimum on tested datasets.

- A more detailed justification from application or real-world dataset perspectives is needed instead of imposing restrictive theoretical assumptions.
