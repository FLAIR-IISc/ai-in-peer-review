This paper studies a particular class of feedforward neural networks that, according to the authors, can be trained to global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method is applied to deep networks with one and two hidden layers, and experiments are conducted on a series of real-world datasets.

Major concerns:
- Restrictive modeling assumptions: The class of networks imposes non-negativity constraints on all weights and maximizes the regularization of these weights. In addition, the optimality guarantee requires the use of a less common activation function (the generalized polynomial). These assumptions feel restrictive and counterintuitive. More detailed and careful justification from the perspective of practical applications or real-world datasets is needed, rather than introducing progressively more restrictive assumptions solely to obtain global optimality.
- Expressive power not convincingly demonstrated: Although the paper claims that the targeted class still has sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and to achieve good performance, the empirical results do not strongly support this. In particular, it appears that a linear SVM performs much better than NLSM1 and NLSM2 even without careful tuning of the linear SVM, whereas the NLSM models benefit from tuning of their network structures. Given that NLSM is a nonlinear model, it is also unclear why the main baseline is a linear SVM rather than a more comparable nonlinear SVM (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels). As a result, the comparisons do not seem fair, and the ensuing conclusions are not convincing. These results, as presented, do not establish the claimed expressive power.
- Limited experimental scope: The UCI datasets used are small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. This limits the ability to assess performance and scalability on modern, higher-dimensional problems.
- Scalability and spectral radius bounds: Based on the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which makes the approach problematic for high-dimensional data. Is there any way to mitigate this issue? As it stands, this could substantially limit applicability to a large proportion of real-world settings.
- Feasibility of the optimality condition: The optimality condition does not appear to be guaranteed by any parameter settings likely to be used in these networks. Although the condition can be checked without running the network, this remains problematic when we lack strong priors or when the candidate parameter space is extremely large. Is there a fast approach to identify a small set of valid candidates?

Questions and suggestions:
- Does the global optimality property stem from the particular objective function used? Please clarify the extent to which the guarantee depends on this special form.
- It would be interesting to see whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically converge to the same global optimum on some of the tested datasets.
- Strengthen the empirical evaluation by: (i) adding nonlinear SVM baselines (e.g., polynomial, RBF) and other competitive nonlinear models; (ii) ensuring comparable levels of hyperparameter tuning across all methods; and (iii) including larger and higher-dimensional datasets to test scalability and practical relevance.
- Provide application-driven motivation and evidence that the non-negativity and maximal regularization constraints are reasonable in practice, and discuss when the generalized polynomial activation is a natural choice.

Overall, while the theoretical aim of achieving global optimality with linear convergence is appealing, the current assumptions appear overly restrictive, the empirical evidence does not convincingly support the claimed expressive power, and scalability concerns remain unresolved. Addressing the fairness of comparisons, expanding the experimental scope, and clarifying how to satisfy (or efficiently search for) the optimality conditions would significantly strengthen the paper.
