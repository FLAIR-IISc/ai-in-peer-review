This paper investigates a specific family of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to global optimality with a linear convergence rate via a nonlinear spectral method. The approach is demonstrated on networks with one and two hidden layers, and empirical results are reported on several real-world datasets. The work examines this constrained class in depth, presenting an algorithm with a verifiable global optimality condition and showcasing experiments. 

As the authors note, the studied class is fairly restrictive and somewhat counterintuitive: it enforces non-negativity on all network weights and includes an objective term that effectively promotes larger weights. In addition, the use of generalized polynomial activations is required to establish the optimality guarantees. These assumptions are not obviously reasonable in many practical settings. More detailed, application-driven justification and discussion would be helpful, rather than further tightening assumptions solely to secure global optimality. The authors claim that, despite these constraints, the models retain sufficient expressivity to learn complex decision boundaries and achieve good performance, but the empirical evidence presented does not strongly substantiate this. It appears that a linear SVM often outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2 even when the linear SVM may not be optimally tuned, while NLSM benefits from architecture selection. Moreover, given that NLSM is nonlinear, it is unclear why a linear SVM is used as a primary baseline instead of a nonlinear SVM (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian kernels). This raises fairness concerns about the comparisons and weakens conclusions regarding the expressive power of the proposed class. The UCI datasets used are also quite small in both sample size and dimensionality. The analysis indicates the spectral radius bounds grow with the number of hidden units, which is troubling for higher-dimensional problems; how can this be mitigated? This limitation could substantially hinder applicability in many realistic scenarios. It also seems that the optimality condition may not be ensured for arbitrary reasonable parameter settings. While the condition can be checked a priori, this remains challenging in the absence of priors or when the candidate parameter space is large. Is there a practical way to efficiently narrow down a small set of valid configurations? Finally, does the global optimality stem primarily from the specialized objective function? It would be informative to test whether stochastic gradient methods can reach the global optimum empirically on some of the evaluated tasks.
