

The paper introduces a specialized class of feedforward neural networks that admit a unique global optimizer, accompanied by an algorithm achieving linear convergence. The mathematical derivation is generally instructive, though several aspects require clarification:  
1. Theorem 3 computes first-order derivatives (as shown in the inequalities), while Theorem 4 proceeds to second-order derivatives. The rationale for this transition is unclear.  
2. The network design treats the final layer distinctly from hidden layers. Specifically, since \( \mathbf{u} \) is a matrix parameterizing connections to hidden layers, the gradient with respect to \( \mathbf{u} \) in \( \mathcal{G}^\Phi \) (Equation 3) appears to be defined in terms of the vectorized form of \( \mathbf{u} \). Similar ambiguities arise regarding constraints bounding \( \mathbf{u} \) within a ball (e.g., in the proof of Theorem 4).  
3. The model maximizes the 1-norm of connection parameters (Equation 2), which contradicts standard weight decay practices (typically minimizing 1-norm). This appears to be solely to ensure strictly positive gradients (placing them in \( \mathcal{V}_{++} \)), using an arbitrarily small \( \epsilon \). This choice lacks practical justification for neural networks, as it necessitates:  
   - All input data to be non-negative,  
   - Strictly positive weights (no zeros allowed),  
   - Unbounded accumulation of weights.  
   The absence of free parameters is unexplained, and the practical viability of this design is questionable.  
The experiments compare the proposed network to SVMs (presumably due to SVM's global optimizer property), but the review does not address whether this network offers tangible advantages over standard neural networks (e.g., ReLU-based architectures) in practice. The comparison seems superficial without demonstrating empirical benefits over widely used alternatives.
