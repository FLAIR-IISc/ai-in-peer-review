

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to achieving globally optimal training for a specific class of neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors demonstrate that for feedforward networks with nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activation functions (with distinct exponents), and a carefully constructed objective function, global optimality can be achieved with linear convergence rates. The key theoretical contribution is that the global optimality conditions reduce to checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix—a condition that is computationally feasible to verify without running the algorithm. The method is first developed for single-hidden-layer networks and then extended to two-hidden-layer architectures, with theoretical guarantees for both cases.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on neural network optimization. Previous work on global optimality, such as Janzamin et al. (2015) and Haeffele & Vidal (2015), either requires impractical checks of the data-generating measure or has complex conditions that are challenging to verify in practice. The authors' approach stands out by providing a condition (spectral radius < 1) that depends only on the network architecture parameters, making it practically applicable. This represents a meaningful advance over prior work in the field.

The quality of the paper is strong, with a well-structured theoretical development that carefully establishes the necessary conditions for global optimality. The proofs are technically sound and build upon recent advances in nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, though some technical details (particularly in the proof strategy) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The significance of the work is demonstrated through experiments on UCI datasets, where the proposed method achieves competitive performance compared to ReLU-based networks and SVMs, while providing theoretical guarantees that previous methods lack.

The main strengths of the paper are:
1. First practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for neural networks
2. Simple verification of global optimality conditions (spectral radius < 1)
3. Theoretical extension to multi-layer networks (demonstrated for two hidden layers)
4. Competitive empirical performance on small datasets
5. Clear demonstration of linear convergence rate

The main weaknesses include:
1. The nonnegativity constraints on inputs and weights limit the method's applicability to specific data types (though the authors note this can be mitigated through data preprocessing)
2. The requirement for distinct activation exponents across hidden units is unnatural and may constrain model expressiveness
3. The spectral radius condition creates a tension between network depth/complexity and parameter scaling, potentially limiting scalability to deeper architectures (as the authors note, increasing hidden units increases the spectral radius, requiring adjustments to parameters that may diminish the nonlinearity of the network)
4. The paper lacks a thorough exploration of how the spectral radius depends on input dimension and network parameters, which would be valuable for understanding practical limitations

The paper's main limitation is that the theoretical guarantees come with significant constraints on the network architecture. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper could better address whether these constraints can be relaxed for more general applications. The claim that "the model is non-standard but expressive enough" requires more evidence beyond the small UCI datasets tested, particularly for higher-dimensional problems where the nonnegativity constraints might be more restrictive.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by bridging nonlinear spectral methods with neural network training. While polynomial activation functions have been explored before (e.g., in Livni et al., 2014), this paper uniquely provides global optimality guarantees under specific constraints, which is novel for NIPS. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems (Gautier et al., 2016) is well-motivated and appropriately cited.

The significance of the work lies in its theoretical contribution to understanding neural network training, though its practical impact may be limited by the constraints. However, as the authors note, this represents an important first step toward globally optimal neural network training, a problem that has received considerable attention in the literature but lacked practical solutions until now.

Arguments for acceptance: This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to neural network optimization. It provides the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees, and the conditions for global optimality are much simpler to verify than previous approaches. The experimental results demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance on small datasets, validating the theoretical claims.

Arguments against acceptance: The nonnegativity constraints and requirement for distinct activation functions limit the model's applicability to a narrow class of problems. The paper does not sufficiently address how the spectral radius condition scales with problem complexity, raising concerns about the method's potential for deeper architectures. The theoretical contribution is significant but may have limited practical impact due to these constraints.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network optimization. While the constraints on the model architecture are significant, the paper provides a valuable foundation for future work on globally optimal neural network training. The method represents a meaningful step toward bridging the gap between theory and practice in neural network optimization. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability concerns and provide more detailed discussion of the spectral radius's relationship with problem complexity.
