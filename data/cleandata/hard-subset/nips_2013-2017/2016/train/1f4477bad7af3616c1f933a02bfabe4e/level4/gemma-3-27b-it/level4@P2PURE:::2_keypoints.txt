- Paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably trains a specific class of feedforward nets to global optimum with linear convergence, tested on one- and two-hidden-layer networks and real datasets.
- The theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive assumptions: non-negativity of weights, strong weight regularization, and use of an uncommon generalized polynomial activation.
- These constraints may limit practical applicability; the authors should justify them with application-specific insights or experiments on more challenging datasets rather than further restricting architecture.
- The claimed expressive power of the targeted network class is not convincingly supported by experiments.
- Empirical comparisons are weak: linear SVMs outperform NLSM, and using a linear SVM baseline is questionable given NLSM’s nonlinearity.
- A more appropriate baseline would be nonlinear SVMs (polynomial or Gaussian kernels) to fairly evaluate expressiveness.
- Experimental evaluation uses small, low-dimensional UCI datasets, which limits evidence of practical effectiveness.
- The spectral radius bounds scale with the number of hidden units, raising scalability concerns for high-dimensional models.
- The optimality condition can be checked without training but has limited utility without prior knowledge or efficient methods to find valid parameter candidates.
- It remains unclear whether observed global optimality is due to the specific objective; authors should examine if SGD can empirically reach global optima on the tested datasets.
