This paper investigates a specific class of neural networks, demonstrating that it possesses a single stationary point. The authors present a fast convergence method to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses an interesting problem: proving that a relatively complex model, when subject to carefully chosen constraints, can exhibit a unique stationary point. Beyond convexity (or its generalizations like geodesic convexity), establishing the uniqueness of a stationary point often relies on fixed-point theory, as is the case here. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed-point coincides with the unique minimizer of the cost function. A limitation of the proposed method is that its convergence proof depends on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. As the authors acknowledge, this restricts the applicability of the method to networks with a limited number of hidden units. Furthermore, the method requires tuning a number of parameters, rendering it rather unintuitive to apply. Critically, for each parameter configuration, the required spectral condition must be verified. The experimental results involved evaluating approximately 150,000 parameter combinations, selecting the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets, which raises questions about the practical benefits of the investigated neural network architecture.
