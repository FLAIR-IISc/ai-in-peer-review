

This paper presents a novel approach for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors demonstrate that under mild assumptions (nonnegative data), their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence rate for one- and two-hidden-layer networks, which to the best of our knowledge is the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks. The key insight is that the global optimality condition depends solely on computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, which can be checked without running the algorithm.

The paper builds upon recent work by Janzamin et al. (NIPS 2015) and Haeffele & Vidal (NIPS 2015), who also addressed global optimality for neural networks but with significant practical limitations. Janzamin et al. require computation of the score function tensor, which depends on the unknown data-generating measure, while Haeffele & Vidal's approach is "challenging to apply in practice." The current paper overcomes these limitations by working with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers), which allows the authors to leverage nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical contribution is strong and well-supported. The authors provide a complete proof of global optimality with linear convergence rate, showing the method is not merely a position paper but a complete technical contribution. The conditions for global optimality are clearly stated and computationally verifiable. The paper also carefully addresses both strengths (linear convergence, no hyperparameters) and limitations (nonnegativity constraints).

*Originality:* This is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training. The authors introduce a novel approach that combines generalized polynomial activations with nonlinear spectral methods, which differs substantially from previous work on global optimality. The paper is clearly distinct from recent NIPS papers on neural network optimization, particularly those by Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is precise, and the proof strategy is carefully explained. The authors provide a detailed roadmap of the proof, making it accessible to readers familiar with fixed-point theory and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The experimental section clearly demonstrates the method's practical feasibility.

*Significance:* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning—understanding why neural networks work well in practice despite non-convex optimization. While the restrictive nonnegativity constraint limits the method's applicability, the authors provide the first practically feasible algorithm with global optimality guarantees for neural networks. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate the method's viability, achieving competitive performance with SVM and ReLU networks.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The nonnegativity constraint on weights is quite restrictive and may limit the model's expressive power. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently explore why this constraint is necessary for their approach. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between the theoretical guarantees and practical expressiveness.

*Clarity:* While the mathematical content is clear, the motivation for using generalized polynomial activations (rather than standard ReLU or sigmoid) could be better explained. The paper mentions that ReLU doesn't make sense in this setting due to nonnegativity requirements, but a more detailed justification would strengthen the paper.

*Originality:* The restriction to nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations limits the applicability of the method. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical impact may be constrained by these assumptions. The authors should more explicitly address how their approach might be extended to more standard neural network architectures.

*Significance:* The experimental results show the method is competitive with SVM and ReLU networks but not superior. Given the restrictive assumptions, the practical significance might be limited to specific application domains where nonnegative data is common (e.g., count data, image pixels). The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential application areas where the method's theoretical guarantees would be particularly valuable.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- First practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees with linear convergence rate
- Clear, well-organized presentation of complex mathematical content
- Demonstrates practical feasibility with experimental results on standard datasets

*Con:*
- Restrictive nonnegativity constraint on weights may limit practical applicability
- Experimental results are competitive but not superior to existing methods
- Generalized polynomial activations are non-standard and may not generalize to broader applications

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding neural network training, providing the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees. While the restrictive assumptions limit its immediate practical impact, the method represents an important step toward understanding why neural networks work well in practice. The theoretical insights and the framework developed could inspire future work on more practical globally optimal training methods.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the discussion of the method's limitations and potential applications. The authors should more clearly articulate the trade-offs between the theoretical guarantees and practical expressiveness, and provide more detailed justification for the nonnegativity constraint. The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training and deserves publication at NIPS.
