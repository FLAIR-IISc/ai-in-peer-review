- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a class of feedforward neural networks with provable global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Method requires nonnegative data and weights and modifies the objective by adding the sum of outputs to the loss.  
- Builds on and extends prior global-optimality results (addresses limitations of Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal).  
- Claims to be the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for a non-trivial neural network model.  
- Theoretical contribution is strong with rigorous proofs and a checkable global-optimality condition ρ(A) < 1.  
- No hyperparameter tuning (e.g., learning rates) required; proofs are well-structured though some technical transitions need clarification.  
- Originality stems from providing a theoretically sound global-optimality method using nonnegativity constraints, with proper literature acknowledgement.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on UCI datasets against SVM and ReLU networks despite being limited to 1–2 hidden layers.  
- Paper is generally well-organized and clearly written with good notation and a logical presentation of theorems and proofs.  
- The nonnegativity constraint on data and weights is highly restrictive and limits practical applicability.  
- The objective modification (adding sum of outputs) appears to be a technical device to ensure strictly positive gradients and needs clearer justification for practice.  
- Comparison to SVM is questionable; stronger comparisons to standard neural-network methods (e.g., SGD on ReLU nets) are needed.  
- Restriction to shallow networks (1–2 hidden layers) limits relevance to deep learning applications.  
- Explanations lacking for the gradient relation wrt u and its vectorization, and for the role and choice of the small parameter ε.  
- Overall: important theoretical advance but practical impact is limited by restrictive assumptions; authors should better address practical limitations and target applications.
