This paper explores a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate using the nonlinear spectral method. The authors apply this method to deep networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluate its performance on a range of real-world datasets. However, the class of neural networks studied is restrictive, imposing non-negativity constraints on the weights and maximizing regularization of these weights. Furthermore, the optimality condition requires the use of a less common activation function, the generalized polynomial. These assumptions are not entirely reasonable and may not be justified in many practical applications. Instead of making more restrictive assumptions to achieve global optimality, it would be helpful to provide detailed explanations from the perspective of applications or real-world datasets.

The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks still possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance. However, the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. In some cases, linear SVM performs better than NLSM1 and NLSM2, even when the linear SVM does not appear to be properly tuned, while the NLSM methods tune their network structures. Moreover, it is surprising that a linear SVM is used as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear SVM, such as polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel. As a result, the comparisons are not entirely fair, and the conclusions drawn from these results are not convincing. In other words, these results do not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate the expressive power of the studied class of neural networks.

The experiments also have some limitations. The datasets from the UCI repository used in the experiments are relatively small in terms of both sample size and feature dimensionality. According to the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which can be problematic for high-dimensional data. It is unclear whether there is a way to address this issue, which could significantly limit the applicability of this method to a large proportion of applications. Furthermore, it appears that the optimality condition is not guaranteed by any setting of parameters that could be used in the studied neural networks. Although the condition can be checked without running the neural networks, it is still problematic if we do not have any prior knowledge or if the possible parameters are extremely large. Is there a fast approach to obtaining a small set of valid candidates? It would be interesting to investigate whether the optimal property of the studied model arises from the special objective function. Additionally, it would be worth exploring whether stochastic gradient descent can also converge to the global optimum empirically on some tested datasets.
