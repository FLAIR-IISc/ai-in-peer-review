This paper investigates a special class of neural networks, where the author succeeds in showing that it has only one stationary point. The author introduces a fast method that converges to the unique fixed-point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper explores an interesting problem of demonstrating that a relatively complex model with wisely chosen constraints can have only one stationary point. Apart from convexity (or its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity), the common practice for showing uniqueness of the stationary point is through using fixed-point theory, an approach also employed in this paper. The author constructs a contractive map, whose fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One drawback of the proposed method is its dependence on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for convergence proof. As the authors mention, this is the reason why they did not attempt to use networks with a larger number of hidden units. Another issue with the method is the presence of many parameters that need to be tuned, making the use of the procedure rather unintuitive. Furthermore, for each choice of parameters, the spectral condition should be verified. In the experimental results, the authors tried approximately 150,000 different combinations of possible parameter values and selected the best of them based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed linear SVM in only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result raises questions about the real power of the neural network being investigated.
