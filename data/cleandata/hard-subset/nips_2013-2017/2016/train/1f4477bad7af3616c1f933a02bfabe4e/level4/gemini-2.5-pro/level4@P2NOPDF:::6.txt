This paper introduces a training method for a specific class of feed-forward neural networks and provides a proof of its optimal convergence under certain assumptions. The authors demonstrate the method's application on a selection of real-world problems. The problem of provably optimal training for neural networks is of significant interest, and theoretical advances in this direction are a welcome contribution.

While the theoretical core of the paper is appreciated, its primary weakness lies in failing to convincingly establish the practical utility and scope of the proposed class of networks. The experimental evaluation and comparison to related work are not comprehensive enough to situate the contribution within the broader landscape of machine learning methods.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Discussion on Model Expressiveness and Practical Scope:** The paper raises the issue of its restricted model's expressiveness twice but does not provide a substantive discussion on the topic. To strengthen the paper, the authors should elaborate on the capabilities and limitations of their network class. For instance, what types of functions or data structures can this model effectively represent, and which are fundamentally beyond its reach? A clear discussion of these trade-offs is necessary for a reader to understand when this theoretically-grounded approach might be preferable to more standard, heuristic-based training methods.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:** The experimental results are limited and do not adequately demonstrate the method's advantages or disadvantages.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is restricted to an SVM, which is an insufficient baseline. The evaluation should be expanded to include other relevant methods, such as standard neural networks of comparable size trained with SGD-based optimizers, and potentially other algorithmic paradigms relevant to the chosen tasks.
    *   **Lack of Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of concurrent approaches for training neural networks, especially those that may also offer theoretical guarantees or operate on similarly restricted architectures. Contrasting the proposed method with these alternatives is essential for positioning the work.
    *   **Absence of Computational Analysis:** The paper provides no information about the computational resources (e.g., training time, memory consumption) required by the proposed algorithm versus the baselines. This information is critical for assessing its practical viability.

**Suggestions for Improving Clarity and Presentation**

The introduction is well-written, but the quality of the presentation could be improved in subsequent sections.

*   **Section 2:** This section does not read fluently and would benefit from a revision to improve its overall flow and clarity.
*   **Motivation for Function Φ:** The definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the motivation for the function Φ is lacking. The role of the epsilon term, in particular, is not explained. (The authors clarified this point in the rebuttal, and this explanation should be incorporated directly into the paper).
*   **Explanation of Theorem 1:** The formal statement of Theorem 1 is dense and difficult to parse. The authors should provide a high-level, informal explanation of what the theorem establishes and its implications *before* presenting the formal statement. This would make the theoretical contribution much more accessible.
*   **Clarification of Convergence Claim:** The statement immediately following Theorem 1—that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations"—is presented as a general fact. If this is an empirical observation from the authors' experiments (as clarified in the rebuttal), the text should be modified to explicitly state this, avoiding an unsupported generalization.

**Minor Typos and Notational Issues**

*   Line 60: "polyomial" should be "polynomial".
*   Line 67: "one model" should be "one can model".
*   Line 67: The variables p1 and p2 are used without being introduced.
*   Line 67: The notation R_{++} is not defined, which makes it difficult to understand the definitions of several other variables that rely on it. This should be explicitly defined for clarity.
