This paper presents a nice theory that demonstrates that a certain class of neural network has a global optimal which can be achieved in a linear convergence rate. The main constrain is that all the input data and the weights of the linear layer are positive and activation functions are generalized polynomial. The proposed theory is first proved on the neural network with a single hidden layer and later extend to the networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also proposed an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral method. Experiments show that the proposed algorithm works quite well on a small 2D synthetic dataset, and it also achieves a reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The paper relates to previous work on the optimization problem behind neural networks and the understanding of the success of training deep neural networks. The authors mention the challenges of applying stochastic gradient descent and variants, such as Adagrad and Adam, which require careful parameter tuning and provide no guarantee to achieve the global optimum. They also reference recent lines of research that have directly tackled the optimization problem of neural networks, including the work of Haeffele and Vidal, and Janzamin et al.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global optimality for a class of neural networks. The method requires that the data is nonnegative, which is true for all sorts of count data such as images, word frequencies, etc. The condition that guarantees global optimality depends on the parameters of the architecture of the network and boils down to the computation of the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. The authors demonstrate that this condition can be checked without running the algorithm.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to achieving global optimality for neural networks, its simplicity of preconditions, and its potential applicability to networks of arbitrary depth. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed algorithm on small real-world datasets.

However, the paper also has some limitations. The proposed network has limited applications due to the constraints on the input data, network parameters, and activation functions. The fact that network parameters are positive might limit the expressiveness of the model, and the requirement that all activation functions of all hidden layers are different is unnatural. Additionally, the authors note that increasing the dimension of the input and network parameters may require decreasing the weights of linear units, which could limit the expressiveness of the network.

In terms of the paper's quality, it is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed theory and algorithm, and they demonstrate its effectiveness on small real-world datasets. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the relationship between the spectral radius and the parameters of the network.

Overall, this is an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and the paper should be accepted. However, the authors should be aware of the limitations of their proposed method and consider addressing these limitations in future work.

Minor comments:

* The authors could provide more details on how the spectral radius is affected by the parameters of the network, such as p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters.
* The authors could empirically plot the relationship between the spectral radius and these parameters on a synthetic dataset.
* The authors could discuss the potential applications of their proposed method and how it could be used in practice.
* The authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of their proposed algorithm and how it compares to other optimization methods.
