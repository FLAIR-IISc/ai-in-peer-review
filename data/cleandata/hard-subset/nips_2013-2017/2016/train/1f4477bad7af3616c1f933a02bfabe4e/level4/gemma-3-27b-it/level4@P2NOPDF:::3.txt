This paper presents a compelling theoretical result demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks possesses a globally optimal solution achievable through linear convergence. The key constraint is that input data and the weights of linear layers must be positive, and activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The theory is initially proven for single-hidden-layer networks and then extended to deeper architectures. The authors also introduce a novel optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experimental results demonstrate effective performance on a small synthetic 2D dataset and reasonable results on several small, real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The strengths of this work are significant. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the first to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior work offering convergence guarantees often requires complex and impractical precondition checks, sometimes even depending on the uncheckable data-generating measure. In contrast, the preconditions presented here are comparatively simple, relying solely on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix comprising the network's parameters. Furthermore, the authors successfully extend their theoretical framework to networks of arbitrary depth and validate it with results on small, real-world datasets.

However, the practical applicability of the proposed network remains limited. The theory imposes several constraints: positive input data and network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (as specified in Theorem 1) less than one. While the non-negativity constraint on input data is likely surmountable – most data can be shifted to satisfy this condition – the requirement for positive network parameters may restrict the model’s expressive power. Similarly, the stipulation that each hidden layer employs a *different* activation function feels unnatural and could further constrain the network’s applicability. 

A critical concern arises from the authors’ observation that satisfying the spectral radius constraint necessitates increasing p1 and p2 and decreasing pw and pu, effectively diminishing the weights of the linear units. My understanding is that this relationship scales unfavorably with input and network dimensionality; higher dimensionality requires proportionally smaller linear weights. When these linear weights become exceedingly small, the activation functions effectively behave linearly. Consequently, adding layers simply introduces additional linear transformations, offering no increase in representational capacity. This raises questions about the method's scalability to deep architectures. The increasing constraint on weight magnitude with increasing dimensionality may fundamentally limit the benefits of depth.

Despite these concerns, this work represents an encouraging initial step. The theoretical results are noteworthy, and the paper’s contribution to understanding convergence properties in neural networks is valuable. I recommend acceptance.

I have a few minor comments for the authors’ consideration. I am interested in understanding the relationship between the parameters p1, p2, pw, pu, the input dimensionality, and the network parameter size on the spectral radius, ρ(A). Specifically, how does ρ(A) scale with increasing input dimensionality – linearly, exponentially, or otherwise? And, correspondingly, what is the required scaling of pw and pu to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical investigation plotting this relationship on synthetic data could provide valuable insights.
