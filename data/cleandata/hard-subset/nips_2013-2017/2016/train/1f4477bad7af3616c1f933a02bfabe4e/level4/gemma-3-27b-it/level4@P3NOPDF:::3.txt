This paper presents a compelling theoretical result demonstrating global optimality and linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The key constraints are positivity of both input data and linear layer weights, coupled with the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The authors first establish this theory for single-hidden-layer networks and then extend it to architectures with multiple hidden layers. They also introduce a novel optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods, and validate its efficacy on a small synthetic 2D dataset and several small UCI datasets, achieving reasonable performance.

**Strengths:** This work represents a significant advance, offering, to the best of my knowledge, the first potentially practical neural network with provable global optimality *and* a convergence rate guarantee. Prior work guaranteeing convergence often relies on preconditions that are difficult or even impossible to verify in practice, frequently depending on the underlying data-generating distribution. In contrast, the preconditions presented here – based on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix representing network parameters – are comparatively straightforward to check. The authors also successfully demonstrate the applicability of their theory to networks of arbitrary depth and provide initial empirical results on real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical elegance, the practical applicability of the proposed framework remains limited. The requirement for positive input data, positive network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius less than 1 (as stated in Theorem 1) imposes significant restrictions. While the positivity constraint on input data can often be addressed via a simple shift, the requirement for positive weights potentially limits model expressiveness. Furthermore, the stipulation that all hidden layers employ *different* activation functions appears unnatural and could further restrict applicability. 

My primary concern relates to the implications of the spectral radius constraint. The authors note that satisfying this constraint necessitates adjusting parameters *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, and *pu*, effectively reducing the magnitude of the linear weights.  If I understand correctly, higher input dimensionality and larger network parameter sizes require proportionally greater reductions in these weights to maintain a spectral radius below one.  As linear weights diminish, the activation functions increasingly approximate linear behavior. Consequently, adding more layers effectively becomes equivalent to stacking linear functions, potentially negating the benefits of increased network depth and limiting expressive power. This raises questions about the scalability of the method to deeper architectures. 

**Minor Comments & Questions:**

I am curious about the precise relationship between the parameters *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, *pu*, input dimensionality, network size, and the spectral radius ρ(*A*). For example, how does ρ(*A*) scale with increasing input dimensionality – linearly or exponentially? And, correspondingly, would satisfying the spectral radius constraint require linear or exponential adjustments to *pw* and *pu*? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical investigation of this relationship on synthetic data, perhaps through plotting, could provide valuable insight.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, this paper presents an encouraging initial contribution to the field. The theoretical results are novel and significant, offering a rare guarantee of global optimality and convergence. While practical application is currently restricted, the work provides a valuable stepping stone towards developing neural networks with more robust theoretical foundations. I believe this paper warrants acceptance.
