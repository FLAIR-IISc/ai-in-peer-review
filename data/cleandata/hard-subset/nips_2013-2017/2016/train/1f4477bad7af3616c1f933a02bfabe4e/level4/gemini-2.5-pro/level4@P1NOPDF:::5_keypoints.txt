- Paper defines a class of feedforward networks with a guaranteed unique global optimum and presents an algorithm with a proven linear convergence rate; mathematical derivations are generally instructive.  
- Clarification requested: Theorem 3 uses first-order derivative inequalities whereas Theorem 4 advances to second-order derivatives—explain the reasoning for this transition.  
- Clarification requested: In G^Φ (3) and elsewhere, is the gradient with respect to the final-layer matrix u taken with respect to a vectorized form of u, and does the ball constraint apply to the vectorized u?  
- Major concern: The objective maximizes the L1-norm of connection parameters, which contradicts standard practice of minimizing parameter norms (weight decay) to prevent overfitting.  
- The use of an arbitrarily small epsilon and norm maximization appears to be a theoretical device to force strictly positive gradients (V_++), but lacks practical justification.  
- Strong model constraints (non-negative inputs, all-positive weights) ensure monotone non-decreasing activations; the practical implications and limitations of this restriction are not sufficiently discussed.  
- Applicability concern: It is unclear how the model applies to real-world datasets that do not naturally satisfy non-negativity and positivity constraints.  
- Experimental concern: Evaluation compares only to SVMs; more relevant baselines would include standard neural networks (e.g., ReLU-based architectures).  
- Unclear what practical advantages the proposed model offers over conventional neural networks that, despite nonconvexity, typically perform well in practice.
