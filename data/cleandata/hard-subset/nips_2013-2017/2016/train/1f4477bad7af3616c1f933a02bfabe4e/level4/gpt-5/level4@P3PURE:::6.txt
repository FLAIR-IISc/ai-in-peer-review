Summary
The paper proposes a method to train—under certain assumptions—a specific type of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate applicability on a selection of real-world problems. The problem is interesting, and theoretical advances in this direction are appreciated.

Main concerns
- Practical relevance and expressiveness: The paper falls short of convincing the reader that the proposed class of neural networks is useful in practice. Since the expressiveness of the restricted model is raised twice in the paper, the authors should add a clear discussion of what their approach can and cannot do, and what is out of reach given the stated restrictions.
- Relation to concurrent work: Concurrent approaches should be discussed and contrasted. This applies to both other training methods for neural networks and alternative algorithmic paradigms.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments do not clarify the limits of applicability of the model. The comparison is only against some SVMs, ignoring other neural-network training methods and other paradigms. I would also have expected information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms evaluated.

Clarity and presentation
- The introduction is well written, but the presentation thereafter could be improved. In particular, Section 2 does not read fluently.
- The loss function is straightforward, but the function Φ lacks motivation. The role of the ε term, which was explained in the rebuttal, should be added to the paper.
- Theorem 1 should be explained informally before being stated. As written, the statement is hard to parse.
- Immediately below Theorem 1: the very general statement that “the nonlinear spectral method … converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum” was explained in the rebuttal to stem from empirical observation; this should be made explicit in the paper.

Minor issues and typos
- Line 60: “polyomial” → “polynomial”.
- Line 67: “one CAN model” (as written) is unclear; please correct or clarify.
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced.
- Line 67: R_{++} is not introduced, which is unfortunate since several other definitions point to it. Thanks for clarifying this in the rebuttal; I had not seen this notation before.
