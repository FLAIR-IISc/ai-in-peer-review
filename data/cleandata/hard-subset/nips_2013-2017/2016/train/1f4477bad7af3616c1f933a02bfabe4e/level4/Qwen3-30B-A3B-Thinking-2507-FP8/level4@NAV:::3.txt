

This paper presents a novel theoretical contribution demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves a global optimum under linear convergence, with the key constraint requiring all input data, linear layer weights, and activation functions to be generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently extended to multi-hidden-layer architectures. The authors propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods. Experimental results indicate strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on select UCI benchmark datasets.  

Pros: This work represents the first practical neural network framework with provable global optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior methods requiring convergence guarantees often necessitate complex, impractical preconditions dependent on the unknown data-generating measure. In contrast, the proposed theory relies on simple preconditions—specifically, the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix derived from network parameters being less than 1. The authors successfully demonstrate applicability to arbitrarily deep networks and achieve meaningful results on real-world datasets.  

Cons: The theoretical constraints significantly limit practical applicability. The requirement for non-negative inputs (which may be mitigated via data shifting) and strictly positive weights restricts model expressiveness. Furthermore, mandating distinct activation functions across all hidden layers is unnatural and potentially restrictive. Critically, satisfying the spectral radius condition necessitates increasing parameters p₁ and p₂ while decreasing weights p_w and p_u, effectively scaling down linear layer weights. As input dimensionality and network depth increase, this scaling forces weights toward zero, causing activations to approximate linearity. In such regimes, deeper architectures merely compose linear transformations, failing to enhance expressiveness. Consequently, I remain concerned about the scalability of this approach to deep networks despite its promising initial results.  

Despite these limitations, the work constitutes a valuable theoretical advance. I recommend acceptance.  

Minor comment: The relationship between parameters (p₁, p₂, p_w, p_u), input dimensionality, network size, and the spectral radius ρ(A) merits further exploration. Specifically, how does ρ(A) scale with input dimension (e.g., linearly or exponentially), and what scaling (linear or exponential) of p_w/p_u is required to maintain ρ(A) < 1? While a theoretical proof may be challenging, an empirical analysis on synthetic data could provide useful insights.
