

This paper investigates a restricted class of feedforward neural networks that achieve global optimality with linear convergence rates via a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM). The approach is applied to one- and two-hidden-layer deep networks, with experiments on standard UCI datasets. However, the core contribution faces significant limitations due to restrictive assumptions and unconvincing empirical validation.  

The authors' network class requires non-negative weights and maximizes weight regularization—a counterintuitive constraint that lacks justification for real-world applicability. Furthermore, the method necessitates a niche activation function (generalized polynomial) to satisfy the optimality condition. These assumptions are overly restrictive and poorly motivated. The authors claim the class retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries, yet the empirical results fail to substantiate this. Crucially, the comparisons against baselines are flawed: linear SVMs (a linear model) are used as baselines for NLSM (a nonlinear model), despite NLSM tuning network structures while SVMs remain unoptimized. This is inherently unfair; a nonlinear SVM (e.g., with polynomial or RBF kernels) would provide a meaningful comparison. The observed performance gaps (e.g., linear SVM outperforming NLSM without tuning) do not demonstrate NLSM’s expressive power but rather highlight the unsuitable baseline choice.  

The experimental design compounds these issues. UCI datasets used are small in both sample size and feature dimensionality, which contradicts the paper’s claim of applicability to complex real-world problems. The authors note that spectral radius bounds for matrix *A* grow with hidden units, rendering the method impractical for high-dimensional data—a critical flaw for most modern applications. The paper offers no solution to this scalability barrier, severely limiting the method’s utility.  

Theoretical concerns further undermine the work. The optimality condition appears fragile: it may not hold for any practical parameter settings, and verifying it requires checking an enormous parameter space without prior knowledge. The authors acknowledge this but provide no efficient method to identify valid parameter subsets. Additionally, the role of the objective function in enabling global optimality is unclear. If the special objective is solely responsible, the method’s generality is questionable. It would be valuable to test whether stochastic gradient descent (SGD) converges to the global optimum on these datasets—this could clarify whether the spectral method’s advantage is algorithmic or inherent to the problem structure.  

Key questions remain unresolved:  
1. How can the spectral radius bound be managed for high-dimensional data?  
2. Are there efficient ways to identify valid parameter sets without exhaustive search?  
3. Does the objective function’s design (beyond the activation and weight constraints) drive the global optimality?  
4. Why does linear SVM outperform NLSM without tuning, given NLSM’s nonlinearity?  

The paper’s contribution is theoretically interesting but lacks practical relevance due to its restrictive assumptions, unconvincing experiments, and unaddressed scalability issues. The authors should: (i) justify the non-negativity and generalized polynomial constraints with real-world motivation or application examples; (ii) replace linear SVM with a nonlinear baseline; (iii) test on larger datasets; and (iv) address the spectral radius scalability problem. Without these, the claim that the studied class "still has enough expressive power" remains unsupported. The current work serves more as a theoretical curiosity than a practical advancement for neural network training.
