Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations and nonnegative weights. The core idea is to reformulate training as a maximization over a positive cone of a modified objective that adds a small positive term and the sum of outputs to the negative cross-entropy loss. Leveraging nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps, the authors construct a fixed-point mapping GΦ whose unique fixed point corresponds to the global maximizer, and they establish a linear convergence rate under a verifiable spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 on a small nonnegative matrix A derived from the architecture and norm parameters. They provide results for one and two hidden layers and show proof-of-concept experiments on several UCI datasets, comparing against SVMs and ReLU networks trained with SGD.

The work connects to recent efforts on provable training of neural networks, e.g., Haeffele and Vidal (matrix/tensor factorization view, but difficult to apply in practice), and Janzamin et al. (tensor methods requiring score function tensors and non-degeneracy conditions). Compared to these, the present method trades off modeling flexibility for a simpler, checkable condition and an explicit iterative scheme with linear convergence.

Strengths:
- A clear theoretical framework that ties the optimization problem to fixed-point theory on cones, yielding a checkable global optimality condition and linear convergence.
- The global optimality guarantee applies to nontrivial network architectures (one and two hidden layers) with generalized polynomial activations.
- The spectral radius condition depends only on architecture and norm parameters and can be verified before training.
- The algorithm is parameter-free with respect to learning rate and empirically converges quickly.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Expressiveness and practical utility are under-explored. The imposed constraints (nonnegative data and weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, output-sum regularization, and large p-norm parameters to secure ρ(A) < 1) raise questions about what functions can be represented and which problems are out of reach. Since the paper itself flags expressiveness concerns, it should include a more systematic discussion (e.g., classes of decision boundaries supported under nonnegativity and normalization, limitations from requiring distinct α, sensitivity to scaling of inputs impacting ρ(A), and the trade-off between increasing p to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 and the resulting geometry of the optimization).
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small, low-dimensional datasets and a narrow set of baselines. Relying primarily on SVM and simple ReLU/SGD comparisons does not fully clarify when the model is competitive. Additional baselines (e.g., logistic regression, tree ensembles, kernel logistic regression, nonnegative matrix factorization methods, or alternative globally convergent/convex surrogates) and ablations (e.g., impact of α choices, role of the output-sum term, sensitivity to data normalization) would better reveal applicability limits.
- Computational aspects are not sufficiently discussed. Please report per-iteration cost, wall-clock convergence vs. SGD, and the overhead of forming A and computing ρ(A) relative to dataset size and network width.
- Clarity issues in the presentation, especially around Section 2, hinder readability. Theorem 1 is difficult to parse without an informal explanation and a roadmap tying assumptions to the spectral radius condition.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears technically sound, and the fixed-point argument is well-motivated. However, several definitions and notational choices make the exposition harder than necessary (see detailed comments). The empirical section demonstrates feasibility but is too small to substantiate practical significance.

Clarity:
The introduction is strong; later sections could be improved for flow and accessibility:
- Motivate Φ explicitly when it is introduced, not only later. In particular, explain the need for the output-sum term and the small ε term (strict positivity of gradients on the cone boundary) in the main text.
- Provide a high-level, informal explanation of Theorem 1 before the formal statement, including an intuitive interpretation of ρ(A) < 1 and how architectural parameters influence it.
- Clarify the meaning and role of all symbols at first use (e.g., R++, pw, pu), and ensure consistent notation throughout.

Originality:
To my knowledge, this is the first practically implementable globally optimal training scheme for this network class, with a simple spectral condition and linear convergence. The approach is original and interesting, though the modeling constraints are restrictive.

Significance:
Conceptually significant for the theory of provable neural network training. Practical impact is less clear due to modeling constraints and limited empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and rigorous framework yielding a globally optimal algorithm with linear convergence.
- Simple, verifiable condition for global optimality, independent of data distributional assumptions beyond nonnegativity.
- Extends to two hidden layers and suggests a path to deeper architectures.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of the model’s expressive power and applicability; unclear practical scope.
- Small-scale experiments and limited baselines; no systematic analysis of computational costs.
- Exposition issues (Section 2, Theorem 1 presentation) and missing definitions hinder accessibility.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a dedicated subsection on expressiveness: characterize functions representable under nonnegativity and generalized polynomial activations; discuss the necessity of distinct α and its implications; analyze how ρ(A) scales with n1, norms, and input scaling, and how choosing large p affects optimization geometry.
- Expand empirical evaluation: include more baselines and ablations; report computational resources and convergence timing; test sensitivity to α, normalization, and the output-sum term.
- Clearly state that the “few (<10) iterations” claim reflects empirical observations; distinguish theory (linear rate with constant ρ(A)) from practice.
- Provide an informal walkthrough of Theorem 1 and a diagram/table summarizing how to compute A, check ρ(A), and choose pw, pu.
- Improve notation and definitions at first use; streamline Section 2 for readability.

Minor issues and typos:
- Clarify and motivate Φ when introduced; include the explanation for the ε term in the main text.
- Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation and intuition. The statement is currently hard to parse.
- After Theorem 1: explicitly state that the “few iterations” remark is an empirical observation.
- Line 60: “polyomial” → “polynomial.”
- Line 67: “one CAN model” → likely “one can model.”
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced (if these refer to pw and pu, please define them on first use).
- R++ is not introduced early; define R+ and R++ before first use, given other definitions point to them.
