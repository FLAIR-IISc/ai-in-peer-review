This paper introduces a compelling theoretical framework demonstrating global optimality and linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The key requirement for this guarantee is positivity of both input data and linear layer weights, coupled with the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The theory is initially established for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently generalized to deeper architectures. Accompanying this theory, the authors propose a novel optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experimental results on a small synthetic dataset and several small UCI datasets demonstrate promising performance. 

Strengths of this work include its novelty – to the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the first to propose a neural network with both practical potential and theoretical convergence guarantees. Existing convergence guarantees often rely on preconditions that are difficult or even impossible to verify in practice, frequently depending on the underlying data distribution. In contrast, the preconditions presented here are comparatively straightforward, hinging on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix derived from the network’s parameters. The theory’s applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and its validation on real-world datasets further contribute to its significance.

However, the proposed network architecture currently exhibits limitations in its applicability. The theory necessitates positive input data, positive network parameters, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (as defined in Theorem 1) less than one. While the positivity constraint on input data is unlikely to be overly restrictive due to the possibility of data shifting, the requirement for positive network parameters may limit model expressiveness. Furthermore, the stipulation that each hidden layer employs a *different* activation function feels somewhat artificial and could hinder broader application.  A critical concern arises from the authors’ observation that satisfying the spectral radius constraint requires adjustments to parameters *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, and *pu* that effectively reduce the magnitude of linear weights.  My understanding is that this reduction becomes more pronounced with increasing input dimensionality and network parameter size.  When linear weights become sufficiently small, the activation functions effectively behave linearly, rendering the addition of further layers redundant and potentially diminishing the network’s representational capacity. This raises questions about the scalability of the method to truly deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this work represents a valuable initial contribution, and I recommend acceptance. 

Minor Comment: I am interested in understanding the relationship between the parameters *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, *pu*, input dimensionality, network size, and the spectral radius *ρ(A)*. Specifically, how does *ρ(A)* scale with input dimension – linearly or exponentially? And, correspondingly, what is the required scaling of *pw* and *pu* to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical investigation of this relationship on synthetic data could be insightful.
