- Presents a theoretical result that a specific class of neural networks has a globally optimal solution achievable with linear convergence under certain constraints.
- Key constraints: input data and linear-layer weights must be positive, and activation functions must be generalized polynomials.
- Theory is proven for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to deeper architectures.
- Introduces a novel optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.
- Experiments show strong performance on a small synthetic 2D dataset and reasonable results on several small UCI datasets.
- Major strength: possibly the first practically usable neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees.
- Preconditions are simpler than prior work, relying on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix of network parameters.
- Practical limitations: positive-weight constraint may restrict expressiveness; requiring each hidden layer to use a different activation is unnatural.
- Critical concern: satisfying the spectral-radius constraint requires increasing p1/p2 and decreasing pw/pu, effectively shrinking linear weights.
- Shrinking linear weights can make activations behave linearly, so added depth yields only additional linear transforms, undermining representational gains.
- Scalability issue: the required reduction in linear weight magnitudes appears to worsen with input/network dimensionality, potentially limiting depth benefits.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance but requests investigation (analytical or empirical) of how ρ(A) scales with input dimensionality and how pw/pu must scale to compensate.
