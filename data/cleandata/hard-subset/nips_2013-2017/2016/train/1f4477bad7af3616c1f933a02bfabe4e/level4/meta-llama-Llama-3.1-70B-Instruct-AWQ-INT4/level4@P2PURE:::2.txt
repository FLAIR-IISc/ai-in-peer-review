This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors apply this method to deep networks with one- and two-hidden layers and conduct experiments on several real-world datasets. However, the class of feedforward neural networks studied is restrictive, imposing non-negativity on the network weights and maximizing their regularization, which may not be reasonable assumptions. Furthermore, the requirement of a generalized polynomial activation function for optimality conditions is not well-justified. Instead of making more restrictive assumptions to achieve global optimality, the authors could provide more detailed explanations from the perspectives of applications or real-world datasets.

The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks still possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance. Nevertheless, the empirical experimental results do not strongly support this claim. Notably, linear SVM performs better than NLSM1 and NLSM2, even when linear SVM is not properly tuned, while NLSM tunes its network structure. It is surprising that a linear SVM is used as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear SVM such as polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel, making the comparisons unfair and the conclusions unconvincing. In other words, these results do not demonstrate the sufficient expressive power of the studied class of neural networks.

The experiments use datasets from the UCI repository, which are too small in terms of both sample size and feature dimensionality. The analysis shows that the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which is problematic for high-dimensional data. It is essential to address this issue, as it may limit the applicability of this method to a large proportion of applications. The optimal condition is not guaranteed by any setting of parameters, and although it can be checked without running the neural network, it remains problematic when there are no priors or an extremely large number of possible parameters. A fast approach to obtaining a small set of valid candidates would be beneficial. It is also unclear whether the optimal property of the studied model stems from the special objective function. It would be interesting to investigate whether stochastic gradient descent can also converge to the global optimum empirically on some tested datasets.
