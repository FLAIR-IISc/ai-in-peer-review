This paper presents a compelling theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks possesses a globally optimal solution achievable with a linear convergence rate. The key constraint is that all input data and the weights of the linear layers must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The authors first prove this theory for single-hidden-layer networks and then extend it to networks with multiple hidden layers. They also propose an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method. Experiments show promising results on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable performance on several small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

**Pros:** As the authors claim, this is the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Previous work offering convergence guarantees often requires complicated and impractical precondition checks – some even depend on the data-generating measure, making them impossible to verify in practice. The theory presented here has comparatively simple preconditions, relying only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix comprised of the network’s parameters. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate the theory's applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and achieve reasonable results on small, real-world datasets.

**Cons:** The proposed network currently has limited applicability. The theory necessitates positive input data and network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (as defined in Theorem 1) less than 1. While the non-negativity constraint on input data shouldn’t significantly limit potential use – most training data can be easily shifted to be entirely positive – requiring positive network parameters may restrict the model’s expressiveness. Additionally, the requirement for distinct activation functions in each hidden layer feels unnatural and could further limit the network’s application. Finally, the authors note that satisfying the spectral radius constraint requires increasing *p1* and *p2* while decreasing *pw* and *pu*, effectively reducing the weights of the linear units. If I understand correctly, higher input dimensions and larger network parameter sizes necessitate smaller linear weights to maintain the spectral radius constraint. Given that very small linear weights cause the activation functions to behave almost linearly, adding more layers in this scenario simply amounts to stacking additional linear functions, which won’t increase the network's expressive power. Consequently, I am somewhat concerned that scaling this method to very deep architectures might prove difficult.

Despite these concerns, this remains an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and I believe this paper should be accepted.

**Minor Comments:** I am curious about how *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, *pu*, the input dimension, and the network parameter size influence the spectral radius, *ρ(A)*. For instance, does *ρ(A)* increase linearly or exponentially with increasing input dimension? And, to counteract this effect, should *pw* and *pu* be decreased linearly or exponentially? I understand that a formal proof might be challenging, but it might be possible to empirically plot this relationship using synthetic data.
