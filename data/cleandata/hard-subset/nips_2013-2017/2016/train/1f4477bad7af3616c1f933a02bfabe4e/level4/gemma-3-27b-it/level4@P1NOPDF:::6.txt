The paper proposes a method to train – under certain assumptions – a specific type of feed-forward neural network. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate the method’s applicability to a selection of “real-world” problems. I find the problem investigated interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is valuable. However, the paper falls somewhat short of convincingly demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed class of neural networks. Specifically, the authors acknowledge the limitations in the expressiveness of their restricted model (doing so twice), and I recommend adding a discussion of potential workarounds and inherent limitations of their approach. Furthermore, concurrent work should be discussed and contrasted with the proposed method.

The experimental results also do not clarify the applicability limitations of the model. I was surprised to find the authors only compared their approach to SVMs, omitting other relevant methods – both alternative training techniques for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. I would also expect some discussion of the computational resources required by each of the three algorithms. 

The introduction is well-written, but the presentation could be improved overall. Section 2, in particular, does not read fluently. There are a few typos throughout the paper, which I’ve listed below. 

Regarding specific remarks: While the definition of the loss function is straightforward, the function $\Phi$ lacks sufficient motivation. The role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal, and I believe this explanation should be incorporated into the paper. Theorem 1 should be explained informally before being formally stated, as the statement itself is difficult to parse. Immediately following Theorem 1, the authors state that "the nonlinear spectral method […] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum." This claim, as explained in the rebuttal, stems from experimental observation and should be clearly stated as such.

Specific typos/clarifications:

* Line 60: “polyomial” should be “polynomial.”
* Line 67: “one CAN model” should be “one can model.”
* Line 67: The variables p1 and p2 are not introduced before being used.
* Line 67: $R_{++}$ is not introduced, which is unfortunate given that several other variable definitions refer to it. Thank you for clarifying this notation in the rebuttal; I had not encountered it previously.
