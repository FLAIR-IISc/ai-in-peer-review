The paper proposes a method for training a specific type of feed-forward neural network under certain assumptions. The authors provide a proof of optimal convergence and demonstrate the applicability of their method to a selection of real-world problems. The problem under investigation is intriguing, and any theoretical advancements in this area are welcome. However, the paper falls short in convincing the reader of the practical utility of the proposed class of neural networks. 

In particular, the authors raise concerns about the expressiveness of their restricted model, but they do not provide sufficient discussion on what can be achieved and what lies beyond the capabilities of their approach. A comparison with concurrent approaches would also be beneficial. The experimental results do not clarify the limitations of the applicability of their model either. Upon reviewing the experimental results, I was surprised to find that the authors only compare their approach to SVM and neglect other methods, including alternative training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Furthermore, I would have expected some information about the computational resources required by the three algorithms.

The introduction is well-written, but the subsequent presentation could be improved. Specifically, Section 2 lacks fluency. There are some typos, which I have listed below. Some specific remarks: The definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the function Φ lacks motivation. Although the role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal, I believe this explanation should be included in the paper as well. Theorem 1 should be informally explained before being stated, as the statement itself is difficult to parse. The authors mentioned in the rebuttal that the general statement "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum," immediately following Theorem 1, is based on their experimental observations. This should be clarified in the paper. 

Additionally, I found the following errors: "polyomial" (line 60), "one CAN model" (line 67), and "p1 and p2" are not introduced (line 67). The notation "R_{++}" is not introduced, which is unfortunate since it is referenced in the definitions of other variables. Thankfully, the authors clarified this in the rebuttal, as I was unfamiliar with this notation before.
