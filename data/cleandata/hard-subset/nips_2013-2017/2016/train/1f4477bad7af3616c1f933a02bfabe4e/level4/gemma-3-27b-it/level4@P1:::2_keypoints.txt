- Studies feedforward nets trainable to a global optimum with linear convergence via a novel nonlinear spectral method.
- Method applied to one- and two-hidden-layer networks and evaluated on real-world datasets.
- Key idea: enforce nonnegative weights and optimize a modified objective (loss plus negative total outputs).
- Authors acknowledge constraints are restrictive but claim models remain sufficiently expressive.
- Builds on prior global-optimum work [8,11] but offers a practically feasible algorithm with guaranteed convergence.
- Requires only nonnegative inputs and a pre-training spectral-radius check of a small matrix.
- Theoretical novelty: global optimality proofs and linear convergence rate vs. SGD’s lack of guarantees.
- Provides explicit spectral-radius conditions to assess architecture suitability.
- Initial empirical validation on benchmark datasets demonstrates empirical viability.
- Major weakness: nonnegative-weight assumption is a strong restriction limiting applicability.
- Experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate expressive power under the constraints.
- Choice of baselines is questionable—comparison to a linear SVM is inadequate; nonlinear SVM (e.g., RBF) would be more relevant.
- Datasets used are small (UCI); scalability to high-dimensional data is unclear and complexity grows with hidden units.
- Current spectral-radius bounds appear conservative; tighter bounds could widen applicability.
- The relationship between the modified objective and achieved optimality is not fully explored; SGD behavior under similar conditions is untested.
- Proposed method for selecting valid candidates lacks discussion on efficient search over potentially large parameter space.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel results, clear optimality conditions, initial empirical support, tackles a core DL training challenge.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, unconvincing experiments, limited scalability, and insufficient analysis of the objective’s role.
- Overall recommendation: significant theoretical contribution but practical limitations; accept with minor revisions to strengthen experiments and scalability discussion.
