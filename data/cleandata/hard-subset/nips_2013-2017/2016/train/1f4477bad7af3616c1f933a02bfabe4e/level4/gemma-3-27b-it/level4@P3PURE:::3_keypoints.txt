- Presents a theoretical result: a class of neural nets has a globally optimal solution with linear convergence under certain conditions.
- Key constraints: input data and linear-layer weights must be positive; activation functions are generalized polynomials.
- Theory proven for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to arbitrarily deep networks.
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method.
- Experiments show strong performance on a small synthetic 2D dataset and reasonable results on several small UCI datasets.
- Major strength: first potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees under comparatively simple preconditions.
- Simpler preconditions rely only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative parameter-derived matrix, avoiding complex prior checks.
- Applicability concern: requirement of positive network parameters may restrict model expressiveness.
- Architectural oddity: requiring all activation functions in each hidden layer to be distinct is unnatural and may hinder application.
- Spectral-radius constraint (ρ(A) < 1) forces adjusting p1, p2, pw, pu, which can shrink linear weights and reduce expressive power as depth and dimension grow.
- Scalability concern: as input dimension and parameter size increase, necessary weight scaling may make added layers effectively linear, limiting depth benefits.
- Minor/question: unclear how ρ(A) scales with input dimension and network size and whether pw and pu must be decreased linearly or exponentially; suggests empirical exploration.
