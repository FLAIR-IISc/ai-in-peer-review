

This paper presents a nonlinear spectral method for achieving globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks with a linear convergence rate. The authors apply their approach to networks with one and two hidden layers and demonstrate empirical results on several UCI datasets. While the theoretical contribution is novel, the class of networks studied imposes restrictive assumptions that warrant careful consideration. The requirement of non-negative weights and the use of generalized polynomial activation functions (with real powers) are unconventional and not well-justified from an application perspective. The authors claim these networks can model complex decision boundaries, but the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. Specifically, the comparison with linear SVM as a baseline is problematic since NLSM is a nonlinear model, and a nonlinear SVM (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels) would be a more appropriate comparison. In fact, linear SVM achieves better or comparable performance to NLSM1/NLSM2 across most datasets, even without tuning, suggesting the proposed method may not offer clear advantages over standard approaches. The UCI datasets used are relatively small in both sample size and dimensionality, limiting the generalizability of the results. The authors note that the spectral radius condition for global optimality becomes increasingly difficult to satisfy as the number of hidden units grows, raising concerns about scalability to high-dimensional datasets. While the condition can be checked without running the algorithm, it is unclear how practitioners would efficiently identify valid parameter configurations given the potentially huge parameter space. It would be valuable to discuss whether the global optimality property is fundamentally tied to the special objective function used in the method or if similar guarantees could be achieved with more standard loss functions. Finally, it would be interesting to empirically test whether standard stochastic gradient descent methods can achieve comparable performance on these datasets when properly tuned, which would help contextualize the advantages of the proposed approach.
