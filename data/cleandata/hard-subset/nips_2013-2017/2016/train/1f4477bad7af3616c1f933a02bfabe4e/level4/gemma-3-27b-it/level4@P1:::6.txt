The paper proposes a method to train – under certain assumptions – a specific type of feed-forward neural network. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate that their method can be applied to a selection of real-world problems. The problem under investigation is interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated. However, the paper falls a bit short of convincingly demonstrating that the proposed class of neural networks is practically useful. In particular, the authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model (and do so twice); I would recommend adding some discussion about what can be achieved and what is out of reach for their approach. Furthermore, concurrent approaches should be discussed and contrasted. The experimental results do not clarify the limitations of the applicability of their model either. When reading the experimental results, I was surprised that the authors only compare their approach to SVMs and omit other methods – both other training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Also, I would have expected some information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms. The introduction is well-written, but the presentation subsequently has room for improvement. In particular, Section 2 does not read fluently.

**Summary of the paper:**

The authors address the challenging non-convex optimization problem inherent in training neural networks. They present a nonlinear spectral method that, under relatively weak data assumptions, guarantees global optimality with a linear convergence rate for a specific class of feedforward neural networks: generalized polynomial neural networks. This represents a significant advance, as existing methods often struggle to find global optima. While the method’s applicability is currently limited to networks with one or two hidden layers, the authors demonstrate its effectiveness on several real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical guarantee of global optimality, which is a major contribution.
*   **Linear Convergence:** The linear convergence rate offers practical advantages over methods that may converge slowly or get stuck in local optima.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods for neural network training is a novel and potentially impactful direction.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors validate their method on several UCI datasets, demonstrating its ability to achieve competitive performance.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of neural network training and the motivation for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Expressiveness:** The restriction to generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights might limit the model’s expressiveness compared to standard neural networks with ReLU activations. The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion of the implications would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The experimental comparison is primarily with SVMs. A broader comparison with other neural network training methods (e.g., different optimizers, network architectures) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper lacks a discussion of the computational cost associated with the nonlinear spectral method compared to other training approaches.
*   **Presentation Clarity:** Some sections, particularly Section 2, could be more clearly written for improved readability.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameters (e.g., the epsilon value, network size) on performance and convergence is not thoroughly investigated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to training neural networks with a guaranteed global optimum.
*   The linear convergence rate is a significant advantage.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical feasibility of the method.
*   The work addresses an important open problem in the field of deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limited expressiveness of the model class may restrict its applicability to real-world problems.
*   The lack of a comprehensive comparison with other methods makes it difficult to assess the method's overall performance.
*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of computational cost.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the practical limitations of the proposed method (model expressiveness, computational cost) are acknowledged, the guarantee of global optimality and the linear convergence rate are compelling. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, contingent on addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more insight into the computational cost. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
