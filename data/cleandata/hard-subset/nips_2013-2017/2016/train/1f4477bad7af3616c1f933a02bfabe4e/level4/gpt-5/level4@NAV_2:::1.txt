This paper studies a particular family of neural networks and shows that the associated optimization problem has a single stationary point. The authors present a fast algorithm that converges to the unique fixed point, which is also the global minimizer of the objective. The work tackles the interesting question of how a relatively expressive model, under carefully designed constraints, can be made to have only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (and generalizations such as geodesic convexity), a common route to establishing uniqueness is via fixed-point theory; this is the approach adopted here. The authors construct a contractive mapping whose fixed point coincides with the unique minimizer of the cost.

A limitation of the method is that the convergence guarantee depends on bounding the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix. As the authors note, this constraint is why they did not experiment with networks having larger numbers of hidden units. Another issue is the proliferation of hyperparameters that require tuning, which makes the procedure somewhat unintuitive in practice; furthermore, for each parameter choice the spectral condition must be verified. In the experiments, approximately 150,000 parameter combinations were explored and the best selected via cross-validation, yet the method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets. This outcome calls into question the practical strength of the neural architecture under consideration.
