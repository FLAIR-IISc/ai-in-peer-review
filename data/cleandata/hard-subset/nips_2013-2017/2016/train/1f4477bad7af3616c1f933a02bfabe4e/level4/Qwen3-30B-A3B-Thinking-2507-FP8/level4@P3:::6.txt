

The paper proposes a method for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method, providing theoretical guarantees for one and two hidden layer networks. The authors demonstrate that under assumptions of nonnegative data and weights (which they argue are reasonable for many applications like images and word frequencies), their approach achieves global optimality with linear convergence rate, unlike standard stochastic gradient descent methods. This work builds upon recent advances in global optimization of neural networks, such as the tensor-based approach of Janzamin et al. (2015) and the matrix factorization perspective of Haeffele and Vidal (2014), but claims to provide a more practically applicable method by avoiding the need for data density estimation and non-degeneracy conditions.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is significant: the authors provide the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks with a clear condition for global optimality (checking the spectral radius of a small matrix). This is a valuable theoretical advance in understanding neural network optimization.

2. The paper is well-structured with a clear proof strategy connecting optimization to fixed-point theory using the Banach fixed-point theorem and Thompson metric. The mathematical development is technically sound and well-justified.

3. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets compared to SVM and ReLU networks, and the method converges much faster than SGD (as shown in Figure 2), which is a practical advantage.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to adequately justify why the restricted model (with nonnegative weights and modified objective) is practically useful. The authors note the expressiveness limitations twice (in Section 1 and Section 2), but don't provide sufficient discussion about what problems are solvable with their approach versus what is out of reach. This is a significant limitation as the value of the method depends on how much the constraints impact practical performance.

2. The experimental evaluation is limited in scope. The authors only compare against SVM and ReLU networks, ignoring other neural network training methods (e.g., Adam, Adagrad) and other algorithmic paradigms. More importantly, they don't discuss computational resources (time, memory) required for their method versus alternatives, which is crucial for practical adoption.

3. The presentation of the main theorem (Theorem 1) is difficult to parse, and the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the conditions before stating the formal theorem. The role of the ε term in the objective function is also not clearly motivated in the main text.

4. The paper claims their method is "practically feasible," but the experimental results don't fully support this claim. For example, the method requires careful parameter selection (n1, α, ρw, ρu) from a large search space, and the authors don't explain how to choose these parameters for new datasets. The computational cost of checking the spectral radius condition for each model configuration is not addressed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization with global optimality guarantees.
- The method provides a concrete alternative to SGD with convergence guarantees and faster convergence in experiments.
- The technical development is sound and well-motivated.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The practical relevance of the constrained model is not convincingly demonstrated.
- The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the claim of practical feasibility.
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the method compares to standard neural network training approaches in terms of both performance and computational cost.

Recommendation: The paper presents an interesting theoretical advance that could be valuable for the community, but the practical significance is not sufficiently demonstrated. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the following:

1. Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the restricted model (nonnegative weights, modified objective) and when the approach is likely to be applicable versus when it's not.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with standard neural network training methods (e.g., Adam, SGD with optimal learning rate), and report computational costs (time, memory) for all methods.

3. Improve the presentation of Theorem 1 with a more intuitive explanation of the conditions before the formal statement, and clarify the role of the ε term.

4. Address the practical parameter selection process for new datasets, including how to choose n1, α, ρw, ρu, etc.

The paper has strong theoretical foundations but would benefit from a more thorough evaluation of practical utility. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
