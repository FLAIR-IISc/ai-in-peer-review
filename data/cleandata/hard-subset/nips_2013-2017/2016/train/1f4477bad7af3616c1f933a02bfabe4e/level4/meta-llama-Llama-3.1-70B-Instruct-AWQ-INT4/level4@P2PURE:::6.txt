The paper proposes a method for training a specific type of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions. The authors provide a proof of optimal convergence and demonstrate the applicability of their method to a selection of "real-world" problems. I find the problem under investigation to be interesting, and any theoretical advancements in this direction are appreciated. However, the paper falls short in convincingly demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed class of neural networks. 

In particular, since the authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model, I recommend adding some discussion on the capabilities and limitations of their approach. Furthermore, a comparison with concurrent approaches would be beneficial. The experimental results do not provide clarity on the limitations of the applicability of their model either. Upon reviewing the experimental results, I was surprised to find that the authors only compare their approach to SVM and neglect other methods, including alternative training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Additionally, I would have expected some information about the computational resources required by the three algorithms. 

The introduction is well-written, but the subsequent presentation could be improved. Specifically, Section 2 lacks fluency. There are some typos, which I have collected below. Some specific remarks: While the definition of the loss function is straightforward, the function Φ lacks motivation; the role of the epsilon term, explained in the rebuttal, should be added to the paper as well. Theorem 1 would benefit from an informal explanation before its formal statement, as the statement itself is hard to parse. 

The general claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum," made immediately after Theorem 1, is based on experimental observations, as explained in the rebuttal. This should be clarified in the paper. Some minor corrections: "polyomial" should be spelled as "polynomial" (line 60), "one CAN model" should be rephrased for clarity (line 67), and the variables p1 and p2 should be introduced before their use (line 67). Additionally, the notation R_{++} is not introduced, which is unfortunate since it is referenced in the definition of other variables. I appreciate the clarification provided in the rebuttal, as I was unfamiliar with this notation.
