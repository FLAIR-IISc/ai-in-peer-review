This paper presents a compelling theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks can achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The primary constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The authors first prove this theory on neural networks with a single hidden layer and then extend it to networks with multiple hidden layers. They also propose an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method. Experiments show that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The main strengths of this paper lie in its novelty and the simplicity of its preconditions. As the authors claim, this is the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with optimal and convergence guarantees. Unlike previous work with convergence guarantees, which often require complicated methods to check their preconditions, this theory has relatively simple preconditions that only depend on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of the network parameters. The authors demonstrate the applicability of their theory to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real datasets.

However, there are some limitations to the proposed network. The theory requires that both the input data and network parameters be positive, the activation functions be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) be smaller than 1. While the non-negative constraint on the input data may not significantly limit potential usage, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the positivity constraint on network parameters might limit the expressiveness of the model. Furthermore, the requirement that all activation functions of all hidden layers be different may also limit its application. Additionally, the authors mention that to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, they need to increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, effectively decreasing the weights of linear units. This raises concerns that as the dimension of the input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units may need to be decreased, potentially reducing the expressiveness of the network. Specifically, when linear weights are very small, activation functions may behave similarly to linear functions, and adding more layers may not increase the network's expressiveness.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging result as an initial attempt. The simplicity of its preconditions and the novelty of its approach make it a valuable contribution to the field.

Minor comments: It would be interesting to investigate how p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius rho(A). For instance, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially with the dimension of the input, and how should pw and pu be adjusted to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical study on synthetic data could provide valuable insights into this relationship.
