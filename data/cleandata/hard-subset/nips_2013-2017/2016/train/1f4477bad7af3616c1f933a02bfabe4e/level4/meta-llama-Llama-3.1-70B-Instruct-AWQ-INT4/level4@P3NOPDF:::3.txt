This paper presents a novel theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks can achieve a global optimum at a linear convergence rate. The primary constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The authors first prove this theory for neural networks with a single hidden layer and then extend it to networks with multiple hidden layers. They also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.

Experiments show that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets). The paper's main contribution is that it presents the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with optimal guarantees and convergence guarantees. Unlike previous work with convergence guarantees, which often require complicated methods to check preconditions that may be impossible to verify in practice due to their dependence on the data-generating measure, the theory proposed in this work has relatively simple preconditions. These preconditions only depend on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of the network's parameters.

The authors demonstrate that the proposed theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real-world datasets. However, the proposed network still has limited applications due to several constraints. The theory requires that both the input data and network parameters be positive, the activation functions be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) be smaller than 1.

While the non-negative constraint on the input data may not significantly limit the potential usage, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the requirement that network parameters be positive might limit the expressiveness of the model. Furthermore, the fact that all activation functions of all hidden layers are different may also limit its application.

The authors note that to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, they need to increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, effectively decreasing the weights of linear units. Additionally, as the dimension of the input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units must be decreased to satisfy the spectral radius constraint. This decrease in weights may cause the activation functions to behave similarly to linear functions, which could limit the expressiveness of the network, especially when adding more layers.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and I believe it should be accepted.

Minor comments:

It would be interesting to investigate how p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius rho(A). For instance, when the dimension of the input increases, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially? To counteract this effect, should pw and pu be decreased linearly or exponentially? While a formal proof may be challenging, it might be possible to empirically plot this relationship using synthetic data.
