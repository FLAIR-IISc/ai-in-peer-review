This paper explores a specific class of neural networks, demonstrating that it possesses only one stationary point. The author introduces a fast method that converges to this unique fixed-point, which is also the global minimum of the cost function. The investigation of a relatively complex model with carefully chosen constraints that yields a single stationary point is an intriguing problem. Beyond convexity and its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity, the common approach to proving the uniqueness of the stationary point is through fixed-point theory, which is the method employed in this paper. By constructing a contractive map, the author shows that its fixed-point corresponds to the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One limitation of the proposed method is its reliance on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for convergence proof. As acknowledged by the authors, this constraint is the reason they refrained from experimenting with networks featuring a larger number of hidden units. Another issue with the method is the presence of numerous parameters that require tuning, rendering the procedure somewhat unintuitive. Furthermore, for each parameter configuration, the spectral condition must be verified. In the experimental results, the authors evaluated approximately 150,000 different combinations of parameter values and selected the best-performing configuration based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed linear SVM in only 2 out of 7 datasets, which raises questions about the true capabilities of the investigated neural network.

The author's approach to demonstrating the uniqueness of the stationary point in a specific class of neural networks is a valuable contribution to the field. Nevertheless, the proposed method's limitations, including its dependence on the spectral radius and the need for extensive parameter tuning, detract from its overall impact. The experimental results, although somewhat underwhelming, do provide insight into the method's potential applications. Overall, the paper presents an interesting problem and a novel approach, but its practical implications are limited by the method's constraints.
