

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rates. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that works under weak assumptions (nonnegative data) and provides a practical algorithm for training one and two hidden layer networks with generalized polynomial activations. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as prior approaches to global optimality either lack practical implementation (Haeffele & Vidal, 2015) or require conditions that are difficult to verify in practice (Janzamin et al., 2015).

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing a rigorous connection between structured matrix factorization and neural network training, using nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides clear conditions for global optimality that can be checked without running the algorithm (by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix). The proof is well-structured, building on fixed point theory and the Banach fixed point theorem, with careful attention to the mathematical details. The supplementary material provides additional proofs that enhance the completeness of the theoretical framework.

The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate the practical viability of the approach, showing competitive performance compared to standard neural networks (ReLU-based) and SVMs. The authors correctly note that their method provides a global optimum guarantee where other neural network training approaches do not, which is a significant theoretical advantage. The comparison with SVM is somewhat surprising but understandable in the context of global optimality guarantees, though a more direct comparison with standard neural network training methods would have been helpful.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training, providing the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees.
2. The mathematical framework is rigorous, well-motivated, and builds on established nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.
3. The algorithm is simple to implement with no free parameters (unlike SGD), which is a significant practical advantage.
4. The experimental results demonstrate that the method can achieve competitive performance on real-world datasets.

Weaknesses:
1. The requirement for nonnegative data and weights (including no zero weights) limits the practical applicability of the method. While the authors note that many datasets (like word frequencies or image pixel counts) naturally satisfy nonnegativity, this constraint is still restrictive compared to standard neural networks.
2. The explanation for why the optimization problem maximizes the 1-norm of connection parameters (instead of minimizing, as in standard weight decay) is somewhat technical and could be better motivated for practitioners. The authors correctly note that this is a technical device to ensure strict positivity of the gradient, but the connection to practical neural network training is not fully developed.
3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when this approach might be preferable to standard neural network training methods. The comparison with SVM is interesting but does not address the key question of when this method would be practically useful versus existing neural network approaches.
4. The transition from first-order to second-order analysis in the proofs (Theorem 3 vs. Theorem 4) is not sufficiently explained, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with the technical details.

Originality: The paper is highly original, clearly differentiating from previous work on global optimality for neural networks. The use of generalized polynomial activations with nonnegative weights and the nonlinear spectral method represents a novel approach that has not been explored in prior NIPS papers.

Significance: This work represents an important theoretical advance in understanding neural network training. While the current implementation has limitations, it opens up new avenues for theoretically grounded neural network training methods. The guarantee of global optimality is a significant contribution to the field, even if the specific constraints limit immediate practical adoption.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The proofs are detailed and rigorous, though some mathematical details could be clarified for broader accessibility. The experimental results are appropriate for demonstrating the practical viability of the approach, though limited to low-dimensional datasets.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though some technical sections (particularly the transition from first to second-order analysis) would benefit from additional explanation. The pseudocode for the algorithm is clear and helpful.

Pro and Con Acceptance:
Pro: This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to neural network training with global optimality guarantees. The method is practical, has no free parameters, and provides a rigorous convergence rate. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and builds on established mathematical results.

Con: The nonnegativity constraint on data and weights significantly limits the range of applications. The connection to standard neural network training practices is not fully developed, and the comparison with standard methods (like SGD) is somewhat limited. The practical advantages of global optimality are not clearly demonstrated in the experiments.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of neural network training. While the constraints limit immediate practical adoption, the method provides a new perspective on training neural networks with theoretical guarantees. The paper is well-written and the theoretical results are sound. The reviewers recommend acceptance with the understanding that the method's practical utility may be limited by the nonnegativity constraint, but the theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS.
