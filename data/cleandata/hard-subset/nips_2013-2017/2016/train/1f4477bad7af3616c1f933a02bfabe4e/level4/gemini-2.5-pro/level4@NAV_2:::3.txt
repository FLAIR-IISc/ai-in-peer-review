This paper presents a significant theoretical result on the globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks, which the authors term Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks. The core contribution is a nonlinear spectral method that, under verifiable conditions, converges linearly to the unique global optimum of a modified objective function. The key constraints of the framework are the non-negativity of both input data and network weights, and the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The authors develop the theory for single hidden layer networks and demonstrate its extension to a two-layer architecture. In contrast to prior work on globally optimal training which often relies on hard-to-verify assumptions (e.g., related to the unknown data-generating distribution), the condition for convergence here depends on the spectral radius of a small, well-defined matrix whose entries are determined by the network's hyperparameters. The practical viability of the method is demonstrated through experiments on several UCI datasets.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to propose a practical algorithm for training a non-trivial neural network with a guarantee of finding the global optimum. This is a highly significant contribution to a central and notoriously difficult problem in deep learning.
*   **Verifiable Conditions for Optimality:** A major strength is that the conditions required for the convergence guarantee (specifically, ρ(A) < 1) are dependent only on the network's architecture and hyperparameters. They can be checked before training begins, which is a substantial improvement over previous methods that rely on properties of the data-generating process or non-degeneracy conditions of tensor decompositions that are difficult to verify in practice.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous and complete theoretical framework, leveraging concepts from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point theorems on cones to establish its claims. The proof strategy is clearly laid out, proceeding from the optimization problem to its reformulation as a unique fixed-point problem for a contraction mapping.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide experimental results on real-world datasets, which is a crucial step that is often missing in highly theoretical papers on this topic. These experiments serve as a valuable proof of concept, showing that the proposed models can achieve competitive performance.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Restrictive Model Assumptions:** The proposed framework relies on several strong constraints that may limit its practical applicability and expressiveness. The non-negativity requirement for all weights prevents the learning of inhibitory connections, a fundamental component of standard neural networks. While non-negative data is common or achievable via shifting, the weight constraint is a more significant limitation.
*   **Unconventional Architectural Constraints:** The requirement that each hidden unit must use a distinct polynomial power (α_i ≠ α_j for i ≠ j) is an unconventional constraint designed to ensure a unique optimum by breaking permutation symmetries. This deviates from standard network design and its impact on learning capacity is not fully explored.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** My main concern relates to the scalability of the method. To satisfy the crucial ρ(A) < 1 condition, the paper notes that one may need to choose sufficiently large values for `p_w` and `p_u`. As the matrix `A`'s entries grow with the number of hidden units (`n1`), classes (`K`), and the scale of the data (`ρ_x`), satisfying the spectral radius constraint for larger, deeper networks on high-dimensional data will likely require increasingly large `p_w`, `p_u` or small `ρ_w`, `ρ_u`. This effectively constrains the norms of the weights. If the weights are forced to be very small, the polynomial activation functions may operate in a near-linear regime, which would severely limit the expressive power of the network and undermine the motivation for building deep architectures. I am concerned that this might make it difficult to extend the approach to the large-scale problems where deep learning currently excels.

**Recommendation:**
Despite these concerns, this paper presents a novel and encouraging theoretical result. It tackles a fundamental problem in deep learning from a new perspective and provides the first truly practical algorithm with global optimality guarantees. The limitations are significant, but they do not detract from the importance of this work as an initial and foundational step. I believe this paper will be of great interest to the NIPS community and will inspire further research in this direction. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**
*   It would be very insightful to include an empirical analysis or discussion on the relationship between the spectral radius ρ(A) and key model parameters such as the input dimension, the number of hidden units, and the number of layers. For instance, plotting how ρ(A) scales as these parameters increase on a synthetic dataset could provide a clearer picture of the practical scalability of the conditions required for the guarantee. This would help practitioners understand how challenging it is to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 for larger models.
