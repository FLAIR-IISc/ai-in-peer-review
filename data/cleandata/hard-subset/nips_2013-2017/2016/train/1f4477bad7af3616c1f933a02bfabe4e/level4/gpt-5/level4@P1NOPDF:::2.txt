This paper studies a restricted class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers (NLSM1 and NLSM2), and evaluated on several real-world datasets.

My main concern is that the model class is highly constrained in ways that seem counterintuitive for practical use. As the authors note, the approach imposes nonnegativity constraints on the network weights and maximizes the regularization term on these weights. In addition, the optimality condition requires a nonstandard activation function (the generalized polynomial). These assumptions are difficult to justify in typical applications. The paper would be stronger with careful motivation grounded in real use cases or datasets, rather than introducing increasingly restrictive assumptions solely to secure global optimality.

The authors claim that, despite these restrictions, the model retains sufficient expressive power to capture complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance. However, the experiments do not provide strong support for this claim. In several cases, a linear SVM—apparently not carefully tuned—still outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2, even though the NLSM models are tuned over network architectures. Moreover, since NLSM is a nonlinear method, using only a linear SVM as a baseline is not an appropriate comparison. Nonlinear SVMs (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian/RBF kernels) should be included, with comparable hyperparameter tuning across methods. Without such baselines and fair tuning, the conclusions about expressive power and performance are not convincing.

The empirical evaluation is also limited by the scale of the datasets. The UCI datasets used are small in both sample size and feature dimensionality, which makes it difficult to assess performance and scalability in realistic, high-dimensional settings. From the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, suggesting potential difficulties for high-dimensional data or larger networks. How can this issue be mitigated in practice (e.g., via alternative parameterizations, tighter bounds, or architectural constraints)? As it stands, this growth may significantly hinder applicability to a broad range of modern tasks.

Another issue is that the optimality condition is not guaranteed for arbitrary parameter settings within the studied network class. Although the condition can be checked a priori, this remains impractical when there is no informative prior and the candidate parameter space is very large. Is there a fast screening or initialization strategy to identify a small set of likely valid candidates, or a principled way to bias the search toward feasible configurations?

Finally, it would help to clarify whether the global optimality achieved here is fundamentally a property of the special objective function, the architectural constraints, or both. It would also be informative to test whether standard stochastic gradient descent can empirically reach the same (presumed global) optimum on some datasets, thereby contextualizing the benefits of the nonlinear spectral method relative to common training procedures.

In summary, while the theoretical guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence is appealing, the practical relevance is limited by restrictive assumptions, insufficiently strong and potentially unfair baselines, small-scale experiments, and unresolved scalability concerns. I encourage the authors to (1) provide stronger application-driven justification for the constraints, (2) include fair comparisons to well-tuned nonlinear baselines, (3) evaluate on larger and higher-dimensional datasets, (4) address the spectral radius growth and feasibility-check bottlenecks, and (5) explore whether standard optimizers can reach comparable solutions in practice.
