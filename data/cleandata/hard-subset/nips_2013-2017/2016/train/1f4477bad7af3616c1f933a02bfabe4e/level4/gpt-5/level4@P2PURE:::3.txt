This paper develops a compelling theory showing that a certain class of neural networks admits a global optimum that can be reached at a linear convergence rate. The key constraints are that all input data and the weights in the linear layers are positive, and the activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for a network with a single hidden layer and then extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments indicate that the algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable results on several small real-world datasets (UCI).

Pros: As the authors claim, this appears to be the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior results with convergence guarantees typically rely on complicated preconditions that are difficult—or even impossible—to verify in practice because they depend on the data-generating distribution. In contrast, the preconditions here are comparatively simple, depending only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix built from the network’s parameters. The authors also show that their theory applies to networks of arbitrary depth and demonstrate reasonable performance on a small real dataset.

Cons: The proposed network still has limited applicability. The theory requires positive inputs and positive network parameters, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius condition (Theorem 1) strictly less than 1. While the nonnegativity of inputs is often manageable (training data can typically be shifted to be positive), constraining all parameters to be positive may limit expressiveness. Requiring all hidden-layer activation functions to be different is also somewhat unnatural and may hinder practical adoption. Finally, the authors state that, to satisfy the spectral-radius constraint, they must increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, which effectively reduces the weights of the linear units. If I understand correctly, as the input dimension and the size of the network parameters grow, meeting the spectral-radius condition forces these linear weights to shrink further. When the linear weights become very small, the activations behave almost linearly; stacking more layers then amounts to composing nearly linear functions, offering little additional expressiveness. This raises concerns about the method’s scalability to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this is an encouraging first step, and I believe the paper should be accepted.

Minor comments: I am curious how p1, p2, pw, pu, the input dimensionality, and the size of the parameter matrices affect the spectral radius ρ(A). For example, as input dimension increases, does ρ(A) grow linearly or exponentially, and should pw and pu be reduced linearly or exponentially to compensate? While a formal analysis may be difficult, plotting this relationship empirically on synthetic data could be informative.
