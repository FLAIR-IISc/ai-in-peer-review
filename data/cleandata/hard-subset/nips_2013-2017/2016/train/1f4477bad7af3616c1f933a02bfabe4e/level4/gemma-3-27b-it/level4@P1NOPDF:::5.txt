The paper discusses and defines a specific type of feedforward neural network for which a unique global optimizer can be found. Consequently, it defines an algorithm to locate this global optimizer, demonstrating a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivation is instructive, though a few points require further clarification. First, Theorem 3 relies on the computation of first-order derivatives in the presented inequalities, while Theorem 4 proceeds to second-order derivatives. What motivates this shift? Second, the network design appears to treat the final layer differently than any hidden layer. Given that *u* is also a matrix representing the parameters connecting to the hidden layer, the gradient with respect to *u* in G<sup>Φ</sup> (3) is simply a gradient computed over the vectorized form of *u*? (Similar questions arise elsewhere, such as when bounding *u* within a ball). 

A key concern – and I may be missing something – is the rationale behind maximizing the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2). Most neural network models employ weight decay to *reduce* the 1-norm. It seems this maximization is driven by the need for an arbitrarily small epsilon in (2) to ensure a strictly positive gradient, placing it within V<sub>++</sub>. This appears necessary for the proof but lacks strong justification in the context of practical neural networks. Is there a hidden degree of freedom here? The requirement for all data to be non-negative, combined with the use of strictly positive weights (excluding even zero values), leads to continuous accumulation. How practical is this design choice? 

In the experiments, the proposed neural network is compared with SVMs – presumably because SVMs also possess a global optimizer. However, in practice, will this type of network offer advantages over more commonly used neural networks (e.g., those employing ReLU) in any meaningful aspect?
