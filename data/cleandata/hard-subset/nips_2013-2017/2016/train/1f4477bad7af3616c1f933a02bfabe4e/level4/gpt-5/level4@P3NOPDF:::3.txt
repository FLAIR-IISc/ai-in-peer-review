Summary
The paper develops a theory for a class of neural networks that guarantees the existence of a global optimum and achieves it with a linear convergence rate under specific conditions. The key assumptions are: (i) all inputs and all weights in the linear layers are nonnegative; (ii) activations are generalized polynomials; and (iii) a spectral-radius condition holds on a nonnegative matrix constructed from network parameters. The theory is first established for single-hidden-layer networks and then extended to multiple hidden layers. Building on this, the authors propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments on a small 2D synthetic dataset and several small UCI datasets indicate the approach is practical within its scope.

Strengths
- The paper provides, to my knowledge, the first practically implementable neural network framework with both global optimality and linear convergence guarantees under verifiable conditions.
- In contrast to prior guarantees that rely on hard-to-check assumptions (e.g., conditions tied to the data-generating distribution), the main precondition here reduces to ensuring the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix (derived from model parameters) is less than one. This is comparatively simple and checkable in practice.
- The theoretical development is clear: single-layer results are presented first, then extended to arbitrary depth, along with an associated nonlinear spectral optimization method.
- Empirical results, while limited in scale, support the claims by demonstrating that the algorithm works well on synthetic data and achieves reasonable performance on small real-world datasets.

Concerns and Limitations
- Applicability is constrained by multiple requirements: nonnegative inputs, nonnegative linear weights, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral-radius constraint (< 1). While input nonnegativity can often be achieved by shifting features, the nonnegativity of weights may limit expressiveness relative to unconstrained networks.
- The requirement that every hidden layer use a different activation function (if that is indeed necessary for the theory) seems unnatural and may further restrict applicability.
- To enforce the spectral-radius constraint, the paper suggests increasing p1 and p2 and decreasing pw and pu, effectively reducing the magnitude of linear weights. As input and parameter dimensionality grows, maintaining ρ(A) < 1 appears to require even smaller linear weights. In the limit, very small linear weights can make activations behave nearly linearly, so stacking more layers adds little expressiveness. This raises concerns about the method’s scalability to deeper architectures.
- The experimental scope is modest. While consistent with the paper’s theoretical focus, broader experiments (e.g., larger or higher-dimensional tasks) would help clarify practical limits imposed by the constraints and the spectral-radius condition.

Questions and Requests for Clarification
- Please clarify the necessity of using different generalized polynomial activations across hidden layers. Is this strictly required by the proofs, or could identical activations be allowed without breaking the guarantees?
- Provide more intuition for how the spectral-radius matrix A is constructed and how its entries relate to model hyperparameters (p1, p2, pw, pu), input dimensionality, and network width. Even if a full analytic bound is difficult, additional discussion would help practitioners reason about feasible regimes.
- Empirically characterize how ρ(A) scales:
  - As input dimension and network width increase, does ρ(A) tend to grow linearly, polynomially, or exponentially?
  - How should pw and pu be adjusted in practice to maintain ρ(A) < 1 as dimensionality increases?
  - A synthetic study plotting ρ(A) as a function of these quantities would be valuable.
- Can the positivity constraints be relaxed partially (e.g., via reparameterizations such as factorizing weights into differences of nonnegative matrices) while retaining some form of guarantee?
- How sensitive is the method to the choice of generalized polynomial degrees and coefficients? Are there guidelines for selecting p1, p2, pw, pu beyond ensuring the spectral-radius constraint?

Suggestions
- Expand the empirical analysis with:
  - Ablations on p1, p2, pw, pu showing their effect on ρ(A), optimization speed, and accuracy.
  - Depth scaling experiments to test the hypothesis that deeper networks become effectively linear under tight spectral constraints.
  - Comparisons to strong baselines trained under similar positivity or monotonicity constraints to isolate the benefits of the proposed optimization method.
- Discuss practical procedures for verifying and enforcing ρ(A) < 1 during training (e.g., projection, adaptive rescaling), and the computational cost.
- Consider whether alternative activation designs within the generalized polynomial family could improve expressiveness under the same constraints.

Overall Assessment
This is a rigorous and thoughtfully presented paper that advances our understanding of when and how neural networks can enjoy global optimality and linear convergence under verifiable assumptions. Despite the limitations on expressiveness and concerns about depth scalability, the work offers a compelling initial framework with clear theoretical value and early evidence of practical viability. I recommend acceptance, with the hope that the authors can add clarifications and empirical characterizations of the spectral-radius trade-offs to strengthen the paper’s practical guidance.
