- Proposes a nonlinear spectral fixed-point method for globally optimal training of feedforward nets with polynomial activations and nonnegative weights.  
- Recasts training as a maximization over a convex cone with normalization and an added sum-of-outputs + ε to ensure gradient positivity.  
- Constructs a multi-homogeneous, order-preserving map and uses nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to prove unique global maximizer and linear convergence.  
- Convergence guarantee requires a checkable spectral-radius condition ρ(A) < 1, where A is a small explicit nonnegative matrix depending on architecture and norm parameters.  
- Method is parameter-free (no learning-rate tuning) with normalization built into the iteration.  
- Extends theoretical guarantees from one to two hidden layers with analogous constructions.  
- Theoretical analysis is clear and technically sound, with well-stated conditions and convergence rates, though some constants and bounds are conservative.  
- Empirical results on small UCI datasets show performance comparable to simple ReLU nets but generally worse than RBF SVMs.  
- Major limitation: restrictive assumptions—inputs and weights must be nonnegative, activations are monomials with distinct exponents, and the objective is modified—limiting expressivity and applicability.  
- Requiring distinct exponents (αi ≠ αj) is nonstandard and complicates model selection.  
- Conditions can be conservative: achieving ρ(A) < 1 often forces large p-norm exponents and ρ(A) grows with hidden units, hurting scalability and performance.  
- Empirical evaluation is modest: small, low-dimensional datasets only, no high-dimensional or structured-data experiments, and no ablations on added objective terms.  
- Unclear how to handle negative-valued features in practice without violating theoretical guarantees.  
- Clarity could be improved by explaining the empirical effect of the added sum+ε terms, providing per-iteration complexity and SGD comparisons, and offering guidance on selecting α and p-values.  
- Novelty: applying multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to neural net training and providing a practically checkable spectral condition is original.  
- Significance is conceptual but limited in practice due to restrictive assumptions and weak empirical performance; broader impact needs relaxed constraints and stronger experiments.  
- Suggested experiments: sensitivity/ablation studies for α and norms, empirical contraction factors vs. ρ(A), runtime comparisons to tuned SGD, and tests on nonnegative high-dimensional data.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong verifiable theoretical guarantees, parameter-free algorithm, and novel nonlinear spectral approach.  
- Cons for acceptance: restrictive assumptions, conservative/scalability-limiting conditions, and limited empirical validation.  
- Minor critique: claim that linear convergence implies very few iterations is overstated; iteration count depends on ρ(A) and problem scaling.
