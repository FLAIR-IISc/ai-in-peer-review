This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NSM) for training a particular class of feedforward neural networks. The main theoretical contribution is a globally convergent algorithm with a provable linear (geometric) convergence rate under a verifiable condition. Global optimality is guaranteed when the spectral radius of a certain small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix is below one. The condition depends on the network architecture and can be checked efficiently. This is a novel and interesting angle that leverages spectral properties to obtain strong guarantees for a nonconvex learning problem.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: The paper provides a checkable sufficient condition for global optimality and linear convergence for a specific network class.
- Conceptual novelty: Framing the training problem through a nonlinear spectral perspective and reducing the verification to a spectral radius test of a nonnegative matrix is original and potentially impactful.
- Practical verifiability: The condition involves a small matrix, making the certification step computationally light.

Limitations and concerns:
- Strong modeling restrictions: The method requires nonnegative weights and very specific, non-standard activation functions. These constraints substantially limit expressivity relative to commonly used architectures (e.g., with signed weights, ReLU, tanh, or sigmoid). It is unclear how often practically relevant problems fit within this constrained class.
- Potential performance impact: The imposed nonnegativity and specialized activations may hinder the network’s ability to model complex decision boundaries. Consistent with this concern, the empirical results show performance that is not competitive with standard SVM baselines on the reported datasets.
- Scope of generalization: The paper does not convincingly argue that the approach extends to broader classes of networks or more commonly used activations without losing its guarantees. The gap between the theoretically elegant framework and typical neural network practice remains large.
- Practical convergence claims: While a linear convergence rate is valuable, the claim that this “typically” leads to convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is not substantiated and may overstate practical speed. Linear convergence guarantees a rate per iteration but does not by itself bound the number of iterations without specifying constants.

Empirical evaluation:
- The experiments, though helpful for illustrating the method, do not demonstrate a clear advantage over strong baselines. In particular, the reported performance trailing SVM weakens the practical case for adoption.
- Additional comparisons would strengthen the empirical section, including: (i) constrained baselines (e.g., projected gradient or coordinate descent with the same nonnegativity and activation constraints), (ii) modern linear/kernel methods, and (iii) runtime and iteration counts with confidence intervals to contextualize the convergence behavior.
- It would also help to report the size of the “small” nonnegative matrix used for the spectral condition across experiments and the cost of computing its spectral radius relative to overall training time.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper is generally well written and the main ideas are presented clearly. The construction of the spectral condition and its relationship to network parameters is a strong point.
- It would improve readability to more explicitly state the architectural assumptions (including any constraints on inputs and bias terms) up front, and to provide intuition for why the spectral radius condition ensures contractivity/global optimality.
- Please temper statements linking linear convergence to very fast practical convergence unless accompanied by empirical evidence across diverse settings and problem scales.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can the nonnegativity constraint be relaxed in any principled way (e.g., via signed reparameterizations or monotone transformations) while preserving global optimality or at least fixed-point convergence?
- Are there broader families of activations (e.g., piecewise-linear monotone functions) for which the same analysis applies?
- How sensitive is the spectral radius condition to network depth/width? Some ablation illustrating the threshold behavior as architecture varies would be informative.
- Can the method incorporate bias terms or input preprocessing to mitigate the expressivity limitations imposed by nonnegativity?
- Provide clear guidance on when the condition is likely to hold in practice and on how practitioners should design architectures to satisfy it.

Overall assessment:
This is a theoretically solid and original contribution that brings a fresh perspective to neural network training via nonlinear spectral methods, with provable global optimality and linear convergence under a verifiable condition. However, the strong modeling restrictions and the underwhelming empirical performance relative to standard baselines limit its practical impact in its current form. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical evaluation, broaden the scope where possible, and clarify the practical implications and limitations of the assumptions. At present, I lean toward rejection due to limited applicability, but I acknowledge the novelty and technical merit of the core idea.
