

The paper introduces a specialized class of feedforward neural networks that admit a unique global minimum, along with an optimization algorithm achieving linear convergence. The mathematical framework is largely sound and offers instructive insights, though several technical points require clarification.  

First, the transition from first-order analysis (Theorem 3) to second-order analysis (Theorem 4) lacks justification. Theorem 3 derives conditions using first-order derivatives (as shown in the inequalities), yet Theorem 4 abruptly shifts to second-order derivatives without explanation. This discontinuity undermines the logical flow and warrants explicit motivation—e.g., whether higher-order terms are necessary for convergence guarantees or to address a specific non-convexity.  

Second, the network architecture treats the final layer distinctly from hidden layers, raising questions about parameter handling. Specifically, the parameter matrix $u$ (connecting to hidden layers) appears to be vectorized in the gradient computation for $G^\Phi$ (Equation 3), yet the analysis does not clarify whether the gradient with respect to $u$ should be interpreted as a matrix derivative or a vectorized form. This ambiguity persists in other contexts, such as the constraint bounding $u$ within a ball (e.g., $\|u\|_2 \leq R$), where the role of vectorization is unclear. If $u$ is inherently a matrix, standard practice would involve matrix calculus or explicit vectorization; the paper’s approach needs formal justification to avoid confusion.  

Third, the model’s requirement to maximize the 1-norm of connection parameters (Equation 2) contradicts standard regularization practices in neural networks (e.g., weight decay minimizes 1-norms to encourage sparsity). The authors argue that the arbitrarily small $\epsilon$ in Equation (2) ensures the gradient remains strictly positive (to lie in $V_{++}$), but this appears to be a technical artifact solely for the proof, not a practical design choice. In real-world applications, maximizing 1-norms would likely lead to unbounded weights and poor generalization, as it actively encourages larger magnitudes rather than penalizing them. The paper does not address why this counterintuitive choice is justified beyond theoretical convenience, nor does it explain how to avoid this issue in practice.  

Fourth, the model’s assumption of non-negative data and strictly positive weights (to prevent zero weights and ensure "additive" behavior) significantly limits its applicability. Most real-world datasets contain mixed-sign features, and neural networks typically benefit from adaptive sign patterns (e.g., via ReLU activations). The paper fails to demonstrate how this constraint can be relaxed or why it is necessary for the global optimality result. Without empirical validation on datasets violating these assumptions, the practical relevance of the model remains questionable.  

Finally, the experimental comparison with SVM is problematic. While SVMs indeed offer global optima, they are fundamentally different from neural networks (e.g., linear vs. nonlinear, kernel-based vs. parametric). The authors justify this comparison by noting SVM’s global optimizer, but this overlooks the primary motivation for using neural networks: their capacity to model complex, hierarchical representations. The paper does not demonstrate that the proposed network outperforms standard neural architectures (e.g., ReLU-based networks) on relevant tasks, nor does it clarify *why* this specific network class offers advantages over existing methods. For instance, if the model’s strict positivity constraint is impractical, how does it compare to ReLU networks—which handle negative inputs naturally—on benchmark datasets?  

In summary, the theoretical contribution is promising but hinges on restrictive assumptions that weaken its practical appeal. The mathematical derivation is instructive but requires sharper exposition to resolve the ambiguities noted above. Crucially, the paper does not establish a clear path to real-world utility: the 1-norm maximization, non-negativity constraints, and lack of comparison to standard neural networks leave the reader uncertain whether this model addresses a meaningful gap in practice. The authors should either justify these assumptions more rigorously or demonstrate their feasibility on non-trivial datasets. Without this, the work risks being perceived as a theoretical curiosity rather than a useful advancement.
