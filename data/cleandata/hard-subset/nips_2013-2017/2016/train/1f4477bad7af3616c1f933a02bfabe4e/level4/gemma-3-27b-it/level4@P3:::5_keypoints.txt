- Presents a novel method for training generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights, claiming global optimality and linear convergence.
- Tackles non-convex optimization by imposing architectural constraints and using a nonlinear spectral method.
- Builds on recent neural-net optimization work and is presented as more practical than prior tensor-based approaches.
- Paper is well-structured with detailed mathematical derivations and clear statement of main results (Theorem 1, Theorem 5).
- Experimental results on small datasets serve as a proof-of-concept supporting theoretical claims.
- Connection to Perron–Frobenius theory and discussion of convergence conditions are insightful.
- Derivations are dense and hard to follow in places, especially the jump from Theorem 3 (first-order) to Theorem 4 (second-order).
- The small epsilon term in the objective appears to be a proof artifact with unclear practical relevance.
- Maximizing the 1-norm of weights (vs. usual weight decay) is unconventional and needs stronger justification.
- Non-negativity constraint may limit applicability beyond count data and its practical limitations are underdiscussed.
- Scalability and computational complexity of the nonlinear spectral method are not fully addressed.
- Empirical validation is limited and does not convincingly outperform or offer clear advantages over SVMs or ReLU networks.
- Comparison to SVMs seems motivated by SVMs’ global optimality rather than a fair capability comparison.
- Claim of “no free parameter” is misleading because architecture choices and positive constants (ρw, ρu, alpha values) still require tuning.
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical contribution but practical limitations, scalability concerns, and weak empirical evidence make the paper borderline; recommendation: Weak Accept if authors address practicality, clarity, and empirical validation.
