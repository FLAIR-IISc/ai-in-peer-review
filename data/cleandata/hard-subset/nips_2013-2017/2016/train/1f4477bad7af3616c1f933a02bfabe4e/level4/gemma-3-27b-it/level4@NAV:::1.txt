This paper presents a compelling analysis of a specific neural network architecture, demonstrating the existence of a single stationary point. The authors propose an efficient algorithm that converges to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses the challenging problem of establishing stationary point uniqueness in a non-convex model through careful constraint design, employing a fixed-point theory approach – a common strategy beyond convexity or geodesic convexity. Specifically, the authors construct a contractive map whose fixed-point represents the cost function’s unique minimizer. 

However, the convergence proof relies on a boundedness condition for the spectral radius of a key matrix, a limitation acknowledged by the authors as restricting the scalability to networks with larger hidden layers. Additionally, the method introduces a significant number of hyperparameters, making practical application and intuitive understanding difficult. Crucially, verifying the spectral condition is required for each parameter configuration. The empirical evaluation involved extensive parameter search – approximately 150,000 combinations – using cross-validation to identify optimal settings. Despite this effort, the proposed method outperformed a linear SVM on only two of seven datasets, raising concerns regarding its practical advantage and the inherent benefits of the investigated neural network structure.
