Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a particular class of feedforward neural networks. The key result is a provable convergence to the global optimum with a linear rate under a condition that depends only on the network architecture. This condition reduces to computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, which can be checked before running the algorithm. The method applies to networks with one or two hidden layers and uses non-standard, generalized polynomial activation functions. The authors provide initial experiments on several UCI datasets and compare against ReLU networks trained with SGD and RBF-kernel SVMs.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: It is new and interesting to introduce a nonlinear spectral method that offers global optimality guarantees for training a class of neural networks.
- Clear verifiable condition: The global optimality condition is transparent and easy to check via the spectral radius of a small matrix determined by the architecture and norms.
- Theoretical guarantees: The method comes with linear convergence and does not require tuning learning rates, addressing a common practical difficulty with SGD.
- Scope: While the focus is on one- and two-hidden-layer networks, the approach is presented as extendable in principle.

Weaknesses
- Strong modeling constraints: The method imposes non-negativity on the network weights. Together with the very specific, non-standard activation functions, this is a significant restriction that may limit expressiveness and practical applicability.
- Empirical performance: The reported results show performance that is not as good as kernel SVMs on several datasets, suggesting that the constraints may hinder accuracy in practice.
- Presentation claim: The statement that linear convergence implies convergence “in a few (less than 10) iterations” is overstated, since linear convergence alone does not guarantee so few iterations.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The paper presents a technically sound method with rigorous guarantees of global optimality and linear convergence under the stated condition. The reliance on nonnegativity and specialized activations is a significant modeling choice that likely explains some of the empirical limitations. Within this restricted class, the theoretical development appears careful and well-motivated.
- Clarity: The core idea and main theorem are clearly presented, including how the spectral-radius condition is constructed and checked. The paper is dense but largely coherent, and it explains the algorithm and convergence guarantees at a high level.
- Originality: The approach of training a class of neural networks via a nonlinear spectral method with a verifiable global optimality condition appears novel. The reliance on generalized polynomial activations and nonnegativity constraints is unconventional in neural network practice.
- Significance: The contribution is theoretically meaningful, offering global optimality with a linear rate for a nontrivial network class. However, the practical impact seems limited by the strong constraints and the observed empirical gap relative to SVMs. The method may still prove valuable as a theoretical benchmark or for specific domains where the constraints are natural.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- New and interesting method with a clean, verifiable condition for global optimality.
- Strong theoretical guarantees, including linear convergence, without the need for learning-rate tuning.
- Demonstrates a path toward practically checkable global optimality in neural network training for a specific class.

Cons:
- Requires nonnegative weights and very specific, non-standard activation functions, which may markedly limit expressiveness.
- Empirical performance lags behind kernel SVMs on several datasets, raising concerns about practical usefulness.
- The claim linking linear convergence to “a few” iterations is overstated.

Minor comment
- Lines 90–91: The sentence claiming that linear convergence “thus” implies convergence in a few (less than 10) iterations is exaggerated. Linear convergence ensures a geometric rate but does not, by itself, bound the number of iterations to a small constant. Consider softening this claim or qualifying it with the actual contraction factor observed in practice.
