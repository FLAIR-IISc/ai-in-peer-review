

The paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, achieving global convergence with a linear rate. The key theoretical contribution lies in establishing that global optimality depends solely on the network architecture parameters, reducing to the spectral radius computation of a small nonnegative matrix. This approach is technically interesting and represents a meaningful advance for the specified network class.  

However, two significant limitations warrant careful consideration. First, the method imposes a strict non-negativity constraint on all network weights, which may severely restrict its applicability to standard neural network architectures where weight sign diversity is crucial for expressive power. Second, the activation functions employed (e.g., specific piecewise-linear forms) are highly specialized and non-standard, potentially limiting the method’s practical utility beyond the narrow scope of the analysis. The numerical experiments provided show modest performance, with the proposed method underperforming relative to SVM baselines on the tested datasets. This suggests that the imposed constraints may indeed hinder competitive empirical results.  

A minor point concerns the claim in lines 90–91: "Note that the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate and thus converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to..." While linear convergence guarantees eventual fast convergence, it does not inherently imply convergence within a small fixed number of iterations (e.g., <10) for all problem instances. The phrasing overstates the practical implication of the convergence rate, and the authors should temper this claim to avoid misleading readers about computational efficiency.  

Overall, the theoretical framework is sound for its restricted setting, but the stringent architectural requirements and lack of empirical competitiveness against established methods present substantial barriers to broader adoption. The work would benefit from discussion of how these limitations might be addressed or relaxed in future extensions.
