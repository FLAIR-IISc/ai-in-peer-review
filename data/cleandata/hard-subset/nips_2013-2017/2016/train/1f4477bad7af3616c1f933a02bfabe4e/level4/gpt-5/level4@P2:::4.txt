Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a class of feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations and nonnegative weights. The core idea is to recast the training objective as a maximization over a convex cone with normalization constraints and an additional term that sums network outputs (plus a small positive offset) to ensure positivity of the gradient on the cone. The authors then construct a multi-homogeneous, order-preserving fixed-point map inspired by (nonlinear) Perron–Frobenius theory and show that, under a condition on the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two), the method converges linearly to the unique global maximizer. The condition ρ(A) < 1 depends only on architecture and norm parameters and can be verified before training. The method extends from one to two hidden layers, and experiments on several UCI datasets show performance comparable to simple ReLU networks but generally below RBF SVMs.

This work connects to recent attempts at provable training for neural networks (e.g., tensor methods of Janzamin et al.; factorization-based global optimality in Haeffele & Vidal) but differs by offering a practically checkable spectral condition and a simple iterative algorithm without learning-rate tuning. It also relates to polynomial neural networks and to the nonlinear Perron–Frobenius literature on cones and multi-homogeneous maps.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a fixed-point scheme with explicit, verifiable conditions ensuring a unique global optimum and linear convergence, derived via Thompson metric contractions.
- Practically checkable guarantee: ρ(A) depends only on architecture and norm parameters; no need to know the data distribution, unlike some tensor approaches.
- Parameter-free iteration: no step-size tuning; normalization built into the map.
- Extension to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.
- Useful proof roadmap and connections to nonlinear spectral theory; the Lipschitz characterization and role of the weighting vector γ are well-motivated.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Restrictive assumptions: inputs and all weights must be nonnegative; activations are monomials with distinct exponents; the objective is modified by adding the sum of outputs and a small positive offset. These constraints may limit expressivity relative to standard architectures and complicate application beyond count/nonnegative data.
- Dependence on distinct exponents: requiring αi ≠ αj to avoid trivial solutions is nonstandard and may make model selection delicate.
- Conservativeness of conditions: ensuring ρ(A) < 1 often requires large p-norm exponents (pw, pu, etc.), and ρ(A) grows with the number of hidden units. This could hinder scalability or force conservative settings that limit performance.
- Empirical evaluation is modest: small, low-dimensional UCI datasets; performance typically lags RBF SVM and is only on par with simple ReLU baselines. No experiments on higher-dimensional or structured data, nor ablations to clarify the impact of the added objective terms or constraints.
- Practicality vs. assumptions: it is unclear how to handle datasets with negative features (e.g., standard normalized inputs) and what preprocessing or offsetting is required without breaking the theory.

Quality
- The theoretical results appear technically sound, with a well-structured path from the optimization problem to fixed-point theory, including positivity, contraction, and uniqueness. The conditions and convergence rates are clearly stated. Some constants and bounds (e.g., the construction of A) are acknowledged to be conservative; tighter analyses would strengthen applicability.

Clarity
- The exposition of the main algorithm, conditions, and proof strategy is generally clear. However, the paper would benefit from:
  - A more explicit discussion of the role and empirical effect of the added “sum of outputs” and ε terms in the objective.
  - A concise complexity analysis per iteration and a comparison to SGD per epoch.
  - Guidance on choosing α, ρu, ρw, and p-values, with sensitivity analyses.
  - Clearer discussion of handling data with negative features (e.g., shifting/scaling) and its implications for guarantees.
  - Cleanup of references (years, formatting) and minor typos.

Originality
- The application of nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to obtain globally optimal training for these networks is novel. The checkable spectral-radius condition and parameter-free fixed-point iteration provide a new angle compared to tensor or factorization approaches.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: advances the discussion on when global training of neural networks is possible with practical checks and linear convergence. However, practical impact is tempered by the restrictive modeling assumptions and relatively weak empirical performance. Broader significance would be enhanced by demonstrating stronger results on more challenging datasets and by relaxing some constraints.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of α and the norm parameters pw, pu (and pv for two layers)? Can the authors provide ablations and heuristics?
- Can inputs with negative entries be accommodated by shifts or learned offsets while preserving guarantees?
- What is the empirical contraction factor vs. the bound ρ(A)? Reporting observed rates would help gauge conservativeness.
- Can the additional objective terms (sum of outputs and ε) be removed or reduced post-training without hurting accuracy? An ablation would clarify their practical role.
- Provide per-iteration time and total runtime comparisons to SGD (with tuned learning rates) on all datasets, not only convergence plots.
- Consider comparisons on higher-dimensional nonnegative data (e.g., bag-of-words text, grayscale images) and discuss scalability as hidden units increase.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, clean theoretical guarantees with a verifiable condition and linear convergence.
- Novel and interesting application of nonlinear spectral theory to neural network training.
- Extension beyond one hidden layer with analogous results.

Cons for acceptance
- Restrictive assumptions (nonnegativity, specific activations, modified objective) limit applicability and expressivity.
- Conservative conditions that may impede scalability.
- Limited and not state-of-the-art empirical performance; small-scale evaluation.

Minor comment
- Lines 90–91: The statement that “linear convergence … typically in a few (less than 10) iterations” is overstated. Linear convergence does not imply a small number of iterations; the constant depends on ρ(A) and problem scaling. Consider tempering this claim.
