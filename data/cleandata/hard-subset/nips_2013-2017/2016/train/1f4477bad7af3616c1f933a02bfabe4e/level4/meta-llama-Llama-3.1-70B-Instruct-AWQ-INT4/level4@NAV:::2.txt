This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be globally optimized with a linear convergence rate using the nonlinear spectral method. The authors applied this method to deep networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluated its performance on various real-world datasets. However, the class of neural networks under consideration is limited and counterintuitive, as it relies on non-negative weights and maximizes regularization. Furthermore, the use of the generalized polynomial activation function is required for optimality, which may not be a reasonable assumption. Instead of imposing restrictive assumptions to achieve global optimality, a more detailed explanation from an application or real-world dataset perspective would be beneficial.

The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks still possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieve good performance. Nevertheless, the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. Surprisingly, linear SVM outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2, even when the linear SVM appears to be suboptimally tuned, while NLSM tunes its network structure. Moreover, it is unusual that a linear SVM is used as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear SVM, such as a polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel. As a result, the comparisons are unfair, and the conclusions drawn from these results are unconvincing. In other words, these results do not demonstrate the sufficient expressive power of the studied class of neural networks.

The experimental evaluation is limited by the use of small datasets from the UCI repository, which are restricted in both sample size and feature dimensionality. According to the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, making it challenging for high-dimensional data. It is essential to address this issue, as it significantly limits the applicability of this method. Furthermore, the optimal condition is not guaranteed by any parameter settings, and checking the condition without running the neural network is still problematic when there are no priors or an extremely large number of possible parameters. A fast approach to obtaining a small set of valid candidates would be beneficial.

It is also worth investigating whether the optimal property of the studied model stems from the special objective function. Additionally, it would be interesting to explore whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically converge to the global optimum on some tested datasets.
