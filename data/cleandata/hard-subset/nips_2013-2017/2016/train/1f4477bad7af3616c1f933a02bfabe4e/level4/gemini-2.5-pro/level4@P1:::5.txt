This paper introduces a novel method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, termed "generalized polynomial neural networks," to a unique global optimum. The authors present a nonlinear spectral method that, under certain checkable conditions on the network architecture, guarantees linear convergence to the globally optimal solution. This work builds on Perron-Frobenius theory for nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones. The key modeling constraints are the requirements for non-negative input data and non-negative network weights. The training is also performed on a modified objective function. The authors demonstrate the practicality of their approach with experiments on several real-world datasets, a step that previous work on globally optimal training has often omitted.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the highly significant and challenging problem of non-convex optimization in neural network training. Providing a practical algorithm with a guarantee of finding the global optimum is a major theoretical contribution. Unlike previous theoretical results that were "challenging to apply in practice" (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal, 2017) or required knowledge of the data-generating distribution (Janzamin et al., 2015), this work presents a method that is directly applicable to data. The convergence conditions are verifiable a priori, which is another strong practical advantage.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is rigorous, skillfully adapting concepts from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, the Thompson metric, and the Banach fixed-point theorem to the problem of training neural networks. The mathematical framework is elegant and provides a complete proof of the convergence guarantee.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The paper is theoretically impressive, but several aspects of the model's formulation, practical applicability, and experimental evaluation could be clarified or strengthened.

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Derivations:** The connection between the general fixed-point theory and its specific application to the neural network objective could be made more explicit.
    *   Could the authors clarify the relationship between the conditions in Lemma 4 and the second-order properties of the objective function Φ? The conditions in Lemma 4 are on the first derivative of the map GΦ. Since GΦ is defined using the gradient of Φ, these conditions implicitly depend on the Hessian of Φ. A more explicit discussion of this would help readers understand how the bounds for the matrix `A` in Theorem 4 are derived.
    *   In the formulation of the iterative map GΦ (Equation 3) and elsewhere, vector-based notations (e.g., norms, component-wise functions) are applied to the weight matrix `u`. While it is understood that these operations apply to the vectorized form of the matrix, explicitly stating the conventions used for matrix norms and gradients would improve the paper's clarity.

2.  **Unconventional Model Formulation:** The model deviates from standard neural networks in ways that warrant further justification and analysis.
    *   The objective function (2) includes a term that maximizes the L1 norm of the network's weights (`∑ wr,l + ∑ ulm`), which is contrary to the common practice of weight decay (L1/L2 minimization) used for regularization. The paper states this is for technical reasons to ensure a strictly positive gradient. While necessary for the proof, the practical implications of this choice are unclear. Does this term act as a form of anti-regularization, and how does it affect the model's generalization capabilities?
    *   The claim that the algorithm has "no free parameter" is slightly misleading. While the iterative update rule itself is parameter-free (unlike SGD with its learning rate), the overall model and the convergence condition depend on numerous hyperparameters (`n1`, `α`, `pw`, `pu`, `ρw`, `ρu`). The experiments confirm that these must be tuned, in this case via random search and cross-validation. The claim should be qualified to refer specifically to the optimization update step.

3.  **Practicality and Expressiveness:** The constraints of the model limit its scope, and its practical advantages over standard networks are not yet fully established.
    *   The model is restricted to non-negative data and learns only non-negative weights. This is a significant constraint that limits its applicability. For example, with non-negative inputs and weights, the network can only represent monotonically increasing functions. It would be beneficial to discuss the class of problems for which this inductive bias is appropriate and where this model might have an advantage.
    *   The experimental comparison to SVM is logical, as SVM optimization is convex and also yields a global optimum. However, the key question is what value this model provides over standard, heuristically-trained deep learning models. The results in Table 1 show that the proposed method is competitive but does not consistently outperform ReLU networks or SVMs. Given the model's constraints, a more compelling case is needed for its practical utility. Are there specific problem domains where the non-negativity is a natural fit and leads to superior performance or interpretability?

In summary, this paper presents a significant and original theoretical result: a practical algorithm for globally optimal training of a non-trivial neural network subclass. The mathematical framework is solid and instructive. However, the practical significance is currently limited by the model's restrictive assumptions (non-negativity) and unconventional objective function. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper discussion of the practical implications of these modeling choices and a more compelling demonstration of where this guaranteed optimality provides a tangible advantage over standard, flexible, and empirically successful neural network architectures.
