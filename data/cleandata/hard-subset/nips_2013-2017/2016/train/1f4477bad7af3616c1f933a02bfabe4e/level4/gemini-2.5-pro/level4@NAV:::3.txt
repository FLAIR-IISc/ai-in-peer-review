This paper introduces a compelling theoretical framework for a specific class of neural networks, demonstrating the existence of a global optimum and proving it can be attained at a linear rate of convergence. This guarantee is subject to several key constraints, namely the non-negativity of both input data and linear layer weights, and the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The theoretical development is initially presented for a single-hidden-layer network and is subsequently extended to multi-layer architectures. To accompany the theory, a nonlinear spectral method is proposed for optimization. Empirical validation on a small 2D synthetic dataset shows strong performance, and the method achieves commendable results on several small-scale real-world UCI datasets.

**Pros:**

A significant contribution of this paper, as highlighted by the authors, is that it appears to be the first to present a potentially practical neural network framework that comes with both optimality and convergence guarantees. This stands in contrast to prior art, where convergence guarantees often rely on complex preconditions that are difficult, if not practically impossible, to verify, particularly those dependent on the underlying data-generating distribution. The framework proposed here, however, has comparatively straightforward preconditions, which hinge on the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix derived from the network's parameters. Furthermore, the authors successfully demonstrate the theory's applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and provide evidence of its effectiveness on small real-world datasets.

**Cons:**

Despite its theoretical elegance, the proposed network's practical applicability is constrained by several requirements. The theory mandates non-negative inputs and network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (as per Theorem 1) of less than one. While the non-negativity constraint on input data may not be a major impediment—as data can often be shifted to be positive—the requirement for non-negative network parameters is more concerning, as it could substantially limit the model's expressive power. Additionally, the stipulation that each hidden layer must have a different activation function is unconventional and may further restrict the model's practical use cases.

A key concern relates to satisfying the spectral radius constraint. As the authors note, this requires increasing `p1` and `p2` while decreasing `pw` and `pu`, which has the effect of reducing the magnitude of the linear weights. My interpretation is that, to maintain this constraint as the dimensionality of the input and network parameters grows, the linear weights must be made progressively smaller. This leads to a potential issue: with very small linear weights, the generalized polynomial activations will behave in a nearly linear fashion. Consequently, stacking additional layers would be akin to composing linear functions, offering no significant increase in the network's expressive capacity. This raises doubts about the framework's scalability to truly deep architectures.

Notwithstanding these limitations, the paper represents an encouraging and valuable initial step in this direction. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.

**Minor Comments:**

I am interested in a more detailed analysis of how the spectral radius `rho(A)` is influenced by the parameters `p1`, `p2`, `pw`, `pu`, as well as by the input dimension and network size. For instance, does `rho(A)` scale linearly or exponentially with an increase in input dimensionality? Correspondingly, would `pw` and `pu` need to be decreased linearly or exponentially to compensate? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical investigation plotting these relationships on synthetic data could provide valuable insights and would be a welcome addition to the paper.
