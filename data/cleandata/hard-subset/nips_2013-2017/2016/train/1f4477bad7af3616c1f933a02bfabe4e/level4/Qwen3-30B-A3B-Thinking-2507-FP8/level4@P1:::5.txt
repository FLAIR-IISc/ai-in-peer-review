

The paper introduces a novel approach for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with provable global optimality guarantees. The authors propose a nonlinear spectral method that achieves linear convergence to the global optimum under weak assumptions (nonnegativity of data), addressing a fundamental limitation of standard stochastic gradient descent methods that lack such guarantees. The work builds upon recent advances in tensor-based neural network training [11] and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory [7, 14], but provides a practically feasible solution that overcomes limitations of prior work.

The main contributions include: (1) a globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective function; (2) a convergence proof demonstrating linear convergence to a unique global optimum under conditions that can be verified before training; and (3) experimental validation on UCI datasets showing competitive performance compared to standard methods.

Strengths:
- The theoretical foundation is rigorous and well-motivated, connecting to established mathematical frameworks. The proof strategy is well-structured, leveraging fixed-point theory and Thompson metrics to establish convergence guarantees.
- The convergence conditions can be checked without running the algorithm (via spectral radius of a small matrix), making the method practically feasible.
- The algorithm has no free parameters (unlike SGD), eliminating the need for careful learning rate tuning.
- The experimental section provides a valuable proof of concept, demonstrating global optimality on real datasets—a gap previously unaddressed in the literature [8, 11].

Weaknesses:
- The nonnegativity constraint on weights and data is highly restrictive for practical applications. Most real-world data (e.g., images after normalization) contains negative values, and the method cannot be directly applied without preprocessing.
- The modified objective function (including the sum of outputs) is not well-justified from a practical perspective. While the authors note this is needed for technical reasons to ensure strictly positive gradients, the paper does not adequately explain why this modification does not significantly impact classification performance.
- The experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which may not reflect performance on more complex, high-dimensional tasks where neural networks typically excel. The comparison with SVM (which also has a global optimizer) is somewhat misleading, as SVMs are not typically used for the same applications as neural networks.
- The paper does not sufficiently address why the nonnegativity constraint is necessary for the proof rather than being an artifact of the mathematical approach.

Quality: The theoretical results are technically sound with rigorous proofs that build upon established mathematical concepts. The convergence guarantees are meaningful, and the conditions for global optimality can be verified before training. However, the practical applicability is limited by the restrictive assumptions, which may prevent the method from being widely adopted in real-world applications.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and the mathematical notation is consistent. The connections to Perron-Frobenius theory are clearly explained. However, the motivation for the modified objective function (the sum of outputs) could be better justified, and some technical details (e.g., the relationship between Theorems 3 and 4) are not clearly articulated, potentially hindering reproducibility.

Originality: This work represents a significant advancement toward globally optimal neural network training. While previous methods [8, 11] have shown global optimality for specific architectures, this is the first approach that demonstrates practical feasibility (i.e., can be implemented and applied to real datasets). The combination of nonlinear spectral methods with neural network training is novel and well-motivated by the mathematical framework.

Significance: The theoretical contribution is significant for advancing the understanding of neural network optimization. However, the practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions. The experimental results show only modest performance gains over standard methods (SVMs and ReLU networks), and the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate why this approach would provide advantages in real-world applications where neural networks are typically used.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides the first practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks with a convergence guarantee.
- The theoretical results are well-motivated and build upon established mathematical frameworks.
- The linear convergence rate is a practical advantage over methods without convergence guarantees.
- The experimental validation on real datasets fills an important gap in the literature.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The nonnegativity constraint severely limits practical applicability to real-world data.
- The modified objective function lacks sufficient justification for real-world applications.
- Experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional datasets, which may not reflect performance on more challenging tasks.
- The method shows only modest performance gains compared to standard approaches on the tested datasets.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding neural network optimization, but its practical significance is limited by restrictive assumptions. The theoretical results are strong and novel, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of practical constraints and potential applications. The experimental section should be expanded to include more challenging datasets and a clearer comparison with standard neural network training methods.
