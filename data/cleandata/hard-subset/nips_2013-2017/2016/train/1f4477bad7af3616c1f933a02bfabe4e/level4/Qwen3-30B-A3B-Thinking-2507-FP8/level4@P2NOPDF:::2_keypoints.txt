- The method enforces non-negative weights and maximizes weight regularization, a counterintuitive and unjustified constraint for real-world use.  
- It requires a niche generalized polynomial activation to satisfy optimality, making assumptions overly restrictive and poorly motivated.  
- Authors claim sufficient expressive power, but empirical results do not substantiate this claim.  
- Baseline comparisons are flawed: linear SVMs (untuned) are used against a nonlinear NLSM that tunes network structure, producing unfair evaluations.  
- A nonlinear SVM (e.g., polynomial or RBF) would be a meaningful baseline but is not included.  
- Experiments use small UCI datasets, contradicting claims of applicability to complex, high-dimensional problems.  
- Spectral radius bounds for matrix A grow with hidden units, making the method impractical for high-dimensional data and unaddressed by the paper.  
- The optimality condition appears fragile and may not hold in practice; verifying it requires searching a huge parameter space with no efficient identification method.  
- The role of the special objective in achieving global optimality is unclear—globality may stem from the objective rather than the method’s generality.  
- The paper does not test whether standard optimizers (e.g., SGD) converge to the same global optimum, leaving the algorithmic advantage of NLSM unresolved.  
- Key unresolved questions: managing spectral radius for high-dimensional data, finding efficient parameter-set identification, understanding the objective’s role, and explaining why linear SVM outperforms NLSM without tuning.  
- Recommendations: justify non-negativity and polynomial constraints with real-world motivations, use nonlinear baselines, evaluate on larger datasets, and address spectral radius scalability.  
- Overall verdict: theoretically interesting but of limited practical relevance due to restrictive assumptions, weak experiments, and unresolved scalability and theoretical issues.
