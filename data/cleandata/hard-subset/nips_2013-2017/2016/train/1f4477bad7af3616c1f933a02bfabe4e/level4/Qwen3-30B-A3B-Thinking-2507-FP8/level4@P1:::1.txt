

This paper presents a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks with guaranteed global optimality under specific constraints. The authors focus on a particular class of networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers), demonstrating that their nonlinear spectral method can achieve global convergence with a linear rate. The work builds upon recent advances in global optimization for neural networks [8, 11], but differs significantly in its practical feasibility and theoretical guarantees. Unlike the tensor-based approach of Janzamin et al. [11], which requires the unknown data-generating measure, or the challenging-to-apply method of Haeffele and Vidal [8], the authors' approach only requires nonnegative data (which holds for many real-world applications like images and word frequencies) and provides a straightforward spectral radius condition that can be verified before training.

The main strength of this paper is its theoretical contribution: a rigorous proof of global optimality with linear convergence for a non-trivial neural network class, which is the first practically feasible method achieving such guarantees. The authors develop a novel framework connecting the optimization problem to fixed-point theory on convex cones, using the Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems. The key insight is that by imposing nonnegativity constraints and modifying the objective function (adding a sum of outputs), they can construct a contractive mapping whose unique fixed point corresponds to the global maximizer. This approach avoids the need for hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD), and the convergence condition can be checked a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix.

The paper's primary limitations relate to practical applicability. First, the nonnegativity constraint on weights is restrictive, potentially limiting the model's representational capacity. Second, the method requires careful selection of multiple parameters (hidden units, activation powers, normalization constants) to satisfy the spectral condition ρ(A) < 1, which the authors verify by testing approximately 150,000 parameter combinations in their experiments. Third, while the theoretical guarantees are compelling, the empirical results are somewhat underwhelming: the method achieves competitive performance on UCI datasets but outperforms kernel SVM on only two out of seven datasets (Cancer, Haberman, and Pima for NLSM2), and is generally slightly worse than SVM while being competitive with ReLU networks. The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting that their model is constrained for theoretical reasons, which makes it challenging to match the flexibility of unconstrained ReLU networks.

The paper's strengths include a technically sound theoretical framework, clear presentation of the key proofs (with many details in the supplementary material), and the first demonstration of globally optimal training for neural networks that yields nontrivial classification results. The weaknesses include the restrictive nonnegativity assumption, the need for extensive parameter tuning to satisfy the spectral condition, and the modest empirical performance compared to standard methods.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- First practically feasible method achieving global optimality for non-trivial neural networks
- Rigorous theoretical proof with linear convergence rate
- Clear connection to established fixed-point theory and Perron-Frobenius theory
- Demonstrates the first globally optimal neural network training with real-world performance

Con arguments for rejection:
- Restrictive nonnegativity constraint on weights that limits model flexibility
- Requires extensive parameter tuning to satisfy the spectral condition
- Empirical results are only marginally competitive with standard methods
- Does not scale to deeper networks due to the spectral radius condition

Overall, this is an important theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network optimization, though its practical utility is limited by the restrictive assumptions and parameter sensitivity. The paper would benefit from more discussion of when and why the nonnegativity constraint is reasonable for real applications, and whether the spectral condition can be relaxed for larger networks. Given the theoretical novelty and the importance of global optimization in neural networks, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the practical limitations more thoroughly.
