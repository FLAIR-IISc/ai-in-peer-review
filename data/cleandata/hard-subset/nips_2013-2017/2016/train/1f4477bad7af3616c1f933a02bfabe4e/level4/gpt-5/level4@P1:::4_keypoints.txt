- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably converges to a global optimum for a class of feedforward networks with a linear convergence rate.  
- Global-optimality condition depends only on architecture and is checked via the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix.  
- Method applies to networks with one or two hidden layers using non-standard generalized polynomial activations.  
- Requires nonnegativity constraints on weights, a strong modeling restriction that may limit expressiveness.  
- Offers linear convergence without learning-rate tuning, addressing a common practical SGD issue.  
- The spectral-radius condition can be verified before running the algorithm, making it transparent and practical to check.  
- Empirical results on UCI datasets lag behind RBF-kernel SVMs and sometimes ReLU+SGD, suggesting practical performance limits.  
- Theoretical contribution is novel and technically sound within the restricted class of networks considered.  
- Presentation is generally clear and the main theorem and condition are well explained, though the paper is dense.  
- Practical significance is limited by the weight nonnegativity and specialized activations, reducing real-world applicability.  
- The claim that linear convergence implies convergence “in a few (less than 10) iterations” is overstated and should be softened or qualified.  
- Overall: valuable theoretical advance and verifiable global-optimality path, but empirical shortcomings and modeling constraints weaken practical impact.
