This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights – with a guarantee of global optimality and a linear convergence rate. The authors address a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape. By imposing constraints on the network architecture and utilizing a nonlinear spectral method, they demonstrate theoretical and empirical results supporting global optimization. The work builds upon recent advances in neural network optimization [8, 11] while offering a potentially more practical solution than prior tensor-based methods [11].

The paper is well-structured and provides a detailed mathematical derivation of the proposed method. The authors clearly outline their main result (Theorem 1) and its extension to networks with two hidden layers (Theorem 5). The inclusion of experimental results, albeit on relatively small datasets, strengthens the claims and provides a proof-of-concept. The connection to the Perron-Frobenius theory and the discussion of the convergence conditions are insightful.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. First, the derivation can be dense and difficult to follow in places. The jump from first-order conditions in Theorem 3 to second-order considerations in Theorem 4 isn’t fully explained, leaving the reader wondering about the justification. The role of the arbitrary small epsilon term in the objective function (2) is acknowledged as being primarily for proof purposes, and its practical relevance isn't well established. The insistence on maximizing the 1-norm of connection parameters, contrary to the typical weight decay regularization used in standard neural networks, requires further justification.  While the non-negativity constraint is noted to be suitable for count data, the practical limitations of this constraint are not thoroughly discussed. Furthermore, the experiments, while demonstrating some competitiveness, are limited in scope and do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing techniques like SVMs or ReLU networks. The comparison to SVM feels somewhat motivated by SVM's global optimality rather than a genuine comparison of model capabilities.  The statement that the method has “no free parameter” is misleading, as parameters like the network architecture (number of hidden units, alpha values) and the positive constants ρw, ρu still require tuning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The authors present a novel approach to globally optimizing neural networks, a significant challenge in the field.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with guarantees of global optimality and linear convergence.
*   **Potential Impact:**  If scalable, the proposed method could offer a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent-based approaches.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity, the authors present the method in a generally clear and organized manner.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practicality:** The non-negativity constraints and maximization of the 1-norm may limit the applicability of the method to certain types of data and tasks.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The computational complexity of the nonlinear spectral method is not fully addressed, raising concerns about its scalability to large datasets and networks.
*   **Weak Empirical Validation:** The experiments are limited and do not provide strong evidence of superior performance compared to established methods.
*   **Derivation Clarity:** The mathematical derivation, particularly the transition between Theorems 3 and 4, could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. However, the practical limitations and scalability concerns, along with the limited empirical validation, raise doubts about its immediate impact.  I believe the paper is borderline.  If the authors can address the concerns regarding practicality, scalability, and provide a more compelling empirical evaluation, it would be a stronger candidate for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance with a request for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the constraints and providing a more thorough empirical evaluation.  A clearer explanation of the mathematical steps, especially the link between Theorem 3 and Theorem 4, would also be beneficial.
