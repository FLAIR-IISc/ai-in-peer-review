This paper proposes a method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions. The authors provide a proof of optimal convergence and demonstrate the method's application on a selection of real-world problems.

The problem under investigation is interesting, and theoretical advances in this direction are a welcome contribution. However, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating that the proposed class of neural networks is useful in practice. A key weakness is the limited discussion on the model's expressiveness. The authors raise this issue twice but do not elaborate on the capabilities and limitations of their restricted architecture. A discussion of what functions are representable and what is out of reach for this approach would significantly strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion and comparison with concurrent approaches. The experimental evaluation is also limited. The results do not clarify the boundaries of the model's applicability, and the comparison is restricted to SVMs, ignoring other relevant methods, including alternative training algorithms for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Additionally, the experiments should include information on the computational resources demanded by the compared algorithms to allow for a practical assessment.

While the introduction is well-written, the quality of the presentation could be improved in subsequent sections. Section 2, in particular, does not read fluently.

Specific remarks:
*   The definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the function $\Phi$ lacks motivation. The authors explained the role of the epsilon term in their rebuttal, and this explanation should be incorporated into the paper.
*   Theorem 1 is difficult to parse and should be preceded by an informal explanation to help the reader build intuition.
*   The statement following Theorem 1, that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum," should be explicitly attributed to the authors' experimental observations (as clarified in the rebuttal) rather than being presented as a general fact.
*   Line 60: "polyomial" should be "polynomial".
*   Line 67: "one CAN model."
*   Line 67: The variables p1 and p2 are used without being introduced.
*   Line 67: The notation R_{++} is not defined. This should be clarified in the text.
