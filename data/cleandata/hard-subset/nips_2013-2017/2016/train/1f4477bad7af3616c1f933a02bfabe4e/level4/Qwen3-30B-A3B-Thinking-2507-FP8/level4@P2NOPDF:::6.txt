

The paper introduces a theoretically grounded method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions, proving optimal convergence rates and demonstrating applicability on selected real-world problems. While the theoretical contribution is valuable and the problem is timely, the work falls short in establishing practical utility. The authors briefly note expressiveness limitations of their restricted model (mentioned twice) but fail to discuss actionable solutions or clarify inherent boundaries of their approach. This omission weakens the case for adopting their framework over more expressive alternatives.  

A more thorough comparison with concurrent methods is essential. The experimental section exclusively compares against SVMs, neglecting other neural network training paradigms (e.g., SGD, backpropagation variants) and relevant algorithmic approaches. This omission obscures the proposed method’s relative strengths and weaknesses. Additionally, the experiments lack computational cost analysis—critical for assessing practical viability—omitting runtime, memory usage, or hardware requirements for all compared algorithms. Without this, it remains unclear whether the theoretical advantages translate to feasible real-world deployment.  

The introduction is well-structured, but Section 2 suffers from poor flow and technical inconsistencies. Key notations (e.g., $R_{++}$ on p.67) and variables (e.g., $p_1$, $p_2$) are undefined, creating confusion. Minor grammatical errors and typos persist (e.g., "polyomial" on p.60, "one CAN model" on p.67). The definition of the loss function is clear, but $\Phi$ lacks motivation. The role of $\varepsilon$—clarified in the rebuttal—must be explicitly incorporated into the main text.  

Theorem 1 requires significant improvement. Its technical statement is difficult to parse; an informal explanation should precede the formal proof. Furthermore, the claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" (immediately after Theorem 1) is presented as a general assertion but stems solely from experimental observations. This should be explicitly qualified as empirical, not theoretical, to avoid misleading readers.  

To strengthen the paper, we recommend:  
1. **Addressing expressiveness limitations**: Discuss practical workarounds for the model’s constraints, and clarify which problems remain unsolvable under the proposed framework.  
2. **Expanding experimental comparisons**: Include relevant baselines (e.g., standard neural network training methods, non-SVM alternatives) and report computational resource demands.  
3. **Clarifying key elements**: Integrate $\varepsilon$’s role into the main text, provide informal context for Theorem 1, and define all notations (e.g., $R_{++}$, $p_1$, $p_2$) upon first use.  
4. **Refining Section 2**: Improve sentence flow, correct grammatical issues, and ensure all mathematical symbols are properly introduced.  

The core theoretical insight is promising, but the current presentation does not sufficiently justify the model’s practical relevance or distinguish it from existing approaches. Addressing these gaps would significantly elevate the contribution. As presented, the work remains primarily of theoretical interest without a compelling case for adoption in applied settings.
