

The paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks with a linear convergence rate. The authors establish that under mild conditions (non-negative data), the method provides global optimality guarantees that depend on the network architecture parameters and can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. This appears to be the first practically feasible method achieving such guarantees for neural networks, representing a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

The work builds upon recent advances in global optimization of neural networks [8, 11], but differs by providing a practically implementable solution rather than theoretical insights. Unlike [8] which is challenging to apply in practice, and [11] which requires the computation of a data-generating measure tensor, the proposed method only requires non-negative weights and data.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous global optimality guarantees
- Linear convergence rate enables efficient training (theoretically)
- Conditions for global optimality are easily verifiable through spectral radius computation
- Demonstrates reasonable performance on several UCI datasets

Weaknesses:
- The requirement for non-negative weights significantly limits the method's applicability to most real-world problems
- The activation functions used (generalized polynomials with specific exponents) are highly non-standard and may not be suitable for most practical applications
- Experimental results show slightly worse performance than kernel SVM on most datasets, which raises concerns about practical utility
- The claim that the method "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" (lines 90-91) is misleading as linear convergence does not necessarily imply few iterations

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that is well-supported by the mathematical analysis. However, the restrictive assumptions limit the immediate practical utility of the method. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the method may be valuable in specific contexts where the non-negativity constraints are naturally satisfied, but the limitations should be carefully considered by potential users.
