

The paper proposes a method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions, establishing optimal convergence guarantees and demonstrating applicability to selected real-world problems. The problem is well-motivated, and theoretical advances in this direction are welcome. However, the paper falls short in convincing readers that the proposed model class offers practical utility. Specifically, since the authors explicitly address the expressiveness limitations of their restricted model (twice), they should elaborate on both the scope of problems solvable by their approach and its inherent limitations. Furthermore, the discussion lacks contrast with concurrent approaches, leaving unclear how this work advances beyond existing methods.

The experimental section significantly undermines the practical claims. Notably, the authors only compare against SVMs, omitting relevant baselines: other neural network training methods (e.g., SGD, Adam) and alternative algorithmic paradigms. This omission prevents meaningful assessment of the method’s relative strengths. Additionally, the paper provides no information on computational resources required by the three algorithms (the proposed method, SVM, and any other baseline), making it impossible to evaluate efficiency trade-offs. Without such details, the experimental results fail to clarify the model’s practical applicability boundaries.

The introduction is clear and well-structured. However, the presentation in later sections requires substantial refinement. Section 2, in particular, lacks fluency. Several technical points require clarification:  
- The definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the role of the ε term lacks motivation. As clarified in the rebuttal, this justification should be integrated into the main text.  
- Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation of its intuition and significance before the formal statement. The current phrasing is difficult to parse.  
- The claim immediately following Theorem 1—that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum"—is presented as a general statement but stems solely from experimental observation, as noted in the rebuttal. This should be explicitly clarified.  

Specific typos and notation issues also require correction:  
- Line 60: "polyomial" → "polynomial"  
- Line 67: "one CAN model" → "one CNN model" (assuming typo)  
- Line 67: Variables p₁ and p₂ are introduced without definition  
- Line 67: Notation ℝ₊₊ is used without definition, though several other variables reference it. This notation was clarified in the rebuttal, but its absence in the main text is problematic.  

In summary, while the theoretical contribution is sound, the paper’s practical relevance and experimental evaluation are inadequately supported. The authors should address expressiveness limitations, include relevant comparisons, report computational costs, and clarify the informal explanations and notation. The current presentation, particularly in Section 2, also needs polishing for clarity and flow. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
