This paper presents a compelling theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves a global optimum at a linear convergence rate. The primary constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The authors first prove this theory for neural networks with a single hidden layer and then extend it to networks with multiple hidden layers. They also propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods. Experimental results show that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The strengths of this paper include its contribution as the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with optimal and convergence guarantees. Unlike previous work with convergence guarantees, which often require complicated methods to verify preconditions that may be impossible to check in practice due to their dependence on the data-generating measure, the theory proposed here has relatively simple preconditions that depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of network parameters. The authors demonstrate that their theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real-world datasets.

However, the proposed network still has limited applications due to several constraints. The theory requires both input data and network parameters to be positive, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) to be less than 1. While the non-negativity constraint on input data may not significantly limit potential usage, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the requirement that network parameters be positive may limit the expressiveness of the model. Furthermore, the assumption that all activation functions across hidden layers are different is unnatural and may also limit the network's applicability. Additionally, the authors note that satisfying the spectral radius constraint requires increasing p1 and p2 while decreasing pw and pu, effectively reducing the weights of linear units. If understood correctly, this implies that as the dimension of input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units must decrease to satisfy the spectral radius constraint. When linear weights are very small, activation functions behave similarly to linear functions, and adding more layers merely adds another linear function, which may not increase the network's expressiveness. This raises concerns about the ease of extending this method to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this is an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and this paper deserves acceptance.

Minor comments: It would be interesting to investigate how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network parameter size affect the spectral radius rho(A). For instance, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially with the input dimension, and how should pw and pu be adjusted to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, empirically plotting this relationship on synthetic data could provide valuable insights.
