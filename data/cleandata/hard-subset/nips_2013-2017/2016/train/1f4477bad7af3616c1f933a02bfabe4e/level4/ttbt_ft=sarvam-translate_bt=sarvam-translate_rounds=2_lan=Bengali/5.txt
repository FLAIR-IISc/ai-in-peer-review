This paper discusses and defines a specific type of feedforward neural network that can be used to find a unique global optimizer. As a product, it defines the algorithm for finding a global optimizer and has a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivation is indicative, although some parts are not very clear to me. 1. Theorem 3 involves the calculation of first-order derivatives, as shown in those inequalities, but theorem 4 reaches the level of second-order derivatives. What is the reason for this? 2. The design of such a network implies that the last layer is considered separately from any hidden layer. Since u is a matrix representing the parameters connecting to the hidden layer, is the gradient with respect to u in G^\Phi (3) a gradient in terms of the vectorized u? (Similar questions are raised in many other places, such as u being confined to a ball). One concern (am I missing something?) is why the model needs to maximize the 1-norm of the connecting parameters in (2). In most NN models, the 1-norm needs to be reduced to minimize weights. It seems that the reason for this is that intentionally small epsilon in (2) is only needed to make the gradient strictly positive, so it is V_++. It seems necessary for their proof, but not well supported in practical NNs. There are no free parameters here, what is the basis for saying this? All the data must be non-negative for this to be true, and then the model uses all positive weights (not even zero), so it keeps adding them. How realistic is this design? In the experiment, the proposed NN is compared to the SVM (because the SVM also has a global optimizer?). However, in reality, does such an NN bring any added value in any case compared to other NNs (e.g., the most used NNs, such as ReLU)?
