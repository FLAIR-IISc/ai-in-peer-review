This paper presents a compelling theory demonstrating the existence of a global optimum in a specific class of neural networks, achievable at a linear convergence rate. The primary restriction is that both the input data and the weights of the linear layer must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The authors first establish this theory for neural networks with a single hidden layer, subsequently extending it to networks with multiple hidden layers. Additionally, they propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods. Experimental results show that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on several small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The strengths of this paper include its novelty in proposing a potentially practical neural network with optimal and convergence guarantees. Unlike previous work with convergence guarantees, which often require complex methods to verify preconditions that may be impractical or even impossible to check, the theory presented here has relatively simple preconditions dependent solely on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of network parameters. The authors demonstrate the applicability of their theory to networks of arbitrary depth and show promising results on small real-world datasets.

However, there are limitations to the proposed network. The theory necessitates positive input data and network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (Theorem 1) less than 1. While the non-negativity constraint on input data may not significantly limit potential applications, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the requirement for positive network parameters might restrict the model's expressiveness. Furthermore, the stipulation that all activation functions across hidden layers be different may also limit its applicability. The authors note that satisfying the spectral radius constraint involves increasing p1 and p2 while decreasing pw and pu, effectively reducing the weights of linear units. This adjustment may compromise the network's expressiveness, particularly in deeper architectures, where adding more layers might not provide additional benefits due to the linearization of activation functions.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging initial attempt, and its acceptance is warranted. Some minor comments include curiosity about the impact of p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network parameter size on the spectral radius rho(A). For instance, how does rho(A) change when the input dimension increases, and what adjustments to pw and pu are necessary to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical analysis using synthetic data could provide valuable insights into these relationships.
