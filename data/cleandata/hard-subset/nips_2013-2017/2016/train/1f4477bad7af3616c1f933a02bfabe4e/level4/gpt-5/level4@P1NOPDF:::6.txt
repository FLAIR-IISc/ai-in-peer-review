This paper proposes a method for training, under certain assumptions, a restricted class of feedforward neural networks. The authors provide optimal convergence guarantees and demonstrate applicability on several “real-world” problems. The problem is interesting, and the theoretical contribution is appreciated.

However, the paper falls short of convincing me that the proposed class of networks is practically useful. Since the expressiveness of the restricted model is raised twice in the paper, I strongly encourage a more explicit discussion of what the model can and cannot represent. Please delineate the scope of tasks for which the approach is suitable and those that are out of reach given the imposed restrictions. In addition, related and concurrent approaches should be discussed and contrasted—both alternative training methods for neural networks and competing algorithmic paradigms.

The experimental section does not clarify the limits of applicability either. The comparisons appear to be only against a few SVM baselines, omitting other neural-network training methods and strong non-NN baselines. This makes it hard to assess the practical value of the approach. I would also expect a discussion of computational resources for the three algorithms considered (e.g., wall-clock time, iteration counts, and memory), as this is crucial for evaluating real-world utility.

The introduction is well written, but the presentation thereafter has room for improvement. In particular, Section 2 is difficult to follow. A few specific suggestions:
- While the loss function is straightforward, the function Φ is insufficiently motivated. You explained the role of the ε term in the rebuttal; please include that explanation in the paper.
- Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation and intuition. As written, the statement is hard to parse.
- Immediately after Theorem 1, you state that “the nonlinear spectral method … converges quickly, typically in a few (less than 10) iterations, to the global maximum.” In the rebuttal, you clarified that this is based on experimental observation. Please state this clearly in the paper and distinguish it from the formal guarantees.

Minor issues and typos:
- Line 60: “polyomial” → “polynomial.”
- Line 67: “one CAN model” → presumably “one can model.”
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are used without prior introduction; please define them when they first appear.
- Line 67: R_{++} is used without definition. This is particularly problematic since several other variables are defined in terms of it. Thanks for clarifying this notation in the rebuttal; please include the definition in the paper.

Overall, the theoretical direction is promising, but the paper needs stronger empirical comparisons, a clearer articulation of the model’s expressiveness and limitations, and improvements to exposition (especially Section 2, the motivation for Φ and ε, and the presentation surrounding Theorem 1). Including these changes would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact and clarity.
