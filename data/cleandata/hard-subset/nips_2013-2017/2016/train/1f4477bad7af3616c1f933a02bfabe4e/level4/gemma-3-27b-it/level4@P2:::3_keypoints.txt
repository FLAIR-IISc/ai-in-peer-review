- Presents a theoretical framework proving global optimality and linear convergence for training feedforward networks under non-negativity and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Theory established for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to arbitrary-depth networks.  
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.  
- Experiments on synthetic and UCI datasets show promising, though preliminary, performance.  
- Strength: first practically viable neural network model claimed to have guaranteed global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Strength: conditions are simple and verifiable via the spectral radius of a computable nonnegative matrix, avoiding intractable preconditions.  
- Strength: extension to multiple layers and reasonable benchmark performance strengthen the contribution.  
- Weakness: applicability is limited by requirements of non-negative data and weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Weakness: shifting data can address non-negativity but positivity constraints on parameters may reduce model expressiveness.  
- Weakness: requirement that all activations in a hidden layer be distinct is unnatural and may hinder practicality.  
- Weakness: satisfying the spectral radius constraint may force decreasing linear-unit weights as input dimensionality and network size grow.  
- Weakness: potential trade-off between satisfying theoretical conditions and maintaining expressiveness, especially in deep networks where weights may diminish to near-linear behavior.  
- Originality: novel approach to global optimality and new use of nonlinear spectral methods in this context.  
- Significance: important theoretical step toward understanding NN training dynamics and designing algorithms with guaranteed convergence.  
- Argument for acceptance: provides a theoretically grounded, more practical alternative to prior methods with strong convergence guarantees.  
- Argument for acceptance: linear convergence rate and extension to deeper networks are notable advantages.  
- Argument against acceptance: restrictive constraints limit immediate applicability and may reduce expressiveness.  
- Argument against acceptance: experimental evaluation is limited in scale.  
- Overall assessment: encouraging and valuable contribution with strong theory and modest practical validation, worth acceptance despite limitations.  
- Minor comment: empirically analyze how parameters (p1, p2, pw, pu), input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius ρ(A).  
- Minor comment: discuss the computational complexity of the proposed nonlinear spectral method and its matrix computations.
