This paper investigates a special class of neural networks, where the authors succeed in showing that it has only one stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method converging to the unique fixed-point that is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper investigates an interesting problem of showing a relatively complex model with wisely chosen constraints can have only one stationary point. Apart from convexity (or its generalizations like geodesic convexity), the common practice for showing uniqueness of the stationary point is through using fixed-point theory; the approach that is used in this paper.

The authors construct a contractive map, that its fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. The proposed method has several strengths, including its ability to guarantee global optimality and its fast convergence rate. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the method's properties and its relationship to existing work on neural networks.

One drawback of the proposed method is its dependence on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. As the authors mention, this is the reason why they did not try networks with larger number of hidden units. Another problem with the method is the many parameters that need to be tuned, which makes the use of the procedure rather unintuitive. Furthermore, for each choice of the parameters, the spectral condition needs to be verified.

In the experimental results, the authors tried about 150,000 different combinations of possible parameter values and chose the best of them based on cross-validation accuracy. Their method, however, could outperform linear-SVM for only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result questions the real power of the neural network they are investigating.

Overall, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of neural networks by introducing a new method that guarantees global optimality. However, the method's limitations and the need for further research to improve its applicability and performance are also acknowledged.

Strengths:
- The paper introduces a new method that guarantees global optimality for a special class of neural networks.
- The method has a fast convergence rate and is based on a thorough analysis of the problem.
- The authors provide a detailed analysis of the method's properties and its relationship to existing work on neural networks.

Weaknesses:
- The method depends on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix, which limits its applicability to networks with a large number of hidden units.
- The method requires many parameters to be tuned, which makes its use rather unintuitive.
- The spectral condition needs to be verified for each choice of parameters.
- The experimental results show that the method outperforms linear-SVM for only 2 out of 7 datasets, which questions its real power.

Acceptance:
Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would recommend accepting it for publication. The paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of neural networks, and its limitations are acknowledged and discussed. However, I would suggest that the authors address some of the weaknesses mentioned above, such as improving the method's applicability to networks with a large number of hidden units and reducing the number of parameters that need to be tuned.
