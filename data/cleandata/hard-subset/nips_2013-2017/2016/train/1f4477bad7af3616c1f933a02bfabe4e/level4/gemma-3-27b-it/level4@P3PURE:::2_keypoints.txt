- Contribution: Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably trains a specific class of feedforward networks (1–2 hidden layers) to global optimality with linear convergence and evaluates it on real datasets.  
- Restrictive assumptions: The theoretical guarantees require non-negativity of weights, maximized regularization, and use of uncommon generalized polynomial activations.  
- Expressive power claim weak: Authors assert sufficient expressive power, but empirical results do not convincingly support this.  
- Unfavorable empirical comparisons: Linear SVMs outperform NLSM1/NLSM2, and NLSM models rely on network structure tuning to perform well.  
- Baseline choice questionable: Using a linear SVM as the primary baseline is inappropriate for a nonlinear method; nonlinear SVMs (polynomial/Gaussian kernels) would be fairer.  
- Limited datasets: UCI datasets used are small in sample size and feature dimensionality, limiting generalizability of conclusions.  
- Scalability concern: Bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with number of hidden units, challenging applicability to high-dimensional problems.  
- Practicality of optimality check: Although the optimality condition can be checked without training, it seems impractical without strong priors or in very large parameter spaces.  
- Parameter search problem: There is no efficient method proposed to quickly identify a manageable set of valid parameter candidates.  
- Role of objective unclear: Unclear whether the achieved global optimality is primarily due to the specific objective function rather than the network class or method.  
- Missing SGD comparison: It would be informative to test whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically reach a global optimum on the same datasets.
