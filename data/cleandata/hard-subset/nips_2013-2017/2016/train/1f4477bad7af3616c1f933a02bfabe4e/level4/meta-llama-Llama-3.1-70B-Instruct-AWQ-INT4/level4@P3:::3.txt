This paper presents a novel theory that demonstrates the existence of a global optimum for a certain class of neural networks, which can be achieved through a linear convergence rate. The main constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The proposed theory is first proved for neural networks with a single hidden layer and later extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a theory that provides a global optimality guarantee for a class of neural networks. This is a significant advancement in the field, as previous work with convergence guarantees often required complicated methods to check preconditions, some of which were even impossible to check in practice. In contrast, the theory proposed in this work has relatively simple preconditions that only depend on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of the network parameters.

The authors demonstrate that the proposed theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real-world datasets (UCI datasets). The experiments also show that the proposed algorithm works well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves competitive performance on some small real-world datasets.

However, the proposed network still has limited applications. The theory requires both input data and network parameters to be positive, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) to be smaller than 1. While the non-negative constraint on the input data may not be a significant limitation, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the fact that network parameters are positive might limit the expressiveness of the model. Additionally, the requirement that all activation functions of all hidden layers are different may be unnatural and limit its application.

The authors note that to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, they need to increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, which effectively decreases the weights of linear units. Furthermore, as the dimension of the input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units must be decreased, which may limit the expressiveness of the network. This raises concerns about the ability to extend this method to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this is still an encouraging result as an initial attempt. The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation and demonstrates the potential of nonlinear spectral methods for optimizing neural networks.

Minor comments:

* It would be interesting to investigate how p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius rho(A). For example, when the dimension of the input increases, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially, and how should pw and pu be adjusted to counteract this effect?
* The authors may want to consider providing more details on the choice of parameters for the experiments, such as the selection of p1, p2, pw, and pu.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neural networks and provides a promising direction for future research. While there are some limitations and concerns, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and it is recommended for acceptance.
