This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The approach is applied to deep networks with one or two hidden layers. Experiments are conducted on a set of real-world datasets. As the authors note, the examined class of feedforward networks is restrictive and counterintuitive, imposing nonnegativity on the weights and maximizing the regularization of those weights. In addition, the less common generalized polynomial activation is required to satisfy the optimality conditions. These assumptions appear unreasonable. Detailed, application-driven explanations grounded in real datasets would be more useful than introducing stricter assumptions merely to obtain a global optimum. The authors claim that the targeted class remains sufficiently expressive to model complex decision boundaries and delivers good performance. However, the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. It appears that linear SVM often outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2 even when the linear SVM seems not carefully tuned, while NLSM variants tune their architectures. Moreover, since NLSM is nonlinear, it is puzzling that a linear SVM is used as the baseline rather than a nonlinear SVM (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian/RBF kernels). As a result, the comparisons are not fair, and the conclusions drawn from these comparisons are not convincing. In other words, these results do not demonstrate sufficient expressive power for the studied networks. In the experiments, the UCI datasets are too small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. According to the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which is problematic for high-dimensional data. Is there any way to address this issue? This limitation would significantly hinder the method’s applicability to a large fraction of real-world problems. It also seems that the optimality condition is not guaranteed for any parameter settings that might be used in the proposed networks. Although the condition can be checked without training, this is still problematic if we lack priors or face an enormous parameter space. Is there a fast way to prune to a small set of valid candidates? Does the model’s optimal property primarily stem from the particular objective function? It would be interesting to see whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically converge to a global optimum on some of the tested datasets.
