This paper presents a nonlinear spectral method for training a particular class of feedforward neural networks, for which the authors prove global optimality and a linear convergence rate. The method is demonstrated on networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluated on several real-world datasets.

While the theoretical guarantees are interesting, the work relies on a series of strong and counterintuitive assumptions. The class of networks considered requires non-negative weights and, unusually, maximizes the regularization term for these weights. Furthermore, the global optimality condition necessitates the use of a non-standard generalized polynomial activation function. These assumptions are quite restrictive, and their practical justification is not well established. The paper would be strengthened by providing clearer motivations for these choices from the perspective of real-world applications, rather than imposing them primarily to achieve a theoretical result.

The authors claim that this class of networks still possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieve strong performance. However, the empirical results presented in the paper do not provide convincing support for this claim. Several issues weaken the experimental evaluation:

1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The proposed models (NLSM1 and NLSM2) are compared against a linear SVM. This comparison is not on equal footing. NLSM is a nonlinear model, and a more appropriate baseline would be a nonlinear SVM, for instance, one using a polynomial or RBF kernel. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed model over existing nonlinear classifiers.

2.  **Baseline Tuning:** The linear SVM baseline appears to perform worse than the NLSM models, but it is unclear if the SVM was properly tuned. In contrast, the NLSM models' network structures were tuned for the experiments. This disparity in tuning efforts makes the comparison less credible.

Given these issues, the current results are insufficient to substantiate the claim of sufficient expressive power.

Furthermore, there are concerns regarding the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed method:

*   **Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on UCI repository datasets that are relatively small, both in sample size and feature dimensionality.
*   **Scalability:** The paper's theoretical analysis indicates that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. This suggests potential scalability issues, particularly for high-dimensional problems that may require a larger number of hidden units. Have the authors considered ways to mitigate this issue? This potential limitation could significantly hinder the method's applicability to many real-world problems.
*   **Optimality Condition:** The optimality condition is not guaranteed to hold for an arbitrary network configuration. Although the condition can be checked a priori, this is problematic when there is no prior knowledge to guide the search for valid parameters, as the search space could be vast. Is there an efficient approach to identify a set of valid parameter candidates?

Finally, a few questions remain about the source of the model's properties. To what extent does the global optimality guarantee stem from the specific, and rather unusual, objective function? It would be an interesting ablation study to investigate whether standard stochastic gradient descent can also empirically converge to the global optimum on these specific datasets. If so, it might suggest that the optimization landscape of this restricted model class is benign, rather than highlighting a unique advantage of the proposed spectral method.
