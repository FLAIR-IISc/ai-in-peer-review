

The paper proposes a method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions, establishing optimal convergence guarantees and demonstrating applicability to select real-world problems. The theoretical contribution is valuable, and the problem is well-motivated. However, the paper falls short in convincing readers that the restricted model class is practically useful. Specifically, while the authors address the expressiveness limitations of their model twice, they do not discuss feasible extensions or inherent limitations of their approach. Concurrent methods should be explicitly contrasted to contextualize the contribution. The experimental evaluation is inadequate: the authors exclusively compare against SVMs, omitting other neural network training methods and algorithmic paradigms. Crucially, computational resource requirements for all compared algorithms are unreported. The introduction is strong, but Section 2 lacks fluency and contains minor issues. Typos and notation gaps require correction: "polyomial" (line 60), "one CAN model" (line 67), undefined \(p_1, p_2\) (line 67), and undefined \(\mathbb{R}_{++}\) (line 67)—which is problematic as multiple subsequent variables reference this notation. The loss function definition is clear, but \(\Phi\) lacks motivation; the rationale for \(\varepsilon\) (clarified in the rebuttal) should be incorporated into the main text. Theorem 1 requires an informal explanation prior to its formal statement, as the current phrasing is difficult to parse. The claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" (immediately following Theorem 1) stems from experimental observation and must be explicitly clarified as such.
