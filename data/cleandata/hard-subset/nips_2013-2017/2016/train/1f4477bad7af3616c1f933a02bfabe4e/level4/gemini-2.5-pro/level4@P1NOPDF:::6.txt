This paper proposes a training method for a specific class of feed-forward neural networks, proving its optimal convergence under certain assumptions. The authors demonstrate the method's application on several real-world problems. The problem under investigation is interesting, and theoretical advances in this area are welcome.

However, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed class of neural networks. Since the authors themselves raise the issue of the model's limited expressiveness (on two occasions), the paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of what this class of models can and cannot represent. What are the practical and theoretical limitations of this approach? Furthermore, a discussion and comparison with concurrent approaches is missing.

The experimental section also fails to clarify the model's limitations and applicability. The evaluation is surprisingly narrow, comparing the proposed method only against an SVM. The authors neglect to include comparisons with other training methods for neural networks or alternative algorithmic paradigms. This makes it difficult to assess the practical value and trade-offs of the proposed method. Additionally, the paper lacks crucial information about the computational resources (e.g., time, memory) required by the compared algorithms.

Regarding the presentation, while the introduction is well-written, the quality declines thereafter. Section 2, in particular, is difficult to follow and lacks fluency.

**Specific Remarks:**

*   While the loss function is defined straightforwardly, the motivation for the function $\Phi$ is unclear. The role of the epsilon term, which was clarified in the author rebuttal, should be explicitly explained in the paper.
*   Theorem 1 is difficult to parse. An informal explanation or intuition preceding the formal statement would greatly improve its readability.
*   The claim immediately following Theorem 1—that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum"—is presented as a general statement. The authors clarified in their rebuttal that this is an empirical observation. The paper should be updated to reflect this, clearly stating that this claim is based on experimental results rather than theoretical guarantees.
