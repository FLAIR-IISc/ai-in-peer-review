This paper examines a specific class of neural networks, where the authors manage to show that there can be only one stable point. The authors present a method that converges to the unique stable point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. This paper investigates an interesting problem: how a relatively complex model with intelligently chosen constraints can have only one stable point. Besides the uniqueness of the stable point (or its generalization, such as geodesic stability), the usual way to show the uniqueness of a stable point is to use stable-point theory; this is the approach used in this paper. The authors construct a compressed map, that its stable point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. A drawback of the proposed method is the dependence of the convergence proof on the chromatic radius of some non-trivial matrices. As the authors note, this is why they did not attempt a network with a large number of hidden units. Another problem with the method is the many parameters that must be tuned, which makes the use of the process rather non-intuitive. Furthermore, the chromatic position must be verified for each choice of parameter. In experimental results, the authors tried approximately 150,000 different combinations of potential parameter values and selected the best among them based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method may only outperform linear-SVM for 2 out of 7 datasets. This result raises questions about the actual strength of the neural network they are testing.
