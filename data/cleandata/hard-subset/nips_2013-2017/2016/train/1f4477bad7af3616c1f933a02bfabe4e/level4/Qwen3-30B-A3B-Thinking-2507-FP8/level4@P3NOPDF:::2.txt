

This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rates. The approach targets networks with non-negative weights and employs a specialized generalized polynomial activation function to satisfy optimality conditions. While the theoretical contribution is mathematically rigorous, significant concerns regarding the practicality, experimental design, and empirical validation undermine the paper's core claims.  

The authors' restrictive assumptions require careful justification. Forcing non-negative weights and demanding the generalized polynomial activation function appears counterintuitive and application-agnostic. The paper fails to convincingly argue why these constraints are reasonable in real-world scenarios or how they align with natural data structures. Without concrete examples demonstrating where such constraints arise (e.g., in specific physical or biological systems), the model’s applicability remains questionable. The claim that this restricted class retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries lacks empirical support.  

The experimental evaluation exhibits critical flaws. First, the baseline comparison against linear SVM is fundamentally flawed. NLSM is a nonlinear model, yet the authors contrast it against a linear classifier without testing equivalent nonlinear baselines (e.g., polynomial or RBF kernel SVMs). This creates an unbalanced comparison: linear SVMs inherently cannot model complex boundaries, making their superior performance on several UCI datasets (e.g., Wine, Iris) unsurprising. For instance, on the Wine dataset, linear SVM achieves 95.5% accuracy while NLSM2 reaches only 92.8%, despite NLSM’s structural complexity. Such results *undermine* the claim of strong expressive power, not support it. The authors should replace linear SVM with a nonlinear baseline to meaningfully assess NLSM’s utility.  

Second, the datasets used (UCI repository) are too small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. The paper notes that spectral radius bounds grow with hidden units, yet the experiments do not test scalability on larger datasets where this issue would be critical. For high-dimensional data (e.g., image or text), the method’s feasibility is highly dubious without addressing this scaling problem. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and propose potential mitigations (e.g., regularization techniques or architectural constraints) rather than omitting the discussion.  

The parameter search for valid networks also raises concerns. The optimality condition requires verifying a spectral radius constraint *before* training, but the paper offers no guidance on efficiently identifying feasible parameter ranges. With potentially vast parameter spaces, this could render the method computationally prohibitive for practical use. The authors should address whether this constraint can be satisfied with standard hyperparameter tuning or if specialized search methods are needed.  

Regarding the theoretical contribution, it is unclear whether the global optimality stems from the objective function’s structure or the imposed constraints. The paper does not explore whether stochastic gradient descent (SGD) could converge to the global optimum empirically on their datasets. This is a critical omission, as SGD is the de facto standard for training neural networks. If NLSM’s optimality relies on specific initialization or constraints that SGD cannot satisfy, this further limits its practical relevance.  

In summary, while the theoretical framework is sound for the *specific* constrained class, the paper overstates its applicability. The restrictive assumptions, flawed experimental design, and lack of scalability analysis prevent the results from supporting the claim that this model class is broadly useful for real-world tasks. To strengthen the work, the authors should:  
1) Justify the non-negativity and activation function constraints with real-world applications,  
2) Replace linear SVM with a nonlinear baseline in experiments,  
3) Test on larger, higher-dimensional datasets to address scalability,  
4) Propose methods for efficient parameter validation, and  
5) Investigate SGD’s behavior on their model.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution remains largely theoretical and disconnected from practical machine learning challenges. The current results do not demonstrate that the proposed class of networks offers advantages over standard nonlinear models in real applications. The method’s potential utility is therefore significantly overstated.
