- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provably trains a restricted class of feedforward networks to global optimality with linear convergence, shown on one- and two-hidden-layer models and real datasets.

- Network class is highly restrictive (non-negative weights, heavy weight regularization, and required generalized polynomial activations), raising practical applicability concerns.

- Authors claim sufficient expressive power for the constrained class, but empirical results do not convincingly support this claim.

- Comparison to linear SVMs is problematic: linear SVMs often outperform NLSM methods, and a fairer baseline would be nonlinear SVMs (polynomial/Gaussian kernels).

- Experiments use small UCI datasets with low dimensionality, limiting conclusions about real-world performance.

- Spectral radius bounds grow with the number of hidden units, suggesting scalability issues for high-dimensional models and unclear remedies.

- Provable optimality depends on specific conditions that are computationally hard to verify over large or continuous parameter spaces; efficient candidate-identification methods are needed.

- Future work should examine whether global optimality stems from the chosen objective and whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically reach global optima on these problems.
