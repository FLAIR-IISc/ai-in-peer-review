### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, termed Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks, to a global optimum. The core contribution is a "nonlinear spectral method" which, under certain conditions, is proven to converge linearly to the unique global maximizer of a modified objective function. The method is built upon a solid theoretical foundation, recasting the optimization problem as finding the unique fixed point of a contraction mapping defined on a positive cone, leveraging tools from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric. The authors present the theory for one-hidden-layer networks and demonstrate its extension to two-hidden-layer networks. A key feature of the method is that the condition guaranteeing global optimality—that the spectral radius ρ(A) of a specific matrix A is less than one—can be checked *a priori* for a given network architecture. The paper contrasts this with prior work on globally optimal training (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele & Vidal, 2015), arguing that the proposed method is more practical as it does not require knowledge of the data-generating distribution or solving a difficult tensor decomposition problem. The authors provide experimental results on several UCI datasets to demonstrate the method's feasibility and performance.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its significant theoretical contribution. Tackling the non-convex nature of neural network optimization with provable guarantees of global optimality is a fundamental and challenging problem. The proposed nonlinear spectral method is a highly original approach, departing from standard gradient-based techniques. The connection drawn between neural network training and fixed-point theory on convex cones is insightful and novel for this community.

2.  **Provable Guarantees:** The paper provides a rare and valuable result: an algorithm with a formal proof of convergence to the global optimum at a linear rate. The condition for this guarantee is explicit and can be verified without running the optimization, which is a strong theoretical property.

3.  **Clarity of Theoretical Exposition:** The authors do a commendable job of outlining their proof strategy. The paper is well-structured, systematically building the theoretical machinery from the optimization problem to the application of the Banach fixed-point theorem in the context of the Thompson metric.

4.  **Inclusion of Experiments:** Unlike some purely theoretical papers on this topic, the authors make a genuine effort to demonstrate their method's practical performance on real-world datasets. This is a crucial step in bridging the gap between theory and practice.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Highly Restrictive Model Assumptions:** The practical applicability of the proposed method is severely limited by its strong assumptions, which seem tailored to fit the theoretical framework rather than being motivated by practical or learning-theoretic considerations.
    *   **Non-negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative data and weights is a major limitation. Many standard preprocessing steps, such as data standardization, are incompatible with this.
    *   **Non-standard Activation and Objective:** The use of "generalized polynomial" activations is unconventional. More critically, the objective function is modified to include the sum of all network outputs as a positive term. This is counter-intuitive from a standard learning perspective and lacks clear justification beyond being a technical necessity for the proof. A more detailed explanation of the implications of this objective would be beneficial.

2.  **Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claim that the model is "rich enough to achieve good performance" is not strongly supported by the experimental results.
    *   **Performance Comparison:** On most datasets, the proposed NLSM1 and NLSM2 methods are outperformed by the baseline RBF-kernel SVM and standard ReLU networks. While the method does outperform ReLU networks on a few datasets, its overall performance is not competitive with well-established nonlinear classifiers. This raises questions about the practical trade-off being made: we gain a theoretical guarantee but appear to lose generalization performance.
    *   **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are limited to small, low-dimensional datasets. The authors rightly note that the bounds ensuring ρ(A) < 1 deteriorate as the number of hidden units grows. Since high-dimensional problems typically require wider networks, this suggests the method may not be scalable to more complex, real-world applications. This is a significant limitation that deserves more prominent discussion.

3.  **Potential Conflict Between Optimality Guarantee and Model Performance:** The condition ρ(A) < 1 can be satisfied by choosing the norm parameters (pu, pw) to be sufficiently large. However, these parameters are also hyperparameters of the model. This creates a potential dilemma where the hyperparameter settings required to guarantee optimality might not be the same as those that yield the best generalization performance. It is unclear how to navigate this trade-off in practice.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Justification for Model Choices:** The authors should provide a more thorough justification for their model's restrictive assumptions. Are there specific application domains (beyond count data) where non-negative weights and the modified objective function are natural or beneficial?
*   **Strengthening Experimental Analysis:**
    *   To better isolate the contribution of the optimization algorithm, it would be highly informative to see a comparison where a standard optimizer like SGD or Adam is used to optimize the *exact same* modified objective function (Eq. 2). This would clarify whether the observed performance is due to the model class and objective, or the optimization method itself.
    *   A more direct discussion of the scalability limitations would strengthen the paper. Could the theoretical bounds on the matrix A be tightened to allow for more hidden units, making the method applicable to larger problems?
*   **Clarification on Hyperparameter Selection:** Please elaborate on the practical trade-off between choosing hyperparameters to ensure the optimality condition (ρ(A) < 1) and choosing them to maximize cross-validation performance. Are these goals often in conflict?

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically elegant and novel solution to the problem of globally optimal neural network training. However, its practical relevance is constrained by strong model assumptions and an experimental evaluation that does not demonstrate a compelling performance advantage. While the theoretical contribution is significant, the paper would be stronger if it addressed the aforementioned weaknesses regarding the practicality and scalability of the proposed framework.
