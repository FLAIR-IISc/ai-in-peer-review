

This paper presents a theoretically sound approach demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves a global optimum with linear convergence. The key constraints require all input data and linear layer weights to be non-negative, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix derived from network parameters to be less than 1. The theory is first established for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently extended to multi-hidden-layer architectures. An optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods is proposed. Experiments demonstrate effective performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on several small UCI real-world datasets.  

Pros: This work represents the first proposal of a potentially practical neural network with provable optimality and convergence guarantees. Previous convergence-guaranteed methods often required complex, practically uncheckable preconditions dependent on the data-generating measure. In contrast, the proposed theory features comparatively simple preconditions solely dependent on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix formed from network parameters. The authors successfully demonstrate applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and achieve reasonable results on small real datasets.  

Cons: The theory's applicability remains limited. The requirements—non-negative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations, and spectral radius < 1—significantly constrain potential use cases. While input non-negativity can likely be addressed via data shifting, the requirement for positive network parameters may limit model expressiveness. Additionally, the necessity for distinct activation functions across all hidden layers is unnatural and restrictive. Crucially, satisfying the spectral radius constraint necessitates increasing parameters \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) while decreasing \(p_w\) and \(p_u\), effectively reducing linear unit weights. As input dimension and network parameters grow, this forces further weight reduction. When weights become sufficiently small, activation functions approximate linearity, rendering additional layers merely additive linear transformations that fail to enhance expressiveness. Consequently, I am concerned that this approach may not scale effectively to deeper architectures. Despite these limitations, this constitutes a promising initial contribution, and I recommend acceptance.  

Minor comments: It would be valuable to explore how \(p_1\), \(p_2\), \(p_w\), \(p_u\), input dimension, and network parameter size influence the spectral radius \(\rho(\mathbf{A})\). Specifically, does \(\rho(\mathbf{A})\) increase linearly or exponentially with input dimension? To counteract this, should \(p_w\) and \(p_u\) be decreased linearly or exponentially? While a formal proof may be challenging, empirical analysis on synthetic data could provide useful insights into this relationship.
