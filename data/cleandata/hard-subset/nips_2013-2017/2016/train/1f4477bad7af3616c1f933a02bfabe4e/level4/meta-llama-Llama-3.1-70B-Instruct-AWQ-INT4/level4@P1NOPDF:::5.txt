The paper discusses and defines a specific type of feedforward neural network for which a unique global optimizer can be obtained. As a result, it introduces an algorithm that finds this global optimizer, achieving a linear convergence rate. Although the mathematical derivation is instructive, a few parts are not entirely clear to me.

Firstly, I have some concerns regarding the transition from first-order to second-order derivatives between Theorem 3 and Theorem 4. Theorem 3 involves the computation of first-order derivatives, as shown in the given inequalities, whereas Theorem 4 operates at the second-derivative level. What triggers this shift?

Secondly, the design of this network architecture appears to treat the last layer separately from any hidden layer. Since u is also a matrix representing the parameters connecting to the hidden layer, I assume the gradient with respect to u in G^\Phi (3) is simply the gradient in terms of the vectorization of u. Similar questions arise in several other places, such as bounding u to be within a ball.

I also have some concerns regarding the model's requirement to maximize the 1-norm of the connection parameters in equation (2). In most neural network models, weight decay involves reducing the 1-norm. It seems that the introduction of an arbitrarily small epsilon in equation (2) is solely to ensure the gradient is strictly positive, thereby belonging to V_++. While this may be necessary for the proof, it does not seem to be well-justified in the context of practical neural networks. What guarantees that there are no free parameters in this design?

Furthermore, the proposed model requires all data to be non-negative and uses all positive weights (not even zero), resulting in continuous addition. How practical is this design? The experimental comparison with SVM is understandable, given that SVM also has a global optimizer. However, in practice, I wonder whether this type of neural network will offer additional value in some aspects compared to other, more commonly used neural networks (e.g., those with ReLU).
