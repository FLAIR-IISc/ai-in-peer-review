- Presents a novel theoretical result showing a class of neural networks attains global optimum with linear convergence when inputs, linear weights, and activations are generalized polynomials.  
- Theory developed for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to multi-hidden-layer architectures.  
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods.  
- Experiments show strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on selected UCI benchmarks.  
- Major positive: first practical neural-network framework with provable global optimality and convergence under simple preconditions (spectral radius of a derived nonnegative matrix < 1).  
- Positive: applicability demonstrated for arbitrarily deep networks and meaningful results on real-world data.  
- Major negative: constraints (non-negative inputs, strictly positive weights, distinct activations per layer) significantly limit practical expressiveness.  
- Critical scalability concern: enforcing the spectral radius condition requires adjusting parameters (increase p1,p2; decrease pw,pu) that scale down linear weights, causing activations to become approximately linear as input dimension and depth grow.  
- Conclusion: despite limitations, the work is a valuable theoretical advance and recommended for acceptance.  
- Minor comment: request empirical/theoretical analysis of how ρ(A) scales with input dimension and network size and what scaling of pw/pu is needed to maintain ρ(A) < 1.
