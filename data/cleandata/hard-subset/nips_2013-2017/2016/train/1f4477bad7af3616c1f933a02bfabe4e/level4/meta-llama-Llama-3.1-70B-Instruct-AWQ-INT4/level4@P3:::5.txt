The paper proposes a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks with a unique global optimizer. The authors introduce a nonlinear spectral method that achieves a linear convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over traditional stochastic gradient descent methods. The paper is well-structured, and the mathematical derivations are instructive, providing a clear understanding of the proposed method.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The authors show that under certain conditions, the proposed method can achieve a global optimum, which is a significant advancement in the field of neural networks. The paper also provides experimental results on several UCI datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. However, there are a few areas that require clarification. Firstly, the transition from Theorem 3 to Theorem 4 seems to involve a jump from first-order derivatives to second-order derivatives, which is not clearly explained. Secondly, the design of the network appears to treat the last layer separately from the hidden layers, which may raise concerns about the practicality of the proposed method.

Another concern is the requirement to maximize the 1-norm of the connection parameters in the objective function. This seems counterintuitive, as most neural network models use weight decay to reduce the 1-norm. The authors justify this by stating that the arbitrarily small epsilon in the objective function is needed to make the gradient strictly positive. However, this may not be a sufficient justification, and further explanation is needed to understand the practical implications of this design choice.

The experimental results are promising, but it is not clear whether the proposed method offers any additional benefits over other neural network models. The comparison with SVM is interesting, but it would be more informative to compare the proposed method with other neural network models, such as those using ReLU activation functions.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of neural networks. However, there are some areas that require further clarification and explanation to fully understand the proposed method and its implications.
