

This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The approach is applied to deep networks with one- and two-hidden layers, and experiments are conducted on several real-world datasets. However, the class of networks studied imposes restrictive and counterintuitive assumptions: non-negativity constraints on all weights and maximization of regularization on these weights. Additionally, the optimality condition requires the less common generalized polynomial activation function. These assumptions appear unjustified without deeper justification from an application or real-world dataset perspective. While the authors claim this class retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries and achieves strong performance, the empirical results do not strongly support this assertion. Notably, linear SVM outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2 in scenarios where linear SVM is not finely tuned, whereas NLSM tunes its network structures. Moreover, the use of linear SVM as a baseline for a nonlinear model (NLSM) is problematic; a nonlinear baseline such as polynomial or Gaussian kernel SVM would be more appropriate, rendering the comparisons unfair and conclusions unconvincing. Thus, the results fail to demonstrate the claimed expressive power. The experiments rely solely on small UCI repository datasets, which are limited in both sample size and feature dimensionality. Given that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, this approach is problematic for high-dimensional data and may severely restrict applicability. The authors should address how to handle this spectral scaling issue. Furthermore, the optimality condition cannot be guaranteed for arbitrary parameter settings, and while it can be verified without training, the absence of priors or the potential for extremely large parameter spaces renders it impractical. It would be valuable to explore efficient methods for identifying a small set of valid parameter candidates. Finally, it remains unclear whether the optimal properties derive from the specific objective function, and it would be insightful to investigate whether stochastic gradient descent also converges to global optima on the tested datasets.
