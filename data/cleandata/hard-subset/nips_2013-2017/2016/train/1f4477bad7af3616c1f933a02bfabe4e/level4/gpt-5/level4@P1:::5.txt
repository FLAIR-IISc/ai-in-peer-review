Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that yields global optimization guarantees for a class of feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations, nonnegative inputs, and strictly positive weights. The main idea is to recast training as a constrained maximization of a modified objective (loss minus cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs and a small linear term on parameters), then apply fixed-point theory on cones using the Thompson metric and Perron–Frobenius–type arguments. Under a checkable condition—spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A is less than 1—the method converges linearly to a unique global maximizer. Theorems are given for one and two hidden layers; in principle the approach could extend to deeper networks. Experiments on several UCI datasets show competitive performance relative to ReLU networks and somewhat below RBF-SVM.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to recent attempts to obtain global guarantees for neural networks via tensor methods and structured matrix factorization (e.g., Janzamin et al., Haeffele & Vidal), but aims to be more practical by avoiding tensor score functions and by delivering a simple spectral condition that can be checked before training. It also leverages nonnegative matrix and nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a fixed-point map GΦ with linear convergence to the unique global optimum under a spectral condition that depends only on architectural/norm parameters (and an upper bound on data norms).
- The condition ρ(A) < 1 is easy to verify in advance and has interpretable dependencies (e.g., increases with the number of hidden units, decreases with pw, pu).
- The analysis is technically interesting, bridging nonlinear eigenproblems and neural training.
- Empirically, the method achieves nontrivial decision boundaries and competitive accuracy on several datasets, with rapid convergence and no learning-rate tuning.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity of derivative orders: Theorem 3 involves Lipschitz bounds that depend on first-order derivatives of GΦ (which themselves involve first-order derivatives of Φ). In Theorem 4, the derivation appears to require second derivatives. What precisely necessitates moving “to the second derivative level” when constructing matrix A in Theorem 4? A short, explicit explanation of where Hessian terms enter (and whether they can be bounded via first-order quantities) would improve readability.
- Treatment and notation for u: The gradient steps for u in GΦ (Eq. 3) seem to operate on the vectorization of the matrix u. Is GΦ defined with respect to vec(u), and are the norms/balls for u the induced vector norms on Rn1×d? Please clarify this consistently wherever balls, gradients, and normalization appear for u (and analogously in the two-layer case for v).
- Modified objective and positivity: The training objective adds the sum of outputs and a positive linear term in the parameters, and an arbitrarily small ε is introduced to ensure ∇Φ ∈ V++. This is opposite to common weight decay (which penalizes large weights). If the linear term is mainly a technical device to guarantee strict positivity and enable the Perron–Frobenius framework, please discuss its practical effect: does it bias parameters to be unnecessarily large? How sensitive are results to ε? Could an alternative formulation (e.g., a barrier or a small entropic regularizer) enforce strict positivity without encouraging growth of the 1-norm?
- “No free parameter” claim: While the method needs no step-size, there remain several design choices (n1, n2, α, β, ρw, ρu, ρv, pw, pu, pv). In particular, pw, pu, pv must satisfy conservative lower bounds to ensure ρ(A) < 1, and α must be chosen with distinct exponents to avoid triviality. Please clarify in what sense the algorithm has “no free parameter,” and provide guidance for selecting these quantities beyond cross-validation.
- Practicality and expressivity: The approach requires nonnegative data and strictly positive weights. Many modern architectures rely on signed weights and activations (e.g., ReLU with negative weights). How restrictive is the nonnegativity constraint in practice? Are there classes of problems (e.g., count data, histograms, nonnegative images) where this inductive bias is natural and advantageous? Also, the requirement αi ≠ αj to ensure uniqueness suggests specific architectural asymmetry; please discuss the trade-off with expressivity and invariance.
- Empirical scope: Results are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, with performance generally below kernel SVM. It would help to provide more insight into failure modes and strengths relative to ReLU/SVM, and to discuss scalability as the number of hidden units grows (since the spectral radius and bounds worsen). The comparison protocol is fair overall, but more detail on the relative hyperparameter search budgets (100 random NLSM configs vs grid searches for ReLU/SVM) and computational cost would strengthen the empirical picture.

Quality
- The theoretical results are carefully developed and appear sound, though some steps (Theorem 3 vs Theorem 4) need clearer exposition of derivative orders and matrix/vectorization conventions.
- The fixed-point contraction analysis and use of the Thompson metric are elegant.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally well organized. Please clarify: (i) how gradients and norms are defined for matrix variables (explicitly state vec(u) and the chosen p-norm), (ii) why Theorem 4 needs second derivatives, and (iii) the practical role and magnitude of ε. A brief intuitive summary of why adding the sum of outputs helps ensure strict positivity of gradients would be helpful.

Originality
- Combining nonlinear spectral methods with constrained polynomial networks to obtain global optimality and linear convergence is novel and interesting relative to tensor or factorization-based guarantees.

Significance
- The significance hinges on practicality under the nonnegativity and modified-objective constraints. The work advances theory meaningfully; demonstrating clear practical advantages on domains naturally satisfying the assumptions (e.g., text counts, nonnegative image features) would enhance impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical guarantees with a simple verifiable condition and linear convergence.
- Bridges important mathematical tools to NN training.
- First empirical demonstration (to my knowledge) of globally optimal NN training with nontrivial performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Restrictive modeling assumptions (nonnegative data/weights, modified objective with positive linear term).
- Open questions about the necessity of second derivatives, vectorization/normalization details, and the “no free parameter” claim.
- Limited empirical scope; performance often below SVM and only competitive with ReLU on small datasets.

Questions for the authors
1) Please clarify why Theorem 4 requires second-order derivatives while Theorem 3 relies on first-order bounds.
2) Is the gradient in GΦ with respect to u computed on vec(u), and are all norms/balls defined in that vector space?
3) How should practitioners set α, ρw, ρu (and pw, pu) in a principled way? How sensitive is performance to ε?
4) Can you better motivate the practical benefit of adding the sum of outputs and a positive linear term on parameters, beyond ensuring strict positivity for the proofs?
5) In what real-world settings do you expect this architecture to offer clear advantages over ReLU networks or SVMs?
