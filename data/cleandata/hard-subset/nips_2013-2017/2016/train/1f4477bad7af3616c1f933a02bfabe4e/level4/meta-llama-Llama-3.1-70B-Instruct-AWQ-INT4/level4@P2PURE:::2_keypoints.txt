- Studies a restricted class of feedforward networks trained to global optimality via a nonlinear spectral method but imposes strong constraints (non-negative weights, maximized regularization).  
- Non-negativity and maximal regularization assumptions are unrealistic and may limit practical applicability.  
- Requires a generalized polynomial activation function for optimality without adequate justification.  
- Authors claim sufficient expressive power despite restrictions, but this is not convincingly supported empirically.  
- Experimental results are weak: linear SVM outperforms NLSM variants even with less tuning, undermining the expressive-power claim.  
- Using only a linear SVM baseline (not nonlinear SVMs like RBF or polynomial kernels) makes comparisons unfair and conclusions unconvincing.  
- Datasets are small UCI sets with low sample size and dimensionality, limiting the experiments’ relevance to real-world/high-dimensional tasks.  
- The spectral radius bound of matrix A grows with number of hidden units, posing scalability and applicability issues for high-dimensional data.  
- The optimality condition is not guaranteed for arbitrary parameter settings; checking parameters is possible but intractable without priors or with many candidates.  
- A fast method to produce a small set of valid parameter candidates is needed but not provided.  
- It’s unclear whether global optimality stems from the special objective; authors should test whether SGD can empirically reach the global optimum on the same datasets.
