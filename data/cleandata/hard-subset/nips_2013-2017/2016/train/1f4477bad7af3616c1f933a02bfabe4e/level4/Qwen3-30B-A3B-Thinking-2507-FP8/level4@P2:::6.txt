

This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors prove that under mild assumptions (nonnegative data and weights), their generalized polynomial neural networks can be trained to global optimality with linear convergence rate. This represents a theoretically significant advance over standard stochastic gradient descent methods, which lack global convergence guarantees. The work builds on recent efforts to achieve global optimality in neural network training [8, 11], but overcomes practical limitations of prior approaches that require data density estimation or face computational challenges.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous proofs demonstrating global convergence under verifiable conditions (Theorem 1). The method provides a rare practical guarantee of global optimality for neural networks, which is a significant theoretical advance. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach through detailed conditions for convergence.
- **Originality:** The paper presents a novel approach to neural network optimization using nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. This differs substantially from prior work [8, 11] by avoiding data density estimation and providing a practically implementable algorithm.
- **Significance:** The method represents a meaningful step toward theoretically grounded neural network training, potentially enabling more reliable and efficient training procedures. The demonstration of global optimality for non-trivial neural network architectures is a valuable contribution to the field.
- **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, with clear definitions and a logical progression from theoretical foundations to algorithmic implementation. The supplementary material provides necessary technical details for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Practical relevance:** The authors acknowledge limitations in model expressiveness due to nonnegativity constraints and specific activation functions, but do not adequately address what practical applications are feasible with their approach. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical expressiveness.
- **Experimental evaluation:** The experimental results are limited to a small set of UCI datasets and comparison only to SVM and ReLU networks. The paper fails to compare with other relevant methods (e.g., other global optimization approaches for neural networks, or alternative constrained optimization techniques). No computational resource metrics (time, memory) are provided, making it difficult to assess practical feasibility.
- **Presentation:** The paper contains several typos (e.g., "polyomial" on page 60) and inconsistent notation (R_{++} introduced without definition). Theorem 1 is particularly difficult to parse without an informal explanation before its formal statement. The claim that "the method converges quickly typically in a few iterations" (immediately after Theorem 1) is based on experimental observation but lacks proper contextualization in the main text.
- **Comparison with related work:** The paper does not sufficiently contrast its approach with concurrent methods. The authors should better explain why their method represents an improvement over existing theoretically grounded approaches, particularly regarding practical applicability.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:*
- Provides the first practically feasible method for globally optimal training of neural networks
- Offers rigorous theoretical guarantees with verifiable conditions
- Demonstrates competitive performance on benchmark datasets
- Avoids hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD variants)
- Well-structured theoretical development

*Con:*
- Limited discussion of practical limitations and applicability
- Insufficient experimental comparison with relevant baselines
- Incomplete treatment of model expressiveness trade-offs
- Presentation issues that hinder readability
- Lack of computational resource analysis

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is strong and significant, the paper falls short in demonstrating the practical relevance of the proposed method. The experimental evaluation is insufficient to convince reviewers that the restricted model class has meaningful real-world utility, and the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental evaluation (including more comprehensive comparisons and computational analysis) and provide a clearer discussion of practical trade-offs before this work could be considered for publication.

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution to neural network optimization, but the current version does not sufficiently establish its practical significance or address key limitations. With the recommended improvements, this could become a valuable contribution to the field.
