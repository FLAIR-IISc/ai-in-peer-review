This paper proposes a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks with a global optimality guarantee using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors focus on a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and a generalized polynomial activation function, which is less common in practice. The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers, and experiments are conducted on several real-world datasets.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a globally optimal training algorithm for a particular class of neural networks. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the optimization problem and propose a nonlinear spectral method to solve it. The method is shown to have a linear convergence rate, and the authors provide a detailed proof of the convergence guarantee.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a comparison with other methods, including stochastic gradient descent and support vector machines.

However, there are some limitations and concerns with the paper. Firstly, the class of neural networks studied is quite restrictive, and the assumptions made to achieve global optimality may not be reasonable in practice. The non-negativity constraint on the weights and the use of a generalized polynomial activation function may limit the expressiveness of the model. Additionally, the experimental results do not provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method, as the performance of the method is not significantly better than that of other methods.

Furthermore, the comparison with other methods is not entirely fair, as the authors use a linear support vector machine as a baseline, whereas a nonlinear support vector machine would be a more appropriate comparison. The authors also do not provide a clear explanation of why the proposed method is better than other methods, such as stochastic gradient descent, which can also converge to a global optimum empirically.

In terms of the applicability of the method, the authors do not provide a clear discussion of how to deal with high-dimensional data, which is a common problem in many applications. The bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which may make the method problematic for high-dimensional data.

Overall, while the paper provides a novel approach to training neural networks with a global optimality guarantee, there are some limitations and concerns that need to be addressed. The authors should provide more evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method and discuss its limitations and potential applications in more detail.
