- Proposes a nonlinear spectral fixed-point method to train feedforward nets with nonnegative weights and polynomial activations to global optimality with linear convergence under a spectral-radius condition.

- Training objective mixes the negative loss with an output-sum positivity term and a small positive bias on weights for technical reasons.

- Main result: if the spectral radius of a specific nonnegative matrix is < 1, the iteration converges linearly to a unique global optimum; the condition is a priori checkable and depends on architecture and norms.

- Framework extends from one to two hidden layers; analysis leverages nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, multihomogeneous maps, and the Thompson metric.

- Experiments on several UCI datasets serve as a proof-of-concept and show fast convergence in practice.

- Claimed advantages vs prior work: avoids score-function tensor estimation, requires only nonnegative data, and reduces global optimality to a spectral-radius check.

- Strength: clear, rigorous optimization framework with explicit, checkable conditions and an a priori computable contraction factor requiring no learning-rate tuning.

- Strength: extension to two hidden layers and discussion of how spectral radius depends on architecture.

- Weakness: strong modeling constraints (nonnegative inputs/weights, polynomial activations, modified objective) that limit applicability.

- Weakness: limited expressivity and empirical validation—small, low-dimensional datasets and mixed performance vs strong baselines.

- Weakness: scalability unclear since bounds imply worse spectral radius and contraction with more hidden units.

- Weakness: baselines are limited; missing comparisons to polynomial-kernel SVMs, nonnegative/constrained NNs, and ablations of the modified objective and constraints.

- Weakness: limited practical guidance for selecting pw, pu, α and architecture to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 in larger models; reliance on random search may not scale.

- Weakness: missing sensitivity/ablation studies on distinct α exponents, output-sum term magnitude, and ε bias versus predictive performance.

- Weakness: unclear handling of signed inputs—no concrete preprocessing or reparameterization recommendations that preserve guarantees.

- Clarity: theoretical development is thorough but dense; needs more intuition on why added terms ensure gradient positivity and how multihomogeneous structure induces contraction; fix incomplete references and notation issues.

- Quality: mathematical development appears sound but relies on conservative bounds; suggest discussing tighter bounds or empirical Lipschitz estimates.

- Originality: novel combination of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory with NN training and an explicit a priori convergence condition.

- Significance: theoretically interesting and potentially influential, but practical impact is uncertain without broader, larger-scale empirical validation on naturally nonnegative datasets.

- Pros for acceptance: strong theory, simple parameter-free iteration, and potential implications for structured optimization.

- Cons for acceptance: restrictive assumptions, modest empirical validation, and limited guidance for scalable parameter selection.

- Suggestions: provide practical recipes for pw/pu/α and architecture choices, add larger nonnegative dataset experiments, report empirical contraction factors/spectral radii, include ablations isolating key modeling choices, and add more aligned baselines and preprocessing strategies for signed data.

- Overall: thought-provoking theoretical contribution that needs stronger empirical validation and clearer practical guidance to increase real-world impact.
