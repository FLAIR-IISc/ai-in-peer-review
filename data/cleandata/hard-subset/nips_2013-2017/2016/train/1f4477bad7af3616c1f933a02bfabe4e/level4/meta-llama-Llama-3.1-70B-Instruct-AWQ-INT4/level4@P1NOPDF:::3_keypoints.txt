- Presents a theory that a specific class of neural networks has a global optimum achievable at a linear convergence rate.
- Requires all input data and linear-layer weights to be positive and activation functions to be generalized polynomials.
- Theory developed for single hidden-layer networks and extended to multi-hidden-layer networks.
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.
- Experiments: strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on small UCI datasets.
- Strength: novel result with simple, verifiable preconditions depending only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative parameter matrix.
- Strength: first potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees (unlike prior works with hard-to-check conditions).
- Applicability: theory extends to arbitrary depth and demonstrates some empirical viability on small real datasets.
- Limitation: positivity requirement for network parameters may limit model expressiveness despite inputs being shiftable to positive.
- Limitation: all hidden-layer activations must be different, which is unnatural and restrictive.
- Limitation: spectral radius constraint (ρ(A) < 1) forces increasing p1/p2 and decreasing pw/pu, effectively shrinking linear weights and reducing expressiveness as depth/dimension grows.
- Concern: scaling behavior unclear — as input/network dimension increases, required weight reductions may prevent useful deep architectures.
- Recommendation: paper is encouraging and should be accepted.
- Minor suggestion: empirically study/plot how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius ρ(A) (e.g., linear vs. exponential growth) and how to adjust pw/pu.
