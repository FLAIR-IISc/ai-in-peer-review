- Presents a novel method for training Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with non-negative weights using a nonlinear spectral formulation.  
- Provides provable global optimality and linear convergence guarantees under specified conditions.  
- Circumvents typical non-convexity by casting training as a nonlinear spectral problem.  
- Thorough theoretical analysis with theorems ensuring global convergence.  
- Connects the approach to Perron–Frobenius theory and non-negative matrix literature for motivation and context.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly presents the theoretical framework.  
- Experiments on several UCI datasets demonstrate feasibility and competitive performance as a proof-of-concept.  
- Strong assumptions (non-negativity of data and weights) limit applicability and may reduce expressiveness.  
- The objective’s maximization of the sum of outputs (to ensure positive gradient) needs clearer explanation for non-expert readers.  
- Derived bounds on parameters (pu and pw) appear conservative and could be tightened or better acknowledged.  
- The relationship between spectral radius and practical convergence speed is not fully explored or intuitively explained.  
- Difference in derivative order between Theorems 3 and 4 is due to Banach fixed-point theorem requirements for smoothness and Lipschitz continuity.  
- Treating u as a matrix and associated gradient computations are mathematically standard and sound.  
- Maximizing the sum of connection parameters is unconventional but essential for enabling Perron–Frobenius–based guarantees.  
- Claim of “no free parameters” is technically correct under constraints but practical impacts of those constraints need further study.  
- Comparison to SVM is reasonable, but more discussion is needed on advantages versus common architectures (e.g., ReLU networks).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical rigor, demonstrated practical feasibility, and significant contribution to optimization theory.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, limited experimental scope (small datasets), and potentially overly conservative parameter bounds.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify explanations and acknowledge limitations.
