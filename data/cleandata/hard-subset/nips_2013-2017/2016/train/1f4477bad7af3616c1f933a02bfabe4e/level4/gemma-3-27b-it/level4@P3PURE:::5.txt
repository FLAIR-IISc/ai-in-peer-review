The paper discusses and defines a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimizer can be obtained. Consequently, it presents an algorithm for finding this global optimizer, demonstrating a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivation is generally instructive, though some aspects require further clarification. Specifically: 1) Theorem 3 relies on the computation of first-order derivatives in the presented inequalities, while Theorem 4 proceeds to utilize second-order derivatives. The reason for this transition warrants further explanation. 2) The network design appears to treat the final layer distinctly from the hidden layers. Given that *u* represents the parameters connecting to the hidden layer, the gradient with respect to *u* in G<sup>Φ</sup> (3) seems to involve the gradient of the vectorization of *u*. (Similar questions arise when bounding *u* within a ball). A key concern is the motivation behind maximizing the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2). Most neural network models employ weight decay to *reduce* the 1-norm. This maximization appears driven by the need for a strictly positive gradient—necessitated by the arbitrarily small epsilon in (2) to ensure inclusion in V<sub>++</sub>—and appears primarily for proof purposes rather than practical neural network design. It is unclear whether this introduces any unconstrained parameters. The requirement for all data to be non-negative and the subsequent use of exclusively positive weights (excluding zero values) leads to cumulative summation. This raises questions regarding the practical applicability of this design. In the experiments, the proposed neural network is compared with SVM, likely due to SVM also possessing a global optimizer. However, a crucial question remains: will this type of neural network offer tangible advantages over more commonly used neural network architectures (e.g., those employing ReLU) in practical applications?
