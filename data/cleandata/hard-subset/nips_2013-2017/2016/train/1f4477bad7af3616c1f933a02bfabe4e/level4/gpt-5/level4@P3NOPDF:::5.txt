Summary
The paper defines a restricted class of feedforward neural networks for which the training objective admits a unique global optimizer and proposes an algorithm with a provable linear convergence rate to this optimum. The mathematical development is interesting and the guarantees are appealing. However, several modeling choices and parts of the analysis are unclear, and the empirical evaluation leaves open whether the proposed networks offer practical advantages over standard architectures.

Strengths
- Clear goal: identifying a nontrivial NN class with a unique global optimizer and an efficiently convergent algorithm.
- Theoretical contribution: linear convergence with a constructive algorithm is valuable if the assumptions are well justified.
- The derivations (as far as can be followed) are instructive and point to structure that enables global optimization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling constraints appear highly restrictive: inputs must be non-negative and weights are constrained to be strictly positive (and, in some places, to have large L1 norm). This yields a monotone network that “only adds up,” which may severely limit expressivity compared to common NNs (e.g., with ReLU and unconstrained signs).
- The objective (2) includes maximizing the L1 norm of the connection parameters, which is opposite to typical regularization (L1/L2 weight decay). This appears driven by technical needs (e.g., to keep gradients in V_++ via an ε-perturbation) rather than by modeling or generalization considerations.
- Practicality is unclear: enforcing positivity and pushing parameters to the boundary (large L1 norm, no zeros) could harm generalization, interpretability, and sparsity, and may lead to sensitivity to feature scaling.
- The experiments compare primarily to SVMs. This is of limited relevance to modern practice; a more informative comparison would include standard MLPs with ReLU and common regularization, as well as monotone/positivity-constrained baselines (e.g., nonnegative networks or deep lattice/monotone models).
- Some parts of the analysis jump in derivative order without clear motivation, and the handling of matrix parameters versus their vectorizations is not always explicit.

Major questions for the authors
1) Theorem 3 vs. Theorem 4: Theorem 3 appears to rely on first-order derivative bounds (as reflected in the inequalities provided), whereas Theorem 4 invokes second derivatives. Please explain precisely:
   - Why the second-order analysis becomes necessary in Theorem 4 (e.g., is it to prove strong convexity/PL condition or to bound curvature for linear convergence)?
   - Whether the second-order quantities are bounded uniformly on the feasible set, and what constants the convergence rate depends on.

2) Parameterization and gradients: The last layer seems to be treated differently from hidden layers. Let u denote the matrix of parameters into a hidden layer.
   - In G^Φ (equation (3)), is ∇u understood as the gradient with respect to vec(u)? If so, please state this explicitly and clarify how norms and inner products are defined (Frobenius vs. vector L2).
   - When you “bound u to be within a ball,” is this a Frobenius-norm ball on the matrix u or an L2 ball on vec(u)? Consistency here matters for both theory and implementation.

3) L1 maximization in (2): What is the modeling or statistical motivation for maximizing the L1 norm of the connection parameters?
   - If the goal is to ensure gradients are strictly positive (membership in V_++), can this be achieved with alternative constraints (e.g., a lower bound per weight or a normalization) without driving the model to the boundary of the feasible set?
   - How sensitive are results to the choice of ε? Does ε vanish in the limit or does it materially affect the solution? Please provide ablations.

4) Positivity and non-negativity: The framework requires non-negative inputs and strictly positive weights (not even zero).
   - How realistic is this in typical datasets with signed features? Is there a principled preprocessing/shift that preserves label information and keeps the theory intact?
   - What expressivity is lost due to enforced monotonicity? Can the model represent subtractive interactions or feature suppression?
   - Are there identifiable “free-parameter” or scaling ambiguities (e.g., simultaneous scaling of a layer’s inputs and inverse scaling of its outgoing weights) under these constraints? If not, what breaks this invariance?

5) Convergence and complexity:
   - Please provide explicit constants for the linear rate (e.g., dependence on Lipschitz/strong-convexity-like parameters) and clarify whether they are data-dependent.
   - What is the per-iteration computational cost relative to standard backprop on an MLP of comparable size? Are there additional projections or normalizations?

6) Empirical evaluation:
   - Why is SVM the main baseline? Please compare against standard ReLU MLPs (with L2 or L1 decay), and, if monotonicity is the core feature, against other monotone or nonnegative architectures.
   - Report model sizes, hyperparameters, and training time. Are results robust across datasets, noise levels, and different feature scalings?
   - Does the proposed network provide benefits such as stability, interpretability, or monotonicity guarantees that standard NNs lack? If so, quantify them.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify all assumptions early: non-negativity of data, positivity and norm constraints on weights, and whether zero weights are allowed anywhere. Discuss implications for expressivity and provide examples of functions that are and are not representable.
- Make the parameter-space geometry precise: specify whether gradients, norms, and balls are defined in matrix Frobenius or vectorized Lp norms; keep this consistent across theorems and algorithms.
- Justify the L1 maximization (or replace it with a better-motivated constraint). Consider an alternative construction that ensures positivity without encouraging arbitrarily large norms; for example, reparameterize weights via exponentials with a normalization per layer.
- Provide ablations on ε, on the positivity constraint (strict vs. non-strict), and on removing the L1-maximization component, to show which pieces are essential for theory versus practice.
- Expand the experimental suite with modern baselines and report statistical significance. If the main selling point is global optimality with linear convergence, demonstrate that this translates into better wall-clock training stability or predictive performance in realistic settings.

Minor comments
- Add a brief intuition for why the second-order terms enter in Theorem 4 and not in Theorem 3.
- Include pseudocode with all projection/normalization steps and state any per-layer constraints explicitly.
- Check notation around G^Φ and ensure consistency of symbols across sections.

Overall, the paper presents an intriguing theoretical direction, but it needs stronger justification of its modeling choices, clearer exposition of the derivative-based arguments, and more compelling empirical evidence against relevant modern baselines.
