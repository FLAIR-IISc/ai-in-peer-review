

The paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, achieving global convergence with a linear rate. The global optimality condition reduces to computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, which depends solely on the network's architectural parameters. This approach is significant for its theoretical clarity and the explicit convergence guarantee, offering a fresh perspective on training neural networks with provable efficiency.  

However, two key limitations warrant careful consideration. First, the method requires all network weights to be nonnegative, a strong constraint that severely restricts applicability. Modern neural networks typically rely on symmetric weight initialization and sign-agnostic optimization, making this requirement impractical for most real-world scenarios. The nonnegativity constraint likely impedes the model's representational capacity and may explain the suboptimal empirical results reported. Second, the activation functions employed—highly specific and non-standard (e.g., piecewise linear forms with strict monotonicity requirements)—further limit the method's generality. These choices appear tailored to satisfy the theoretical conditions rather than reflecting common practice, raising questions about whether the approach can generalize beyond the narrowly defined settings tested.  

The experimental results, which compare the method against SVMs, show marginal or inferior performance. While the paper attributes this to the constrained architecture, the nonnegativity and activation limitations likely contribute significantly to the observed shortcomings. Without addressing these constraints, the practical utility of the method remains questionable, as even minor deviations from the required weight signs or activation forms could destabilize the convergence guarantees.  

A minor but important clarification is needed regarding the convergence claim. The statement in Lines 90–91 that the method "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" due to its linear convergence rate is misleading. Linear convergence implies a geometric reduction in error per iteration, but the number of iterations required for practical accuracy depends heavily on the problem's condition number and initial error. A linear rate does not inherently guarantee convergence within a small fixed number of iterations (e.g., <10), and this claim should be qualified or removed to avoid overstating the method's practical efficiency.  

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with a clean global convergence proof under specific constraints. However, the nonnegativity weight requirement and highly specialized activation functions constitute significant barriers to broader adoption. The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate advantages over standard baselines like SVMs, and the convergence claim requires revision. The authors should address these limitations in the final version, either by relaxing the constraints (e.g., exploring sign-agnostic variants) or providing stronger justification for their necessity. Without such refinements, the method's impact will be confined to a narrow theoretical niche rather than advancing practical neural network training.
