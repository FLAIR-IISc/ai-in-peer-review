- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of constrained feed-forward nets (1–2 hidden layers) with nonnegative weights/inputs and distinct polynomial activations via a modified objective.

- Main theoretical result: a normalized-gradient fixed-point iteration is a contraction in the Thompson metric under a checkable spectral-radius condition, giving a unique global optimum and linear convergence without a learning rate.

- Connects nonlinear Perron–Frobenius / multi-homogeneous map theory to neural network training and positions work relative to hardness results and tensor/matrix-factorization global methods.

- Strength: novel, practically implementable globally optimal procedure with a clear, verifiable spectral condition and linear convergence guarantee.

- Strength: strong theoretical depth using Banach contraction and explicit Lipschitz construction; extends from one to two hidden layers.

- Strength: clear high-level motivation and practical advantages (no step-size tuning; condition computable before training).

- Weakness: restrictive model assumptions (nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct exponents, modified objective with output-sum) with limited analysis of expressiveness and failure modes.

- Weakness: empirical evaluation limited to small UCI datasets and narrow baselines (RBF-SVM, ReLU+SGD); missing comparisons to constrained/monotone models, NMF-like, tensor methods, and strong classical baselines.

- Weakness: lacks computational evidence (wall-clock times, iteration counts, per-iteration complexity) to back claims of speed and few iterations.

- Weakness: presentation/readability issues — dense Section 2, Theorem 1 lacks informal explanation, epsilon/output-sum not motivated in main text, notation/typos hinder flow.

- Weakness: no ablation or sensitivity studies on key components (role of distinct α, nonnegativity, output-sum term, p-norm choices) or behavior as spectral radius approaches 1.

- Quality: theoretically solid and assumptions/checkable condition are clear, but empirical validation is insufficient to establish practical benefits.

- Clarity: introduction is clear but later technical sections need intuitive walkthroughs and clearer definitions of quantities and motivations.

- Originality: original integration of multi-homogeneous PF theory with NN training, making global-optimality more implementable than prior approaches.

- Significance: strong theoretical significance; practical impact currently uncertain due to modeling constraints and limited experiments.

- Suggestions: add an informal walkthrough before Theorem 1, motivate epsilon/output-sum in main text, analyze expressiveness/limitations, expand experiments and baselines, include ablations and computational metrics, and fix notation/typos.

- Pros for acceptance: rigorous, checkable global-optimality result; novel theoretical connection; simple parameter-free iteration with promising convergence.

- Cons for acceptance: unclear practical relevance, missing broader baselines and computational/ablation analyses, and presentation issues around key theorem and definitions.

- Specific comments/typos: define R_{++} and p_w/p_u on first use, clarify "few iterations" is empirical, fix typos ("polyomial"→"polynomial", "one CAN model"→"one can model"), and add a guiding paragraph or schematic for the fixed-point mapping.

- Overall: theoretically strong and elegant contribution but borderline acceptance due to limited empirical/practical validation; leans positive on theory, negative on experiments.
