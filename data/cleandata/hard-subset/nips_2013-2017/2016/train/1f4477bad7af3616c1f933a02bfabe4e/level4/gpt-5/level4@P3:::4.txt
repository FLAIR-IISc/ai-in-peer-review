Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provides global optimality guarantees with linear convergence for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The networks use nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations (real powers), and the objective is modified by adding the sum of outputs along with a small positive term to ensure strictly positive gradients. The key theoretical result shows that, under a condition expressed as the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A being less than 1, the proposed fixed-point iteration converges linearly to the unique global maximizer. The condition depends only on architectural parameters and simple norms of the data, can be checked before training, and the approach is extended from one to two hidden layers. Empirically, the method is tested on several low-dimensional UCI datasets, where it is competitive with ReLU networks on several tasks but generally trails an RBF-kernel SVM.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against two strands of work that provide guarantees for training neural networks: (i) tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) which require access to the score function and non-degeneracy conditions; and (ii) matrix factorization viewpoints (Haeffele and Vidal), whose practical applicability is limited. Compared to these, the current method’s condition (ρ(A) < 1) is checkable from model parameters and simple data norms, without estimating complex data-generating quantities. The theoretical tools draw on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, multihomogeneous maps, and contraction mappings in the Thompson metric. The model class relates to polynomial neural networks and to nonnegative models such as NMF, but the training guarantee and fixed-point construction are novel.

Quality
- Theory: The main theorems are technically sound and carefully stated. The contraction argument via Thompson metric is appropriate, and the mapping is well designed to fit multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory. The spectral radius condition is explicit and pre-checkable. Some bounds appear conservative (as the authors note), which may limit practicality in larger architectures. Several proofs are deferred to the supplement; the structure of the main argument is nevertheless clear.
- Assumptions: The method requires nonnegative inputs and weights, distinct activation exponents, p-norm constraints on layers, and a modified objective that includes the sum of outputs plus a small positivity term. These are strong and collectively alter the modeling setup relative to standard neural networks. The paper justifies the added terms by the need for strictly positive gradients but does not isolate their empirical effect.
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation is modest (low-dimensional datasets, small networks) and shows competitive or slightly inferior performance to SVM overall, with some wins for the two-layer NLSM. The baselines (ReLU, SVM) are reasonably tuned, but there is no ablation to disentangle the effects of nonnegativity constraints, polynomial activations, and the modified objective from the algorithmic benefits of the spectral method. Reporting actual values of ρ(A), iteration counts, and time per iteration would better substantiate “practical feasibility.”

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with helpful background and a clear statement of assumptions. However:
- Some notation is dense, and the cross-entropy loss is not explicitly written where first referenced.
- There are formatting and referencing issues (e.g., incomplete citations, a stray “th Conference…” fragment).
- The role and magnitude of the added output-sum term versus the small positivity term could be explained more clearly, including an ablation.
- The preprocessing needed to ensure nonnegative inputs for the UCI datasets should be described.

Originality
The use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and fixed-point mappings to obtain a global optimum for a nontrivial network class with a checkable spectral condition is original and interesting. The results go beyond existing guarantees that rely on strong data assumptions or are difficult to deploy in practice. The distinct-exponent polynomial activation architecture and the exact fixed-point form are also novel within neural network training.

Significance
The theoretical contribution is notable: a provably globally optimal training procedure with linear convergence for a class of networks, together with a practical pre-check for the condition. However, the strong modeling restrictions (nonnegative data and weights, nonstandard activations, modified objective) limit applicability, and the empirical benefits are not yet compelling relative to strong baselines. The work is likely to stimulate follow-up research on broader architectures or weaker conditions, but immediate impact on standard practice seems moderate.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous theoretical framework with global optimality and linear convergence under an explicit, checkable condition.
- Novel application of nonlinear spectral methods and multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to neural network training.
- Extension to two hidden layers, with analogous guarantees.
- Implementation appears simple (no learning rate tuning) once the condition is met.

Cons
- Strong restrictions: nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, norm constraints, and a modified objective that changes the learning problem.
- Conservative sufficient conditions may prevent scaling to larger models.
- Limited empirical evaluation on small datasets; performance typically below SVM.
- Insufficient ablations to separate algorithmic advantages from modeling constraints.
- Presentation issues: some missing details and minor editorial mistakes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations comparing: (a) the same constrained architecture trained by projected/normalized gradient methods; (b) with and without the output-sum term; (c) varying α distributions. This would clarify what drives performance and the necessity of each modeling choice.
- Report ρ(A), iteration counts, and wall-clock time per iteration, and study behavior as ρ(A) approaches 1.
- Clarify data preprocessing to enforce nonnegativity and discuss robustness to feature scaling and shifts.
- Expand experiments to higher-dimensional datasets if feasible, or discuss practically relevant use cases where nonnegativity is natural (e.g., vision with nonnegative pixel intensities, count data).
- Improve exposition: explicitly write the loss function when first used, correct citations, and streamline notation where possible.

Minor comment
- The claim that linear convergence “typically” leads to only a few (e.g., <10) iterations is too strong. Linear convergence rates vary widely with the contraction factor; please temper this statement or support it with empirical iteration counts across datasets.

Overall assessment
A theoretically strong and original contribution that introduces a new angle on globally optimal neural network training. The current empirical evidence and modeling constraints limit practical significance, but the work is promising and could be influential for theory and specialized applications.
