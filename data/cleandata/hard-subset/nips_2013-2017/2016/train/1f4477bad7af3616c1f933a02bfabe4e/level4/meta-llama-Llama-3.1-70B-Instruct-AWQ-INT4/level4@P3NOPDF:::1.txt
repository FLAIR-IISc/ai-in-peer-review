This paper explores a specific class of neural networks, where the author successfully demonstrates that it possesses only one stationary point. The author introduces a fast method that converges to the unique fixed-point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper investigates an intriguing problem, namely showing that a relatively complex model, with carefully chosen constraints, can have only one stationary point.

Apart from convexity (or its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity), the common approach for proving the uniqueness of the stationary point is through the use of fixed-point theory, which is the method employed in this paper. The author constructs a contractive map, the fixed-point of which is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One drawback of the proposed method is its dependence on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for the convergence proof. As the authors mention, this is the reason they did not attempt to use networks with a larger number of hidden units. Another limitation of the method is the presence of numerous parameters that require tuning, making the use of the procedure rather unintuitive. Furthermore, for each choice of parameters, the spectral condition must be verified.

In the experimental results, the authors tried approximately 150,000 different combinations of possible parameter values and selected the best of them based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed linear SVM in only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result raises questions about the real power of the neural network being investigated.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to proving the uniqueness of the stationary point in a specific class of neural networks. However, the limitations of the method, including its dependence on the spectral radius and the need for extensive parameter tuning, detract from its potential impact. Additionally, the experimental results, while promising, do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
