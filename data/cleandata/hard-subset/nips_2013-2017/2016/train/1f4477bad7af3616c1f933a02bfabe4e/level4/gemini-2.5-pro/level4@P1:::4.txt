This paper introduces a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The core contribution is an algorithm that, under certain conditions, is guaranteed to converge to the global optimum of the training objective at a linear rate. The method is derived from the theory of nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones. To achieve this guarantee, the model requires non-negative weights and input data, uses generalized polynomial activation functions, and optimizes a modified objective function. The condition for guaranteed global optimality can be checked a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small, explicitly defined matrix. The authors provide theoretical analysis for networks with one and two hidden layers and present experimental results on several UCI datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses the highly challenging non-convex nature of neural network training. To my knowledge, this is the first proposed method that is both practically feasible and comes with a guarantee of finding the global optimum. This is a significant theoretical departure from the vast majority of training methods based on stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which offer no such guarantees. The connection drawn between neural network training and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is elegant and novel.

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The work is technically sound and provides a complete theoretical framework. The convergence proof, based on the Banach fixed-point theorem applied in a space with the Thompson metric, is well-established. The conditions required for the guarantee are clearly stated and, importantly, are verifiable before training begins.

3.  **Practicality of the Algorithm:** Unlike some prior works on globally optimal training (e.g., using tensor methods), this algorithm is directly implementable and does not require knowledge of the underlying data distribution. The lack of hyperparameters like a learning rate is a notable practical advantage over SGD and its variants, which often require tedious tuning.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Highly Restrictive Model Assumptions:** The primary concern with this work lies in the strong constraints imposed on the network architecture and data, which may severely limit its practical applicability and expressive power.
    *   **Non-negativity of Weights:** Standard neural networks rely on a combination of positive (excitatory) and negative (inhibitory) weights to learn complex decision boundaries. Enforcing non-negativity is a very strong constraint that likely reduces the model's capacity.
    *   **Non-standard Architecture:** The choice of generalized polynomial activation functions is unusual and seems motivated primarily by the needs of the theoretical framework rather than by practical performance benefits. The paper also notes that common activations like ReLU are incompatible with this framework.
    *   **Modified Objective Function:** The algorithm optimizes a modified objective that includes the negative loss plus the sum of all network outputs. While this is necessary for the technical proofs (i.e., to ensure a strictly positive gradient), the impact of optimizing this surrogate objective on generalization performance is not well-justified. It is unclear if this objective is always aligned with the goal of minimizing classification error.

2.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results serve as a valuable proof of concept but also highlight the practical cost of the aforementioned constraints. On several datasets, the proposed method is outperformed by a standard RBF-kernel SVM, and its performance is only comparable to or slightly better than conventional ReLU networks on some tasks. This raises a crucial question about the trade-off: the theoretical guarantee of global optimality appears to come at the cost of achieving state-of-the-art empirical results.

### Minor Comments

*   The claim that the method "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" seems slightly overstated. While the convergence rate is proven to be linear, the constant factor is the spectral radius `ρ(A)`, which could be close to 1. The actual number of iterations required for convergence can still be large in such cases. It would be more precise to state that the method has a guaranteed linear convergence rate, where the rate constant can be computed.

*   The paper shows that the spectral radius `ρ(A)` increases with the number of hidden units, making the condition `ρ(A) < 1` more difficult to satisfy for larger networks. Given that the theoretical bounds used to construct `A` are described as conservative, a discussion of how these bounds might be tightened, and the implications for the scalability of the method to larger and more complex problems, would strengthen the paper.
