This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of one- and two-hidden-layer feedforward neural networks, proving that this method achieves a global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The approach is evaluated on a series of real-world datasets.

However, the practical relevance of this work is limited by several restrictive and counterintuitive assumptions. As the authors note, the method requires non-negative weights and maximization of the weight regularization term. Furthermore, the theoretical guarantees rely on a non-standard "generalized polynomial" activation function. These assumptions appear to be motivated by the goal of achieving a global optimum rather than by practical applications. The paper would be strengthened by providing a clear justification for why this specific model class is relevant for real-world problems.

The authors claim that their proposed class of networks possesses sufficient expressive power and achieves good performance, but the empirical results do not convincingly support this. The experiments show that a linear SVM often outperforms the proposed NLSM1 and NLSM2 models, even when the SVM does not appear to be properly tuned, while the NLSM models were. The choice of a linear SVM as the primary baseline is questionable, as NLSM is a nonlinear model. A fair comparison would require benchmarking against strong nonlinear baselines, such as SVMs with polynomial or Gaussian kernels. Consequently, the current comparisons are not fair, and the conclusions drawn from them regarding the model's expressive power are unconvincing.

Several other aspects of the work raise questions:

*   **Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on small UCI repository datasets, which are limited in both sample size and feature dimensionality.
*   **Scalability:** The theoretical analysis shows that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. This raises concerns about the method's scalability to high-dimensional data, which may require a larger number of hidden units. This issue could significantly hinder the method's applicability. Is there a way to mitigate this problem?
*   **Practicality of Optimality Condition:** It is unclear if the optimality condition can be met by any practical parameter settings. Although the condition can be checked a priori, this is not helpful if the space of possible valid parameters is intractably large and there are no priors to guide the search. Is there an efficient approach to identify a feasible set of candidate parameters?
*   **Source of Optimality:** Does the global optimality guarantee stem from the specialized objective function? It would be insightful to investigate whether a standard method like stochastic gradient descent also empirically converges to the global optimum on the tested datasets. This would help contextualize the contribution of the proposed spectral method.
