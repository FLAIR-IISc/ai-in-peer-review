This paper presents a compelling theoretical framework demonstrating that a specific class of feedforward neural networks can be trained globally optimally with a linear convergence rate. The key constraint is that both input data and network weights must be non-negative, and the activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is initially proven for networks with a single hidden layer and then extended to those with multiple layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method and demonstrate its effectiveness on synthetic and real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

**Pros:** As the authors claim, this is the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network model with guaranteed global optimality and convergence. Previous work offering convergence guarantees often involves complex preconditions, some practically impossible to verify as they depend on the unknown data-generating measure. The approach presented here has comparatively simpler preconditions, relying only on the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix composed of the network’s parameters. The authors also demonstrate the theory's applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable performance on small real-world datasets.

**Cons:** The proposed network has limitations in its applicability. The theory requires non-negative input data and network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (as defined in Theorem 1) less than one. While the non-negativity constraint on input data shouldn’t significantly restrict usage – most data can be easily shifted to be positive – the requirement of non-negative network parameters might limit the model's expressiveness. The condition that all activation functions in each hidden layer must be distinct is also somewhat unnatural and could limit applicability. Furthermore, the authors note that satisfying the spectral radius constraint necessitates adjusting parameters, potentially decreasing the weights of linear units. If the input dimension and network parameters increase, this could lead to diminishing linear unit weights, effectively reducing the network to a series of linear functions and potentially hindering expressiveness. This raises concerns about the method's scalability to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this remains an encouraging initial attempt, and I believe the paper should be accepted. 

**Minor comments:** I am curious about the relationship between parameters p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimensionality, and network parameter size, and the spectral radius ρ(A). For example, how does ρ(A) scale with increasing input dimensionality – linearly or exponentially? And correspondingly, should pw and pu be decreased linearly or exponentially to counteract this effect? While a formal proof might be challenging, it might be possible to empirically plot this relationship using synthetic data.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Offers a novel approach to globally optimal neural network training.
*   Theoretical soundness: The theoretical guarantees are clearly stated and potentially impactful.
*   Practical feasibility: While constrained, the method demonstrates practical applicability on small datasets.
*   Addresses a difficult problem: Tackles the challenging problem of non-convex optimization in neural networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited applicability: The constraints imposed on the network architecture and data may restrict its broader usage.
*   Scalability concerns: The potential for diminishing weights in higher-dimensional settings raises concerns about scalability to deep architectures.
*   Modest empirical results: Performance on UCI datasets is competitive but not state-of-the-art.
