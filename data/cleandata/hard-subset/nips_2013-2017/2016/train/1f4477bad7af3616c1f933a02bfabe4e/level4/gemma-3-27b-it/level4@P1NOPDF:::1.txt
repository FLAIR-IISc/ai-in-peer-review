This paper investigates a specific class of neural networks and demonstrates that it possesses a single stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method for converging to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The paper tackles an interesting problem: showing that a relatively complex model, when subject to carefully chosen constraints, can exhibit a unique stationary point. Beyond convexity (or its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity), demonstrating uniqueness typically relies on fixed-point theory – the approach taken in this work. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. 

A key drawback of the proposed method is that its convergence proof depends on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. As the authors note, this limitation prevented them from exploring networks with a larger number of hidden units. Another issue is the number of parameters requiring careful tuning, which makes the procedure rather unintuitive to use. Moreover, for each parameter setting, the spectral condition must be explicitly verified. 

In their experiments, the authors evaluated approximately 150,000 different parameter combinations, selecting the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result raises questions about the practical advantages of the neural network architecture under investigation.
