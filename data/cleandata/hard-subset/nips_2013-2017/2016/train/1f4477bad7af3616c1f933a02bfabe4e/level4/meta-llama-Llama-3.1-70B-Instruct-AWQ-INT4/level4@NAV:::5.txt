This paper introduces a specific type of feedforward neural network that allows for the existence of a unique global optimizer, along with an algorithm that can achieve this optimizer with a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivation provided is informative, although some parts require further clarification. 

1. The transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4 is not immediately clear. What motivates this shift in derivative order?

2. The network design appears to differentiate between the last layer and hidden layers, treating them distinctly. Specifically, the gradient of u in G^\Phi (3) seems to be computed with respect to the vectorization of u, which also represents the parameters connecting to the hidden layer. Similar questions arise in other sections, such as the bounding of u within a ball. 

A concern is the necessity of maximizing the 1-norm of connection parameters in (2), which contrasts with the typical practice of weight decay in neural networks, where reducing the 1-norm is often desired. The introduction of an arbitrarily small epsilon in (2) appears to be a mathematical construct to ensure a strictly positive gradient, but its practical implications for neural networks are unclear. The absence of free parameters in this model also warrants further justification. 

Furthermore, the proposed model's requirement for all data to be non-negative and its use of exclusively positive weights may limit its practical applicability. The experimental comparison with SVM is interesting, given SVM's own global optimizer. However, it remains to be seen whether this type of neural network offers additional benefits over more commonly used neural networks, such as those with ReLU activation functions.
