- Introduces NLSM to train "Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks" with provable linear-rate convergence to a unique global optimum under certain assumptions.  
- Requires non-negative inputs, strictly positive weights, and generalized polynomial activations (real powers ≥ 1).  
- Reformulates training as a maximization enabling use of Perron–Frobenius theory and shows the update is a contraction in the Thompson metric.  
- Extends the framework from one to two hidden layers and provides experiments on UCI datasets.  
- Theoretical development is rigorous and logically structured; main claims hold under the condition ρ(A) < 1.  
- Mathematical exposition is highly technical and may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory.  
- Clarity issue: the connection between Lemma 4 (derivative-based conditions) and the construction of matrix A in Theorem 4 is not fully transparent.  
- Clarity issue: operations on multi-dimensional weight tensors should be explicitly stated as applied to vectorized weights when using vector norms/metrics.  
- Highly original approach applying nonlinear spectral methods and fixed-point theory to neural-network training.  
- Theoretical significance: demonstrates a non-trivial class of neural networks can be trained to global optimality, enriching understanding of optimization landscapes.  
- Practical significance is limited by strong constraints (non-negative data and strictly positive weights) that reduce expressive power and applicability.  
- The objective function is unconventional and appears motivated by technical proof requirements rather than standard ML principles (e.g., it encourages large weights).  
- Claim of "no free parameter" is misleading because architecture and hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, pw, pu) must be chosen to meet ρ(A) < 1, typically via cross-validation.  
- Experiments act as proof-of-concept but do not convincingly show practical advantage over ReLU networks or SVMs; performance is mixed and sometimes worse.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, strong provable convergence to a unique global optimum, parameter-free iterative updates, and solid mathematics.  
- Cons against acceptance: severe practical limitations from model constraints, non-standard proof-driven objective, and lack of clear empirical superiority.  
- Final stance: technically strong and original but practically constrained; paper would benefit from deeper discussion of limitations and justification of the learning objective, yet is deemed a worthwhile theoretical contribution.
