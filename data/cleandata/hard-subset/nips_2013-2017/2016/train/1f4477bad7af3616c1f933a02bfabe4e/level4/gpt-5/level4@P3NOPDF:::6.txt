Summary
The paper proposes a method for training, under specific assumptions, a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks using a nonlinear spectral approach. The authors provide optimal convergence guarantees and demonstrate applicability on a set of real-world tasks. The problem is interesting and theoretical advances in this direction are valuable. However, the paper does not yet make a fully convincing case for the practical relevance and scope of the proposed model class.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for studying provably convergent training methods for neural networks.
- Nontrivial theoretical contribution with optimal convergence guarantees.
- Empirical demonstrations on real datasets.
- Well-written introduction that frames the problem and contributions.

Main weaknesses and requests for revision
- Practical utility and expressiveness: The paper twice raises the question of expressiveness for the restricted network class, but does not articulate what functions or tasks are within reach and what are out of scope. Please:
  - Provide a clear discussion of representational limitations and capabilities of the proposed architecture.
  - Include examples or theory illustrating where the approach is expected to perform well or poorly.
  - If possible, add ablations or synthetic experiments that probe expressiveness (e.g., tasks known to require certain nonlinear interactions).

- Positioning relative to concurrent methods: The related work and experiments focus primarily on SVM baselines, omitting comparisons to:
  - Other neural network training methods (e.g., SGD/Adam for comparable architectures; other spectral or tensor-based methods; convex relaxations).
  - Other learning paradigms beyond SVMs that are competitive on the chosen tasks (e.g., logistic regression, kernel methods beyond linear/RBF SVM, tree-based ensembles).
  Please expand the related work to contrast assumptions, guarantees, and empirical performance, and add representative baselines in experiments.

- Experimental scope and clarity: The experiments do not clarify the applicability limits of the method. Please:
  - Report computational resources for all compared algorithms (hardware, wall-clock time, iteration counts, memory), and provide a sense of scaling with data size and model size.
  - Include sensitivity analyses (e.g., to hyperparameters such as the epsilon term in Φ).
  - Clarify the selection of datasets and tasks and why they are representative of intended use cases.

- Theorem 1 presentation: The statement is difficult to parse on first reading.
  - Add an informal explanation before the formal statement, outlining assumptions, the optimization landscape, and the main implication for practice.
  - Consider simplifying notation or breaking the theorem into parts (assumptions, conclusion, remarks) to aid readability.

- Convergence claim after Theorem 1: The text states that the nonlinear spectral method “converges quickly, typically in fewer than 10 iterations to the global maximum.” In the rebuttal you indicated this is an empirical observation. Please make this explicit in the paper and support it with:
  - Plots or tables showing iteration counts across datasets and hyperparameters.
  - A discussion of conditions under which rapid convergence is or is not observed.

- Motivation for Φ and epsilon: While the loss is straightforward, the function Φ is insufficiently motivated and the role of epsilon was only clarified in rebuttal. Please incorporate that explanation, including:
  - Why epsilon is needed (e.g., numerical stability, ensuring positivity).
  - How its value is chosen in practice and its effect on performance and convergence.

Clarity and presentation
- The introduction is strong, but Section 2 is hard to follow. Consider reordering to present:
  1) model and assumptions,
  2) loss and Φ with motivation,
  3) algorithm with intuition,
  4) theorem (informal then formal),
  5) proofs or proof sketches.
- Add a brief “design choices” subsection to collect motivations for the architecture, Φ, and any constraints.
- Where possible, give intuition alongside formal results.

Minor comments and typos
- Line 60: “polyomial” → “polynomial.”
- Line 67: “one CAN model” → likely “one can model.”
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are used without prior definition; please introduce them when first needed.
- Line 67: R_{++} is not introduced; please define this notation (e.g., strictly positive reals). Since several other variables depend on it, define it at first use.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Provide a concise taxonomy of tasks where the assumptions plausibly hold, and where they likely fail.
- Add at least one baseline using a comparable neural architecture trained with standard gradient methods, to separate the effect of architecture from training algorithm.
- Include a small synthetic benchmark designed to highlight the method’s strengths (and another to illuminate its limitations), with controlled ground truth.
- Report runtime and memory for all methods, and if feasible, include complexity analysis per iteration and per epoch.

Overall assessment
This is a promising paper with solid theoretical contributions and interesting empirical signals. To reach its full potential, it needs clearer articulation of the method’s scope and expressiveness, stronger positioning relative to concurrent approaches, and more informative experimental comparisons (including resource usage). With these revisions, the work would make a more compelling case for both its theoretical and practical impact.
