This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, termed Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks. The main contribution is a provably convergent algorithm that finds the unique global optimum of a modified objective function at a linear rate. This work positions itself in the recent line of research aiming to provide global optimality guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem of training neural networks, citing previous theoretical works like Janzamin et al. (2015) and Haeffele & Vidal (2015). Unlike these prior approaches, which the authors argue are challenging to apply in practice, the proposed method is demonstrated on several real-world datasets. The key theoretical requirements for the guarantee are non-negativity of the data and network weights, and a condition on the spectral radius of a matrix derived from the network's architectural parameters, which can be checked a priori. The paper develops the theory for one and two hidden layer networks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents what appears to be the first practically feasible algorithm for training a non-trivial neural network architecture with a guarantee of finding a unique global optimum. The linear convergence rate is also a very strong theoretical result.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical framework, which connects the neural network optimization problem to the Banach fixed-point theorem via nonlinear spectral methods and the Thompson metric, is elegant and well-developed. The derivation is thorough and provides an instructive example of applying fixed-point theory to machine learning problems.
*   **Simplicity of the Algorithm:** The resulting algorithm is an iterative mapping that does not require tuning of learning rates, which is a significant practical advantage over gradient-based methods.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Clarity of Theoretical Derivations:** The connection between the general framework and its application to the neural network could be clarified. Specifically, Lemma 4 and Theorem 3 establish conditions based on the first derivatives of the mapping `G_Φ`. However, applying this to the neural network in Theorem 4 seems to involve second-order derivative information of `Φ`. A more explicit explanation of how the bounds in Theorem 4 are derived from the conditions in Lemma 4 would improve the clarity of the proof strategy.
*   **Notation:** The notation for gradients with respect to matrix parameters (e.g., `u`) is sometimes ambiguous. For instance, in equation (3) and subsequent lemmas, it's not immediately clear if `∇uΦ` refers to the gradient with respect to a vectorized version of `u`, and how the norm and other operations are consequently defined. Clarifying this would help readers follow the mathematical details.
*   **Objective Function Design:** The motivation behind the objective function in Equation (2) is not fully justified from a machine learning perspective.
    *   The term `+ sum(wrl) + sum(ulm)` effectively encourages maximizing the L1-norm of the parameters. This is contrary to standard regularization techniques like weight decay, which penalize large parameter norms to prevent overfitting. The paper states this is for technical reasons related to the proof; a more intuitive explanation for this modeling choice would be beneficial.
    *   The role of the small constant `epsilon` is described as necessary to ensure the gradient is strictly positive. This feels like a technical requirement for the proof machinery rather than a well-motivated component of the model.
*   **Practicality and Scope:** The model imposes strong constraints: all input data and all network weights must be strictly positive. This significantly limits the applicability of the method to specific data types like count data (e.g., images, word frequencies) and prevents the model from learning inhibitory connections, which are fundamental in many neural computations.
*   **Claim of "No Free Parameters":** While the algorithm avoids a learning rate, the claim of having no free parameters is an overstatement. The model's architecture itself—the number of hidden units `n1`, the powers `α`, and the norm constraints `ρw`, `ρu`—are all critical hyperparameters that need to be chosen, as demonstrated by the cross-validation strategy in the experiments.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments serve as a good proof of concept. However, the comparison to standard ReLU networks could be strengthened. While the proposed NLSM models are competitive, it would be valuable to discuss in which scenarios (beyond the theoretical guarantee) this more constrained model might be preferable to a standard, unconstrained ReLU network. The paper notes that ReLU networks can achieve zero test error on Iris and Banknote, which the proposed models cannot. An analysis of this performance gap in relation to the model's constraints would be insightful.
