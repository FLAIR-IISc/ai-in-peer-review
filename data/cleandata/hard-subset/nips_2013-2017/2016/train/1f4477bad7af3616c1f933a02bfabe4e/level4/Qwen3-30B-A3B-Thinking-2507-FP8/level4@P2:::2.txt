

This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors demonstrate that under certain constraints (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations), their approach achieves linear convergence rates for one- and two-hidden layer networks, providing the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for neural networks. The theoretical framework builds on Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed point analysis, with the key condition being that the spectral radius of a specific matrix is less than 1, which can be checked without running the algorithm.

The paper's strengths include a rigorous theoretical development with well-structured proofs, a clear demonstration of linear convergence rates without requiring careful hyperparameter tuning (unlike stochastic gradient descent), and an interesting connection to nonlinear eigenvalue problems on convex cones. The experimental results on UCI datasets provide empirical validation, showing competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVM baselines.

However, several significant weaknesses require attention. First, the assumptions imposed on the network (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations) are highly restrictive and lack justification for real-world applications. The paper does not adequately explain why these constraints are reasonable or how they might be justified in practical scenarios. The generalized polynomial activation is particularly unusual and not well-motivated for common machine learning tasks.

Second, the experimental evaluation has critical flaws. The authors compare their nonlinear model against a linear SVM, which is inappropriate as a baseline for a nonlinear method. A fair comparison would include nonlinear SVMs (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels). The results in Table 1 show that linear SVM outperforms NLSM1/NLSM2 on most datasets, which undermines the claim that the proposed model has sufficient expressive power. The authors also do not explain why NLSM performs better on certain datasets (Cancer, Haberman, Pima) while lagging on others.

Third, the experimental setup uses small, low-dimensional UCI datasets that do not demonstrate the method's scalability to larger, more complex problems. The paper acknowledges that the bounds on the spectral radius grow with the number of hidden units, making the method potentially impractical for high-dimensional data. The authors do not address how to handle this limitation or suggest strategies for efficient parameter selection that satisfy the global optimality condition. Without such guidance, the practical utility of the method remains questionable.

Additionally, the paper does not clarify whether the global optimality property is fundamentally tied to the specific objective function used or if similar guarantees might be achievable with standard loss functions. The authors claim the method is "practically feasible," but the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate this for real-world applications.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, but the practical relevance is limited by the restrictive assumptions. The authors should provide stronger motivation for their constraints and address the experimental shortcomings. The comparison with linear SVM is particularly problematic, and the paper would benefit from including nonlinear baselines and discussing how to handle high-dimensional data.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the global optimization of neural networks, but the practical applicability is limited by the restrictive assumptions and inadequate experimental validation. The method is novel in its theoretical approach, but the experimental results do not sufficiently support the claim that the proposed network class has sufficient expressive power for real-world applications.

Pro:
- Novel theoretical framework with rigorous global optimality guarantees
- Linear convergence rate without hyperparameter tuning
- Well-structured theoretical development with clear proofs
- Practical condition (spectral radius < 1) that can be checked without running the algorithm

Con:
- Restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations) lack justification for real-world applications
- Inappropriate experimental comparison with linear SVM instead of nonlinear baselines
- Small, low-dimensional datasets fail to demonstrate scalability to practical problems
- No discussion of how to efficiently find valid parameter settings for the global optimality condition
- Experimental results do not convincingly support the claim of sufficient expressive power
