- Develops a theoretical framework showing a family of neural networks admits globally optimal training with linear convergence.  
- Key constraints: nonnegative inputs and weights, and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Theory proven for one-hidden-layer, extended to two hidden layers, with a principled path to deeper networks.  
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.  
- Empirical validation on a 2D synthetic task and small UCI datasets shows competitive performance.  
- Major pro: appears to be the first practical method with both global optimality and linear convergence guarantees.  
- Advantage: precondition reduces to checking the spectral radius < 1 of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix (verifiable a priori).  
- Analysis in principle generalizes to deeper architectures and shows reasonable small-scale empirical results.  
- Major con: applicability is limited by requirements of nonnegative inputs/weights, polynomial activations, and the spectral radius condition.  
- Nonnegative weight constraint can reduce model expressivity despite input shifting being manageable.  
- The need for distinct exponents (different α’s) for hidden units is unnatural and may limit adoption.  
- Enforcing the spectral radius condition often requires larger p-exponents and smaller norm radii, effectively shrinking linear weights.  
- As input dimension and model size grow, damping weights may push the model toward near-linear behavior, limiting scalability to deep architectures.  
- Minor request: provide a systematic (theoretical or empirical) characterization of how ρ(A) scales with pw, pu, ρw, ρu, input dimensionality, and network width/depth, e.g., via synthetic plots.
