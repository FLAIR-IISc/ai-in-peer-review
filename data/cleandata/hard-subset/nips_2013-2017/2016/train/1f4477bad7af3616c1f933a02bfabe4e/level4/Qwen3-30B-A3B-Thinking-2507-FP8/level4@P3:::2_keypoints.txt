- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training certain feedforward networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rates.  
- Targets networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations (distinct real powers α ≥ 1) and assumes nonnegative input features.  
- Achieves global optimality for one- and two-hidden-layer networks under mild data assumptions.  
- Avoids density estimation required by tensor-based approaches and gives a computationally checkable global-optimality condition via the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Main technical contribution: transforms training into a fixed-point problem solved by a contraction mapping, with convergence governed by the spectral radius of a K×K (classes × layers) matrix.  
- Provides rigorous global convergence proof and practical convergence check; algorithm does not require SGD-like hyperparameter tuning.  
- Paper is generally clear with well-structured theorems and proofs, with technical details appropriately placed in supplementary material.  
- Exposition could better explain practical implications and how to choose architecture parameters (n₁, α, ρ_w, ρ_u) in real applications.  
- Originality: connects neural network training to nonlinear eigenvalue problems on convex cones, extending Perron-Frobenius theory to multihomogeneous settings.  
- Significance limited by restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, specialized polynomial activations, nonnegative features), reducing applicability to standard deep learning.  
- Experimental evaluation weaknesses: unbalanced baselines (linear SVM used instead of nonlinear SVM), small/low-dimensional UCI datasets, and limited empirical scale.  
- Empirical results are unconvincing: NLSM underperforms linear SVM on most datasets and only marginally beats ReLU networks; dataset-specific performance lacks analysis.  
- Key technical limitation: convergence condition depends on spectral radius ρ(A), which grows with the number of hidden units, harming scalability to high-dimensional problems.  
- Recommendation: needs more compelling large-scale experiments or discussion/techniques to relax assumptions and address scalability; current practical limitations warrant rejection.
