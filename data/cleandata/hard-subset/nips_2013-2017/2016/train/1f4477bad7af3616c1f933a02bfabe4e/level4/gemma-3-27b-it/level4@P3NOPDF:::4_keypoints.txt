- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward networks with a provable linear convergence guarantee.  
- Key condition for global optimality is checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix determined by the network architecture.  
- Major theoretical strength: a provably optimal training algorithm with a linear convergence guarantee and an interesting spectral-analysis perspective.  
- Significant practical limitation: requires nonnegative weights throughout the network, which is nonstandard and may severely restrict representational capacity.  
- Paper needs a deeper explanation of why non-negativity is essential for the theory and exploration of possible relaxations or workarounds.  
- Uses non-standard, highly tailored activation functions that may not generalize to other architectures or tasks.  
- Authors should justify activation choices and compare performance to common activations (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh).  
- Experimental results are limited and only compare to SVMs, yielding at-best comparable performance.  
- Recommend more comprehensive experiments: compare to similarly sized feedforward nets trained with SGD/Adam, include hyperparameter sensitivity and architecture variations.  
- Minor but important critique: claiming "typically in a few (<10) iterations" is misleading — linear convergence does not guarantee small iteration counts without reporting the rate constant.  
- Overall recommendation: lean reject unless authors address non-negativity, activation justification, broaden experiments, and temper convergence claims.
