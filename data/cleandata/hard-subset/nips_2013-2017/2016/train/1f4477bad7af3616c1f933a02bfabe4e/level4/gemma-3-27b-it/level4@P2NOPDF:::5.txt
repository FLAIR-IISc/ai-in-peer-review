This paper introduces a specific class of feedforward neural networks with the notable property of possessing a globally optimal solution that can be efficiently found via a derived algorithm exhibiting linear convergence. The mathematical presentation is largely well-executed and provides valuable insight into the optimization landscape of this network architecture, though some aspects require clarification.

Specifically, the transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4 is unclear. The justification for this shift in required derivative order would benefit from further elaboration. Additionally, the treatment of the final layer appears distinct from the hidden layers. Given that 'u' represents the parameters connecting the hidden layer, the gradient calculation with respect to 'u' in G<sup>Φ</sup> (Equation 3) seems to involve the gradient of the vectorized 'u'. This observation extends to other points in the paper, such as the bounding of 'u' within a ball constraint. 

A central question concerns the motivation for maximizing the 1-norm of the connection parameters in Equation (2). This objective is unconventional, as standard neural network training often employs weight decay to *minimize* the 1-norm. The paper suggests this maximization is merely to ensure a strictly positive gradient, placing the solution within V<sub>++</sub> and facilitating the proof. However, this justification feels primarily driven by theoretical convenience and lacks compelling practical motivation within the context of standard neural network training. It is not clear if this maximization introduces any effectively free parameters. The requirement for entirely non-negative data and the resulting all-positive weight configuration—avoiding even zero weights— raises concerns about the accumulation of activations and the potential limitations this imposes. 

The practical applicability of this network design is questionable. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the constraints imposed by the architecture appear restrictive. The experimental evaluation compares the proposed network to Support Vector Machines (SVMs), likely due to the latter's also possessing a globally optimal solution. However, the paper does not adequately address whether this specialized network offers advantages over more commonly used architectures (e.g., those employing ReLU activations) in practical applications. A clearer demonstration of the benefits, beyond the theoretical guarantee of global optimality, is needed to establish the value proposition of this approach. Ultimately, the paper presents an interesting theoretical result but falls short of demonstrating compelling practical relevance.
