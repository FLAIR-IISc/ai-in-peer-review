- Introduces a theory showing a class of neural networks has a global optimum reachable with linear convergence.
- Main constraints: input data and linear-layer weights must be positive, and activations are generalized polynomials.
- Theory proven for single hidden-layer networks and extended to multiple hidden layers.
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method.
- Experiments: strong on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable on several small UCI datasets.
- Key advantage: first potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees with relatively simple preconditions (spectral radius of a nonnegative parameter matrix).
- Theory applies to arbitrary depth and yields reasonable empirical results on small real-world datasets.
- Major limitation: applicability restricted by positivity constraints on inputs and parameters, polynomial activations, and requirement that spectral radius < 1.
- Non-negativity of inputs is manageable (can shift data), but requiring positive network parameters may limit expressiveness.
- Requiring all hidden layers to have different activation functions is unnatural and may reduce applicability.
- Spectral-radius constraint forces increasing p1,p2 and decreasing pw,pu (reducing linear weights), raising scalability concerns as deep networks may become effectively linear.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance despite the limitations.
- Minor comment: unclear how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius ρ(A); suggests empirical plots to study scaling.
