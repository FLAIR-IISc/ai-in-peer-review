This paper explores a specific class of neural networks, demonstrating that it possesses a unique stationary point. The author introduces a fast method that converges to this fixed point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper investigates an intriguing problem: showing that a relatively complex model, with carefully chosen constraints, can have only one stationary point. 

Apart from convexity (or its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity), the common approach to establishing the uniqueness of the stationary point is through fixed-point theory, which is the method employed in this paper. The author constructs a contractive map whose fixed point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. 

One limitation of the proposed method is its reliance on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for the convergence proof. As the authors acknowledge, this is the reason they did not attempt to use networks with a larger number of hidden units. Another issue with the method is the presence of numerous parameters that require tuning, making the procedure somewhat unintuitive. Furthermore, for each choice of parameters, the spectral condition must be verified. 

In the experimental results, the authors tested approximately 150,000 different combinations of parameter values and selected the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets, which raises questions about the true capabilities of the neural network being investigated.
