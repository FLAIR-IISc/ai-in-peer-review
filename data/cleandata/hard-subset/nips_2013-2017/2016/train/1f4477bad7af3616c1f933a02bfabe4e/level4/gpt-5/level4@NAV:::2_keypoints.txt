- The paper studies a restrictive class of feedforward networks trainable to global optimality via a nonlinear spectral method, applied to 1–2 hidden layers with experiments on real datasets.  
- Key model assumptions are counterintuitive and restrictive: nonnegativity constraints on weights, heavy regularization, and uncommon generalized polynomial activations.  
- These strong assumptions seem unreasonable and not justified by application-driven explanations tied to real data.  
- Authors’ claim of sufficient expressivity is not convincingly supported by experiments.  
- Empirical comparisons are unfair: linear SVM baselines (rather than nonlinear SVMs) often outperform NLSM variants despite weaker tuning of the SVM.  
- UCI datasets used are too small in sample size and feature dimensionality to demonstrate real-world applicability.  
- Theoretical bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with hidden-unit count, creating scalability problems for high-dimensional data.  
- The optimality condition is not guaranteed for arbitrary parameter settings and checking many candidates over a large parameter space is impractical; fast pruning methods are needed.  
- It’s unclear whether the model’s global-optimal property stems from the particular objective; the reviewer suggests testing whether SGD can empirically reach global optimum on some datasets.
