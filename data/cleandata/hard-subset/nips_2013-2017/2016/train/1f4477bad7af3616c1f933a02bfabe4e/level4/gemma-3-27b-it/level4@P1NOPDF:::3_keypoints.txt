- Presents a theory showing a class of neural networks has a global optimum with linear convergence under positivity and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Proves results for single-hidden-layer nets and extends them to arbitrarily deep networks.  
- Introduces an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method.  
- Experiments on a small 2D synthetic dataset and several small UCI datasets show promising/reasonable performance.  
- Novelty: first potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees, with simpler, verifiable preconditions than prior work.  
- Preconditions rely on the spectral radius of a nonnegative parameter matrix (ρ(A) < 1), which is comparatively simple.  
- Applicability concern: requires positive input data and positive network parameters, which may restrict expressiveness despite inputs being shiftable.  
- Architectural constraint: activations must be generalized polynomials and distinct per hidden layer, which feels unnatural and limiting.  
- Spectral-radius constraint forces adjusting p1,p2 up and pw,pu down, effectively reducing linear weights.  
- Scaling worry: in high dimensions or larger networks, required small linear weights make activations nearly linear, so added depth may not increase expressiveness.  
- Overall verdict: encouraging initial result and should be accepted despite limitations.  
- Minor/question: requests analysis or empirical plots of how p1,p2,pw,pu, input dimension, and network size affect ρ(A) (linear vs exponential dependence and how to counteract it).
