This paper investigates a specific class of neural networks and successfully demonstrates the existence of a single stationary point. The authors introduce a fast convergence method to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The paper tackles the interesting problem of showing that a relatively complex model, when subject to carefully chosen constraints, can possess only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (or its generalizations such as geodesic convexity), demonstrating the uniqueness of stationary points often relies on fixed-point theory – the approach utilized in this work. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. 

A key drawback of the proposed method is that its convergence proof depends on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. As the authors acknowledge, this limitation currently restricts the applicability of the method to networks with a limited number of hidden units. Furthermore, the method involves numerous parameters requiring careful tuning, making it rather unintuitive to apply. Critically, for each parameter setting, the required spectral condition must be verified. In the experimental evaluation, the authors explored approximately 150,000 parameter combinations, selecting the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result casts doubt on the practical benefits of the investigated neural network architecture.
