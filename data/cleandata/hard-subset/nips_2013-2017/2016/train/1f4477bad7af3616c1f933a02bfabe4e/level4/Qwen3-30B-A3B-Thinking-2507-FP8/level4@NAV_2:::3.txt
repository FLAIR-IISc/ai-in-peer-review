

This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees under specific conditions. The authors demonstrate that feedforward networks with generalized polynomial activations (where weights and input data are nonnegative) can be trained to global optimality with linear convergence rates. The approach differs significantly from previous work on global optimality [8, 11], which required impractical conditions such as knowledge of the data-generating measure or complex tensor decomposition checks. Instead, the authors' method only requires verifying that the spectral radius of a small matrix (constructed from network parameters) is less than one, a computationally feasible condition. The framework is initially developed for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to two hidden layers, with experiments showing competitive performance on UCI datasets. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first practically implementable method for globally optimal neural network training.

Strengths: The paper's main strength lies in its practicality—unlike prior work that provides theoretical guarantees but is infeasible for real applications [8, 11], this method offers a concrete algorithm with simple preconditions. The global optimality guarantee with linear convergence is theoretically sound and well-supported. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate that the constrained model remains expressive enough for practical classification tasks. The paper is technically rigorous, with clear theorems and proofs that properly contextualize the work within nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory [7, 14].

Weaknesses: The main limitation is the nonnegativity constraint on both input data and network weights, which restricts applicability to certain problem domains. While data can often be shifted to be nonnegative, the weight constraint is more restrictive and potentially limits expressiveness. The requirement that all activation functions in the network must have distinct exponents (α_i ≠ α_j) is theoretically necessary but appears unnatural and may complicate practical implementation. Additionally, the spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 becomes increasingly difficult to satisfy as network size increases, potentially forcing very small weight magnitudes that reduce the model's capacity to learn complex patterns. This could limit scalability to deeper architectures, as the authors note that very small weights may cause the network to behave nearly linearly, diminishing the benefit of adding more layers.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The proof structure is well-organized, building from fixed point theory to the specific neural network application. The experimental validation, while limited to small UCI datasets, provides concrete evidence that the method achieves reasonable performance without requiring parameter tuning.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, though the mathematical notation can be dense. The authors effectively explain the key innovations and their relationship to prior work. The supplementary material appropriately contains some intermediate proofs.

Originality: This work is highly original in providing a practically implementable method for globally optimal neural network training. The use of nonlinear spectral methods to achieve this represents a novel approach distinct from existing tensor-based or matrix factorization methods [8, 11].

Significance: The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training. While the constraints limit immediate applicability, the method provides a valuable theoretical benchmark and could inspire further work on practical globally optimal training approaches. The results demonstrate that global optimality is achievable without prohibitive computational requirements, which is significant for the field.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First practically feasible method for globally optimal neural network training
- Simple, verifiable conditions (spectral radius check) for global optimality
- Linear convergence rate without parameter tuning
- Demonstrated performance on real-world datasets
- Clear theoretical contribution to understanding neural network optimization

Arguments against acceptance:
- Nonnegativity constraints on weights may limit practical applicability
- Requirement for distinct activation functions is unnatural and restrictive
- Potential scalability issues for deeper networks due to spectral radius constraints
- Limited to small, low-dimensional datasets in experiments

The paper represents a significant theoretical advance with potential practical implications. While the constraints are notable, they are well-justified within the theoretical framework, and the authors provide a compelling case for the method's practical feasibility. The work addresses a fundamental gap in the neural network literature and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
