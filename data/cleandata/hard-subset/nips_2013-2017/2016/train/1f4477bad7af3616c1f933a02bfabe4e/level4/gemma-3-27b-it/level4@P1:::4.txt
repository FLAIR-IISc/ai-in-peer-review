The paper presents a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimizing the training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks, achieving a linear convergence rate. The method's global optimality guarantee hinges on the spectral radius of a small, nonnegative matrix dependent on the network’s architecture. This approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to stochastic gradient descent-based methods, which often lack guarantees of reaching a global optimum.

However, I have concerns regarding the limitations imposed by the method. Requiring non-negativity on the network’s weights is a significant constraint, and the use of generalized polynomial activations, while mathematically convenient for the analysis, deviates from standard practice. These restrictions could potentially limit the expressive power of the network and hinder performance. The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance on some datasets, do not definitively surpass state-of-the-art methods like SVMs, raising questions about the practical advantage of this approach. 

The paper is well-structured and provides a detailed theoretical analysis. The authors acknowledge the non-standard nature of their model and attempt to demonstrate its expressiveness through experimental results. The proofs are rigorous, although some are relegated to the supplementary material, which is acceptable. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory provides a solid mathematical foundation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The primary strength is the theoretical guarantee of global optimality with a linear convergence rate, a valuable contribution in the field of neural network training.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods for neural network optimization is a novel and potentially fruitful direction.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed and mathematically sound analysis of the proposed method.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results, even if not state-of-the-art, demonstrates the feasibility and practical applicability of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Constraints:** The non-negativity constraint on weights and the specific form of the activation functions are significant limitations that may restrict the network’s expressiveness and generalizability.
*   **Performance Compared to SOTA:** The experimental results do not consistently outperform established methods like SVMs, questioning the practical benefits in terms of accuracy.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The method's scalability to larger, more complex datasets and deeper networks is not fully addressed, and the authors themselves acknowledge the computational burden grows with the number of hidden units.
*   **Overstated Convergence:** The statement regarding fast convergence ("typically in a few iterations") seems potentially exaggerated, as a linear convergence rate doesn’t inherently guarantee rapid convergence without knowing the constant factor.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: optimization.
*   The guarantee of global optimality, even under specific constraints, is a significant contribution.
*   The work opens up new avenues for research in spectral methods for neural network training.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong constraints imposed by the method limit its practical applicability.
*   The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   The scalability of the method remains a concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with potential for further research. However, the significant constraints and limited empirical advantage raise concerns about its practical impact. I would lean towards a **weak reject**. The theoretical novelty is valuable, but the limitations need to be addressed before the method can be considered a significant advancement in the field. I suggest the authors focus on relaxing the constraints or demonstrating substantial improvements in performance on more challenging datasets in future work.
