This paper presents an elegant theoretical framework demonstrating that for a particular class of neural networks, a global optimum can be found with a linear convergence rate. The primary constraints are that the input data and network weights must be non-negative, and the activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is first developed for networks with a single hidden layer and then extended to deeper architectures. Based on this theory, the authors propose an optimization algorithm using a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves competitive performance on several small real-world (UCI) datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  As the authors claim, this appears to be the first work to propose a potentially practical method for training neural networks that comes with guarantees of both global optimality and a linear convergence rate.
2.  In contrast to prior work, where guarantees often rely on preconditions that are complicated or practically impossible to verify (e.g., those depending on the unknown data-generating measure), the conditions here are straightforward. The guarantee depends only on the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix constructed from the network's hyperparameters, a condition that can be checked before training begins.
3.  The authors demonstrate that their theory can be extended to networks of arbitrary depth and provide empirical results on small real-world datasets that are competitive with standard methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The proposed framework is limited by several strong constraints: the input data and network weights must be non-negative, the activation functions must be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius condition from Theorem 1 must be satisfied (i.e., ρ(A) < 1).
2.  The non-negativity constraint on the input data may not be a major barrier, as many datasets can be easily shifted to meet this requirement. However, the non-negativity constraint on the network weights could significantly limit the model's expressive power.
3.  Furthermore, the requirement that each hidden unit within a layer must have a distinct exponent (α) in its activation function is an unconventional requirement that may also limit the model's practical utility.
4.  Finally, there is a concern about scalability. To satisfy the spectral radius constraint (ρ(A) < 1), the hyperparameters must be chosen carefully. Specifically, as the authors note, the weight norms (ρ_w, ρ_u) may need to be small, while the exponents (p_w, p_u) may need to be large. My understanding is that as the input dimension or the number of hidden units increases, satisfying this constraint requires the weight norms to become even smaller. This is concerning because if the weights are forced to be very small, the non-linearities might have a diminished effect, potentially limiting the benefits of adding more layers. Consequently, I am concerned that this approach may not scale effectively to deep architectures or high-dimensional problems, despite the theoretical extension to multiple layers.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents a novel and encouraging result. It is a strong foundational piece of work in a challenging area, and I recommend its acceptance.

**Minor Comments:**

I am curious about the relationship between the hyperparameters (p_w, p_u, ρ_w, ρ_u), the input dimensionality, the network size, and the spectral radius ρ(A). For example, does ρ(A) grow linearly or exponentially with the input dimension? To counteract this growth, would the weight norms (ρ_w, ρ_u) need to be decreased polynomially or exponentially? While an analytical proof may be difficult, an empirical study on synthetic data illustrating these relationships would be a valuable addition to the paper.
