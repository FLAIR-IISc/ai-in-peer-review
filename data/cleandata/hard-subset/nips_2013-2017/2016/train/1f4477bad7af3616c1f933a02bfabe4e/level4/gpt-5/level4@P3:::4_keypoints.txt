- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) with global optimality and linear convergence for feedforward nets with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Adds the sum of outputs plus a small positive term to the objective to ensure strictly positive gradients.  
- Main theorem: if the spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 for a small nonnegative matrix A, a fixed-point iteration converges linearly to the unique global maximizer.  
- The spectral condition depends only on architecture and simple data norms and can be checked before training; extension provided for two hidden layers.  
- Empirical tests on low-dimensional UCI datasets show competitive performance with ReLU networks but generally worse than RBF SVM.  
- Positions work relative to tensor methods and matrix-factorization approaches, arguing its condition is more checkable and practical.  
- Theoretical tools include nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, multihomogeneous maps, and Thompson-metric contraction arguments.  
- Main theorems are technically sound and well-structured, though some proofs are deferred to the supplement.  
- Some bounds appear conservative, potentially limiting scalability to larger architectures.  
- Strong modeling assumptions: nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct polynomial activation exponents, p-norm constraints, and a modified objective.  
- The paper does not isolate empirical effects of added objective terms or nonnegativity constraints through ablations.  
- Experiments are modest in scale and lack reporting of ρ(A), iteration counts, and per-iteration runtime to substantiate practical feasibility.  
- Presentation issues: dense notation, missing explicit cross-entropy formula, formatting/citation errors, and unclear preprocessing for nonnegativity.  
- Originality: novel application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and fixed-point construction to obtain checkable global guarantees.  
- Significance: strong theoretical advance but limited practical impact due to restrictive assumptions and modest empirical gains.  
- Pros: rigorous global-optimality framework, checkable condition, extension to two layers, and simple implementation without learning-rate tuning.  
- Cons: restrictive assumptions, conservative sufficient conditions, limited empirical evaluation, lack of ablations, and minor editorial problems.  
- Suggested improvements: add ablations (constraint vs algorithm effects), report ρ(A) and timings, clarify preprocessing and robustness, expand experiments or motivate domains with natural nonnegativity, and polish exposition.  
- Minor: statement about needing only a few iterations under linear convergence is too strong and should be tempered or empirically supported.  
- Overall: theoretically strong and original but empirically and practically limited by modeling constraints; promising direction for follow-up work.
