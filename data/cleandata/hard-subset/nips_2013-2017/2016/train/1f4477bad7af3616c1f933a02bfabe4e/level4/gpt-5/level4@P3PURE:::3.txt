Summary:
This paper develops a theoretical framework showing that a certain class of neural networks admits a global optimum that can be reached at a linear convergence rate. The main constraints are that all input data and the weights of the linear layer are positive, and the activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for networks with a single hidden layer, then extended to multiple hidden layers. The authors also introduce an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments indicate that the algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable results on small real-world datasets (UCI).

Pros:
- As claimed by the authors, this appears to be the first work proposing a potentially practical neural network with both an optimality guarantee and a convergence guarantee.
- Prior results with convergence guarantees often rely on preconditions that are difficult or impossible to verify in practice (e.g., they depend on the data-generating measure). In contrast, the preconditions here are comparatively simple and depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix constructed from the network parameters.
- The theory is shown to apply to networks of arbitrary depth, and the paper demonstrates reasonable empirical performance on small real datasets.

Cons:
- The proposed network still has limited applicability. The theory requires positive inputs and network parameters, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (Theorem 1) strictly less than 1.
- While the nonnegativity constraint on inputs may be manageable—most training data can be shifted to be positive—the requirement that network parameters be positive may reduce the model’s expressiveness.
- The requirement that all hidden layers use different activation functions is somewhat unnatural and may further limit applicability.
- To satisfy the spectral radius constraint, the authors indicate that one must increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, which effectively reduces the weights of the linear units. If I understand correctly, as the input dimensionality and the size of the network parameters grow, satisfying the spectral radius constraint forces even smaller linear weights. When these weights are very small, the activation functions behave almost linearly, so adding more layers amounts to stacking linear functions and does not increase expressiveness. This raises concerns about the practicality of extending the approach to deeper architectures.

Minor comments:
- I am curious how p1, p2, pw, pu, the input dimension, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius ρ(A). For example, as input dimensionality increases, does ρ(A) grow linearly or exponentially, and to counteract that, should pw and pu be decreased linearly or exponentially? While a formal proof may be difficult, it could be informative to empirically plot these relationships on synthetic data.

Recommendation:
Despite the concerns above, this is an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and I recommend acceptance.
