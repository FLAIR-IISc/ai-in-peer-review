## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission [Paper ID]

This paper presents a method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under a defined set of assumptions. The authors provide theoretical guarantees of optimal convergence and demonstrate empirical performance on a selection of real-world problems. I find the problem addressed to be interesting, and theoretical advances in neural network training are always valuable. However, the paper does not fully convince me of the practical utility of this restricted network class.

A key concern revolves around the expressiveness of the proposed model. The authors acknowledge this limitation on multiple occasions. I recommend expanding the discussion to explore potential avenues for addressing these limitations, and explicitly outlining what types of problems remain beyond the scope of their approach. A clearer articulation of the model’s strengths and weaknesses would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient context within the broader research landscape. A comprehensive discussion of concurrent work is needed, with a clear contrast between the proposed method and alternative approaches. This comparison should extend beyond training methods to include other relevant algorithmic paradigms.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating some success, does not adequately address the model’s applicability limits. The choice to compare only against Support Vector Machines (SVMs) feels narrow, particularly given the recent proliferation of neural network architectures and training techniques. Comparisons against other neural network training methods, and a broader range of algorithms targeting similar tasks, would provide a more compelling assessment. Crucially, the paper omits any discussion of computational resources (e.g., training time, memory requirements) demanded by each method. Including this information is vital for evaluating the practicality of the proposed approach.

The writing quality is uneven. The introduction is well-crafted, but the subsequent presentation requires improvement. Section 2, in particular, lacks fluency and could benefit from restructuring. Several typos and notational inconsistencies were observed (detailed below). 

Specific points to address:

*   **Motivation for Φ:** The function Φ, central to the loss function, lacks clear motivation as presented. The explanation provided in the rebuttal regarding the role of the epsilon term should be incorporated directly into the paper.
*   **Theorem 1 Presentation:** Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation of its core idea. The theorem statement itself is dense and difficult to parse.
*   **Convergence Claim Following Theorem 1:** The assertion that “the nonlinear spectral method […] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum” immediately following Theorem 1 appears to be based on experimental observation (as clarified in the rebuttal). This should be explicitly stated, and supported with appropriate citations or further explanation.  The strength of this claim feels overstated without further justification.
*   **Notational Clarity:** Several instances require clarification:
    *   Page 60: "polyomial" should be "polynomial."
    *   Page 67: "one CAN model" should be "one can model."
    *   Page 67: The variables p1 and p2 are used without prior introduction.
    *   Page 67: R<sub>++</sub> is not defined, which is problematic as several subsequent variable definitions refer to it.



In summary, while the theoretical contribution of this work is potentially valuable, the paper falls short of demonstrating its practical relevance. Addressing the concerns regarding model expressiveness, comparative analysis, experimental thoroughness, and writing clarity will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on substantial revisions addressing these points.
