- Proposes a theoretically grounded method with provable global optimality and linear convergence for networks with non-negative inputs, non-negative weights, and generalized polynomial activations under a spectral radius condition (Theorem 1).  
- Establishes theory for single-hidden-layer networks and extends it to arbitrary-depth architectures; proposes a nonlinear spectral optimization algorithm validated on synthetic 2D and small UCI datasets.  
- Key strength: first practical convergence guarantees for neural networks with tractable, computable conditions (spectral radius) rather than distribution-dependent preconditions.  
- Empirical extension to deeper networks and real-data experiments strengthen the contribution and advance theoretically sound deep learning.  
- Weakness: requirement that all weights be non-negative restricts model expressiveness compared to standard networks using both positive and negative weights.  
- Weakness: mandating distinct generalized polynomial activations per hidden layer is unnatural/practically inconvenient and lacks justification.  
- Weakness: satisfying ρ(A)<1 often requires scaling parameters that push weights toward zero as input dimension or depth grows, making deep networks effectively linear and undermining depth’s value.  
- Authors acknowledge the trade-off but do not address how weight scaling impacts expressiveness or scalability in practice.  
- Minor comment: need analysis of how ρ(A) scales with input dimension (linear vs exponential) and whether required weight decay is correspondingly severe.  
- Minor comment: clarify quantitative interaction between activation parameters (p1, p2) and weight parameters (pw, pu) in determining ρ(A).  
- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical advance and promising foundation despite restrictive assumptions; weaknesses are substantive but not fatal.  
- Recommendation: accept.
