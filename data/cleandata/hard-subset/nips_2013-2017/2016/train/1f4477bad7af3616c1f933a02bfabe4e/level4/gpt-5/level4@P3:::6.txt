Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that yields globally optimal training for a class of feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations, nonnegative weights, and nonnegative inputs. The authors show that, under conditions that can be verified a priori via the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, the proposed fixed-point iteration converges linearly to a unique global optimum of a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus a sum of network outputs (and a small positive constant for technical reasons). The method is presented for one hidden layer and extended to two hidden layers; the generalization to deeper networks is sketched conceptually.

The work is grounded in the nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous mappings, using the Thompson metric and contraction mapping arguments to establish uniqueness and linear convergence. The results are positioned relative to recent global training guarantees for neural networks (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.) and earlier complexity results showing NP-hardness even for shallow nets. Compared to tensor-based or structured-factorization approaches, the proposed method requires only nonnegativity of data and weights and a checkable spectral-radius condition, avoiding dependence on generative densities or nondegeneracy of tensor decompositions.

Strengths:
- A clear theoretical contribution: global optimality with a linear convergence rate for a nontrivial (albeit constrained) neural network class.
- Conditions for convergence are explicit and verifiable by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix tied to architectural and norm parameters.
- The fixed-point formulation is principled and leverages established spectral theory; the link between critical points and fixed points is carefully developed.
- Extension from one to two hidden layers, with analogous guarantees, broadens the scope beyond the simplest architectures.
- Empirical results, while modest, demonstrate feasibility on real datasets, which prior global-optimality works often lack.

Weaknesses:
- Practical applicability is limited by strong constraints: nonnegative inputs, nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, and a modified training objective that adds the sum of outputs.
- The paper raises, but does not address in depth, the expressiveness of the constrained model. Guidance on what decision boundaries/functions are in or out of reach is limited.
- Theorem statements (especially Theorem 1) are difficult to parse without an informal summary; important design choices (e.g., the epsilon term and added output-sum) are insufficiently motivated in the main text.
- Experimental evaluation is narrow (small UCI datasets) and comparisons are limited. There is no head-to-head against constrained baselines (e.g., projected gradient under the same nonnegativity and norm constraints), nor analysis of computation time or memory use.
- The spectral-radius bounds appear conservative; the paper notes this but does not quantify tightness or provide practical guidance when ρ(A) ≥ 1.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is strong. The fixed-point theory is appropriate, with a credible path: positivity of gradients on S+, existence of the maximizer in S++, equivalence of critical points and fixed points, and contraction in the Thompson metric under spectral-radius conditions. The extension to two layers is carefully adapted.
- Assumptions are clearly stated (nonnegativity; distinct exponents; choice of p-norms and radius parameters), but their implications for modeling are under-explored.
- Empirical support is limited; while the method converges quickly, there is little analysis of statistical performance versus constraints or of sensitivity to parameter choices (α, pw, pu, ρ values).

Clarity:
- The introduction is clear and well motivated. Subsequent sections are dense; Section 2 in particular would benefit from an informal overview of Theorem 1 and a plain-language description of the map GΦ and the role of A and ρ(A).
- The objective Φ needs more motivation in the main text: explain why the sum-of-outputs term and the small epsilon are required (to ensure strict positivity of gradients and to apply Perron–Frobenius arguments), and discuss how this affects probabilistic interpretation and calibration of the classifier.
- Some notation is introduced late or not at all in the main text (e.g., R++, pw/pu are implied but not always explicit), and there are minor typos (see Minor comments).

Originality:
- The use of nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous contraction arguments for globally optimal training of neural networks with explicit convergence rates is novel and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated for architectures of this complexity.
- The design choice to require distinct exponents α across hidden units and to analyze the associated invariance/permutation issues is insightful.

Significance:
- The theoretical guarantee is potentially impactful as a rare, practical global-optimality result for neural networks. However, real-world significance is tempered by modeling constraints (nonnegativity; modified objective) and by limited experimental validation on higher-dimensional or larger-scale tasks.
- If extended with less conservative conditions and broader empirical evidence, this line could influence how constrained neural models are designed and optimized.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide an informal prelude to Theorem 1: state in words what architectures are covered, what needs to be checked (ρ(A) < 1), and what is guaranteed (unique global optimum, linear convergence), before presenting the formal statement.
- Move the explanation of the ε and output-sum terms into the main text, right when Φ is introduced, and discuss their practical effect on training and predictions (e.g., calibration or bias).
- Expand the discussion of expressiveness: characterize hypothesis classes induced by nonnegativity and generalized polynomial activations; illustrate what functions are unattainable; include a synthetic suite probing decision boundary complexity versus α choices.
- Add baselines that isolate the algorithmic contribution: (i) projected gradient or mirror descent on the same constrained model and objective; (ii) the same architecture without the output-sum term; (iii) unconstrained polynomial networks. Report wall-clock time, iterations, and memory.
- Discuss preprocessing for datasets that are not naturally nonnegative (e.g., shifts or transforms), and the implications for invariances and performance.
- Clarify what happens when ρ(A) ≥ 1: do iterates fail to converge, converge sublinearly, or cycle? Provide a simple adaptive strategy (e.g., increasing pw/pu per the derived bounds) to enforce ρ(A) < 1.
- Report per-iteration complexity and total runtime versus SGDs on the same hardware; include the cost of computing A and its spectral radius (which is negligible but should be stated).
- Improve readability of Section 2 by trimming technical asides to the supplement and adding a flow diagram or pseudocode early (the algorithm box can be referenced sooner).

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong and novel theoretical guarantees with clear, checkable conditions.
- Elegant use of nonlinear spectral theory and fixed-point contractions.
- First empirical demonstration (to my knowledge) of a globally optimal neural training method on real datasets.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical scope is limited by modeling constraints and a modified objective.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow, with missing baselines and no runtime analysis.
- Presentation (especially Section 2 and Theorem 1) needs additional informal explanation and motivation.

Minor comments and typos:
- “polyomial” → “polynomial.”
- “one CAN model” → “one can model.”
- Introduce R++ and the notation pw, pu explicitly where S+ and S++ are first defined; p′ as the Hölder conjugate should be recalled when used.
- Ensure consistent notation: p1 and p2 appear but seem to refer to pw and pu.
- Define Ψ and sets J, Jc promptly and consider moving technical bounds to the supplement with an intuitive summary in the main text.
- Clarify the claim that the method converges “typically in fewer than 10 iterations” as an empirical observation, not a theoretical guarantee.

Overall evaluation:
A theoretically strong and original paper with promising but constrained practical applicability. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can substantially improve clarity, broaden and strengthen the empirical evaluation (including fair constrained baselines and runtime), and better articulate the modeling trade-offs and expressiveness. Otherwise, it remains a valuable theoretical contribution that could benefit from a revision focusing on presentation and empirical scope.
