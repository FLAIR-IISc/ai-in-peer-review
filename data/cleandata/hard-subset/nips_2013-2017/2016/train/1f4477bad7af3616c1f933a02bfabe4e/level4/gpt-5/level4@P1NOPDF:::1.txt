This paper investigates a special class of neural networks and shows that the objective has a single stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method that converges to the unique fixed point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper tackles the interesting problem of demonstrating that a relatively complex model, under carefully chosen constraints, can have only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (and generalizations such as geodesic convexity), a common approach to proving uniqueness of a stationary point is via fixed-point theory; this is the approach taken here. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One drawback is that the convergence proof depends on bounding the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix. As the authors note, this is why they did not experiment with networks having a larger number of hidden units. Another issue is the large number of hyperparameters that must be tuned, which makes the procedure rather unintuitive. Furthermore, for each parameter setting, the spectral condition must be verified.

In the experiments, the authors explored roughly 150,000 combinations of parameter values and selected the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets. This result calls into question the practical power of the neural network under study.
