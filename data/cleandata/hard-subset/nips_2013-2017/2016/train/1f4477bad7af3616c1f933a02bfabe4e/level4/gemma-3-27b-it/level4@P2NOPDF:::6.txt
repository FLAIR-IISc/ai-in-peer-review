This paper introduces a novel method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under defined assumptions, and provides a convergence guarantee. The theoretical contribution is valuable, as advancements in understanding neural network training dynamics are always welcome. However, the paper doesn't fully establish the practical utility of the proposed approach.

A key concern is the limited discussion of the model’s expressiveness. The authors acknowledge the restrictive nature of their network architecture on multiple occasions. Expanding the paper to address the capabilities *and* limitations of this approach – outlining what types of problems it can reasonably tackle and where it falls short – would significantly strengthen the work.  Furthermore, a more comprehensive comparison with concurrent methods is needed. The paper would benefit from a discussion contrasting the proposed method with related work, including alternative neural network training techniques and different algorithmic paradigms.

The experimental evaluation, as presented, doesn’t adequately illuminate the applicability of the model. The comparison is limited to SVMs, omitting relevant baselines such as other neural network training methods.  Providing results against a broader range of techniques would offer a more informative assessment of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses. Additionally, including information regarding the computational resources (e.g., runtime, memory usage) required by each algorithm would enhance the reproducibility and practical assessment of the results.

While the introduction is well-written and engaging, the subsequent presentation could be improved. Section 2, in particular, lacks clarity and does not flow logically.  The paper also contains several minor typographical errors (detailed below). 

Specifically, the function $\Phi$ introduced in the loss function lacks sufficient motivation. The explanation provided in the rebuttal regarding the role of the epsilon term should be incorporated directly into the paper to improve understanding.  Theorem 1 would be more accessible if an informal explanation of its implications were provided *before* the formal statement. The theorem’s statement itself is dense and difficult to parse.  

Immediately following Theorem 1, the claim that the “nonlinear spectral method […] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum” appears to be based on experimental observation, as clarified in the rebuttal. This should be explicitly stated to avoid misleading the reader.

Finally, I noted the following minor issues:

*   Page 60: “polyomial” should be “polynomial”.
*   Page 67: “one CAN model” should be “one can model”.
*   Page 67: The variables *p1* and *p2* are used without prior introduction.
*   Page 67:  $R_{++}$ is not defined, which is problematic given its frequent use and the existing variables referring to it. The rebuttal clarification was helpful, but this should be included in the paper itself.
