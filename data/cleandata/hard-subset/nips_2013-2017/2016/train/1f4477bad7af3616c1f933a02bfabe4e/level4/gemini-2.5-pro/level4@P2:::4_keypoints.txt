- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method for one- or two-hidden-layer networks with provable convergence to a unique global optimum; iterative, parameter-free, linear rate, checkable via the spectral radius of a small non-negative matrix.

- Reformulates training as finding a unique fixed point on the cone of non-negative weights and, using the Thompson metric, proves the map is a contraction so Banach’s theorem yields uniqueness and linear convergence.

- Positioned as an advance over prior globally optimal training work by claiming better practical applicability and verifiability.

- Novel theoretical contribution connecting neural network training to fixed-point theory on cones.

- Provides a verifiable pre-training condition (ρ(A) < 1) computable from the network architecture.

- Parameter-free algorithm that does not require tuning learning rates or similar hyperparameters.

- First empirical demonstration on real-world datasets for a method that guarantees global optimality.

- Strong restriction: requires non-negative inputs and weights, preventing inhibitory connections and limiting applicability.

- Uses non-standard "generalized polynomial" activations with distinct powers per neuron, an unconventional choice with unclear expressivity relative to ReLU/sigmoid.

- Employs a modified objective (negative cross-entropy plus an output-maximizing term and small regularizer) motivated by theory but lacking clear ML justification.

- Modest empirical performance: outperformed by RBF-SVM and only competitive with simple ReLU networks on UCI datasets, suggesting practical cost of theoretical constraints.

- The claim of being "very fast" is overstated because linear convergence rate depends on ρ(A); if ρ(A) ≈ 1 convergence can be slow.

- Needs more intuition about the effect and bias introduced by the output-maximizing term in the objective.

- Scalability concern: the convergence condition ρ(A) < 1 may become harder to satisfy as hidden units increase, limiting extension to larger networks.

- Overall: theoretically impressive and original, but practical impact is limited by restrictive assumptions that appear to trade predictive performance for provable optimality.
