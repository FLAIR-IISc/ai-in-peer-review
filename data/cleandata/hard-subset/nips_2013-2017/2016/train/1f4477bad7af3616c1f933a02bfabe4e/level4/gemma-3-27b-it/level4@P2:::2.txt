This paper investigates the global optimization of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using a novel nonlinear spectral method. The authors demonstrate, under relatively weak data assumptions, that these networks can be trained to a global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers, and its performance is evaluated on several real-world datasets.

The core contribution lies in providing a practically feasible algorithm guaranteeing global optimality, a significant challenge in neural network training. This is achieved by imposing constraints – namely, non-negativity on network weights and the use of generalized polynomial activations – and formulating the training problem as a maximization problem inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors also present a clear condition, verifiable before training, for guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum.

However, the restrictive nature of the imposed constraints raises concerns. The non-negativity requirement on weights and the use of generalized polynomials, while enabling theoretical guarantees, are arguably counterintuitive and limit the applicability of the method. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the practical implications and potential workarounds would strengthen the paper. A clearer justification of *why* these constraints are necessary for achieving global optimality, beyond simply stating they are required, would be beneficial. 

The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance, do not strongly support the claim of expressive power. The comparison to a *linear* SVM is questionable, given that the proposed method is nonlinear. A more appropriate comparison would involve nonlinear kernels (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian) to assess the method's ability to model complex relationships. The relatively small size of the UCI datasets used (both in terms of samples and dimensionality) also limits the generalizability of the findings.

A critical limitation appears to be the scaling of the spectral radius bound with the number of hidden units. This suggests potential difficulties in applying the method to high-dimensional data, a key challenge in many real-world applications. Addressing this scalability issue, or at least discussing potential mitigation strategies, is crucial. The authors mention the need to explore parameter settings to satisfy the convergence condition; a more efficient method for identifying a feasible parameter set, rather than exhaustive search, would be valuable. 

Finally, exploring whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically achieve similar global optimality on these datasets, despite lacking the theoretical guarantee, could provide valuable insights. This would help contextualize the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method compared to more standard training techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a novel, theoretically grounded algorithm for globally optimizing a specific class of neural networks.
*   Achieves a linear convergence rate, a significant improvement over typical stochastic gradient descent methods.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on several UCI datasets.
*   The condition for global optimality can be checked *before* training, offering practical value.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The restrictive assumptions (non-negativity, generalized polynomials) limit the applicability of the method.
*   Experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate superior performance compared to more established methods.
*   The scalability issue regarding the spectral radius bound is a significant concern.
*   The comparison to a linear SVM baseline is not appropriate.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but is limited by its practical constraints and somewhat unconvincing empirical results. While the guarantee of global optimality is valuable, the restrictive assumptions and scalability concerns hinder its broader applicability. I would lean towards **weak reject** at this time. Addressing the concerns raised above – particularly regarding scalability, benchmark selection, and justification of assumptions – could significantly improve the paper's impact.
