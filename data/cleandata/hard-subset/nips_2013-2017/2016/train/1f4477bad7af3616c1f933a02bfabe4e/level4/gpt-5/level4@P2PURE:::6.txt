The paper proposes a method for training—under specified assumptions—a particular class of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and show that the method can be applied to a selection of real-world problems. I find the problem interesting, and any theoretical progress in this direction is welcome. However, the paper falls short of convincing me that the proposed network class is practically useful.

Since the authors twice raise the issue of the model’s expressiveness, I recommend adding a clear discussion of what the approach can and cannot accomplish, explicitly delineating its scope and limitations. Concurrent approaches should also be discussed and contrasted, both theoretically and empirically.

The experimental results do not clarify the limits of applicability of the model. I was surprised that the comparisons are restricted to SVMs, ignoring other training methods for neural networks as well as other algorithmic paradigms. I would also have expected information about the computational resources required by the three algorithms.

The introduction is well written, but the subsequent presentation could be improved; Section 2, in particular, does not read smoothly. There are also several typos (see below).

Specific remarks:
- While the loss function is straightforward, the function Φ lacks motivation; the role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal and should be added to the paper.
- Theorem 1 should be introduced informally before being stated; as written, the statement is hard to parse.
- Immediately after Theorem 1, the paper claims that “the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly, typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum.” In the rebuttal, the authors clarified that this is based on experimental observations; the paper should make this explicit.

Typos and notation:
- Line 60: polyomial → polynomial.
- Line 67: “one CAN model” is unclear; please revise.
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced.
- Line 67: R_{++} is not introduced, which is unfortunate since several other variables refer back to it. Thanks for clarifying this in the rebuttal; I was not familiar with this notation.
