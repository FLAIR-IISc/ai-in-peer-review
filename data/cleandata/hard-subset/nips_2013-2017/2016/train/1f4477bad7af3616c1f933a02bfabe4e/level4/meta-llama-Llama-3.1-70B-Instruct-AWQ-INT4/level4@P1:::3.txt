This paper presents a nice theory that demonstrates a certain class of neural network has a global optimum that can be achieved in a linear convergence rate. The main constraint is that all the input data and the weights of the linear layer are positive and activation functions are generalized polynomial. The proposed theory is first proved on the neural network with a single hidden layer and later extended to the networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral method.

Experiments show that the proposed algorithm works quite well on a small 2D synthetic dataset, and it also achieves a reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

Pros: As claimed by the authors, this is the first work that proposed a potentially practical neural work with optimal guarantee and convergence guarantee. Previous work that have convergence guarantees requires a complicated method to check its preconditions, some of them even impossible to check in practice, as they depend on the data-generating measure. The theory proposed in this work has comparatively simple preconditions that only depend on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consists of the parameters of the network. The authors also demonstrate that the proposed theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth and show a reasonable result on small real datasets.

Cons: The proposed network still has limited applications. The theory requires both the input data and network parameters be positive, activation functions be generalized polynomials, and spectral radius (Theorem 1) be smaller than 1. While the non-negative constraints on the input data may not block too much potential usage, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the fact that network parameters are positive might limit the expressiveness of the model. Additionally, the requirement that all activation functions of all hidden layers are different is unnatural and may also limit its application.

Furthermore, the authors note that to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, they need to increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, which effectively decreases the weights of linear units. If the linear weight is very small, the activation functions will almost act the same as a linear function. In that case, adding more layers is just adding another linear function, which won't increase the expressiveness of the network. This raises concerns that this method might not be easily extended to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this is still an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and this paper should be accepted.

Minor comments: It would be interesting to investigate how p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius rho(A). For example, when the dimension of input increases, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially, and in order to counteract that, should we linearly or exponentially decrease pw and pu. While it may not be easy to prove that, it might be possible to empirically plot that relationship on a synthetic data.
