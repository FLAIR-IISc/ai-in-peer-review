- Paper studies a constrained family of feedforward networks trained to global optimality via a nonlinear spectral method, with algorithms and experiments on one- and two-hidden-layer models.  
- The model class is restrictive and counterintuitive: it enforces non-negativity of all weights and uses an objective that promotes larger weights, plus requires generalized polynomial activations.  
- These assumptions are not clearly practical; the authors should provide more application-driven justification instead of tightening assumptions for theoretical guarantees.  
- Empirical evidence is weak: claims of sufficient expressivity and competitive performance are not well substantiated.  
- A linear SVM often outperforms NLSM1/NLSM2, and using a linear SVM as the primary baseline (instead of nonlinear kernels) raises fairness concerns.  
- The UCI datasets used are small in sample size and dimensionality, limiting the strength of empirical validation.  
- Analysis shows spectral radius bounds grow with number of hidden units, which is problematic for higher-dimensional problems and may limit applicability.  
- The global optimality condition may not hold for many reasonable parameter settings and is hard to ensure a priori without priors or an efficient way to narrow valid configurations.  
- It is unclear whether global optimality comes mainly from the specialized objective; authors should test whether standard stochastic gradient methods can reach the global optimum empirically.
