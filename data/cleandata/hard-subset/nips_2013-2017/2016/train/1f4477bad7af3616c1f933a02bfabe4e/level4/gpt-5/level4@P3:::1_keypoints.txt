- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) with provable global convergence for feedforward NNs with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Networks use nonnegative inputs/parameters and activations (u⊤x)^α with distinct α per hidden unit to avoid permutation degeneracies.  
- Training maximizes negative cross-entropy plus sum-of-outputs and a small parameter-sum term, with parameters constrained to p-norm spheres.  
- Algorithm iteratively applies a normalized (p′)-power gradient map GΦ inspired by multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory.  
- Main theorem: if spectral radius ρ(A)<1 for a small explicit matrix A, there is a unique positive maximizer and GΦ converges linearly to it with rate ≤ρ(A).  
- The spectral condition depends only on model/normalization parameters and simple data norms and can be checked before training.  
- Paper extends the method to two hidden layers and reports experiments on several UCI datasets versus ReLU+SGD and RBF-SVM.  
- Strength: clear, checkable theoretical guarantee using Thompson-metric contraction and nonlinear Perron–Frobenius arguments.  
- Strength: more practical than prior global-optimality work by avoiding score-function estimation or tensor identifiability checks.  
- Strength: simple algorithm without learning-rate tuning and with a priori bounds on rate/iterations from ρ(A).  
- Strength: extends beyond single layer and justifies design choices like distinct α values.  
- Weakness: guarantees apply to a modified objective under strong nonnegativity and norm constraints; implications for standard risk, calibration, and class-probability bias are not discussed.  
- Weakness: spectral condition seems conservative, possibly forcing large p norms and small hidden-layer widths; tighter analysis or empirical calibration is needed.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited to small, low-dimensional datasets; scalability and wall-clock performance versus strong baselines are not convincingly shown.  
- Weakness: heavy hyperparameter/architecture tuning (n1, α, ρ, pw, pu, etc.) and need to pick p-norms to satisfy ρ(A)<1 makes deployment nontrivial.  
- Weakness: baselines are not fully apples-to-apples; should include nonnegativity-constrained networks trained by projected SGD or NMF-based methods.  
- Clarity issues: typos, missing constants (e.g., ε), incomplete references, and occasional notation inconsistencies; motivation for the sum-of-outputs term needs clearer explanation.  
- Missing analysis: no brief complexity analysis of one GΦ iteration for one/two layers and no wall-clock/iteration cost reporting.  
- Originality: novel synthesis of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius, Thompson-metric contraction, and NN training yielding a practical, checkable global-optimality condition.  
- Significance: theoretically interesting and could stimulate follow-up work, but current empirical impact is modest and applicability may be limited.  
- Suggested improvements: provide heuristics/sensitivity analyses for choosing α and norm/ρ parameters, ablations on sum-of-outputs and ε, and tighter or data-dependent spectral bounds.  
- Suggested improvements: add nonnegativity-constrained baselines, report wall-clock time and verification cost for ρ(A), and clarify preprocessing and its effect on guarantees.  
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant, verifiable theoretical contribution and an implementable globally convergent method for a nontrivial NN class.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation, conservative conditions and heavy tuning undermine practical impact; guarantees apply to a nonstandard objective.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising and technically sound but needs stronger empirical validation, tighter bounds, and clearer practical guidance.
