This paper studies a special class of neural networks and shows that, under the proposed constraints, the associated cost function has a single stationary point. The authors introduce a fast algorithm that provably converges to the unique fixed point, which is also the global minimum. The problem is interesting: it demonstrates that a relatively complex model, with carefully selected constraints, can be made to have only one stationary point.

Beyond convexity (and generalizations such as geodesic convexity), a common route to uniqueness is fixed-point theory, which is the approach taken here. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

A key drawback is that the convergence proof depends on bounding the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix. As the authors note, this requirement is precisely why they did not experiment with networks having a larger number of hidden units. Another practical limitation is the large number of parameters that must be tuned, which makes the procedure unintuitive; moreover, for each parameter setting the spectral condition must be re-verified.

Empirically, the authors explored roughly 150,000 combinations of parameter values and selected the best via cross-validation accuracy. Nevertheless, the method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets. This outcome calls into question the practical power of the neural network under investigation.
