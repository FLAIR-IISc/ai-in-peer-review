

The paper proposes a specialized class of feedforward neural networks that admit a unique global optimizer, accompanied by an algorithm achieving linear convergence. While the mathematical derivation is generally instructive, several points require clarification.  

First, Theorem 3 computes first-order derivatives (via inequalities), yet Theorem 4 proceeds to second-order derivatives. The rationale for this jump in derivative order is unclear and warrants explicit justification. Second, the network architecture treats the final layer distinctly from hidden layers. Since the weight matrix $u$ parameterizes connections to hidden layers, the gradient $\nabla_u \mathcal{G}^\Phi$ in Equation (3) should correspond to the vectorized form of $u$. This point is ambiguous, and similar confusions arise in constraints bounding $u$ within a ball (e.g., in Theorem 2).  

A critical concern pertains to the objective in Equation (2), which maximizes the 1-norm of connection parameters. In standard neural networks, weight decay minimizes such norms to prevent overfitting. Here, the maximization appears motivated solely by the need for arbitrarily small $\epsilon$ in Equation (2) to ensure gradients remain strictly positive (i.e., in $V_{++}$). This suggests $\epsilon$ serves primarily as a technical artifact for the proof rather than a practical requirement. The paper does not convincingly justify why this design choice is necessary or beneficial in real-world applications.  

Furthermore, the model’s reliance on non-negative data and strictly positive weights (with no zeros) implies a mechanism that continuously accumulates positive contributions. This severely restricts applicability, as most real-world datasets contain mixed-sign features. The authors should address how this limitation impacts practical deployment.  

The experimental evaluation compares the proposed network solely against SVM, citing SVM’s global optimizer as a rationale. However, this comparison is misleading: SVM solves a convex optimization problem, whereas the proposed network is non-convex (despite claiming a global optimizer). A more meaningful evaluation would benchmark against standard neural networks (e.g., ReLU-based architectures) to demonstrate tangible advantages in accuracy, efficiency, or robustness. The paper fails to establish why this specialized network offers *any* meaningful benefit over widely adopted alternatives in practice.  

In summary, while the theoretical contribution is intriguing, key gaps undermine its practical relevance: the role of $\epsilon$ lacks justification, the non-negative weight constraint is overly restrictive, and the experimental comparison is inadequate. The authors should clarify the derivative-order transition, explicitly resolve the $u$-gradient ambiguity, and provide a stronger justification for the 1-norm maximization. Crucially, they must demonstrate concrete advantages over standard neural network architectures through appropriate baselines. Without these, the work’s practical significance remains questionable.
