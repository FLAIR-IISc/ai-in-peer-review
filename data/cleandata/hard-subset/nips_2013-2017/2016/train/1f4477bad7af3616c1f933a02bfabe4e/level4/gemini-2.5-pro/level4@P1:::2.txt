This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a particular class of feedforward neural networks, guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum at a linear rate. The proposed framework is developed for networks with generalized polynomial activation functions and non-negative weights. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis based on nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point theorems, deriving a condition on the spectral radius of a matrix related to the network's architecture that ensures the global optimality guarantee. The method is demonstrated on one- and two-hidden-layer networks and evaluated on several real-world UCI datasets.

This work is theoretically significant as it presents one of the first practically feasible algorithms for globally optimal neural network training, a problem that is NP-hard in the general case. The core strength of the paper lies in its novel and rigorous mathematical framework. The ability to check the optimality condition before training begins and the absence of optimization-related hyperparameters like learning rates are also notable advantages over standard methods like SGD.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit the practical impact of its contributions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Restrictive Model Assumptions:** The guarantee of global optimality comes at the cost of several strong and non-standard assumptions. The model requires non-negative weights and data, uses a less common "generalized polynomial" activation function, and, most critically, modifies the objective to include a term that maximizes the sum of all network weights. These constraints appear to be primarily motivated by the requirements of the theoretical analysis rather than by practical modeling considerations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a more compelling justification for this specific model class from an application perspective. Why would one want to maximize the sum of weights in a real-world scenario? Without this motivation, the proposed model feels more like a theoretical curiosity than a practical tool.

2.  **Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claim that the proposed model is "expressive enough to achieve good performance" is not strongly supported by the experiments.
    *   **Performance Comparison:** On the tested UCI datasets, the proposed NLSM1 and NLSM2 models are often outperformed by the baseline RBF-kernel SVM and standard ReLU networks. While NLSM shows strong performance on a few datasets (e.g., Pima, Haberman), it performs poorly on others where the baselines achieve perfect or near-perfect scores (e.g., Iris, Banknote). Given that the NLSM architecture itself is tuned via cross-validation, its inability to consistently match or exceed the performance of standard, well-understood models questions its practical utility.
    *   **Choice of Baselines:** The comparison could be more insightful. While an RBF-SVM is a reasonable nonlinear baseline, the fundamental contribution is an optimization algorithm for a specific objective. A crucial experiment would be to optimize the exact same objective function (Equation 2) using a well-tuned batch or stochastic gradient method. This would help disentangle the benefits of the novel optimization landscape from the benefits of the proposed spectral method. If SGD also consistently finds the global optimum for this objective, it would suggest the main contribution is the design of a tractable loss surface rather than the optimization algorithm itself.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are confined to small, low-dimensional datasets. As the authors correctly note, the theoretical bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. Since the number of required hidden units typically grows with data complexity and dimensionality, this raises serious doubts about the method's applicability to larger-scale problems where neural networks are most impactful. Can the authors suggest any potential strategies to tighten these bounds or otherwise mitigate this scalability issue? Without a clear path forward, the method appears limited to a niche of small-scale problems.

4.  **Practicality of Satisfying the Optimality Condition:** The global optimality guarantee is conditional on `ρ(A) < 1`. While this condition can be checked a priori, the paper does not propose an efficient method for finding hyperparameters (e.g., `n1`, `pw`, `pu`, `ρw`, `ρu`) that satisfy it. The current approach seems to rely on random search followed by a check. This could require an expensive and potentially vast search over model configurations, which undermines the practical advantage of a "parameter-free" optimization algorithm. Is there a more principled way to select a valid set of architectural parameters?

In summary, the paper presents a novel and theoretically elegant method for globally optimal training of a constrained neural network. However, its practical significance is undermined by the restrictive nature of the model, unconvincing empirical results, and major scalability concerns. To make a stronger case for acceptance, the authors should provide better motivation for their model's architectural choices and demonstrate its value on more challenging problems or through more rigorous and insightful experimental comparisons.
