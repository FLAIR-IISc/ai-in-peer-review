- Paper presents theory showing a class of neural networks attains global optimality with linear convergence under positivity and generalized-polynomial activations.  
- Theory covers single-hidden-layer networks and extends to multiple hidden layers.  
- Authors propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.  
- Experiments show strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on small UCI datasets.  
- Main strength: first potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees.  
- Preconditions are relatively simple, depending only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix of network parameters.  
- Theory applies to networks of arbitrary depth and shows reasonable empirical results on small datasets.  
- Major limitation: requires all inputs and linear-layer weights to be positive, which may limit model expressiveness.  
- Activation functions must be generalized polynomials and all hidden layers must use different activations, restricting applicability.  
- Spectral-radius (<1) constraint forces increasing p1,p2 and decreasing pw,pu, effectively shrinking linear-unit weights and reducing expressiveness as dimension grows.  
- Concern that when linear weights become very small, deeper networks collapse to near-linear behavior, undermining extension to deep architectures.  
- Recommendation: accept the paper despite limitations.  
- Minor comment/question: request empirical or analytic study of how p1,p2,pw,pu, input dimension, and parameter size affect the spectral radius rho(A) (e.g., linear vs. exponential scaling).
