### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for the globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks, which the authors call Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks. The central idea is to formulate the training problem as finding the unique fixed point of a contractive mapping. By imposing non-negativity constraints on network weights and input data, and by optimizing a modified objective function, the authors leverage the Banach fixed-point theorem to design an iterative algorithm that is guaranteed to converge to the unique global optimum at a linear rate. The paper positions this work as a practically feasible alternative to other theoretical approaches for global neural network optimization, such as those based on tensor methods or structured matrix factorization, which are often difficult to apply in practice. The condition for convergence can be checked *a priori* for a given model architecture by computing the spectral radius of a small, well-defined matrix.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

*   **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this work is its rigorous theoretical result. In a field dominated by heuristic, gradient-based optimization, this paper provides a practical algorithm that provably finds the unique global optimum for a non-trivial neural network model. The guarantee of global optimality with a linear convergence rate is a notable achievement.

*   **Novel Methodology:** The application of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point theory on a space equipped with the Thompson metric is an elegant and creative approach to tackling the challenging non-convex nature of neural network training. The connection established between the optimization problem and the fixed point of a contraction mapping is the technical core of the paper and is well-developed.

*   **A Priori Convergence Check:** A key feature of the method is that the condition guaranteeing convergence—that the spectral radius ρ(A) of a particular matrix is less than one—can be verified before training commences. This provides a clear, upfront check on whether the global optimality guarantee holds for a chosen network architecture.

*   **Parameter-Free Optimization Algorithm:** Once the model architecture is fixed, the iterative update rule itself is deterministic and does not require tuning of hyperparameters like learning rates, which is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent and its variants.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance)**

*   **Restrictive Model Assumptions:** The method's applicability is constrained by a set of strong assumptions that limit its practical utility.
    *   The requirement of non-negative weights and input data restricts the method to specific domains like count-based data or images, and prevents the learning of inhibitory connections.
    *   The model uses generalized polynomial activations, excluding more common and powerful activation functions like ReLUs.
    *   The objective function is non-standard, including a term that encourages maximizing the total network output. The effect of this modification on model generalization is not well understood.

*   **Scalability of the Convergence Guarantee:** The convergence guarantee is contingent on the condition ρ(A) < 1. As the authors acknowledge, the entries of matrix A, and thus its spectral radius, grow with model complexity (e.g., number of hidden units, depth) and data characteristics (e.g., maximum data norm). This makes it challenging to satisfy the condition for the larger, deeper networks typically required for complex, high-dimensional datasets. This limitation is reflected in the experiments, which are confined to small-scale UCI datasets.

*   **Unconvincing Empirical Performance:** While the paper is commendable for being one of the first to provide empirical results for a globally optimal training method on real datasets, the performance is not compelling. The proposed NLSM models are often not competitive with, and are sometimes outperformed by, standard baselines like an RBF-kernel SVM and simple ReLU networks. This raises critical questions about the trade-off between provable optimality and the expressive power of the model class. The benefits of achieving a global optimum are diminished if that optimum corresponds to a suboptimal solution in terms of test accuracy.

*   **Complexity of Model Selection:** Although the optimization algorithm is parameter-free, selecting a valid model architecture is complex. It requires choosing multiple hyperparameters (e.g., number of units n1, exponents α, norms ρw and ρu) and then ensuring the spectral condition holds. The paper proposes a random search over these parameters, which seems rather inefficient and unintuitive.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a theoretically elegant and original piece of work on neural network optimization. The proof of convergence to a unique global optimum is a significant theoretical result. However, the practical relevance of the method is questionable due to the highly restrictive model assumptions, the scalability issues inherent in the convergence condition, and the modest performance in empirical evaluations. While the paper is valuable as a theoretical exploration of provable optimization, the proposed model architecture does not currently appear to be a competitive alternative to standard neural networks for practical machine learning applications.
