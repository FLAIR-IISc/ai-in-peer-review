- Presents a novel theory proving the existence of a global optimum for a class of neural networks with linear convergence.
- Requires positivity of input data and linear-layer weights, and activation functions to be generalized polynomials.
- Theory is proved for a single hidden layer and extended to multiple hidden layers.
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods.
- Main contribution: a global optimality guarantee with simple preconditions depending on the spectral radius of a nonnegative parameter matrix.
- Empirically shown to work on arbitrary-depth networks and achieves reasonable/competitive results on small UCI and synthetic datasets.
- Limitations: positive-weight constraint may reduce model expressiveness and the requirement that all hidden activations differ is unnatural.
- Spectral-radius (<1) requirement forces adjusting p1,p2,pw,pu (reducing linear-unit weights), which may hurt expressiveness as input/network dimension grows and challenge scalability to deep architectures.
- Overall an encouraging, theoretically solid initial result demonstrating the potential of nonlinear spectral methods.
- Minor recommendations: analyze how p1,p2,pw,pu and dimensions affect rho(A), provide more experimental parameter details, and expand discussion of limitations and future work.
- Reviewer verdict: strengths outweigh weaknesses; recommended for acceptance.
