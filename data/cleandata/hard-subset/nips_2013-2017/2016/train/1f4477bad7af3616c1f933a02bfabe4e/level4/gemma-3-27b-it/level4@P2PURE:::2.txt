This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that are provably trainable to a global optimum with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method was applied to deep networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluated on several real-world datasets. However, the assumptions required to achieve these theoretical guarantees are restrictive and potentially limit the practical applicability of the approach. Specifically, the method necessitates non-negativity constraints on network weights, strong regularization of these weights, and the use of the relatively uncommon generalized polynomial activation function. Justification for these constraints, perhaps through application-specific insights or demonstrating benefits on more challenging datasets, would be more compelling than further restricting the network architecture to ensure global optimality. 

While the authors claim their targeted network class retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries, the empirical results do not strongly support this assertion. The comparisons to linear SVMs appear unfavorable; linear SVMs achieve superior performance even with potentially suboptimal tuning, while the nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) requires network structure optimization. Furthermore, given that NLSM is a nonlinear model, using a linear SVM as a baseline seems questionable. A comparison against nonlinear SVMs with polynomial or Gaussian kernels would provide a more appropriate and fair evaluation. Consequently, the current results do not convincingly demonstrate the expressive power of the studied neural network class.

The datasets used, sourced from the UCI repository, are relatively small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. The analysis also reveals that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A scale with the number of hidden units, potentially posing a significant challenge for high-dimensional data. Addressing this scalability issue is crucial for broader applicability. 

Finally, while the authors note the optimality condition can be verified without network training, its practical utility is limited without prior knowledge or when the parameter space is vast. Developing a fast method for identifying a small set of valid parameter candidates would be valuable. It is also worth investigating whether the observed global optimality stems from the specific objective function employed, and whether stochastic gradient descent might empirically converge to a global optimum on tested datasets.
