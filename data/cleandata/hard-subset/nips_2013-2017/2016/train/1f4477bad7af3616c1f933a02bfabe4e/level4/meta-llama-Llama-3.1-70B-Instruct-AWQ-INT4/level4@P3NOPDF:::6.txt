The paper proposes a method to train, under certain assumptions, a specific type of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate that their method can be applied to a selection of "real-world" problems. The problem under investigation seems interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated. However, the paper falls short in convincing the reader that the proposed class of neural networks is useful in practice.

In particular, the authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model, but they do not provide sufficient thoughts on what can be done and what is out of reach for their approach. A discussion on concurrent approaches and how they contrast with the proposed method would be beneficial. Furthermore, the experimental results do not clarify the limitations of applicability of their model. When reading the experimental results, I was surprised that the authors only compare their approach to some SVM and ignore other methods, including other training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms.

Additionally, I would have expected some information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms. The introduction is well-written, but the presentation has some room for improvement. Specifically, Section 2 does not read fluently, and there are some typos that need to be corrected.

Some specific remarks on the paper are as follows: While the definition of the loss function is straightforward, the function Φ lacks motivation. The role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal, and I think this should be added to the paper as well. Theorem 1 should be explained informally before being stated, as the statement itself is hard to parse. The authors mentioned in the rebuttal that the statement "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" stems from their experimental observation. This should be clarified in the paper.

There are also some minor errors that need to be corrected. On line 60, "polyomial" should be spelled as "polynomial." On line 67, the phrase "one CAN model" should be rephrased for clarity. Additionally, the variables p1 and p2 are not introduced, and the notation R_{++} is not defined, which is unfortunate since there are several other variables whose definition points to that one. Thankfully, the authors clarified this in the rebuttal, but it would be beneficial to include this explanation in the paper itself.

Overall, the paper has some interesting ideas, but it needs to be improved in terms of presentation and clarity. With some revisions to address the mentioned issues, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.
