This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that are provably trainable to a globally optimal solution with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method is applied to deep networks with one and two hidden layers, and evaluated on a series of real-world datasets. As the authors acknowledge, this class of networks is restrictive, requiring non-negativity constraints on the weights and maximization of weight regularization – assumptions that are somewhat counterintuitive. Furthermore, the method relies on the use of generalized polynomials as activation functions, which are not commonly employed. While these assumptions enable the theoretical guarantees, they limit the practical appeal of the approach. More detailed justification, grounded in application-specific considerations or analyses on real-world datasets, would be beneficial, rather than further restricting the model to achieve global optimality.

The authors claim that, despite these constraints, the targeted class of neural networks retains sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves competitive performance. However, the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. In the presented experiments, linear SVMs appear to outperform the proposed NLSM1 and NLSM2 models, even in cases where the SVM’s hyperparameters appear to be sub-optimally tuned, while the NLSM models benefited from network structure optimization. Moreover, given that NLSM is a nonlinear model, the choice of a *linear* SVM as a baseline is questionable. A more appropriate comparison would involve nonlinear SVMs, such as those employing polynomial or Gaussian kernels. Consequently, the comparisons currently presented feel unfair, and the conclusions drawn from them are not entirely convincing. The results, as they stand, do not definitively demonstrate the expressive power of the studied neural network class.

The datasets used from the UCI repository are relatively small, both in terms of sample size and feature dimensionality. Based on the analysis presented, the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A scale with the number of hidden units, which poses a challenge for high-dimensional data. Is there a strategy for mitigating this issue? Overcoming this limitation is crucial, as it currently prevents the method’s application to a large range of practical problems.

The theoretical optimality condition, while checkable without training, appears difficult to satisfy in practice. If no prior knowledge exists or the parameter space is vast, verifying this condition becomes problematic. Is there a faster approach to identify a small set of valid parameter candidates? Furthermore, it is worth considering whether the global optimality of this model stems from the specific objective function employed. Finally, it would be interesting to investigate whether stochastic gradient descent could also empirically converge to a global optimum on the tested datasets.
