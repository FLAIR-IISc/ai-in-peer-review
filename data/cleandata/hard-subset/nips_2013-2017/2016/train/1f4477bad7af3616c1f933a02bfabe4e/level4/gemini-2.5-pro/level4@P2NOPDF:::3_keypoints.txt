- The paper presents a theoretical framework proving a class of neural networks has a global optimum reachable with linear convergence, under non-negative inputs/weights and generalized polynomial activations.

- Theory is established for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to multi-layer architectures; a nonlinear spectral-method optimization algorithm is proposed.

- Experiments on a 2D synthetic dataset and small UCI datasets provide a proof-of-concept that the algorithm performs well on small-scale problems.

- Strength: One of the first potentially practical network architectures with provable global optimality and convergence — a significant contribution.

- Strength: Preconditions for guarantees are relatively simple and directly checkable (spectral radius of a specific non-negative parameter matrix < 1) compared to prior work.

- Strength: Theoretical framework generalizes from shallow to deep networks, and empirical results support this extension.

- Weakness: Strong constraints (non-negativity and activation form) may limit practical applicability and expressive power.

- Weakness: Requiring all network parameters to be non-negative prevents learning inhibitory connections, reducing expressiveness relative to standard networks.

- Weakness: Mandating different activation functions for each hidden layer is unnatural and complicates model design and hyperparameter tuning.

- Weakness/concern: The spectral radius constraint ρ(A) < 1 may force weights to shrink as dimensions or hidden sizes grow, causing generalized polynomial activations to behave nearly linearly and undermining depth/expressiveness, raising scalability concerns.

- Conclusion: Despite limitations, the paper offers important theoretical advances and is recommended for acceptance.

- Minor comment: Request analysis or empirical study of how ρ(A) scales with input dimension, hidden units, and hyperparameters (p1, p2, pw, pu) to inform hyperparameter tuning and assess scalability.
