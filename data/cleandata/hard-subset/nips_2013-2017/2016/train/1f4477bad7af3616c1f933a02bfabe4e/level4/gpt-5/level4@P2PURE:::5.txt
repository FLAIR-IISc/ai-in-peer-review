The paper introduces a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimum can be obtained. As a consequence, it proposes an algorithm that finds this global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivations are instructive, but several aspects remain unclear to me:

- Theorem-level derivatives: Theorem 3 relies on first-order derivatives (as reflected in the stated inequalities), whereas Theorem 4 escalates to second-order derivatives. What necessitates this jump from first to second order?

- Treatment of layers and parameterization: The architecture seems to treat the final layer differently from the hidden layers. Since u is a matrix of parameters connected to a hidden layer, is the gradient with respect to u in G^Φ (3) simply the gradient with respect to vec(u)? Similar ambiguities appear elsewhere, e.g., when constraining u to lie within a ball.

- Objective in (2): Why does the model maximize the ℓ1-norm of the connection parameters in (2)? In most NN models, weight decay is used to reduce the ℓ1-norm. It appears that the arbitrarily small ε in (2) is introduced primarily to make the gradient strictly positive so it lies in V_++, which seems needed for the proof but is not well motivated for practical neural networks. (Am I missing something?)

- Absence of free parameters and positivity constraints: What guarantees that there is no free parameter? The formulation requires all data to be non-negative and enforces strictly positive weights (not even zero), so activations only accumulate. How practical is this design?

- Experimental comparison and practical value: The experiments compare the proposed NN to SVM, presumably because SVM also admits a global optimizer. In practice, in what respects does this class of NNs offer additional value over commonly used architectures (e.g., ReLU networks)?
