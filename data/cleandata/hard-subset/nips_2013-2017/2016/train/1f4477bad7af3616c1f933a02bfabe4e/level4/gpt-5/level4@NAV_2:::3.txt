This paper develops a compelling theoretical framework showing that a specific family of neural networks admits globally optimal training with a linear convergence rate. The key constraints are that both the inputs and all network weights are nonnegative, and the activations are generalized polynomial functions. The theory is established first for one-hidden-layer architectures and then extended to two hidden layers, with a principled path to deeper networks. The authors derive an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method and validate it on a simple 2D synthetic task and several small real-world UCI datasets, where it performs competitively.

Pros: As the authors argue, this appears to be the first practically viable training method for neural networks that comes with both global optimality and linear convergence guarantees. Prior guarantees either hinge on conditions that are difficult or infeasible to verify in practice (e.g., assumptions involving the data-generating density and score tensors), or are acknowledged to be hard to deploy. In contrast, the precondition here reduces to checking that the spectral radius of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix (depending only on model hyperparameters) is less than one—something one can verify a priori. The paper also outlines how the analysis generalizes in principle to deeper architectures and provides reasonable empirical performance on small datasets.

Cons: The applicability of the model is still restricted. The framework requires nonnegativity of both inputs and parameters, generalized polynomial activations, and the spectral radius condition (Theorem 1) to hold. While shifting inputs to be nonnegative is often manageable, the nonnegativity of weights can reduce expressivity. Moreover, the requirement that hidden units use distinct exponents (different α’s) is somewhat unnatural and may limit adoption. Finally, to enforce the spectral radius constraint in practice, one typically needs larger p-exponents (pw, pu) and smaller norm radii (ρw, ρu), effectively shrinking the magnitude of linear weights. As the input dimension and model size grow, the need to dampen these weights may increase, pushing the model toward nearly linear behavior; in that regime, additional layers add little expressive power. This raises concerns about scalability to genuinely deep architectures. Despite these caveats, the result is encouraging as a first step, and I believe the paper merits acceptance.

Minor comments: I would be interested in a more systematic characterization (theoretical or empirical) of how ρ(A) scales with pw, pu, ρw, ρu, input dimensionality, and network width/depth. For instance, does ρ(A) tend to grow linearly or exponentially with input dimension or number of hidden units, and how should pw, pu (and/or ρw, ρu) be adjusted to compensate? Even if a full proof is difficult, empirical plots on synthetic data illustrating these relationships would be very informative.
