Summary:
This paper studies a restricted class of neural networks and proves that, under specific constraints, the loss has a single stationary point, which is also the global minimizer. The authors construct a contractive map whose unique fixed point coincides with this minimizer, and they propose a fast fixed-point iteration that converges to it. The work illustrates that, beyond convexity or geodesic convexity, fixed-point theory can establish uniqueness and enable globally convergent training for certain nonconvex models.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity and originality: The paper provides a compelling example in which a relatively complex, constrained neural network admits a unique stationary point, established via a contractive mapping. This is an elegant and instructive use of fixed-point theory in a nonconvex setting.
- Constructive algorithmic contribution: The contractive map yields a practical iteration with a convergence guarantee (when the spectral condition holds), linking theory to an implementable method.
- Potential impact on theory-guided architecture design: The result may inspire further work on constraints or parameterizations that ensure uniqueness and global convergence.

Weaknesses:
- Fragile convergence condition: The guarantee depends on bounding the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix. Verifying this bound appears burdensome and problem-specific, and the paper acknowledges this as the reason for not scaling to networks with more hidden units. This substantially limits applicability.
- Heavy and unintuitive hyperparameterization: The method involves many tunable parameters, with little guidance on principled selection. Moreover, for each configuration the spectral condition must be checked, compounding the complexity.
- Experimental limitations: Despite searching approximately 150,000 hyperparameter combinations and selecting by cross-validation accuracy, the proposed method outperforms a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets. This raises concerns about practical utility, especially given the tuning effort.
- Empirical scope: The experimental section would benefit from stronger baselines and a clearer accounting of computational cost. As presented, the evidence does not convincingly demonstrate advantages over simpler models.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can the spectral-radius requirement be replaced by a simpler, easily verifiable sufficient condition stated directly in terms of the tunable parameters? Even conservative bounds that obviate per-configuration spectral checks would be valuable.
- Are there theoretical size or parameter-range bounds (e.g., on hidden-layer width, weights, or step sizes) that guarantee contractivity and would enable scaling beyond the small networks considered?
- Provide sensitivity analyses to show how convergence and accuracy depend on key parameters. This could motivate a reduced set of hyperparameters or principled defaults that make the procedure more user-friendly.
- Report runtime, number of iterations to convergence, and overall resource usage relative to baselines, under comparable tuning budgets. Without this, it is hard to assess the practical efficiency of the method.
- Offer guidance on when this architecture should excel (e.g., data distributions or structural assumptions), to explain the mixed empirical results and help practitioners decide when the approach is appropriate.

Overall assessment:
The paper offers an interesting theoretical contribution that cleanly applies fixed-point theory to obtain a unique stationary point and a globally convergent algorithm for a constrained neural network class. However, the reliance on a difficult-to-verify spectral condition, the large hyperparameter burden, limited scalability, and modest empirical performance weaken the practical impact. I lean toward rejection in the current form. I would be more positive if the authors could (i) provide parameter-independent or easy-to-check sufficient conditions ensuring contractivity for larger models, and (ii) strengthen the empirical section with stronger baselines, ablations/sensitivity analyses, and efficiency metrics that demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed approach.
