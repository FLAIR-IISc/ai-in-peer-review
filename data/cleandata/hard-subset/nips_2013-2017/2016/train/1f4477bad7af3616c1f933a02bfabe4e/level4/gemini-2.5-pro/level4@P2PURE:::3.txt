This paper presents a theoretical framework demonstrating that for a specific class of neural networks, a global optimum exists and can be found with a linear convergence rate. The primary constraints of this framework are that all input data and linear layer weights must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The theory is first developed for networks with a single hidden layer and is subsequently extended to multi-layer architectures. An accompanying optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method is also proposed. Experimental results on a 2D synthetic dataset demonstrate strong performance, and the method achieves reasonable results on several small real-world UCI datasets.

**Pros:**

*   The key contribution of this work, as highlighted by the authors, is that it presents one of the first potentially practical frameworks for neural networks that includes both optimality and convergence guarantees.
*   In contrast to prior work, where convergence guarantees often rely on preconditions that are difficult or impossible to verify in practice (e.g., dependencies on the unknown data-generating distribution), the proposed theory has simpler preconditions. Specifically, the conditions depend only on the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix constructed from the network's parameters, making them readily verifiable.
*   Furthermore, the theoretical results are shown to apply to networks of arbitrary depth, and the empirical validation on real datasets, while limited to small-scale problems, is promising.

**Cons:**

*   Despite its theoretical elegance, the proposed framework has several limitations that may restrict its practical applicability. The theory requires positive input data, positive network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (from Theorem 1) of less than one.
*   The constraint on positive inputs is likely a minor issue, as most datasets can be shifted to satisfy this condition. However, the requirement for all network parameters to be positive is more restrictive and may significantly limit the model's expressive power.
*   The requirement that each hidden layer must have a different activation function is also unconventional and could further limit practical use cases.
*   Finally, a significant concern relates to the scalability of this approach to deep architectures. To satisfy the spectral radius constraint, the authors note that parameters `pw` and `pu` must be decreased (while `p1` and `p2` are increased), effectively reducing the magnitude of the linear weights. It appears that as the dimensionality of the input and network parameters grows, the weights must become even smaller to maintain the constraint. When these weights are very small, the generalized polynomial activations behave approximately as linear functions. In such a regime, stacking additional layers would be akin to stacking linear transformations, offering no significant increase in the network's expressiveness. This raises doubts about the practical utility of this framework for deep models.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging and novel theoretical result. As an initial step towards provably convergent training for neural networks, its contributions are significant, and I recommend acceptance.

**Minor Comments:**

I am curious about the quantitative relationship between the spectral radius `rho(A)` and the various model parameters (`p1`, `p2`, `pw`, `pu`, input dimension, layer sizes). For instance, as the input dimension increases, does `rho(A)` grow linearly or exponentially? Consequently, must `pw` and `pu` be decreased linearly or exponentially to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be difficult, an empirical study on synthetic data illustrating these relationships would be a valuable addition to the paper.
