

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to achieving globally optimal training for a specific class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors focus on generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and real-valued activation functions (powers), demonstrating that under certain conditions (nonnegative data, specific parameter constraints), their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence. This work builds upon recent advances in global optimization for neural networks, particularly the tensor-based approach by Janzamin et al. (NIPS 2015) and the structured matrix factorization method by Haeffele and Vidal (NIPS 2015). Unlike previous work that either requires estimating the data-generating measure (Janzamin et al.) or provides impractical guarantees (Haeffele and Vidal), the authors claim their method is both theoretically sound and practically feasible.

The paper's main strengths lie in its strong theoretical contributions. The authors provide a rigorous proof of global optimality with linear convergence under relatively weak assumptions (nonnegative data), and they show how to check the convergence conditions by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix. This avoids the need for hyperparameter tuning that plagues stochastic gradient descent approaches. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance compared to standard neural networks (with ReLU) and SVMs, which is significant given that previous global optimization methods for neural networks have not included real-data experiments.

The paper is well-structured and technically sound, with detailed proofs and a clear development of the nonlinear spectral method. The theoretical framework is elegantly developed using fixed-point theory and Thompson metrics, and the main theorem (Theorem 1) is well-motivated. The extension to two-hidden-layer networks (Theorem 5) shows the approach's potential for broader application.

However, several important concerns warrant attention. First, the requirement for nonnegative data and weights is quite restrictive, limiting the method's applicability to a narrow range of problems (e.g., count data like images and word frequencies). The paper does not sufficiently address how this constraint affects practical utility, particularly since most real-world datasets do not naturally conform to nonnegative features. Second, the modified objective function (which includes maximizing the sum of outputs) appears to be primarily a technical device to ensure strictly positive gradients for the proof, rather than a natural extension of standard neural network training. This raises questions about the method's practical relevance, as it deviates significantly from conventional neural network optimization.

The paper's claim that there are "no free parameters" in the algorithm requires careful interpretation. While the method doesn't require learning rate tuning, it does require choosing several architectural parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) and verifying the spectral radius condition, which necessitates careful parameter selection. The authors acknowledge this in their experiments by selecting parameters via cross-validation, but the paper doesn't provide guidance for choosing these parameters in practice.

The experimental section is somewhat limited in scope, focusing only on low-dimensional UCI datasets. The comparison with ReLU networks is insufficiently contextualized; while the authors show competitive performance, they don't adequately address the practical trade-offs between their method and standard neural networks (e.g., computational cost, scalability to high-dimensional data). The claim that their method is "practically feasible" is somewhat undermined by the fact that the spectral radius condition grows with the number of hidden units, making the method potentially infeasible for deeper or larger networks.

Regarding the draft reviewer's specific concerns: The first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 are sufficient for the fixed-point theorem, while Theorem 4 requires bounding the second derivatives to establish the Lipschitz condition for the contraction mapping. The treatment of the last layer is consistent with the mathematical framework of the proof, and the 1-norm maximization is a technical requirement for the proof's gradient positivity condition, not a practical recommendation. The arbitrarily small ε is indeed a technical artifact to ensure strict positivity in the proof, though it's not a standard practice in neural network optimization.

In terms of NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with complete proofs, though the practical applicability is limited by restrictive assumptions.
- Clarity: Well-written with clear mathematical development, though some technical details (like the objective modification) could be better motivated.
- Originality: The approach is novel in providing a practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks, with clear distinctions from prior work.
- Significance: The theoretical contribution is significant for optimization in deep learning, but the practical significance is somewhat constrained by the restrictive assumptions.

Pro: Theoretically sound global optimality guarantee with linear convergence, practical feasibility compared to prior global methods, and competitive results on benchmark datasets.

Con: Restrictive nonnegative data and weight constraints limit practical applicability, the modified objective lacks practical motivation, and the method's scalability to larger networks is questionable.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization, but its practical value is limited by the restrictive assumptions. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical trade-offs and potential extensions to more general settings. Given the high theoretical quality and the novelty of the approach, I recommend acceptance, though the authors should address the practical limitations in the final version.
