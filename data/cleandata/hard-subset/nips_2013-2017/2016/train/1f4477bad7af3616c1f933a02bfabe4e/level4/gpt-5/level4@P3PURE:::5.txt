This paper defines a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimizer exists, and it proposes an algorithm that provably finds this optimizer with a linear convergence rate. The theoretical development is interesting and, in several places, instructive. However, a number of points need clarification, and several modeling choices raise concerns about practicality.

Strengths
- Clear and ambitious goal: to characterize a network class with a unique global optimum and to provide an algorithm that attains it.
- The convergence guarantee (linear rate) is appealing, and the derivations are generally insightful.

Points needing clarification
1) Theorem 3 vs. Theorem 4 (order of derivatives)
- Theorem 3 appears to rely on first-order derivatives as reflected in the presented inequalities, while Theorem 4 advances to second-order reasoning. What necessitates this shift? Is there a gap that first-order conditions cannot close (e.g., establishing strict convexity/concavity or uniqueness) that forces the use of a second-derivative argument? Please clarify the precise role of first- vs. second-order conditions and whether the inequalities in Theorem 3 are insufficient for the conclusions drawn in Theorem 4.

2) Treatment of the last layer and the parameter u
- The architecture seems to treat the last layer differently from hidden layers. Since u is a matrix of parameters connecting to the hidden layer, is the gradient with respect to u in G^Φ (3) taken with respect to the vectorization of u? Similarly, when bounding u to lie within a ball, is the norm applied to the vectorized parameter (equivalently, the Frobenius norm of the matrix), or is a different norm intended? This ambiguity recurs in multiple places; a consistent statement of shapes and norms would help.

Modeling and practicality concerns
- Objective in (2): maximizing the 1-norm of connection parameters. This is at odds with common practice in neural networks, where weight decay reduces parameter norms (L1 or L2). My reading is that the arbitrarily small ε in (2) serves to ensure gradients are strictly positive so that they lie in V_{++}. If so, this seems motivated by the proof rather than by practical considerations. Could you justify this choice empirically or provide an alternative formulation that retains the theoretical properties without requiring 1-norm maximization?

- “No free parameter” claim. What precisely warrants the statement that there is no free parameter here? If the formulation fixes ε > 0 and imposes norm or positivity constraints, those are effectively hyperparameters. Clarifying what is or is not user-tuned would strengthen the presentation.

- Positivity constraints and data requirements. The approach appears to require nonnegative data and strictly positive weights (not even zero), so activations are always accumulative. How practical is this in real-world settings with signed inputs or features that benefit from inhibitory interactions? If zero weights are disallowed, how is sparsity expressed? More generally, what are the limitations of the “all-positive” design for representation power?

Experimental evaluation
- The comparison to SVM is understandable given both methods’ global optimality. However, for practical relevance, it would be important to demonstrate whether this network class offers advantages over commonly used neural networks (e.g., ReLU-based MLPs), such as improved accuracy, robustness, calibration, sample efficiency, or training stability. As it stands, it is unclear in what scenarios practitioners would prefer this architecture over standard NNs.

Overall assessment and suggestions
- The paper’s theoretical aim is compelling, and the linear convergence result is a notable contribution for the considered network class. To strengthen the work:
  - Clarify the necessity of second-order arguments in Theorem 4 relative to the first-order inequalities in Theorem 3.
  - Make the treatment of u precise (matrix vs. vectorization) and state clearly which norms are used when constraining parameters.
  - Justify the maximization of the L1 norm in (2) beyond facilitating proofs; explain the role of ε and V_{++} in a way that connects to practice.
  - Discuss the practicality of requiring nonnegative data and strictly positive weights, and articulate when this design is advantageous.
  - Expand experiments to include comparisons with standard ReLU networks of similar capacity to assess whether the guaranteed global optimum translates into tangible performance benefits.

I may be missing some intended interpretations, but addressing these questions would greatly improve both clarity and practical impact.
