This paper introduces a novel class of feedforward neural networks with a provably optimal global optimizer, and presents an algorithm to find this optimizer with a demonstrated linear convergence rate. The mathematical development is generally well-presented and insightful, though some aspects could benefit from further clarification. Specifically: 1) The transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4 is not immediately obvious – a more detailed explanation of this shift would be helpful. 2) The network architecture appears to treat the final layer distinctly from the hidden layers. Given that ‘u’ represents parameters connecting to the hidden layers, the gradient calculation with respect to ‘u’ in G<sup>Φ</sup> (3) seems to involve the gradient of the vectorization of ‘u’. This pattern, and the subsequent bounding of ‘u’ within a ball, arises in multiple places and warrants further discussion. 

A key question concerns the motivation for maximizing the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2), a design choice that contrasts with the typical weight decay regularization employed in most neural networks. While the introduction of a small epsilon appears to ensure a strictly positive gradient for inclusion in V<sub>++</sub>, its justification in the context of practical neural network training is unclear and feels primarily driven by the requirements of the proof.  Furthermore, the constraint of strictly positive weights, coupled with the requirement for non-negative data, raises concerns about the practicality of this design and the potential for unbounded accumulation.  The experimental comparison to SVM, justified by SVM’s own global optimality, doesn’t fully address the question of whether this network structure offers advantages over more conventional neural network architectures (e.g., those utilizing ReLU activations) in real-world applications.  It would be beneficial to understand what specific scenarios might warrant the use of this specialized network over existing methods.
