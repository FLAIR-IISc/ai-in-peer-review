This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method that provably trains a specific class of feedforward neural networks with a linear convergence rate. The condition ensuring global optimality depends on network architecture parameters and reduces to checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. This reduction is elegant and, to my knowledge, novel in the context of neural network training.

The proposed framework is conceptually interesting: applying spectral methods to obtain global optimality guarantees under explicit, verifiable conditions is a compelling idea. The clarity of the condition—via the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix—adds to the appeal and potential practicality of the approach within its intended scope.

However, the method’s applicability appears quite limited. It requires nonnegativity constraints on the network weights and relies on nonstandard, highly specific activation functions. These restrictions are strong and likely to curtail the expressive power of the resulting models compared with commonly used architectures. In practice, such constraints may hinder performance on typical tasks, as suggested by the empirical results, where the method underperforms standard SVM baselines.

The empirical evaluation reinforces this concern: despite the attractive theory, the reported performance relative to SVMs is not competitive. This gap raises questions about the practical utility of the approach beyond the specialized setting where its assumptions hold. It would be helpful for the paper to more directly discuss the trade-off between the strong theoretical guarantees and the restrictive modeling choices, and to clarify for which application domains these constraints are acceptable or even beneficial.

Minor comment (L.90–91): The claim that “the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate and thus converges quickly, typically in a few (less than 10) iterations” is overstated. Linear convergence does not, by itself, imply convergence in a small, fixed number of iterations; the actual speed depends on the contraction factor and problem scaling, among other factors. A more measured statement or empirical evidence (e.g., iteration counts across datasets) would be preferable.

In summary, the paper offers an original and theoretically solid contribution by leveraging nonlinear spectral analysis to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate for a restricted class of neural networks. Yet the stringent nonnegativity and activation-function constraints—and the relatively weak empirical performance—substantially limit its practical relevance. Clarifying the scope of applicability, tempering claims about convergence speed, and providing stronger empirical support would strengthen the paper.
