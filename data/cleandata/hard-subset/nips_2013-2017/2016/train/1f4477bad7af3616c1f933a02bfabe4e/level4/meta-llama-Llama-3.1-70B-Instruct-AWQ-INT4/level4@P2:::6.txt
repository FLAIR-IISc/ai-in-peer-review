The paper proposes a method to train, under certain assumptions, a specific type of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate that their method can be applied on a selection of "real-world" problems. The problem under investigation seems interesting and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear summary of the main ideas and their relation to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors do a good job of explaining the optimization problem behind neural networks and the limitations of traditional training methods. The introduction is well-written and provides a good background on the topic.

The main contribution of the paper is the proposal of a nonlinear spectral method for training generalized polynomial neural networks. The authors prove that this method converges to the global optimum under certain assumptions and demonstrate its effectiveness on a selection of UCI datasets. The experimental results are promising, and the authors provide a good comparison with other methods, including stochastic gradient descent and support vector machines.

However, the paper falls a bit short in convincing that the proposed class of neural networks is useful in practice. The authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model but do not provide a thorough discussion on its limitations and potential applications. Additionally, the experimental results do not clarify the limitations of applicability of their model.

Some specific remarks:

* The definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the function Φ lacks motivation. The role of the epsilon term is not clearly explained, and it would be helpful to provide more context.
* Theorem 1 is hard to parse, and it would be helpful to provide an informal explanation before stating it.
* The statement that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" is not clearly supported by the experimental results. It would be helpful to provide more context and clarify that this is an experimental observation.
* There are some typos and notation issues, such as the use of "polyomial" instead of "polynomial" and the introduction of variables without clear definitions.

Overall, the paper provides a good contribution to the field of neural networks and optimization. However, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper provides a novel method for training neural networks with a proven convergence guarantee.
* The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on a selection of UCI datasets.
* The paper provides a good comparison with other methods, including stochastic gradient descent and support vector machines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper falls short in convincing that the proposed class of neural networks is useful in practice.
* The experimental results do not clarify the limitations of applicability of their model.
* There are some typos and notation issues that need to be addressed.
